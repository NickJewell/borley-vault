# T. S. Frankland

Of Trinity College, Cambridge. Frankland attended a planchette séance at the [[rectory-cottage|Borley Priory cottage]] on the evening of 8 June 1948, in a company that included Mr and Mrs [[james-turner|James Turner]], the Reverend [[john-dening|John Dening]], and [[mrs-g-taylor|Mrs G. Taylor of Chelsea]].

## The Séance

The séance began at about 12:30 am using a wine glass on a table. Messages were received from an entity claiming to be "Harry Bull," who stated he would haunt Borley for "4004 years" and, when asked whose footsteps had been heard on the churchyard path, replied "Lionel Foyster." The entity then alleged "foul play" in connection with the death of the late [[lionel-foyster|Reverend L. A. Foyster]] — allegations the sitters sensibly did not pursue.

Frankland was notably sceptical throughout. He watched the sitters closely and noticed that "whenever the wine glass they used had moved, the flexor tendons controlling the index fingers of the sitters on the base of the wineglass became constricted" — indicating unconscious muscular pressure.

He proposed to demonstrate his point:

> "'You know, nothing will happen if I put my finger on the glass... like this...' He wanted to demonstrate his point — whereupon the glass immediately executed three wide circles, clearly with a motive power that was far greater than at any time during the evening."

Frankland, "greatly startled," asked why they had been sent to the church. The reply: "Pray." He then asked who was in the church now. The letters **C-H-R-I-S-T** were spelled out slowly and firmly. Frankland immediately ended the experiment.

He later told Underwood that "since the supposed entity was a priest in his lifetime on earth, the answer to his question was an extremely proper one; no one but a clergyman would have thought of such a response."

> "He was entirely convinced that something had moved the glass on this occasion; his own finger, laid gently on it, could not possibly have had the power to propel it."

Of the dozen or so wine-glass sessions in which [[john-dening|Dening]] had participated at Borley and elsewhere, Frankland and Dening agreed that this was the only occasion on which the glass seemed genuinely "alive."
