# T. S. Frankland

Of Trinity College, Cambridge. Frankland attended a planchette séance at the [[rectory-cottage|Borley Priory cottage]] on the evening of 8 June 1948, in a company that included Mr and Mrs [[james-turner|James Turner]], the Reverend [[john-dening|John Dening]], and [[mrs-g-taylor|Mrs G. Taylor of Chelsea]].

## The Séance

The séance began at about 12:30 am using a wine glass on a table. Messages were received from an entity claiming to be "Harry Bull," who stated he would haunt Borley for "4004 years" and, when asked whose footsteps had been heard on the churchyard path, replied "Lionel Foyster." The entity then alleged "foul play" in connection with the death of the late [[lionel-foyster|Reverend L. A. Foyster]] — allegations the sitters sensibly did not pursue.

Frankland was notably sceptical throughout. He watched the sitters closely and noticed that "whenever the wine glass they used had moved, the flexor tendons controlling the index fingers of the sitters on the base of the wineglass became constricted" — indicating unconscious muscular pressure.

He proposed to demonstrate his point:

> "'You know, nothing will happen if I put my finger on the glass... like this...' He wanted to demonstrate his point — whereupon the glass immediately executed three wide circles, clearly with a motive power that was far greater than at any time during the evening."

Frankland, "greatly startled," asked why they had been sent to the church. The reply: "Pray." He then asked who was in the church now. The letters **C-H-R-I-S-T** were spelled out slowly and firmly. Frankland immediately ended the experiment.

He later told Underwood that "since the supposed entity was a priest in his lifetime on earth, the answer to his question was an extremely proper one; no one but a clergyman would have thought of such a response."

> "He was entirely convinced that something had moved the glass on this occasion; his own finger, laid gently on it, could not possibly have had the power to propel it."

Of the dozen or so wine-glass sessions in which [[john-dening|Dening]] had participated at Borley and elsewhere, Frankland and Dening agreed that this was the only occasion on which the glass seemed genuinely "alive."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**T. S. Frankland** (frequently referred to in the sources as **Tommy Frankland**) was an **"eccentric young man who was larger than life"** who became a dedicated and experienced investigator of the Borley Rectory hauntings [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, specific investigations, and a dramatic shift in his personal skepticism regarding the haunting:

**Biographical Details and Background**
*   **Education and Profession:** Frankland was an undergraduate student at **Trinity College, Cambridge**, and a **Company Director** who maintained a deep interest in psychic activity [1, 2]. 
*   **Personal Life:** Hailing from a wealthy family, he maintained a flat in Cadogan Place (or Cadogan Square) in London [1, 2]. He was known to frequently put his **Rolls Royce** at the disposal of fellow investigator Peter Underwood, with whom he investigated other hauntings in the Cotswolds and at Langenhoe [1, 2]. 
*   **Religious Affiliation:** He was associated with the Rev. Dr. George MacLeod’s Iona Community [2]. 
*   **Death:** The sources note sadly that Frankland **"died far too young"** [2].

**The July 1947 Cellar Excavations**
During **July 1947**, Frankland assisted James Turner (then living in the Rectory Cottage) with a physical excavation of the ruined Rectory's cellars [2]. 
*   Working late into a Saturday night, Frankland and Turner shifted a great deal of rubble to discover the opening of a well, an event marked by **"a great escape of gas"** [2]. 
*   The following day, they dug into the hard clay to a depth of six feet and broke through the brick sides and bottom of the well [2]. Despite hours of strenuous digging, they found no bones or objects of interest, and the area was subsequently filled in and planted with rose bushes [2].

**The June 1948 Séances and the "Wine-Glass" Experiment**
Frankland is best known in the Borley literature for his participation in a highly dramatic séance held at the Rectory Cottage on the evening of **June 8, 1948** [3]. He sat alongside James and Catherine Turner, the Rev. John C. Dening, and Mrs. Gay Taylor [1, 3]. 
*   **The Church Visit:** During an early session around 12:30 p.m., the entity purported to be the deceased Rev. Harry Bull [1]. Frankland, who was **"becoming sceptical of the proceedings,"** challenged the entity, asking if it intended to manifest since it was the 21st anniversary of Bull's death [1]. The entity responded, **"Go to the church now,"** prompting the entire group to relocate to the locked church's porch [1].
*   **Skepticism of the Sitters:** Later that night, while waiting for the rest of the party to return from the church, Frankland attempted to put a nervous Mrs. Turner at ease [4]. He explained his skepticism, noting that he had watched the other sitters closely and observed that the **flexor tendons controlling their index fingers became constricted** whenever the wine-glass moved [4]. He believed this indicated they were applying unconscious muscular pressure to the glass, telling her that **"hocus-pocus of any plausible kind could appear to be supernormal"** once the group had entered a subjective state of mind [4].
*   **The Paranormal Movement:** To playfully demonstrate his point, Frankland said, **"You know, nothing will happen if I put my finger on the glass... like this..."** [4]. The moment he touched the glass, it inexplicably **"executed three wide circles, clearly with a motive power that was far greater than at any time during the evening"** [4]. 
*   **The "C-H-R-I-S-T" Communication:** Greatly startled by the force, Frankland asked the glass, **"Why were we sent to the church?"** The glass promptly spelled out, **"Pray"** [4]. He then asked, **"Who is in the church now?"** to which the glass slowly and firmly spelled **"C-H-R-I-S-T"** [5]. Frankland immediately ended the experiment [5].

Following this event, Frankland stated he was entirely convinced that something paranormal had moved the glass, as the light touch of his single finger could not possibly have propelled it [5]. He further concluded that because the entity claimed to be a priest, the response was **"an extremely proper one; no one but a clergyman would have thought of such a response"** [5]. 

**Later Investigations**
Frankland continued his involvement with the Borley site into the 1950s. On **September 24, 1950**, he returned to Borley alongside Peter Underwood, John C. Dening, and Peter Heywood specifically to examine a mysterious "Face in the Wall"—a carved stone face that James Turner had recently discovered cemented into the brickwork of the old stables [6].
