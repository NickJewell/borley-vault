# T. S. Frankland

Also known as **Tommy Frankland**: described as an "eccentric young man who was larger than life" [1]. An undergraduate at **Trinity College, Cambridge**, and a Company Director who maintained a deep interest in psychic activity [1, 2]. He hailed from a wealthy family and maintained a flat in Cadogan Place (or Cadogan Square) in London, and was known to put his Rolls Royce at the disposal of fellow investigator [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] for investigations in the Cotswolds and at [[langenhoe|Langenhoe]] [1, 2]. He was associated with the Rev. Dr. George MacLeod's Iona Community [2]. The sources note sadly that Frankland "died far too young" [2].

Frankland attended a planchette séance at the [[rectory-cottage|Borley Priory cottage]] on the evening of 8 June 1948, in a company that included Mr and Mrs [[james-turner|James Turner]], the Reverend [[john-dening|John Dening]], and [[mrs-g-taylor|Mrs G. Taylor of Chelsea]].

## The July 1947 Cellar Excavations

During **July 1947**, Frankland assisted [[james-turner|James Turner]] with a physical excavation of the ruined Rectory's cellars [2]. Working late into a Saturday night, they shifted a great deal of rubble and discovered the opening of a well, an event marked by "a great escape of gas" [2]. The following day, they dug into the hard clay to a depth of six feet and broke through the brick sides and bottom of the well [2]. Despite hours of strenuous digging, they found no bones or objects of interest; the area was subsequently filled in and planted with rose bushes [2].

## The Séance

The séance began at about 12:30 am using a wine glass on a table. During an early session, the entity purported to be the deceased Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]]. Frankland, who was "becoming sceptical of the proceedings," challenged the entity, asking if it intended to manifest since it was the 21st anniversary of Bull's death. The entity responded: "Go to the church now" — prompting the entire group to relocate to the locked church's porch [1].

Messages were received from an entity claiming to be "Harry Bull," who stated he would haunt Borley for "4004 years" and, when asked whose footsteps had been heard on the churchyard path, replied "Lionel Foyster." The entity then alleged "foul play" in connection with the death of the late [[lionel-foyster|Reverend L. A. Foyster]] — allegations the sitters sensibly did not pursue.

Frankland was notably sceptical throughout, watching the sitters closely and observing that "whenever the wine glass they used had moved, the flexor tendons controlling the index fingers of the sitters on the base of the wineglass became constricted" — indicating unconscious muscular pressure. He told Mrs Turner that "hocus-pocus of any plausible kind could appear to be supernormal" once the group had entered a subjective state of mind [4].

He proposed to demonstrate his point:

> "'You know, nothing will happen if I put my finger on the glass... like this...' He wanted to demonstrate his point — whereupon the glass immediately executed three wide circles, clearly with a motive power that was far greater than at any time during the evening."

Frankland, "greatly startled," asked why they had been sent to the church. The reply: "Pray." He then asked who was in the church now. The letters **C-H-R-I-S-T** were spelled out slowly and firmly. Frankland immediately ended the experiment [5].

He later told Underwood that "since the supposed entity was a priest in his lifetime on earth, the answer to his question was an extremely proper one; no one but a clergyman would have thought of such a response."

> "He was entirely convinced that something had moved the glass on this occasion; his own finger, laid gently on it, could not possibly have had the power to propel it."

Of the dozen or so wine-glass sessions in which [[john-dening|Dening]] had participated at Borley and elsewhere, Frankland and Dening agreed that this was the only occasion on which the glass seemed genuinely "alive."

## Later Investigations

Frankland continued his involvement with the Borley site into the 1950s. On **24 September 1950**, he returned to Borley alongside [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]], [[john-dening|John C. Dening]], and Peter Heywood specifically to examine a mysterious "Face in the Wall" — a carved stone face that [[james-turner|James Turner]] had recently discovered cemented into the brickwork of the old stables [6].
