# Mrs Alex English (née Ethel Beenham)

Former secretary to [[harry-price|Harry Price]], who served as note-taker and reporter during the critical August 1943 Borley excavation. With twelve years of experience as Price's assistant in many investigations, she was "ideal for the purpose."

## The August 1943 Excavation

Mrs English interrupted her domestic duties to join Price at [[liston-rectory|Liston Rectory]] as a guest of [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. and Mrs Henning]], accompanying the digging party to the Rectory ruins. She was the person keeping contemporaneous written notes of every discovery.

Her handwriting is the original record of the two most significant finds:

- **1:45 p.m., August 17, 1943:** "Jaw-bone (pig?) found" — immediately revised by Dr. Bailey's expert identification as human.
- **1:50 p.m., August 17, 1943:** "Part of skull dug up" — no ambiguity; immediately confirmed as human.

These minute-by-minute notes were kept in real time as Jackson excavated. They constitute the primary contemporaneous document for the discovery of the [[bones-excavation|Borley human remains]], and their precision — with exact times and initial field identifications — gives them particular evidential weight.

## Significance

Price specifically acknowledges Mrs English's contribution as "indispensable." In an excavation of this nature — where the chain of custody and circumstances of discovery would later be scrutinised — having an experienced, named note-taker present whose records could be cited and examined was methodologically important. Her notes are deposited with the Borley dossier at the University of London.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mrs. Alex English (née Ethel Beenham)** was a key assistant to the psychical researcher Harry Price and a participant in some of the most famous events surrounding the Borley Rectory hauntings. A thorough reference profile of her involvement includes the following details:

**Professional Background and Early Career**
Ethel Beenham worked as Harry Price's secretary at the University of London Council for Psychical Investigation [1]. She was employed by Price intermittently between the years 1928 and 1935 [2]. Although she did not accompany him to Borley Rectory during this initial secretarial period, she remained closely connected to his work [2]. 

**The 1939 Psychic Fête**
She was present at the Rectory ruins for the famous "psychic fête" held on June 21, 1939 [1]. The event was organized to raise funds for Borley Church, and Miss Beenham attended alongside twenty-five members of Price's Ghost Club [1]. 

**The 1943 Cellar Excavations**
By 1943, having married and become Mrs. Alex English, she played a vital role in Harry Price's famous excavations of the Rectory cellars [3, 4]. The official digging logs record her as being present on Day 1 (August 17), Day 2 (August 18), and Day 3 (August 30) of the investigation, assisting Price and the Rev. A.C. Henning [5]. Price publicly expressed his gratitude to her in his book *The End of Borley Rectory*, stating that for secretarial work during the diggings, "Mrs Ethel English was indispensable" [3].

**Defense of Harry Price and the SPR Omission**
Following Harry Price's death, journalist Charles Sutton published an article in the *Inky Way Annual* accusing Price of fraudulently faking poltergeist phenomena (specifically stone-throwing) at Borley [6]. According to researcher Robert J. Hastings, Mrs. English reacted to Sutton's allegations with **"astonished incredulity"** [6]. 

In 1949, the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) investigator Mollie Goldney formally interviewed Mrs. English regarding Price's integrity, in the presence of SPR Council Member Miss Ina Jephson [2]. Goldney's own notes from the interview described Mrs. English as having made **"a most genuine effort to record the facts as she remembered them"** [2]. 

However, this interview highlights a major controversy and **omission in the Borley literature**: Hastings explicitly pointed out that although Mrs. English provided testimony defending Price against the *Inky Way* allegations, the highly critical authors of the 1956 *Borley Report* (which included Goldney herself) deliberately chose *not* to quote Mrs. English's defensive testimony in their final publication [6].
