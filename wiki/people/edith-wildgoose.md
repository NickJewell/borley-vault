# Edith May Dytor (later Mrs Wildgoose)

State Registered Nurse (SRN). Employed as nurse-companion at [[borley-rectory]] from approximately April to November 1932 — eight months of continuous residence, longer than almost any of [[harry-price]]'s official observers. She answered an advertisement in the *Nursing Mirror* placed by [[marianne-foyster]] seeking a nursemaid for the recently adopted baby [[john-emery|John Emery]]. She arrived at the Rectory just two weeks after the Foysters acquired John. At 21 years old, she was, as [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] later recalled, "blond and very beautiful" with "blue eyes and a very clear complexion" — though Marianne often misremembered her name as "Mary Dytor" [5].

Later she worked as a nurse at Derbyshire Royal Infirmary, where [[trevor-hall]] eventually located and interviewed her in the early 1950s as part of his investigation of the Foyster period.

## Conditions at Borley

Edith arrived to find:

- Baby John was just two weeks old
- [[marianne-foyster|Mrs Foyster]] was attractive and well-educated, a Roman Catholic (rosary under her pillow; fluent French); she was anxious and taking small white pills (Luminal, a barbiturate with side effects including confusion and hallucinations)
- [[lionel-foyster|Mr Foyster]] was much older, crippled with arthritis and with swollen hands; "very tight with money" due to diminished investments — meals consisted of rice and macaroni cooked in water; she found him a good and kind man who adored the children
- [[francois-darles|François d'Arles]] dominated the household: a short, stoutish man, "manipulative and sly, sometimes cruel, belittling Mrs Foyster." He would slyly accuse Marianne of trying to poison him and manipulate Edith into confiscating Marianne's Luminal. Edith believed Marianne was actually frightened of d'Arles, and that Lionel was intensely jealous of him. Edith neither liked nor trusted d'Arles.

She saw the [[wall-writings]] immediately on arrival and presumed they could only have been written by Mrs Foyster, as Adelaide was too young to write. The first night she heard limping footsteps outside her room — frightening her — until she realised it was simply [[lionel-foyster|Lionel]]'s arthritic gait.

Contrary to the majority view, after reading Lionel's manuscript for *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*, she concluded it was most likely **Lionel** who was behind the strange activity, not Marianne — a minority view but worth noting as the professional opinion of someone who lived in the household for eight months.

Edith also noted that Marianne received expensive gifts from a London diplomat named [[santiago-monk|Santiago Monk]] — a claim Marianne later denied [15].

## Baby John's Death

On 20 September 1932, at only four and a half months old, baby John Emery died of convulsions and protein malnutrition and was buried in an unmarked grave in Borley churchyard. Both Marianne and d'Arles were deeply grieved, and reportedly spent much time weaving floral garlands for his grave [10].

## Medical Observations and the Paternity Question

One of Edith's most significant contributions to the Borley narrative was her professional assessment of Marianne Foyster's physical condition. Based on her nursing experience, **Edith was convinced that Marianne had recently given birth** [8, 9]. This fuelled speculation that baby John was not actually adopted but was the illegitimate child of Marianne and d'Arles (see [[john-emery]]).

## Adelaide's Nun Sighting

Once, [[adelaide-foyster]] came downstairs to say there was a lady she didn't recognise on the doorstep. When Edith and Marianne reached the front door, no one was there. When asked to describe the visitor, Adelaide said it was a woman wearing black with something white around her face "like a nun." *(single witness; childhood account)*

## Interactions with Investigators

Edith was present during an uninvited visit by Harry Price and [[lord-charles-hope|Lord Charles Hope]] following a minor fire in the schoolhouse wing [21]. During this visit, she was sent out for a walk with the children so the investigators could inspect the house and the wall writings [21].

Decades later, she served as a crucial, highly credible witness for Trevor Hall during his 1953 and 1956 research for *The Borley Report* [2, 9, 22].

## A Contradicted Stone-Throwing Incident

[[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] later claimed that she saw rocks tumbling down the stairs and witnessed Miss Dytor "hi-tailing it away" from the scene in terror [17, 19]. However, investigator [[robert-wood|Robert Swanson]] pointed out that Edith did not arrive at Borley until *after* this violent phase of the haunting had ended, and Edith herself flatly denied the event ever occurred [17, 20].

> **Contradiction:** Marianne dismissed Edith's denial, stating, "She denied it, but I thought it was funny and didn't say much" [20].

## Chronological Contradiction

> **Contradiction:** Several texts record that Edith lived at the Rectory from April or May 1932 until November 1932 [2, 6]. However, another account states that after baby John died in September, Marianne asked her to stay on to care for Adelaide and François Jr., and she remained at Borley until January 1933 before returning to Derby [7].

## No Paranormal Experience

Mrs Wildgoose gave the SPR a signed statement: she had "no unaccountable experiences during her residence in 'the most haunted house in England'" and was convinced the rectory was not subject to paranormal disturbances. She described [[borley-rectory]] under the Foysters as "a strange household, aptly dominated by Mr d'Arles." She noted that Mrs Foyster "loathed" Borley, and that active preparations for the [[jonquille-et-cie|flower-shop venture]] in London with d'Arles were already under way in 1932.

The SPR notes that her testimony was given "willingly" and that she spoke of her experiences at Borley with complete equanimity. She was not frightened, not credulous, and not suggestible — yet experienced nothing.

## Significance

The presence of an independent professional nurse for eight months in the rectory during 1932 — the period immediately following the peak [[foyster-phenomena]] of 1930–32 and the [[marks-tey-spiritualist-circle|Marks Tey Circle]] visit — with a complete absence of paranormal experience, supports the SPR's hypothesis that the phenomena were dependent on Marianne's presence and willingness to produce them. By April 1932 the phenomena had largely ceased.
