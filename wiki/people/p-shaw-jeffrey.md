# P. Shaw Jeffrey (1862 – 1952)

Oxford contemporary of [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] who stayed at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in the summer vacations of 1885 and 1886. One of the most important early witnesses, providing evidence of poltergeist activity pre-dating Harry Price's involvement by more than forty years.

## Identity

Full name and early career not given in sources. Later headmaster of Colchester Royal Grammar School (1899–1916); afterwards lived in Cape Town, South Africa. He died in 1952 aged approximately 90.

## Experiences at Borley

Shaw Jeffrey and [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] were born in the same year (1862) and went to Oxford together — Jeffrey at Queen's, Bull at Exeter College. In the long vacations Jeffrey stayed at Borley Rectory — certainly in 1885 and 1886, possibly other years too. He described the Bull family vividly: seventeen children at home simultaneously during vacations; Henry Bull was a "hedge parson" who drove from the Bishop's Palace in a tandem after ordination and shot rabbits from the drawing-room floor on Sunday afternoons. Harry Bull was always asleep; someone had to be sent to find him in one of the arbours.

He wrote a long letter to [[harry-price|Price]] from Cape Town (March 5, 1942) and an article for the *Cape Times* — both Price's most important direct source on the 1885–86 period. His testimony received no outside prompting: he wrote only after reading a review of Price's book in a South African journal.

**What Jeffrey witnessed**:
- Saw the [[the-nun|phantom nun]] "several times" in the Rectory grounds
- Heard the phantom coach "go clattering by" so often that "I used to sleep through the noise" — the first recorded witness to habituate to the phenomenon
- "Lots of small adventures": stones falling, his boots found on top of a wardrobe (locked room)
- The tradition was already current in 1885 "that the Rectory had been built on the site of a monastery or convent"

### The French Dictionary Incident

The most significant recorded episode: Jeffrey had been regularly using a large French dictionary for several days. It went missing. Then one night he was woken by "a big bump on the floor" and found the dictionary on the floor with its spine "a good deal knocked about." **His bedroom door was locked.** The dictionary had apparently been transported back into his locked room in the night.

[[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] later noted (in *The End of Borley Rectory*, Ch. 13) that a *French* dictionary may be another "indicator" pointing to [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]'s French nationality.

### The Felix Bull Poltergeist (August 1886)

More dramatic: Felix Bull (brother of Henry Bull) held a neighbouring rectory with a "crazy" watch-tower. He came to lunch at Borley to report that a Poltergeist was troubling his household. The following day Jeffrey and Harry Bull drove over. The cook blamed "our new housemaid." They went upstairs to find the maid making beds at the far end of a long room with three windows.

Harry Bull said: "Well, Mary, cook says you can show us a few tricks. What about it?" The maid said nothing, but:
- A tooth-glass came flying from the wash-stand behind her back, circled round the room, and hit the door-jamb just above Jeffrey's head.
- The fender and fire-irons moved clean across the room with a clatter.

"The maid never spoke a word. Nor did we. We bolted." The maid subsequently went to London and became a medium — Jeffrey identifies her as a physical medium under poltergeist conditions rather than a fraud.

## Evidence Quality

Shaw Jeffrey was described as "a very respected and respectable man" and his account was never retracted. He remained consistent through 1941 when he reiterated his experiences in the *Cape Times* — fifty-five years after the events themselves. He explicitly confirmed Harry Bull had shared his own experiences of the nun with him.

In February 1950, [[eric-dingwall]] wrote to Shaw Jeffrey requesting statements for the SPR inquiry. Shaw Jeffrey declined, citing his advanced age and the danger of inaccuracy over such a distance of time. He died two years later.

> **Note:** The SPR investigators could not interview him before the 1956 Report; they were therefore unable to use his testimony either positively or negatively.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**P. Shaw Jeffrey M.A. (Oxon)** (whose first name is also recorded as **Percy**) was an educator and a contemporary of the Rev. Harry Foyster Bull. His retrospective accounts of his visits to the Bull family are highly significant in the Borley literature because they mark the **first reported paranormal activity at Borley Rectory**, dating back to the mid-1880s [1-3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, specific events, quotes, and striking textual contradictions regarding his involvement:

**Biographical Details and Associations**
*   **Life Dates:** He was born in 1862 (the same year as his friend Harry Bull) and died in 1952 at the age of 89 [2, 4-6].
*   **Career:** He served as the headmaster of Colchester Royal Grammar School from 1899 to 1916 [1, 2]. In his later retirement years, he resided at the Lenox Hotel, Gardens, in Cape Town, South Africa [2, 7].
*   **University Contradiction:** The sources contradict each other regarding his specific Oxford college. One account states he was a student at **Wadham College, Oxford** [8], while another notes he was studying at **Queen’s College** [6]. 
*   **Physical Appearance and the Bull Sisters:** Jeffrey stayed at Borley Rectory as a guest during the long summer vacations around 1885 and 1886 [2, 8]. While there, he became the object of a fierce romantic rivalry between two of the teenage Bull sisters, Caroline ("Dodie") and Freda [6]. In her 1885 diary, Dodie described him as **"not very tall"** with **"fair hair and moustache, very good features, lovely dark gray eyes and long lashes and eyebrows and splendid teeth and sunburnt complexion,"** defiantly writing, **"I mean to go in for him... Freda had better not thwart me for I mean to have him"** [6]. 
*   **Interests:** He was deeply fascinated by **"folklore, witchcraft, the evil eye, charms and amulets and the rest of it"** [9].

**Reported Phenomena (c. 1885–1886)**
Jeffrey claimed to have experienced numerous disturbances during his stays, providing some of the earliest chronological "evidence" for the haunting:
*   **Apparitions and Sounds:** He stated emphatically that he saw the famous ghost nun several times and heard the phantom coach-and-four **"sweep down the much-too-narrow lane beside the Rectory so often that he used to sleep through the noise"** [2, 4, 9, 10].
*   **Poltergeist Activity:** He reported stones falling from nowhere and claimed his boots were mysteriously moved from the floor to the top of his wardrobe [2, 4, 8, 9, 11].
*   **The French Dictionary Incident:** His most famous adventure involved a large French dictionary he had been using for his studies. It went missing for several days until one night, he was awakened by a loud bump or thump. Upon lighting his candle, he found the dictionary sprawling on the floor, **"a good deal knocked about,"** despite his bedroom door being securely locked [4, 8, 9, 11].
*   *The Phythian-Adams Theory:* Canon W.J. Phythian-Adams later utilized this specific incident to formulate a bizarre theory, suggesting that the ghost of the murdered French nun (Marie Lairre) had "borrowed" Jeffrey's dictionary in the 1880s to learn English words so that she could produce the famous wall-writings asking for help fifty years later [11-13].

**Later Writings, Quotes, and Skepticism**
Jeffrey's claims were not made public until decades after the events, which subjected his testimony to heavy scrutiny by skeptics:
*   **Publications and Letters:** After reading Harry Price's first Borley book, Jeffrey wrote a review article for the *Cape Times* in January 1941, describing Harry's father, Henry Bull, as a **"squarson"** (half squire, half parson) [2, 7, 14]. In a March 1942 letter to Price, he affirmed his memories, providing the quote: **"This is badly written, but as far as I know it is strictly true"** [14]. 
*   **The "Cliques" Admission:** In his correspondence, Jeffrey noted that the large Bull household was divided into several different cliques who were **"only dimly aware of each other"** [13, 15]. Skeptics, such as W.H. Salter, seized upon this specific quote, arguing that the "poltergeist" events (like the missing dictionary and moved boots) were merely practical jokes played by the opposing groups of children—most likely the jealous teenage sisters competing for his affection [9, 13, 15, 16].
*   **The 1950 SPR Inquiry:** When Dr. Eric Dingwall wrote to Jeffrey in February 1950 asking him to provide a formal statement for the SPR's critical report, Jeffrey declined due to his advanced age. He provided the following poignant quote: **"I am afraid I shall be no use to you. I am 88 in a few days’ time; my memory is quite untrustworthy and the events at Borley now 65 years ago are so remote that they now seem quite unreal and I should not be prepared to swear to them."** He added, **"No amount of discussion would elicit any further facts from the dried-up reservoir of my memory"** [5]. Despite this admission of a failing memory, defenders point out that he never explicitly retracted or denied his earlier evidence [2, 17].
*   **The "Dodie" Bull Diary Contradiction:** A major contradiction to Jeffrey's accounts surfaced when Caroline "Dodie" Bull's contemporary 1885 diary was discovered. The diary extensively chronicled daily life at the Rectory and explicitly mentioned Jeffrey as the object of her crush, yet it **contained absolutely no mention of any haunting, stone-throwing, or unusual experiences**, severely undermining Jeffrey's later claims that dramatic supernatural phenomena occurred during that exact period [18].
