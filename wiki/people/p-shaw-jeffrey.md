# P. Shaw Jeffrey (1862 – 1952)

Oxford contemporary of [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] who stayed at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in the summer vacations of 1885 and 1886. One of the most important early witnesses, providing evidence of poltergeist activity pre-dating Harry Price's involvement by more than forty years.

## Identity

Full name and early career not given in sources. Later headmaster of Colchester Royal Grammar School (1899–1916); afterwards lived in Cape Town, South Africa. He died in 1952 aged approximately 90.

## Experiences at Borley

Shaw Jeffrey and [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] were born in the same year (1862) and went to Oxford together — Jeffrey at Queen's, Bull at Exeter College. In the long vacations Jeffrey stayed at Borley Rectory — certainly in 1885 and 1886, possibly other years too. He described the Bull family vividly: seventeen children at home simultaneously during vacations; Henry Bull was a "hedge parson" who drove from the Bishop's Palace in a tandem after ordination and shot rabbits from the drawing-room floor on Sunday afternoons. Harry Bull was always asleep; someone had to be sent to find him in one of the arbours.

He wrote a long letter to [[harry-price|Price]] from Cape Town (March 5, 1942) and an article for the *Cape Times* — both Price's most important direct source on the 1885–86 period. His testimony received no outside prompting: he wrote only after reading a review of Price's book in a South African journal.

**What Jeffrey witnessed**:
- Saw the [[the-nun|phantom nun]] "several times" in the Rectory grounds
- Heard the phantom coach "go clattering by" so often that "I used to sleep through the noise" — the first recorded witness to habituate to the phenomenon
- "Lots of small adventures": stones falling, his boots found on top of a wardrobe (locked room)
- The tradition was already current in 1885 "that the Rectory had been built on the site of a monastery or convent"

### The French Dictionary Incident

The most significant recorded episode: Jeffrey had been regularly using a large French dictionary for several days. It went missing. Then one night he was woken by "a big bump on the floor" and found the dictionary on the floor with its spine "a good deal knocked about." **His bedroom door was locked.** The dictionary had apparently been transported back into his locked room in the night.

[[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] later noted (in *The End of Borley Rectory*, Ch. 13) that a *French* dictionary may be another "indicator" pointing to [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]'s French nationality.

### The Felix Bull Poltergeist (August 1886)

More dramatic: Felix Bull (brother of Henry Bull) held a neighbouring rectory with a "crazy" watch-tower. He came to lunch at Borley to report that a Poltergeist was troubling his household. The following day Jeffrey and Harry Bull drove over. The cook blamed "our new housemaid." They went upstairs to find the maid making beds at the far end of a long room with three windows.

Harry Bull said: "Well, Mary, cook says you can show us a few tricks. What about it?" The maid said nothing, but:
- A tooth-glass came flying from the wash-stand behind her back, circled round the room, and hit the door-jamb just above Jeffrey's head.
- The fender and fire-irons moved clean across the room with a clatter.

"The maid never spoke a word. Nor did we. We bolted." The maid subsequently went to London and became a medium — Jeffrey identifies her as a physical medium under poltergeist conditions rather than a fraud.

## Evidence Quality

Shaw Jeffrey was described as "a very respected and respectable man" and his account was never retracted. He remained consistent through 1941 when he reiterated his experiences in the *Cape Times* — fifty-five years after the events themselves. He explicitly confirmed Harry Bull had shared his own experiences of the nun with him.

In February 1950, [[eric-dingwall]] wrote to Shaw Jeffrey requesting statements for the SPR inquiry. Shaw Jeffrey declined, citing his advanced age and the danger of inaccuracy over such a distance of time. He died two years later.

> **Note:** The SPR investigators could not interview him before the 1956 Report; they were therefore unable to use his testimony either positively or negatively.
