# P. Shaw Jeffrey (1862 – 1952)

Full name also recorded as **Percy Shaw Jeffrey M.A. (Oxon)**. Oxford contemporary of [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] who stayed at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in the summer vacations of 1885 and 1886. One of the most important early witnesses, providing evidence of poltergeist activity pre-dating [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s involvement by more than forty years.

## Identity

Born 1862 (the same year as his friend Harry Bull); died 1952 aged approximately 89–90. Later headmaster of Colchester Royal Grammar School (1899–1916); in his retirement years he lived at the Lenox Hotel, Gardens, Cape Town, South Africa.

> **Contradiction:** The sources contradict each other regarding his specific Oxford college. One account states he was a student at **Wadham College, Oxford**, while another notes he was studying at **Queen's College**.

He was deeply fascinated by "folklore, witchcraft, the evil eye, charms and amulets and the rest of it." While staying at Borley he became the object of a fierce romantic rivalry between two of the teenage Bull sisters, Caroline ("Dodie") and Freda. In her 1885 diary, [[dodie-bull-diary|Dodie]] described him as "not very tall" with "fair hair and moustache, very good features, lovely dark gray eyes and long lashes and eyebrows and splendid teeth and sunburnt complexion," defiantly writing, "I mean to go in for him... Freda had better not thwart me for I mean to have him."

## Experiences at Borley

Shaw Jeffrey and [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] were born in the same year and went to Oxford together. In the long vacations Jeffrey stayed at Borley Rectory — certainly in 1885 and 1886, possibly other years too. He described the Bull family vividly: seventeen children at home simultaneously during vacations; Henry Bull was a "squarson" (half squire, half parson) who drove from the Bishop's Palace in a tandem after ordination and shot rabbits from the drawing-room floor on Sunday afternoons. Harry Bull was always asleep; someone had to be sent to find him in one of the arbours.

**What Jeffrey reportedly witnessed**:
- Saw the [[the-nun|phantom nun]] "several times" in the Rectory grounds
- Heard the phantom coach "go clattering by" so often that "I used to sleep through the noise" — the first recorded witness to habituate to the phenomenon
- "Lots of small adventures": stones falling, his boots found on top of a wardrobe (locked room)
- The tradition was already current in 1885 "that the Rectory had been built on the site of a monastery or convent"

## The French Dictionary Incident

The most significant recorded episode: Jeffrey had been regularly using a large French dictionary for several days. It went missing. Then one night he was woken by "a big bump on the floor" and found the dictionary on the floor with its spine "a good deal knocked about." **His bedroom door was locked.** The dictionary had apparently been transported back into his locked room in the night.

[[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] later noted (in *The End of Borley Rectory*, Ch. 13) that a *French* dictionary may be another "indicator" pointing to [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]'s French nationality — he even suggested the ghost had "borrowed" Jeffrey's dictionary in the 1880s to learn English words so she could produce the wall-writings fifty years later.

## The Felix Bull Poltergeist (August 1886)

More dramatic: Felix Bull (brother of Henry Bull) held a neighbouring rectory with a "crazy" watch-tower. He came to lunch at Borley to report that a poltergeist was troubling his household. The following day Jeffrey and Harry Bull drove over. The cook blamed "our new housemaid." They went upstairs to find the maid making beds at the far end of a long room with three windows.

Harry Bull said: "Well, Mary, cook says you can show us a few tricks. What about it?" The maid said nothing, but:
- A tooth-glass came flying from the wash-stand behind her back, circled round the room, and hit the door-jamb just above Jeffrey's head.
- The fender and fire-irons moved clean across the room with a clatter.

"The maid never spoke a word. Nor did we. We bolted." The maid subsequently went to London and became a medium — Jeffrey identifies her as a physical medium under poltergeist conditions rather than a fraud.

## Later Writings

He wrote a review article for the *Cape Times* in January 1941 and a long letter to Price from Cape Town (March 5, 1942), confirming: "This is badly written, but as far as I know it is strictly true." His testimony received no outside prompting: he wrote only after reading a review of Price's book in a South African journal. He also explicitly confirmed Harry Bull had shared his own experiences of the nun with him.

In February 1950, [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] wrote to Shaw Jeffrey requesting statements for the SPR inquiry. Jeffrey declined, citing his advanced age: "I am afraid I shall be no use to you. I am 88 in a few days' time; my memory is quite untrustworthy and the events at Borley now 65 years ago are so remote that they now seem quite unreal and I should not be prepared to swear to them." He added, "No amount of discussion would elicit any further facts from the dried-up reservoir of my memory." He died two years later. The SPR investigators could not therefore use his testimony either positively or negatively in the 1956 Report.

## Sceptical Challenges

Several contradictions undermine Jeffrey's accounts:

- **The "Cliques" Admission:** Jeffrey himself noted that the large Bull household was divided into several different cliques who were "only dimly aware of each other." Sceptics, such as W.H. Salter, seized upon this, arguing that "poltergeist" events (like the missing dictionary and moved boots) were merely practical jokes played by the competing teenage sisters — most likely the jealous Dodie and Freda competing for Jeffrey's affection.
- **The Dodie Bull Diary:** A major contradiction surfaced when Caroline "Dodie" Bull's contemporary 1885 diary was discovered. The diary extensively chronicled daily life at the Rectory and explicitly mentioned Jeffrey as the object of her crush, yet it **contained absolutely no mention of any haunting, stone-throwing, or unusual experiences** during that exact period, severely undermining Jeffrey's later claims.

Despite his 1950 admission of a failing memory, defenders point out that he never explicitly retracted or denied his earlier evidence, and remained consistent through 1941 — fifty-five years after the events themselves.
