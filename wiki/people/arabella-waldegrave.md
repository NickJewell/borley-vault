# Arabella Waldegrave (1687–?)

Granddaughter of King James II; daughter of [[henry-lord-waldegrave|Henry, first Baron Waldegrave]] and [[henrietta-fitzjames|Henrietta FitzJames]]. Proposed by researcher [[georgina-dawson|Mrs. Georgina Dawson]] as the true identity of the Borley Rectory nun-ghost. The theory was later demolished by [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] on linguistic grounds.

## Biography

- **Born**: 1687
- **Father**: Henry, first Baron Waldegrave of Chewton
- **Mother**: Henrietta FitzJames, natural daughter of James II and [[arabella-churchill|Arabella Churchill]]
- **Grandmother**: Arabella Churchill; great-uncle: John Churchill, first Duke of Marlborough; King James II was her grandfather through her mother

Following the deposition of King James II in 1688, Arabella's loyal Catholic parents followed the King into exile in France, taking the one-year-old Arabella with them [1, 4, 6]. At age seven (1694) she was placed in the English Benedictine convent school at **Pontoise**, near Paris, where her aunt (Arabella FitzJames, known as "Sister Ignatia") and a cousin were already nuns [1, 4–7]. The school was explicitly an English House on French soil, conducted in English for English Catholic exiles.

According to [[georgina-dawson|Dawson]]'s research, Arabella is mentioned in the Pontoise archives. She apparently left — or was dismissed from — Pontoise, and was reportedly a "naughty girl" [6]. Foley's *Records* indicate she went to Paris and became a nun. **After this, she disappears entirely from the historical record** — an extraordinary circumstance for a person of royal blood whose family is otherwise meticulously documented, which led to intense speculation among psychical researchers.

Note: early researchers heavily confused Arabella Waldegrave with her aunt Arabella FitzJames. The latter was the illegitimate daughter of James II who became the adult nun "Sister Ignatia" at Pontoise; Arabella Waldegrave was merely a young schoolgirl there during the same period [5, 22].

## Dawson's Theory

[[georgina-dawson|Mrs. Dawson]] proposed that Arabella eventually became a spy or agent, either for the Stuart Pretenders or for the British Government in London, and that she made her way to Borley, where she was murdered — hence the nun-ghost. Her mother, Henrietta, was definitely expelled from the French Court in 1695 as a spy for the British Government, lending circumstantial plausibility to the idea of espionage in the family.

Dawson's research is described by Price as "a magnificent piece of research work" running to 20,000 words, covering Borley's church, rectors, the Rectory, and the Waldegrave family in detail.

## The Mary Lycett Theory

In 1952, researcher Mary Lycett expanded on Dawson's theory by constructing an elaborate, highly romanticised theory that **Arabella Waldegrave and the legendary French nun "[[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]" were the exact same person** [4, 11]. Lycett theorised that Arabella was forced into a Parisian novitiate against her will, taking the name *Soeur Marie Lairre* [12]. According to this narrative, she fell in love with her cousin, Henry Waldegrave, and the two eloped to England, where Henry hid Arabella at the disused Borley Rectory. When his father, Lord Waldegrave, discovered the marriage and treachery, a terrible confrontation ensued; Henry, in a jealous fury, strangled Arabella/Marie, threw her body in a stream, and later scattered her bones under the Rectory floor [15].

## Phythian-Adams's Demolition

[[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] examined the theory carefully and rejected it for a specific reason: **Arabella was English-educated**.

The Benedictine convent at Pontoise was an English House on French soil. The nuns were English; the education was English. "The school was English: that is precisely why Arabella was sent there." Therefore Arabella could not have written the [[wall-writings|Borley wall-messages]] with their distinctively French vocabulary (the word *trompée* written "in a flowing hand" unlike the stiff English lettering around it), and could not have been a French novice from Havre named Marie Lairre.

Additionally, Phythian-Adams argued that if Arabella was a spy for a Protestant/Hanoverian government, it made no sense that her ghost would repeatedly ask for strict Catholic rites via the Borley wall-writings and planchette séances [18]. He also considered it absurd that a royal grandchild would be executed for espionage by her own staunchly Catholic family in their safest English haven [18, 19].

Instead, Phythian-Adams proposed a mundane explanation: that Arabella likely eloped with an "undesirable lover" from outside the faith or her social standing, causing her embarrassed family to quietly disown her, which would explain her sudden erasure from family records [17, 19].

> **Contested:** Phythian-Adams's argument is linguistic; Dawson's research traced the royal bloodline and the missing person. Neither fully accounts for all the evidence.

## The Truth: Countess Mary Waldegrave's Revelation

The mystery of Arabella's fate was finally resolved by Countess Mary Waldegrave, a modern member of the family. She revealed that **Arabella never disappeared at all** [20]. She had indeed become a nun in Paris and actively maintained a correspondence with her mother and with her brother, Lord James Waldegrave, while he was serving as Ambassador to Paris [20]. The illusion of her disappearance occurred simply because the English conventual archives were incomplete, with the missing records detailing her life actually being held in Canada [21].

## Significance

The Arabella theory is one of four competing "nun-ghost" identities in the Borley record. Even in its rejected form, it produced Dawson's invaluable 20,000-word historical dossier on the Waldegrave family, which became a primary reference for understanding Borley's Catholic history.
