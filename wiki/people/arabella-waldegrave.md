# Arabella Waldegrave (1687–?)

Granddaughter of King James II; daughter of [[henry-lord-waldegrave|Henry, first Baron Waldegrave]] and [[henrietta-fitzjames|Henrietta FitzJames]]. Proposed by researcher [[georgina-dawson|Mrs. Georgina Dawson]] as the true identity of the Borley Rectory nun-ghost. The theory was later demolished by [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] on linguistic grounds.

## Biography

- **Born**: 1687
- **Father**: Henry, first Baron Waldegrave of Chewton
- **Mother**: Henrietta FitzJames, natural daughter of James II and [[arabella-churchill|Arabella Churchill]]
- **Grandmother**: Arabella Churchill; great-uncle: John Churchill, first Duke of Marlborough; King James II was her grandfather through her mother

In 1688, aged one, she was taken with her family to France when James II fled to Paris. At age seven (1694) she was sent to the Benedictine convent school at **Pontoise**, near Paris, where her aunt and cousin were nuns. Some fifteen members of the Waldegrave family had professed as nuns; Pontoise was explicitly an English House on French soil, conducted in English for English Catholic exiles.

According to Dawson's research, Arabella is mentioned in the Pontoise archives. She apparently left — or was dismissed from — Pontoise. Foley's Records indicate she went to Paris and became a nun. **After this, she disappears entirely from the historical record** — an extraordinary circumstance for a person of royal blood whose family is otherwise meticulously documented.

## Dawson's Theory

[[georgina-dawson|Mrs. Dawson]] proposed that Arabella eventually became a spy or agent, either for the Stuart Pretenders or for the British Government in London, and that she made her way to Borley, where she was murdered — hence the nun-ghost. Her mother, Henrietta, was definitely expelled from the French Court in 1695 as a spy for the British Government, lending circumstantial plausibility to the idea of espionage in the family.

Dawson's research is described by Price as "a magnificent piece of research work" running to 20,000 words, covering Borley's church, rectors, the Rectory, and the Waldegrave family in detail.

## Phythian-Adams's Demolition

[[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] examined the theory carefully and rejected it for a specific reason: **Arabella was English-educated**.

The Benedictine convent at Pontoise was an English House on French soil. The nuns were English; the education was English. "The school was English: that is precisely why Arabella was sent there." Therefore Arabella could not have written the [[wall-writings|Borley wall-messages]] with their distinctively French vocabulary (the word *trompée* written "in a flowing hand" unlike the stiff English lettering around it), and could not have been a French novice from Havre named Marie Lairre.

> **Contested:** Phythian-Adams's argument is linguistic; Dawson's research traced the royal bloodline and the missing person. Neither fully accounts for all the evidence.

## Significance

The Arabella theory is one of four competing "nun-ghost" identities in the Borley record. Even in its rejected form, it produced Dawson's invaluable 20,000-word historical dossier on the Waldegrave family, which became a primary reference for understanding Borley's Catholic history.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Arabella Waldegrave** (born 1687) was the daughter of Henry, first Lord Waldegrave, and Henrietta FitzJames, making her the granddaughter of King James II and his mistress Arabella Churchill [1-5]. 

A thorough reference profile of her life, and her subsequent entanglements with the Borley Rectory ghost legends, includes the following specific details, theories, and historical contradictions:

**Early Life and Exile**
Following the deposition of King James II in 1688, Arabella's loyal Catholic parents followed the King into exile in France, taking the one-year-old Arabella with them [1, 4, 6]. At the age of seven, in 1694, she was placed in the English Benedictine convent school at Pontoise, near Paris, where her aunt (Arabella FitzJames, known as "Sister Ignatia") and a cousin were already nuns [1, 4-7]. 

**The Legend of Her "Disappearance"**
According to local historian Georgina Dawson, Arabella was a "naughty girl" who either left or was dismissed from Pontoise [6]. While Foley's Records indicated she went to Paris and became a nun, Dawson claimed that after this point Arabella "vanished into thin air" [6]. The complete lack of trace of a king's grandchild led to intense speculation among psychical researchers looking to identify Borley's famous "phantom nun." 

**The Espionage Theory**
Dawson hypothesized that Arabella became a spy—either for the Stuarts or for the British Government—and that her covert activities became so dangerous she was murdered at Borley, effectively becoming the ghost that haunted the Rectory [1, 7-10]. 

**The Mary Lycett Theory: Conflation with "Marie Lairre"**
In 1952, researcher Mary Lycett expanded upon this by constructing an elaborate, highly romanticized theory that **Arabella Waldegrave and the legendary French nun "Marie Lairre" were the exact same person** [4, 11]. 
Lycett theorized that Arabella was forced into a Parisian novitiate against her will, taking the name *Soeur Marie Lairre* [12]. According to this narrative, she fell in love with her cousin, Henry Waldegrave, and the two eloped to England, encountering Arabella's mother, Henrietta (now a Hanoverian spy), who entrusted her daughter with secret documents [12, 13]. Henry hid Arabella at the disused Borley Rectory, but when his father, Lord Waldegrave, discovered the marriage and the treachery, a terrible confrontation ensued [14, 15]. Lycett claimed that Henry, in a jealous fury, strangled Arabella/Marie, threw her body in a stream, and later scattered her bones under the Rectory floor [15].

**Contradictions and Debunking by Canon Phythian-Adams**
Canon W. J. Phythian-Adams systematically dismantled the idea that Arabella Waldegrave was the Borley ghost, pointing out several major historical and logical contradictions:
*   **The Language Barrier:** Phythian-Adams noted that the convent at Pontoise, though on French soil, was a branch of an English House transplanted to the Continent [16]. Arabella was raised and educated by English women; it was "inconceivable that she could not write her own language" [16]. Therefore, she could not be the author of the broken-English messages or the French word *"trompée"* found in the Borley wall-writings [16, 17].
*   **The Religious Contradiction:** If Arabella was a spy for a Protestant/Hanoverian government, it made no sense that her ghost would repeatedly ask for strict Catholic rites—such as a Requiem Mass, holy water, and incense—via the Borley wall-writings and planchette séances [18].
*   **Lack of Motive:** Phythian-Adams argued it was absurd to believe a royal grandchild would be executed for espionage by her own staunchly Catholic family in their safest English haven [18, 19]. 

Instead of murder, Phythian-Adams proposed a mundane explanation: he suggested Arabella likely eloped with an "undesirable lover" from outside the faith or her social standing, causing her embarrassed family to quietly disown her, which would explain her sudden erasure from family records [17, 19].

**The Truth: Countess Mary Waldegrave's Revelation**
The mystery of Arabella's fate was finally resolved in recent years by Countess Mary Waldegrave, a modern member of the family. She revealed that **Arabella never disappeared at all** [20]. She had indeed become a nun in Paris and actively maintained a correspondence with her mother, as well as with her brother, Lord James Waldegrave, while he was serving as Ambassador to Paris [20]. The illusion of her disappearance occurred simply because the English conventual archives were incomplete, with the missing records detailing her life actually being held in Canada [21]. 

**Lesser-Known Biographical Confusion**
The sources also record that early researchers investigating the case heavily confused Arabella Waldegrave with her aunt, Arabella FitzJames. The latter was the illegitimate daughter of James II, whereas Arabella Waldegrave was his granddaughter. It was Arabella FitzJames who became the adult nun "Sister Ignatia" at Pontoise, while Arabella Waldegrave was merely a young schoolgirl there during the same period [5, 22].
