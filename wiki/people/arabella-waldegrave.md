# Arabella Waldegrave (1687–?)

Granddaughter of King James II; daughter of [[henry-lord-waldegrave|Henry, first Baron Waldegrave]] and [[henrietta-fitzjames|Henrietta FitzJames]]. Proposed by researcher [[georgina-dawson|Mrs. Georgina Dawson]] as the true identity of the Borley Rectory nun-ghost. The theory was later demolished by [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] on linguistic grounds.

## Biography

- **Born**: 1687
- **Father**: Henry, first Baron Waldegrave of Chewton
- **Mother**: Henrietta FitzJames, natural daughter of James II and [[arabella-churchill|Arabella Churchill]]
- **Grandmother**: Arabella Churchill; great-uncle: John Churchill, first Duke of Marlborough; King James II was her grandfather through her mother

In 1688, aged one, she was taken with her family to France when James II fled to Paris. At age seven (1694) she was sent to the Benedictine convent school at **Pontoise**, near Paris, where her aunt and cousin were nuns. Some fifteen members of the Waldegrave family had professed as nuns; Pontoise was explicitly an English House on French soil, conducted in English for English Catholic exiles.

According to Dawson's research, Arabella is mentioned in the Pontoise archives. She apparently left — or was dismissed from — Pontoise. Foley's Records indicate she went to Paris and became a nun. **After this, she disappears entirely from the historical record** — an extraordinary circumstance for a person of royal blood whose family is otherwise meticulously documented.

## Dawson's Theory

[[georgina-dawson|Mrs. Dawson]] proposed that Arabella eventually became a spy or agent, either for the Stuart Pretenders or for the British Government in London, and that she made her way to Borley, where she was murdered — hence the nun-ghost. Her mother, Henrietta, was definitely expelled from the French Court in 1695 as a spy for the British Government, lending circumstantial plausibility to the idea of espionage in the family.

Dawson's research is described by Price as "a magnificent piece of research work" running to 20,000 words, covering Borley's church, rectors, the Rectory, and the Waldegrave family in detail.

## Phythian-Adams's Demolition

[[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] examined the theory carefully and rejected it for a specific reason: **Arabella was English-educated**.

The Benedictine convent at Pontoise was an English House on French soil. The nuns were English; the education was English. "The school was English: that is precisely why Arabella was sent there." Therefore Arabella could not have written the [[wall-writings|Borley wall-messages]] with their distinctively French vocabulary (the word *trompée* written "in a flowing hand" unlike the stiff English lettering around it), and could not have been a French novice from Havre named Marie Lairre.

> **Contested:** Phythian-Adams's argument is linguistic; Dawson's research traced the royal bloodline and the missing person. Neither fully accounts for all the evidence.

## Significance

The Arabella theory is one of four competing "nun-ghost" identities in the Borley record. Even in its rejected form, it produced Dawson's invaluable 20,000-word historical dossier on the Waldegrave family, which became a primary reference for understanding Borley's Catholic history.
