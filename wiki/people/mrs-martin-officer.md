# Mrs Martin

An officer's wife who was staying with the Rev. and Mrs [[rev-ac-henning|Henning]] at Liston Rectory in September 1943. Mentioned by Henning in a letter of September 15, 1943.

## Cold Spot Experience

Mrs Martin went to look at the Rectory one Sunday evening on her way to church, with no prior knowledge of the "cold spot" or of what the relevant experience felt like.

Henning wrote: *"An officer's wife (Mrs Martin) who is staying with us, went to look at the Rectory on Sunday night on the way to the church, and became cold all over, as Miss Reid did. She had not read your book, and did not know of Miss Reid's experience until we told her later."*

The [[cold-spot|cold spot]] on the first-floor landing outside the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] had by this point been experienced by numerous independent visitors, and the [[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]]'s measurements in June 1943 had confirmed that the temperature anomaly was physically real, not imagined. Mrs Martin's experience in September 1943, made in ignorance of all prior accounts, provides one more independent data point.

The significance of Mrs Martin's experience, like that of [[miss-reid|Miss Reid]] in 1937 and [[rupert-haig|Rupert Haig]] in 1937, lies precisely in its independence: she was not expecting it, had not read about it, and had it anyway.
