# Nancy Goodale (née Fisher)

Daughter of the Rev [[rev-stewart-fisher|Stewart Travers Fisher]], rector of Liston from 1893 until the amalgamation in 1936, and granddaughter of the Rev [[rev-thomas-ruggles-fisher|Thomas Ruggles Fisher]]. Together, her father and grandfather held the Liston living continuously for nearly eighty years (from 1857 to 1936), paralleling the Bull family's long incumbency at Borley. Nancy did not grow up in the Old Rectory at Liston; her father, having spent his boyhood there, preferred to build a modern house (known as "Highfield" / the New Rectory, which still exists today).

Nancy Goodale was well acquainted with the Bull family at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], having grown up in the adjacent parish. She became a close friend of [[claudine-mathias|CM]] and provided testimony relayed through her, including on behalf of Dr Alec Ritchie's family. She actively assisted modern investigators by discussing the case with **Ted Ritchie**, the son of the local physician Dr. Alec Ritchie, whose patients had included [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]].

## Bull Family Observations

Nancy's perspective is that of a genteel neighbour rather than a servant:

- Described the Bulls as "very old-fashioned people, but courteous and sociable; they loved to hold tennis parties in the summer"
- Noted their peculiar, hesitant manner of speech and an eccentric family habit of adding "er" to names when addressing one another ("Ethel-er", "Harry-er"), with the exception of Alfred, who was known as "Ally"
- Observed the disruption caused when the Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] married [[ivy-brackenbury|Ivy Brackenbury]]: Ivy and her daughter were "smart and up-to-date," providing a stark contrast to the "old-fashioned and rather frumpish Bull sisters." Nancy confirmed the two sets of women genuinely disliked each other, and felt this tension may have meant Harry and Ivy's marriage "may not have been a very happy one"
- Recalled [[mabel-smith|Mabel Smith]] saying she was "going to make Borley hum!" — implying she was involved in writing to *The Daily Mirror*, possibly motivated by her novel [[murder-at-the-parsonage|*Murder at the Parsonage*]]

This last detail severely contradicts Mrs Smith's later claims of being a nervous, shy woman terrorised by the Rectory's atmosphere; investigators have noted it suggests Mabel Smith may have deliberately orchestrated publicity.

## Mrs Fisher's Footsteps in Snow

Nancy's mother experienced an apparently paranormal incident at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in the early or mid-1920s. The Fishers had spent an evening as guests of Harry and Ivy Bull and, because heavy snow began falling, stayed overnight. In the morning, Mrs Fisher left the front door first and walked toward the main gate. She heard the sound of footsteps crunching in the snow directly behind her, assumed it was her husband, and turned round — he was not there, nor was anyone else. He was still inside having a parting conversation with his hosts.

## Harry Bull's Confidences

Nancy stated that her father and the Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] were close friends, and that Bull frequently confided his paranormal experiences. Bull told him he had seen the phantom nun, had seen and heard the mysterious coach, and that the unaccountable ringing of the Rectory's bells greatly upset him because **he believed it was a precursor of bad news**.

## Memory of the Foysters

Nancy met the Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] only once, during a garden party at Borley Rectory. She recalled he appeared to be a very sick man, and specifically remembered that **"the food was awful."**
