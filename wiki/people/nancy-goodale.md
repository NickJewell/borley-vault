# Nancy Goodale (née Fisher)

Daughter of the Rev Stewart Travers Fisher, rector of Liston from 1893 until the amalgamation in 1936. The Fisher family held the Liston living for nearly 80 years (her grandfather from 1857, then her father). Nancy's father chose to live in his own new house (known as "Highfield" / the New Rectory) rather than the Old Rectory — which later became the home of [[rev-lanfranc-mathias|the Mathiases]].

Nancy Goodale was well acquainted with the Bull family at Borley, having grown up in the adjacent parish. She became a close friend of [[claudine-mathias|CM]] and provided testimony relayed through her on behalf of Dr Alec Ritchie's family.

## Bull Family Observations

Nancy's perspective is that of a genteel neighbour rather than a servant:
- Confirmed the Bulls were "very old-fashioned people, but courteous and sociable; they loved to hold tennis parties in the summer"
- The Bulls' curious custom of adding "er" to names when addressing each other ("Ethel-er", "Harry-er") led neighbours to call them "the Bull-ers" — Alfred alone escaped as "Ally"
- Confirmed sharp contrast between the old-fashioned Bull sisters and Ivy Brackenbury (Harry's new wife) — "smart and up-to-date in appearance and manner"
- Recalled Mabel Smith saying she was "going to make Borley hum!" — implying she was involved in writing to *The Daily Mirror*, possibly motivated by her novel *Murder at the Parsonage*

## Mrs Fisher's Footsteps in Snow

Nancy's mother experienced an apparently paranormal incident at Borley Rectory. The Fishers had spent an evening as guests of Harry and Ivy Bull and, because heavy snow began falling, stayed overnight. In the morning, Mrs Fisher left the front door first and walked toward the main gate. She heard footsteps crunching in the snow behind her, assumed it was her husband, and turned round — he was not there, nor was anyone else. He was still inside having a parting conversation with his hosts.
