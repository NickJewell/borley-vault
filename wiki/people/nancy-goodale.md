# Nancy Goodale (née Fisher)

Daughter of the Rev Stewart Travers Fisher, rector of Liston from 1893 until the amalgamation in 1936. The Fisher family held the Liston living for nearly 80 years (her grandfather from 1857, then her father). Nancy's father chose to live in his own new house (known as "Highfield" / the New Rectory) rather than the Old Rectory — which later became the home of [[rev-lanfranc-mathias|the Mathiases]].

Nancy Goodale was well acquainted with the Bull family at Borley, having grown up in the adjacent parish. She became a close friend of [[claudine-mathias|CM]] and provided testimony relayed through her on behalf of Dr Alec Ritchie's family.

## Bull Family Observations

Nancy's perspective is that of a genteel neighbour rather than a servant:
- Confirmed the Bulls were "very old-fashioned people, but courteous and sociable; they loved to hold tennis parties in the summer"
- The Bulls' curious custom of adding "er" to names when addressing each other ("Ethel-er", "Harry-er") led neighbours to call them "the Bull-ers" — Alfred alone escaped as "Ally"
- Confirmed sharp contrast between the old-fashioned Bull sisters and Ivy Brackenbury (Harry's new wife) — "smart and up-to-date in appearance and manner"
- Recalled Mabel Smith saying she was "going to make Borley hum!" — implying she was involved in writing to *The Daily Mirror*, possibly motivated by her novel *Murder at the Parsonage*

## Mrs Fisher's Footsteps in Snow

Nancy's mother experienced an apparently paranormal incident at Borley Rectory. The Fishers had spent an evening as guests of Harry and Ivy Bull and, because heavy snow began falling, stayed overnight. In the morning, Mrs Fisher left the front door first and walked toward the main gate. She heard footsteps crunching in the snow behind her, assumed it was her husband, and turned round — he was not there, nor was anyone else. He was still inside having a parting conversation with his hosts.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Nancy Goodale (née Fisher)** serves as a vital first-hand source of information regarding the personalities and private lives of the key figures involved in the early years of the Borley Rectory haunting. Through her interviews with the authors of *Borley Rectory - The Final Analysis*, she provided unique local context and lesser-known biographical details that challenge several prevailing narratives about the Rectory's residents [1-3].

**Biographical Details and Background**
*   **A Clerical Dynasty:** She was the daughter of the **Rev. Stewart Travers Fisher** and the granddaughter of the **Rev. Thomas Ruggles Fisher** [1]. Together, her father and grandfather held the living of the nearby parish of Liston continuously for nearly eighty years (from 1857 to 1936), paralleling the Bull family's long incumbency at Borley [1]. 
*   **Residence:** She did not grow up in the Old Rectory at Liston; her father, having spent his boyhood there, preferred to build a modern house [1]. Thus, Nancy grew up in the family's private home known as the New Rectory (which still exists today under the name "Highfield") [1]. 
*   **Research Contributions:** Beyond providing her own memories, Nancy Goodale actively assisted modern investigators by discussing the Borley case with **Ted Ritchie**, the son of the local physician Dr. Alec Ritchie, whose patients had included Marianne Foyster [4]. 

**Her Family's Paranormal Experiences**
While Nancy did not report any paranormal experiences of her own, she revealed that her parents were intimately connected to the Borley phenomena:
*   **The "Crunching Footsteps":** In the early or mid-1920s, her parents spent an evening as guests of Harry and Ivy Bull at Borley Rectory [5]. A heavy snowstorm forced them to stay the night [5]. When departing the next morning, Nancy's mother walked out of the front door first and distinctly **heard the sound of footsteps crunching in the snow directly behind her** [5]. Assuming it was her husband, she turned around, but the path behind her was completely empty [5]. 
*   **Harry Bull's Confidences:** Nancy stated that her father and the Rev. Harry Bull were close friends, and that Bull spoke frequently to him about his paranormal experiences [5]. Bull confided that he had seen the phantom nun, had seen and heard the mysterious coach, and that the unaccountable ringing of the Rectory's bells greatly upset him because **he believed it was a precursor of bad news** [5]. 

**Insights on the Bull Family**
Having known the Bulls well during her youth, Nancy provided specific character assessments that contrast with other historical portrayals:
*   **Demeanor and Eccentricities:** She described the Bulls as old-fashioned, courteous, and sociable, recalling that they loved to host summer tennis parties [2]. She noted their peculiar, hesitant manner of speech and an eccentric family habit of adding "er" to their names when addressing one another (e.g., "Ethel-er" or "Harry-er"), with the exception of Alfred, who was known as "Ally" [2].
*   **The Arrival of Ivy Bull:** Nancy keenly observed the disruption caused when the Rev. Harry Bull married Ivy Brackenbury [2]. She recalled that Ivy and her daughter were "smart and up-to-date," providing a stark contrast to the "old-fashioned and rather frumpish Bull sisters" [2]. Nancy confirmed that the two sets of women genuinely disliked each other, adding that this tension undoubtedly put pressure on Harry and Ivy's marriage, which Nancy felt **"may not have been a very happy one"** [2].

**Contradicting Mabel Smith**
Nancy Goodale's brief interactions with the Rev. Guy Eric Smith's wife, Mabel, severely contradict Mrs. Smith's later claims of being a nervous, shy woman who was terrorized by the Rectory's atmosphere:
*   Nancy recalled Mrs. Smith explicitly stating that she was **"going to make Borley hum!"** [3]. 
*   Investigators have noted that this highly specific quote suggests Mabel Smith may have deliberately orchestrated her husband's letter to the *Daily Mirror* to drum up publicity for a novel she was trying to publish, making her later denials of the haunting appear potentially hypocritical [3]. 

**Memory of the Foysters**
Nancy's exposure to the Foyster household was much more limited, centering on a single event that left a rather unpleasant impression:
*   She met the Rev. Lionel Foyster only once, during a garden party at Borley Rectory [6]. 
*   She distinctly remembered that he appeared to be a very sick man, and she specifically recalled that **"the food was awful"** [6].
