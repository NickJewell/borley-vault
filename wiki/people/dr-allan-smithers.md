# Dr Allan Smithers

Presbyterian clergyman at **St Ansgar's Church, Salmonhurst**, New Brunswick, who married [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]] and [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Shaw]] on 28 August 1922 — and who immediately raised a canonical objection to the marriage.

## The Wedding and Its Objection

Smithers performed the marriage ceremony at St Ansgar's in Salmonhurst. But during the reception that followed, he was shocked to overhear Lionel boast that he had **baptised Marianne as a child** at Oughtrington, when she was seven years old and he was a curate there. Smithers believed that this made the marriage invalid in the eyes of the Presbyterian church — a spiritual father could not, in his view, also be a husband. He considered it a form of **spiritual incest**.

Despite Smithers's awkward intervention at the reception, the marriage was valid in civil law and the couple proceeded with their honeymoon. The theological objection, however, lingered. [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] later told the story as one in which [[lionel-foyster|Lionel]] was untroubled, having already resolved it within his own theology.

## Significance

Smithers's objection became one of the arguments that Lionel Foyster used later to justify tolerating Marianne's affairs with other men — citing Dr Smithers's view, he reasoned that their marriage had never been truly valid in the eyes of the Church, and therefore some latitude was appropriate. This rationalisation was quoted by both [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] and investigators in explaining the complex domestic arrangements at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Dr. Allan Smithers** is a minor but notable figure in the Borley Rectory story, recorded exclusively for his role in officiating the marriage between the **Rev. Lionel Algernon Foyster** and his much younger wife, **Marianne Shaw**. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following facts, events, quotes, and controversies regarding his involvement:

**The Wedding in Canada (August 1922)**
Dr. Smithers was the clergyman who performed the marriage ceremony for Lionel and Marianne at St. Ansgar’s church in Salmonhurst, New Brunswick, Canada [1, 2]. The texts present a minor chronological contradiction regarding the exact date, recording the wedding as occurring on either August 22 or August 28, 1922 [1, 3].

**The Wedding Reception and "Spiritual Incest"**
A major incident occurred during the wedding reception when Lionel Foyster boasted to Dr. Smithers that he had personally baptized Marianne when she was a little girl [2, 4]. Dr. Smithers was deeply disturbed and shocked to hear this information [2, 4]. 

Dr. Smithers reportedly told the couple that **while their marriage was perfectly valid in the eyes of the law, it would not be regarded as valid in the eyes of the church** (specifically noted in one text as the Presbyterian church) because Lionel stood in the relationship of a "spiritual father" to his new bride [2, 4]. Smithers firmly believed that a spiritual father could not become a husband, characterizing their union as a sort of **"spiritual incest"** [2].

**Impact and Later Justifications by Marianne**
According to Marianne Foyster’s later testimony to researchers Iris Owen and Pauline Mitchell, Dr. Smithers's pronouncement "shocked Lionel very much" [4]. Decades later, Marianne used Dr. Smithers's comments as a convenient explanation for her husband's highly unusual domestic arrangements. She claimed that because Dr. Smithers had convinced Lionel that their marriage was sacramentally invalid, this provided Foyster with the theological justification to accept her many extra-marital affairs and her later bigamous marriage to Henry Fisher [4, 5]. 

**Contradictions and Criticisms of the Event**
The veracity and theological soundness of Dr. Smithers's alleged pronouncement have been severely questioned by Borley critics and researchers:
*   **A "Very Strange" Comment:** Investigators Owen and Mitchell noted that **Dr. Smithers's comment seemed "very strange"** to them, although Marianne fervently assured them that the exchange had truly happened [4]. 
*   **No Doctrinal Basis:** Author Robert Wood (in *The Widow of Borley*) fiercely ridiculed the entire episode, arguing that Dr. Smithers's objection to the marriage had **"no basis in doctrine or practice"** [6]. 
*   **An Absurd Excuse:** Wood further argued that it was completely absurd to believe a highly educated, deeply religious Anglo-Catholic clergyman like Foyster would be genuinely disturbed by such a "silly idea" from Smithers, or that he would ever use such flawed theological logic as an excuse to condone his wife's flagrant adultery [6].
