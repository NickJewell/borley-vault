# Dr Allan Smithers

Presbyterian clergyman at **St Ansgar's Church, [[salmonhurst-new-brunswick|Salmonhurst]]**, New Brunswick, who married [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]] and [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Shaw]] — and who immediately raised a canonical objection to the marriage.

## The Wedding in Canada

> **Contradiction:** The sources record the wedding as occurring on either **22 August** or **28 August 1922** [1, 3].

Smithers performed the marriage ceremony at St Ansgar's in Salmonhurst, New Brunswick [1, 2].

## The Wedding Reception and "Spiritual Incest"

A major incident occurred during the wedding reception when [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] boasted to Dr Smithers that he had personally baptised Marianne when she was a little girl at Oughtrington, when she was seven years old and he was a curate there [2, 4]. Smithers was deeply disturbed and shocked to hear this [2, 4].

Smithers reportedly told the couple that **while their marriage was perfectly valid in the eyes of the law, it would not be regarded as valid in the eyes of the church** — specifically the Presbyterian church — because Lionel stood in the relationship of a "spiritual father" to his new bride [2, 4]. Smithers firmly believed that a spiritual father could not become a husband, characterising their union as a sort of **"spiritual incest"** [2].

Despite Smithers's awkward intervention, the marriage was valid in civil law and the couple proceeded with their honeymoon. The theological objection, however, lingered.

## Impact and Marianne's Later Justifications

According to [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]]'s later testimony to researchers [[iris-owen|Iris Owen]] and Pauline Mitchell, Dr Smithers's pronouncement "shocked Lionel very much" [4]. Decades later, Marianne used Smithers's comments as a convenient explanation for her husband's highly unusual domestic arrangements. She claimed that because Smithers had convinced Lionel that their marriage was sacramentally invalid, this provided Foyster with the theological justification to accept her many extra-marital affairs and her later bigamous marriage to [[henry-fisher|Henry Fisher]] [4, 5].

[[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] later told the story as one in which [[lionel-foyster|Lionel]] was untroubled, having already resolved it within his own theology.

## Contradictions and Criticisms

The veracity and theological soundness of Smithers's alleged pronouncement have been severely questioned:

- **A "very strange" comment:** Investigators Owen and Mitchell noted that Smithers's comment seemed "very strange" to them, although Marianne fervently assured them the exchange had truly happened [4].
- **No doctrinal basis:** Author Robert Wood (in *The Widow of Borley*) fiercely ridiculed the episode, arguing that Smithers's objection had "no basis in doctrine or practice" [6].
- **An absurd excuse:** Wood further argued that it was completely absurd to believe a highly educated, deeply religious Anglo-Catholic clergyman like Foyster would be genuinely disturbed by such a "silly idea" from Smithers, or that he would use such flawed theological logic as an excuse to condone his wife's flagrant adultery [6].

## Significance

Smithers's objection became one of the arguments that [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] used later to justify tolerating [[marianne-foyster|Marianne's]] affairs with other men — reasoning that their marriage had never been truly valid in the eyes of the Church, and therefore some latitude was appropriate. This rationalisation was quoted by both Marianne and investigators in explaining the complex domestic arrangements at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].
