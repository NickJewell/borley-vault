# John Herringham (Rector of Borley)

Rector of Borley from 1819 to 1862; the son of [[william-herringham|William Herringham]] who preceded him. The two Herringhams held the Borley living continuously for 55 years, and John's departure was immediately followed by the arrival of [[henry-bull|the Rev Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]], who commissioned [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in 1863.

## The Herringham Line

John Herringham was inducted in 1819, directly succeeding his father William. This father-son pattern of consecutive rectors was common in the Victorian and Edwardian periods; similarly the Bull family produced a father-son succession at Borley from 1862 to 1927, and the Fisher family at [[liston-rectory|Liston]] followed the same pattern.

The Herringham rectory that stood on the site of the future Borley Rectory was described in 1848 (during John's incumbency) as a "modern building" — confirming that it was constructed sometime after 1807 and did not long predate the major Victorian rectory that replaced it in 1863.

## Documentary Evidence

[[len-sewell|Leonard Sewell]] discovered a tithe map of Borley dated 1841 that shows the layout of the site during John Herringham's incumbency: a building occupying part of the site of the later large rectory, and another house in the position of the rectory cottage. A still earlier map dated 1772 showed three buildings on the combined site: one where the Bull rectory would be built, one where the cottage still stands, and a third where the present farm barns are. The position of the third building is of interest in the context of possible tunnel routes.

## Significance

John Herringham held Borley for 43 years — longer than any other modern rector except the combined Bull incumbencies. His departure in 1862 marks the end of the Herringham era and the beginning of the period that gave Borley its reputation.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**John Philip Herringham** (sometimes recorded as Joh. P. Herringham) served as the Rector of Borley for over four decades in the 19th century. He provides the historical bridge between the early 19th-century parish and the famous 65-year ecclesiastical dynasty of the Bull family that succeeded him. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, events, specific quotes, and contradictions regarding his life and incumbency:

**Biographical Details and Family Tragedy**
*   **Early Life:** John Philip Herringham was the son of the Rev. William Herringham (who served as Borley’s rector from 1807 to 1819) and his wife, Anne [1, 2]. 
*   **Family Tragedy:** His family’s life at Borley was overshadowed by grief due to the premature deaths of all three of his sisters. Two of the sisters were interred in a vault in the churchyard within sight of the Rectory, and all three are commemorated inside Borley Church with a marble plaque [2].
*   **Induction and Career:** Upon his father's death in 1819, John Philip assumed the Borley living [2]. The sources note an interesting (if slightly confused) detail regarding his career ambition: it is stated that Herringham **"gave up a career path that would have most likely taken him to a bishopric in order to settle in the little village"** [3]. *(Note: The source text somewhat confusingly splits his name, adding "and his son Philip was happy to spend his whole career there," though historical records confirm John Philip was the single incumbent during this period [1, 3, 4]).*

**Contradictions Regarding His Death and Incumbency Dates**
There is a minor historical contradiction in the sources regarding the exact end of his life and tenure:
*   Several historical summaries and lists of rectors state that Herringham’s incumbency ended with his death in **1862**, which is the year Henry Dawson Ellis Bull was inducted to the living [1, 5, 6].
*   However, a direct transcription of his gravestone located in the churchyard explicitly contradicts this 1862 date, reading: **"In memory of John Philip Herringham - 42 years - Rector of this Parish - died 4th Dec: 1861 - Aged 72."** [7]. 

**Residency, the "Window Tax," and Paranormal Suspicions**
A significant mystery surrounds Herringham's choice of residence during his incumbency. 
*   While his father, William, had built a "good modern residence" known as the Herringham Rectory in the early 1800s, John Philip chose to abandon it in his later years [8, 9]. Instead, he moved across the road to live in **Borley Place**, a 16th- or 17th-century house [8, 9]. 
*   **The Contradiction of the Move:** White’s Gazetteer for 1848 specifically described the Herringham Rectory as being a "good modern residence," making John Philip's refusal to live there highly puzzling [8, 9]. 
*   **Theories:** Some historians suggested that the financial burden of the **Window Tax** (which was still in operation during his tenure) forced him to move to a smaller property [9]. However, Borley researcher Ivan Banks postulated a much darker reason: he suggested Herringham chose Borley Place because **the old Rectory had already become the site of paranormal activity** [9]. Following his death, the Herringham Rectory was demolished to make way for the famously haunted Bull Rectory [4, 10, 11].

**The 1858 "Ghost Nun" Quote**
Herringham is directly linked to one of the earliest alleged sightings of the famous Borley ghost nun. During the 1970s, an elderly woman named Mabel Smith (no relation to the later Rev. Guy Smith's wife) recalled a story told to her by her mother, Mary.
*   Mary had taken Bible lessons from John Herringham. One day, in either 1858 or 1859, while they were inside Borley Church, the rector suddenly interrupted the lesson and stated: **"Mary, there is a nun in my churchyard"** [12].
*   However, when the rector and the young girl went outside to investigate, there was no one in sight [12].
