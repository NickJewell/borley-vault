# John Herringham (Rector of Borley)

**John Philip Herringham** (sometimes recorded as Joh. P. Herringham) served as Rector of Borley from 1819 until 1861 or 1862, the son of [[william-herringham|William Herringham]] who preceded him. The two Herringhams held the Borley living continuously for 55 years, and John's departure was immediately followed by the arrival of [[henry-bull|the Rev Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]], who commissioned [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in 1863.

## Family and Early Life

John Philip Herringham was the son of the Rev. William Herringham (who served as Borley's rector from 1807 to 1819) and his wife Anne [1, 2]. His family's life at Borley was overshadowed by grief: all three of his sisters died prematurely. Two were interred in a vault in the churchyard within sight of the Rectory, and all three are commemorated inside Borley Church with a marble plaque [2]. Upon his father's death in 1819, John Philip assumed the Borley living [2].

The sources note an interesting detail regarding his career: it is stated that Herringham **"gave up a career path that would have most likely taken him to a bishopric in order to settle in the little village"** [3].

## Incumbency Dates: A Contradiction

> **Contradiction:** Several historical summaries and lists of rectors state that Herringham's incumbency ended with his death in **1862** — the year [[henry-bull|Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] was inducted to the living [1, 5, 6]. However, a direct transcription of his gravestone in the churchyard explicitly contradicts this, reading: **"In memory of John Philip Herringham - 42 years - Rector of this Parish - died 4th Dec: 1861 - Aged 72."** [7]

## Residence, the "Window Tax," and Paranormal Suspicions

A significant mystery surrounds Herringham's choice of residence during his incumbency. While his father William had built a "good modern residence" — the Herringham Rectory — in the early 1800s, John Philip chose to abandon it in his later years and instead moved across the road to live in **Borley Place**, a 16th- or 17th-century house [8, 9]. This was puzzling because White's *Gazetteer* for 1848 had specifically described the Herringham Rectory as a "good modern residence" [8, 9].

Two explanations have been proposed: some historians suggest the financial burden of the **Window Tax** (still in operation during his tenure) forced him to a smaller property [9]; while [[ivan-banks|Ivan Banks]] postulated a darker reason — that the old Rectory had already become the site of paranormal activity and Herringham chose to leave it [9]. Following his death, the Herringham Rectory was demolished to make way for the famously haunted Bull Rectory [4, 10, 11].

The [[herringham-rectory|Herringham Rectory]] that stood on the site was described in 1848 as a "modern building," confirming that it was constructed sometime after 1807 and did not long predate the major Victorian rectory that replaced it in 1863.

## The 1858 "Ghost Nun" Quote

Herringham is directly linked to one of the earliest alleged sightings of the famous Borley ghost nun. During the 1970s, an elderly woman named Mabel Smith (no relation to the later [[rev-eric-smith|Rev. Guy Smith]]'s wife) recalled a story told to her by her mother, Mary, who had taken Bible lessons from John Herringham. One day, in either 1858 or 1859, while they were inside Borley Church, the rector suddenly interrupted the lesson and stated: **"Mary, there is a nun in my churchyard"** [12]. When they went outside to investigate, there was no one in sight [12]. (single witness; reported second-hand decades later)

## Documentary Evidence

[[len-sewell|Leonard Sewell]] discovered a tithe map of Borley dated 1841 that shows the layout of the site during John Herringham's incumbency: a building occupying part of the site of the later large rectory, and another house in the position of the rectory cottage. A still earlier map dated 1772 showed three buildings on the combined site — one where the Bull rectory would be built, one where the cottage still stands, and a third where the present farm barns are. The position of the third building is of interest in the context of possible [[tunnels|tunnel routes]].

## Significance

John Herringham held Borley for 43 years — longer than any other modern rector except the combined Bull incumbencies. His departure in 1861/62 marks the end of the Herringham era and the beginning of the period that gave Borley its reputation.
