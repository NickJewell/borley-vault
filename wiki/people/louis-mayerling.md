# Louis Mayerling (born George Carter, 4 September 1913)

Author of *We Faked the Ghosts of Borley Rectory* (Pen Press, c. 2000–2002), claiming the entire [[borley-rectory|Borley haunting]] was "an ingeniously orchestrated hoax." Subsequent investigation exposed his claims as fiction or fabrication; on BBC Television he admitted he had "exaggerated here and there." Universally regarded in the Borley literature as the perpetrator of a massive literary hoax. He died in Norfolk in 2002.

## Identity

**Born:** George Carter, 4 September 1913, at 63 Mayes Road, Wood Green, London. Father: James Carter (kitchen porter). Mother: Amy Carter (formerly Cook). Name changed to "Louis Mayerling" by deed poll — confirmed by a 1953 marriage certificate bearing the annotation "Louis Mayerling (name changed by deed poll)."

His book claimed he was born in Vienna "of Amelia Romanov," placed in foster care with the Carter family, became a child prodigy violinist and jazz pianist performing under the pseudonym "Lee Lennox," played the banjo in a wartime Berlin nightclub while impersonating Marlene Dietrich, escaped occupied Europe by walking over the Pyrenees, knew Marilyn Monroe and George Bernard Shaw, lived with T.E. Lawrence of Arabia, acted as driver-companion to the Prince of Wales and Mrs. Simpson, worked with Frank Whittle on the first jet engine, and piloted a one-man submarine into Singapore harbour to blow it up.

## The Book's Claims

Mayerling claimed to have spent years at Borley Rectory among the Bulls, Foysters, and Smiths, "constantly being invited to accept the place as his second home," and to have been personally responsible for most of the alleged phenomena. Specific debunked claims include:

- **The Cellar Séance:** He claimed to have attended an early 1930s séance in the cellars with [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]], Montague Norman (Governor of the Bank of England), George Bernard Shaw, Sir Bernard Spilsbury, Maurice Barbanell, and T.E. Lawrence. Lawrence supposedly left hurriedly because he objected to Shaw's presence.
- **Faking the Piano:** He took credit for the Rectory's piano playing itself, claiming he plucked the strings with a stick through a missing back and a wall grille — a claim disproved by [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville's]] architectural plans, which show no such grille existed.
- **Faking the Mirror Tapping:** He claimed the famous 1929 [[blue-room|Blue Room]] séance was faked by tenants of the Rectory cottage tapping on the bedroom window with a pole — physically absurd given the layout.
- **The Ghost Club:** He claimed to investigator Stewart P. Evans that he had personally started the Ghost Club with [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] in 1938.
- **Correspondence with Marianne:** He also claimed correspondence with [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]].

## Refutation

[[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood's]] review identified eight specific factual impossibilities. Underwood noted Mayerling described the large dining room as "small and dark," referenced secret "hidey-holes" that meticulous surveys proved didn't exist, and claimed to receive a letter from the Smiths inviting him to their final sermon at a time inconsistent with the known chronology. He also mistakenly captioned a photograph of an unknown man as [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]].

[[alan-gregson|Alan Wesencraft]] (Harry Price Library, 42 years' experience) confirmed he had never encountered any mention of Mayerling or George Carter in the files of Price, Baines, Dingwall, or Hall, or from any of the many people he had interviewed. [[edward-cooper|Edward Babbs]]: "Far from Borley Rectory being Mayerling's second home, one has to consider if he ever went there."

[[vincent-oneil|Vincent O'Neil]] (Marianne's adopted son): **"I no longer believe that Mayerling knew my mother, and if he met her at all, it was only for a brief period of time. As with the other Borley-related memories from his book, the time spent with her happened only in his imagination. Unfortunately he has lived with these false ideas so long he probably now believes them to be true."**

Colin Wilson (*Daily Mail*, 3 January 2001): "It seems fairly clear that the book is intended either as fiction or as a hoax."

## The "Freudian Slip" and Confession

During a non-confrontational interview with investigator Stewart P. Evans on **March 17, 2001** (when Mayerling was frail and recovering from an operation in Norfolk), Mayerling made a fatal admission: he stated that Harry Price's 1940 book *The Most Haunted House in England* was what **"got me interested in the first place"** — proving he was not involved in the actual historical events, which predated his stated source of interest by twenty-one years. Evans called this "condemned out of his own mouth."

In his own book, Mayerling also recounted that while arrested in Germany as a transvestite, he passed the time by entertaining others with stories, writing: "...much debate followed my completely fictitious tales of Borley Rectory... I became noted for my tales of 'hauntings' – wildly exaggerated whenever I felt like it." Investigators concluded his entire book was merely an extension of these fictitious tales.

BBC Television, December 2000 (*The Ghosts of Christmas Past*): Mayerling admitted, **"I have exaggerated here and there."**
