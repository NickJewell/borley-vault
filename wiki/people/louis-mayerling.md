# Louis Mayerling (born George Carter, 4 September 1913)

Author of *We Faked the Ghosts of Borley Rectory* (Pen Press, 2000), claiming the entire [[borley-rectory|Borley haunting]] was "an ingeniously orchestrated hoax." Subsequent investigation exposed his claims as fiction or fabrication; on BBC Television he admitted he had "exaggerated here and there."

## Identity

**Born:** George Carter, 4 September 1913, at 63 Mayes Road, Wood Green, London. Father: James Carter (kitchen porter). Mother: Amy Carter (formerly Cook). Name changed to "Louis Mayerling" by deed poll (marriage certificate dated 1953 records the change).

His book claimed he was born in Vienna "of Amelia Romanov," placed in foster care with the Carter family, became a child prodigy violinist, knew Marilyn Monroe, George Bernard Shaw, T.E. Lawrence, and the Prince of Wales, and was a driver-companion to the Prince.

## The Book's Claims

Mayerling claimed to have spent years at Borley Rectory among the Bulls, Foysters, and Smiths, "constantly being invited to accept the place as his second home," and to have been personally responsible for most of the alleged phenomena. He also claimed correspondence with Marianne Foyster.

## Refutation

[[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]]'s review identified eight specific factual impossibilities. Alan Wesencraft (Harry Price Library, 42 years' experience) confirmed he had never encountered any mention of Mayerling or George Carter in the files of Price, Baines, Dingwall, or Hall, or from any of the many people he had interviewed. Edward Babbs: "Far from Borley Rectory being Mayerling's second home, one has to consider if he ever went there."

[[vincent-oneil|Vincent O'Neil]] (Marianne's adopted son): "I no longer believe that Mayerling knew my mother, and if he met her at all, it was only for a brief period of time. As with the other Borley-related memories from his book, the time spent with my mother happened only in his imagination."

BBC Television, December 2000 (*The Ghosts of Christmas Past*): Mayerling admitted, "I have exaggerated here and there."

## The Stewart Evans Interview (17 March 2001)

Evans conducted a "friendly and totally non-aggressive" interview. Key exchange: Mayerling said Price's book "got me interested in the first place." Price's *Most Haunted House in England* was published in 1940. Mayerling's claimed 1919 (age 6) activities at Borley therefore predate his stated source of interest by 21 years — an impossibility Evans called "condemned out of his own mouth."

Mayerling also claimed: "I started The Ghost Club with Eric Dingwall in 1938"; "I worked with Frank Whittle on the jets, on his first jet engine"; and that he went "to Singapore on a one-man submarine to help blow up the harbour along with two others, they were killed."

## Colin Wilson's Assessment

"It seems fairly clear that the book is intended either as fiction or as a hoax." (*Daily Mail*, 3 January 2001.)

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Louis Mayerling** (real name **George Carter**) is universally regarded in the Borley literature as the perpetrator of a massive literary hoax [1, 2]. In his book, *We Faked the Ghosts of Borley Rectory* (published around 2000 or 2002), he claimed that the entire Borley haunting was "merely an ingeniously orchestrated hoax" in which he had personally participated [1-3]. 

**Real Biographical Details**
Mayerling was born George Carter on **September 4, 1913**, at 63 Mayes Road, Wood Green, London [4, 5]. His parents were James Carter, a kitchen porter, and Amy Carter (formerly Cook) [4, 5]. A marriage certificate from 1953 confirms his true identity, bearing the annotation "Louis Mayerling (name changed by deed poll)" [4, 5]. He died in Norfolk in 2002 [2]. 

**Grandiose and Fabricated Claims**
Carter invented a wildly fantastical and impossible background for himself. He claimed to have been born in Vienna to Amelia Romanov, asserting he was a child prodigy violinist, a dancer, and a jazz pianist who played under the pseudonym "Lee Lennox" [2, 6, 7]. He claimed to have played the banjo in a wartime Berlin nightclub while impersonating Marlene Dietrich, before escaping occupied Europe by walking over the Pyrenees [7]. Furthermore, he boasted of knowing Marilyn Monroe and George Bernard Shaw, living with T.E. Lawrence of Arabia, acting as driver-companion to the Prince of Wales and Mrs. Simpson, working with Frank Whittle on the first jet engine, and piloting a one-man submarine into Singapore harbour to blow it up [2, 3, 8].

**Claims Regarding Borley Rectory**
Mayerling claimed he had known Borley Rectory since 1913 (when he was an infant) and spent so much time among the Bulls, Smiths, and Foysters that he was continually invited to treat the house as a second home [4, 9]. Specific (and thoroughly debunked) claims he made about the Rectory include:
*   **The Cellar Séance:** He claimed to have attended an early 1930s séance in the slimy, amphibian-infested cellars of the Rectory with Marianne Foyster, Montague Norman (Governor of the Bank of England), George Bernard Shaw, Sir Bernard Spilsbury, Maurice Barbanell, and T.E. Lawrence [10]. Lawrence supposedly left hurriedly because he objected to Shaw's presence [10]. Mayerling claimed the séance ended with a sudden flash of light that left him temporarily blind and with long-term visual problems [10].
*   **Faking the Piano:** He took credit for the Rectory's piano playing itself, claiming he plucked the strings with a stick through a missing back and a wall grille—a claim disproved by Sidney Glanville's architectural plans, which show no such grille existed [11]. 
*   **Faking the Mirror Tapping:** He claimed the famous 1929 Blue Room séance with Harry Price was faked by tenants of the Rectory cottage tapping on the bedroom window with a pole, which investigators noted was physically absurd [12].
*   **The Ghost Club:** He bizarrely claimed to the investigator Stewart P. Evans that he had personally started the Ghost Club with Eric Dingwall in 1938 [13]. 

**Contradictions and Exposure**
Investigators completely dismantled Mayerling's story. Peter Underwood, who reviewed the book, pointed out numerous glaring contradictions: Mayerling described the large dining room as "small and dark," referenced secret "hidey-holes" that meticulous surveys proved didn't exist, and claimed to receive a letter from the Smiths days after they left the Rectory inviting him to their final sermon (they left in July 1929 but preached the final sermon in April 1930) [10, 14]. He also mistakenly captioned a photo of an unknown man as Lionel Foyster, blaming the error on his publisher and his own poor eyesight [15, 16]. 

Furthermore, no one associated with the case—including Harry Price, the Bulls, the Foysters, the Smiths, or the curator of the Harry Price Library, Alan Wesencraft—ever mentioned Louis Mayerling or George Carter [4, 17]. Vincent O'Neil (Marianne Foyster's adopted son) publicly rejected the book, stating: **"I no longer believe that Mayerling knew my mother... The time he spent with her happened only in his imagination. Unfortunately he has lived with these false ideas so long he probably now believes them to be true"** [18, 19]. 

**The "Freudian Slip" and Confession**
Mayerling's ultimate exposure came from his own lips and pen. During a non-confrontational interview with investigator Stewart P. Evans on March 17, 2001 (when Mayerling was frail and recovering from an operation in Norfolk), Mayerling made a fatal "Freudian slip" [20-22]. When asked about his involvement, he admitted that Harry Price's 1940 book *The Most Haunted House in England* was what **"got me interested in the first place"**—proving he was not involved in the actual historical events [8, 22, 23]. 

Additionally, in his own book, Mayerling recounted that while arrested in Germany as a transvestite, he passed the time by entertaining others with stories, writing: **"...much debate followed my completely fictitious tales of Borley Rectory... I became noted for my tales of 'hauntings' – wildly exaggerated whenever I felt like it"** [5, 23]. Investigators concluded that his entire book was merely an extension of these fictitious tales [23, 24].
