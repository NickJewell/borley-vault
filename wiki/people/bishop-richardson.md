# Bishop Richardson (of Fredericton)

Anglican bishop of the Diocese of Fredericton, New Brunswick, Canada, during [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]]'s Canadian incumbencies (c. 1910–1929). His diocesan registers are the principal documentary evidence for the chronology of Foyster's Canadian postings.

## Diocesan Register Entries

Robert Wood quotes directly from Bishop Richardson's register:

**Jan 15, 1927** — "The Bishop accepted the resignation of the Rev. L.A. Foyster from the parish (mission) of Drummond to take effect from Feb 7th, 1927."

**Feb 26, 1927** — "The Bishop on February 13th, instituted the Rev. Lionel Algernon Foyster into the Rectory of St Paul's church in the county of Westmorland [Sackville]."

**July 22, 1929** — "The Bishop accepted the resignation of the Rev. L.A. Foyster (to take effect Sept 15th next) from Parish of Sackville."

**Sept 9, 1929** — "The Bishop, Sept 7th 1929, granted leave of absence from his duties in the space of twelve months to Rev. Lionel Algernon Foyster."

## Alleged Pressure to Leave

[[ian-greenwood|Ian Greenwood Shaw]] alleged that Bishop Richardson grew so displeased with [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]]'s conduct (affairs at Salmonhurst and Sackville) that he "asked Foyster to leave the diocese." Wood notes that "no records about this survive in the diocese of Fredericton" and that Ian's account is uncorroborated; he cannot confirm or deny the allegation.

Wood does conclude from the register entries alone that the move from Salmonhurst to Sackville (a more urban posting) may have been partly due to Foyster's deteriorating health and the desire for a less isolated living, rather than exclusively due to scandals caused by Marianne. His final resignation and extended leave of absence in 1929 preceded the return to England and the Borley appointment.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Bishop Richardson** served as the Bishop of Fredericton in New Brunswick, Canada, and was the spiritual superior to the Rev. Lionel Algernon Foyster during the latter's missionary work in North America [1-3]. He enters the Borley Rectory story through his official and personal dealings with the Foysters prior to their move to the haunted rectory in England. 

A thorough reference profile based on the sources includes the following details:

**Official Register Entries Regarding Lionel Foyster**
Bishop Richardson’s diocesan registers provide a precise timeline of Lionel Foyster’s final years in Canada:
*   **January 15, 1927:** The Bishop accepted Foyster’s resignation from the parish (mission) of Drummond, which took effect on February 7, 1927 [2]. 
*   **February 13, 1927:** Bishop Richardson instituted Foyster into his new Rectory at St. Paul’s Church in Sackville, Westmorland County (the entry was recorded on February 26) [4].
*   **July 22, 1929:** The Bishop accepted Foyster’s resignation from Sackville, effective September 15 [5].
*   **September 7, 1929:** The Bishop granted Foyster a leave of absence from his duties [5]. 
*   **June 20, 1930:** The Bishop officially transferred Lionel Foyster to the diocese of Chelmsford in England, paving the way for him to take up the living at Borley [6].

**The Scandals of Marianne Foyster and Foyster's Dismissal**
While the official registers simply show resignations and transfers, Ian Shaw (Marianne Foyster’s son) provided a much more scandalous behind-the-scenes account of the Bishop's relationship with the Foysters. 

According to Shaw, Marianne became involved with other men in Canada, including a young Anglican clergyman and a Roman Catholic priest [7]. These scandals are alleged to have **greatly displeased the Bishop of Fredericton** when they came to his notice [8]. Despite the embarrassment, Lionel Foyster adamantly defended his wife; the sources note that he **"would hear nothing against his adored wife, not even from his spiritual superior, the Bishop of Fredericton"** [3]. 

When the Foysters moved to Sackville, Marianne reportedly soon got herself into trouble again [8]. Ian Shaw claimed that **"Eventually the Bishop of Fredericton could stand no more, and he asked Foyster to leave the diocese"** [8]. Borley author Robert Wood concludes that the Bishop finally losing patience with Marianne was one of the three primary reasons (alongside failing health and the 1929 Wall Street Crash) that drove the Foysters to leave Canada in 1930 and take refuge in Borley Rectory [9]. 

*(Note: The sources specify that no records confirming these specific scandals or the Bishop's disciplinary actions survive in the diocesan records of Fredericton, making Ian Shaw's claims uncorroborated by official Church documents [10].)*

**Source Contradiction: The Leave of Absence**
The texts contain a minor contradiction regarding the exact length of the leave of absence granted by the Bishop in late 1929:
*   Extracts from Bishop Richardson's own register state that on September 7, 1929, he granted Foyster a leave of absence **"in the space of twelve months"** [5]. 
*   However, another passage states that in October 1929, Lionel was granted **"three months’ leave of absence from New Brunswick's Bishop of Fredericton"** in order to be treated at St. Luke’s hospital in London for his declining health [11].
