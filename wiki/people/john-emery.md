# John Evemond Emery

(Born 25 April 1932; died 20 September 1932) A newborn baby adopted by [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] who lived at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] briefly before dying at the age of four and a half months — one of the tragic stories of Borley.

## Background and Official Identity

Official records indicate that the child was born on **25 April 1932** at 30 Swanage Road, Southend-on-Sea, Essex — an address belonging to a midwife [1–3]. He was an illegitimate child; the space on his birth certificate for the father's name was left blank, and the mother's address was recorded as being in Kent [3].

Marianne Foyster brought the baby to Borley Rectory in **May 1932**, stating she had adopted him from a London agency [2, 3]. To help care for the child, she advertised in the *Nursing Mirror* and hired a State Registered Nurse, Miss Edith M. Dytor (see [[edith-wildgoose|Mrs Wildgoose]]), to serve as nursemaid and companion [2, 4].

## The Paternity Question

A major historical contradiction surrounds whether John Emery was truly adopted or was, in fact, Marianne Foyster's biological child:

- **The Nurse's Testimony:** Miss Dytor stated it was her **professional medical opinion that Marianne had recently given birth** [2, 3, 5, 6].
- **The Birth Certificate Anomaly:** When investigator Trevor Hall tracked down the birth certificate, he found the child was registered simply as "John Emery" (without the middle name "Evemond"), and while the mother's surname was not recorded as Foyster, **her initials were exactly "M.E.R." — identical to those of Marianne** [3].
- **The Suspected Father:** It was strongly rumoured that the baby was the result of a liaison between Marianne and [[francois-darles|François d'Arles]] (Frank Pearless), who was living at the Rectory Cottage at the time [2, 6]. [[ian-greenwood|Ian Greenwood]], Marianne's son from a previous marriage, visited Borley in late 1932 and reported that **d'Arles was utterly convinced the child was his** [2].
- **The Subterfuge Theory:** Researchers note that if the child was Marianne's, she had good reason to construct an elaborate adoption story. She was married to an elderly, sick man and they had been childless for ten years; suddenly announcing a pregnancy would have invited obvious scandal [7]. The BRC suggests this was a pattern: Marianne later claimed an adopted baby as her own to ensnare [[henry-fisher|Henry Fisher]] into a bigamous marriage, and may have done the same to d'Arles at Borley.

> **Contradiction:** Documentary evidence (the birth certificate, the illegitimate Kent mother's record) points firmly to adoption, while Miss Dytor's professional medical testimony and the circumstantial evidence of the "M.E.R." initials point to biological maternity. The question has never been definitively resolved.

## Circumstances of Death

The baby lived for only about four and a half months [1]. The official cause of death was recorded as **convulsions and protein malnutrition (marasmus)** [2, 3]. He was buried in an unmarked grave in [[borley-church|Borley churchyard]] [2, 3].

> **Contradiction:** One record claimed he was buried on **12 September 1932**, while the official death certificate — reported by Marianne Foyster herself — states he died on **20 September 1932** [3].

Both Marianne and François d'Arles were exceptionally distressed by the infant's death [6]. Ian Greenwood witnessed d'Arles weeping and praying at the child's grave, and the pair made floral tributes to place upon it [2, 6].

## Omission and Photographic Evidence

Despite the intense scrutiny of the [[foyster-incumbency|Foyster incumbency]] by early psychical researchers, baby John Emery was **entirely omitted from Harry Price's foundational books** on the Borley haunting [3, 5]. A surviving photograph shows Marianne Foyster holding the baby on the lawn at Borley Rectory, described by the BRC as "a touching reminder of his presence in the Borley story" [8].

## Significance

The episode is primarily significant for what it reveals about the complex domestic arrangements during the Foyster incumbency — a rector's wife with a lover (d'Arles) living in the [[rectory-cottage|Rectory Cottage]], a nurse-companion employed who was misled about the child's origins, and a broader pattern of fabricated personal histories that [[robert-wood|Robert Wood]] documented in *The Widow of Borley*.
