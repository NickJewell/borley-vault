# John Evemond Emery

(Born 25 April 1932; died 20 September 1932) A newborn baby adopted by [[marianne-foyster]] who lived at [[borley-rectory]] briefly before dying at the age of four and a half months — one of the tragic stories of Borley.

## Background

John Emery was an illegitimate child born at 30 Swanage Road, Southend-on-Sea. His natural mother lived in Kent; the Southend address was the home of a midwife. He was given up immediately to a London adoption agency through which [[marianne-foyster]] adopted him and brought him to Borley in May 1932. At the same time Marianne employed a state-registered nurse, Miss Edith M. Dytor (see [[edith-wildgoose]]), as nursemaid and companion to help look after the boy.

## Circumstances of Death

John Emery died of protein malnutrition and convulsions and was buried in an unmarked grave in Borley churchyard.

## The Paternity Question

Due to statements made by [[edith-wildgoose|Mrs Wildgoose]] to [[trevor-hall]], mystery grew around the child. Mrs Wildgoose believed — based on information supplied by both Marianne and [[lionel-foyster]] — that the child was Marianne's own, supported by her professional opinion as an SRN that the rector's wife had recently given birth. This led to the suggestion that the child was actually the result of Marianne's relationship with [[francois-darles|Francois d'Arles]].

[[ian-greenwood]] visited Borley towards the end of 1932 and told Hall that d'Arles was convinced the child was his and had witnessed d'Arles praying at the baby's grave.

However, the documentary evidence points firmly to adoption: John Emery's birth records show an illegitimate child given up by a Kent woman. The BRC suggests this scenario is a pattern: Marianne later claimed an adopted baby as her own to ensnare [[henry-fisher]] into a bigamous marriage. She may have done the same to d'Arles at Borley.

A photograph exists of Marianne holding baby John Emery on the lawn at Borley Rectory, described by the BRC as "a touching reminder of his presence in the Borley story."

## Significance

The episode is primarily significant for what it reveals about the complex domestic arrangements during the [[foyster-incumbency]] — a rector's wife with a lover (d'Arles) living in the [[rectory-cottage]], a nurse-companion employed who was misled about the child's origins, and a broader pattern of fabricated personal histories that [[robert-wood]] documented in *The Widow of Borley*.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**John Evemond Emery** (April 25, 1932 – September 20, 1932) was an infant who briefly lived at Borley Rectory during the incumbency of the Rev. Lionel Foyster and his wife, Marianne. His short life and tragic death form one of the darker, non-paranormal subplots of the Borley saga, surrounded by intense speculation regarding his true parentage.

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, contradictions, and controversies:

**Birth and Official Identity**
Official records indicate that the child was born on **April 25, 1932**, at 30 Swanage Road, Southend-on-Sea, Essex, an address belonging to a midwife [1-3]. He was an illegitimate child; the space on his birth certificate for the father’s name was left blank, and the mother's address was recorded as being in Kent [3]. 

Marianne Foyster brought the baby to Borley Rectory in **May 1932**, stating that she had adopted him from a London agency [2, 3]. To help care for the child, she advertised in the *Nursing Mirror* and hired a State Registered Nurse, Miss Edith M. Dytor (later Mrs. Wildgoose), to serve as a nursemaid and companion [2, 4].

**The Parentage Contradiction and François d'Arles**
A major historical contradiction exists regarding whether John Emery was truly adopted or if he was, in fact, Marianne Foyster’s biological child:
*   **The Nurse's Testimony:** Miss Dytor stated that it was her **professional medical opinion that Marianne had recently given birth** [2, 3, 5, 6].
*   **The Birth Certificate Anomaly:** When skeptical investigator Trevor Hall later tracked down the birth certificate, he discovered the child was registered simply as "John Emery" (without the middle name "Evemond") [3]. More strikingly, while the mother's surname was not recorded as Foyster, **her initials were exactly "M.E.R."—identical to those of Marianne** [3].
*   **The Suspected Father:** It was strongly rumored that the baby was the result of a sexual liaison between Marianne and François d'Arles (Frank Pearless), who was living at the Rectory Cottage at the time [2, 6]. Ian Greenwood, Marianne's son from a previous marriage who visited Borley in late 1932, reported that **d'Arles was utterly convinced the child was his** [2].
*   **The Subterfuge Theory:** Researchers note that if the child *was* Marianne's, she had good reason to construct an elaborate adoption subterfuge. She was married to an elderly, sick man, and they had been childless for ten years; suddenly announcing a pregnancy would have invited obvious scandal and suspicion from friends and relatives [7].

**Death, Burial, and Date Contradictions**
Tragically, the baby lived for only about four and a half months [1]. The official cause of death was recorded as **convulsions and protein malnutrition (marasmus)** [2, 3]. 

A specific contradiction exists in the Borley records regarding the timeline of his death:
*   One record claimed he was buried on **September 12, 1932** [3].
*   However, the official death certificate, which was reported by Marianne Foyster herself, definitively states that he died on **September 20, 1932** [3]. 

Both Marianne and François d'Arles were exceptionally distressed by the infant's death [6]. Ian Greenwood witnessed d'Arles weeping and praying at the child's grave, and the pair used to make floral tributes to place upon it [2, 6]. The baby was buried in an **unmarked grave in Borley churchyard** [2, 3]. 

**Omission from Early Literature and Photographic Evidence**
Despite the intense scrutiny of the Foyster incumbency by early psychical researchers, baby John Emery was **entirely omitted from Harry Price's foundational books** on the Borley haunting [3, 5]. Today, one of the few tangible reminders of his brief presence in the story is a surviving photograph showing Marianne Foyster holding the baby on the lawn, with Borley Rectory in the background [8].
