Gardener (in some accounts described as chauffeur/manservant) to [[rev-ac-henning]] at Liston Rectory. He accompanied Henning on several visits to [[borley-rectory]] during Price's tenancy and witnessed phenomena both inside the house and outside the ruins after the 1939 fire.

## Phenomena Witnessed

**The strange coat (June 1, 1937)**: Mayes went through the Rectory on June 1, 1937 — searching behind all doors — and confirmed the "strange coat" was not there. [[harry-price]] and Ellic Howe found it the following day, June 2. Therefore the coat appeared between the afternoon of June 1 and that of June 2. This is one of the few disappearances/appearances with a tight documented time window.

**The strange light (1937–38)**: Mayes saw the mysterious light in an upper Rectory window on November 8, 1937, when the place was shut and unoccupied. His account is cited in Appendix C, where he and Henning also described hearing "furniture being thrown about" from inside the locked empty Rectory.

**Phantom horses post-fire (March 16, 1939)**: Two weeks after the fire, cycling home from Borley at night, Mayes heard what sounded like horses stampeding toward him. He jumped off his bicycle and "stood flat against the fence and hedge in order to allow the horses to pass him." The sounds came nearer, "finally passed him." No horses. He heard the galloping hoofs dying away in the distance for 3–4 minutes total. The road outside the Rectory was known to sound hollow — Edward Cooper had speculated a tunnel ran beneath it — and the phenomenon of phantom hoofbeats on that lane recurs through multiple witnesses ([[harry-bull]], [[walter-bull]], [[edward-cooper]]).

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Herbert Mayes (d. 1951)** was a local resident of Borley Green who was employed as a casual gardener by the Rev. Lionel Foyster, and subsequently worked as a handyman and chauffeur-gardener for the Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning [1, 2]. Described by a contemporary as a "very delicate man," he was a quintessential countryman who worked in the fields with his peers [2, 3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, events, quotes, and specific involvement in the case:

**The Foyster Incumbency and Marianne's "Designs"**
During the 1930s incumbency of the Rev. Lionel Foyster, Mayes performed casual gardening work at the Rectory [2]. A lesser-known biographical detail recorded by village contemporaries is that the vivacious, much younger **Marianne Foyster apparently "had designs" on Mayes**, though it remains a matter of conjecture how the delicate gardener dealt with her overtures [2]. 

When Marianne was away from the Rectory, Mayes was occasionally tasked with looking after the ailing, arthritis-stricken Rector [4]. During these times, Mayes experienced severe auditory phenomena in the house:
*   **Footsteps:** While standing alone in the hall, Mayes heard footsteps on the upstairs landing twice within a 45-minute period, but upon investigation, found no one there [5].
*   **The Crashing Crockery:** On two occasions, Mayes heard a terrific crash from the top of the stairs [4, 5]. He provided the specific quote: **"It was just as if someone with a tray full of crockery had dropped it and smashed the lot"** [5]. Despite searching the house, he found nothing damaged and no one about [4, 5]. *(Note: Skeptics later suggested that the young children, Adelaide Foyster and François d'Arles Jr., might have been playing tricks on him during Marianne's absences [4]).*

**The Price Tenancy and the "Strange Coats"**
When Harry Price rented the empty Rectory for his year-long investigation in May 1937, Rev. Henning offered Mayes's services to assist Price in any way necessary [6]. 

Mayes inadvertently played a key role in the mystery of the "strange coat" apport. On June 1, 1937, the day before Price and Ellic Howe arrived to set up their Base Room, Mayes went all over the Rectory, looking behind all the doors [7, 8]. He discovered an "old tattered jacket" behind the door of Room No. 7, which he burned on a bonfire, and another coat which he removed and sent to a jumble sale [7, 9]. Crucially, Mayes confirmed that the famous dirty, blue serge coat that Price and Howe subsequently found hanging in the Blue Room on June 2 was *not* in the house during his June 1 sweep, suggesting it had paranormalally "appeared" overnight [8].

**The Window Lights (November 1937)**
On November 8 (or 9), 1937, Mayes witnessed a bright, unaccountable light shining in one of the windows of the locked and unoccupied Rectory [1, 10, 11]. He observed this anomaly alongside Mr. and Mrs. Payne of Borley Place [4, 10].

**The Phantom Horses (March 16, 1939)**
Mayes's most famous experience occurred just weeks after the Rectory had been gutted by fire. At about 9:00 p.m. on March 16, 1939, Mayes was cycling home in the dark past the blackened ruins when he heard a terrific clatter of horses' hooves galloping down the lane towards him [12-14]. 

Thinking that a local farmer, Mr. Barnes, had loose horses stampeding in the narrow lane, Mayes jumped off his bicycle and pushed himself flat against the hedge to avoid being trampled [3, 15, 16]. He recounted the terrifying experience with the following specific quote:
> **"The horses got closer and closer, about four I should say all at different trots. I couldn't see any so I swung my bike round into the road but nothing passed me, nothing I could see, only the noise of the hooves and they gradually faded away down the hill. I can tell you I was scared"** [3, 15].

Rev. Henning noted that Mayes was initially very reluctant to discuss the incident, fearing that the villagers would laugh at him [17]. However, Mayes stuck firmly to his story [17, 18].

**Interviews and B.B.C. Broadcast**
Harry Price officially interviewed Mayes about his experiences on March 30, 1939 [11, 19]. In May 1947, Mayes was interviewed by investigator Peter Underwood, who found him to be a highly impressive witness who firmly believed in what he had experienced and could not be shaken from his story [20, 21]. 

Because of his steadfast testimony, Mayes was selected to participate in a B.B.C. Home Service radio broadcast entitled *The Haunted Rectory*, which aired on June 29, 1947, during which he personally recounted the tale of the phantom horses to the nation [15, 21-23].
