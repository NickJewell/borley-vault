# Herbert Mayes (d. 1951)

Local resident of Borley Green; employed initially as a casual gardener by [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]] during the Foyster incumbency, and subsequently as handyman and chauffeur-gardener to [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. A. C. Henning]] at Liston Rectory. Described by contemporaries as a "very delicate man" and a quintessential countryman who worked in the fields with his peers [2, 3]. He accompanied Henning on several visits to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] during Price's tenancy and witnessed phenomena both inside the house and outside the ruins after the 1939 fire.

## The Foyster Incumbency

During the 1930s incumbency of [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]], Mayes performed casual gardening work at the Rectory [2]. A detail recorded by village contemporaries is that the vivacious, much younger [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] apparently "had designs" on Mayes [2]. When Marianne was away from the Rectory, Mayes was occasionally tasked with looking after the ailing, arthritis-stricken Rector [4]. During these times, Mayes experienced severe auditory phenomena:

- **Footsteps:** While standing alone in the hall, Mayes heard footsteps on the upstairs landing twice within a 45-minute period, but upon investigation found no one there [5].
- **The crashing crockery:** On two occasions, Mayes heard a terrific crash from the top of the stairs [4, 5]. He provided the specific quote: **"It was just as if someone with a tray full of crockery had dropped it and smashed the lot"** [5]. Despite searching the house, he found nothing damaged and no one about. Sceptics later suggested that the young children, [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide Foyster]] and François d'Arles Jr., might have been playing tricks on him during Marianne's absences [4].

## The Price Tenancy and the "Strange Coat"

When [[harry-price|Harry Price]] rented the empty Rectory for his year-long investigation in May 1937, [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Henning]] offered Mayes's services to assist Price [6].

Mayes inadvertently played a key role in the mystery of the "strange coat" apport. On **June 1, 1937** — the day before Price and Ellic Howe arrived to set up their Base Room — Mayes went all over the Rectory, looking behind all the doors [7, 8]. He discovered an "old tattered jacket" behind the door of Room No. 7, which he burned on a bonfire, and another coat which he removed and sent to a jumble sale [7, 9]. Crucially, Mayes confirmed that the famous dirty, blue serge coat that Price and Howe subsequently found hanging in the Blue Room on **June 2** was *not* in the house during his June 1 sweep, suggesting it had appeared overnight [8]. This is one of the few disappearances/appearances with a tight documented time window.

## The Window Light (November 1937)

On **November 8 (or 9), 1937**, Mayes witnessed a bright, unaccountable light shining in one of the windows of the locked and unoccupied Rectory [1, 10, 11]. He observed this anomaly alongside Mr. and Mrs. Payne of Borley Place [4, 10]. His account is cited in Appendix C, where he and Henning also described hearing "furniture being thrown about" from inside the locked empty Rectory.

## Phantom Horses (March 16, 1939)

Mayes's most celebrated experience occurred just weeks after the Rectory had been gutted by fire. At about 9:00 p.m. on **March 16, 1939**, cycling home in the dark past the blackened ruins, he heard a terrific clatter of horses' hooves galloping down the lane towards him [12–14]. Thinking that a local farmer had loose horses stampeding in the narrow lane, he jumped off his bicycle and pushed himself flat against the hedge [3, 15, 16]. He recounted the experience with the following specific quote:

> **"The horses got closer and closer, about four I should say all at different trots. I couldn't see any so I swung my bike round into the road but nothing passed me, nothing I could see, only the noise of the hooves and they gradually faded away down the hill. I can tell you I was scared."** [3, 15]

Rev. Henning noted that Mayes was initially very reluctant to discuss the incident, fearing that the villagers would laugh at him [17]. The road outside the Rectory was known to sound hollow — [[edward-cooper|Edward Cooper]] had speculated a tunnel ran beneath it — and the phenomenon of phantom hoofbeats on that lane recurs through multiple witnesses ([[harry-bull]], [[walter-bull]], [[edward-cooper]]).

## Later Interviews and BBC Broadcast

[[harry-price|Harry Price]] officially interviewed Mayes about his experiences on March 30, 1939 [11, 19]. In May 1947, [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] interviewed him and found him a highly impressive witness who firmly believed in what he had experienced and could not be shaken from his story [20, 21].

Because of his steadfast testimony, Mayes was selected to participate in a BBC Home Service radio broadcast entitled *The Haunted Rectory*, which aired on **June 29, 1947**, during which he personally recounted the tale of the phantom horses to the nation [15, 21–23].
