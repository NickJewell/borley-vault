Helen Glanville (later **Helen Carter**) is the only daughter of [[sidney-glanville]] (Price's chief official observer). A 27-year-old woman at the time of the investigation, she had never used a Planchette before her father experimented with it in October 1937. She is the central figure in the two most dramatic events in the MHH record: the naming of "Mary Lairre" and the "[[sunex-amures]]" fire prediction.

## Biographical Details

Helen lived at the Glanville family home in Streatham, South London, and later resided at Ivy Cottage in Fittleworth and in Pulborough, West Sussex [2, 4, 5]. She married George Carter, a man who notably had zero interest in the Borley case and had never read [[harry-price|Harry Price's]] books [2]. A lesser-known detail is that she was a personal friend of Arthur H. Foyster's daughter [4]. Her mother, by contrast, "always disliked Borley and everything to do with it" [4] — a striking counterpoint to the enthusiasm that drove the rest of the family's involvement.

## The Planchette Sessions

**Session 1 — alone at Streatham, October 28, 1937**:

Helen was shown the earlier Borley Planchette scripts but given *no information about their content*. Alone at home in Streatham (not at [[borley-rectory]]), she produced a series of striking communications. Without a question being asked, the very first word the planchette wrote was reportedly **"Marianne"** — the name that had frequently appeared in the mysterious [[wall-writings]] at Borley [3, 9]. The entity then identified herself as a nun named **"Lairre"** from a convent in Le Havre [7, 10, 11], and the session produced:

- The name "Lairre" (confirmed on follow-up: "Mary Lairre")
- "Are you buried in the garden?" → Yes
- "When did you pass over?" → *1667*, month *May*, day *17*
- "Did they hurt you?" → Yes
- "What did you want before you passed over?" → *Water*
- "Was the monk's order that of St. Benedict?" → Yes

The entity also gave investigators a directive to search for her remains in the **"well in the cellar"** that had been **"filled in"** [7, 12, 13] — a clue that directly inspired [[harry-price|Harry Price's]] 1943 cellars excavations (see [[bones-excavation]]).

**Session 2 — with family and Kerr-Pearse, October 31, 1937**:

- Name confirmed: *Mary Lairre*
- Age at death: *19*; Status: *novice*
- "Were you murdered?" → Yes; "How?" → *Stran...* (strangled)
- "Whose fault?" → *[[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave]]*
- "Are you French?" → Yes; "Where from?" → *Havre*
- Nunnery: *Bure* ([[bures-en-bray|Bures]])
- Requests: Requiem Mass (by Roman Catholic priest), burial, holy water, incense

On **December 12 and 19, 1937**, further sessions with her brother [[roger-glanville]] produced communications from an entity named "Henry", who confirmed that bones belonging to "Lairre" would be found under the cellar floor [19–21].

**Blind Test — January 8, 1938**:

To test the planchette's validity, Helen and Squadron-Leader [[alan-cuthbert]] acted as sitters while Roger Glanville silently wrote questions on paper so the operators had no idea what was being asked. During this blind test, an entity identifying itself as "Jeanne Waldegrave" communicated with them [21, 22].

**Session 3 — "[[sunex-amures]]," March 27, 1938** (with brother Roger only):

Five months after the October sessions, something prompted Helen to get out the Planchette. She asked "Does anyone want to speak?" — immediately "yes." "Who are you?" — the Planchette wrote in a large scrawly hand:

> "Sunex Amures and one of his men MEAN TO BURN THE RECTORY (o-night at 9 o'clock end of the haunting go to the rectory and you will be able to see us enter into our own and under the ruins you will find bone of murdered wardens under the ruins..."

"In which room will the fire start?" → **"Over the hall."**

The script was sent to Price's office immediately and preserved. Eleven months later, on February 27, 1939 at midnight, [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] burned — the [[fire-1939|fire]] starting over the hall.

## Significance and Contested Evidence

Helen's primary contribution to the Borley investigation occurred not at the Rectory itself but at her home in Streatham. Price regards the Sunex Amures prediction — sent to him in writing before the [[fire-1939|fire]] — as the strongest single piece of evidence for something genuinely supernormal, and the only advance-documented fulfilled prediction in the Borley record. Her solo session produced: a French name ("Lairre"), a date of death (May 17, 1667), a cause (strangulation), a perpetrator ([[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave]]), an origin (Havre), and a nunnery ([[bures-en-bray|Bures]]) — all without, Price and [[sidney-glanville|Glanville]] argued, prior access to relevant information.

The highly skeptical authors of the 1956 *[[the-borley-report|Borley Report]]* ([[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], and [[trevor-hall]]) challenged this assessment heavily. They argued that the messages originated entirely in the subconscious mind of the operator and pointed out a logical flaw: because the scripts consisted mostly of direct answers to questions rather than spontaneous statements, they argued that Miss Glanville **"must have known a good deal about Borley which enabled her to ask so many detailed questions"** [23, 24]. The competing-theories question — genuine psychic communication, unconscious knowledge, or motivated production — remains unresolved.

## Later Views

Helen attended [[harry-price|Harry Price's]] lantern slide lecture at the [[ghost-club]] on **April 18, 1939** [25]. Years later, following her father's death and the publication of the highly critical *[[the-borley-report|Borley Report]]*, she wrote to investigator [[peter-underwood]] on **February 19, 1959** [4], defending both Price and her father's work:

> "I hated the whole thing and thought it a disgraceful and underhand attack on Price, the entire book was nothing but criticism taken as truth, except Price's. I can't remember now if my father read the complete book or not, but I know that the part he saw changed very little his conviction that some very strange and unexplained things happened at Borley." [4]
