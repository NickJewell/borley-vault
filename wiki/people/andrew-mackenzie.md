# Andrew MacKenzie

SPR member who contributed a critical assessment of Harry Price's methodology at Borley Rectory to Ivan Banks's *The Enigma of Borley Rectory* (Chapter 24). MacKenzie had extensive experience investigating spontaneous haunting cases and took a more sceptical position than [[dr-vernon-harrison|Dr. Vernon Harrison]] or [[guy-lyon-playfair|Guy Lyon Playfair]].

## Main Criticism: Pre-Suggestion

MacKenzie's central objection concerns the [[blue-book-of-instructions|Blue Book of Instructions]] that Price issued to his team of observers:

> "Mr. Price issued an invitation to investigators to come to the Rectory and report their findings to him. I have in front of me as I write a copy of his Instructions for Observers with, under the phrase, 'Possible phenomena which may be experienced,' a list of such phenomena as bell-ringing, movement of objects, footsteps, forms or apparitions, raps or knocks, perfumes, lights, apports, disappearances, and thermal variations."

MacKenzie argues that such a list could create self-fulfilling expectation in "anyone of a nervous disposition," causing them to attribute normal events to ghosts. He would have preferred Price to ask observers simply to "report anything of an unusual nature."

He quotes Dr. D. J. West's *Psychical Research Today* (1954): "Once a place has achieved a really good reputation for haunting, more and more susceptible persons will see things there, and odd events and coincidences that would in the normal way pass unnoticed will be attributed to ghosts."

MacKenzie also cites Professor F. J. M. Stratton (President of the SPR 1953–55), who deliberately withheld publication of alleged hauntings at Abbey House, Cambridge, to prevent contaminating witness testimony.

## Banks's Rejoinder

Banks respectfully disagrees: if observers were given no prior information, they would ask "What exactly are we supposed to be looking for?" He also argues that Price's observers were not gullible — men like [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] were far from unquestioning. And he reiterates that the Borley evidence does not depend solely on the observer year; the case stretches from 1863 to 1972.

## Significance

MacKenzie represents the mainstream SPR sceptical position regarding investigator methodology — the concern that Price's approach was more likely to produce confirmatory bias than rigorous data. His critique has never been fully answered, but Banks argues it is an insufficient basis on which to dismiss the entire body of evidence.
