# Andrew MacKenzie

Experienced psychical investigator and SPR member who contributed a critical assessment of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s methodology at Borley Rectory to Ivan Banks's *The Enigma of Borley Rectory* (Chapter 24) [1, 2]. Having personally carried out investigations into "literally hundreds of spontaneous cases, including many of hauntings," MacKenzie stood firmly in the camp of researchers who opposed Price's methodology [2]. MacKenzie had extensive experience investigating spontaneous haunting cases and took a more sceptical position than [[dr-vernon-harrison|Dr. Vernon Harrison]] or [[guy-lyon-playfair|Guy Lyon Playfair]].

## Main Criticism: Pre-Suggestion

MacKenzie believed that Price's books on the haunting were "very misleading" and bluntly stated that the Borley Rectory case "provides an example of how an investigation should not be carried out" [2, 3]. His central objection concerns the [[blue-book-of-instructions|Blue Book of Instructions]] that Price issued to his team of observers:

> "Mr. Price issued an invitation to investigators to come to the Rectory and report their findings to him. I have in front of me as I write a copy of his Instructions for Observers with, under the phrase, 'Possible phenomena which may be experienced,' a list of such phenomena as bell-ringing, movement of objects, footsteps, forms or apparitions, raps or knocks, perfumes, lights, apports, disappearances, and thermal variations."

MacKenzie argues that such a list could create self-fulfilling expectation in "anyone of a nervous disposition," causing them to attribute normal events to ghosts [2]. He would have preferred Price to ask observers simply to "report anything of an unusual nature."

He quotes Dr. D. J. West's *Psychical Research Today* (1954): "Once a place has achieved a really good reputation for haunting, more and more susceptible persons will see things there, and odd events and coincidences that would in the normal way pass unnoticed will be attributed to ghosts."

## The Abbey House Comparison

To highlight Price's alleged mishandling of the case, MacKenzie contrasted Price's highly publicised methods with the investigation of Abbey House in Cambridge [4]. That case was overseen by Professor F. J. M. Stratton, a noted astronomer and SPR President (1953–55) [4]. For forty years, Stratton deliberately withheld stories of the alleged hauntings at Abbey House from publication because he "believed that publication of the stories… might contaminate the testimony of possible future witnesses" [4]. Because of this strict confidentiality, the first reliable report on Abbey House was not published until 1972 [4]. MacKenzie lamented that "it is a great pity that Harry Price did not observe Professor Stratton's reticence while the haunting of Borley Rectory, if such it was, was in progress" [4].

## Banks's Rejoinder

Banks respectfully disagrees on several fronts [5]:

- **On instructions:** Banks conceded that MacKenzie's point was "fair enough," but argued that if observers were given no prior information, they would be left directionless — wondering what exactly they were supposed to be looking for. Withholding all specific information could have hampered the investigation just as much as providing too much detail [5].
- **On observer objectivity:** Banks felt MacKenzie was unfairly misjudging the potential objectivity of Price's team. Men like [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] were intelligent, critical individuals, far from gullible or unquestioning [5].
- **On scope of criticism:** Banks challenged MacKenzie's assertion that Price's books were the problem, reminding readers of [[robert-hastings|Robert Hastings]]'s conclusion that the true foundation of the Borley hauntings rested on Price's raw, collected files — rather than his published and sometimes imperfectly edited books [3, 5]. Banks also argued that the Borley evidence does not depend solely on the observer year; the case stretches from 1863 to 1972.

## Significance

MacKenzie represents the mainstream SPR sceptical position regarding investigator methodology — the concern that Price's approach was more likely to produce confirmatory bias than rigorous data. His critique has never been fully answered, but Banks argues it is an insufficient basis on which to dismiss the entire body of evidence.
