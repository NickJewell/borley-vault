# John Osborne Harley

(Also recorded variously as James Osborne Harley, John Meighen Osborne Harley, or simply J. Harley [1–4]) Local resident of Cavendish, Suffolk, within a few miles of Borley. Came from a family friendly with the Bulls. In **1922**, aged fifteen and convalescing from whooping cough, he was sent to board at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] for several weeks to receive Latin lessons from [[harry-bull|Reverend Harry Bull]] [3, 4]. He was interviewed by [[peter-underwood|Underwood]] at his home in Cavendish in September 1952.

## Ghost-Watching Sessions

Harley frequently joined the rector in interminable ghost-watching sessions inside the large summer-house facing the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] or in the little Gothic summer-house at the bottom of the rose garden — treating these vigils as a welcome excuse to avoid his Latin grammar lessons [1, 3, 4]. He observed that the atmosphere was so psychically charged that "every innocent inflection of summer's lovely voice would assume paranormal significance, and such normal sounds as distant thunder, the sound of a cart in the nearby farm, or a sudden breeze would bring us to our feet in anticipation."

Harry Bull never appeared disappointed when sessions were sterile; they were rituals to him. He obtained "considerable satisfaction from his 'attunement with the spirits.'" Harley summarised the rector's casual relationship with the supernatural: **"He could hail a spectre as easily as I can hail a friend"** [6]. He observed that Bull "appeared to be much happier in the presence of the purported ghosts than he was in the company of his parishioners. They bored him, or most of them did, whereas the ghosts stimulated him" [5].

Harley also vividly remembered the rector's intense love for animals — he never remembered Harry Bull without thinking of his cats [1]. Bull kept dozens of them; Harley once counted thirty, and the rector knew all of them by name, allowing them to follow him around the grounds like the Pied Piper [7].

## The Sensation of Being Watched

Inside the Rectory, Harley always felt "he was being watched... not by anybody or anything hostile but rather by somebody or some thing who had a more than cursory interest in his movements." A sensation he never experienced anywhere else and found impossible to explain. "It was not at all frightening." He also noted that his family occasionally visited the Rectory for tea but strictly avoided evening visits because **"Strange things happen at the Rectory at night"** [1].

## The Bell Rings Twice

During his 1922 stay, Harley was awakened at about 2:00 a.m. by a violent, loud peal from the large courtyard bell. Harry Bull rushed out of his bedroom carrying a candle and wearing his favourite plum-coloured smoking jacket in a highly distressed state, fearing the bell foretold a family misfortune [9, 10]. When Harley suggested a servant might have rung it, Bull dismissed the idea: **"Who on earth would come and ring the courtyard bell like that at this time of night? No, it's the nun, she does sometimes."** [9] Bull then swore the young Harley to secrecy: "Oh good boy, good boy, I'm sure I can trust you. I don't suppose it will happen again . . ." — whereupon it rang again, "Even louder, it seemed, and more violent than on the first occasion." Bull: "I've never known it ring twice in quick succession like that before." The servants, whose bedrooms were nearer the courtyard, must have heard it, but said nothing the next day.

## The "Mothball" Promise

Harley is highly significant in the Borley literature because he recorded a specific post-mortem promise made by Harry Bull. While putting away textbooks one evening, Bull told Harley that a spirit could only get a living person's attention through a violent physical reaction. Bull promised that if he were discontented after his own death, he would not use standard phenomena to communicate: **"no footsteps or bell-ringing and anything like that... I'll make my presence felt in some really different way — I know, I'll throw mothballs about! How about that? Mothballs, then you'll know it's me!"** [7] During the later investigations by Harry Price, showers of mothballs were indeed inexplicably thrown inside the Rectory.

## After Borley

**15 June 1929** — When the *Daily Mirror* articles appeared about the Borley hauntings, Harley (then living at Nottingham Place, London) wrote to the press recounting his 1922 experiences [12, 13].

> **Contradiction:** Some sources state this letter was published in the *Daily Mirror* [12], while others state it appeared in the *Daily Mail* [11].

Harry Price cited Harley's letter as vital independent testimony corroborating the Misses Bull's claims regarding their brother's beliefs [13].

**September 1952** — Harley was officially interviewed by Underwood and Dr. Peter Hilton-Rowe at his home in Cavendish [2, 3]. He shared a humorous anecdote about Harry Bull's lifelong paranormal curiosity: upon hearing that "odd things" were occurring at [[pentlow|Pentlow]] Rectory, Bull **"borrowed the family coach and drove over to Felix's"** — only to find the resident out and the cook dismissively stating: **"Well, Master Harry, if there's any foolishness going on, it's all along of our new housemaid, that's what I say!"** [6]
