# John Osborne Harley

James Osborne Harley of Cavendish (Suffolk), within a few miles of Borley. Came from a family friendly with the Bulls. Boarded at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in 1922 for Latin tuition under [[harry-bull|Reverend Harry Bull]], aged 15. Interviewed by Underwood at his home in Cavendish in September 1952.

## Ghost-Watching Sessions

Harley joined Harry Bull on long hours of watching in the large summer-house facing the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] or in the little Gothic summer-house at the bottom of the rose garden — sessions so psychic in atmosphere that "every innocent inflection of summer's lovely voice would assume paranormal significance, and such normal sounds as distant thunder, the sound of a cart in the nearby farm, or a sudden breeze would bring us to our feet in anticipation."

Harry Bull never appeared disappointed when sessions were sterile; they were rituals to him. He obtained "considerable satisfaction from his 'attunement with the spirits.'"

## The Sensation of Being Watched

Inside the Rectory, Harley always felt "he was being watched... not by anybody or anything hostile but rather by somebody or some thing who had a more than cursory interest in his movements." A sensation he never experienced anywhere else and found impossible to explain. "It was not at all frightening."

## The Bell Rings Twice

At about 2 a.m., the large courtyard bell rang violently. Harry Bull burst into Harley's bedroom, trembling and muttering:

> "Did you hear it? Did you hear it? If you did, for goodness sake, don't tell anybody. I hate this sort of thing being talked about. Promise me you won't tell anybody."

When Harley promised, Bull calmed: "Oh good boy, good boy, I'm sure I can trust you. I don't suppose it will happen again . . ." Whereupon it rang again — "Even louder, it seemed, and more violent than on the first occasion." Bull: "I've never known it ring twice in quick succession like that before." Then: "No, it's the nun, she does sometimes." The servants, whose bedrooms were nearer the courtyard, must have heard it, but said nothing the next day.

## Harry Bull's Character

"A puckish, lovable man." He could "hail a spectre as easily as I can hail a friend." He always seemed much happier in the presence of the purported ghosts than in the company of his parishioners: "They bored him, or most of them did, whereas the ghosts stimulated him."

Harry Bull adored cats — Harley counted thirty on one occasion, but the rector knew each by name; they followed him about "putting one in mind of the Pied Piper." He had at least twenty most of the time he was at Borley.

## The Mothball Promise

Harry Bull told Harley that if after his death he was "dissatisfied with his successor, he would not use footsteps or bell-ringing and anything like that... I'll make my presence felt in some really different way — I know, I'll throw mothballs about! How about that? Mothballs, then you'll know it's me!"

Years later, Harley read in a newspaper that in June 1929, investigators arriving at the deserted Rectory were met by "a shower of mothballs." His comment: "Well, dear old Harry Bull always was a man of his word."

(The same promise is recorded in the BBC 1947 broadcast in Underwood's lecture [bps-01]; see also [hbrs-ch05].)

## After Borley

When the *Daily Mirror* articles appeared in June 1929, Osborne Harley wrote to the paper about his days with Harry Bull. He interpreted the thrown mothball as Harry Bull fulfilling his promise.
