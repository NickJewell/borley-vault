# John Osborne Harley

James Osborne Harley of Cavendish (Suffolk), within a few miles of Borley. Came from a family friendly with the Bulls. Boarded at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in 1922 for Latin tuition under [[harry-bull|Reverend Harry Bull]], aged 15. Interviewed by Underwood at his home in Cavendish in September 1952.

## Ghost-Watching Sessions

Harley joined Harry Bull on long hours of watching in the large summer-house facing the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] or in the little Gothic summer-house at the bottom of the rose garden — sessions so psychic in atmosphere that "every innocent inflection of summer's lovely voice would assume paranormal significance, and such normal sounds as distant thunder, the sound of a cart in the nearby farm, or a sudden breeze would bring us to our feet in anticipation."

Harry Bull never appeared disappointed when sessions were sterile; they were rituals to him. He obtained "considerable satisfaction from his 'attunement with the spirits.'"

## The Sensation of Being Watched

Inside the Rectory, Harley always felt "he was being watched... not by anybody or anything hostile but rather by somebody or some thing who had a more than cursory interest in his movements." A sensation he never experienced anywhere else and found impossible to explain. "It was not at all frightening."

## The Bell Rings Twice

At about 2 a.m., the large courtyard bell rang violently. Harry Bull burst into Harley's bedroom, trembling and muttering:

> "Did you hear it? Did you hear it? If you did, for goodness sake, don't tell anybody. I hate this sort of thing being talked about. Promise me you won't tell anybody."

When Harley promised, Bull calmed: "Oh good boy, good boy, I'm sure I can trust you. I don't suppose it will happen again . . ." Whereupon it rang again — "Even louder, it seemed, and more violent than on the first occasion." Bull: "I've never known it ring twice in quick succession like that before." Then: "No, it's the nun, she does sometimes." The servants, whose bedrooms were nearer the courtyard, must have heard it, but said nothing the next day.

## Harry Bull's Character

"A puckish, lovable man." He could "hail a spectre as easily as I can hail a friend." He always seemed much happier in the presence of the purported ghosts than in the company of his parishioners: "They bored him, or most of them did, whereas the ghosts stimulated him."

Harry Bull adored cats — Harley counted thirty on one occasion, but the rector knew each by name; they followed him about "putting one in mind of the Pied Piper." He had at least twenty most of the time he was at Borley.

## The Mothball Promise

Harry Bull told Harley that if after his death he was "dissatisfied with his successor, he would not use footsteps or bell-ringing and anything like that... I'll make my presence felt in some really different way — I know, I'll throw mothballs about! How about that? Mothballs, then you'll know it's me!"

Years later, Harley read in a newspaper that in June 1929, investigators arriving at the deserted Rectory were met by "a shower of mothballs." His comment: "Well, dear old Harry Bull always was a man of his word."

(The same promise is recorded in the BBC 1947 broadcast in Underwood's lecture [bps-01]; see also [hbrs-ch05].)

## After Borley

When the *Daily Mirror* articles appeared in June 1929, Osborne Harley wrote to the paper about his days with Harry Bull. He interpreted the thrown mothball as Harry Bull fulfilling his promise.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**John Osborne Harley** (also recorded in the sources variously as **James Osborne Harley**, **John Meighen Osborne Harley**, or simply **J. Harley**) was a local resident of Cavendish who grew up a few miles from Borley Rectory and was a close friend of the Bull family [1-4]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following specific events, dates, quotes, and lesser-known biographical details regarding his involvement:

**The 1922 Visit and Evasion of Latin Lessons**
In **1922**, when Harley was a fifteen-year-old boy convalescing from an attack of whooping cough, he was sent to stay at Borley Rectory for several weeks to receive extended Latin lessons from the Rev. Harry Bull [3, 4]. Harley was already familiar with the local ghost stories and was more intrigued than frightened [4]. He frequently joined the rector in interminable ghost-watching sessions inside the large summer-house facing the "Nun's Walk" or the Gothic summer-house at the bottom of the garden, treating these vigils as a welcome excuse to avoid his Latin grammar lessons [1, 3, 4]. 

Harley observed that the rector **"appeared to be much happier in the presence of the purported ghosts than he was in the company of his parishioners. They bored him, or most of them did, whereas the ghosts stimulated him"** [5]. He summarized the rector's casual relationship with the supernatural by stating: **"He could hail a spectre as easily as I can hail a friend"** [6].

Harley also vividly remembered the rector's intense love for animals, noting that he never remembered Harry Bull without thinking of his cats [1]. Bull kept dozens of them—Harley once counted thirty—and the rector knew all of them by name, allowing them to follow him around the grounds like the Pied Piper [7].

**The Late-Night Bell Incident**
Harley stated that he himself never saw the famous phantom nun, but he had no doubt the Rectory was haunted [8]. During his 1922 stay, he was awakened at about 2:00 a.m. by a violent, loud peal from the large bell in the courtyard [7, 9]. Harry Bull rushed out of his bedroom carrying a candle and wearing his favorite plum-colored smoking jacket (or dressing-gown) in a highly distressed state, fearing the bell foretold a family misfortune [9, 10]. When Harley suggested that Mrs. Bull or a servant might have rung it, the rector dismissed the idea, stating: **"Who on earth would come and ring the courtyard bell like that at this time of night? No, it's the nun, she does sometimes"** [9]. Bull then swore the young Harley to secrecy about the incident [9]. Harley also noted that his family occasionally visited the Rectory for tea but strictly avoided evening visits because **"Strange things happen at the Rectory at night"** [1].

**The "Mothball" Promise**
Harley is highly significant in the Borley literature because he recorded a specific post-mortem promise made by Harry Bull. While putting away textbooks one evening, Bull told Harley that a spirit could only get a living person's attention through a violent physical reaction, such as breaking glass [11-13]. Bull promised that if he were discontented after his own death, he would not use standard phenomena to communicate: **"no footsteps or bell-ringing and anything like that... I’ll make my presence felt in some really different way – I know, I’ll throw mothballs about! How about that? Mothballs, then you’ll know it’s me!"** [7]. During the later investigations by Harry Price, showers of mothballs were indeed inexplicably thrown inside the Rectory [10]. 

**The 1929 Letter to the Press**
Several years after Harry Bull's death, Harley—who was now grown up and living at Nottingham Place, London—read sensational newspaper reports about the Borley hauntings and the supposed return of the late rector's spirit [12, 13]. He subsequently wrote a letter to the press (dated **June 15, 1929**) recounting his 1922 experiences [13]. *(Note: The sources contain a minor contradiction regarding where this letter was published; some state he wrote to the Daily Mirror [12], while others state his letter appeared in the Daily Mail [11]).* Harry Price cited Harley's letter as vital independent testimony that corroborated the Misses Bull's claims regarding their brother's beliefs [13]. 

**Later Interviews and the Pentlow Anecdote**
Harley was officially interviewed about his experiences by the researchers Peter Underwood and Dr. Peter Hilton-Rowe at his home in Cavendish in **September 1952** [2, 3]. In addition to recounting his memories of the 1922 visit, Harley shared a humorous anecdote demonstrating Harry Bull's lifelong fascination with the paranormal. He recalled a time when they heard that "odd things" were occurring at Pentlow Rectory. Harry Bull immediately **"borrowed the family coach and drove over to Felix’s"**, only to find the resident out and the cook dismissively stating: **"Well, Master Harry, if there’s any foolishness going on, it’s all along of our new housemaid, that’s what I say!"** [6].
