# Colonel Westland

The Borley sources record this investigator variously as **Lieut.-Col. F. C. Westland** and **Colonel Arthur Westland** [1–3] — an official observer during [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s tenancy of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] (1937–38). He was an officer attached to the Royal Army Ordnance Corps, described as a shrewd observer, and also a highly skilled draftsman and photographer [3]. He and his wife, F. Westland, signed Harry Price's Haunted House Declaration Form on 7 June 1937, becoming early official observers during Price's year-long rented tenancy [2].

## The Westland Floor Plans

During his time at Borley, Colonel Westland took careful measurements of the rooms and drafted detailed floor plans in ink on linen, dated 23 July 1937 [1, 4]. The sources note these drawings were "by far the most accurate floor plans produced of Borley Rectory," featuring correct proportions and including details missing from the plans drawn by [[sidney-glanville]] [1].

However, Harry Price deliberately chose not to publish Westland's superior plans in his books. Price instead used Glanville's drawings as a "political gesture" to avoid offending Glanville (his "Chief Investigator"), because Westland was only involved in the early stages of the investigation [1]. Westland's plans survive unpublished in the University of London Library [1].

## Key Investigations and Discoveries (June–July 1937)

Westland was known for submitting meticulous, minute-by-minute logs recording everything from weather conditions to precise times of events [5].

**The Blue Box:** During their first overnight vigil on 25–26 June 1937, the Westlands discovered a flat, 3-inch diameter blue box containing pins on a lower shelf in a ground-floor passage cupboard [6]. During their second visit (7–9 July), they found the box had moved half an inch out of the chalked circle they had drawn around it — one of the more controlled instances of unexplained object displacement reported by the observer programme [7].

**Wall Writings:** Westland spent an enormous amount of time discovering, tracing, and taking "perfect photographs" of the famous [[wall-writings|Borley wall pencilings]], including the "Marianne" messages [3, 8]. On 7 July, he noted the word "Adelaide" with a "4" and a "P" above it in the scullery [9, 10].

**The Piece of Wood:** On 9 July at 9:20 p.m., he found a 3x1x1-inch piece of wood on the kitchen passage stairs. Mrs. Westland had seen it earlier but had assumed it was a prop for her husband's photography [7].

**The Pet Terrier:** The Westlands brought their Aberdeen terrier with them on a visit. The dog behaved perfectly normally — a fact noted in contrast with the terrified reactions of [[captain-gregson|Captain Gregson]]'s dogs later on [4].

## Sceptical Explanations and Contradictions

The sources repeatedly use Westland's honest and logical reporting to challenge Price's paranormal claims.

**The Blue Coat:** Price made much of a "strange" blue coat that supposedly materialised as an apport in the Rectory. However, on 7 July, Westland found a blue serge lady's jacket hanging behind the door of Room 7 and recorded a highly logical explanation: the door opened against the wall, so the jacket was completely hidden from view unless someone stepped into the room and shut the door behind them [11, 12].

**The Missed Nail File:** On 29 June 1937, two RAF officers found a steel nail file in the same cupboard where Westland had found the blue pin-box [13, 14]. Critics note that Westland was scrupulously honest in his reports, frequently admitting that objects "appearing" (or doors found unlocked) were likely due to his own forgetfulness or an omission in his previous rounds, rather than a ghost [7, 12, 14].

> **Contradiction:** The chaotic nature of tracking the wall writings is illustrated by the fact that the "Adelaide" inscription Westland carefully noted on 7 July was completely missed by another diligent observer, [[mark-kerr-pearse]], during his summary of the walls on 13 July [10].

## Post-Fire Psychic Fête

Colonel and Mrs. Westland returned to the ruins of Borley Rectory on 21 June 1939 to attend the [[psychic-fete-1939|Psychic Fête]] organised to raise funds for the church [16, 17]. While exploring the gutted shell of the house, they were amazed to discover that the small blue pin-box was still sitting untouched inside its chalked ring in the very cupboard where they had originally found it two years prior. They took the box home with them as a souvenir [17].
