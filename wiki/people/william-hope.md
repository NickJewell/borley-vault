# William Hope

Crewe-based **spirit photographer** who had successfully managed to fool the British College of Psychic Science [1, 2]. His exposure as a fraud by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] in 1922 was one of the most significant events in Price's early career — and permanently fractured his relationship with Arthur Conan Doyle.

## Background

Hope practised spirit photography from Crewe, claiming to photograph auras and apparitions of the dead. Doubts about the authenticity of his photographs had circulated since **1909**, when a committee set up by the *Daily Mail* examined his spirit photographs and concluded they were entirely fake [2]. The [[spr|Society for Psychical Research]] had been trying to investigate Hope since before the First World War. They eventually appointed [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] to investigate him, who in turn approached [[harry-price|Harry Price]] to help prove that Hope was an "exploitative fraud" [2, 4].

Hope nonetheless attracted a devoted Spiritualist following, including Conan Doyle, who defended him vigorously.

## The Exposure

To trap Hope, Price consulted the **Imperial Dry Plate Company at Cricklewood**, which agreed to **invisibly mark a set of photographic plates with the company's trademark — a rampant lion** [4].

On **28 February**, Price attended a sitting with Hope in Holland Park and invited the medium to use these specially supplied plates [4]. Price watched Hope's movements very carefully in the semi-darkness. When the sitting was over, the developed plate showed an image of Price with **"a charming female form over his shoulders"** — apparently supposed to be Price's mother [4]. When subsequently developed by another photographer, it was clear that **the rampant-lion trademark was entirely missing**, proving beyond a doubt that Hope had secretly switched the plates during the sitting [4].

The exposure was published in the *Journal of the Society for Psychical Research* in May 1922, causing an absolute furore [3]. **Conan Doyle** immediately published a "vigorous defence" of Hope's work, dismissing the experiment as a direct attack on Spiritualist beliefs and claiming Price and Dingwall had "set Hope up" [3]. The exposure of Hope left relations between Price and Conan Doyle deeply strained, resulting in a fractured relationship that would fluctuate between comradeship and open hostility for the rest of Doyle's life (he died 1930) [3, 5]. The practice of spirit photography effectively passed into oblivion.

> **Contested:** Doyle responded that Price and Dingwall had "set Hope up" and that spiritualists' beliefs were being unfairly attacked.

## Significance for Borley

The Hope affair established Price's public reputation as a fraud-exposer and sceptic — the persona that the *Daily Mirror* and the Smiths were buying into when they called him to [[borley-rectory|Borley]] in 1929. It also permanently damaged his relationship with Doyle, removing the most powerful Spiritualist defender Price might have had when the Borley project came under attack in the 1950s.
