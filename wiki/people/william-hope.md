# William Hope

Crewe-based **spirit photographer** whose exposure as a fraud by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] in 1922 was one of the most significant events in Price's early career — and permanently fractured his relationship with [[sir-arthur-conan-doyle|Arthur Conan Doyle]].

## Background

Hope practised spirit photography from Crewe, claiming to photograph auras and apparitions of the dead. Doubts about the authenticity of his photographs had circulated since 1909, when the *Daily Mail* set up a committee to examine them and concluded they were fake. Hope nonetheless attracted a devoted spiritualist following, including Conan Doyle, who defended him vigorously.

## The Exposure

The Society for Psychical Research had been trying to investigate Hope for years. In 1922, [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] appointed himself and [[harry-price|Harry Price]] to conduct the sting. They consulted the **Imperial Dry Plate Company at Cricklewood**, which agreed to invisibly mark photographic plates with the company's trademark — a rampant lion — and supply these to Hope for use in a sitting.

At the sitting in Holland Park on 28 February, Hope photographed Price and produced an image of "a charming female form over his shoulders" — apparently Price's mother. But when the developed plates were examined, the rampant-lion trademark was absent, proving Hope had switched the plates.

The exposure was published in the SPR's journal in May 1922. **[[sir-arthur-conan-doyle|Arthur Conan Doyle]]** immediately responded, claiming Price and Dingwall had set Hope up and defending Hope's work. This produced an enduring and bitter tension between Price and Doyle that would last until Doyle's death in 1930.

## Significance for Borley

The Hope affair established Price's public reputation as a fraud-exposer and sceptic — the persona that the *Daily Mirror* and the Smiths were buying into when they called him to Borley in 1929. It also permanently damaged his relationship with Doyle, removing the most powerful spiritualist defender Price might have had when the Borley project was under attack in the 1950s.

> **Contested:** Doyle responded that Price and Dingwall had "set Hope up" and that spiritualists' beliefs were being unfairly attacked.
