# John, third Marquess of Bute (1847–1900)

Scottish nobleman, architect, and psychical researcher, described in the sources as a man "who was deeply interested in such things and was a well-known psychical researcher." He rented Ballechin House, Strathtay, Perthshire in 1897 and commissioned a systematic investigation of its alleged hauntings — the case [[harry-price|Price]] presents as the closest parallel to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

## Involvement with Ballechin

In **1892** Lord Bute happened to meet Father [[father-hayden-sj|Hayden, S.J.]], at a chance encounter. Father Hayden recounted an extraordinary nine-night experience at Ballechin House, where he had been plagued by inexplicable, terrifying noises — including the sound of a calf or big dog falling against his bedroom door. Bute, deeply interested in psychical research, was so impressed by this evidence that he decided to personally rent Ballechin House.

In 1896 a Spanish family rented Ballechin and experienced extensive phenomena; their butler Mr. Harold Sanders later wrote to *The Times* with a detailed account. Bute rented the house from **February 3, 1897** and commissioned two experienced investigators, Colonel Lemesurier Taylor and Miss A. [[goodrich-freer|Goodrich-Freer]], to conduct a thorough investigation with a staff of servants. They remained for approximately three months.

## Publication and Legacy

Lord Bute and Miss Goodrich-Freer co-authored a report detailing their findings, published by Arthur Pearson in **1899** as *The Alleged Haunting of B--- House*. The book generated a national sensation comparable to the publicity later surrounding Price's Borley monographs. [[harry-price|Harry Price]] explicitly drew the parallel, noting that Lord Bute's systematic, funded approach to investigation — assembling independent observers and producing a published report — prefigures Price's own observer program at Borley thirty years later.

## Correspondences with Borley

Price drew up a parallel column comparing Ballechin with Borley. Key correspondences: both houses had a phantom nun seen many times haunting the grounds but never the house; both had Roman Catholic associations throughout; both cases were reported to psychical researchers by a Roman Catholic priest; both investigators rented the house and assembled panels of independent observers; exorcisms by both Anglican and RC priests failed in both cases; dogs reacted violently in both cases; maids refused to remain in both houses.

## Death

Bute died in **1900** before the Borley hauntings became publicly known. The Ballechin phenomena reportedly abruptly ceased at some point after the investigation.
