# Flight-Lieutenant Caunter

RAF officer and one of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s official observers during the 1937–38 Borley tenancy. Caunter conducted observational periods at the Rectory alongside [[carter-jonas|Group-Captain R. Carter Jonas]].

## November 1938 Visit

In November 1938, some months after Price's tenancy had ended and the Rectory was empty and unoccupied, Caunter and Carter Jonas were on leave and decided to pay an unannounced visit. They told no one they were going.

Nothing particularly dramatic occurred, but both observers independently reported **"an overpowering smell of incense"** in one of the rooms. The significance lay in the circumstances: the house had been empty for some time, all windows were presumably closed, and there was no mundane source for such an odour.

This account is cited by Price as one of the recurring olfactory phenomena at Borley — lavender, incense, and cooking smells were reported by multiple independent witnesses across different periods of the haunting. The smell of incense in particular carries Catholic associations that Price regarded as "indicators" pointing toward the [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] narrative.

## Séance Participation

Caunter is also recorded as a sitter at the table-tipping séance held at Borley Rectory on the night of October 23–24, 1937, alongside [[roger-glanville|Squadron-Leader R. H. Glanville]], [[alan-cuthbert|Squadron-Leader Alan J. Cuthbert]], and [[mark-kerr-pearse|M. Kerr-Pearse]]. These sessions produced some of the earliest systematic Planchette evidence about the buried nun. See [[communicating-entities]] for the séance records.

> **Contested:** Price's enthusiasm for incense as evidence has been challenged by [[andrew-clarke|Andrew Clarke]], who identified the nearby [[bush-boake-allen|Bush Boake Allen]] essence factory as a plausible source of olfactory phenomena.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Flight-Lieutenant C. Caunter** (sometimes referred to as a "Flying Officer" [1]) was an RAF officer and a friend of Flight-Lieutenant (later Air Commodore) R. Carter Jonas [1, 2]. He served as one of Harry Price's "official observers" during the year-long tenancy investigation of Borley Rectory [1]. He was one of the many witnesses and participants interviewed by investigator Peter Underwood, who noted that he "treasured the hours" he spent speaking with him [3].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, dates, quotes, and minor contradictions regarding his involvement:

**The First Visit and the "Nail File Apport" (June 1937)**
Caunter and Jonas visited the Rectory for an overnight vigil on **June 29–30, 1937** [2, 4, 5]. They stayed awake for the entire night, taking only a two-hour rest in the Base Room [6]. During this visit, they experienced a notable physical anomaly. 

At 7:00 p.m., during one of their periodical rounds of the house, they discovered a small steel nail file in a ground-floor pantry or store room cupboard [2, 7]. Caunter recorded the specific details of this discovery in a separate report, noting that the object had inexplicably "appeared" since an earlier inspection:
> **"Everything was as we had last seen it, except that in a cupboard or small store room on the ground floor in which we had previously noted a small circular blue box standing in a chalked circle, we now noticed on one of the right-hand shelves a small steel nail file. About a quarter of the nail file was overhanging the edge of the shelf."** [7]

(A minor contradiction exists regarding this event: Colonel Westland, who had inspected the house just days before, had noted the blue box in the cupboard but had not reported seeing any nail file, leading investigators to wonder how it got there [6, 8].) 

**The Psychometry Sequel (1944)**
Years later, in July 1944, this same nail file was used in an experiment with a Canadian psychometrist, Lieutenant Ian Aitchison [9]. Upon handling Caunter's discovered nail file, Aitchison made a dramatic claim: **"Taken from a person who was drowned. A pond with very thick weeds round the edge and very isolated, but I am at the bottom of that pond. I didn’t feel anything. I was pushed in. In other words, I was murdered. The nail-file has been taken from my body. I don’t like that one."** [5].

**The Unnatural Silence of the Rectory**
Despite finding the nail file, Caunter and Jonas neither saw nor heard anything else unusual during their June 1937 stay [6]. In fact, Caunter was struck by the oppressive stillness of the building, providing the following quote in his report:
> **"During the entire stay the house has been very quiet. We both remarked that it was the quietest house we had ever stayed in. We did not hear the usual creaks and noises usually heard in all houses during the night."** [6]

**The Second Visit and the Smell of Incense (November 1938)**
Caunter and Jonas made a final, "unofficial" visit to the Rectory in **early November 1938**, shortly after the building had been sold to Captain W.H. Gregson [10, 11]. Because they were just about to be deployed to Egypt, they only stayed for one night [11]. 

During this visit, the Rectory was completely empty and locked up, but the two men experienced a powerful olfactory phenomenon [11, 12]. As Jonas later reported in a letter to Harry Price sent from Ismailia, Egypt, on December 12, 1938, they found everything much as it had been before, **"except in one place upstairs there was an overpowering smell of incense, which we had not noticed on previous visits"** [12].

**Contradictions in the Record**
The texts contain a minor typographical error regarding his name. While most passages and his official listing as a witness identify him as "Caunter" [2, 13, 14], the timeline entry summarizing his June 1937 visit mistakenly records his name as **"Flt Lt C. Gaunter"** [4]. Furthermore, he is inconsistently referred to by rank, being listed both as a "Flight-Lieutenant" [2, 5] and as an "RAF Flying Officer" [1].
