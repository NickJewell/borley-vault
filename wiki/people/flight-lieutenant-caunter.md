# Flight-Lieutenant Caunter

**Flight-Lieutenant C. Caunter** (also referred to inconsistently as an "RAF Flying Officer" [1]) was an RAF officer and a friend of Flight-Lieutenant (later Air Commodore) [[carter-jonas|R. Carter Jonas]] [1, 2]. He served as one of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s official observers during the year-long tenancy investigation of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], and was one of many witnesses later interviewed by [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]], who noted that he "treasured the hours" he spent speaking with him [3].

> **Contradiction:** The timeline entry summarising his June 1937 visit mistakenly records his name as "Flt Lt C. Gaunter" [4].

## First Visit and the Nail File Apport (June 1937)

Caunter and Carter Jonas conducted an overnight vigil on **June 29–30, 1937**, staying awake for the entire night with only a two-hour rest in the Base Room [6]. During their periodical rounds at 7:00 p.m., they discovered a small steel nail file in a ground-floor pantry or store-room cupboard — a location they had previously inspected without seeing it [2, 7]. Caunter recorded the discovery:

> **"Everything was as we had last seen it, except that in a cupboard or small store room on the ground floor in which we had previously noted a small circular blue box standing in a chalked circle, we now noticed on one of the right-hand shelves a small steel nail file. About a quarter of the nail file was overhanging the edge of the shelf."** [7]

Colonel [[colonel-westland|Westland]], who had inspected the house just days before, had noted the blue box but reported no nail file, reinforcing the anomaly of its appearance [6, 8]. Despite finding the object, Caunter and Jonas saw and heard nothing else unusual during their stay. Caunter was struck by the oppressive stillness of the building:

> **"During the entire stay the house has been very quiet. We both remarked that it was the quietest house we had ever stayed in. We did not hear the usual creaks and noises usually heard in all houses during the night."** [6]

### The Psychometry Sequel (1944)

Years later, in July 1944, the nail file was used in an experiment with a Canadian psychometrist, [[ian-aitchison|Lieutenant Ian Aitchison]] [9]. Upon handling the nail file, Aitchison made a dramatic claim: **"Taken from a person who was drowned. A pond with very thick weeds round the edge and very isolated, but I am at the bottom of that pond. I didn't feel anything. I was pushed in. In other words, I was murdered. The nail-file has been taken from my body. I don't like that one."** [5]

## Séance Participation

Caunter is also recorded as a sitter at the table-tipping séance held at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] on the night of October 23–24, 1937, alongside [[roger-glanville|Squadron-Leader R. H. Glanville]], [[alan-cuthbert|Squadron-Leader Alan J. Cuthbert]], and [[mark-kerr-pearse|M. Kerr-Pearse]]. These sessions produced some of the earliest systematic Planchette evidence about the buried nun. See [[communicating-entities]] for the séance records.

## Second Visit and the Smell of Incense (November 1938)

Caunter and Carter Jonas made a final "unofficial" visit to the Rectory in **early November 1938**, shortly after the building had been sold to [[captain-gregson|Captain W.H. Gregson]] and while it stood completely empty [10, 11]. Because they were about to be deployed to Egypt, they stayed only one night [11].

Both observers independently reported **"an overpowering smell of incense"** in one of the upstairs rooms — something they had not noticed on previous visits. As Carter Jonas later wrote to Price from Ismailia, Egypt, on December 12, 1938, they found everything much as it had been before, **"except in one place upstairs there was an overpowering smell of incense, which we had not noticed on previous visits"** [12].

> **Contested:** Price's enthusiasm for incense as evidence has been challenged by [[andrew-clarke|Andrew Clarke]], who identified the nearby [[bush-boake-allen-factory|Bush Boake Allen]] essence factory as a plausible source of olfactory phenomena.
