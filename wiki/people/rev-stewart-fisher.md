# Rev. Stewart Travers Fisher

Rector of Liston from the death of his father [[rev-thomas-ruggles-fisher|Rev Thomas Ruggles Fisher]] in 1893 until the amalgamation of the Borley and Liston livings in 1936 — a tenure of about 43 years. He was the father of [[nancy-goodale|Nancy Goodale (née Fisher)]].

## The Fisher Dynasty at Liston

Together with his father Thomas Ruggles Fisher (incumbent 1857–1893), Stewart Travers Fisher completed an almost unbroken 79-year family hold on the Liston living. Liston and Borley are adjacent parishes, and the Fisher dynasty at Liston ran concurrently with the Bull dynasty at Borley (1862–1927).

## His Own Home

Unlike many clergymen of the period, Stewart Travers Fisher had his own modern house built at Liston rather than living in the old rectory. He and his family lived in this private home, which became known as "Highfield." Following the departure of Fisher's family, [[violet-oates|Violet Oates]] (sister of Captain Lawrence Oates of Antarctic fame) lived in it.

The old Liston rectory, by contrast, became the residence of the Rev Alfred Clifford Henning from 1936 and subsequently of the [[rev-lanfranc-mathias|Rev Lanfranc Mathias]] from 1955.

## His Connection to the Borley Story

As a clergyman who lived immediately adjacent to Borley, Stewart Fisher was well acquainted with the Bull family and with Harry Bull's views on the haunting. According to [[nancy-goodale|Nancy Goodale's]] account to [[claudine-mathias|CM]], the Rev Stewart Fisher and his wife had once spent the night at Borley Rectory as guests of Harry and Ivy Bull during a snowstorm. When Mrs Fisher walked out of the front door the next morning, she heard footsteps crunching in the snow behind her, assumed it was her husband, and turned — only to find no one there.

The London Gazette notice of 23 November 1928 records Stewart Fisher as the incumbent of Liston at the time of the Order in Council uniting the Borley and Liston benefices, which came into effect in 1936.
