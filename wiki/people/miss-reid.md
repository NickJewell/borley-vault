# Miss Reid

Official observer at Borley Rectory during [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s tenancy. She visited the Rectory in August 1937 and experienced the most dramatic recorded encounter with the "cold spot" — the temperature anomaly on the first-floor landing outside the [[blue-room|Blue Room]].

## The August 1937 Experience

Quoting from the official report: at the cold spot Miss Reid "had a feeling of terror, a sensation of pins and needles all over, and felt very cold, although it was a warm evening. We felt her hands, which were indeed icy."

She recovered, but when the group returned to the same spot about fifteen minutes later, "the same thing happened again." Twice in the same evening, at the same location, the cold spot affected her physically in a way that observers could verify by touching her hands.

## Significance

Miss Reid's experience became a benchmark account for the cold spot phenomenon — the clearest early documentation that the effect involved a physical chill measurable to third parties, not merely a subjective impression. The Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|A. C. Henning]] referenced her experience when telling [[harry-price|Price]] about [[mrs-martin-officer|Mrs Martin]]'s similar September 1943 encounter. Squadron-Leader [[sqn-ldr-horniman|Horniman]] also referenced her experience when his unnamed friend independently felt cold at the same spot in the same month.

The [[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]] later (June 1943) confirmed that the cold spot represents a measurable 11°F temperature anomaly — see [[longmuir-heap]]. Miss Reid's experience, in the context of the broader evidence, thus represents the first well-documented subjective encounter with what was subsequently established as a genuine physical phenomenon.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Alice Reid** (frequently referred to in the texts simply as Miss Reid) was a personal friend of the Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning and his wife [1, 2]. Her singular but significant contribution to the Borley saga stems from an intense physical reaction she had to a specific area in the Rectory, a location that subsequently became famous among investigators as the **"Cold Spot"** [1, 3, 4]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, quotes, specific events, and chronological contradictions regarding her experience:

**Biographical Details and Demeanor**
*   Despite her dramatic experience at the Rectory, the official observer's report goes out of its way to describe Miss Reid as a **"very level-headed person"** who **"does not in any way pretend to be psychic"** [2].

**The August 1937 "Cold Spot" Incident**
Because Miss Reid was interested in the alleged haunting, the Hennings decided to show her over the empty Rectory during a visit in August 1937 [1]. 
*   While walking on the first-floor landing outside the infamous Blue Room, Miss Reid suddenly had a severe reaction [1, 2, 4].
*   Despite it being a warm summer evening, the official report notes that she was overcome by a **"feeling of terror, a sensation of ‘pins and needles’ all over, and felt very cold"** [2, 4].
*   The other members of the group touched her hands to verify her distress and found that they were **"indeed icy"** [2, 4, 5]. Strikingly, no one else in the party experienced any unusual coldness or similar feelings while standing in that area [1, 2, 5].
*   Miss Reid moved away from the landing and quickly recovered, feeling warmer and more at ease [1, 2, 4]. However, when the group returned to the exact same spot about fifteen minutes later, the terrifying and freezing sensations repeated themselves identically [1, 2, 4, 5]. 
*   Investigators later conducted controlled experiments with thermometers at this specific point on the landing, indicating that the temperature there could drop as much as 11 degrees Fahrenheit lower than the surrounding area [1]. 

**Chronological Contradiction**
The sources contain a minor discrepancy regarding the exact date of Miss Reid's visit and which of Harry Price's official observers was present to witness it:
*   One chronological entry places the event on **August 5, 1937**, and notes that the investigator **Sidney Glanville** was inspecting the house with them [5].
*   However, other entries and the direct quotation of the official report state the event took place on or around **August 23, 1937**, and that it was witnessed and recorded by the investigator **Mark Kerr-Pearse** during his extended stay at the Rectory [2, 3].
