# I. S. Longmuir and A. Heap

Members of the [[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]] — a group consisting predominantly of undergraduates from St. John's College, Cambridge, who conducted a series of scientific investigations at the ruined [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] between 1939 and 1944 under the leadership of Dr. [[ajb-robertson|Andrew J. B. Robertson]]. Their temperature measurements at the Borley "cold spot" produced the most rigorous scientific evidence that the temperature anomaly previously reported by many witnesses was a **real physical effect**, not a psychological one.

## Visit No. 14 — The 11°F Cold Spot Measurement (June 19–20, 1943)

Using two thermometers placed at the cold spot on the first-floor landing outside the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] — one enclosed in a glass test-tube (insulated from drafts), one freely exposed — the pair recorded temperatures every minute from 1:00 a.m. to 3:00 a.m.:

| Time | Enclosed thermometer | Exposed thermometer |
|------|---------------------|---------------------|
| 1:00 a.m. | 60.2°F | 65.1°F |
| 1:10 a.m. | 57.0°F | 65.1°F |
| 2:00 a.m. | 54.4°F | 65.1°F |
| 3:00 a.m. | 54.0°F | 65.1°F |

The exposed thermometer remained **perfectly stable** at 65.1°F throughout — suggesting the ambient air temperature was constant. Yet the enclosed thermometer dropped by **11.1°F** over the course of three hours. The thermometers were verified accurate the next day.

This result is physically anomalous: in normal conditions, an insulated thermometer should equilibrate with ambient temperature, not diverge from it. [[ajb-robertson|Robertson]] attempted a mathematical analysis of possible mechanisms, including a speculative "heat removal" process, but could not produce a fully satisfactory conventional explanation. The cold spot's physical reality was thereby established: not merely the psychological impression described by previous witnesses, but a measurable temperature anomaly.

## Other Observations from Visit No. 14

At **11:15 p.m.**, while both men were stationed in the sewing room, they "heard twice the sound of rubble falling in the cellar." Longmuir specifically "detected the sound of glass."

At **1:17 a.m.**, they observed a **"white shape at the base of the tree near the glasshouses"**, estimated to be about six feet high. Longmuir shone his torch directly at the figure, but nothing was visible in the beam's light; when he switched the torch off, the white shape was still there. The men ruled out moonlight as the cause, noting "the moon was down behind the trees." Maintaining the scientific approach typical of the Cambridge Commission, they concluded the anomaly had a natural origin and **"put it down to the bark of the tree fluorescing."** When they returned from taking their temperature readings three minutes later at 1:20 a.m., the shape was no longer visible.

Longmuir also noticed a "subclinically fruity smell" that drifted through the passage for about ninety minutes. He could not attribute it to any natural source; his colleague did not notice it, likely due to catarrh. This parallels multiple earlier reports of unexplained odours at Borley.

## Visit No. 16 (July 16–17, 1943)

I. S. Longmuir returned to Borley approximately a month later for the Cambridge Commission's 16th visit, spanning the night of July 16–17, 1943. He was accompanied by J. H. Angel and I. W. Broomhead. The sources record that during this subsequent visit, absolutely "no phenomena reported."
