# I. S. Longmuir and A. Heap

Cambridge University students and members of the [[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]]; co-investigators on the night of June 19, 1943 (Visit 14). Their temperature measurements at the Borley "cold spot" produced the most rigorous scientific evidence that the temperature anomaly previously reported by many witnesses was a **real physical effect**, not a psychological one.

## The 11°F Cold Spot Measurement

Using two thermometers placed at the cold spot on the first-floor landing outside the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] — one enclosed in a glass test-tube (insulated from drafts), one freely exposed — the pair recorded temperatures every minute from 1:00 a.m. to 3:00 a.m.:

| Time | Enclosed thermometer | Exposed thermometer |
|------|---------------------|---------------------|
| 1:00 a.m. | 60.2°F | 65.1°F |
| 1:10 a.m. | 57.0°F | 65.1°F |
| 2:00 a.m. | 54.4°F | 65.1°F |
| 3:00 a.m. | 54.0°F | 65.1°F |

The exposed thermometer remained **perfectly stable** at 65.1°F throughout — suggesting the ambient air temperature was constant. Yet the enclosed thermometer dropped by **11.1°F** over the course of three hours. The thermometers were verified accurate the next day.

This result is physically anomalous: in normal conditions, an insulated thermometer should equilibrate with ambient temperature, not diverge from it. [[ajb-robertson|Robertson]] attempted a mathematical analysis of possible mechanisms, including a speculative "heat removal" process, but could not produce a fully satisfactory conventional explanation.

**The cold spot's physical reality was now established**: it was not merely the psychological impression described by previous witnesses, but a measurable temperature anomaly.

## Other Observations

Longmuir also noticed a "subclinically fruity smell" that drifted through the passage for about ninety minutes. Longmuir could not attribute it to any natural source; his colleague did not notice it, likely due to catarrh. This parallels multiple earlier reports of unexplained odours at Borley.

A white shape was observed at the base of a tree near the glasshouses at 1:17 a.m. — roughly six feet high — visible when Longmuir's torch was switched off, absent when illuminated. The observers attributed this to possible fluorescence of tree bark, though the shape disappeared by 1:20 a.m.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**I. S. Longmuir** and **A. Heap** were members of the "Cambridge Commission," a group consisting predominantly of undergraduates from St. John's College, Cambridge, who conducted a series of scientific investigations at the ruined Borley Rectory between 1939 and 1944 under the leadership of Dr. Andrew J. B. Robertson [1-3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following specific events, dates, and quotes regarding their involvement:

**Visit No. 14 (June 19–20, 1943)**
Longmuir and Heap conducted their primary investigation together during the Cambridge Commission's 14th visit to the Rectory on the night of June 19–20, 1943 [4, 5]. During this overnight vigil, they reported several specific auditory, thermal, and visual anomalies:
*   **Auditory Phenomena:** At 11:15 p.m., while both men were stationed in the sewing room, they "heard twice the sound of rubble falling in the cellar" [5]. Longmuir specifically "detected the sound of glass" [5].
*   **Thermal Variations:** At 1:00 a.m., the two men went upstairs to take temperature readings at the famous "cold spot," utilizing Room 11 as their base of operations [5]. During this time, they recorded a measurable temperature drop of 1°C on the cold spot [4].
*   **The "White Shape" Sighting:** At 1:17 a.m., they observed a "white shape at the base of the tree near the glasshouses," which they estimated to be "about six feet high" [5]. Longmuir shone his torch directly at the figure, but nothing was visible in the beam's light; however, when he switched the torch off, the white shape was still there [5]. The men firmly ruled out moonlight as the cause because "the moon was down behind the trees in the background" [5]. Maintaining the skeptical and scientific approach typical of the Cambridge Commission, they concluded the anomaly had a natural origin and **"put it down to the bark of the tree fluorescing"** [5]. When they returned from taking their temperature readings three minutes later at 1:20 a.m., they noted that the shape was no longer visible [5].

**Visit No. 16 (July 16–17, 1943)**
I. S. Longmuir returned to Borley Rectory approximately a month later for the Cambridge Commission's 16th visit, spanning the night of July 16–17, 1943 [6, 7]. For this investigation, he was accompanied by J. H. Angel and I. W. Broomhead [6]. The sources record that during this subsequent visit, absolutely "no phenomena reported" [6]. 

The available texts contain no further biographical details or records of A. Heap and I. S. Longmuir beyond these specific investigations.
