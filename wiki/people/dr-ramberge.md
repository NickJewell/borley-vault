# Dr. Ramberge

A Catholic clergyman — described as "late Dean of Lichfield" — who resided at Borley Hall for a month alongside the priest [[john-cox-devon|John Cox / John Devon]] during the spring of 1561, sharing the table of [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Sir Edward Waldegrave]].

## Source

Ramberge is named in Cox/Devon's confession, as transcribed from the *Calendars of State Papers Domestic* by Banks. The verbatim passage (with Banks's modernisation of spelling) reads:

> "The said within John Devon Clarke sayeth that on Tuesday next following the Friday within written, he came to Sir Edward Waldegrave, Knight, to his house at Borley in Essex and there made his abode one month **in the company of Doctor Ramberge, late Dean of Lichfield**, and daily did eat and drink at the only table of the said Sir Edward Waldegrave."

## Significance

The presence of a former Dean of Lichfield at Borley Hall — alongside a fugitive Papist priest — suggests that Waldegrave's Borley estate was functioning as more than a private household. It appears to have served as a refuge or meeting place for prominent Catholic clergy in hiding following the Elizabethan religious settlement.

The descriptor "late Dean of Lichfield" implies Ramberge had been removed from his deanery, almost certainly because of his Catholic faith. Banks does not trace his subsequent fate.

Ramberge is not mentioned further in the Borley literature, but his presence at Borley Hall in 1561 is recorded in a primary state document and helps establish the pattern of organized Catholic activity at the site that Banks argues contributed to whatever residual atmosphere attaches to Borley.

**Dr. Ramberge** (recorded in the texts as the "late Dean of Lichfield") has a very brief presence in the Borley Rectory sources. He appears exclusively in the historical documents relating to the Tudor-era Lord of the Manor, **Sir Edward Waldegrave**, who was persecuted and ultimately imprisoned in the Tower of London for his adherence to the Catholic faith during the reign of Queen Elizabeth I [1, 2].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the available texts reveals only one specific event and quote regarding his connection to Borley:

**Residence at Borley Hall (circa 1561)**
Dr. Ramberge is mentioned in a translated Elizabethan document concerning the interrogation of a Catholic priest named John Devon Clarke [2]. This document was part of the evidence gathered by Protestant authorities who suspected Waldegrave of a Catholic reformation plot against the Queen [2, 3]. 

According to the testimony, Dr. Ramberge spent an extended period residing at Waldegrave's home in Essex alongside the priest. The document records the following specific quote:
> **"The said within John Devon Clarke (sic) sayeth that on Tuesday next following the Friday within written, he came to Sir Edward Waldegrave, Knight, to his house at Borley in Essex and there made his abode one month in the company of Doctor Ramberge, late Dean of Lichfield, and daily did eat and drink at the only table of the said Sir Edward Waldegrave."** [2].

The provided sources do not record any further biographical details, contradictions, or subsequent events regarding Dr. Ramberge's life or his fate following Sir Edward Waldegrave's arrest.
