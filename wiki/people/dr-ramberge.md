# Dr. Ramberge

A Catholic clergyman — described as "late Dean of Lichfield," implying he had been removed from his deanery, almost certainly because of his Catholic faith — who resided at [[borley-place|Borley Hall]] for a month alongside the priest [[john-cox-devon|John Cox / John Devon]] during the spring of 1561, sharing the table of [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Sir Edward Waldegrave]].

## Source

Ramberge is named in Cox/Devon's confession, as transcribed from the *Calendars of State Papers Domestic* by Banks. The verbatim passage (with Banks's modernisation of spelling) reads:

> "The said within John Devon Clarke sayeth that on Tuesday next following the Friday within written, he came to Sir Edward Waldegrave, Knight, to his house at Borley in Essex and there made his abode one month **in the company of Doctor Ramberge, late Dean of Lichfield**, and daily did eat and drink at the only table of the said Sir Edward Waldegrave." [2]

This document was part of the evidence gathered by Protestant authorities who suspected [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Waldegrave]] of a Catholic reformation plot against Queen Elizabeth I [2, 3].

## Significance

The presence of a former Dean of Lichfield at [[borley-place|Borley Hall]] — alongside a fugitive Papist priest — suggests that Waldegrave's Borley estate was functioning as more than a private household. It appears to have served as a refuge or meeting place for prominent Catholic clergy in hiding following the Elizabethan religious settlement.

Banks does not trace Ramberge's subsequent fate. His presence at Borley Hall in 1561 is recorded in a primary state document and helps establish the pattern of organised Catholic activity at the site that Banks argues contributed to whatever residual atmosphere attaches to Borley.
