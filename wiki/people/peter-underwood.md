# Peter Underwood (PU)

(Born 1923.) Co-author (with [[paul-tabori]]) of *Ghosts of Borley* (1973), identified in the book as "PU." A prolific author, broadcaster, and paranormal investigator, Underwood served as the President and Chief Investigator of the [[ghost-club]] from 1960 to 1993, eventually becoming its Life President [1, 2]. Over more than fifty years he visited Borley literally scores of times and personally interviewed almost everyone connected to the haunting [1, 3, 4].

## Biographical Details

Born in Letchworth Garden City, Hertfordshire, and educated privately [5]. He married Joyce Elizabeth Davey in 1944 (who died in 2003), and they had a son, Chris (born 1946), and a daughter, Pamela (born 1949) [5]. He lived successively in Richmond, Twickenham, Walton-on-Thames, Bentley (for nearly thirty years), and on the Surrey/Sussex border [5, 6].

He authored forty-six books between 1971 and 2003, translated into multiple languages [7]. In 1976 his head was sculpted in bronze by Patricia Finch, winner of the Gold Medal for Sculpture in Venice [7]. He was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society of Arts in 1987 [1].

He joined the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) in 1947 [1]. That same year, following an exchange of correspondence regarding his first visit to Borley, [[harry-price|Harry Price]] personally invited him to join the exclusive [[ghost-club]]; Underwood became member number 496 [1, 8, 9]. He eventually succeeded Dr [[paul-tabori|Paul Tabori]] as the Literary Executor to the Harry Price Estate [11, 12].

## Entry into the Borley Case

Underwood first read Harry Price's *The Most Haunted House in England* in the 1940s, and read the sequel *The End of Borley Rectory* on the exact day it was published in 1946 [13]. He had corresponded extensively with Price, and an appointment was set for them to meet in person in April 1948. Tragically, Price died of a sudden heart attack on Easter Monday, 29 March 1948 — the day before they were scheduled to meet [14, 15].

Underwood visited Borley for the first time on **17–18 May 1947**, accompanied by his friend and fellow Ghost Club member Tom Brown. They spent the night in the grounds and met the new owners of the site, [[james-turner|James]] and [[catherine-turner|Catherine Turner]] [9, 13, 16–19].

## Declared Position

Underwood believes some of the Borley phenomena were genuine. He rejects the indictment against [[harry-price|Harry Price]] made by the [[spr-investigation|1956 SPR report]], which he publicly called a "biased and uncharitable attack" [34, 35]. Nevertheless, his view of Price was highly nuanced: in his *Dictionary of the Supernatural* (1978) he concluded: **"He was a mixture of the two [saintly researcher and absolute fraud]... there is evidence that, where his personal self-esteem was involved, he was capable of the most extraordinary double-dealing, spite, and intrigue"** [36].

## Role

Underwood spent many years gathering and analysing the "vast material" on the Borley case. He conducted first-hand interviews with many surviving witnesses, including [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]], Mr J. Harley, and Lionel Foyster's brother. His personal interviews with ageing witnesses are a primary source for accounts of the Bull period.

## Investigative Actions and Paranormal Experiences

On the night of **4–5 March 1950**, Underwood and investigator [[laurence-gafford|Laurence C. Gafford]] kept an overnight vigil in the Turners' lounge at the Rectory Cottage. While the Turners were asleep, both investigators simultaneously heard heavy footsteps and muffled voices overhead at 2:15 a.m. and again at 4:15 a.m., each time ceasing after about twenty seconds [20–23].

In late February 1950, Underwood and Gafford carried out experiments outside the main entrance of the Rectory site, convincing themselves that the road was hollow and likely contained a [[tunnels|tunnel]], though excavation was impossible at the time [21, 24].

In 1975–77, Underwood formally applied for a Home Office licence to exhume the famous bone fragments (believed by some to be the nun "[[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]") from Liston churchyard for modern carbon dating. Despite support from the local rector, the Home Secretary Merlyn Rees officially refused the request in **July 1977** [25, 26].

Underwood submitted samples of the mysterious [[wall-writings|Borley wall writings]] to a professional graphologist, Lewis T. Ackermann. The expert concluded that, with the exception of the "Edwin" message, all the writings had been executed by the same person: [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] [27–31].

## On Marianne Foyster

Underwood was deeply sceptical of Marianne's later claims denying the haunting, writing to the SPR Journal in 1979: **"It is quite obvious to anyone who has simply examined her various statements that Marianne has been leading people up the garden path for years... any impartial enquire into the mysteries of Borley must find Marianne unreliable and decidedly unimpressive as a witness to anything"** [37, 38].

## Interactions with Key Borley Figures

He visited the surviving Bull siblings (Ethel, Constance, and Alfred) at Chilton Lodge roughly nine or ten times during the 1950s [39–41], noting that [[ethel-bull|Ethel]] was the undisputed leader of the family [42]. When author Robert Wood published *The Widow of Borley* (1992) — aggressively attacking Marianne Foyster and dismissing the Borley witnesses — Underwood cancelled Wood's scheduled talk at the Ghost Club, writing: **"I met and talked with Ethel, Constance and Alfred Bull, Edwin Whitehouse, Alfred Henning, Mollie Goldney, Guy L'Estrange and Sidney Glanville among others and I do not recognise the pictures you paint of these people"** [43].

In February 1954, Underwood wrote to [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] asking permission to publish her late husband's manuscript, *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*, which she refused [44]. Decades later, her adopted son [[vernon-oneil|Vincent]] found this very letter in her dresser after she died, sparking his own quest to uncover her secret past [45]. In 1997 Vincent travelled from America to London hoping to finally meet Underwood on a Ghost Tour, but Underwood cancelled his appearance, leaving Vincent bitterly disappointed [46]; they did eventually cross paths briefly in a London hotel [47].

## The Borley Artifacts

As the premier archivist of the case, Underwood amassed a unique collection of original Borley relics:
- **The Great Borley Bell:** Presented to him by the Harry Price Estate/University of London — the bronze courtyard bell that had hung at the Rectory since 1863 [48, 49].
- **The "Fifteen Months" Manuscript and Inkwell:** Lionel's original manuscript alongside a wooden inkwell carved by the Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]], given to him by Lionel's brother [[arthur-foyster|Arthur Foyster]] [48, 50].
- **The "HB" Boundary Stone:** A boundary marker from the Rectory grounds, given to him by James Turner [48, 51].

## Lesser-Known Publication Detail

Underwood is the sole author of *The Ghosts of Borley* (1973). **He actually wrote the book entirely by himself**: [[paul-tabori|Dr Paul Tabori]]'s name was added as a joint author purely to avoid legal or copyright difficulties over the use of Harry Price's archive material, as Tabori was the Literary Executor at the time [52, 53].
