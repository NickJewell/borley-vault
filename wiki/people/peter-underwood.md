# Peter Underwood (PU)

Co-author (with [[paul-tabori]]) of *Ghosts of Borley* (1973). Identified in the book as "PU."

## Role

Spent many years gathering and analysing the "vast material" on the Borley case. Conducted first-hand interviews with many of the surviving witnesses, including [[ethel-bull]], Mr J. Harley, and L. A. Foyster's brother. His personal interviews with ageing witnesses are a primary source for accounts of the Bull period.

## Declared Position

Believes some of the Borley phenomena were genuine. Rejects the indictment against [[harry-price]] made by the 1956 SPR report.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Peter Underwood** (born 1923) is a central figure in the post-Harry Price era of Borley Rectory research. A prolific author, broadcaster, and paranormal investigator, he served as the President and Chief Investigator of the Ghost Club from 1960 to 1993, eventually becoming its Life President [1, 2]. Over the course of more than fifty years, he visited Borley literally scores of times and personally interviewed almost everyone connected to the haunting [1, 3, 4]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, specific events, quotes, and lesser-known facts regarding his life and his deep involvement with the Borley case:

### **Biographical Details and Affiliations**
*   **Early Life and Family:** Born in Letchworth Garden City, Hertfordshire, he was educated privately [5]. He married Joyce Elizabeth Davey in 1944 (who died in 2003), and together they had a son, Chris (born 1946), and a daughter, Pamela (born 1949) [5]. He lived in Richmond, Twickenham, Walton-on-Thames, Bentley (for nearly 30 years), and on the Surrey/Sussex border [5, 6].
*   **Career and Legacy:** He authored forty-six books between 1971 and 2003, with his works translated into multiple languages [7]. In 1976, his head was sculpted in bronze by Patricia Finch, winner of the Gold Medal for Sculpture in Venice [7]. He was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society of Arts in 1987 [1]. 
*   **Affiliations:** He joined the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) in 1947 [1]. That same year, following an exchange of correspondence regarding his first visit to Borley, Harry Price personally invited him to join the exclusive Ghost Club; Underwood became member number 496 [1, 8, 9]. Upon marking twenty-five years of service as the Ghost Club's president, members presented him with an inscribed silver quaich [10]. He eventually succeeded Dr. Paul Tabori as the Literary Executor to the Harry Price Estate [11, 12].

### **Entry into the Borley Case and Harry Price**
*   **First Encounters:** Underwood first read Harry Price's *The Most Haunted House in England* in the 1940s, and read the sequel, *The End of Borley Rectory*, on the exact day it was published in 1946 [13].
*   **The Missed Meeting:** Underwood had corresponded extensively with Harry Price and an appointment was set for them to meet in person in April 1948. Tragically, Price died of a sudden heart attack on Easter Monday, 29 March 1948—the day before they were scheduled to meet [14, 15].
*   **First Visit:** Underwood visited Borley for the first time on **17–18 May 1947**, accompanied by his friend and fellow Ghost Club member Tom Brown. They spent the night in the grounds and met the new owners of the site, James and Catherine Turner [9, 13, 16-19].

### **Investigative Actions and Paranormal Experiences**
*   **The Rectory Cottage Vigil (1950):** On the night of **4–5 March 1950**, Underwood and investigator Laurence C. Gafford kept an overnight vigil in the Turners' lounge at the Rectory Cottage. While the Turners were asleep, both investigators simultaneously heard heavy footsteps and muffled voices/mutterings overhead at **2:15 a.m.**, and again at **4:15 a.m.** On both occasions, the sounds ceased after about twenty seconds. The men had spent many hours at the cottage previously in all conditions without ever hearing anything similar [20-23].
*   **Hollow Road Discovery:** In late February 1950, Underwood and Gafford carried out experiments outside the main entrance of the Rectory site, convincing them beyond doubt that the road was hollow and likely contained a tunnel, though excavation was impossible at the time [21, 24].
*   **The Exhumation Attempt (1975–1977):** Underwood formally applied for a Home Office licence to exhume the famous bone fragments (believed by some to be the nun "Marie Lairre") from Liston churchyard to subject them to modern carbon dating. Despite the support of the local rector, the Home Secretary, Merlyn Rees, officially refused the request in July 1977 [25, 26].
*   **Graphology and the Wall Writings:** Underwood submitted samples of the mysterious Borley wall writings to a professional graphologist, Lewis T. Ackermann. The expert concluded that, with the exception of the "Edwin" message, all the writings had been executed by the same person: Marianne Foyster [27-31].

### **Opinions and Direct Quotes**
*   **On the Paranormal:** Summarizing his approach to ghost hunting, Underwood famously stated: **"Ninety Eight percent of reported hauntings have a natural and mundane explanation, but it is the other two percent that have interested me"** [32, 33].
*   **On Harry Price:** Though he fiercely defended Price against the SPR's critical *Borley Report* (which he publicly called a "biased and uncharitable attack" [34, 35]), Underwood held a highly nuanced view of the man. In his *Dictionary of the Supernatural* (1978), he concluded: **"He was a mixture of the two [saintly researcher and absolute fraud]... there is evidence that, where his personal self-esteem was involved, he was capable of the most extraordinary double-dealing, spite, and intrigue"** [36].
*   **On Marianne Foyster:** Underwood was deeply skeptical of Marianne's later claims denying the haunting, writing to the SPR Journal in 1979: **"It is quite obvious to anyone who has simply examined her various statements that Marianne has been leading people up the garden path for years... any impartial enquire into the mysteries of Borley must find Marianne unreliable and decidedly unimpressive as a witness to anything"** [37, 38].

### **Interactions with Key Borley Figures**
*   **The Bull Family:** He visited the surviving Bull siblings (Ethel, Constance, and Alfred) at Chilton Lodge roughly nine or ten times during the 1950s [39-41]. He noted that Ethel was the undisputed leader of the family [42]. When Ethel discovered Underwood's wife Joyce liked blue-and-white china, she gave her a beautiful miniature plate that had once graced Borley Rectory [41].
*   **Defending the Witnesses:** When author Robert Wood published *The Widow of Borley* (1992)—a book aggressively attacking Marianne Foyster and dismissing the Borley witnesses—Underwood cancelled Wood's scheduled talk at the Ghost Club. In a stinging letter, he wrote: **"I met and talked with Ethel, Constance and Alfred Bull, Edwin Whitehouse, Alfred Henning, Mollie Goldney, Guy L’Estrange and Sidney Glanville among others and I do not recognise the pictures you paint of these people"** [43].
*   **Marianne's Son (Vincent O'Neil):** In February 1954, Underwood wrote to Marianne Foyster asking permission to publish her late husband's manuscript, *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*, which she refused [44]. Decades later, her adopted son Vincent found this very letter in her dresser after she died, sparking his own quest to uncover her secret past [45]. Years later, in 1997, Vincent travelled from America to London hoping to finally meet Underwood on a Ghost Tour, but Underwood cancelled his appearance, leaving Vincent bitterly disappointed [46]. They did eventually cross paths briefly in a London hotel, where Underwood initially walked away before returning to stand beside him [47].

### **The Borley Artifacts**
As the premier archivist of the case, Underwood amassed a unique collection of original Borley relics:
*   **The Great Borley Bell:** Presented to him by the Harry Price Estate/University of London, this was the bronze courtyard bell that had hung at the Rectory since 1863. He kept it at his cottage in Hampshire and later at his home on the Surrey border [48, 49].
*   **The "Fifteen Months" Manuscript & Inkwell:** Marianne’s brother-in-law, Arthur Foyster, gave him Lionel's original manuscript alongside a wooden inkwell carved by the Rev. Harry Bull [48, 50].
*   **The "HB" Boundary Stone:** A boundary marker from the Rectory grounds, given to him by James Turner [48, 51].

### **Lesser-Known Publication Detail**
Underwood was the author of the highly regarded 1973 book *The Ghosts of Borley*. However, **he actually wrote the book entirely by himself**. Dr. Paul Tabori’s name was added as a joint author purely to avoid any legal or copyright difficulties over the use of Harry Price's archive material, as Tabori was the Literary Executor at the time [52, 53].
