# C. G. Browne

Resident of Pound Hall, Long Melford, Suffolk. Co-witness, with [[rosemary-williams|Miss Rosemary M. Williams]], of one of the most striking post-fire apparition accounts at Borley Rectory.

## The Blue Room Apparition (March 26, 1939)

Approximately one month after the Rectory fire of February 27–28, 1939, Browne and Williams were among a group of friends examining the ruins by moonlight on the night of Sunday, March 26, 1939. The pair were standing on the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]], gazing at the blackened shell, when both independently observed a figure at what had been the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] window.

As Price quotes Williams's report:

> *"I had the impression of someone in a light buff-coloured coat, but as the figure approached the window, I could see it was a woman clothed in blue. She remained for several seconds and then turned towards the wall and, as it seemed, walked through it."*

Browne confirmed Miss Williams's account in full.

## Physical Impossibility

The sighting has particular evidential weight because of what it required if fraudulent: the floor of the Blue Room had collapsed in the fire. Anyone standing at the Blue Room window would have been standing on nothing — or on charred, rotten rafters. Captain Gregson had posted "Keep Out" notices warning the building was dangerous. Access to the window from inside the ruin under those conditions would have been extremely hazardous for any living person.

Price notes that even had an interloper somehow reached the window level, Browne and Williams — standing on the Nun's Walk — had a clear view of the approach; no one was seen arriving or departing.

The sighting stands as one of the best-attested post-fire phenomena, with two independent witnesses observing simultaneously under open-air moonlight conditions.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Charles Graham Browne** (often recorded in the sources as **C. G. Browne** or **Charles Browne**) was a resident of Pound Hall, Long Melford, who became an important firsthand witness to one of the most famous post-fire apparitions at Borley Rectory [1-3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts and events regarding his involvement:

**The Visit to the Ruins (March 26, 1939)**
Exactly a month after Borley Rectory was gutted by a massive fire, Mr. Browne visited the burnt-out shell on the night of **Sunday, March 26, 1939** [3]. He was part of a "ghost-hunting" group that included **Miss Rosemary M. Williams** (the daughter of Captain Williams of Borley Lodge) and other friends [3, 4]. Arriving at the Rectory gates at approximately 10:30 p.m., the party found it to be a cold, bright, and clear starlit night, with the moon in its first quarter providing enough illumination to clearly see the ruined building [3, 4]. Because the property's owner, Capt. W. H. Gregson, had warned them not to walk inside among the dangerous, charred rafters, the group wandered around the exterior of the house in small groups [4]. 

**The Blue Room Apparition**
While the group was standing on the Nun's Walk looking up at the house, Miss Williams suddenly called out that she could see the figure of a small woman dressed in blue standing in the burnt-out aperture of the famous Blue Room window [3, 4]. When she alerted the rest of the party, most of them disbelieved her, admitting they had not been looking up at the window at the time [4]. 

**Browne** was the sole exception. He corroborated Miss Williams's claim, stating that he too had seen **"for a flash, something resembling a figure of a woman disappearing from the window"** [4]. The sources note a minor distinction between the two witnesses' accounts: while Miss Williams watched the figure for several seconds before it appeared to turn and walk through the wall, **Browne saw it for a much shorter duration, catching sight of the apparition just as it vanished from the window** [1, 3]. 

**The Investigation and Interviews**
A few days after the event, on **March 29, 1939**, investigator **Harry Price** and the **Rev. Alfred C. Henning** personally interviewed Browne and Miss Williams regarding their sighting [5, 6]. Price recorded that Browne was **"quite definite about the appearance of the figure"** [6]. Crucially, both Browne and Williams helped establish that the apparition could not have been a living person playing a trick; they confirmed that the floorboards and rafters of the Blue Room had been completely destroyed during the fire, meaning there was absolutely nothing for a human being to stand on at that window [4, 6, 7]. 

*(Note: The sources also briefly mention an "Arthur Browne" [8, 9], but he was a cousin of the Rev. Lionel Foyster who visited the Rectory to assist with an exorcism during 1931, and is a different individual from the Charles G. Browne involved in the 1939 sighting).*
