# C. G. Browne

**Charles Graham Browne** (often recorded as C. G. Browne or Charles Browne) of Pound Hall, Long Melford, Suffolk. Co-witness, with [[rosemary-williams|Miss Rosemary M. Williams]], of one of the most striking post-fire apparition accounts at Borley Rectory [1-3].

## The Blue Room Apparition (March 26, 1939)

Approximately one month after the Rectory fire of February 27–28, 1939, Browne visited the burnt-out shell on the night of **Sunday, March 26, 1939** [3]. He was part of a "ghost-hunting" group that included Miss Rosemary M. Williams (the daughter of Captain Williams of Borley Lodge) and other friends [3, 4]. Arriving at the Rectory gates at approximately 10:30 p.m., they found a cold, bright, clear starlit night with the moon in its first quarter providing enough illumination to see the ruined building clearly [3, 4]. Because the property's owner, [[captain-gregson|Capt. W. H. Gregson]], had warned them not to walk inside among the dangerous charred rafters, the group wandered around the exterior in small groups [4].

While the group was standing on the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] looking up at the house, Miss Williams suddenly called out that she could see the figure of a small woman dressed in blue standing in the burnt-out aperture of the famous [[blue-room|Blue Room]] window. As Price quotes Williams's report:

> *"I had the impression of someone in a light buff-coloured coat, but as the figure approached the window, I could see it was a woman clothed in blue. She remained for several seconds and then turned towards the wall and, as it seemed, walked through it."*

Most of the party disbelieved her, admitting they had not been looking at the window at the time [4]. Browne was the sole exception. He corroborated Miss Williams's claim, stating that he too had seen **"for a flash, something resembling a figure of a woman disappearing from the window"** [4].

> **Note:** While Miss Williams watched the figure for several seconds before it appeared to turn and walk through the wall, Browne saw it for a much shorter duration, catching sight of the apparition just as it vanished [1, 3].

## Physical Impossibility

A few days after the event, on **March 29, 1939**, [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and the [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Alfred C. Henning]] personally interviewed Browne and Miss Williams [5, 6]. Both confirmed that the floorboards and rafters of the Blue Room had been completely destroyed during the fire, meaning there was absolutely nothing for a human being to stand on at that window [4, 6, 7]. Price recorded that Browne was **"quite definite about the appearance of the figure"** [6].

The sighting has particular evidential weight because of what it required if fraudulent: the floor of the Blue Room had collapsed, and anyone standing at that window would have been standing on nothing, or on charred, rotten rafters. Browne and Williams — standing on the Nun's Walk — had a clear view of the approach; no one was seen arriving or departing.

> **Note:** The sources also briefly mention an "Arthur Browne" [8, 9], a cousin of Rev. Lionel Foyster who visited the Rectory to assist with an exorcism in 1931 — this is a different individual from Charles G. Browne.
