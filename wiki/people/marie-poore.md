# Marie Poore

Wife of Mr A. C. Poore and mother of fellow Borley witness [[hilary-wright|Hilary Wright]]. Following her husband's retirement, Marie and A. C. Poore relocated from the Middlesex area to Borley [1], purchasing a bungalow named **The Willows** on Lower Road — the first residence encountered when approaching Borley from the direction of Rodbridge Corner [1]. They were frequently disturbed by strangers knocking to ask for directions to the church and the "haunted rectory," an enduring nuisance that continued more than thirty years after the Rectory's destruction by fire [1].

## Phantom Coach Sighting — Early 1970s

On a clear night around midnight, returning from a pantomime rehearsal at the Theatre Royal in Bury St Edmunds, both Mrs Poore and her husband saw what appeared to be an **old-fashioned horse-drawn coach with small lamps** crossing the large sloping field to the rear of their house, moving toward them. In November 1973, Mr A. C. Poore wrote to investigator [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] detailing the event [2].

Returning from Bury St Edmunds at around 12:30 a.m., the couple crossed the River Stour at Rodbridge and suddenly saw "a shape with two lights coming towards us just beyond our bungalow on the sloping fields between us and Borley Church" [2]. Their initial thought was that it must be a tractor, but before they could process that improbability the shape and lights abruptly disappeared [2]. Mr Poore noted there was absolutely no natural explanation — no mist, gate posts, hedges, water, or earth heaps, only a gentle slope of ploughed field — and asked: "So was it the coach?" [3].

**Investigation:** Her husband examined the field the next morning. There were **no tyre marks, wheel marks, or hoof marks** anywhere in the area.

**The old road:** An elderly local resident then told Mrs Poore of a tradition that the original road between Borley and Rodbridge had been **straight** rather than following the present L-shaped route of Hall Lane and Lower Road. The coach appeared to be travelling on exactly this line. This tradition was subsequently confirmed by a map dated **1777**, which shows it was indeed the original highway.

This links the sighting to the more general [[phantom-coach|phantom coach]] phenomenon and to the idea that paranormal phenomena may follow geographical or historical paths.

## The Lost Diary of Ethel Bull

Perhaps Marie Poore's most historically significant contribution to the Borley saga is her discovery of a tantalising lost document [4]. While living in Borley, she became friends with the [[payne-family|Payne family]] of Borley Place and through these contacts met a Sudbury resident named **Eileen Livock**, who had known the Bull family well [4]. Eileen Livock showed Marie Poore a diary that had been kept by [[ethel-bull|Miss Ethel Bull]] when she was a young girl [4].

Mrs Poore described the diary as resembling a school exercise book rather than a pocket book, with entries written in pencil [4]. Most significantly, she recalled that it contained casual, matter-of-fact entries to the general effect of: **"we saw the nun in the garden today"** [4].

Despite the obvious historical value such a contemporary document would hold for psychical researchers, investigators have so far been unsuccessful in locating this diary [4].

## Phantom Cat Sighting — 1974

Mrs Poore also reported a large cat dashing into the road and then vanishing, at the same spot where her daughter [[hilary-wright|Hilary Wright]] had seen the same [[phantom-cat|phantom cat]] phenomenon in the same year (1974).
