# Dennis Wheatley

(Born 8 January 1897; died 10 November 1977) British author dubbed the "Prince of Thriller Writers" by *The Times Literary Supplement*. He published fifty-four books, of which only nine were the black magic stories for which he became famous [3]. At the height of his powers, one of the world's best-selling authors in a writing career spanning the early 1930s to the mid-1970s. He is featured alongside other notable twentieth-century ghost hunters and psychical researchers in [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]]'s book *The Ghost Hunters* [2].

## Writing Career and Occult Research

Wheatley's most acclaimed occult thriller, *The Devil Rides Out*, was published by Hutchinson in December 1934 and was considered the twentieth century's natural successor to classic gothic novels like *Dracula* and *Frankenstein* [3].

While researching *The Devil Rides Out*, Wheatley deliberately sought out and made the acquaintance of several notable experts in the occult [3]. These figures included the infamous Aleister Crowley, the Rev. Montague Summers, the Egyptian occultist Rollo Ahmed, and the psychical researcher [[harry-price|Harry Price]] [3]. Price personally showed Wheatley around his [[national-laboratory|National Laboratory of Psychical Research]], then located in the basement flat of 13 Roland Gardens, South Kensington [3].

## Annotated Library and the Tabori Biography

Wheatley habitually annotated the books in his personal library that he utilised for research [4]. Following his death, his library was auctioned in 1979 [4]. Included in the sale was his copy of [[paul-tabori|Paul Tabori]]'s 1950 biography, *Harry Price – The Biography of a Ghost-Hunter*, which bore Wheatley's handwritten inscription on the half-title page: "Used by me when writing my stories with occult background/Dennis Wheatley" [4].

When Sphere Books produced the *Dennis Wheatley Library of the Occult* series in the mid-1970s (ultimately 45 volumes), Tabori's *Harry Price — The Biography of a Ghost-Hunter* was Number 7 in the series; Wheatley's introduction dealt with the Borley case.

## The Suppressed Photograph

Wheatley relayed a significant story about a suppressed apparition photograph, recorded inside the cover of his copy of the [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Alfred Henning]]'s *Haunted Borley* booklet (which the rector had personally sent to him) [5].

According to Wheatley's inscription, broadcaster and *Daily Mail* book reviewer [[kenneth-allsop|Kenneth Allsop]] had been sent by his editor to investigate the ruins of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] alongside a staff photographer, likely in the early 1940s [5, 6]. Because the Borley haunting was actively being "debunked" in the press at the time, Allsop's editor specifically mandated that the resulting article must take a sceptical tone [5]. After camping in the grounds for several nights without encountering anything unusual, Allsop wrote a sceptical piece based on his uneventful stay [6].

However, when the photographer developed his film, **the figure of a nun could be "quite clearly seen" on one of the prints** [5]. When Allsop presented the anomalous photograph to his editor, the editor refused to publish it, flatly telling the journalist, "No, I just don't print it" [5]. Neither the article nor the photograph has been made public.

## Significance

Wheatley's involvement with Borley is primarily as cultural context — his black magic novels were directly influenced by the same late-Victorian/interwar occult milieu in which Borley's haunting developed. The suppressed Allsop photograph story, if accurate, represents another instance of journalistic self-censorship around the Borley case.
