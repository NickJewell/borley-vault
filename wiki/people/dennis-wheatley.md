# Dennis Wheatley

(Born 8 January 1897; died 10 November 1977) British author dubbed the "Prince of Thriller Writers" by *The Times Literary Supplement*. At the height of his powers, one of the world's best-selling authors in a writing career spanning the early 1930s to the mid-1970s.

## Connection to Harry Price

While researching *The Devil Rides Out* (published December 1934) — his most famous book, described as the twentieth century's natural successor to *Dracula* and *Frankenstein* — Wheatley made the acquaintance of several experts in occult matters, including [[harry-price]]. Price showed Wheatley around the [[national-laboratory|National Laboratory of Psychical Research]] at its location in the basement flat of 13 Roland Gardens, South Kensington.

When [[paul-tabori]] published his biography *Harry Price — The Biography of a Ghost-Hunter* in 1950, Wheatley used it as a research resource and annotated his copy. When his library was auctioned in 1979, the copy bore the inscription: "Used by me when writing my stories with occult background — Dennis Wheatley."

## The Suppressed Photograph

Wheatley relayed a significant story about a suppressed apparition photograph. Broadcaster Kenneth Allsop, then a journalist, was sent by his editor to investigate the ruins of [[borley-rectory]]. He and a staff photographer spent several nights camped in the Rectory grounds and saw nothing unusual. When the photographer developed his plates, one showed "the clear outline of a nun standing in the gap where one of the old drive gates had been located." When Allsop showed this to his editor, the editor suppressed both the article and the photograph. Neither has been made public.

Wheatley recorded this story inside the cover of his copy of [[rev-ac-henning|Henning]]'s *Haunted Borley* booklet.

## The Borley Library of the Occult

In the mid-1970s, Sphere Books produced the *Dennis Wheatley Library of the Occult* series (ultimately 45 volumes). Tabori's *Harry Price — The Biography of a Ghost-Hunter* was Number 7 in the series; Wheatley's introduction dealt with the Borley case.

## Significance

Wheatley's involvement with Borley is primarily as cultural context — his black magic novels were directly influenced by the same late-Victorian/interwar occult milieu in which Borley's haunting developed. The suppressed Allsop photograph story, if accurate, represents another instance of journalistic self-censorship around the Borley case, similar to the editorial decisions that shaped the original [[newspaper-articles|Daily Mirror coverage]] in 1929.
