# St John Saunders

Also referred to in the texts as **S. John Saunders**. A **publisher from Chelmsford** and lay investigator and journalist [1, 2], who paid multiple visits to Borley during the 1950s and was personally interviewed by Borley researchers [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] and Dr. Peter Hilton-Rowe [1].

## The April 1961 Vigil

On **Saturday, 8 April 1961**, Saunders organised an all-night vigil at the haunted site with fellow enthusiasts **Captain [[captain-ben-martindale|Ben Martindale]] from Ockenham** and **Mr. [[karl-barton|Karl Barton]], BSc**, a civil engineer [2]. Arriving at 4:00 p.m., the party assembled in the churchyard because the church was locked, and after several excursions to the former Rectory site, hid among the trees to wait [1].

During the early hours, Saunders and his companions reported experiencing a diverse range of auditory, visual, and mechanical anomalies:

- **Voices and Footsteps:** At roughly 3:00 a.m., the party heard a **disembodied voice whispering** on the roadside opposite the Rectory site, approximately 10 yards behind their parked cars, though nothing was visible [1, 2]. A quarter of an hour later, they heard **"loud and menacing" footsteps** that seemed to approach them, pause about 20 feet away, and completely cease [1].
- **Visual Anomalies:** At 4:00 a.m., the group observed **"vivid blue pinpoints of light"** hovering and dancing among the trees over the Rectory garden at a height of about ten to fifteen feet [1, 2]. Saunders was certain these lights could not be explained by natural causes such as street lighting or reflections [2].
- **Mechanical and Battery Interference:** During the vigil, the group suffered extreme and unexplained equipment failures. **Two new torches, their car headlights, and a press photographer's floodlight all ceased functioning simultaneously** [1, 2]. One car inexplicably stalled on Hall Lane, and **Saunders discovered his own watch had suddenly stopped** [1]. This pattern of simultaneous equipment failure also reportedly appeared during Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth's later technical investigations.

In a letter written to Peter Underwood on **11 April 1969**, Saunders was "quite sure that street lighting, reflections, and similar natural explanations did not provide the answer to the pin-points of light that hovered over the Rectory garden at a height of ten to fifteen feet" [2]. He later published a full account in an article titled **"The Ghosts of Borley,"** which appeared in the **December 1978 issue of the *East Anglian Monthly*** [1].
