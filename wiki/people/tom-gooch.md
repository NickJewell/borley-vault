# Mr and Mrs Tom Gooch

A farmer and a schoolmistress (she taught at a girls' church school in the next village to Borley) who purchased approximately five acres of the former Borley Rectory orchard and garden for £300 — reported in the *Evening News* of March 14, 1946. The purchase was made sometime in 1945–46, after the demolition of the Rectory ruins.

## Their Plans

The Gooches were described by the *Evening News* reporter as "a matter-of-fact couple" who intended to build their home on the land — which included the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] and the area where the phantom nun was said to have walked for nearly a century.

## Mrs Gooch's Experiences

When questioned closely, Mrs Gooch admitted to having "heard 'noises' and witnessed some rather odd goings-on in the days of the old Rectory." She had never seen the headless coachman. However, when she had attended a church council meeting in the Rectory proper, a picture fell from the wall and "bits of brick and twig came hurtling down the chimney."

## Significance

The Gooches represent the post-war chapter of the Borley story: new, secular, practical owners who had no connection to the haunting mythos, yet even Mrs Gooch acknowledged personal experiences. Their planned house — which would cover the Nun's Walk — provoked speculation from Price about what manifestations, if any, would occur in a new structure on the site. The story of what actually happened on the site after 1946 falls outside Price's published account.

See also [[nuns-walk]] and [[the-nun]] for the phenomena associated with this ground.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mr. Thomas Alfred Gooch** (who died on February 13, 1974, aged 85) and his wife **Mabel Gooch** (who died three years later in 1977) were prominent local figures during the later, post-demolition era of the Borley Rectory haunting [1, 2]. Described by researchers and contemporaries as a "matter-of-fact couple" and **"practical and sensible country people"** who did not seek publicity [2, 3], their experiences and involvement provide a fascinatingly grounded perspective on the Borley legend. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, events, quotes, and contradictions regarding the couple:

**Biographical Details and Connection to Borley**
*   **Tom Gooch** was a local Suffolk farmer and laborer who occasionally worked for the writer James Turner (a resident of the Rectory Cottage) [1, 3]. 
*   **Mabel Gooch** was a school-mistress at a girls’ church school in a neighboring village and also served as the church organist at Borley Church [1, 3, 4]. 

**The Purchase of the Rectory Grounds**
In early **1946**, the Gooches made headlines when they purchased the south-eastern, lower part of the former Rectory grounds—comprising five acres of orchard and gardens—for **£300** [3-5]. 
*   **Building over the Nun's Walk:** Tom Gooch used the land to build a workshop and later erected the first of four bungalows to appear on the site [1, 4, 6, 7]. Notably, this bungalow was built directly over the legendary "Nun’s Walk," prompting researchers to speculate whether the ghost would object to her path being built over [3, 8, 9].

**Tom Gooch's Experiences and Contradictions**
Tom Gooch and his employer James Turner both claimed that they did not really believe in the ghostly records of Borley Rectory [10]. Nevertheless, Gooch reported experiencing paranormal phenomena, which led to a notable contradiction investigated by Peter Underwood:
*   **The Gates and the Wall (May 16, 1947):** Gooch claimed that at about 9:00 p.m. while working on the roof of his new building, the heavy wrought-iron gates he had installed **suddenly threw themselves wide open** despite there being no wind. He further claimed that a protecting stone and cement wall he had built two weeks prior spontaneously collapsed [8, 11]. 
*   **The Contradiction:** When investigator Peter Underwood examined the site later that same evening, he found the top edge of the wall smooth and the cement unbroken, explicitly noting that he **"could find nothing to substantiate Tom Gooch’s story that part of the wall had fallen down"** [12].
*   **The 1947 Broadcast and Quote:** On June 29, 1947, Tom Gooch participated in a BBC radio broadcast about Borley. Interestingly, he omitted the story of the collapsed wall during the broadcast, though he did recount the gates swinging open and stones being thrown [7, 12]. Summarizing his pragmatic attitude toward the haunting, Gooch famously declared on the air: **"There are queer things happening here at the Rectory, such as stones throwing and gates swing open. But that don’t worry me, if nothing happened worse than that I can get on with my job. Most things that do bother me is the damned people that get in my way all the time"** [7].

**Mabel Gooch's Experiences**
Mrs. Gooch was far more forthcoming about witnessing "odd goings-on" and had several inexplicable experiences both at the Rectory and inside Borley Church:
*   **Rectory Phenomena:** She recalled that during the days of the old Rectory, while she was attending a church council meeting in the building, **a picture suddenly fell off the wall and bits of brick and twig came hurtling down the chimney** [3].
*   **Church Phenomena:** As the church organist, Mrs. Gooch claimed personal knowledge of heavy objects moving inside the locked Borley Church at night. She and churchwarden Mrs. Pearson once deliberately hid a church register before locking up, only to find it moved several yards away the next morning [2, 13]. She also reported finding the altar lamp inexplicably smashed on two separate occasions [13]. 
*   **The Apparition by the Garage (1949):** On August 20, 1949, while standing near the Gooches' garage, a friend of Mrs. Gooch's claimed to see a figure dressed in white or light-colored clothing disappear behind a hedge in the Gooches' garden. They waited for the figure to reappear, but it never did [2, 13-16].
