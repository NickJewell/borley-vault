# Mr and Mrs Tom Gooch

**Mr. Thomas Alfred Gooch** (died 13 February 1974, aged 85) and his wife **Mabel Gooch** (died 1977) were prominent local figures during the post-demolition era of the Borley story. Described by researchers and contemporaries as a "matter-of-fact couple" and "practical and sensible country people" who did not seek publicity [2, 3], their experiences provide a fascinatingly grounded perspective on the Borley legend.

Tom Gooch was a local Suffolk farmer and labourer who occasionally worked for the writer [[james-turner|James Turner]], then a resident of the [[rectory-cottage|Rectory Cottage]] [1, 3]. Mabel Gooch was a school-mistress at a girls' church school in a neighbouring village and also served as the church organist at [[borley-church|Borley Church]] [1, 3, 4].

## The Purchase of the Rectory Grounds

In early 1946, the Gooches made headlines when they purchased the south-eastern, lower portion of the former Rectory grounds — comprising five acres of orchard and garden including the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] — for **£300** [3–5], as reported in the *Evening News* of 14 March 1946. Tom Gooch used the land to build a workshop and later erected the first of four bungalows to appear on the site. This bungalow was built directly over the legendary Nun's Walk, prompting researchers to speculate whether the phantom nun would object to her path being built over [3, 8, 9].

## Tom Gooch's Experiences

Despite describing himself as a man who did not really believe in the ghostly records of Borley Rectory, Gooch reported experiencing paranormal phenomena [10]:

**The Gates and the Wall (16 May 1947):** While working on the roof of his new building, Gooch claimed that at about 9:00 p.m. the heavy wrought-iron gates he had installed **suddenly threw themselves wide open** despite there being no wind. He further claimed that a protecting stone and cement wall he had built two weeks prior spontaneously collapsed [8, 11].

> **Contradiction:** When investigator [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] examined the site later that same evening, he found the top edge of the wall smooth and the cement unbroken, explicitly noting that he "could find nothing to substantiate Tom Gooch's story that part of the wall had fallen down" [12].

**The 1947 BBC Broadcast:** On 29 June 1947, Tom Gooch participated in a BBC radio broadcast about Borley. He omitted the story of the collapsed wall during the broadcast, though he did recount the gates swinging open and stones being thrown [7, 12]. He famously declared on the air: **"There are queer things happening here at the Rectory, such as stones throwing and gates swing open. But that don't worry me, if nothing happened worse than that I can get on with my job. Most things that do bother me is the damned people that get in my way all the time"** [7].

## Mabel Gooch's Experiences

Mrs. Gooch was far more forthcoming about witnessing "odd goings-on" and had several inexplicable experiences both at the Rectory and inside [[borley-church|Borley Church]]:

- **Rectory Phenomena:** She recalled that during the days of the old Rectory, while attending a church council meeting in the building, **a picture suddenly fell off the wall and bits of brick and twig came hurtling down the chimney** [3].
- **Church Phenomena:** As the church organist, Mrs. Gooch claimed personal knowledge of heavy objects moving inside the locked Borley Church at night. She and churchwarden [[mrs-pearson-church|Mrs. Pearson]] once deliberately hid a church register before locking up, only to find it moved several yards away the next morning [2, 13]. She also reported finding the altar lamp inexplicably smashed on two separate occasions [13].
- **The Apparition by the Garage (1949):** On 20 August 1949, a friend of Mrs. Gooch's claimed to see a figure dressed in white or light-coloured clothing disappear behind a hedge in the Gooches' garden near their garage. They waited for the figure to reappear, but it never did [2, 13–16] (single witness).

## Significance

The Gooches represent the post-war chapter of the Borley story: new, secular, practical owners with no connection to the haunting mythos, yet even Mrs Gooch acknowledged personal experiences. Their bungalow built over the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] provoked speculation from [[harry-price|Price]] about what manifestations, if any, would occur in a new structure on the site.
