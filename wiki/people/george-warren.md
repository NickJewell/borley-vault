# George Warren

Seventy-nine-year-old grocer and postmaster at **Marks Tey**, Essex; head of the [[marks-tey-spiritualist-circle|Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle]] that conducted the famous January 1932 séance at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] — after which the poltergeist activity largely ceased.

## Background

Warren and his wife [[emily-warren|Emily]] ran the post office at Station Road, Marks Tey, a village eight miles from Borley. Behind the shop was a back room where their group of spiritualist friends — drawn from across East Anglia — met for séances. Warren had investigated several cases of haunting and generally been successful in resolving them.

## Approach to the Foysters

In January 1932, Warren wrote to [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]] offering his help, having heard about the phenomena at the Rectory. Lionel declined initially, but Warren persisted with a second polite letter. Lionel agreed on one condition: Warren must not openly accuse his wife of being responsible. If Warren concluded that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] was the cause, he should simply say it was a case in which he was unable to help.

A date was arranged, but Warren, [[hf-frost|H.F. Frost]] and [[captain-deane|Captain Deane]] arrived unexpectedly after midnight on 22 January, telling Lionel that the "helpers on the other side" had warned them that Marianne's spirit protectors could not hold out much longer.

## The January 1932 Séance

The following night — 23 January — the full Marks Tey Circle arrived, including [[guy-lestrange|Guy L'Estrange]] as the medium, Emily Warren, Frost, Deane, and a **Mr Footman**. Emily Warren brought a planchette. After the séance in the Blue Room, L'Estrange — channelling a spirit he identified as the Borley nun, who named herself **Evangeline Westcott** — said he was confident there would be no more disturbances.

The next morning, the Foysters noted the house felt "entirely different" — warmer, changed. The maid Katie also noticed. The poltergeist activity did indeed largely cease.

Warren led the group with a steady, considerate manner that the Foysters found very different from the "bombastic" Harry Price and Mollie Goldney. The Foysters "really took to" the Marks Tey group.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**George Warren** (also referred to in one text as **C. W. Warren**) was the head of the Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle, a group that intervened at Borley Rectory during the height of the poltergeist disturbances during the Foyster incumbency. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following specific facts, dates, events, contradictions, and quotes regarding his involvement:

**Biographical Details and Contradictions in Name**
*   **Profession and Residence:** George Warren was a **79-year-old grocer and postmaster** residing in Marks Tey, a village located eight miles from Borley [1]. He was a local tradesman [2].
*   **The Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle:** Warren and his wife, Emily, were the leaders and the only actual Marks Tey residents of a loose-knit group of East Anglian spiritualists known as the Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle [1, 3]. They held regular séances in the back of the post office in Station Road [1]. He had previously investigated several cases of haunting and had generally been successful in bringing them to an end [1].
*   **Name Contradiction:** While most historical accounts identify him as "George Warren" [1], investigator Harry Price, quoting Captain V. M. Deane (a member of the Circle), specifically identifies him as **"Mr C. W. Warren"** [2]. 

**Initial Contact and the Conditions of Intervention (January 1932)**
In January 1932, having heard about the severe disturbances at Borley Rectory, Warren wrote to the Rev. Lionel Foyster offering his help, assuring him that the "whole trouble could be removed" [1]. 

Because Marianne Foyster was tired of being accused of faking the phenomena by psychic investigators, Lionel initially declined the offer [4, 5]. However, Warren sent a very polite reply insisting that his only desire was to help [4, 5]. Foyster relented, but laid down a strict condition: **if Warren concluded that Marianne was responsible for the phenomena, he must not openly accuse her, but simply state that Borley was a case in which he was unable to help** [5].

**The January 1932 Visits**
Warren's intervention began dramatically just after midnight on **January 22, 1932**, when he and a colleague, H. H. Frost, arrived unexpectedly at the Rectory [6, 7]. They claimed they had received an urgent message from "W. T. Stead"—the leader of their spirit "helpers on the other side"—warning them that Marianne’s spirit protectors could not hold out much longer [6-8]. 

They arranged for a full séance the following day. On **January 23**, Warren returned with his wife Emily (who brought a planchette board) and other members, including a medium named Guy L'Estrange [9-11]. The night was incredibly violent, featuring bottles dashing down the stairs, broken glass strewn in the kitchen passage, and bells ringing [12, 13]. The party stayed until 5:00 a.m. [12, 13].

**Specific Paranormal Experiences Involving Warren**
Warren was directly involved in several striking supernatural events at the Rectory:
*   **Physical Attack by an Entity:** During a séance on the night of January 23–24, an evil entity reportedly targeted him. The official report states that **"a determined attempt was made by the entity on Mr. [Warren] who protected himself with light but experienced intense fear and horror for a short time"** [14].
*   **Seeing a Ghostly Investigator (June 1932):** During a subsequent visit in June 1932, while the group was sitting in the Rectory study, Warren claimed to see the **ghost of a well-known psychic investigator in this life**, an apparition that no one else in the room could see [15, 16].
*   **Acting as a Medium (April 26, 1932):** Although he often brought other mediums to Borley, Warren himself acted as the "principal psychic" during a séance on April 26, 1932 [2]. He went into a trance and was controlled by the spirit of a nun who identified herself as "Evangeline Westcott" [17, 18]. Assuming her personality, Warren made the sign of the Cross, indicated her veil and rosary, and used a planchette to write: **"Virgin! Do forgive me! Oh, Mother of Jesus, help me"** [17]. After being comforted by the sitters, Warren buried his face in a bowl of primroses, smiled, and relinquished control [17]. 

**Later Life and Death**
Following the interventions by Warren and his circle, the violent poltergeist phenomena at Borley Rectory were reported to have abruptly ceased for nearly two years [18, 19]. Warren and his colleagues believed their efforts had successfully laid the ghosts and took credit for restoring peace to the house [19]. 

Tragically, this victory came at a great personal cost to Warren. Following his visits to the Rectory, his health rapidly declined. It is recorded that he **"soon submitted to illness that he attributed to evil spirits from Borley"**, and he eventually died from this condition [19].
