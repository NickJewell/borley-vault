# George Warren

Seventy-nine-year-old grocer and postmaster at **Marks Tey**, Essex; head of the [[marks-tey-spiritualist-circle|Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle]] that conducted the famous January 1932 séance at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] — after which the poltergeist activity largely ceased.

## Background

Warren and his wife [[emily-warren|Emily]] ran the post office at Station Road, Marks Tey, a village eight miles from Borley. Behind the shop was a back room where their group of spiritualist friends — drawn from across East Anglia — met for séances. Warren had investigated several cases of haunting and generally been successful in resolving them.

## Approach to the Foysters

In January 1932, Warren wrote to [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]] offering his help, having heard about the phenomena at the Rectory. Lionel declined initially, but Warren persisted with a second polite letter. Lionel agreed on one condition: Warren must not openly accuse his wife of being responsible. If Warren concluded that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] was the cause, he should simply say it was a case in which he was unable to help.

A date was arranged, but Warren, [[hf-frost|H.F. Frost]] and [[captain-deane|Captain Deane]] arrived unexpectedly after midnight on 22 January, telling Lionel that the "helpers on the other side" had warned them that Marianne's spirit protectors could not hold out much longer.

## The January 1932 Séance

The following night — 23 January — the full Marks Tey Circle arrived, including [[guy-lestrange|Guy L'Estrange]] as the medium, Emily Warren, Frost, Deane, and a **Mr Footman**. Emily Warren brought a planchette. After the séance in the Blue Room, L'Estrange — channelling a spirit he identified as the Borley nun, who named herself **Evangeline Westcott** — said he was confident there would be no more disturbances.

The next morning, the Foysters noted the house felt "entirely different" — warmer, changed. The maid Katie also noticed. The poltergeist activity did indeed largely cease.

Warren led the group with a steady, considerate manner that the Foysters found very different from the "bombastic" Harry Price and Mollie Goldney. The Foysters "really took to" the Marks Tey group.
