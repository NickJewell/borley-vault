# George Warren

Seventy-nine-year-old grocer and postmaster at **Marks Tey**, Essex; head of the [[marks-tey-spiritualist-circle|Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle]] that conducted the famous January 1932 séance at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] — after which the poltergeist activity largely ceased.

> **Contradiction:** While most historical accounts identify him as "George Warren," [[harry-price|Harry Price]], quoting Captain V. M. Deane (a member of the Circle), specifically identifies him as **"Mr C. W. Warren"** [2].

## Background

Warren and his wife [[emily-warren|Emily]] were the leaders and the only actual Marks Tey residents of a loose-knit group of East Anglian spiritualists known as the [[marks-tey-spiritualist-circle|Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle]]. They held regular séances in the back of the post office in Station Road, Marks Tey, a village eight miles from Borley [1, 3]. Warren had investigated several cases of haunting and had generally been successful in bringing them to an end [1].

## Approach to the Foysters

In **January 1932**, having heard about the severe disturbances at Borley Rectory, Warren wrote to [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]] offering his help, assuring him that the "whole trouble could be removed" [1]. Because [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] was tired of being accused of faking the phenomena, Lionel initially declined [4, 5]. However, Warren sent a very polite reply insisting that his only desire was to help [4, 5]. Foyster relented on one condition: **if Warren concluded that Marianne was responsible for the phenomena, he must not openly accuse her, but simply state that Borley was a case in which he was unable to help** [5].

## The January 1932 Visits

Warren's intervention began dramatically just after midnight on **January 22, 1932**, when he and a colleague, [[hf-frost|H.F. Frost]], arrived unexpectedly at the Rectory, claiming they had received an urgent message from "W. T. Stead" — the leader of their spirit "helpers on the other side" — warning them that Marianne's spirit protectors could not hold out much longer [6–8].

They arranged for a full séance the following day. On **January 23**, Warren returned with his wife Emily (who brought a planchette board) and other members, including medium [[guy-lestrange|Guy L'Estrange]] and [[captain-deane|Captain Deane]] [9–11]. The night was incredibly violent, featuring bottles dashing down the stairs, broken glass strewn in the kitchen passage, and bells ringing; the party stayed until 5:00 a.m. [12, 13].

The Foysters found Warren's steady, considerate manner very different from the "bombastic" [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and [[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]]. The Foysters "really took to" the Marks Tey group.

## Paranormal Experiences Involving Warren

Warren was directly involved in several striking supernatural events at the Rectory:

- **Physical Attack by an Entity:** During the séance of January 23–24, an evil entity reportedly targeted him. The official report states that **"a determined attempt was made by the entity on Mr. [Warren] who protected himself with light but experienced intense fear and horror for a short time"** [14].
- **Seeing a Ghostly Investigator (June 1932):** During a subsequent visit in June 1932, while the group was sitting in the Rectory study, Warren claimed to see the **ghost of a well-known psychic investigator in this life**, an apparition that no one else in the room could see (single witness) [15, 16].
- **Acting as a Medium (April 26, 1932):** Warren himself acted as the "principal psychic" during a séance on April 26, 1932 [2]. He went into a trance and was reportedly controlled by the spirit of a nun who identified herself as "Evangeline Westcott." Assuming her personality, Warren made the sign of the Cross, indicated her veil and rosary, and used a planchette to write: **"Virgin! Do forgive me! Oh, Mother of Jesus, help me"** [17]. After being comforted by the sitters, Warren buried his face in a bowl of primroses, smiled, and relinquished control [17].

## Aftermath and Death

The morning after the January 1932 séance, the Foysters noted the house felt "entirely different" — warmer, changed. The maid Katie also noticed. The poltergeist activity did indeed largely cease for a considerable period.

Following his visits to the Rectory, Warren's health reportedly rapidly declined. It is recorded that he **"soon submitted to illness that he attributed to evil spirits from Borley"**, and he eventually died from this condition [19].
