# Katie (Foysters' Maid)

A young maid employed by the Foysters at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], named only as "Katie" in the sources. She was a local girl, approximately 14 years old, hired in **September 1931** after an earlier maid had fled upon hearing the ghost stories — breaking a period during which the Foysters had been without any resident domestic help. [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] sought a younger, less experienced girl she could train herself. The two got on very well immediately. Katie had been in the household "only a week or so" when she witnessed one of the most dramatic poltergeist incidents of the entire Foyster period. She is listed by name in both Appendix D and Appendix E of *The Most Haunted House in England*.

> **Note:** Only her first name is recorded. She must not be confused with the legendary "[[kate-boreham|Katie Boreham]]," a maid from 1888 whose name emerged during later séances. Katie's surname, age beyond "young," and later history are unknown from the sources.

## Arrival and Character

When [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] explained the strange occurrences in the house to her, Katie reportedly declared bravely that if Mrs. Foyster and young [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide]] weren't afraid, then she wasn't either. Despite this brave front, her coping mechanism for sleeping in the "most haunted house in England" was to bury her head under the bedclothes every night and leave her lamp burning until she fell asleep.

## The "Missing Potatoes" Incident (September 26, 1931)

Katie was directly involved in a bizarre vanishing-object phenomenon on the evening of a total lunar eclipse. She and Marianne were preparing dinner together in the kitchen when [[lionel-foyster|Lionel]] invited them into the library to view the blood-red eclipse. Upon returning, **Katie took the lid off a saucepan of boiling potatoes on the stove, only to discover it was completely empty**. When a stunned Katie reported this to her mistress, Marianne merely roared with laughter and joked: **"You must have eaten them yourself, Katie!"**

## The November 13 Incident

On the evening of Friday, November 13, 1931, [[dom-richard-whitehouse]] was staying at the Rectory while [[lionel-foyster]] was delayed in London by fog. After the children were put to bed, Katie, [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]], and [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard]] withdrew to the kitchen with doors and windows closed.

Dom Richard describes what Katie witnessed alongside him and Marianne:

> "Without warning of any kind, there was a sudden crash right underneath her chair, a bottle bursting into fragments on the stone floor beneath her. A quarter of an hour or so later, precisely the same thing happened to me, as I was drawing up a small chair to sit down to my supper. Katie was kept busy sweeping up the broken glass and looked quite mystified, having been in the house only a week or so."

Most significantly, all three witnesses together — Katie, Marianne, and Dom Richard — saw a bottle rise into the air:

> "Suddenly, before our very eyes, a bottle poised itself in mid-air within a foot or so of the kitchen ceiling. It remained here for a second or two and then fell with a crash on the floor before us. I repeat that all three of us witnessed this incident."

Dom Richard describes the full sequence: a bottle rolled from the top of the back stairs into the kitchen without breaking; small china struck his shoulder; more objects were thrown; the bells in the passage rang repeatedly; and the large yard bell also rang out sharply.

## Marianne's Later Recollections

In her 1958 interview with investigator Robert Swanson, Marianne provided some dismissive details about Katie that slightly contradict the established timeline:

- Marianne described her as "just quite a child... and not very good at anything."
- Marianne claimed Katie **only stayed for about four or five weeks because "she got lonesome and went home,"** insisting it was not because she was frightened.

> **Contradiction:** If Katie was hired in September 1931 and was present for the exploding bottles on November 13, her employment lasted slightly longer than the four to five weeks Marianne later recalled.

Marianne also explicitly pointed out that because Katie was employed "long after the appearance of the wall writings," the young maid could not have been responsible for the mysterious graffiti that had appeared on the Rectory walls earlier that year.

Aside from experiencing the supernatural phenomena, Katie's other recorded duties included occasionally taking the children to local parties.

## Significance

Katie is a key witness because she was a complete newcomer to the household with no prior investment in the haunting's history, and her reaction — "quite mystified" — is consistent with genuine novelty. The bottle-in-mid-air incident is one of the few Borley phenomena witnessed simultaneously by three independent named persons, all of whom are on record (multiple witnesses). She also appears in Appendix D as a witness to bell-ringing, footsteps, and knocks — indicating her exposure to phenomena extended beyond this one dramatic night.
