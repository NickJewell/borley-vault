# Katie (Foysters' Maid)

A young maid employed by the Foysters at Borley Rectory, named only as "Katie" in the sources. She had been in the household "only a week or so" when she witnessed one of the most dramatic poltergeist incidents of the entire Foyster period. Listed by name in both Appendix D and Appendix E of *The Most Haunted House in England*.

## The November 13 Incident

On the evening of Friday, November 13, 1931, [[dom-richard-whitehouse]] was staying at the Rectory while [[lionel-foyster]] was in London on business. Katie, [[marianne-foyster]], and Dom Richard had taken refuge in the kitchen with doors and windows closed.

Dom Richard describes what Katie witnessed alongside him and Marianne:

> "Without warning of any kind, there was a sudden crash right underneath her chair, a bottle bursting into fragments on the stone floor beneath her. A quarter of an hour or so later, precisely the same thing happened to me, as I was drawing up a small chair to sit down to my supper. Katie was kept busy sweeping up the broken glass and looked quite mystified, having been in the house only a week or so."

Most significantly, all three witnesses together — Katie, Marianne, and Dom Richard — saw a bottle rise into the air:

> "Suddenly, before our very eyes, a bottle poised itself in mid-air within a foot or so of the kitchen ceiling. It remained here for a second or two and then fell with a crash on the floor before us. I repeat that all three of us witnessed this incident."

Dom Richard describes the sequence: a bottle rolled from the top of the back stairs into the kitchen without breaking; small china struck his shoulder; more objects were thrown; the bells in the passage rang repeatedly; and the large yard bell also rang out sharply.

## Significance

Katie is a key witness because she was a complete newcomer to the household with no prior investment in the haunting's history, and her reaction — "quite mystified" — is consistent with genuine novelty. The bottle-in-mid-air incident is one of the few Borley phenomena witnessed simultaneously by three independent named persons, all of whom are on record.

She also appears in Appendix D as a witness to bell-ringing, footsteps, and knocks — indicating her exposure to phenomena extended beyond this one dramatic night.

> **Note:** Only her first name is recorded. Her surname, age beyond "young," and later history are unknown from the MHH source.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Katie** (whose surname is not recorded in the texts) was a young local girl employed as a resident maid at Borley Rectory during the incumbency of the Rev. Lionel and Marianne Foyster. The sources emphasize that she must not be confused with the legendary "Katie Boreham," a maid from 1888 whose name emerged during later séances [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, and biographical details regarding the Foysters' maid:

**Hiring and Coping with the "Haunting"**
Katie was hired in **September 1931**, breaking a period during which the Foysters had been without any resident domestic help [2]. After an earlier maid had fled upon hearing the ghost stories, Marianne Foyster sought a younger, less experienced girl whom she could train herself, eventually finding the local 14-year-old Katie [3]. 
*   The two got on very well immediately [3]. 
*   When Marianne explained the strange occurrences in the house to her, **Katie bravely declared that if Mrs. Foyster and young Adelaide weren't afraid, then she wasn't either** [3]. 
*   Despite this brave front, her coping mechanism for sleeping in the "most haunted house in England" was to bury her head under the bedclothes every night and leave her lamp burning until she fell asleep [3].

**The "Missing Potatoes" Incident (September 26, 1931)**
Katie was directly involved in a bizarre vanishing object phenomenon on the evening of a total lunar eclipse. 
*   Katie and Marianne were preparing dinner together in the kitchen when Lionel invited them into the library to view the blood-red eclipse [4]. 
*   Upon returning to the kitchen, **Katie took the lid off a saucepan of boiling potatoes on the stove, only to discover it was completely empty** [2, 4]. 
*   When a stunned Katie reported this to her mistress, Marianne merely roared with laughter and joked, **"You must have eaten them yourself, Katie!"** [4]. 

**The Exploding Bottles Incident (Friday, November 13, 1931)**
Katie was a primary witness to one of the most violent and famous poltergeist episodes of the Foyster incumbency. 
*   With Lionel delayed in London by fog, the young priest Edwin (Richard) Whitehouse was keeping an eye on the household [1]. 
*   After the children were put to bed, Katie, Marianne, and Edwin withdrew to the kitchen [1]. 
*   Suddenly, **a bottle materialized from nowhere and exploded with a loud crash beneath Marianne's chair**, scattering glass everywhere [1]. 
*   As a mystified Katie swept up the shards, **another bottle dashed itself to pieces on the floor, resulting in more sweeping up for the young maid** [1]. Another bottle then rolled through the door without breaking [1]. *(Note: In one account, Edwin Whitehouse noted that the bottle poised itself in mid-air near the ceiling before crashing, an event he claimed all three of them witnessed [5]).*

**Marianne's Later Recollections and Contradictions**
In her 1958 interview with the investigator Robert Swanson, Marianne Foyster provided some dismissive details about Katie that slightly contradict the established timeline of her stay:
*   Marianne described her as "just quite a child... and not very good at anything" [6].
*   Marianne claimed Katie **only stayed for about four or five weeks because "she got lonesome and went home,"** insisting it was not because she was frightened by the ghosts [6]. *(Contradiction: If Katie was hired in September 1931 and was present for the exploding bottles on November 13, her employment lasted slightly longer than the 4-5 weeks Marianne later recalled).*
*   Marianne also explicitly pointed out that because **Katie was employed "long after the appearance of the wall writings"**, the young maid could not have been responsible for the mysterious graffiti that plagued the Rectory walls earlier that year [6, 7]. 

Aside from experiencing the supernatural phenomena, Katie's other recorded duties included occasionally taking the children out to local parties, which she did on one unspecified date while Marianne was sick in bed [8].
