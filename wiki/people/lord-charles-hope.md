# Lord Charles Hope (20 February 1892 – 1962)

Member of the aristocracy; the Eton-educated second son of John Adrian Louis Hope, the First Marquess of Linlithgow and Deputy-Lieutenant of West Lothian. An amateur champion golfer. One of the early investigators at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] who visited in July 1929, shortly after [[harry-price]] first arrived. His *contemporary* handwritten notes from the time, preserved in the SPR files, constitute the most important corroboration of [[charles-sutton]]'s fraud accusation against Price — though those notes have subsequently been disputed.

Hope was a dignified, long-time member of the [[spr|Society for Psychical Research]], serving as a Council Member and financial backer for [[harry-price|Harry Price's]] [[national-laboratory|National Laboratory of Psychical Research]]. He participated in a 1934 broadcast series on psychical research called *"Inquiry Into the Unknown"* alongside author Gerald Heard and Sir Oliver Lodge.

## The July 1929 Visits

Lord Charles Hope visited [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] on two separate occasions: **July 5–6, 1929** (with Price and [[lucie-kaye|Lucy Kaye]]) and **July 28–29, 1929** (when he returned without Price). He was 37 years old at the time.

**5 July visit:** During this first visit, Hope witnessed a shower of keys and the appearance of a St. Ignatius medal. He noticed exposed bell wires in the passage outside the Smiths' bedroom, surmising that a person could easily ring the bells by pulling them. He suspected the young maid, [[mary-pearson|Mary Pearson]], of playing tricks, but also formed "the definite suspicion that Mr Price might be responsible for some at least of the phenomena which occurred while I was present." Price famously quoted Hope as challenging the poltergeist: **"If they want to impress us, let them give us a phenomenon now"** — which was immediately followed by a loud bell clanging.

His suspicions of Price were inflamed by an incident involving a loose piece of linoleum, seen in the bathroom, that appeared on the stairs shortly after the party left the bathroom — with Price at the rear of the group. Price attempted to shift blame by arguing that Hope had been the last to leave the bathroom. However, [[lucie-kaye|Lucy Kaye]] corroborated Hope's assertion that Price had actually brought up the rear. Hope noted that Price appeared visibly "displeased" by this, and later wrote that the incident **"struck me as especially unfavourable."**

**29 July visit:** This was the occasion on which [[charles-sutton|Charles Sutton]] of the *Daily Mail* was also present.

## Contemporary Notes — The Sutton Incident

Hope's notes from shortly after the July 29 visit record:

> "Miss Kaye said that last Friday when she was present at Borley with Price and correspondent of *Daily Mail* — Sutton — they were standing outside in the garden when a bit of glass was detached... A large half brick was thrown crashing downstairs. Miss Kaye said H.P. gave a shudder as this happened but that Sutton accused H.P. of having thrown it and he had to swear by everything that he had not done so."

Subsequently, Hope visited Sutton at Hope's own flat, and Sutton told him:

> "He had quite clearly seen Mr Price throw a large stone (at the top of the stairs, I think) that he had there and then accused him and made him turn out his pockets in which there were several similar stones. He explained he had not been able to publish this incident as his editor had feared a libel action, as there was no witness other than Miss Kaye."

## Written Request to Price (1932)

Having lost faith in Price's investigation, Hope wrote a stern letter to Price on **April 1, 1932**: **"I would be obliged if you could find it possible not to mention my name in connection with your 'Borley Rectory' lecture on Wednesday, or if you do, please say I was not impressed and thought the phenomena were produced by normal means."** Price did not invite Hope to participate in his later investigations beginning in 1937.

## The Split with Harry Price

The relationship ultimately fractured into lifelong hostility. The final, bitter break occurred over the Austrian medium [[rudi-schneider|Rudi Schneider]]. Hope arranged to test Schneider himself under the auspices of the SPR without informing Price, who had managed Schneider's experiments for years. Price viewed this as a "filthy trick," and the two men became fierce enemies, with Hope transferring his loyalty entirely to the SPR.

## Evidence to the SPR

Lord Charles made his notes and subsequent correspondence available to the SPR investigators. He also arranged for [[kathleen-goldney|KMG]] to meet [[charles-sutton|Charles Sutton]] at luncheon in January 1949 to hear Sutton's story in full.

**Quotes on Other Witnesses**

- **On the Bull sisters:** Visiting the surviving Bull sisters at Chilton Lodge in 1929, he remarked sarcastically to [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] in 1956: **"Well, whatever criticisms are levelled at the Bull sisters, they can never be accused of changing their story!"**
- **On Mabel Smith:** When [[mabel-smith|Mrs. Mabel Smith]] later claimed she had never believed the Rectory was haunted, Hope wrote to [[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]] in June 1949: **"I can't understand her present point of view as, at the time, I was so sorry for her as she seemed so scared... (but perhaps she too saw through Price later)."**

## Contradictions in the Borley Record

> **Contradiction:** Although Hope claimed his 1949 submission was based on his "contemporary notes" from 1929, researcher [[robert-hastings|Robert J. Hastings]] later demonstrated the notes were written from memory *twenty years later*, immediately after Hope had attended a 1949 luncheon with Sutton. Hastings pointed out several errors in Hope's memory, concluding that Hope had unconsciously incorporated Sutton's 1949 claims into his own "recollections."

[[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] also noted a contradiction in Hope's methodology: while Hope frequently lectured SPR members on the absolute necessity for precise, detailed information, "on his own evidence, he relied on undated notes on scraps of paper, hearsay, and uncorroborated evidence when seeking to discount the Borley phenomena in the SPR Report."
