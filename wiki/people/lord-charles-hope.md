# Lord Charles Hope

Member of the aristocracy; one of the early investigators at Borley Rectory who visited in July 1929, shortly after [[harry-price]] first arrived. His *contemporary* handwritten notes from the time, preserved in the SPR files, constitute the most important corroboration of [[charles-sutton]]'s fraud accusation against Price.

## The July 1929 Visits

Lord Charles Hope visited Borley Rectory on two separate occasions: 5 July and 29 July 1929. His notes were handwritten at the time and were later made available to the SPR investigators.

**5 July visit:** Hope was present when various "phenomena" occurred. His notes record that he formed "the definite suspicion that Mr Price might be responsible for some at least of the phenomena which occurred while I was present." He specifically noted the incident of a loose piece of linoleum, seen in the bathroom, that appeared on the stairs shortly after the party left the bathroom — with Price at the rear of the group.

**29 July visit:** This was the occasion on which [[charles-sutton]] of the *Daily Mail* was also present. Miss Kaye's subsequent account (recorded in Hope's notes) describes Sutton having accused Price "to his face" and "made him turn out his pockets in which there were several similar stones." Miss Kaye recommended Hope see Sutton directly.

## Contemporary Notes — The Sutton Incident

Hope's notes from shortly after the July 29 visit record:

> "Miss Kaye said that last Friday when she was present at Borley with Price and correspondent of *Daily Mail* — Sutton — they were standing outside in the garden when a bit of glass was detached... A large half brick was thrown crashing downstairs. Miss Kaye said H.P. gave a shudder as this happened but that Sutton accused H.P. of having thrown it and he had to swear by everything that he had not done so."

Subsequently, Hope visited Sutton at Hope's own flat, and Sutton told him:

> "He had quite clearly seen Mr Price throw a large stone (at the top of the stairs, I think) that he had there and then accused him and made him turn out his pockets in which there were several similar stones. He explained he had not been able to publish this incident as his editor had feared a libel action, as there was no witness other than Miss Kaye."

## Written Request to Price (1932)

In April 1932, Lord Charles wrote to Price: "I would be obliged if you could find it possible not to mention my name in connection with your 'Borley Rectory' lecture on Wednesday, or if you do, please say I was not impressed and thought the phenomena were produced by normal means."

Price did not invite Hope to participate in his later and fuller investigations beginning in 1937.

## Evidence to the SPR

Lord Charles made his notes and subsequent correspondence available to the SPR investigators. He also arranged for KMG ([[kathleen-goldney]]) to meet [[charles-sutton]] at luncheon in January 1949 to hear Sutton's story in full. He furnished signed notes on his own experiences for the SPR file.

## Significance

The contemporary nature of Hope's notes — written at the time of the events, not reconstructed from memory years later — gives his evidence particular weight as corroboration for Sutton's accusation. The SPR used Hope's notes extensively in building its case that Price produced fraudulent phenomena.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

Based on the Borley sources, Lord Charles Hope was a prominent, highly connected, and ultimately skeptical investigator who played a critical role in the early investigations of Borley Rectory. His involvement is characterized by his initial collaboration with Harry Price, his swift detection of suspected fraud, and a subsequent bitter fallout that heavily influenced posthumous criticisms of Price's work. 

Here are the specific facts, events, biographical details, quotes, and contradictions recorded about him suitable for a thorough reference article:

**Lesser-Known Biographical Details**
*   **Background:** Born on February 20, 1892, Lord Charles Hope was the Eton-educated second son of John Adrian Louis Hope, the First Marquess of Linlithgow and Deputy-Lieutenant of West Lothian [1, 2]. He died in 1962 [1]. 
*   **Personal Life:** Aside from his work in psychical research, he was an amateur champion golfer [2]. 
*   **Psychical Research:** He was a dignified, long-time member of the Society for Psychical Research (SPR), but also served as a Council Member and financial backer for Harry Price's National Laboratory of Psychical Research (NLPR) [1-3]. He participated in a 1934 broadcast series on psychical research called *"Inquiry Into the Unknown"* alongside author Gerald Heard and Sir Oliver Lodge [3].

**The 1929 Borley Visits and Early Suspicions**
Lord Charles visited Borley Rectory with Harry Price and Price's secretary, Lucy Kaye, on **July 5–6, 1929**, and returned again without Price (who was ill) on **July 28–29, 1929** [4, 5]. He was 37 years old at the time [2].
*   **Witnessing Phenomena:** During his first visit, he witnessed a shower of keys and the appearance of a St. Ignatius medal bearing Latin words and the head of a monk [6, 7]. Price famously quoted Hope as challenging the poltergeist: **"If they want to impress us, let them give us a phenomenon now"**, which was immediately followed by a loud bell clanging [8].
*   **Suspecting the Maid and Price:** From his very first evening at the Rectory, Hope was deeply skeptical. He noticed exposed bell wires in the passage outside the Smiths' bedroom, surmising that a person could easily ring the bells by pulling them [9, 10]. He suspected the young maid, Mary Pearson, of playing tricks, but also **suspected Harry Price of actively faking the phenomena** [11].
*   **The Linoleum Incident:** Hope’s suspicions of Price were inflamed by an incident involving a piece of linoleum. A loose square of linoleum seen in the bathroom was shortly afterward found propped on the main stairs. Price attempted to shift the blame by arguing that Hope had been the last to leave the bathroom. However, Lucy Kaye corroborated Hope's assertion that Price had actually brought up the rear. Hope noted that Price appeared visibly "displeased" by this, and Hope later wrote that the incident **"struck me as especially unfavourable"** [10, 12, 13].

**The Split with Harry Price**
The relationship between Hope and Price ultimately fractured into lifelong hostility.
*   **The 1932 Ultimatum:** Having lost faith in Price's Borley investigation, Hope wrote a stern letter to Price on **April 1, 1932**, demanding to be disassociated from the case: **"I would be obliged if you could find it possible not to mention my name in connection with your 'Borley Rectory' lecture on Wednesday, or if you do, please say I was not impressed and thought the phenomena were produced by normal means"** [12, 14]. Consequently, Price did not invite Hope to participate in his 1937–1938 tenancy investigations [12].
*   **The Rudi Schneider Rift:** The final, bitter break between the two men occurred over the Austrian medium Rudi Schneider. Hope arranged to test Schneider himself under the auspices of the SPR without informing Price, who had managed Schneider's experiments for years and exchanged thousands of letters with him [15, 16]. Price viewed this as a "filthy trick," and the two men became fierce enemies, with Hope transferring his loyalty entirely to the SPR [17, 18].

**Quotes and Opinions on Other Witnesses**
*   **On the Bull Sisters:** Hope visited the surviving Bull sisters at Chilton Lodge in 1929. Decades later, in 1956, he sarcastically remarked to the investigator Peter Underwood: **"Well, whatever criticisms are levelled at the Bull sisters, they can never be accused of changing their story!"** [16, 19].
*   **On Mabel Smith:** When Mrs. Mabel Smith claimed years later that she had never believed the Rectory was haunted, Hope was stunned. He wrote to SPR investigator Mollie Goldney in June 1949: **"If you like, do quote me to Mrs. Smith as saying that I can't understand her present point of view as, at the time, I was so sorry for her as she seemed so scared... (but perhaps she too saw through Price later)"** [20].

**Contradictions in the Borley Record Regarding Hope**
*   **The Charles Sutton Contradiction:** Hope's notes were used extensively in the highly critical 1956 *Borley Report* to support claims by journalist Charles Sutton that Sutton had caught Price throwing stones in the Rectory. Hope submitted notes claiming that, shortly after the 1929 incident, Lucy Kaye had confessed to him that Price had suffered a heart attack because Sutton had caught him cheating [21, 22]. However, researcher Robert J. Hastings later exposed a major contradiction in Hope's testimony: Although Hope claimed his 1949 submission was based on his "contemporary notes" from 1929, Hastings proved the notes were written from memory *twenty years later*, immediately after Hope had attended a 1949 luncheon with Sutton [23]. Hastings pointed out several errors in Hope's memory, concluding that Hope had unconsciously incorporated Sutton's 1949 claims into his own "recollections" [23].
*   **Hypocrisy on Evidence:** Peter Underwood pointed out a contradiction in Hope's methodology as an investigator. While Hope frequently lectured SPR members on the absolute necessity for precise, detailed information, Underwood noted that **"on his own evidence, he relied on undated notes on scraps of paper, hearsay, and uncorroborated evidence when seeking to discount the Borley phenomena in the SPR Report"** [24].
