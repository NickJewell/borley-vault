# Margaret Finch (d. 4 June 2002, aged 97)

Wife of [[alfred-finch|Alfred Finch]]; served as a **resident maid at Borley Rectory during the 1920s** under [[harry-bull|Rev Harry Bull]]. She died on 4 June 2002 aged 97, while *The Final Analysis* was in advanced preparation; the authors record her death in the preface and sent condolences.

## Testimony on the Rectory in the Bulls' Time

Her first-hand knowledge of the rectory in its prime is unique among documented witnesses:

- The rectory was "**a lovely house, kept in excellent condition**" — completely contradicting the gloomy image it acquired later
- "Overfilled with furniture and pictures by the standards of today, but this was fairly typical of affluent Victorian and Edwardian residences"
- Harry Bull had separate bedroom from Ivy; she describes him as a "slightly strange man who seemed to live in his own dream world"
- Ivy Bull always wore clothes that rustled; came downstairs backwards to check stair-dusting — "once a nurse, always a nurse"
- Harry Bull took his last surviving cat to church with him
- Mr Brown, the head gardener, lived in the rectory cottage and would make faces through the dining-room windows during morning prayers to try to make the servants laugh

## The Christmas Parties

She recalled the annual Christmas parties for parishioners at the rectory: tobacco for the men, sweets for the children. Hubert Bull pushed her mother's pram up Hall Lane unprompted.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Margaret Finch** (who died on June 4, 2002, at the age of 97) is recorded in the Borley sources as a vital, first-hand witness to the everyday life at Borley Rectory during the 1920s and beyond [1, 2]. Alongside her husband, Alfred Finch, she provided researchers with a wealth of detailed, pragmatic information that sharply contrasted with the sensational ghost stories surrounding the property [1-3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following biographical details, specific memories, and her firm stance on the hauntings:

**Biographical Details and Life in Borley**
Margaret and her husband Alfred lived for most of their long lives in a semi-detached cottage at Borley Green [4]. The 1935 electoral roll lists them as electors 23 and 24, living "near the Green" [5]. They had a daughter, Kathleen (later Kathleen King) [6], and Margaret had a small baby in the early 1930s [7]. During the 1920s, Margaret was employed as a resident domestic servant (maid) by the Rev. Harry Foyster Bull, living in the Rectory alongside another young female servant [2, 3]. 

**Her Stance on the "Hauntings"**
Margaret Finch’s testimony provides a major contradiction to the popular Borley mythology. Despite working as a live-in maid during the 1920s, she was **adamant that nothing unusual ever occurred there** [3]. She stated that she heard absolutely no talk of ghosts from the Rev. Harry Bull, and she was not even aware at the time she lived there that the Rectory was supposed to be haunted [3]. 

**Recollections of the Bull Family (1920s)**
Margaret shared several intimate, and sometimes amusing, details about the eccentric Bull household:
*   **The Rectory’s Condition:** In contrast to descriptions of the house being gloomy or decayed, Margaret was "very definite" that during her time there, the Rectory was a **"lovely house and was kept in excellent condition"** [2]. She noted that the grounds were kept immaculate by two full-time gardeners [8].
*   **The Rev. Harry Bull:** She described him as a **"slightly strange man who seemed to live in his own dream world all the time"** [9]. 
*   **Morning Prayers and Practical Jokes:** Harry Bull would hold morning prayer services for the servants in the dining room, standing with his back to the windows. Margaret recalled that the head gardener, Mr. Brown, would frequently appear outside the windows and "indulge in various antics" behind the Rector's back to try and make the servants laugh [10].
*   **Ivy Bull (Harry’s Wife):** Margaret remembered Ivy primarily for two quirks: she always wore clothes that rustled, and she had a habit of **"coming down the stairs backwards in order to see that they had been dusted properly"** [11]. 
*   **Family Tensions:** She noted the poor relationship between Harry Bull and his stepdaughter, Constance Brackenbury, recalling how Constance would make faces at her stepfather behind his back, and once amused the servants by picking up his waistcoat with tongs, implying it was unfit to touch with bare hands [10].
*   **Christmas Treats:** Margaret and Alfred fondly remembered the annual Christmas party the Bulls threw for the villagers, dispensing gifts of tobacco for the men and sweets for the children [12].

**Encounters with Later Rectors**
Margaret remained in Borley through subsequent incumbencies and remembered the later figures well:
*   **The Smiths (1928–1929):** She and Alfred remembered the Rev. Guy Eric Smith and his wife, Mabel, very well during their brief stay [13].
*   **The Foysters (1930–1935):** Margaret described the Rev. Lionel Foyster as a very tall man who was bent over and crippled by arthritis [7]. Although she found him kindly, she had a specific disagreement with him: he once remonstrated with her for not attending church. Margaret explained that she had a small baby whom she could not leave alone, nor could she bring the baby to church where it might cry and disrupt the service. Foyster brushed this off as a mere excuse, insisting it wouldn't matter if the baby was noisy. Margaret, however, **"was not prepared to take the risk and remained firm about the matter"** [7, 14]. She and Alfred also remembered the Rector's young, vivacious wife, Marianne, very well [4].
*   **The Hennings (1936 onwards):** Margaret and her husband, along with their daughter Kathleen, were also well acquainted with the next rector, the Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning, and his wife [6]. 

**Observations on the Coopers**
Finally, Margaret and Alfred remembered Mr. and Mrs. Edward Cooper—who lived in the Rectory cottage from 1916 to 1920 and claimed to have seen the phantom coach and the ghostly nun. The Finches described the Coopers as being **"sensible and rather religious"** people [15].
