# Margaret Finch (d. 4 June 2002, aged 97)

Wife of [[alfred-finch|Alfred Finch]]; served as a **resident maid at Borley Rectory during the 1920s** under [[harry-bull|Rev Harry Bull]]. She died on 4 June 2002 aged 97, while *The Final Analysis* was in advanced preparation; the authors record her death in the preface and sent condolences.

Margaret and Alfred lived for most of their long lives in a semi-detached cottage at Borley Green [4]. The 1935 electoral roll lists them as electors 23 and 24, living "near the Green" [5]. They had a daughter, [[kathleen-king|Kathleen]] (later Kathleen King) [6]. During the 1920s, Margaret served as a resident domestic maid under the Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Foyster Bull]], living in the Rectory alongside another young female servant [2, 3].

## Testimony on the Rectory in the Bulls' Time

Margaret Finch's first-hand knowledge of the rectory in its prime is unique among documented witnesses. Despite working there as a live-in maid, she was **adamant that nothing unusual ever occurred there** [3]. She stated that she heard absolutely no talk of ghosts from the Rev. Harry Bull and was not even aware at the time that the Rectory was supposed to be haunted [3].

Her recollections of the house itself directly contradict its later gloomy reputation: she was "very definite" that during her time there the Rectory was a **"lovely house and was kept in excellent condition"** [2]. She noted that the grounds were kept immaculate by two full-time gardeners [8].

Specific memories of the Bull household included:

- Harry Bull would hold morning prayer services for the servants in the dining room, standing with his back to the windows; the head gardener, **Mr Brown**, would "indulge in various antics" behind the Rector's back to try and make the servants laugh [10]
- Harry Bull had separate bedroom from Ivy; she described him as a **"slightly strange man who seemed to live in his own dream world all the time"** [9]
- Ivy Bull always wore clothes that rustled and had a habit of **"coming down the stairs backwards in order to see that they had been dusted properly"** — "once a nurse, always a nurse" [11]
- She noted the poor relationship between Harry Bull and his stepdaughter, Constance Brackenbury, recalling how Constance would make faces at her stepfather behind his back, and once amused the servants by picking up his waistcoat with tongs, implying it was unfit to touch with bare hands [10]
- Harry Bull took his last surviving cat to church with him

## The Christmas Parties

Margaret and Alfred fondly recalled the annual Christmas parties the Bulls threw for the villagers, dispensing tobacco for the men and sweets for the children [12]. She also recalled that Hubert Bull pushed her mother's pram up Hall Lane unprompted.

## Encounters with Later Rectors

Margaret remained in Borley through subsequent incumbencies:

- **The Smiths (1928–1929):** She and Alfred remembered [[rev-eric-smith|Rev. Guy Eric Smith]] and his wife [[mabel-smith|Mabel]] very well during their brief stay [13].
- **The Foysters (1930–1935):** Margaret described [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]] as a very tall man who was bent over and crippled by arthritis [7]. Although she found him kindly, she had a specific disagreement with him: he once remonstrated with her for not attending church. Margaret had a small baby she could not leave alone, nor bring to church, but Foyster brushed this off as a mere excuse. Margaret "was not prepared to take the risk and remained firm about the matter" [7, 14]. She and Alfred also remembered the young, vivacious [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] very well [4].
- **The Hennings (1936 onwards):** Margaret, Alfred, and their daughter Kathleen were also well acquainted with [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning]] and his wife [6].

## Observations on the Coopers

Margaret and Alfred remembered [[edward-cooper|Mr and Mrs Edward Cooper]] — who lived in the Rectory cottage from 1916 to 1920 and claimed to have seen the [[phantom-coach|phantom coach]] and the ghostly [[the-nun|nun]]. The Finches described the Coopers as being **"sensible and rather religious"** people [15].
