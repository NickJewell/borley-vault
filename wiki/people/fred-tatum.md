# Fred Tatum

The eventual husband of [[mary-pearson|Mary Pearson]], who had been a maid at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] during the [[rev-eric-smith|Smith]] incumbency. His connection to Borley has been used by critics of the haunting as a possible mundane explanation for some of the apparitions reported during the late 1920s.

## Biographical Details

Tatum is described in the sources as an unemployed visitor and a **"local bad lad who'd been convicted for stealing rabbits"**, who performed odd jobs around the Rectory and its garden [2, 3].

> **Contradiction:** The sources contradict each other on his age. One text describes him as **"about 16 or 17 years of age"** at the time [1]. Another explicitly identifies him as **"25-year-old Fred Tatum"** [3], and a third states he was **"nine years older than the 17 year old Pearson"** — making him approximately 26 [2].

## The "Headless Spook" Pranks

Tatum's most significant contribution to the Borley legend is his role as a suspected earthly cause for several paranormal sightings. Both Fred and Mary were known to join in the fun regarding local ghostly gossip [4]. Tatum would often play the fool by **walking around the Rectory garden with his coat draped over his head, pretending to be a headless spook** — specifically recorded as having done this during a rainstorm [3, 5].

Because of these pranks, skeptics and later investigators heavily suspected that the "headless figure" or even the famous phantom nun reported by witnesses during this period were actually sightings of Fred Tatum [4, 6].

## As Sceptical Explanation

[[mabel-smith|Mrs Smith]], in one of her later accounts, revealed that Fred Tatum was frequently at the Rectory during her time there — though she claimed to have known nothing about it at the time [6]. The suggestion made by critics was that Tatum's unauthorised presence in or around the Rectory grounds might account for some of the "shadowy figures" and unexplained presences reported during the Smith incumbency.

[[harry-price|Harry Price]] noted that [[mary-pearson|Mary Pearson]] had also told him she had seen "a man apparently without a head behind a tree" — and that by 1952 she had "admitted that her statement was untrue," with the implication being that the figure was Tatum, whom she was reluctant to identify at the time.

This explanation cannot account for sightings predating the Smith period, including those attributed to [[harry-bull|Rev. Harry Bull]] — who had also spoken about seeing a headless figure in the garden, and had described it to [[lady-whitehouse|Lady Whitehouse]] and others.

## Events at the Rectory and Mrs. Smith's Testimony

Tatum was present alongside the Smiths and Mary Pearson for several notable events:

- **The Tipped Furniture:** The Smiths visited the Rectory with Fred and Mary and found a piece of furniture mysteriously tipped over on its side, despite the doors and windows being securely locked [7].
- **The Loaded Revolver Incident:** After the Smiths had moved to Long Melford, Mrs. Smith recorded in a signed statement that one of the investigators, an Army man, had a loaded revolver under his coat, prompting her to explicitly warn **"Mary and Fred to keep out of the way"** [8].
- **Investigator Visits:** On the night of [[harry-price|Harry Price's]] first visit (June 12, 1929), Fred went home before the late-night séance [9]. During a subsequent visit by [[lord-charles-hope|Lord Charles Hope]], Fred and Mary were chatting in the kitchen when the drawing-room bell inexplicably rang four times [10].

## Opinion of Harry Price

Fred Tatum held a highly unfavourable view of [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and suspected the investigator of fabricating evidence [11]. In an interview given years later to researcher Mrs. C. Baines, Tatum recounted an incident in the Rectory's scullery when a storm lantern suddenly went out; regarding this event, Tatum stated he was **"strongly of the opinion Price had manipulated the wick"** [11].

## Later Life

Following their time at Borley, Fred Tatum and [[mary-pearson|Mary Pearson]] married secretly [2]. By the 1950s, when interviewed by SPR investigators [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] and [[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]], Fred and Mary were still married, had ten children, and were living in an overcrowded house in Sudbury [12]. Fred became a widower when Mary tragically died following a fall down a flight of stairs in 1966 [11, 13].
