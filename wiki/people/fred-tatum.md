# Fred Tatum

The eventual husband of [[mary-pearson|Mary Pearson]], who had been a maid at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] during the [[rev-eric-smith|Smith]] incumbency. His connection to Borley was used by critics of the haunting as a possible mundane explanation for some of the apparitions reported during the late 1920s.

## As Sceptical Explanation

[[mabel-smith|Mrs Smith]], in one of her later accounts, revealed that Fred Tatum was frequently at the Rectory during her time there — though she claimed to have known nothing about it at the time. The suggestion made by critics was that Tatum's unauthorised presence in or around the Rectory grounds might account for some of the "shadowy figures" and unexplained presences reported during the Smith incumbency. 

[[harry-price|Harry Price]] noted that [[mary-pearson|Mary Pearson]] had also told him she had seen "a man apparently without a head behind a tree" — and that by 1952 she had "admitted that her statement was untrue," with the implication being that the figure was Tatum, whom she was reluctant to identify at the time.

This explanation cannot account for sightings predating the Smith period, including those attributed to the [[harry-bull|Reverend Harry Bull]] — who had also spoken about seeing a headless figure in the garden, and had described it to Lady Whitehouse and others.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Fred Tatum** is a minor but notable figure in the Borley Rectory narrative, primarily known as the boyfriend—and eventual husband—of Mary Pearson, the teenage maid who worked at the Rectory during the brief incumbency of the Rev. G. Eric Smith and his wife Mabel (1928–1929) [1, 2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, contradictions, and biographical details regarding his involvement:

**Biographical Details and Contradictions in Age**
Tatum is described in the sources as an unemployed visitor and a **"local bad lad who'd been convicted for stealing rabbits"**, who performed odd jobs around the Rectory and its garden [2, 3]. There is a notable contradiction in the sources regarding his age. One text describes him as a young man of **"about 16 or 17 years of age at that time"** [1]. However, another source explicitly identifies him as **"25-year-old Fred Tatum"** [3], and a third text states he was **"nine years older than the 17 year old Pearson"** [2], which would make him approximately 26.

**The "Headless Spook" Pranks**
Tatum's most significant contribution to the Borley legend is his role as a suspected earthly cause for several of the paranormal sightings. Both Fred and Mary were known to join in the fun regarding the local ghostly gossip [4]. Tatum would often play the fool by **walking around the Rectory garden with his coat draped over his head, pretending to be a headless spook** [3]. It is specifically noted that he did this during a rainstorm [5]. 

Because of these pranks, skeptics and later investigators heavily suspected that the "headless figure" or even the famous phantom nun reported by witnesses during this period were actually nothing more than flesh-and-blood sightings of Fred Tatum [4, 6]. 

**Events at the Rectory and Mrs. Smith's Testimony**
Fred Tatum was frequently at the Rectory, though Mrs. Mabel Smith claimed in one of her later accounts (in 1929) that she **"knew nothing about it at the time"** [6]. Despite this claim of ignorance, Tatum was present alongside the Smiths and Mary Pearson for several notable events:
*   **The Tipped Furniture:** The Smiths visited the Rectory with Fred and Mary and found a piece of furniture mysteriously tipped over on its side, despite the doors and windows being securely locked [7].
*   **The Loaded Revolver Incident:** After the Smiths had moved to Long Melford, they accompanied Harry Price and others to the empty Rectory for a midnight vigil. Mrs. Smith recorded in a signed statement that one of the investigators, an Army man, had a loaded revolver under his coat, prompting her to explicitly warn **"Mary and Fred to keep out of the way"** [8]. 
*   **Investigator Visits:** On the night of Harry Price's first visit (June 12, 1929), Fred went home before the late-night séance took place [9]. During a subsequent visit by investigator Lord Charles Hope, Fred and Mary were chatting in the kitchen when the drawing-room bell inexplicably rang four times [10]. 

**Opinion of Harry Price**
Fred Tatum held a highly unfavorable view of Harry Price and suspected the investigator of fabricating evidence [11]. In an interview given years later to researcher Mrs. C. Baines, Tatum recounted an incident in the Rectory's scullery when a storm lantern suddenly went out; regarding this event, Tatum stated he was **"strongly of the opinion Price had manipulated the wick"** [11]. 

**Later Life**
Following their time at Borley, Fred Tatum and Mary Pearson married secretly [2]. By the 1950s, when they were interviewed by Society for Psychical Research (SPR) investigators Eric Dingwall and Mollie Goldney, Fred and Mary were still married, had ten children, and were living in an overcrowded house in Sudbury [12]. Fred became a widower when Mary tragically died following a fall down a flight of stairs in 1966 [11, 13].
