# Dr Nandor Fodor

(Born Berengszasz, Hungary, 13 May 1895; died 17 May 1964) Practising New York psychoanalyst and psychical researcher. One-time leading light of the Metaphysical Society and Research Officer of the International Institute for Psychical Research.

Fodor studied law and then worked as a journalist. Much interested in the Borley case, he mentioned it in several books including *On the Trail of the Poltergeist* (1958), *Mind Over Space* (1962) and the monumental *Encyclopaedia of Psychic Science* (1933).

## Relationship with Harry Price

Fodor's relationship with [[harry-price]] was complex. He wrote candidly about it in a review in [[eileen-garrett]]'s *Tomorrow* (winter 1956):

> "I am not trying to defend Harry Price on the basis of personal feelings. I have never been his friend, nor his enemy. I did not like him because he was a very difficult man to like, and easy to resent ... He was far too selfish, intent on his own glory and intensely jealous. That was his character. I accepted him for what he was, admiring his courage, his ability and his achievements. In fact, I openly acknowledged him, in a court case in London, in 1939, as the outstanding psychical researcher in England of that day. I still hold to this opinion."

## Attack on the Borley Report

Fodor deplored the attacks on Price after Price's death. His scathing review of the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] (1956) became famous:

> "Never before in the history of psychical research has there been such a scandalous piece of writing... the book will do more discredit to psychical research than all the fraud laid at Harry Price's door will do to him... _Dingwall_ has been out to wreck psychical research in Great Britain for thirty years... Mrs Goldney has a penetrating mind but God help her subjects. There is no limit to her imagination on the negative side... She is eminently capable of convincing almost any witness that he did not see what he saw."

This review, published in Garrett's *Tomorrow*, became a key document in the pro-Price camp's response to the SPR report.

## Assessment

[[colin-wilson]] described Fodor as a man "who deserves to be remembered as one of the prominent ghost-hunters of the twentieth century" and noted his investigation of a poltergeist case at Thornton Heath, London (published in *The Haunted Mind*, Helix Press, 1959) as "deserving the status of a classic." Wilson observed that if Fodor had Price's talent for publicity, his Thornton Heath case might have been as famous as Borley.

Fodor was also notable for bringing psychoanalytic methods to poltergeist investigation, proposing that poltergeist agents were releasing unconscious psychological conflicts — an approach that influenced later researchers including [[iris-owen]]'s Philip Experiment.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Dr. Nandor Fodor** was a prominent Hungarian-born psychoanalyst, journalist, and psychical researcher. While he did not conduct on-site investigations at Borley Rectory himself, he played a crucial role in the post-haunting Borley literature as one of the fiercest defenders of investigator Harry Price following Price's death. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, publications, quotes, and specific opinions regarding his involvement in the case:

**Biographical Details and Background**
*   **Life Dates:** Born in Berengszasz, Hungary, on May 13, 1895, and died on May 17, 1964 [1]. 
*   **Professions:** He studied law and worked as a journalist before becoming a practicing New York psychoanalyst and psychical researcher [1].
*   **Affiliations:** He was a "leading light" of the Metaphysical Society and served as the Director of Research for the International Institute for Psychical Research [1], [2]. 
*   **Published Works:** Dr. Fodor authored the monumental *Encyclopaedia of Psychic Science* (1933) and later books that mentioned the Borley case, including *On the Trail of the Poltergeist* (1958) and *Mind Over Space* (1962) [1]. He also co-authored *The Story of the Poltergeist Down the Centuries* (1953) with Hereward Carrington [3]. 

**Relationship with Harry Price**
Fodor was a contemporary of Harry Price, and because he served as the Director of Research of a competing institute, the two men were professional rivals [2]. In his writings, Fodor was remarkably candid about his personal distaste for Price:
*   Fodor explicitly stated, **"I have never been his friend, nor his enemy. I did not like him because he was a very difficult man to like, and easy to resent. I do not think he liked me or appreciated me in any way"** [2]. 
*   He described Price's character as being **"far too selfish, intent on his own glory and intensely jealous,"** noting that Price **"resented rivalry to a point of obsession"** [2]. 
*   Despite this personal antipathy, Fodor deeply respected Price's professional capabilities. He noted: **"I accepted him for what he was, admiring his courage, his ability and his achievements. In fact, I openly acknowledged him, in a court case in London, in 1939, as the outstanding psychical researcher in England of that day. I still hold to this opinion"** [4]. 
*   *Analytical Insight:* Later commentators, such as Ivan Banks, have argued that Fodor's defense of Price carries significant weight precisely because Fodor openly admitted he disliked Price "as a person" [5]. Banks noted that defending the work of someone you dislike is of much greater value than attacking the work of an enemy [5]. 

**The Defense Against the SPR "Borley Report" (1956)**
When the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) published its highly critical *Borley Report* (authored by Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) accusing Harry Price of fraud, Fodor launched a blistering counter-attack in a review titled *"Was Harry Price a Fraud?"* published in the Winter 1956 issue of Eileen Garrett’s *Tomorrow* magazine [2], [6], [7]. 
*   He deplored the attack on Price, especially since Price was dead and unable to defend himself, declaring: **"Never before in the history of psychical research has there appeared in print such a scandalous piece of writing by three people, two of whom were collaborators and friends of Harry Price ... the book is a discredit to psychical research"** [8], [9], [4].
*   **Critique of Dr. Eric Dingwall:** Fodor claimed that Dingwall **"has been out to wreck psychical research in Great Britain for thirty years"** [4].
*   **Critique of Mrs. Mollie Goldney:** Fodor unleashed a scathing assessment of SPR investigator Mollie Goldney’s aggressive interviewing tactics, which he believed skewed the testimony of Borley witnesses. He wrote: **"Mrs. Goldney has a penetrating mind, but God help her subjects. There is no limit to her imagination on the negative side... she possess a steam-roller personality, capable of crushing any witness and any independent opinion around her... Having seen Mrs. Goldney at work. . . I am still haunted by the pitiful state in which she left her victim. She is eminently capable of convincing almost any witness that he did not see what he saw"** [4], [10]. 

**Other Cases and Legacy**
*   **The Amherst Mystery:** Fodor described the 19th-century Amherst haunting in Nova Scotia as **"one of the most famous poltergeist cases in the world"** [11]. (This case was later used by skeptics to suggest that the Borley rector, Lionel Foyster, might have modeled his own claims upon it [12], [13]). 
*   **The Thornton Heath Poltergeist:** Fodor personally investigated a poltergeist case involving a Mrs. Fielding in Thornton Heath, London, which he detailed in his 1959 book *The Haunted Mind* [14]. 
*   Author Colin Wilson praised Fodor as a man **"who deserves to be remembered as one of the prominent ghost-hunters of the twentieth century,"** adding that Fodor's Thornton Heath investigation "deserves the status of a classic" [14]. Wilson mused that if Fodor had possessed Harry Price's flair for publicity, he could have made the Thornton Heath case as famous as Borley Rectory [14].
