# Dr Nandor Fodor

(Born Berengszasz, Hungary, 13 May 1895; died 17 May 1964) Practising New York psychoanalyst, journalist, and psychical researcher. He studied law before working as a journalist, then became a psychoanalyst. A "leading light" of the Metaphysical Society and Director of Research for the International Institute for Psychical Research — which made him a professional rival of [[harry-price|Harry Price]].

Fodor authored the monumental *Encyclopaedia of Psychic Science* (1933) and later books that mentioned the Borley case, including *On the Trail of the Poltergeist* (1958) and *Mind Over Space* (1962). He also co-authored *The Story of the Poltergeist Down the Centuries* (1953) with [[hereward-carrington|Hereward Carrington]]. He did not conduct on-site investigations at Borley Rectory himself, but played a crucial role in the post-haunting literature as one of the fiercest defenders of Harry Price following Price's death.

## Relationship with Harry Price

Fodor's relationship with [[harry-price|Price]] was complex: a professional rival who personally disliked the man, yet professionally respected him. He wrote candidly in a review in [[eileen-garrett|Eileen Garrett]]'s *Tomorrow* (winter 1956):

> "I am not trying to defend Harry Price on the basis of personal feelings. I have never been his friend, nor his enemy. I did not like him because he was a very difficult man to like, and easy to resent ... He was far too selfish, intent on his own glory and intensely jealous. That was his character. I accepted him for what he was, admiring his courage, his ability and his achievements. In fact, I openly acknowledged him, in a court case in London, in 1939, as the outstanding psychical researcher in England of that day. I still hold to this opinion."

He described Price as "far too selfish, intent on his own glory and intensely jealous," noting Price "resented rivalry to a point of obsession." Later commentators, such as [[ivan-banks|Ivan Banks]], have argued that Fodor's defence of Price carries particular weight precisely because he openly admitted he disliked Price as a person — defending the work of someone you dislike is of greater evidential value than attacking the work of an enemy.

## Attack on the Borley Report

Fodor deplored the attacks on Price after his death, launching a blistering counter-attack in *"Was Harry Price a Fraud?"* — a review of the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] (1956) published in the Winter 1956 issue of *Tomorrow*. His review became famous:

> "Never before in the history of psychical research has there been such a scandalous piece of writing... the book will do more discredit to psychical research than all the fraud laid at Harry Price's door will do to him... _Dingwall_ has been out to wreck psychical research in Great Britain for thirty years... Mrs Goldney has a penetrating mind but God help her subjects. There is no limit to her imagination on the negative side... She is eminently capable of convincing almost any witness that he did not see what he saw."

Of Mollie Goldney specifically, he wrote: she "possesses a steam-roller personality, capable of crushing any witness and any independent opinion around her... Having seen Mrs. Goldney at work... I am still haunted by the pitiful state in which she left her victim."

## Other Cases and Legacy

Fodor was notable for bringing psychoanalytic methods to poltergeist investigation, proposing that poltergeist agents were releasing unconscious psychological conflicts — an approach that influenced later researchers including [[iris-owen|Iris Owen]]'s Philip Experiment. He personally investigated a poltergeist case involving a Mrs. Fielding in Thornton Heath, London, detailed in *The Haunted Mind* (Helix Press, 1959).

[[alan-gauld|Colin Wilson]] praised Fodor as "one of the prominent ghost-hunters of the twentieth century" and noted that if Fodor had Price's talent for publicity, his Thornton Heath case might have been as famous as Borley.

Fodor described the 19th-century Amherst haunting in Nova Scotia as "one of the most famous poltergeist cases in the world." This case was later used by sceptics to suggest that [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] might have modelled his own Borley claims upon it.
