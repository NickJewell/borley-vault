# S. J. de Lotbinière

Head of the BBC's Outside Broadcasts Department at the time of his visit to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] as one of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s official observers during the 1937–38 tenancy. He was among the most professionally significant names on Price's observer roster.

During his observational period at the Rectory, de Lotbinière heard 'clicks' and a 'scrabbling' noise for which no normal explanation was found. These were among the more common categories of auditory phenomena reported during Price's tenancy.

His presence as an official observer illustrates the professional calibre of participants [[harry-price|Harry Price]] attracted through his *Times* advertisement — the list included BBC journalists, academics, lawyers, and military officers.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Seymour J. de Lotbinière, O.B.E.** (frequently referred to in the sources as **S. J. de Lotbinière**, **Lotbiniere**, and occasionally misspelled as **Lothimière**) was an official observer during Harry Price's tenancy investigation of Borley Rectory. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, specific investigations, quotes, and his role in a major controversy regarding the case:

**Biographical Details and Professional Background**
*   **BBC Career:** De Lotbinière was a highly respected employee of the British Broadcasting Corporation (BBC). Having joined the BBC in **1932**, he rose to several prominent positions, including **Director of Outside Broadcasts, Head of Television Outside Broadcasts, and Assistant Controller of all Television Programmes** [1]. 
*   **Later Work:** Years after his involvement at Borley, he produced a television film about the Dracula story, during which he revisited the topic of the Borley mysteries on location in Essex [1]. 

**The First Rectory Visit (July 21, 1937)**
De Lotbinière first visited Borley Rectory on **July 21, 1937**, arriving at 4:00 p.m. alongside Harry Price and Major Henry Douglas-Home [2-4]. De Lotbinière compiled a meticulous, minute-by-minute contemporary report of the night's events [5]. 
*   **Physical Anomalies:** During his initial 8:15 p.m. examination of the house, he noted several small wall marks (which he ringed with chalk) and discovered that **"the clapper of the bell communicating with the Blue Room had been tied up with a piece of silk material"** [6]. 
*   **Auditory Phenomena and the "Challenge":** In the early hours of the morning, while standing in the Blue Room, the men heard a sharp click or crack from the stairs [7]. De Lotbinière directly challenged the presumed entity, stating: **"If they want to impress us, let them do something now"** [8]. Before he had even finished speaking, a sharp, hard answering crack was heard in the passage outside [7, 8]. De Lotbinière rationally suggested in his report that the noise could simply be natural thermal "contraction," though Price disagreed [8]. 
*   **Rustling Sounds:** The party also heard loud rustling and "scrabbling" sounds lasting seven to ten minutes coming from the Base Room immediately beneath them, which sounded like someone turning over the paper wrappings in a chocolate box that had been left there [7, 9]. 

**The Wall Markings and the Douglas-Home Contradiction**
De Lotbinière's meticulously timed report from this night became a crucial piece of documentary evidence in refuting later accusations of fraud made against Harry Price:
*   **The Accusation:** Twelve years after the visit (in August 1949), Major Henry Douglas-Home wrote a letter to Lord Charles Hope accusing Price of faking the rustling noises with cellophane paper in his pocket, and faking the wall markings, claiming that after their first tour of the house they **"never found another mark"** [5, 10, 11]. 
*   **The Refutation:** De Lotbinière's contemporary 1937 report explicitly recorded that the party made five distinct tours of the house (at 8:15 p.m., 9:15 p.m., 11:00 p.m., 1:10 a.m., and 5:15 a.m.). In direct contradiction to Douglas-Home, de Lotbinière officially reported finding *new* wall markings on the first four of these tours [5]. 
*   Furthermore, de Lotbinière's report located the mysterious "rustling" sounds in the Base Room (on the floor below them), making it highly improbable that the sound was caused by Price rustling cellophane in his pocket while sitting right next to them in the pitch-dark Blue Room [9, 11].

**The Second Visit (December 14, 1937)**
De Lotbinière returned to the Rectory on **December 14, 1937**, accompanied by Major Douglas-Home and fellow BBC broadcaster John Snagge [12]. 
*   This visit was uneventful. De Lotbinière reported finding some small marks on the walls, but rationally dismissed them as being **"evidently made by someone measuring up the walls, probably for some such purpose as redecoration"** because they were in straight, graduated lines [13]. 
*   Because things seemed **"so quiet and uneventful,"** the party decided to abandon the vigil early and return to London [12]. 

**Personal Stance on the Haunting**
During the actual 1937 investigation, de Lotbinière maintained a highly objective and mildly skeptical attitude, concluding his official report with the cautious disclaimer: **"I cannot say for certain that the 'manifestations' could not have been done by one or other of us"** [12]. 

However, his perspective seemed to solidify over time. Over thirty-five years later, when discussing his experiences on the set of his Dracula film, he remained absolutely convinced that the fresh wall markings he had personally witnessed appearing between his timed tours in July 1937 **"could not have been executed by human hands"** [1].
