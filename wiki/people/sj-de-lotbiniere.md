# S. J. de Lotbinière

**Seymour J. de Lotbinière, O.B.E.** (frequently referred to as **S. J. de Lotbinière**, and occasionally misspelled as **Lothimière**) was Head of the BBC's Outside Broadcasts Department at the time of his visit to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] as one of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s official observers during the 1937–38 tenancy. He was among the most professionally significant names on Price's observer roster, illustrating the calibre of participants Price attracted through his *Times* advertisement — the list included BBC journalists, academics, lawyers, and military officers.

Having joined the BBC in **1932**, he rose to several prominent positions, including **Director of Outside Broadcasts, Head of Television Outside Broadcasts, and Assistant Controller of all Television Programmes** [1]. Years after his Borley involvement, he produced a television film about the Dracula story, during which he revisited the topic of the Borley mysteries on location in Essex [1].

## The First Rectory Visit (July 21, 1937)

De Lotbinière first visited Borley Rectory on **July 21, 1937**, arriving at 4:00 p.m. alongside Harry Price and Major Henry [[major-douglas-home|Douglas-Home]] [2–4]. He compiled a meticulous, minute-by-minute contemporary report of the night's events [5].

During his initial 8:15 p.m. examination of the house, he noted several small wall marks (which he ringed with chalk) and discovered that **"the clapper of the bell communicating with the Blue Room had been tied up with a piece of silk material"** [6].

In the early hours of the morning, while standing in the [[blue-room|Blue Room]], the men heard a sharp click or crack from the stairs [7]. De Lotbinière directly challenged the presumed entity: **"If they want to impress us, let them do something now"** [8]. Before he had even finished speaking, a sharp, hard answering crack was heard in the passage outside [7, 8]. De Lotbinière rationally suggested in his report that the noise could simply be natural thermal "contraction," though Price disagreed [8].

The party also heard loud rustling and "scrabbling" sounds lasting seven to ten minutes coming from the Base Room immediately beneath them — sounding like someone turning over the paper wrappings in a chocolate box left there [7, 9].

## The Wall Markings and the Douglas-Home Contradiction

De Lotbinière's meticulously timed report became a crucial piece of documentary evidence in refuting later accusations of fraud against Harry Price.

Twelve years after the visit (in August 1949), Major [[major-douglas-home|Henry Douglas-Home]] wrote a letter to [[lord-charles-hope|Lord Charles Hope]] accusing Price of faking the rustling noises with cellophane paper in his pocket and faking the wall markings, claiming that after their first tour of the house they **"never found another mark"** [5, 10, 11].

De Lotbinière's contemporary 1937 report directly contradicted this: it explicitly recorded that the party made five distinct tours of the house (at 8:15 p.m., 9:15 p.m., 11:00 p.m., 1:10 a.m., and 5:15 a.m.), and in direct contradiction to Douglas-Home, officially reported finding *new* wall markings on the first four of these tours [5]. Furthermore, de Lotbinière's report located the mysterious "rustling" sounds in the Base Room (on the floor below them), making it highly improbable that the sound was caused by Price rustling cellophane in his pocket while sitting right next to them in the pitch-dark Blue Room [9, 11].

## The Second Visit (December 14, 1937)

De Lotbinière returned on **December 14, 1937**, accompanied by Major Douglas-Home and fellow BBC broadcaster [[john-snagge|John Snagge]] [12]. This visit was uneventful. De Lotbinière found some small marks on the walls but rationally dismissed them as **"evidently made by someone measuring up the walls, probably for some such purpose as redecoration"** because they were in straight, graduated lines [13]. Because things seemed **"so quiet and uneventful,"** the party decided to abandon the vigil early and return to London [12].

## Personal Stance

During the actual 1937 investigation, de Lotbinière maintained a highly objective and mildly sceptical attitude, concluding his official report: **"I cannot say for certain that the 'manifestations' could not have been done by one or other of us"** [12]. However, his perspective appeared to solidify over time. Over thirty-five years later, when discussing his experiences on the set of his Dracula film, he remained absolutely convinced that the fresh wall markings he had personally witnessed appearing between his timed tours in July 1937 **"could not have been executed by human hands"** [1].
