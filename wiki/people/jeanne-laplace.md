# Mlle Jeanne Laplace

Psychometrist tested by [[harry-price|Harry Price]]; cited by Price as one of the very few persons he has verified as possessing a genuine faculty of psychometry (tactile clairvoyance).

## The Tillyard Prediction

Price cites the Laplace case as context for his discussion of [[ian-aitchison|Lieutenant Ian Aitchison]]'s psychometrizing of the Borley relics. When handling a letter sent to Price by Dr. R. J. Tillyard, F.R.S. (an Australian entomologist and parapsychologist), Laplace gave **fifty-three "impressions"** about the letter writer, of which **forty were correct or reasonably correct**.

Most striking was a prediction she made twice: that Tillyard **"would die through a railroad or automobile accident."** The prediction was made in 1928 and published at the time. On **January 13, 1937**, Tillyard was killed in a motor-car smash near Canberra, Australia.

## Context

Price invokes Laplace's verified record to suggest that Aitchison's psychometric impressions of the Borley relics deserve serious attention — if psychometry can produce results that are verifiably accurate (forty out of fifty-three, plus the predictive element), then the impressions of the Borley bones and pendants may not be merely fantasy. Price is careful to note he "believes in psychometry" as a faculty, while acknowledging that the mechanism is unknown.

Laplace is one of Price's few fully named and attested positive cases; the quality of her performance distinguishes her in his estimation from the many mediums and sensitives he investigated and found wanting.
