# Mlle Jeanne Laplace

Psychometrist tested by [[harry-price|Harry Price]]; cited by Price as one of the very few persons he has verified as possessing a genuine faculty of psychometry (tactile clairvoyance).

## The Tillyard Prediction

Price cites the Laplace case as context for his discussion of [[ian-aitchison|Lieutenant Ian Aitchison]]'s psychometrizing of the Borley relics. When handling a letter sent to Price by Dr. R. J. Tillyard, F.R.S. (an Australian entomologist and parapsychologist), Laplace gave **fifty-three "impressions"** about the letter writer, of which **forty were correct or reasonably correct**.

Most striking was a prediction she made twice: that Tillyard **"would die through a railroad or automobile accident."** The prediction was made in 1928 and published at the time. On **January 13, 1937**, Tillyard was killed in a motor-car smash near Canberra, Australia.

## Context

Price invokes Laplace's verified record to suggest that Aitchison's psychometric impressions of the Borley relics deserve serious attention — if psychometry can produce results that are verifiably accurate (forty out of fifty-three, plus the predictive element), then the impressions of the Borley bones and pendants may not be merely fantasy. Price is careful to note he "believes in psychometry" as a faculty, while acknowledging that the mechanism is unknown.

Laplace is one of Price's few fully named and attested positive cases; the quality of her performance distinguishes her in his estimation from the many mediums and sensitives he investigated and found wanting.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mlle Jeanne Laplace** was a practitioner of psychometry (also known as tactile clairvoyance), a faculty defined by investigators as the ability to divine the properties, character, or associations of an object through physical contact [1]. 

Although she was not directly involved in the Borley Rectory investigations, her name is recorded in the Borley sources by Harry Price to establish the validity of psychometry before he introduces the psychometric readings of the Borley relics performed by the Canadian psychometrist Lieutenant Ian Aitchison [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the text reveals the following specific facts, dates, and quotes regarding her abilities:

**The Tillyard Letter and 1928 Prediction**
Price cited Mlle Laplace as one of the "very few persons" he believed genuinely possessed the psychometric faculty [1]. To prove this, he recorded an experiment wherein Mlle Laplace handled a letter sent to Price by the entomologist **Dr. R. J. Tillyard, F.R.S.** [1]. 
*   During the reading, which took place while Tillyard was still alive in **1928**, she produced **fifty-three "impressions"**, of which Price noted that **"forty of which were correct or reasonably correct"** [1].
*   Her most notable impression was a specific, twice-repeated prediction regarding Tillyard's ultimate fate. She stated definitively that he **"would die through a railroad or automobile accident"** [1].

**Fulfillment of the Prediction (1937)**
Price noted that he had published Mlle Laplace's prediction at the time it was made in 1928 [1]. Tragically, the prophecy was fulfilled several years later when Dr. Tillyard was killed in a **"motor-car smash near Canberra, Australia, where he lived"** on **January 13, 1937** [1]. Price used the stunning accuracy of Laplace's reading to bolster the credibility of psychometric impressions in psychical research before detailing the results of the Borley relics [1].
