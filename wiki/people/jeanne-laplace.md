# Mlle Jeanne Laplace

Psychometrist tested by [[harry-price|Harry Price]]; cited by Price as one of the very few persons he has verified as possessing a genuine faculty of psychometry (tactile clairvoyance) [1]. Although she was not directly involved in the Borley Rectory investigations, her name appears in the Borley sources because Price invokes her verified record to establish the credibility of psychometry before introducing the psychometric readings of the Borley relics performed by the Canadian psychometrist [[ian-aitchison|Lieutenant Ian Aitchison]] [1].

## The Tillyard Prediction

Price cites the Laplace case as context for his discussion of Aitchison's psychometrizing of the Borley relics. When handling a letter sent to Price by the entomologist **Dr. R. J. Tillyard, F.R.S.**, Laplace gave **fifty-three "impressions"** about the letter writer, of which Price noted that **"forty of which were correct or reasonably correct"** [1].

Most striking was a specific, twice-repeated prediction she made: that Tillyard **"would die through a railroad or automobile accident."** The prediction was made in **1928** and published at the time [1]. On **13 January 1937**, Tillyard was killed in a "motor-car smash near Canberra, Australia, where he lived" [1].

## Context in Price's Argument

Price invokes Laplace's verified record to suggest that Aitchison's psychometric impressions of the Borley bones and pendants deserve serious attention — if psychometry can produce results that are verifiably accurate (forty out of fifty-three, plus the predictive element), then the impressions of the Borley relics may not be merely fantasy. Price is careful to note he "believes in psychometry" as a faculty, while acknowledging that the mechanism is unknown.

Laplace is one of Price's few fully named and attested positive cases; the quality of her performance distinguishes her in his estimation from the many mediums and sensitives he investigated and found wanting.
