# Dr. Vernon Harrison

SPR member who attended Harry Price's Ghost Club meetings from early 1937 until the Blitz ended further gatherings. Harrison knew Price personally — though not closely — and contributed a detailed written assessment of Price and Borley to Ivan Banks's *The Enigma of Borley Rectory* (Chapter 24).

## Personal Knowledge of Price

Harrison was thirty-one years younger than Price. He met Price, spoke with him, and heard him speak at pre-war Ghost Club meetings:

> "Price was not a good speaker, but he was an enthusiastic chairman who did not attempt to dominate the discussion. He engaged good and often well-known speakers, and the meetings over which he presided were not only socially enjoyable, they were also highly informative. From them, I learned a good deal."

At those meetings, Harrison also had the opportunity to speak with several official observers who had been present at Borley vigils: "they told me from their experience that it was 'a strange place'."

## Assessment of Price

Harrison offers a balanced and precise portrait:

> "Price was not a trained scientist. He was a businessman, journalist and publicist by inclination, and an entrepreneur. He loved a good story; and in the narration of such stories, scientific exactitude could suffer. He could be tetchy and vindictive. He could jump to false conclusions. However, these defects do not, in my experience, make him unique among successful businessmen."

On the credit side, he acknowledges Price's formidable knowledge, his extraordinary library, and his experimental energy with physical mediums.

## View on Borley

Harrison is clear that he does not believe Price fabricated the Borley phenomena:

> "I do not think that Price invented the Borley phenomena for the purpose of publicity. The records show that there were disturbances at Borley long before Price became interested in the case, and they continued after his death."

He concludes with a measured endorsement: "I am of the opinion that Price presented the Borley case in good faith, though his accounts may not be wholly accurate."

He also makes a practical point about the limits of the investigation: "Price had Borley under observation for about a year... there are limits to what can be asked of voluntary, unpaid investigators working at their own expense. Criticism is cheap and easy. Investigation is not."

## Significance

Harrison's testimony is particularly valuable because it is neither hagiography nor attack. He had direct personal knowledge of Price and of the observers, and his assessment — Price was flawed but sincere, Borley was genuinely strange — represents the view that Banks presents as most likely to be correct.

Banks uses Harrison's point about the one-year observation period to make a broader argument: that Price should be judged on his full seventeen-year involvement with Borley, not just the 1937–38 observer tenancy.
