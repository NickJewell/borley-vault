# Dr. Vernon Harrison

[[spr|SPR]] member, former President of the Royal Photographic Society, and one of the most balanced retrospective appraisers of the Borley case. He attended [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s [[ghost-club|Ghost Club]] meetings from early 1937 until the Blitz ended further gatherings. Harrison was thirty-one years younger than Price: he met him, spoke with him, and heard him speak at those pre-war meetings, though he never claimed an intimate acquaintance.

## Personal Knowledge of Price

Harrison recalls the Ghost Club sessions with precise appreciation:

> "Price was not a good speaker, but he was an enthusiastic chairman who did not attempt to dominate the discussion. He engaged good and often well-known speakers, and the meetings over which he presided were not only socially enjoyable, they were also highly informative. From them, I learned a good deal."

At those meetings Harrison also had the opportunity to speak with several official observers who had kept vigils at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]]. He recalled speaking with [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]], Price's chief observer, who described the Rectory to him as "a very queer place." The general consensus among the observers was that Borley was "a strange place."

On 18 January 1981, Harrison participated in a special Ghost Club meeting held at the Savage Club — the centenary celebration entitled *Memories of Harry Price* — organised to mark the hundredth anniversary of Price's birth.

## Assessment of Price

Harrison offers a balanced and precise portrait:

> "Price was not a trained scientist. He was a businessman, journalist and publicist by inclination, and an entrepreneur. He loved a good story; and in the narration of such stories, scientific exactitude could suffer. He could be tetchy and vindictive. He could jump to false conclusions. However, these defects do not, in my experience, make him unique among successful businessmen."

On the credit side, he acknowledges Price's "formidable" knowledge, his extraordinary library, his "unbounded energy and enthusiasm," and his experimental energy with physical mediums.

## Defence of Price Against Fraud Allegations

Drawing on his expertise as former President of the Royal Photographic Society, Harrison definitively dismissed the claim that Price had faked an incriminating photograph of Austrian medium [[rudi-schneider|Rudi Schneider]] — a charge that had been one of the SPR's most pointed allegations [1–3]. He wrote separately about this in his article "Harry Price and the Rudi Schneider Phenomena" for *Psychical Studies* (Winter 1987).

Regarding Borley specifically, Harrison is unambiguous:

> "I do not think that Price invented the Borley phenomena for the purpose of publicity. The records show that there were disturbances at Borley long before Price became interested in the case, and they continued after his death."

He goes further, rejecting the fraud hypothesis more broadly: "Nor do I find very credible the claim that Price produced some of the phenomena himself" [6]. His conclusion was measured but clear: "I am of the opinion that Price presented the Borley case in good faith, though his accounts may not be wholly accurate" [6].

## Critique of the Investigation

While defending Price's overall integrity, Harrison acknowledged that the year-long observational tenancy had flaws — "lost opportunities and questions left unanswered" [9]. He argued, however, that Price did more than most contemporaries to bring the case to light, while others were "content to sit back and criticise" [8]. His most memorable summary of the practical difficulties:

> "There are limits to what can be asked of voluntary, unpaid investigators working at their own expense. Criticism is cheap and easy. Investigation is not." [8, 9]

## Later Scholarship

Harrison later authored *H.P. Blavatsky and the SPR* (1997), a work that echoed other researchers in criticising the SPR's historical debunking methods — an outlook consistent with his defence of Price at Borley. He contributed a detailed written assessment of Price and Borley to Ivan Banks's *The Enigma of Borley Rectory* (Chapter 24), alongside [[guy-lyon-playfair]] and [[andrew-mackenzie]].

## Significance

Harrison's testimony is particularly valuable because it is neither hagiography nor attack. He had direct personal knowledge of Price and of the observers, and his assessment — Price was flawed but sincere, Borley was genuinely strange — represents the view that Banks presents as most likely to be correct. Banks uses Harrison's point about the one-year observation period to make a broader argument: that Price should be judged on his full seventeen-year involvement with Borley, not merely the 1937–38 observer tenancy.
