# Dr. Vernon Harrison

SPR member who attended Harry Price's Ghost Club meetings from early 1937 until the Blitz ended further gatherings. Harrison knew Price personally — though not closely — and contributed a detailed written assessment of Price and Borley to Ivan Banks's *The Enigma of Borley Rectory* (Chapter 24).

## Personal Knowledge of Price

Harrison was thirty-one years younger than Price. He met Price, spoke with him, and heard him speak at pre-war Ghost Club meetings:

> "Price was not a good speaker, but he was an enthusiastic chairman who did not attempt to dominate the discussion. He engaged good and often well-known speakers, and the meetings over which he presided were not only socially enjoyable, they were also highly informative. From them, I learned a good deal."

At those meetings, Harrison also had the opportunity to speak with several official observers who had been present at Borley vigils: "they told me from their experience that it was 'a strange place'."

## Assessment of Price

Harrison offers a balanced and precise portrait:

> "Price was not a trained scientist. He was a businessman, journalist and publicist by inclination, and an entrepreneur. He loved a good story; and in the narration of such stories, scientific exactitude could suffer. He could be tetchy and vindictive. He could jump to false conclusions. However, these defects do not, in my experience, make him unique among successful businessmen."

On the credit side, he acknowledges Price's formidable knowledge, his extraordinary library, and his experimental energy with physical mediums.

## View on Borley

Harrison is clear that he does not believe Price fabricated the Borley phenomena:

> "I do not think that Price invented the Borley phenomena for the purpose of publicity. The records show that there were disturbances at Borley long before Price became interested in the case, and they continued after his death."

He concludes with a measured endorsement: "I am of the opinion that Price presented the Borley case in good faith, though his accounts may not be wholly accurate."

He also makes a practical point about the limits of the investigation: "Price had Borley under observation for about a year... there are limits to what can be asked of voluntary, unpaid investigators working at their own expense. Criticism is cheap and easy. Investigation is not."

## Significance

Harrison's testimony is particularly valuable because it is neither hagiography nor attack. He had direct personal knowledge of Price and of the observers, and his assessment — Price was flawed but sincere, Borley was genuinely strange — represents the view that Banks presents as most likely to be correct.

Banks uses Harrison's point about the one-year observation period to make a broader argument: that Price should be judged on his full seventeen-year involvement with Borley, not just the 1937–38 observer tenancy.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Dr. Vernon Harrison** was a member of the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) and the **former President of the Royal Photographic Society** [1-3]. He provided a balanced, retrospective appraisal of the Borley Rectory case and Harry Price’s role within it, while defending Price against claims of fraud. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, quotes, and lesser-known biographical details about his connection to the case:

**Lesser-Known Biographical Details**
During the time of the Borley investigations in the late 1930s, Harrison was still a student and was exactly **31 years junior to Harry Price** [3]. Decades later, he authored two notable works on the history of psychical research: the article "Harry Price and the Rudi Schneider Phenomena" for *Psychical Studies* in Winter 1987, and the 1997 book *H.P. Blavatsky and the SPR*, which echoed other researchers in criticizing the SPR's historical debunking methods [1, 4].

**Interactions with Harry Price and the Ghost Club**
Though he never claimed to know Price intimately, Harrison met him and heard him speak on a number of occasions at pre-war meetings of the Ghost Club from the beginning of 1937 until the Blitz ended the gatherings [3]. He recalled that Price was **"not a good speaker, but he was an enthusiastic chairman who did not attempt to dominate the discussion"** [3]. Harrison noted that these meetings were highly informative and socially enjoyable, adding, **"From them, I learned a good deal"** [3]. 

Many years later, on **January 18, 1981**, Harrison participated in a special Ghost Club meeting held at the Savage Club entitled *Memories of Harry Price*, which was organized to celebrate the centenary of Price's birth [5].

**Conversations with Borley Observers**
During the pre-war Ghost Club meetings, Harrison had the opportunity to speak directly with several of the official observers who had kept vigils at Borley Rectory [6]. He specifically recalled speaking with Sidney Glanville, Price's chief observer, who described the Rectory to him as **"a very queer place"** [7]. The general consensus from the observers Harrison spoke to was that Borley was a **"strange place"** [6]. 

**Defense of Harry Price**
Harrison staunchly defended Price against the harsh retrospective attacks from the SPR. His defense rested on several specific points:
*   **The Rudi Schneider Photograph:** Using his expertise as the former President of the Royal Photographic Society, Harrison definitively **dismissed the claim that Price had faked an incriminating photograph** of the Austrian medium Rudi Schneider [2].
*   **Pre-existing Phenomena:** Regarding Borley, Harrison did not believe Price invented the phenomena for publicity, arguing: **"The records show that there were disturbances at Borley long before Price became interested in the case, and they continued after his death"** [6].
*   **Rejection of Fraud:** He firmly stated, **"Nor do I find very credible the claim that Price produced some of the phenomena himself"** [6]. 
*   **Good Faith:** Ultimately, Harrison concluded, **"I am of the opinion that Price presented the Borley case in good faith, though his accounts may not be wholly accurate"** [6].

**Critique of the Investigation**
While supportive of Price's overall integrity and praising his **"formidable"** knowledge and **"unbounded energy and enthusiasm,"** Harrison acknowledged that Price's year-long observational tenancy of the Rectory had flaws [8]. Looking back, he noted one could point to **"lost opportunities and questions left unanswered"** [9]. 

However, Harrison argued that Price did more than most of his contemporaries to bring the case to light while others were "content to sit back and criticise" [8]. In a memorable quote regarding the practical difficulties of psychical research, Harrison concluded: **"there are limits to what can be asked of voluntary, unpaid investigators working at their own expense. Criticism is cheap and easy. Investigation is not"** [8, 9].
