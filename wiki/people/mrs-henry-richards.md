# Mrs Henry Richards

A member of the Council of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s [[national-laboratory|National Laboratory of Psychical Research]] (NLPR). On 13 October 1931, she was one of the party Price brought to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] for his first visit during the [[lionel-foyster|Foyster]] incumbency.

## October 1931 Foyster Visit

The party staying at the Bull Hotel in [[long-melford|Long Melford]] comprised Price, Mrs Richards, [[kathleen-goldney|Mrs K. M. Goldney]], [[miss-may-walker|Miss May Walker]], and [[james-ballantyne|Mr James Ballantyne]] (the chauffeur). After arriving and dining at the Rectory, Mrs Richards contributed a luncheon basket including a bottle of Burgundy and a bottle of Sauterne. Price drew the corks; the Burgundy turned to black ink in the glass.

> Price later described the group's conclusion that evening: "every serious 'incident' had occurred when Mrs Foyster was not within sight or under the control of the visitors."

The following morning, while Miss Walker returned to London, Mrs Richards and Mrs Goldney returned to the Rectory with Price. Price gently confronted Mr Foyster with the suggestion that his wife might have produced the manifestations. Mrs Richards's specific role in that confrontation is not recorded; [[kathleen-goldney|Mrs Goldney]] is noted to have "intervened on [Marianne's] behalf."

## Context

No further details about Mrs Richards's identity or subsequent involvement with Borley are recorded in *Ghosts of Borley*. Her presence on the NLPR Council confirms her standing within Price's investigative circle.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mrs. Henry Richards** (whose first name is also recorded in the texts as **Clarice**) was a psychical researcher and a Council Member of Harry Price's National Laboratory of Psychical Research [1-4]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following specific details, events, quotes, and contradictions regarding her involvement in the Borley Rectory saga:

**The October 1931 Visit**
Mrs. Richards's primary connection to the case centers entirely on a significant two-day investigation she undertook during the tumultuous Foyster incumbency. 
*   On **October 13–14, 1931**, she traveled to Borley alongside Harry Price, Mrs. Kathleen "Mollie" Goldney, and Miss May Walker [1, 5-7].
*   The group was driven to the site by **Mrs. Richards's personal chauffeur, James Ballantyne** [2, 6, 7].
*   Because they were investigating at night, arrangements had been made for the party to stay at the **Bull Hotel** in the nearby village of Long Melford [2, 4]. 
*   Over the course of their stay, Mrs. Richards and the rest of the party witnessed several instances of "phenomena," which ultimately culminated in Price famously accusing Marianne Foyster of faking the occurrences [1, 3].

**Marianne Foyster's Impression and Quote**
Despite the visit ending with Price leveling accusations of fraud, Marianne Foyster retained a surprisingly fond memory of Mrs. Richards. Years later, recalling the investigators returning from their hotel with a picnic basket or hamper, Marianne offered the following specific quote, contrasting Mrs. Richards favorably against her colleague:
> **"There was a charming lady - a Mrs. Richards her name was, I think - and a woman named Mrs. Goldney. She was the vulgar type...."** [8].

**The Foyster Manuscript Contradiction**
Mrs. Richards's presence at Borley was utilized by later skeptics to highlight a specific chronological flaw and memory lapse in the Rev. Lionel Foyster's written accounts. 
*   In his manuscript *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*, the Rector inaccurately recorded the composition of Price's arriving party, stating: **"Mr Price arrived with another gentleman and two ladies"** [7]. 
*   Investigators from the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) later pointed out this contradiction, noting that the party in fact consisted of Price, *three* ladies (Goldney, Richards, and Walker), and Mrs. Richards's chauffeur. They used this lapse in memory to demonstrate that Foyster was fallible and to question his overall reliability as a witness to the exact details of the phenomena [7].

**Later Acknowledgement**
Beyond her 1931 visit, Mrs. Richards is briefly noted in the foreword of a Borley compilation by the renowned ghost hunter Peter Underwood. He lists her among the notable Borley figures with whom he had personally spent time, stating that he learned something from each of them and gained **"a tremendous insight into the mystery that was and is Borley"** [9].
