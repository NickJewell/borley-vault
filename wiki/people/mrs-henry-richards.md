# Mrs Henry Richards

Also recorded as **Clarice Richards**. A Council Member of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s [[national-laboratory|National Laboratory of Psychical Research]] (NLPR). On 13–14 October 1931, she was one of the party Price brought to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] for his first visit during the [[lionel-foyster|Foyster]] incumbency. She is briefly noted in the foreword of a Borley compilation by [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] as one of the Borley figures with whom he had personally spent time, through whom he gained "a tremendous insight into the mystery that was and is Borley."

## October 1931 Foyster Visit

The party staying at the Bull Hotel in [[long-melford|Long Melford]] comprised Price, Mrs Richards, [[kathleen-goldney|Mrs K. M. Goldney]], [[miss-may-walker|Miss May Walker]], and Mr **James Ballantyne** (Mrs Richards's personal chauffeur). After arriving and dining at the Rectory, Mrs Richards contributed a luncheon basket including a bottle of Burgundy and a bottle of Sauterne. Price drew the corks; the Burgundy turned to black ink in the glass.

> Price later described the group's conclusion that evening: "every serious 'incident' had occurred when Mrs Foyster was not within sight or under the control of the visitors."

The following morning, while Miss Walker returned to London, Mrs Richards and Mrs Goldney returned to the Rectory with Price. Price gently confronted Mr Foyster with the suggestion that his wife might have produced the manifestations. Mrs Richards's specific role in that confrontation is not recorded; [[kathleen-goldney|Mrs Goldney]] is noted to have "intervened on [Marianne's] behalf."

## Marianne Foyster's Impression

Despite the visit ending with Price levelling accusations of fraud, [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] retained a surprisingly fond memory of Mrs Richards. Years later, recalling the investigators returning from their hotel with a picnic basket, she offered the following quote contrasting Mrs Richards favourably against her colleague:

> "There was a charming lady — a Mrs. Richards her name was, I think — and a woman named Mrs. Goldney. She was the vulgar type...."

## The Foyster Manuscript Contradiction

Mrs Richards's presence at Borley was used by later sceptics to highlight a chronological flaw in the Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]]'s written accounts. In his manuscript *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*, the rector recorded the composition of Price's arriving party as: "Mr Price arrived with another gentleman and two ladies." The SPR investigators pointed out that the party in fact consisted of Price, *three* ladies (Goldney, Richards, and Walker), and Mrs Richards's chauffeur — using this lapse in memory to question Foyster's reliability as a witness to exact details.

## Context

No further details about Mrs Richards's subsequent involvement with Borley are recorded in the sources.
