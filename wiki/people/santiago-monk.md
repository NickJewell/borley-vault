# Santiago Monk

(Born 1866; died London, 25 July 1939) Personal acquaintance and admirer of [[marianne-foyster]], and a family friend of the Foysters from their time in Canada between 1922 and 1929. A Chilean diplomat at the Embassy in London, listed as Commercial Attaché until 1939. Marianne described him as her "spiritual godfather."

## Relationship with Marianne

During the first year or so of the Foysters' stay at [[borley-rectory]] — particularly during 1932 — Marianne visited Monk in London and, according to Miss Dytor (see [[edith-wildgoose]]), received expensive presents from him.

In a letter to [[ian-greenwood]] written in May 1956, Marianne denied receiving gifts from Monk: "She must have kept them for I never saw those." She admitted seeing him "about twice a year" in London but described him as "an old man of 76" who was well known by [[lionel-foyster]] and insisted there was "no affair there."

## Marianne's Fantasies

When living with [[francois-darles|Francois d'Arles]] in Wimbledon (see [[jonquille-et-cie]]), Marianne told friends she was Santiago Monk's daughter by the imaginary "Countess Sarah von Kiergraff, late of Schleswig-Holstein" — one of several elaborate false identities Marianne invented for herself. She also told the Fentons (Mr and Mrs Fenton, owners of a local chemist's shop) that she was the daughter of a Chilean diplomat, a graduate of a Cambridge ladies' college, and that Pearless was Foyster's son.

## Significance

Monk's role is primarily as evidence of Marianne's complicated social life during the Foyster period — a middle-aged woman of independent connections maintaining relationships beyond the Rectory and beyond the d'Arles affair. The BRC treats the relationship as non-sexual based on Marianne's own denials and the age gap (he was 76 when she knew him in London). His death in July 1939 — five months after the [[fire-1939|Rectory fire]] — removes any possibility of his testimony being obtained.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

Based on the Borley sources, **Santiago Monk** was a diplomat, a personal friend of the Foyster family, and an unwitting character in Marianne Foyster’s elaborate personal fantasies. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following biographical details, facts, quotes, and contradictions regarding his connection to the Borley Rectory story:

**Biographical Details**
*   **Birth and Death:** Born in **1866**, he died in London on **25 July 1939** [1].
*   **Profession:** He was an Englishman who served as a diplomat in the Chilean Embassy in London, where he was officially listed as a **Commercial Attaché until 1939** [1-3].

**Relationship with the Foysters**
*   Monk was a family friend of the Foysters dating back to their time living in Canada between **1922 and 1929** [1].
*   Marianne Foyster greatly admired him and affectionately referred to him as her **"spiritual godfather"** [1, 3, 4]. 
*   During the Foysters' first year at Borley Rectory (at least during 1932), Marianne made trips up to London to visit Monk [1, 3]. 

**Allegations of an Affair and Contradictions**
Marianne's visits to Monk became a subject of suspicion and gossip, leading to direct contradictions in the historical record:
*   **The Accusation:** Miss Edith May Dytor (later Mrs. Wildgoose), who lived at the Rectory as Marianne's nurse-companion from May to November 1932, claimed that during these London trips, Marianne received expensive presents from the diplomat [3, 5]. Marianne's son, Ian Shaw, also later questioned his mother about her relationship with Monk [6].
*   **Marianne's Rebuttal:** In a letter to her son Ian written around 21 May 1956, Marianne vigorously denied having an affair with Monk or receiving gifts from him. She admitted to seeing him "about twice a year" in London, but stressed that he was an old man of 76 and was well known to her husband, Lionel [7]. She firmly stated: **"There was also no affair there"** [7]. Rebutting Miss Dytor’s claims about the expensive gifts, Marianne added the specific quote: **"she must have kept them for I never saw those"** [7].

**The "Countess von Kiergraff" Fantasy**
Later in life, particularly while running the *Jonquille et Cie* florist shop in Wimbledon with her lover Frank Pearless (Francois d'Arles), Marianne wove the "dependable Santiago Monk" into a series of fictitious and grandiose stories about her own background [8, 9]. 
*   She told acquaintances, such as Mr. and Mrs. Fenton, that she was not the daughter of her real father (an unsuccessful tutor named William Shaw) but was instead **"Marianne Monk"**, the daughter of Santiago Monk and a fabricated aristocrat she named the **Countess Sarah von Kiergraff, late of Schleswig-Holstein** [2, 8, 10, 11]. 
*   To explain her marriage to the much older Lionel Foyster, she falsely claimed that because her diplomat father was constantly away on business, Foyster had merely raised her as a child [8, 10].

**Séance Anomalies and Researcher Confusion**
*   Santiago Monk's name unexpectedly appeared during a Borley planchette séance. An entity claiming to be Harry Bull was asked, **"Do you know Santiago Monk?"** to which it replied **"Yes."** When asked, **"Is he Pearless?"** the entity replied **"No"** [12]. 
*   Because his name appeared in this obscure séance transcript, later Borley researcher Ivan Banks—apparently unaware of the Chilean diplomat's identity—was left thoroughly baffled. Banks theorized in his writings that "Santiago Monk" might literally refer to a **"Spanish Catholic monk"** who was somehow historically connected to the ghost nun [13]. 

**Later Speculation**
Sceptical investigator Trevor H. Hall, while researching Marianne's life, found a witness who claimed that while Marianne was living under the name "Voyster" at Gyppeswyck Road in Ipswich, she was often visited by an "elderly gentleman" [4]. Hall rhetorically and sarcastically speculated: **"Was this the magnificent Santiago Monk, her ‘spiritual godfather’, come to her lodgings to offer spiritual guidance?"** [4]. *(Note: As Monk died in 1939, this would only be possible if her time at Gyppeswyck Road predated his death).*
