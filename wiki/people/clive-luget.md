# Rev. Clive Luget

Rector of Middleton, Essex (a tiny village one mile south of Sudbury in Suffolk). Made multiple visits to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] during the 1930s and 1940s, becoming one of the most significant clerical witnesses of the [[marianne-foyster|Foyster period]]. Described by Underwood as "a remote and lonely man... kind and thoughtful but one moment talking of the Virgin Mary visiting him and the next completely rational."

## First Visit and Subsequent Visits

Luget first visited at the invitation of [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]], who was worried that the ghosts would affect his much younger wife. He had visited scores of haunted houses but "had never encountered anything like Borley Rectory." He returned many times, always enjoying theological discussion with Lionel and finding Marianne "delightful, always cheerful and charming, an excellent hostess."

## Personal Phenomena Witnessed

### Ghost Nun
Luget personally saw the [[the-nun|ghost nun]] on at least one occasion.

### "Marianne" Written on Blank Paper
About to leave a room he had examined minutely, Luget thought he heard a sound behind him, turned, and found "a sheet of paper in the middle of the room" bearing a pencilled "Marianne" in letters about three inches high. The room had been empty and checked.

### Blue Room Pencil
In the Blue Room with Marianne and Lionel, all three heard "a scribbling sound from the room behind them," turned to see a pencil drop to the floor, and found a message asking for prayers in 2–3 inch letters on the wall. All three dropped to their knees and prayed. Nothing further happened.

### Harry Bull's Dressing-Gown
Walking with Marianne in an upstairs corridor, both saw "the figure of a man in a plum-coloured dressing-gown come out of a room ahead of them" — and then completely disappear. Later, Mrs Payne of Borley Place (who had known the Bulls for years) confirmed that [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] "almost lived in" a plum-coloured dressing-gown.

### Stone-Throwing
Pelted with pebbles coming down the main stairs with no one above; the child was in the kitchen.

### Localised Burning Smell
Traced a burning smell to the kitchen passage; nothing found — no smoke or smell elsewhere, no evident origin.

## Attempted Exorcisms

Luget and a brother clergyman performed at least three escalating attempts:
1. Holy water from the Well of Our Lady of Walsingham, with a mild exorcism — no effect.
2. An elaborate exorcism rite, plus complete creosote fumigation — no effect.
3. Four priests together blessing the entire house from top to bottom — no appreciable effect.

## Assessment of Marianne

Luget's portrait of [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] is one of the more sympathetic in the literature. He saw her as:
- Not frightened by the phenomena but actively interested and seeking an explanation
- Very protective of the children at all times
- Showing anxiety only at signs of fire or very loud noises
- "Flirtatious" in a way that helped with visitors — "exaggerated and not understood by some villagers"
- Entirely capable and attentive to Lionel despite the strain

He attributed her unpopularity in the village partly to "narrow-mindedness" at a Bull-family rector being married to a much younger woman, and partly to "plain envy."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**The Rev. Clive Luget** (variously recorded in the sources as **Father Clive Lugent** or **Revd Clive Lugret**) was an Anglican priest and the Rector of Middleton, Essex, a tiny village just a mile south of Sudbury [1-3]. Regarded as a "great authority" on paranormal matters, he visited Borley Rectory many times during the 1930s and 1940s and became a central witness to some of its most spectacular phenomena [1, 3].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, and lesser-known details regarding his life and involvement:

**Lesser-Known Biographical Details and His Own Ghost**
Described as a complex, remote, and somewhat lonely individual, Luget possessed psychic powers and spoke nonchalantly about interacting with spirits [3-5]. He sincerely **believed he had been visited by the Virgin Mary** [5, 6]. A charming and ironic biographical detail is that he was a devoted gardener who built a beloved flower-bordered path leading to his own rectory at Middleton; following his death, **his own ghost was reportedly seen walking upon this pathway** [4]. 

**Connection to Borley and the Foysters**
Luget knew the previous rector, the Rev. Harry Bull, who had freely discussed the ghost nun with him [7]. Later, he was invited to the Rectory by the Rev. Lionel Foyster, who was worried about how the hauntings were affecting his young wife, Marianne [6]. Luget defended Marianne against the village's hostility, attributing her unpopularity to **"narrow-mindedness" and "just plain envy"** over an older rector having a much younger, vivacious wife [5]. He noted that she brought a **"touch of lightness and merriment to the rather sombre Rectory"** and observed that she was rarely frightened by the phenomena, but rather fascinated by it, only showing anxiety when loud crashes occurred or there were signs of fire [5]. 

**Paranormal Experiences at Borley**
Luget told investigators he had visited scores of haunted houses, but had never encountered anything like Borley [6]. During his visits, he claimed to be a firsthand witness to an incredible array of manifestations:
*   **Apparitions:** He personally saw the famous phantom nun [6]. Furthermore, while walking along an upstairs corridor with Marianne, they both saw the **figure of a man in a plum-coloured dressing-gown** emerge from a room and completely vanish. (When Marianne later asked a local resident if Harry Bull had owned such a garment, she was told, "Yes, indeed, he almost lived in it") [8]. 
*   **Poltergeist Activity & Apports:** He witnessed the **apportation of an empty bottle** from nowhere and the **levitation of a pencil** [3, 6]. He was also pelted by a handful of pebbles that chased him and Lionel Foyster down the main staircase [9]. 
*   **Phantom Messages:** After inspecting a room and turning to leave, he heard a sound and found a freshly written sheet of paper bearing the name "Marianne" in three-inch letters [6]. On another occasion in the Blue Room, he, Lionel, and Marianne heard a scribbling sound behind them; they turned just in time to see a pencil drop to the floor and a newly written message on the wall asking for prayers, prompting all three to drop to their knees [8].
*   **Auditory & Olfactory Phenomena:** He heard ghostly music and singing coming from empty rooms, and once investigated a burning smell in the kitchen passage that had no discernible origin or smoke [3, 6, 9].

**Exorcisms, Rituals, and Contradictions**
Luget attempted several intense exorcisms at the Rectory. In one instance, he and a brother clergyman went through the house using **incense and holy water obtained specially from the Well of Our Lady of Walsingham** [10]. They performed an elaborate exorcism rite, which they followed up with a **"complete fumigation of the house with creosote"** [10]. The next day, they enlisted two more priests to sprinkle holy water, bless, and exorcise every single room [10]. 

The sources record a slight contradiction regarding the effectiveness of his interventions. In his *Diary of Occurrences*, Lionel Foyster recorded that after Father "Lugent" visited, said prayers, and bade evil spirits depart, **"The house was much quieter on the night after his visit"** [1]. However, investigator Peter Underwood noted that Luget's extreme exorcism measures ultimately had "little effect" on the enduring phenomena [3, 10]. 

Luget's experiences were officially recorded when he was interviewed at his home by psychical investigators Peter Underwood and Tom Brown in 1948, details of which were later published in *Borley Postscript* [3, 4].
