# Rev. Clive Luget

Rector of Middleton, Essex (a tiny village one mile south of Sudbury in Suffolk). Made multiple visits to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] during the 1930s and 1940s, becoming one of the most significant clerical witnesses of the [[marianne-foyster|Foyster period]]. Described by Underwood as "a remote and lonely man... kind and thoughtful but one moment talking of the Virgin Mary visiting him and the next completely rational."

## First Visit and Subsequent Visits

Luget first visited at the invitation of [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]], who was worried that the ghosts would affect his much younger wife. He had visited scores of haunted houses but "had never encountered anything like Borley Rectory." He returned many times, always enjoying theological discussion with Lionel and finding Marianne "delightful, always cheerful and charming, an excellent hostess."

## Personal Phenomena Witnessed

### Ghost Nun
Luget personally saw the [[the-nun|ghost nun]] on at least one occasion.

### "Marianne" Written on Blank Paper
About to leave a room he had examined minutely, Luget thought he heard a sound behind him, turned, and found "a sheet of paper in the middle of the room" bearing a pencilled "Marianne" in letters about three inches high. The room had been empty and checked.

### Blue Room Pencil
In the Blue Room with Marianne and Lionel, all three heard "a scribbling sound from the room behind them," turned to see a pencil drop to the floor, and found a message asking for prayers in 2–3 inch letters on the wall. All three dropped to their knees and prayed. Nothing further happened.

### Harry Bull's Dressing-Gown
Walking with Marianne in an upstairs corridor, both saw "the figure of a man in a plum-coloured dressing-gown come out of a room ahead of them" — and then completely disappear. Later, Mrs Payne of Borley Place (who had known the Bulls for years) confirmed that [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] "almost lived in" a plum-coloured dressing-gown.

### Stone-Throwing
Pelted with pebbles coming down the main stairs with no one above; the child was in the kitchen.

### Localised Burning Smell
Traced a burning smell to the kitchen passage; nothing found — no smoke or smell elsewhere, no evident origin.

## Attempted Exorcisms

Luget and a brother clergyman performed at least three escalating attempts:
1. Holy water from the Well of Our Lady of Walsingham, with a mild exorcism — no effect.
2. An elaborate exorcism rite, plus complete creosote fumigation — no effect.
3. Four priests together blessing the entire house from top to bottom — no appreciable effect.

## Assessment of Marianne

Luget's portrait of [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] is one of the more sympathetic in the literature. He saw her as:
- Not frightened by the phenomena but actively interested and seeking an explanation
- Very protective of the children at all times
- Showing anxiety only at signs of fire or very loud noises
- "Flirtatious" in a way that helped with visitors — "exaggerated and not understood by some villagers"
- Entirely capable and attentive to Lionel despite the strain

He attributed her unpopularity in the village partly to "narrow-mindedness" at a Bull-family rector being married to a much younger woman, and partly to "plain envy."
