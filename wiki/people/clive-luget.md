# Rev. Clive Luget

Rector of Middleton, Essex (a tiny village one mile south of Sudbury in Suffolk). Made multiple visits to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] during the 1930s and 1940s, becoming one of the most significant clerical witnesses of the [[marianne-foyster|Foyster period]]. Described by [[peter-underwood|Underwood]] as "a remote and lonely man... kind and thoughtful but one moment talking of the Virgin Mary visiting him and the next completely rational." Also recorded in the sources as **Father Clive Lugent** or **Revd Clive Lugret** [1-3].

## Biographical Notes

Regarded as a "great authority" on paranormal matters, Luget possessed psychic powers and spoke nonchalantly about interacting with spirits [3-5]. He sincerely **believed he had been visited by the Virgin Mary** [5, 6]. He was a devoted gardener who built a beloved flower-bordered path leading to his own rectory at Middleton; following his death, **his own ghost was reportedly seen walking upon this pathway** [4].

## First Visit and Subsequent Visits

Luget knew the previous rector, the [[harry-bull|Rev. Harry Bull]], who had freely discussed the ghost nun with him [7]. He was then invited to the Rectory by [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]], who was worried that the hauntings were affecting his young wife. Luget had visited scores of haunted houses but "had never encountered anything like Borley Rectory" [6]. He returned many times, always enjoying theological discussion with Lionel and finding [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] "delightful, always cheerful and charming, an excellent hostess."

## Personal Phenomena Witnessed

**Ghost Nun.** Luget personally saw the [[the-nun|ghost nun]] on at least one occasion [6].

**Harry Bull's Dressing-Gown.** Walking with Marianne in an upstairs corridor, both saw "the figure of a man in a plum-coloured dressing-gown come out of a room ahead of them" — and then completely disappear [8]. When Marianne later asked a local resident if [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] had owned such a garment, she was told: "Yes, indeed, he almost lived in it" [8].

**"Marianne" Written on Blank Paper.** About to leave a room he had examined minutely, Luget thought he heard a sound behind him, turned, and found "a sheet of paper in the middle of the room" bearing a pencilled "Marianne" in letters about three inches high. The room had been empty and checked [6].

**Blue Room Pencil.** In the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] with Marianne and Lionel, all three heard "a scribbling sound from the room behind them," turned to see a pencil drop to the floor, and found a message asking for prayers in 2–3 inch letters on the wall [8]. All three dropped to their knees and prayed.

**Poltergeist Activity and Apports.** He witnessed the apportation of an empty bottle from nowhere and the levitation of a pencil [3, 6]. He was pelted by a handful of pebbles that chased him and Lionel Foyster down the main staircase [9].

**Localised Burning Smell.** Traced a burning smell to the kitchen passage; nothing found — no smoke or smell elsewhere, no evident origin [3, 6, 9].

**Ghostly Music.** He heard ghostly music and singing coming from empty rooms [3, 6, 9].

## Attempted Exorcisms

Luget and a brother clergyman performed at least three escalating attempts, eventually enlisting two more priests:

1. Holy water from the Well of Our Lady of Walsingham, with a mild exorcism — no effect.
2. An elaborate exorcism rite, plus a **"complete fumigation of the house with creosote"** — no effect.
3. Four priests together blessing and exorcising every single room from top to bottom — no appreciable effect [10].

> **Contradiction:** Lionel Foyster recorded in his *Diary of Occurrences* that after Father "Lugent" visited and bade evil spirits depart, **"The house was much quieter on the night after his visit"** [1]. However, Underwood noted that Luget's extreme measures ultimately had "little effect" on the enduring phenomena [3, 10].

## Assessment of Marianne

Luget's portrait of Marianne is one of the more sympathetic in the literature. He saw her as:
- Not frightened by the phenomena but actively interested and seeking an explanation
- Very protective of the children at all times
- Showing anxiety only at signs of fire or very loud noises
- "Flirtatious" in a way that helped with visitors — "exaggerated and not understood by some villagers"
- Entirely capable and attentive to Lionel despite the strain

He attributed her unpopularity in the village partly to **"narrow-mindedness"** and partly to **"just plain envy"** over an older rector having a much younger, vivacious wife [5], and observed that she brought "a touch of lightness and merriment to the rather sombre Rectory" [5].

Luget's experiences were officially recorded when he was interviewed at his home by Peter Underwood and Tom Brown in 1948, details of which were later published in *Borley Postscript* [3, 4].
