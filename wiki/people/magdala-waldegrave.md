# Magdala Waldegrave (d.1598)

Third daughter of Sir [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Edward Waldegrave]] and Frances Neville. She married **John Southcote** and died in 1598. Commemorated by a painted memorial tablet on the north wall of the chancel in [[borley-church|Borley Church]].

## The Memorial Tablet

The painted tablet was installed after her death in 1598. It depicts Magdala kneeling in prayer, wearing:
- A flat headdress and ruff
- Tight-fitting bodice and loose skirt
- A sleeveless mantle over her shoulders

The monument is flanked by Ionic columns, with a shield of arms above the cornice.

## Significance

Magdala's tablet provides physical evidence — visible in Borley Church to this day — of the Waldegrave family's intimate connection with the church across generations. The [[waldegrave-monument|Waldegrave monument]] in the nave commemorates her father Sir Edward; her own tablet on the chancel wall commemorates her separately.

[[harry-price|Price]] notes that in addition to the famous altar-tomb of Sir Edward, the church contains Magdala's tablet — establishing that at least two members of the Waldegrave family had permanent memorial presence in Borley Church. The coffins of the family were likely interred in the crypt beneath.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Magdala Waldegrave** (d. 1598) was the **third daughter of Sir Edward Waldegrave** and the **wife of John Southcote** [1]. 

The Borley sources record her primarily in relation to her historic memorial within Borley Church. Following her death in 1598, a **painted tablet bearing her effigy** was erected to her memory [1, 2].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific details about her monument:

**Location and Architectural Features**
*   The monument is set into the **north wall of the chancel** in Borley Church [1-3]. 
*   It is flanked by **Ionic columns** and features a **shield of arms above the cornice** [1]. 
*   Directly above Magdala's head, the tablet depicts **"cherubs encircled with rays of glory"** [2].

**The Effigy's Pose and Clothing**
*   **The Pose:** The sources offer slightly varying but complementary descriptions of her posture. One text records that the effigy is shown **"kneeling in prayer"** [1], while another provides the more specific detail that she is **"kneeling on a cushion with an open book before her"** [2].
*   **The Clothing:** Both detailed accounts agree exactly on her Elizabethan attire, describing her as wearing a **flat headdress, a ruff, a tight-fitting bodice, a loose skirt, and a sleeveless mantle** draped over her shoulders [1, 2].
