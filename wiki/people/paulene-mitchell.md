# Paulene Mitchell

Canadian journalist and book reviewer who, together with [[iris-owen|Iris Owen]], conducted the key post-Borley interview with [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] in 1979, and also found [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide Tower Cartwright]].

## Background

Paulene Mitchell (frequently spelled **Pauline Mitchell** in the sources) was a book reviewer and columnist for a local newspaper in Hamilton, Canada, located about sixty miles from Toronto [1]. Unlike most figures involved in the Borley case, she had no prior knowledge of paranormal phenomena, psychical research, or the background of the story [1]. She became intrigued by the haunting simply by picking up a book on the subject, specifically regarding the whereabouts of [[lionel-foyster|Lionel]] and [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]]'s adopted daughter, Adelaide [1, 2].

## Finding Adelaide

Mitchell made the first significant discovery: in **1976**, she wrote a letter to a local newspaper in New Brunswick, Canada, asking for information about the Tower family [2]. The publication of this letter quickly put her in touch with **[[edna-tower|Edna Tower]]**, Adelaide's biological sister, who explained that Adelaide had unhappy memories of her life at Borley Rectory and did not wish to speak about it — and that Marianne had kept Adelaide separated from her blood relatives [2].

## Tracing Marianne

Continuing her search, Mitchell successfully achieved what many seasoned investigators (including [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]]) had long struggled to do: she tracked down [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]], who was living under the name Marianne O'Neil in La Crosse, Wisconsin [1, 3]. Marianne was shocked to have been traced and thought her Borley past was firmly behind her [3, 4].

Realising she needed expert assistance, Mitchell reached out to **[[iris-owen|Iris M. Owen]]**, the Assistant Program Director of the New Horizons Research Foundation in Toronto.

> **Contradiction:** One source states Mitchell called Owen in 1976 [2], while another places her request for help in January 1977 [1].

## The Marianne Interviews (1979)

Mitchell and Owen met Marianne at the Stoddard Hotel in La Crosse, Wisconsin [5, 6]. Marianne only agreed to share her version of events under two strict conditions: first, that Mitchell and Owen would do everything in their power to ensure her son Vince (her son [[vernon-oneil|Robert Vincent O'Neil]]) never learned of her Borley past; and second, that nothing would be published until after her death [4, 6]. They continued to interview her by telephone and letter over subsequent months, and Marianne answered questions relatively freely within those constraints.

Mitchell and Owen secured a grant from the Perrott-Warrick Fellowship of Trinity College, Cambridge, in the **autumn of 1978** to research and publish Marianne's story [10–12]. They produced an unpublished typescript in **March 1979** titled *The Alleged Haunting of Borley Rectory* [10]. An abridged version was subsequently published in the *Journal of the Society for Psychical Research* in **September 1979** [10, 11, 13].

During the interviews, Marianne flatly denied the haunting, claiming that the majority of the poltergeist events were completely invented by her husband Lionel as part of a fictional book he was writing for financial gain [7–9]. She did, however, admit that some minor poltergeist activity occurred that she could not explain [7, 8].

Mitchell and Owen fully believed Marianne's account, concluding their report: **"We feel that what Marianne has told us makes sense. It is logical, and, what is more important, everything she has told us fits in with what is already known about the Borley hauntings during the Foyster incumbency. To us it has the ring of truth"** [14, 15].

The interviews provided [[iris-owen|Iris Owen]] with the material for the **New Horizons Foundation Report** (published 1986/1991), which introduced the "admixture of truth and fraud" framework for interpreting the Borley phenomena.

## Criticism and Scepticism

Mitchell and Owen's report was met with fierce backlash from other Borley historians, who felt the two Canadian researchers were naive to trust Marianne Foyster's testimony.

[[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] replied to the report in June 1980, arguing that Mitchell and Owen **"had dropped into the trap of taking Marianne Foyster at face value, which is risky to say the least"** [16]. He firmly stated that any impartial inquirer must find Marianne **"unreliable and decidedly unimpressive as a witness to anything"** [17].

Robert Wood (author of *The Widow of Borley*) was equally scathing, stating that Mitchell and Owen **"try to present an absurdly charitable picture of Marianne"** and had **"simply ignore[d] the evidence"** of her long history of pathological lying, bigamy, and deceit [18, 19]. Wood criticised them for losing sight of the real world outside psychical research and doctoring the facts to suit their own charitable prejudices [18, 20].
