# Paulene Mitchell

Toronto book reviewer and researcher who, together with [[iris-owen|Iris Owen]], conducted the key post-Borley interview with [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] in 1979, and also found [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide Tower Cartwright]].

## Background

Paulene Mitchell was a book reviewer in Toronto, Canada, who became intrigued by the Borley case after reading about it. She contacted [[iris-owen|Iris Owen]], an experienced physical researcher and British immigrant to Canada, to collaborate.

## Finding Adelaide

Paulene made the first significant discovery: she tracked down [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide Tower Cartwright]] — [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]]'s adopted daughter, now living in Manchester, England. She wrote to the local newspaper in New Brunswick (where the Tower family was from) asking for information about Adelaide's biological family. Within weeks she was in contact with **[[edna-tower|Edna Tower]]**, Adelaide's sister, who explained that Adelaide had unhappy memories of her life at Borley Rectory and did not wish to speak about it.

## The Marianne Interviews (1979)

With Iris Owen, Paulene then traced [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] in La Crosse, Wisconsin. Marianne was initially very reluctant — she had been badly treated by earlier investigators and was terrified of exposure. But Paulene eventually persuaded her that by giving her own version of events she would finally stop the continual speculation.

They met Marianne at the Stoddard Hotel in La Crosse. Paulene and Iris continued to interview her by telephone and letter over subsequent months. Marianne wrote full accounts of her life and answered questions relatively freely, with two conditions: Vince (her son [[vernon-oneil|Robert Vincent O'Neil]]) must not be contacted, and nothing was to be published until after her death.

The interviews provided [[iris-owen|Iris Owen]] with the material for the **New Horizons Foundation Report** (published 1986/1991), which introduced the "admixture of truth and fraud" framework for interpreting the Borley phenomena.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Paulene Mitchell** (frequently spelled **Pauline Mitchell** in the sources) was a Canadian journalist who played a pivotal role in the latter years of the Borley Rectory saga by tracking down and interviewing the Rectory's most elusive surviving witness, Marianne Foyster. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, events, dates, quotes, and controversies regarding her involvement:

**Biographical Details and Background**
*   **Occupation:** In the late 1970s, Mitchell was a book reviewer and columnist for a local newspaper in Hamilton, Canada, located about 60 miles from Toronto [1].
*   **Lack of Psychic Background:** Unlike most figures involved in the case, she had absolutely no prior knowledge of paranormal phenomena, psychical research, or the background of the Borley story [1]. 

**Tracking Down the Foysters (1976–1977)**
Mitchell's entry into the Borley mystery began simply because she had picked up a book on the haunting and became intrigued, specifically regarding the whereabouts of Lionel and Marianne Foyster's adopted daughter, Adelaide [1, 2].
*   In **1976**, Mitchell wrote a letter to a local newspaper in New Brunswick, Canada. The publication of this letter quickly put her in touch with Edna Tower, Adelaide's biological sister, uncovering the fact that Marianne had kept Adelaide separated from her blood relatives [2].
*   Continuing her search, Mitchell successfully achieved what many seasoned investigators (including Trevor Hall) had long struggled to do: she tracked down Marianne Foyster, who was living under the name Marianne O'Neil in La Crosse, Wisconsin [1, 3].
*   Realizing she needed expert assistance, Mitchell reached out to **Iris M. Owen**, the Assistant Program Director of the New Horizons Research Foundation in Toronto. *(Note: The sources contain a minor date contradiction regarding this contact; one source states Mitchell called Owen in 1976 [2], while another places her request for help in January 1977 [1])*

**The La Crosse Interviews**
Mitchell made the initial contact with Marianne, who was shocked to have been traced and thought her Borley past was firmly behind her [3, 4]. 
*   Marianne eventually agreed to meet with Mitchell and Owen at the Stoddard Hotel (or a local hotel) in La Crosse, Wisconsin [5, 6].
*   **Conditions of the Interview:** Marianne only agreed to share her version of the events under two strict conditions: first, that Mitchell and Owen would do everything in their power to ensure her son Vince never learned of her Borley past; and second, that they would not publish anything about the interview until after her death [4, 6]. 

**The 1979 Report and Quotes**
During the interviews, Marianne flatly denied the haunting, claiming that the majority of the poltergeist events were completely invented by her husband, Lionel Foyster, as part of a fictional book he was writing for financial gain [7-9]. She did, however, admit that some minor poltergeist activity occurred that she could not explain [7, 8].
*   Mitchell and Owen secured a grant from the electors to the Perrott-Warrick Fellowship of Trinity College, Cambridge, in the **autumn of 1978** to research and publish Marianne's story [10-12]. 
*   They produced an unpublished typescript in **March 1979** titled *The Alleged Haunting of Borley Rectory* [10]. An abridged version was subsequently published in the *Journal of the Society for Psychical Research* (SPR) in **September 1979** [10, 11, 13].
*   Mitchell and Owen fully believed Marianne's new version of events, concluding their report with the following specific quote: **"We feel that what Marianne has told us makes sense. It is logical, and, what is more important, everything she has told us fits in with what is already known about the Borley hauntings during the Foyster incumbency. To us it has the ring of truth"** [14, 15].

**Criticism and Scepticism**
Mitchell and Owen's report was met with fierce backlash from other Borley historians, who felt the two Canadian researchers were incredibly naive to trust Marianne Foyster's testimony.
*   **Peter Underwood** replied to the report in June 1980, arguing that Mitchell and Owen **"had dropped into the trap of taking Marianne Foyster at face value, which is risky to say the least"** [16]. He firmly stated that any impartial inquirer must find Marianne **"unreliable and decidedly unimpressive as a witness to anything"** [17].
*   **Robert Wood** (author of *The Widow of Borley*) was equally scathing, stating that Mitchell and Owen **"try to present an absurdly charitable picture of Marianne"** and had **"simply ignore[d] the evidence"** of her long history of pathological lying, bigamy, and deceit [18, 19]. Wood criticized them for losing sight of the real world outside psychical research and doctoring the facts to suit their own charitable prejudices [18, 20].
