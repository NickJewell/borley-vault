# Paulene Mitchell

Toronto book reviewer and researcher who, together with [[iris-owen|Iris Owen]], conducted the key post-Borley interview with [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] in 1979, and also found [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide Tower Cartwright]].

## Background

Paulene Mitchell was a book reviewer in Toronto, Canada, who became intrigued by the Borley case after reading about it. She contacted [[iris-owen|Iris Owen]], an experienced physical researcher and British immigrant to Canada, to collaborate.

## Finding Adelaide

Paulene made the first significant discovery: she tracked down [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide Tower Cartwright]] — [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]]'s adopted daughter, now living in Manchester, England. She wrote to the local newspaper in New Brunswick (where the Tower family was from) asking for information about Adelaide's biological family. Within weeks she was in contact with **[[edna-tower|Edna Tower]]**, Adelaide's sister, who explained that Adelaide had unhappy memories of her life at Borley Rectory and did not wish to speak about it.

## The Marianne Interviews (1979)

With Iris Owen, Paulene then traced [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] in La Crosse, Wisconsin. Marianne was initially very reluctant — she had been badly treated by earlier investigators and was terrified of exposure. But Paulene eventually persuaded her that by giving her own version of events she would finally stop the continual speculation.

They met Marianne at the Stoddard Hotel in La Crosse. Paulene and Iris continued to interview her by telephone and letter over subsequent months. Marianne wrote full accounts of her life and answered questions relatively freely, with two conditions: Vince (her son [[vernon-oneil|Robert Vincent O'Neil]]) must not be contacted, and nothing was to be published until after her death.

The interviews provided [[iris-owen|Iris Owen]] with the material for the **New Horizons Foundation Report** (published 1986/1991), which introduced the "admixture of truth and fraud" framework for interpreting the Borley phenomena.
