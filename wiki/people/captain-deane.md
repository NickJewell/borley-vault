# Captain V.M. Deane

Captain V.M. Deane resided at "The Willows" in Braiswick, Colchester. A dedicated psychical researcher, he was a member of [[harry-price]]'s [[ghost-club]] (holding Membership Number 136) and a much-respected participant in the [[marks-tey-spiritualist-circle]]. He studied the Borley case for many years and visited the haunted Rectory on several occasions. He frequently provided transportation for the Circle's mediums and members to Borley Rectory, on one occasion apologising to the Foysters for arriving unannounced because a "message came from the other side for us to go over at once" [3, 4]. He published his findings in articles titled "Borley Rectory Problems," which appeared in *Light* (January 1941) and *Psychic Science* (April 1941) [6, 7].

## Views on Borley

Deane theorised that the [[foyster-phenomena]] during the Foyster incumbency was consistent with what one would expect from a "demented female" spirit — more precisely, that the spectacular poltergeist activity was "more or less consistent with the actions one would expect from a demented female" [1, 6, 12]. He further proposed an "agent" theory: that the percipients (the Foysters, the mediums) might unconsciously act as agents through whom an unknown physical power drew its energy to produce manifestations [11].

Deane felt sure that the Marks Tey group had been successful in laying the ghost following their January 1932 visit, which brought the *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House* period to a close. However, he noted that after they banished the entity from the Rectory, the trouble reportedly transferred to Marks Tey, where it severely affected the health of the Circle's leader, Mr. Warren (who eventually died), while paradoxically restoring the health of the invalid [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] [13].

Deane was present at a séance in the Rectory on 26 April 1932 when the medium was apparently controlled by the ghost nun herself.

Deane believed the "demented nun" theory proved the existence of an afterlife, but cautioned that it proved a future life is "not necessarily all 'Peace, perfect peace'." He concluded his 1941 article with the ominous warning to sceptics: "Beware! Beware! Beware!" [12].

## Records and Cross-Examinations

Deane was meticulous in his record-keeping, possessing the entire shorthand records of the Marks Tey Circle's séances for the twelve months of 1932 [2, 7, 9, 10]. He stated that he had "cross-examined the principal witnesses for hours on end" [2, 9, 10].

[[peter-underwood]] talked with Captain Deane at a Ghost Club meeting in London in 1948 [1, 8]. Deane stated he "believed that there was not the slightest shadow of a doubt that in full lamplight bottles and stones fell amongst batches of from three to five witnesses who saw, heard and handled them" [2, 10]. This is a significant endorsement from an experienced investigator who was present for some of the most spectacular phenomena of the Foyster period.

## Contradictions with the Accepted Borley Narrative

Deane's records contain two significant contradictions with the established [[harry-price]] narrative:

**The Nun's Identity:** While Price championed the theory that the ghost was a French nun named [[marie-lairre|"Marie Lairre"]], Deane noted that during the Marks Tey séances, the nun actually identified herself as "Evangeline Westcott" (and her father as "John Westcott") [14]. Because these names contradicted Price's books and could not be verified in local ecclesiastical records, Deane suggested they be "pigeon-holed for the time being" [14].

> **Contradiction:** Deane recorded a private conversation with Price at the [[psychic-fete-1939|Borley psychic fête]] in June 1939, in which Price allegedly admitted that "he accepted the phenomena as veridical but preferred the physical to the spiritual theory as the explanation" [15, 16]. This sharply contradicted Price's later public claim in *The End of Borley Rectory* that the "spirit hypothesis" was the most reasonable solution [15–17].
