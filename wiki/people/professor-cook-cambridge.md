# Professor Cook (Cambridge)

A Cambridge University academic who investigated the alleged haunting at [[borley-rectory]] during the [[lionel-foyster]] incumbency and concluded the phenomena had a normal human cause. His visit was never mentioned in [[harry-price]]'s books, making him one of the deliberately suppressed investigative voices of the Foyster period.

## Investigation and findings

The visit is recorded only through a letter from Lionel Foyster's brother, Arthur Foyster, to Sidney Glanville (preserved in the Locked Book), written in August 1937:

> *'Prof. Cook of Cambridge, whom I met at Aldeburgh, told me he had been to Borley, and investigated the matter, while the phenomena were supposed to be active and found there was nothing in it. [He] was apparently rather keen on this sort of thing and only came to the conclusion he did after a very thorough investigation of the full particulars he got and with great disappointment.'*
> — A.H. Foyster to Sidney Glanville, 28 August 1937, quoted in *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, essay 12

Clarke notes that Cook's investigation caused "so much upset that it was left out of the Foysters' subsequent chronicle." His conclusion — reached after "a very thorough investigation" and apparently with genuine regret — aligned with those of nearly every other informed visitor during the Foyster period, including [[harry-price]] himself on his second visit, the [[spr-investigation]], and John Braithwaite.

## Context

Professor Cook was not alone. Clarke observes in essay 12 that "almost without exception" the investigators who visited during the Foyster period, including the SPR team led by [[william-salter]], concluded the phenomena were produced by human agency. What distinguished Cook's visit was that it was sufficiently definitive to be suppressed not only from Price's books but also from Lionel Foyster's own account.

The fact that Arthur Foyster met Cook casually at Aldeburgh and learned of his investigation suggests Cook had no ongoing contact with the Borley circle and his findings were not formally reported anywhere.

## Significance

Cook represents the pattern identified throughout [[andrew-clarke]]'s Sidelights: the systematic suppression of negative findings during the Foyster period. He is one of the clearest examples of an investigator who visited, concluded nothing supernatural was occurring, and was then written out of the record.

> **Contradiction:** Cook's investigation is unrecorded in Price's *The Most Haunted House in England* or *The End of Borley Rectory*, despite both books presenting Borley as an exhaustively documented case.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Professor Cook** of Cambridge University was an academic who conducted one of the most important early investigations of Borley Rectory during the mid-1930s [1, 2]. Despite his thorough examination of the case, his findings were largely suppressed in contemporary accounts because they conflicted with the paranormal narrative.

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following details regarding his involvement:

**Investigation and Skeptical Conclusions**
*   **Introduction to the Case:** Professor Cook was introduced to the Borley haunting by William H. Salter, an investigator known for his skepticism regarding the phenomena and his hostility toward Harry Price [1].
*   **The Investigation:** Cook was described as being "rather keen on these sort of things" and approached the haunting with a genuine interest [3]. He visited Borley Rectory while the phenomena were supposedly active, conducting a "very thorough investigation of the full particulars" and interviewing witnesses in the early stages of the case [1-3]. 
*   **His Verdict:** Following his extensive examination, Cook found himself unable to believe in the haunting [1]. He concluded with "great disappointment" that "there was nothing in it" [2, 3]. 

**Omission from the Borley Literature**
Cook's skeptical conclusions "very much annoyed" the rector, the Rev. Lionel Foyster [3]. Because his findings caused so much upset, Cook's visit was deliberately left out of the Foysters' subsequent written chronicles of the haunting [2]. Furthermore, Cook's opinions and findings are completely omitted from the "orthodox" Borley literature published by Harry Price and others [3].

**Interactions with A.H. Foyster**
What is known of Professor Cook's investigation comes primarily from the records of Lionel Foyster's brother, A.H. Foyster [2]. 
*   A.H. Foyster met Professor Cook at Aldeburgh, where Cook personally relayed his disappointing conclusions regarding the Rectory [2, 3]. 
*   In an August 28, 1937, letter to investigator Sidney Glanville (which was preserved in the famous *Locked Book*), A.H. Foyster detailed Cook's findings and added a revealing postscript: **"I never mentioned to my brother that I met Prof. Cook as I understand that Cook’s conclusions very much annoyed him"** [3].

**The 1936 Dressing Gown Discussion**
The sources record one specific instance of Cook interviewing a witness alongside A.H. Foyster. In 1936, the two men jointly discussed a bizarre incident with a female acquaintance of the Foysters who had previously called at the Rectory [4]. Marianne Foyster had told the woman that her husband was out, but the caller insisted she was mistaken, providing the quote: **"I saw him in an upstairs room—he was wearing a dressing gown"** [4].
