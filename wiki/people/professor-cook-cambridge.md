# Professor Cook (Cambridge)

A Cambridge University academic who investigated the alleged haunting at [[borley-rectory]] during the [[lionel-foyster]] incumbency and concluded the phenomena had a normal human cause. His visit was never mentioned in [[harry-price]]'s books, making him one of the deliberately suppressed investigative voices of the Foyster period.

## Investigation and findings

The visit is recorded only through a letter from Lionel Foyster's brother, Arthur Foyster, to Sidney Glanville (preserved in the Locked Book), written in August 1937:

> *'Prof. Cook of Cambridge, whom I met at Aldeburgh, told me he had been to Borley, and investigated the matter, while the phenomena were supposed to be active and found there was nothing in it. [He] was apparently rather keen on this sort of thing and only came to the conclusion he did after a very thorough investigation of the full particulars he got and with great disappointment.'*
> — A.H. Foyster to Sidney Glanville, 28 August 1937, quoted in *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, essay 12

Clarke notes that Cook's investigation caused "so much upset that it was left out of the Foysters' subsequent chronicle." His conclusion — reached after "a very thorough investigation" and apparently with genuine regret — aligned with those of nearly every other informed visitor during the Foyster period, including [[harry-price]] himself on his second visit, the [[spr-investigation]], and John Braithwaite.

## Context

Professor Cook was not alone. Clarke observes in essay 12 that "almost without exception" the investigators who visited during the Foyster period, including the SPR team led by [[william-salter]], concluded the phenomena were produced by human agency. What distinguished Cook's visit was that it was sufficiently definitive to be suppressed not only from Price's books but also from Lionel Foyster's own account.

The fact that Arthur Foyster met Cook casually at Aldeburgh and learned of his investigation suggests Cook had no ongoing contact with the Borley circle and his findings were not formally reported anywhere.

## Significance

Cook represents the pattern identified throughout [[andrew-clarke]]'s Sidelights: the systematic suppression of negative findings during the Foyster period. He is one of the clearest examples of an investigator who visited, concluded nothing supernatural was occurring, and was then written out of the record.

> **Contradiction:** Cook's investigation is unrecorded in Price's *The Most Haunted House in England* or *The End of Borley Rectory*, despite both books presenting Borley as an exhaustively documented case.
