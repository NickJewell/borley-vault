# Professor Cook (Cambridge)

A Cambridge University academic who investigated the alleged haunting at [[borley-rectory]] during the [[lionel-foyster]] incumbency and concluded the phenomena had a normal human cause. His visit was never mentioned in [[harry-price]]'s books, making him one of the deliberately suppressed investigative voices of the Foyster period.

## Investigation and Findings

Professor Cook was introduced to the Borley haunting by [[william-salter|William H. Salter]], an investigator known for his scepticism regarding the phenomena and his hostility toward Harry Price [1]. Cook was described as being "rather keen on these sort of things" and approached the haunting with genuine interest [3]. He visited Borley Rectory while the phenomena were supposedly active during the early stages of the case, conducting a "very thorough investigation of the full particulars" and interviewing witnesses [1–3].

Following his extensive examination, Cook concluded with "great disappointment" that "there was nothing in it" [2, 3]. His sceptical conclusions "very much annoyed" the rector, the Rev. Lionel Foyster [3], and because his findings caused so much upset, Cook's visit was deliberately left out of the Foysters' subsequent written chronicles of the haunting [2]. His opinions are also completely omitted from the "orthodox" Borley literature published by Harry Price and others [3].

The visit is recorded only through a letter from Lionel Foyster's brother, [[arthur-foyster|Arthur Foyster]], to [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] (preserved in the Locked Book), written in August 1937:

> *'Prof. Cook of Cambridge, whom I met at Aldeburgh, told me he had been to Borley, and investigated the matter, while the phenomena were supposed to be active and found there was nothing in it. [He] was apparently rather keen on this sort of thing and only came to the conclusion he did after a very thorough investigation of the full particulars he got and with great disappointment.'*
> — A.H. Foyster to Sidney Glanville, 28 August 1937, quoted in *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, essay 12

Arthur Foyster added a revealing postscript: **"I never mentioned to my brother that I met Prof. Cook as I understand that Cook's conclusions very much annoyed him"** [3].

The fact that A. H. Foyster met Cook casually at Aldeburgh suggests Cook had no ongoing contact with the Borley circle and his findings were not formally reported anywhere.

## The 1936 Dressing Gown Discussion

The sources record one specific instance of Cook interviewing a witness alongside A. H. Foyster. In 1936, the two men jointly discussed a bizarre incident with a female acquaintance of the Foysters who had previously called at the Rectory [4]. Marianne Foyster had told the woman that her husband was out, but the caller insisted she was mistaken, providing the quote: **"I saw him in an upstairs room—he was wearing a dressing gown"** [4].

## Context and Significance

Professor Cook was not alone. [[andrew-clarke|Andrew Clarke]] observes in essay 12 that "almost without exception" the investigators who visited during the Foyster period — including the SPR team led by William Salter — concluded the phenomena were produced by human agency. What distinguished Cook's visit was that it was sufficiently definitive to be suppressed not only from Price's books but also from Lionel Foyster's own account.

Cook represents the pattern identified throughout Clarke's *Sidelights*: the systematic suppression of negative findings during the Foyster period. He is one of the clearest examples of an investigator who visited, concluded nothing supernatural was occurring, and was then written out of the record.

> **Contradiction:** Cook's investigation is unrecorded in Price's *The Most Haunted House in England* or *The End of Borley Rectory*, despite both books presenting Borley as an exhaustively documented case.
