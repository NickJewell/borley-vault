# José Simmonds (née Bacon)

Daughter of Bob and Betty Bacon, who purchased and occupied [[rectory-cottage|Borley Rectory Cottage]] from [[james-turner]] in 1951. The Bacon family (including José, her brother Terry, and her maternal grandparents Mr and Mrs Williams) lived there until 1972. José was interviewed by Edward Babbs in 2002.

## Half-Figure at the Window — Late 1950s

On one occasion while descending the stairs at the cottage, José saw through the window at the foot of the stairs **the lower half of a person** walking past outside. The figure appeared to be clothed in a **full-length black skirt**, moving with a striding, masculine gait. No one should have been outside at that point. When she investigated, no one was there. The authors suggest the "skirt" may have been a priest's robes.

## Deep Breathing — Early 1960s

On an evening when she and a young man friend were sitting in the darkened living room of the cottage, **deep and heavy breathing** was heard from an empty corner of the room. They were frightened; her friend ran from the room, at which point the sounds stopped. No other living creature was present capable of producing the sound except the family's budgerigar.

## The Bacon Family at the Cottage

The family were interested in the alleged haunting and willing to discuss it with responsible visitors. Betty Bacon had spiritualist leanings and family séances were held. Her son Terry and her father also claimed to have seen the spectral nun on several occasions.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**José Simmonds (née Bacon)** was the daughter of Robert (Bob) and Betty Bacon, who owned and resided in the Borley Rectory Cottage (later called Borley Priory) during the "Later Borley" period. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, specific paranormal experiences, and later contributions regarding her time at Borley:

**Family Background and Residency**
*   **Move to Borley:** In **April 1951**, José moved into the Rectory Cottage along with her parents, her brother Terrence (Terry), and her maternal grandparents, Mr. and Mrs. Henry Williams [1-3]. Her family had purchased the cottage, the grounds, and the old Rectory site from the writer James Turner [4]. 
*   **Duration of Stay:** She and her family lived at the Borley site for over twenty years, eventually moving away in **early 1972** [2, 4].

**Participation in Séances**
During her youth at Borley, José was a direct participant in paranormal investigations. Between **1955 and 1957**, she took part in a series of planchette séances organized by the investigator **Len Sewell** [5]. These sessions, held at the family's cottage, included José, her brother Terry, her parents, her grandmother Mrs. Williams, and Sewell himself [5].

**Personal Paranormal Experiences**
In **2002**, José was interviewed by the researcher Eddie Brazil and recounted two specific, highly unusual experiences that happened to her while living at the cottage [6]:

*   **The Figure at the Window (Late 1950s):** While walking down the stairway of the cottage, José looked through a window located at the foot of the stairs and saw the lower half of a person walking past [6]. She noted a specific contradiction in the figure's appearance: **the person was clothed in a full-length black skirt, yet was "striding in a masculine fashion"** [6]. When she investigated, no one was there [6]. (Researchers later theorized that what she perceived as a black skirt may have actually been a priest's robes [6]).
*   **The Heavy Breathing (Early 1960s):** One evening, José and a young male friend were sitting together in the dark on the settee in the cottage's living room [7]. Suddenly, they heard **"deep and heavy breathing"** emanating from a corner of the room [7]. The terrifying sounds caused her friend to run out of the room, at which point the breathing abruptly stopped [7]. José noted that the sounds were completely inexplicable, as the only other living creature in the room at the time was a pet budgerigar [7].

**Later Contributions to Borley Research**
In addition to sharing her first-hand accounts in her 2002 interview, José Simmonds made a significant visual contribution to the Borley historical record. She supplied numerous original photographs (specifically listed as Figures 24, 47, 48, 49, 51, 52, 53, 61, 62, and 63) from her family's time at the site for the 2003 book *Borley Rectory: The Final Analysis* [8].
