# Canon Lambert

A clergyman who was a long-standing personal friend of the Bull family at [[borley-rectory]] and who recalled that the Bull sisters discussed the haunting stories in a conspicuously light-hearted, mischievous manner — as if the legend were a family joke rather than a genuine belief.

## Testimony

Canon Lambert is cited in [[andrew-clarke]]'s essay 11 ("Bullsheet! The Bulls at Borley Rectory") as evidence that the Bull family themselves did not take the haunting legends entirely seriously:

> *Canon Lambert, who was a family friend of the Bulls maintained that the Bull sisters used to talk to him of the hauntings, but never in a serious vein. It was as if it were an extended family joke, to be related with pride, but with a slight air of mischief.*
> — *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, essay 11

This observation is consistent with other evidence assembled by Clarke: [[dodie-bull]]'s 1885 diary contains no mention of genuine hauntings; several of the Bull brothers ([[gerald-bull]], [[alfred-richard-graham-bull]], Walter Bull) were explicitly sceptical; and [[ivy-brackenbury]] and her daughter [[constance-brackenbury]] experienced nothing in nearly ten years at the Rectory.

## Significance

Lambert's testimony supports Clarke's thesis that the haunting legend originated as a mixture of borrowed local folklore and family storytelling, which was never intended to be taken as documentary fact until the intervention of Harry Price and the Daily Mirror in 1929 transformed it into an international story. The "slight air of mischief" he detected suggests the Bull sisters were aware of the constructed quality of the legend even as they perpetuated it.

Lambert is a corroborating voice for [[p-shaw-jeffrey]]'s contrasting testimony (which was enthusiastic but written fifty years after the events) and for [[ernest-ambrose]]'s more careful recollection that the sisters were "very down to earth women, not given to exaggeration."

> **Contested:** Lambert's account is not cited in Harry Price's books, which present the Bull sisters as sincere and credible long-term witnesses to genuine phenomena.

As I noted in our previous exchange, a careful review of the provided Borley sources confirms that there is no mention of a **Canon Lambert**. 

The texts do, however, comprehensively record the activities of several other prominent Canons involved in the Borley case, including:
*   **Canon W. J. Phythian-Adams** (Canon of Carlisle, who conducted the famous analysis of the wall-writings and proposed the "Marie Lairre" theory) [1].
*   **Canon H. Lawton** (who stayed at the Rectory as a *locum tenens* for a month in 1933 and insisted no phenomena occurred) [2-4].
*   **Canon Edward Powell** (the formidable Rural Dean and vicar of Belchamp St Paul, who witnessed an apparition in the early 1960s) [5, 6].
*   **Canon J. H. Leslie Pennell** (the Scottish clergyman who took over the combined parishes of Foxearth, Pentlow, Borley, and Liston in the late 1960s) [7].
*   **Canon Harold Anson** (who corresponded with Harry Price regarding the historical plausibility of a 17th-century nunnery in England) [8].
*   **Canon A. W. Stote-Blandy** (Vicar of Colehill and Canon of Sarum, who provided an alternate interpretation of the "light" wall-writing) [9].

Additionally, the sources document exactly three individuals with the surname **Lambert**:
*   **G. W. Lambert**, who served as the President of the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) and signed the preface to the 1956 *Borley Report* [10].
*   **Richard S. Lambert**, a personal friend of investigator Harry Price and the editor of *The Listener*, who took the famous photograph of Price on the Rectory balcony and enthusiastically offered to help write the Borley book before his wife convinced him to withdraw from the project [11-13].
*   **Mr. A. V. C. Lambert**, the patron of the Liston benefice and brother of Mrs. C. Fahie (a woman who provided century-old hearsay evidence regarding the phantom nun) [14].
