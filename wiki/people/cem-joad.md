# Dr Cyril Edwin Mitchinson Joad

(Born Durham, 12 August 1891; died Hampstead, 9 April 1953) Popular philosopher and regular member of the BBC radio Brains Trust. Member of the Harry Price [[ghost-club]]. Visited [[borley-rectory]] as one of [[harry-price]]'s [[official-observers]] during the [[price-tenancy]].

## Experience at Borley

On 28 July 1937 — the traditional [[july-28th]] date of the phantom nun's annual appearance — Joad visited the Rectory. He witnessed [[wall-writings|wall writing]] and was struck by a bar of soap "mysteriously propelled" (see Chronology, 28 July 1937). This soap-throwing incident in full lamplight was considered by [[peter-underwood]] to be one of the more evidential events of the tenancy.

## Academic Position

Joad was Head of the Philosophy Department at Birkbeck College, London, between 1930 and 1953. He headed the [[university-of-london-council|University of London Council for Psychical Investigation]] in 1934 and supported [[harry-price]]'s idea of a London university chair in psychical research.

His review of [[mhh|*MHH*]] (published in *The Spectator*, 18 October 1940) is notable: it prompted [[henry-lawton|Canon Lawton]] to write a letter to the magazine stating that he had experienced nothing unusual during his month at the Rectory in 1933.

## Publications

Author of over 70 books and pamphlets published between 1917 and 1954 including *The Guide to Modern Thought* (1933), *The Book of Joad* (1943), *How To Write, Think and Speak Correctly* (1939) and *The Recovery of Belief* (1952).

## Later Life

Joad's reputation was damaged in 1948 when he was convicted of travelling on a train without a valid ticket. Though a minor offence, it was widely reported and led to his removal from the BBC Brains Trust. He died in 1953 of cancer.

[[peter-underwood]] interviewed Joad in connection with his Borley experiences.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Dr. Cyril Edwin Mitchinson Joad** (born in Durham on August 12, 1891; died in Hampstead on April 9, 1953) was a popular philosopher, Head of the Philosophy Department at Birkbeck College, London (1930–1953), and a regular panellist on the BBC radio *Brains Trust* [1, 2]. He was a prolific author who published over 70 books and pamphlets between 1917 and 1954, including *The Guide to Modern Thought* (1933) and *The Book of Joad* (1943) [2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following details regarding his psychical research background, his experiences at Borley Rectory, and the later controversies surrounding his testimony:

**Involvement in Psychical Research**
Joad was deeply involved in the academic pursuit of the paranormal alongside Harry Price. He was a member of Price's exclusive *Ghost Club* and headed the *University of London Council for Psychical Investigation* in 1934, strongly supporting Price's campaign to establish a London university chair for the subject [1, 2]. 

A lesser-known and rather bizarre biographical detail in the sources reveals that Joad once travelled to the Hartz mountains with Harry Price, where the two men attempted—unsuccessfully—to **transform a goat into a "fair youth" with the aid of a magic spell** [3].

**The Borley Visit (July 28, 1937)**
Joad visited Borley Rectory as one of Harry Price's official observers [1, 4]. He conducted his observational period on **July 28, 1937** (the traditional "anniversary" of the ghost nun's appearances), alongside Mr. A.C. Elliot Smith and the Rev. Alfred Henning [5, 6]. During this single visit, Joad experienced two notable paranormal phenomena:
*   **The Phantom Squiggles:** Joad was "considerably intrigued" by the mysterious wall-writings [7]. He and fellow observer Mark Kerr-Pearse made a careful tour of all existing pencil marks on the whitewashed walls, then securely locked themselves inside the Base Room for an hour to cook sausages and drink tea [8]. When they emerged around 8:00 p.m., they discovered a fresh pencil squiggle on the wall in the passage immediately outside their door [8]. Joad reported being "reasonably certain" that the mark had not been there before, that his companion did not make it, and that no intruder could have entered the sealed house unheard [8].
*   **The Flying Soap and the Temperature Drop:** While stationed in a sealed room, **Joad was struck on the cheek by a piece of soap** that appeared to be "mysteriously propelled" [1]. Joad later discussed this visit with his fellow *Brains Trust* panellist, Commander A.B. Campbell, adding the detail that a thermometer he had placed on the mantelpiece in the same closed room had registered a sudden, inexplicable drop of ten degrees [9].

**Quotes and Theories on the Haunting**
Joad wrote an article in the London *Star* (October 21, 1940) titled "What I think about Ghosts," where he theorized on the nature of the Borley entities [10]. He suggested that the "spirit hypothesis does not necessarily mean that we survive as immortal souls... It may mean merely that something survives" [10]. He posited that this surviving element might combine with matter to move small objects, ring bells, or rap on tables, arguing: **"Fantastic and far-fetched? No doubt, but is it any more so than the facts which it is designed to explain?"** [11]. 

Regarding the wall-writings, Joad questioned "whether a ghost, a discarnate spirit, or a 'psychic factor' can sharpen a lead pencil," noting that the idea of a poltergeist "apporting" a pencil and materializing fingers to write with was "incredible" [12, 13].

**Contradictions and Misrepresentations**
The sources highlight two significant contradictions involving Joad's connection to Borley:
*   **The SPR Report Misrepresentation:** Investigator Robert J. Hastings noted that the skeptical Society for Psychical Research (SPR) *Borley Report* grossly misrepresented Joad's stance on the wall-writings [14]. The SPR authors quoted Joad's word "incredible" out of context to falsely suggest he denied the paranormality of the squiggles, deliberately omitting his carefully written report in which he strongly endorsed their genuineness [14].
*   **The Louis Mayerling Hoax:** In his highly dubious book exposing the haunting as a hoax, Louis Mayerling claimed that Joad had met Marianne Foyster at Borley, and that Joad later told him in a BBC studio that he "was dead against it" and wished he had never had anything to do with the case [15, 16]. Investigator Peter Underwood exposed this as a total fabrication, noting that Joad only visited the Rectory once in 1937—two years after Marianne Foyster had vacated it—and that Joad had personally confessed to Underwood that **"he would have given a lot to have met Marianne"** [17].
