# Dr Cyril Edwin Mitchinson Joad

(Born Durham, 12 August 1891; died Hampstead, 9 April 1953) Popular philosopher and regular member of the BBC radio Brains Trust. Head of the Philosophy Department at Birkbeck College, London, 1930–1953. Member of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s [[ghost-club]]. Prolific author of over 70 books and pamphlets published between 1917 and 1954, including *The Guide to Modern Thought* (1933), *The Book of Joad* (1943), and *The Recovery of Belief* (1952) [1, 2].

## Involvement in Psychical Research

Joad was deeply involved in the academic pursuit of the paranormal alongside Price. He headed the *University of London Council for Psychical Investigation* in 1934 and strongly supported Price's campaign to establish a London university chair for the subject [1, 2]. A lesser-known biographical detail in the sources reveals that Joad once travelled to the Hartz mountains with Price, where the two men attempted — unsuccessfully — to **transform a goat into a "fair youth" with the aid of a magic spell** [3].

## Experience at Borley — July 28, 1937

Joad visited [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] as one of Price's official observers on **[[july-28th|July 28, 1937]]** — the traditional anniversary date of the phantom nun's appearances — alongside Mr. A.C. Elliot Smith and the [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Alfred Henning]] [5, 6].

**The Phantom Squiggles.** Joad was "considerably intrigued" by the mysterious [[wall-writings|wall-writings]] [7]. He and fellow observer [[mark-kerr-pearse|Mark Kerr-Pearse]] made a careful tour of all existing pencil marks, then securely locked themselves inside the Base Room for an hour to cook sausages and drink tea [8]. When they emerged around 8:00 p.m., they discovered a fresh pencil squiggle on the wall in the passage immediately outside their door [8]. Joad reported being "reasonably certain" that the mark had not been there before, that his companion did not make it, and that no intruder could have entered the sealed house unheard [8].

**The Flying Soap and the Temperature Drop.** While stationed in a sealed room, **Joad was struck on the cheek by a piece of soap** that appeared to be "mysteriously propelled" [1]. He later discussed the visit with his fellow *Brains Trust* panellist, Commander A.B. Campbell, adding that a thermometer placed on the mantelpiece in the same closed room had registered a sudden, inexplicable drop of ten degrees [9]. This soap-throwing incident in full lamplight was considered by [[peter-underwood]] to be one of the more evidential events of the tenancy.

## Theories on the Haunting

In an article in the London *Star* (October 21, 1940), Joad theorised on the nature of the Borley entities, suggesting that the "spirit hypothesis does not necessarily mean that we survive as immortal souls... It may mean merely that something survives" and that this surviving element might combine with matter to move small objects, ring bells, or rap on tables [10, 11]. Regarding the wall-writings, he questioned "whether a ghost, a discarnate spirit, or a 'psychic factor' can sharpen a lead pencil," noting that the idea of a poltergeist "apporting" a pencil and materialising fingers to write with was "incredible" [12, 13].

Joad's review of [[mhh|*The Most Haunted House in England*]] (published in *The Spectator*, 18 October 1940) prompted [[canon-lambert|Canon Lawton]] to write a letter to the magazine stating that he had experienced nothing unusual during his month at the Rectory in 1933.

## Contradictions and Misrepresentations

> **Contradiction:** Investigator [[robert-hastings|Robert J. Hastings]] noted that the [[spr|SPR]] *Borley Report* grossly misrepresented Joad's stance on the wall-writings [14]. The SPR authors quoted Joad's word "incredible" out of context to falsely suggest he denied the paranormality of the squiggles, deliberately omitting his carefully written report in which he strongly endorsed their genuineness [14].

> **Contradiction:** In his dubious hoax-exposure book, Louis Mayerling claimed that Joad had met [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] at Borley and later told him in a BBC studio that he "was dead against it" and wished he had never had anything to do with the case [15, 16]. Peter Underwood exposed this as a fabrication, noting that Joad only visited the Rectory once in 1937 — two years after Marianne had vacated it — and that Joad had personally confessed to Underwood that **"he would have given a lot to have met Marianne"** [17].

## Later Life

Joad's reputation was damaged in 1948 when he was convicted of travelling on a train without a valid ticket. Though a minor offence, it was widely reported and led to his removal from the BBC Brains Trust. He died in 1953 of cancer.
