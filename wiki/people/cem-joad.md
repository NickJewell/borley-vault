# Dr Cyril Edwin Mitchinson Joad

(Born Durham, 12 August 1891; died Hampstead, 9 April 1953) Popular philosopher and regular member of the BBC radio Brains Trust. Member of the Harry Price [[ghost-club]]. Visited [[borley-rectory]] as one of [[harry-price]]'s [[official-observers]] during the [[price-tenancy]].

## Experience at Borley

On 28 July 1937 — the traditional [[july-28th]] date of the phantom nun's annual appearance — Joad visited the Rectory. He witnessed [[wall-writings|wall writing]] and was struck by a bar of soap "mysteriously propelled" (see Chronology, 28 July 1937). This soap-throwing incident in full lamplight was considered by [[peter-underwood]] to be one of the more evidential events of the tenancy.

## Academic Position

Joad was Head of the Philosophy Department at Birkbeck College, London, between 1930 and 1953. He headed the [[university-of-london-council|University of London Council for Psychical Investigation]] in 1934 and supported [[harry-price]]'s idea of a London university chair in psychical research.

His review of [[mhh|*MHH*]] (published in *The Spectator*, 18 October 1940) is notable: it prompted [[henry-lawton|Canon Lawton]] to write a letter to the magazine stating that he had experienced nothing unusual during his month at the Rectory in 1933.

## Publications

Author of over 70 books and pamphlets published between 1917 and 1954 including *The Guide to Modern Thought* (1933), *The Book of Joad* (1943), *How To Write, Think and Speak Correctly* (1939) and *The Recovery of Belief* (1952).

## Later Life

Joad's reputation was damaged in 1948 when he was convicted of travelling on a train without a valid ticket. Though a minor offence, it was widely reported and led to his removal from the BBC Brains Trust. He died in 1953 of cancer.

[[peter-underwood]] interviewed Joad in connection with his Borley experiences.
