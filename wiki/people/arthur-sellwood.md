# Rev. Arthur Sellwood

Rector of St Andrew's Church, Great Cornard, whose pastoral assistance to [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]] during the poltergeist period included an attempted blessing and exorcism of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

## Background

Sellwood arrived in Great Cornard around 1930, the same time as the Foysters settled at Borley. While the Foysters were staying in lodgings at Great Cornard before moving into the Rectory, Lionel regularly attended St Andrew's and acquainted himself with Sellwood.

## Pastoral Support

When poltergeist activity escalated dramatically in early March 1931, Lionel walked to Great Cornard to discuss the situation with Sellwood, arriving at the rectory clearly "desperately troubled." Sellwood was sympathetic and offered to help. He first suggested blessing the Rectory with **holy water from the shrine at Walsingham**, which he kept in supply.

Lionel and [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] duly performed this blessing on the Rectory — during which a stone was thrown and hit Lionel on the shoulder — but the activity did not stop. Lionel wrote to Sellwood outlining the failure; Sellwood concluded that an **exorcism** would be necessary.

## The Exorcism

On 11 March 1931, Sellwood arrived at the Rectory accompanied by **[[bernard-smith-rector|Bernard Smith]]**, a distant cousin of Lionel and rector at Newton Green. Smith had brought a censer and incense. As there was no Anglican rite of exorcism, Sellwood referred to the **Catholic ritual from 1614**, still current at the time:

> *We exorcise you, every unclean spirit, every Satanic power, every incursion of the infernal adversary, every legion, every diabolical sect and gathering in the name and by the virtue of our Lord Jesus Christ.*

The house was fumigated from attic to cellar. Though it was quiet immediately afterwards, the phenomena resumed in Sellwood's absence the same afternoon. The exorcism had not worked.

## Later Visits

Sellwood returned on 13 March with [[alfred-richard-graham-bull|Alfred Bull]] in tow to try to assess the situation. During this visit, Marianne was struck on the head by a thrown piece of metal. Alfred Bull seized a stick and rushed upstairs but found nothing.

Sellwood was one of the most credible clerical witnesses to the phenomena during the Foyster period: a fellow Anglican minister with no personal stake in the haunting legend and no prior relationship with the Rectory.
