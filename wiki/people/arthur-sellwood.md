# Rev. Arthur Sellwood

Rector of St Andrew's Church, Great Cornard (also recorded in the sources as the **Revd A. H. Sellwood**), whose pastoral assistance to [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]] during the poltergeist period included an attempted blessing and exorcism of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

## Background

Sellwood arrived in Great Cornard around 1930, the same time as the Foysters settled at Borley. While the Foysters were staying in lodgings at Great Cornard before moving into the Rectory, Lionel regularly attended St Andrew's and acquainted himself with Sellwood.

## Pastoral Support

In the early spring of 1931, the alleged poltergeist phenomena at Borley Rectory became increasingly violent and dangerous, prompting [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Foyster]] to consult the Bishop of Chelmsford and several neighbouring priests, including Sellwood and the Rev. [[clive-luget|Clive Luget]] [1]. When poltergeist activity escalated dramatically in early March 1931, Lionel walked to Great Cornard to discuss the situation with Sellwood, arriving at the rectory clearly "desperately troubled." Sellwood was sympathetic and offered to help. He first suggested blessing the Rectory with **holy water from the shrine at Walsingham**, which he kept in supply [1].

Lionel and [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] duly performed this blessing on the Rectory — during which a stone was thrown and hit Lionel on the shoulder — but the activity did not stop. Lionel wrote to Sellwood outlining the failure; Sellwood concluded that an **exorcism** would be necessary.

## The Exorcisms of March 1931

**The March 11 Exorcism:** On the morning of March 11, 1931, Sellwood arrived at the Rectory accompanied by **[[bernard-smith-rector|Bernard Smith]]**, a distant cousin of Lionel and rector at Newton Green, six miles away — and Smith's wife [2]. Smith brought a censer and incense. Because there was no Anglican rite of exorcism, Sellwood referred to the **Catholic ritual from 1614**, still current at the time:

> *We exorcise you, every unclean spirit, every Satanic power, every incursion of the infernal adversary, every legion, every diabolical sect and gathering in the name and by the virtue of our Lord Jesus Christ.*

The priests went through the entire building from the attic to the cellar, fumigating every room and passage with burning incense and blessing the house with holy water obtained specifically from the shrine of Our Lady of Walsingham [1, 2]. Though the house was quiet immediately afterwards, the phenomena resumed in Sellwood's absence the same afternoon. The exorcism had not worked [1].

**The March 13 Visit:** Two days later, on Friday March 13, Sellwood returned to the Rectory [3]. On this occasion he brought with him [[alfred-richard-graham-bull|Alfred Bull]] — brother of the late Borley rectors — whom Sellwood had personally informed about the disturbing activity taking place in the house [3]. During this visit, [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] was struck on the head by a thrown piece of metal. Alfred Bull seized a stick and rushed upstairs but found nothing.

## The "Warm Stone" Phenomenon

Sellwood was reportedly a firsthand witness to physical poltergeist activity at Borley. Following a talk given by psychical investigator [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] to the London Appreciation Society, an audience member approached Underwood and identified himself as Arthur Sellwood's son [4]. The son relayed that his father had once been standing in the hall of Borley Rectory in the company of [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] and [[bernard-smith-rector|Bernard Smith]] when a stone inexplicably fell from nowhere [4]. According to the account, when Sellwood picked the stone up, he found that it felt warm [4].

## The Unrealised Interview

Although [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] was eager to corroborate this story and speak with the clergyman directly, the interview never took place [4]. At the time Underwood learned of the incident, Mr. Sellwood senior was in poor health; his doctor had ordered him to "take things very quietly," meaning he could not agree to a personal interview to place his experiences on the official record [4].

## Significance

Sellwood was one of the most credible clerical witnesses to the phenomena during the Foyster period: a fellow Anglican minister with no personal stake in the haunting legend and no prior relationship with the Rectory.
