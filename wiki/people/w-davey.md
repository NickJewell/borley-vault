# W. Davey

Of Whitton Church Lane, Ipswich. Visited the ruins of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] on Easter Monday, **10 April 1939** — approximately six weeks after the fire — specifically to see the ruins left behind by the disastrous February fire. He noted that upon arrival the grounds seemed "placid and peaceful enough" [1]. He promptly documented his experiences in a letter to [[harry-price|Harry Price]] the following day, **11 April 1939** [1].

## Camera Phenomenon

Davey had owned his camera for five years without fault. While standing on the lawn in front of the ruins to photograph them, he took a photograph and closed the camera. The device "**immediately flew open with a smart click**," completely spoiling the latent picture inside [1]. He attempted a second photograph, but the camera flew open again the moment he pressed the button [1]. Davey abandoned the photographic part of his visit, emphasising in his letter that he had owned the camera for five years and it had never before behaved in such a manner [1].

His experience is listed in Appendix D as "A Camera Phenomenon — Mr. W. Davey" — a foreshadowing of the pattern of photographic and electrical equipment failures later documented across multiple investigators and attributed by Babbs/Mathias to [[ley-lines-at-borley|ley-line]] electromagnetic effects.

## The "Something"

Having given up on photography, Davey made a "mental resolution" to leave — at which point he "**noticed something at the very spot where Captain Gregson lost his dog**" [1]. He does not describe or name what this mysterious "something" actually was; he was entirely unable to define or articulate it precisely. It was evidently enough to impress him as anomalous.

## Significance

Davey is one of a handful of post-fire visitors who reported anomalous experiences at the ruins before the Cambridge Commission's systematic investigation began (September 1939). His camera malfunction anticipates [[ley-lines-at-borley|the ley-line hypothesis]] proposed in later analysis — multiple Borley investigators reported mechanical and electrical failures at the site.
