# W. Davey

Of Whitton Church Lane, Ipswich. Visited the ruins of Borley Rectory on Easter Monday, April 10, 1939 — approximately six weeks after the fire. Reported a camera malfunction phenomenon and an unspecified "something" at the spot where [[captain-gregson|Captain Gregson]] had lost his dog.

## Camera Phenomenon

Davey had owned his camera for five years without fault. He went onto the Rectory lawn to photograph the ruins. After taking each photograph and closing the camera, it "immediately flew open with a smart click" — spoiling the latent image. This happened twice. He abandoned the photographic part of his visit.

Writing to Price the following day (April 11, 1939):

> "he had had the camera for five years, and never before had the camera behaved like it."

His experience is listed in Appendix D as "A Camera Phenomenon — Mr. W. Davey" — a foreshadowing of the pattern of photographic and electrical equipment failures later documented across multiple investigators and attributed by Babbs/Mathias to ley-line electromagnetic effects.

## The "Something"

Having given up on photography, Davey made a "mental resolution" to leave — at which point he "noticed something at the very spot where Captain Gregson lost his dog." He does not describe or name what he noticed. It was evidently enough to impress him as anomalous but not enough to articulate precisely in his letter to Price.

## Significance

Davey is one of a handful of post-fire visitors who reported anomalous experiences at the ruins before the Cambridge Commission's systematic investigation began (September 1939). His camera malfunction anticipates [[ley-lines-at-borley|the ley-line hypothesis]] proposed in later analysis — multiple Borley investigators reported mechanical and electrical failures at the site.

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mr. W. Davey** was a post-fire visitor to Borley Rectory who reported experiencing a bizarre mechanical malfunction and an undefinable visual anomaly during his trip. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following specific details regarding his involvement:

**Biographical Details and Visit**
*   **Residence:** He lived at **Whitton Church Lane, Ipswich** [1].
*   **The Visit:** Davey visited the Borley Rectory site on **Easter Monday, April 10, 1939**, specifically to see the ruins left behind by the disastrous February fire [1, 2]. He noted that upon his arrival, the grounds seemed **"placid and peaceful enough"** [1].

**The Camera Malfunction**
Davey is best known in the Borley literature for experiencing what Harry Price described as a "camera phenomenon" [1]. 
*   While standing on the lawn in front of the ruins, Davey took a photograph and then closed his camera [1]. The device **"immediately flew open with a smart click,"** which completely spoiled the latent picture inside [1]. 
*   He attempted to take a second picture of the Rectory, but the camera once again flew open the moment he pressed the button [1]. 
*   Davey emphasized the strangeness of this event by pointing out that **he had owned the camera for five years and it had never before behaved in such a manner** [1].

**The Undefined Sighting**
*   Deciding to give up his photographic efforts as a "bad job," Davey immediately experienced a second anomaly [1]. He reported that he **"noticed something at the very spot where Captain Gregson lost his dog"** [1]. 
*   However, he was entirely unable to define or describe what this mysterious "something" actually was [1]. 

**Documentation**
Davey promptly documented these experiences and submitted them in a letter to Harry Price the very next day, on **April 11, 1939** [1].
