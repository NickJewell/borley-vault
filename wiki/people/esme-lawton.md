# Esme Lawton

Daughter of [[henry-lawton|Rev. Henry Lawton]] and [[irene-lawton|Irene Lawton]]; born September 1933 during or immediately after their summer residence at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]]; goddaughter of [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]].

## Background

Irene Lawton was heavily pregnant during the family's month-long stay at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in August 1933, while [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] was in hospital. Esme was born in September 1933, as the family's stay was ending.

[[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]] — the eldest surviving daughter of [[henry-bull|Rev. Henry Dawson Bull]], a long-term resident of the area, and one of the four sisters present at the famous 1900 nun sighting — became Esme's godmother. This connection formed during [[henry-lawton|Henry Lawton]]'s acquaintance with Ethel at Great Cornard and Chilton Lodge.

## Significance

The choice of Ethel Bull as godmother is a biographical footnote that illustrates how tightly the various human threads of the Borley story were interwoven. The Lawton family, who arrived as complete outsiders, departed with a permanent family link to the Bull dynasty through Esme's baptism.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Esme Lawton** was the daughter of Canon Henry Lawton and his Welsh wife, Irene Lawton [1, 2]. While she was not an active participant in the Borley Rectory hauntings, her birth and family connections provide specific details relevant to the investigation of the case. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts and contradictions regarding her:

**Birth and Chronological Contradiction**
The sources contain a minor contradiction regarding the exact timing and location of Esme's birth. The *Borley Rectory Companion* states that Irene Lawton **"gave birth to a baby girl during her stay at Borley"** while the family was residing at the Rectory as *locum tenens* for the Foysters in August and September 1933 [3, 4]. However, another account notes that Irene was "heavily pregnant" during their month-long stay in August, and that Esme was actually **born "that September"**, presumably after their stay at the Rectory had concluded [2, 5]. 

**Godmother**
During the Lawtons' stay at Borley, Canon Lawton became well acquainted with many of the locals. He developed a particular friendship with Miss Ethel Bull, one of the famous Bull sisters who had reportedly witnessed the phantom nun in 1900 [5]. As a result of this close acquaintance, **Ethel Bull became Esme Lawton's godmother** [5, 6].

**Significance to the Skeptical Investigation**
Esme's impending birth played a notable role in the skeptical arguments against the Borley phenomena, particularly those compiled by the Society for Psychical Research (SPR). Because Irene Lawton was pregnant with Esme, she spent a considerable amount of time resting indoors at the Rectory [4]. She frequently sat in the twilight by the open window of the drawing room overlooking the "Nun's Walk"—considered ideal conditions for a ghost sighting—yet she experienced absolutely nothing paranormal [4, 7]. 

Furthermore, when Canon Lawton discovered Lionel Foyster's sensational manuscript about the hauntings (*Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*) on his first day at the Rectory, he deliberately kept it a secret from his family so as **not to alarm his pregnant wife** [2]. The fact that a normal family—including a highly pregnant mother who spent most of her time in the house's most "haunted" rooms—experienced no phenomena was used by critics like Trevor Hall to heavily discount the claims made during the Foyster incumbency [4, 7, 8].
