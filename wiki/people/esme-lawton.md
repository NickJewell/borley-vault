# Esme Lawton

Daughter of [[henry-lawton|Rev. Henry Lawton]] and [[irene-lawton|Irene Lawton]]; born September 1933 during or immediately after their summer residence at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]]; goddaughter of [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]].

## Birth and Chronological Contradiction

Irene Lawton was heavily pregnant during the family's month-long stay at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in August 1933, while [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] was in hospital.

> **Contradiction:** The *Borley Rectory Companion* states that Irene **"gave birth to a baby girl during her stay at Borley"** [3, 4]. However, another account notes that Irene was "heavily pregnant" during their stay in August, and that Esme was actually **born "that September"**, presumably after their stay at the Rectory had concluded [2, 5].

## Connection to the Bull Family

During the Lawtons' stay at Borley, [[henry-lawton|Canon Lawton]] became well acquainted with many of the locals. He developed a particular friendship with [[ethel-bull|Miss Ethel Bull]], one of the famous Bull sisters who had reportedly witnessed the phantom nun in 1900 [5]. As a result of this close acquaintance, **Ethel Bull became Esme Lawton's godmother** [5, 6].

## Significance

The choice of Ethel Bull as godmother is a biographical footnote illustrating how tightly the various human threads of the Borley story were interwoven. The Lawton family, who arrived as complete outsiders, departed with a permanent family link to the Bull dynasty through Esme's baptism.

Esme's impending birth also played a role in the sceptical arguments against the Borley phenomena. Because Irene was pregnant with Esme, she spent considerable time resting indoors at the Rectory [4]. She frequently sat in the twilight by the open window of the drawing room overlooking the [[nuns-walk|"Nun's Walk"]] — conditions considered ideal for a ghost sighting — yet experienced absolutely nothing paranormal [4, 7]. Furthermore, when Canon Lawton discovered [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]]'s sensational manuscript *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House* on his first day at the Rectory, he deliberately kept it a secret from his family **so as not to alarm his pregnant wife** [2]. The fact that a normal family — including a heavily pregnant mother resting in the house's most "haunted" rooms — experienced no phenomena was used by critics such as [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] to heavily discount the claims made during the Foyster incumbency [4, 7, 8].
