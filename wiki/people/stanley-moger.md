# Stanley Moger OBE FAI FCIA

A local surveyor and estate agent operating out of the Estate Office, Halstead, Essex [1–3]. His post-nominals (OBE, FAI — Fellow of the Auctioneers and Estate Agents Institute, FCIA — Fellow of the Chartered Institute of Arbitrators) indicate a senior professional with, as he noted, "over forty years" of local experience. In addition to his primary business, his firm, Messrs Stanley Moger, acted as estate agents for Mrs. Harry Bull ([[ivy-brackenbury|Ivy Brackenbury]]); as late as 1944, some Borley residents were still paying their rent to her through his agency [4].

## The 1938 Commission

Following the end of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s year-long tenancy on 19 May 1938, the Ecclesiastical Commissioners (based at 1 Millbank, Westminster) were eager to sell the massive, dilapidated property [5, 7]. Acting on correspondence dated 4 May 1938 sent to Borley's rector, [[rev-ac-henning|the Rev. A. C. Henning]], the Commission instructed Moger to inspect the house and advise on a reserve price in the event the property had to be taken to auction [1, 7]. Moger visited the Rectory in mid-May 1938, shortly after Price's departure [5, 8].

## The 1938 Report (26 May 1938)

On 26 May 1938, Moger submitted his official valuation to the Commissioners — a document that later commentators noted possessed an ethos **"as depressing and gloomy as the building which it described"** [2, 7]. The report described a building in serious decline: the verandah on the east side was "much dilapidated, part down and dangerous"; the greenhouse and heating sheds on the south side were in the same condition; the interior was "roaming and quite out-of-date, requiring much expenditure in refitting and modernising"; and the decorative condition was "decidedly old and of cold appearance."

He also provided a room-by-room description offering a rare contemporary specification of the building's layout: all ground-floor rooms had a ceiling height of 10ft 9in; the first-floor rooms 9ft 6in. The rectory contained 20 rooms, plus a chapel in the small tower, three staircases, and extensive outbuildings.

Under the specific heading **"LOCAL RUMOUR,"** Moger bluntly detailed the problem:

> "The Rectory is supposed to be HAUNTED and a few years ago was the Hunting Ground of many hundred of Spiritualists and Inquisitive persons, in fact, so many visited the site that the Police had to handle the matter. This, as you may imagine, is a considerable detriment to selling, as many view but turn away on this account." [4]

Moger's report was fatalistic regarding the building's physical future: **"If not disposed of within a reasonable time, I fear the end will be unfortunately as is the case today with large, out-of-date Residences — Demolition for the value of the Materials after cost of pulling down, which in this case would be merely the Bricks, Slates, Doors and Windows"** [3]. He concluded with a recommended reserve of **£450** and suggested an auction in late August or early September if no private offer was received [3].

## Outcome

Moger's proposed auction never took place. Later that autumn, [[captain-gregson|Captain W. H. Gregson]] made a private offer for the Rectory which the Church accepted, allowing him to take possession in December 1938. The building was sold well below Moger's modest recommendation — sources differ on whether the price was £350 or £500.

## Significance

The Moger report is the only formal professional assessment of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] made before it was destroyed by [[fire-1939|fire]] in February 1939, nine months after the report. It is unusual among surveyor's reports in explicitly referencing an alleged haunting as a factor affecting value. Moger's prediction of demolition proved accurate, though it was fire rather than a wrecking ball that ended the Rectory.
