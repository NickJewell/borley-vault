# Stanley Moger OBE FAI FCIA

A local surveyor based at the Estate Office, Halstead, Essex, who was instructed by the Ecclesiastical Commissioners in 1938 to prepare a condition report on [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] prior to a proposed auction sale. His report, dated 26 May 1938, is one of the key contemporary documents about the rectory's state in its final months.

## The 1938 Report

Moger addressed his report to the Ecclesiastical Commissioners at 1 Millbank, Westminster, at the request of the [[rev-ac-henning|Rev Alfred Clifford Henning]], who was acting on the Commissioners' behalf to dispose of the property. The report described a building in serious decline: the verandah on the east side was "much dilapidated, part down and dangerous"; the greenhouse and heating sheds on the south side were in the same condition; the interior was "roaming and quite out-of-date, requiring much expenditure in refitting and modernising"; and the decorative condition was "decidedly old and of cold appearance."

He also provided a room-by-room description that offers a rare contemporary specification of the building's layout: all ground-floor rooms had a ceiling height of 10ft 9in; the first floor rooms 9ft 6in. The rectory contained 20 rooms, plus a chapel in the small tower, three staircases, and extensive outbuildings.

The report's most striking feature is its frank mention of the haunting:

> "The Rectory is supposed to be HAUNTED and a few years ago was the Hunting Ground of many hundred of Spiritualists and Inquisitive persons, in fact, so many visited the site that the Police had to handle the matter. This, as you may imagine, is a considerable detriment to selling, as many view but turn away on this account."

He recommended a reserve of **£450** and suggested that if not sold within a reasonable time, demolition for the value of materials would be the likely outcome.

## Professional Credentials

Moger's post-nominals (OBE, FAI — Fellow of the Auctioneers and Estate Agents Institute, FCIA — Fellow of the Chartered Institute of Arbitrators) indicate a senior professional. He noted "over forty years" of local experience.

## Significance

The Moger report is the only formal professional assessment of Borley Rectory made before it was destroyed by fire in February 1939, nine months after the report. It is unusual among surveyor's reports in explicitly referencing an alleged haunting as a factor affecting value. The rectory was eventually sold not at auction but by private sale to [[captain-gregson|Capt William Hart Gregson]] for £350 (or £500 — sources differ), well below even Moger's modest recommendation.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Stanley Moger, OBE, FAI, FCIA**, was a local surveyor and estate agent operating out of the Estate Office in nearby Halstead, Essex [1-3]. In addition to his primary business, his firm, Messrs Stanley Moger, acted as the estate agents for Mrs. Harry Bull (Ivy Brackenbury); as late as 1944, some Borley residents were still paying their rent to her through his agency [4]. 

In the Borley Rectory saga, Moger is best known for producing a highly pessimistic 1938 valuation report—commonly referred to in the literature as the **"Moger Inventory"**—which candidly evaluated the negative financial impact of the building's haunted reputation [5, 6].

**The 1938 Commission and Rectory Visit**
Following the end of ghost-hunter Harry Price's year-long tenancy on 19 May 1938, the Ecclesiastical Commissioners (based at 1, Millbank, Westminster) were eager to sell the massive, dilapidated property [5, 7]. Acting on correspondence dated 4 May 1938 sent to Borley's rector, the Rev. A. C. Henning, the Commission instructed Moger to inspect the house and advise on a reserve price in the event the property had to be taken to auction [1, 7]. Moger visited the Rectory in mid-May 1938, shortly after Price's departure [5, 8]. 

**The Moger Inventory and Report (26 May 1938)**
On 26 May 1938, Moger submitted his official valuation to the Commissioners, a document that later commentators noted possessed an ethos **"as depressing and gloomy as the building which it described"** [2, 7]. Avoiding any attempt at "hard selling," Moger took the incredibly rare step for a professional surveyor of explicitly citing a haunting as a real estate liability [6]. 

Under the specific heading **"LOCAL RUMOUR,"** Moger bluntly detailed the problem:
*   **"The Rectory is supposed to be HAUNTED, and a few years ago was the Hunting Ground of many hundreds of Spiritualists and Inquisitive persons, in fact, so many visited the site that the Police had to handle the matter"** [4]. 
*   Highlighting the effect this notoriety had on prospective buyers, he added: **"This, as you may imagine, is a considerable detriment to selling, as many view but turn away on this account"** [4].

**Moger's Predictions and Final Valuation**
Moger's report was fatalistic regarding the building's physical future. Under the heading "Future," he accurately predicted the Rectory's ultimate demise:
*   **"If not disposed of within a reasonable time, I fear the end will be unfortunately as is the case today with large, out-of-date Residences - Demolition for the value of the Materials after cost of pulling down, which in this case would be merely the Bricks, Slates, Doors and Windows"** [3]. 
*   He further warned the Commissioners that the bare **"site value would be very little and most difficult to dispose of"** [3].

Concluding his assessment, Moger signed off with his professional recommendation: **"Taking everything into account, I regret that I cannot recommend a higher reserve than Four Hundred and Fifty Pounds [£450]"** [3]. He suggested that if an acceptable private offer was not received, an auction should be held in late August or early September [3]. 

Ultimately, Moger's proposed auction never took place. Later that autumn, Captain W. H. Gregson made a private offer of £500 for the Rectory, which the Church accepted, allowing him to take possession of the property in December 1938 [1, 5].
