# Marianne Foyster (26 January 1899 – 18 December 1992)

Born **Mary Anne Emily Rebecca Shaw** on 26 January 1899 at 5 Guy Wood Cottages, Romiley, near Stockport; died 18 December 1992 in Utah, aged 93. The most controversial figure in the entire Borley case. Wife of [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]] during the five-year incumbency (1930–1935) — the most intense poltergeist period in the Rectory's history. Died without ever discussing Borley with her adopted son [[vincent-oneil|Vincent O'Neil]].

## Origins (per O'Connor, hbrs-ch08)

Named after her paternal grandmother Mary Ann, who had died in a lunatic asylum in Macclesfield. Only daughter of Annie and William Shaw; had an older brother Geoffrey. Father was a failed schoolteacher and publican; mother gave piano lessons. Family moved repeatedly — Romiley, Widnes, Oughtrington (Lymm, Cheshire), then Drumnadreagh (Co. Antrim, Northern Ireland).

**Baptised by [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]]** on 19 June 1906 at St Peter's, Oughtrington, when she was 7 and he was a curate there aged 28.

### First Marriage and Abandoned Son

At 15, became engaged to Harold Greenwood (21), an English clergyman's son. Greenwood disappeared; Marianne was left as a teenage single mother. Her parents raised the baby as her "younger brother." The child, **Ian Shaw**, was raised without her involvement; she left for munitions work in Coventry. The loss of Ian haunted her entire life. He eventually resented her deeply.

### Canada (1922–1930)

Sailed 5 August 1922 to Quebec with £30. Married Lionel at Salmonhurst, New Brunswick, 28 August 1922 — she declared herself a spinster. The marriage was "a mutually beneficial arrangement, not a love match." She was 23; he was 45.

In Canada she learned Danish, to ski, skate, dance and golf; began smoking cigarettes; studied at Mount Allison Ladies' College. Diagnosed with polycystic ovaries; surgery left her unable to have more children. Adopted baby Barbara Tower (renamed Adelaide) in 1928.

While convalescing at Amherst Hospital, heard the story of Esther Cox — a violated teenager at the centre of 1878 poltergeist phenomena. O'Connor draws explicit parallels. (hbrs-ch08)

## Character

"Bright, vivacious and intelligent" — [[harry-price|Harry Price's]] first impression. Her husband "obviously doted on her." Physically attractive; twenty-one years Lionel's junior. Irish Catholic background; firm believer in Satan (whom she called "Tantrabobous"); wore a religious scapular her entire life.

Eileen Garrett, who interviewed her in 1958, described her as "extremely Irish, born in Antrim, and filled with all the imagination, superstition, legends... of my race" — with "a quite beguiling personality once she is off her guard." Garrett also noted: "she quite unblushingly told Mr. Swanson that she had every intention of lying to him... her lying is a game."

## Coming to Borley

The Foysters came to Borley in October 1930 primarily because of Lionel's health — arthritis and a heart condition made Canadian winters impossible. He had suffered a heart attack in 1925. Marianne described the decision in her autobiography: "Lion had been told that he could not undertake country mission work again... when Borley was offered to him for the second time, we both realized that it was an ideal spot for a sick man."

They knew Borley had a reputation. Marianne later said her initial attitude was "it was a lot of tall stories" — she was "impatient and annoyed at being stopped by the local people every time she put her nose out of the door." But she genuinely loved the house: "I made a very beautiful garden there, and I really did like the house. I was happy at Borley."

## The Apparition She Saw

In her unpublished autobiography (Chapter 7), Marianne describes an old man with stooped shoulders and an aquiline face who walked through the study and up the stairs: "He wore a queer kind of long top coat with braid on the lapels." She followed him, saw him in the summerhouse, and both she and Adelaide saw him go into the churchyard. She asked Mary Pearson who he was; Pearson turned "a queer kind of greenish white" and gasped: "Mr. Harry. He did wear such a garment."

This is the experience the SPR interpreted as Marianne claiming to see Harry Bull's ghost — but in her own account it reads as a flesh-and-blood encounter, not a ghostly one.

To Robert Swanson (1958) she denied seeing Harry Bull at all: "I never saw Harry Bull." She then described seeing a man in the garden who Lionel refused to acknowledge: "He said that that was how his cousin used to dress and that no one had been to see him." She saw the same figure "several times."

> **Contradiction:** Her autobiography describes an apparently flesh-and-blood man she identified as Harry Bull via Pearson's reaction. Her Swanson testimony denies any Harry Bull apparition while describing the same events slightly differently.

## The Accusations Against Her (SPR 1956)

The 1956 SPR report (Dingwall, Goldney, Hall) presents the most rigorous systematic case against Marianne. See [[the-borley-report]] for the full analysis.

**The 99/103 count:** The SPR analysed the Foyster *Diary of Occurrences* (1931) incident by incident, classifying all 103 phenomena:
- Class 1 (occurred while Foyster absent; only Marianne's word): 24
- Class 2 (natural cause explainable but for Marianne's denial): 11
- Class 3 (occurred with Marianne alone, result later inspected): 25
- Class 4 (both Foysters present but position ambiguous): 30
- Class 5 (apparitions etc., Marianne only): 9
- Class 6 (trivial, natural cause): 3
- Class 7 (not dependent on Marianne): 1

Result: **99 of 103 incidents depend entirely on Marianne's sincerity.**

**The Amherst parallel:** The SPR documents 19 explicit parallels between the Foyster period and the Great Amherst Mystery (Esther Cox, Nova Scotia, 1878–79) — same duration (~15 months), same phenomena types, same pattern of cessation during the focus-person's absence, wall-writings addressed to the young woman, small fires, abrupt cessation after an "exorcism." The Foysters had lived in Sackville, Nova Scotia (five miles from Amherst) from 1928–30. Moreover, in *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*, Lionel used the pseudonym "Mr Teed" for one character — the name of Esther Cox's sister and brother-in-law in whose house the Amherst manifestations occurred

**The wall-writings:** Miss Braithwaite (August 1931, pre-SPR investigation) independently identified the wall-writings as "undoubtedly Mrs Foyster's" handwriting; her brother Sir John Braithwaite concurred. The SPR noted the "i" in the wall-message "Marianne" is dotted with a downward dash — identical to Marianne's normal signature. The most carelessly written message appeared on the one occasion when Edwin Whitehouse left Marianne alone on the landing for "a couple of minutes"

**Price's private verdict:** Price privately believed Marianne was responsible from 1931 onward, documented in four letters spanning his entire subsequent connection with the case. He wrote to Fraser-Harris in October 1931: "we are convinced that the many phenomena we saw were fraudulent... We think that the rector's wife is responsible." He suppressed this view entirely in his books — keeping the Foyster period as the centrepiece of his haunting evidence while privately dismissing it

**SPR's motives analysis (12 points):**
1. She wanted to leave Borley (cold, inconvenient, isolated); Lionel was determined to stay
2. During Marianne's one documented prolonged absence, phenomena virtually ceased
3. No poltergeist-like activity followed Marianne to her subsequent addresses
4. When Price and party controlled Marianne's movements (14 Oct 1931), only one minor bell-ringing occurred
5. 99/103 Foyster Diary incidents depend on her good faith
6. Price's consistent private opinion over 17 years
7. Borley was "creepy" physically but not dangerous; she had motive to amplify this
8. She was 31, physically attractive; Lionel was 52–57, arthritic; she needed social stimulation
9. Phenomena ended at the moment accusations from all sides became overwhelming — and she had already arranged the flower-shop alternative life with d'Arles
10. The pattern matches the Amherst case, which she had direct access to in Nova Scotia
11. Mr Foyster had occasional doubts (his silence in his manuscripts on the wine trick and on Edwin's most sensational incidents)
12. The Esther Cox "textbook" was available — Hubbell's book ran to 55,000 copies across 10+ editions

## The Defence

The counterarguments:
- 12+ independent witnesses (not just Marianne) saw phenomena during the Foyster period
- The smouldering-room fire incident (witnessed by Sir George and Lady Whitehouse) could not plausibly be staged
- [[dom-richard-whitehouse]] witnessed the stiletto levitate onto his lap while watching Marianne "who had not moved hand or foot"
- Phenomena had been reported since 1863 — before Marianne was born
- Her own letter (1956): "There were a great many things which he and I went through which no one can explain away"

## The Owen/Mitchell Finding (1979)

[[iris-owen|Iris Owen]] and Pauline Mitchell tracked Marianne down in La Crosse, Wisconsin in 1977 under a promise that nothing would be published until after her death. Their report (published SPR Journal, September 1979) is the most sympathetic and analytically sophisticated assessment of Marianne's account.

**Key finding:** The dominant factor in the Foysters' life was not the haunting but **Lionel's terminal prognosis**. He was writing a fictional best-seller (modelled on Hubbell's *Amherst Mystery*) to solve their financial problems. Most Foyster-period phenomena were either fabricated for the book or genuine minor poltergeist activity, with some trickery by others (including village children, Edwin Whitehouse, possibly d'Arles). 

Marianne confirmed she, Lionel, and Ian all "answered" the wall writings by writing underneath them — but denies initiating them.

Owen's assessment: "I completely believe that her version of what happened is the correct one." She described her as "a delightful person — witty, charming, clever — but someone to whom life had dealt a hard hand."

Owen concluded Marianne's overriding concern was Lionel's health and their financial future: "Far from wishing to leave the rectory, as has been alleged, they were happy there."

## The 1958 Swanson Interviews

Private detective Robert Swanson, hired by [[eileen-garrett|Eileen Garrett]] at [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall's]] instigation, interviewed Marianne twice: in Jamestown, North Dakota (February 1958) and in New York (May 11–12, 1958).

### Key admissions and denials:

**The fake ghost:** Marianne admitted making a luminous cardboard figure on pulleys to frighten d'Arles, suspecting him of staging phenomena: "We made up a very crude figure... We happened to have some luminous paint... this did scare d'Arles."

**Bells:** She and Ian tested the attic wires and found "the bells could be made to ring by tampering with them." Attributed loose wires to rats or bats.

**Stone throwing:** "Lionel could have been responsible" and "the stone throwing stopped when Lionel got sick to the point where he couldn't walk."

**The stiletto:** "I certainly don't remember any stiletto incident... I have never, to the best of my beliefs, even seen a stiletto."

**Wall writings:** "I had nothing to do with them." Thought Edwin Whitehouse wrote them. But confirmed she "answered" them.

**Wedding ring:** Her ring was "at least 3–4 sizes too big" — its disappearances and reappearances were down to her carelessness. "It was quite a joke."

**The Blue Room:** Calls the room "pale gentian-pink" — not blue. Confirms it had only one window, contradicting Whitehouse's floor plan.

**The manuscript:** "It was supposed to be more or less fiction — the whole thing was."

**What puzzled her:** "I think there was something at Borley that wasn't a human being — certain rooms in the house were unhappy rooms... there were unexplained drops in temperature... the church too had a different feeling compared to many churches."

**Marianne's bottom line to Swanson:** "I made a very beautiful garden there, and I really did like the house. I was happy at Borley."

## Her Autobiography

Written at Swanson and Garrett's urging after the New York interviews; never completed. O'Neil considers it the most reliable record — written "in the privacy of her own room, without someone hounding her."

Key sentences:
- "Many strange incidents occurred at Borley, including the ghost lights, the bells, and the **apparitions**" — the word "apparitions" used voluntarily
- "The house had been quiet enough for **over three and one-half years** when we left Borley Rectory" — confirming roughly 18 months of genuine activity followed by a long calm period

## Harry Price and Mrs. Goldney

In her autobiography, Marianne describes observing Price and Mrs. Goldney: "Price and Mrs. Goldney were constantly running out together... I sneaked out of the garden door and slipped through the side door. They got a surprise. So did I. **They were in the throes of an affair.**"

She also explicitly states the wine-to-ink conversion was Price's magician trick: "Price pulled his wine into ink trick."

## Marriages (a complicated history)

The appendix to *Ghosts of Borley* and other research reveal four marriages, several possibly bigamous:
1. Harold Greenwood (1914) — possibly never legally dissolved
2. [[lionel-foyster]] (1922, Canada) — described herself as spinster (potentially bigamous)
3. Henry Francis Fisher (February 23, 1935 — *while Foyster was still alive and living upstairs*) — bigamous
4. [[robert-oneil|Robert Vincent O'Neil]] (August 11, 1945, four months after Foyster's death) — probably also bigamous if Fisher undivorceed

## American Life

After Lionel's death, Marianne came to America in 1946 with Robert O'Neil and adopted infant Vincent. They settled in various states. She worked as a teacher (briefly), usherette, salesperson. Robert died in a road accident. She spent her final years in La Crosse, Wisconsin and later elsewhere.

She never discussed Borley with Vincent. She feared that Tantrabobous might overhear any discussion of Borley's darker side.

## Wood's Additional Details (wob)

Robert Wood's *The Widow of Borley* adds several details not found in other sources:

**Village confederate:** Ian Shaw testified to Wood that **Willy Palmer**, a village boy, was Marianne's confederate — he rigged a collapsing shelf in the pantry that deposited pots and pans on the floor at the tug of a string.

**D'Arles description:** Multiple people described Frank Pearless (d'Arles) as "huge sexually," "Rasputin," "a thoroughly evil man." He tried to blackmail the Foysters by threatening to expose Foyster's domestic arrangements to the Bishop of Chelmsford.

**Marianne on killing Foyster:** Eileen Garrett's interview notes from New York (1958) record Marianne saying that killing Foyster "had often been in her mind, but that he was too cunning for anything like that to happen to him." (The remark about Foyster writing "imaginative stories... all kinds of imaginative stories which were to be presented about Borley" also comes from this interview.)

**Foyster's "Spiritual Father" justification:** Lionel baptized Marianne on 19 June 1906 at St Peter's, Oughtrington (she was seven, he was curate). He later claimed this sacramental role made him her "Spiritual Father" — a relationship he used as theological justification for Marianne's sexual relationships with other men.

## Clarke's Assessment (slbr-12, slbr-13, slbr-14, slbr-20)

Andrew Clarke's analysis adds important new dimensions:

**Affair timeline:** Clarke confirms Marianne initiated the affair with [[francois-darles|Frank Pearless]], beginning c. May–June 1931. *"I think it was several weeks before we had sexual relations."* The relationship lasted nearly three years and was not casual — though entirely physical, without emotional attachment.

**Frank as confederate:** Clarke's key contribution: the wall-writings coincide with the start of the Pearless affair (spring 1931); the spectacular phenomena of the Marks Tey night were a joint production with Pearless operating the cellar trapdoor and string mechanism; both agreed the Marks Tey Circle visit should be used as a staged finale to end the haunting.

**Wall-writings graphology:** Multiple independent graphologist analyses (Lewis Ackerman, Peter Underwood's submission) confirm all the wall-writings (except one word, "Edwin") are in Marianne's handwriting. The early scribbles ("m's and u's") were probably by Adelaide or Douglas Pearless (bright children of the same age); the developed messages were Marianne's response to spiritualist encouragement.

**The Wimbledon flower shop:** After leaving Lionel at the Rectory, Marianne and Pearless ran a flower shop in Wimbledon (not Lionel's account of events). Clarke: *"The former Frank Peerless was only useful to Marianne in bed. He did very little work at the flower shop."*

## Companion Additional Details (brc-ii-ef)

**Full name:** Mary Anne Emily Rebecca Shaw (not "Marianne Emily Rebecca Shaw" — per Companion's correction)

**First marriage details:** Married Harold Gifford Greenwood (born 2 June 1893), first by Sheriff's warrant at Stranraer, Scotland on 8 June 1914, then at St Anne's Cathedral, Belfast on 12 November 1914. Greenwood deserted under pressure from his mother and eventually went to New Zealand. Later married again; died 13 August 1961.

**Ian Shaw (son):** Raised by Marianne's parents as her "younger brother." Eventually became [[ian-greenwood|Ian Shaw]], residing in Aylesbury, died 1986. His bitterness over abandonment made him Hall's primary source of damaging material.

**Henry Francis Fisher:** The bigamous marriage on 23 February 1935 was not just for companionship — it was a calculated financial survival strategy. Lionel's pension was £125 plus £16 from rents; medicine cost up to £8/month. See [[henry-fisher]].

**Pope's Award:** Before her death, Marianne received the Pope's Award for "devoted services to humanity" for her work with the elderly.

**Companion's verdict:** "She had been a driven woman" — her own description. Not a simple villain, but "someone to whom life had dealt a hard hand." Owen's final assessment: "a delightful person — witty, charming, clever."

## Legal Status of the Marriages (brfa-app)

Babbs and Mathias consulted lawyers and resolved the question definitively:
- The **1914 marriage to Harold Gifford Greenwood** was **legal** — the Age of Marriage Act 1929 raised the age of consent to 16, but before it the age was 12 for females. Marianne gave her age as 17 but this does not affect the marriage's validity.
- No divorce or presumption-of-death order was ever obtained for Greenwood's disappearance; a legal separation arranged by her father's solicitor did not terminate the marriage under English law.
- Therefore the **marriage to Foyster (28 August 1922) was bigamous**, as were all subsequent marriages.

## Assessment in *The Final Analysis* (brfa-ch04)

Babbs and Mathias favour the view that Lionel Foyster was duped about Marianne's first marriage (they "give him the benefit of the doubt"). They find Robert Wood's portrayal of Foyster as a knowing pervert and paedophile unconvincing. Their own position: "the balance of probability is that some of the phenomena were genuine and that the rector was becoming mentally ill."

On Marianne herself: "Sometimes she was adamant that she did not 'haunt' the rectory, and at other times she admitted that she had been responsible for fraudulent phenomena. There is the possibility that she was psychic... There is also the possibility that some genuine phenomena were added to by one or more individuals."
