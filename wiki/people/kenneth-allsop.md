# Kenneth Allsop

Journalist, book reviewer for the *Daily Mail*, and later a prominent television presenter, broadcaster, and interviewer. In the Borley literature he is primarily recorded for a professional journalistic visit to the site with photographer [[godfrey-thurston-hopkins|Godfrey Thurston Hopkins]] in late 1954. He died in 1973.

## The *Picture Post* Investigation (Late 1954)

The editors of *Picture Post*, then a popular and respected magazine, dispatched Allsop as reporter and Hopkins as photographer to Borley to research a feature story on the haunting. During this visit, Hopkins took a mid-afternoon photograph of the remaining gatepost at the northeast entrance — the spot where [[fred-cartwright|Fred Cartwright]] had historically reported seeing the phantom nun. When the film was developed, it revealed a "wispy, black and cloud-like shape" or shadowy outline hovering just inside the gateway. Both Allsop and Hopkins maintained that nothing was visible to the naked eye at the time the photograph was taken.

The photograph was deemed genuine by the magazine's photographic department and published in the **January 1, 1955** issue of *Picture Post* under the caption **"Is this the Borley ghost?"** — illustrated with both the original photograph and a clean comparison photograph taken from the same position. See [[picture-post-photograph]].

## The Dennis Wheatley Contradiction

The famous novelist [[dennis-wheatley|Dennis Wheatley]] recorded a highly embellished version of Allsop's visit in the introduction to a book and inscribed a shortened version inside his personal copy of [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Alfred Henning's]] pamphlet *Haunted Borley*.

> **Contradiction:** Wheatley claimed the visit took place "probably in the early 1940s" when the Rectory ruins were still standing and that Allsop was sent by his editor at the *Daily Mail* specifically to write a debunking article. According to Wheatley, after several nights camping on the grounds, Allsop wrote a skeptical piece — but when the developed photographs allegedly showed **"the clear outline of a nun standing in the gap where one of the old drive gates had been located,"** the editor refused to run the story: **"No, I just don't print it."** Wheatley asserted that both article and photograph were suppressed and "neither has been made public to this day." The sources implicitly suggest that Wheatley's dramatic tale of a suppressed 1940s *Daily Mail* photograph is a misdated, garbled version of the very real 1954 *Picture Post* assignment, whose resulting photograph was, in fact, widely published.

## Later Career

At the time of the Borley visit, Allsop was working in print journalism. He subsequently became a prominent presence on British television as a presenter and interviewer, most notably on the BBC programme *Tonight*, and also wrote popular natural history books.

## Significance

The Allsop/Hopkins visit represents a rare instance of a major mainstream magazine conducting a serious photographic investigation at Borley with professional journalistic and photographic standards. The editorial decision to publish Hopkins's photograph — with caveats but without dismissal — and to commission a comparison photograph, was an unusually careful approach to paranormal reportage for a mass-circulation magazine.
