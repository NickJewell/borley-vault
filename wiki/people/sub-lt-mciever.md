# Sub-Lt Alastair McIver, RN

One of [[miss-susanna-dudley|Miss Susanna Dudley]]'s companions on a visit to [[borley-church|Borley Church]] on 20 February 1949. On the path to the church, McIver was one of three who encountered the unexplained intense smell that [[sub-lt-crossley|Sub-Lt Jonathan Crossley]] could not detect at all. McIver said it was "the most disgusting thing he had ever smelt."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Sub-Lieutenant Alastair McIver (or MacIver), RN**, appears briefly in the Borley Rectory sources as a post-demolition visitor who experienced an unexplained olfactory phenomenon near the church [1-3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following details regarding his involvement:

*   **The Visit and Companions:** On **Sunday, 20 February 1949**, Sub-Lt McIver visited Borley accompanied by three friends: fellow Royal Navy officer **Sub-Lt Jonathan Crossley, RN**, **Miss Audrey Sawrey-Cookson**, and **Miss Susanna Dudley** (of Stradishall Manor, near Newmarket) [1, 3]. 
*   **Purpose:** The group made the excursion specifically hoping to catch a glimpse of the famous **phantom "Nun"**, but they were ultimately disappointed by the "elusive lady" and failed to see any apparitions [3]. 
*   **The Phenomenon:** Finding the weather chilly and very windy, the party decided to seek shelter and crossed the road to examine Borley Church [2, 3]. It was during this walk that McIver and his friends encountered an overpowering, **"strange localised smell half way up the church path"** [1-3]. 
*   **"Embalmed Body" Odor:** Miss Dudley specifically described the overpowering odor they encountered as being **"like an embalmed body"** [2]. 
*   **Sensory Contradiction:** Interestingly, the texts note a specific contradiction regarding the group's sensory experience: while McIver and the two women clearly smelled the odor, the fourth member of the party, Sub-Lt Crossley, **smelled nothing** [2].
*   **Documentation:** The details of this visit, and the full report of the unusual smell, were documented and provided to researchers by his companion, Miss Susanna Dudley [3].

The sources contain no further biographical details or subsequent involvement regarding Alastair McIver beyond this specific 1949 incident.
