# William H. Salter (9 March 1880 – 21 July 1969)

President of the Society for Psychical Research 1947–48; Honorary Treasurer 1920–31; Honorary Secretary 1924–48. MBE 1918. The man who instigated the formal SPR examination of Harry Price's Borley investigation. Peter Underwood found him "overbearing, over-conscious of the importance of the SPR and violently anti-Price and also anti-Borley."

## Early Opposition to Price

As early as November 1931 — seventeen years before the SPR inquiry — Salter wrote confidentially to a Cambridge professor complaining about Price's involvement at Borley: "Mr. Price went down there with one or two of his friends and, as I heard afterwards, 'created a most unfavourable impression'... I am anxious, especially since Price's intervention, that this matter should be treated in strictest confidence."

He also actively tried to interfere with Price's work: he visited [[ethel-bull]] at [[chilton-lodge]] attempting to persuade her and her sisters to "have nothing to do with Price." He tried to get [[lionel-foyster]] to sever his link with Price. Both refused. John Randall later described this as "a clear example of unprofessional conduct... the SPR is not a psychic police force."

## Instigating the Borley Report

Following Price's death in March 1948, Salter (then SPR President) was informed by journalist Charles Sutton's 1948 *Inky Way Annual* piece that Price had allegedly been caught faking phenomena at Borley. He used this as the pretext to commission a formal investigation. He recruited [[eric-dingwall]] and [[kathleen-goldney]], both of whom had personal history with Price, and later introduced [[trevor-hall]] to the project. The resulting [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] (1956) was, in the view of its critics, Salter's vehicle for settling a longstanding personal grievance.

## Verdict

Salter commented in his book *Zoar* (1961) that during Price's tenancy Borley "was invaded week by week by groups whose hunger for sensation much exceeded, in most cases, their competence as investigators" — a summary that many felt described the SPR's own approach to the case. He was never troubled by having failed to investigate Borley when Price asked him to, nor by the fact that the SPR had turned down multiple opportunities to investigate the Rectory before and during Price's tenancy.
