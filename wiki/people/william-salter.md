# William H. Salter (9 March 1880 – 21 July 1969)

President of the Society for Psychical Research 1947–48; Honorary Treasurer 1920–31; Honorary Secretary 1924–48. MBE 1918. The man who instigated the formal SPR examination of Harry Price's Borley investigation. Peter Underwood found him "overbearing, over-conscious of the importance of the SPR and violently anti-Price and also anti-Borley."

## Early Opposition to Price

As early as November 1931 — seventeen years before the SPR inquiry — Salter wrote confidentially to a Cambridge professor complaining about Price's involvement at Borley: "Mr. Price went down there with one or two of his friends and, as I heard afterwards, 'created a most unfavourable impression'... I am anxious, especially since Price's intervention, that this matter should be treated in strictest confidence."

He also actively tried to interfere with Price's work: he visited [[ethel-bull]] at [[chilton-lodge]] attempting to persuade her and her sisters to "have nothing to do with Price." He tried to get [[lionel-foyster]] to sever his link with Price. Both refused. John Randall later described this as "a clear example of unprofessional conduct... the SPR is not a psychic police force."

## Instigating the Borley Report

Following Price's death in March 1948, Salter (then SPR President) was informed by journalist Charles Sutton's 1948 *Inky Way Annual* piece that Price had allegedly been caught faking phenomena at Borley. He used this as the pretext to commission a formal investigation. He recruited [[eric-dingwall]] and [[kathleen-goldney]], both of whom had personal history with Price, and later introduced [[trevor-hall]] to the project. The resulting [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] (1956) was, in the view of its critics, Salter's vehicle for settling a longstanding personal grievance.

## Verdict

Salter commented in his book *Zoar* (1961) that during Price's tenancy Borley "was invaded week by week by groups whose hunger for sensation much exceeded, in most cases, their competence as investigators" — a summary that many felt described the SPR's own approach to the case. He was never troubled by having failed to investigate Borley when Price asked him to, nor by the fact that the SPR had turned down multiple opportunities to investigate the Rectory before and during Price's tenancy.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**William H. Salter** (born in London on **9 March 1880**; died **21 July 1969**) was a lawyer who was called to the bar in 1905, and later became a prominent, if highly controversial, figure in the history of Borley Rectory [1, 2]. He was introduced to psychical research by his wife, the psychologist and psychic Helen Verrall, whom he married in 1915 [2]. Awarded an MBE in 1918, Salter held several major roles within the **Society for Psychical Research (SPR)**, serving as its **Honorary Treasurer (1920–1931)**, **Honorary Secretary (1924–1948)**, and ultimately its **President (1947–1948)** [1].

He was described by peers as possessing a **"rock-like integrity"** and bearing an **"odd resemblance to an extremely intelligent and respectable white Aberdeen terrier"** [1, 2]. However, his detractors, including investigator Peter Underwood, found him to be **"overbearing, over-conscious of the importance of the SPR and violently anti-Price and also anti-Borley"** [1].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following events, contradictions, and ethical controversies surrounding his involvement:

**The 1931 Interference and Unprofessional Conduct**
Salter was drawn into the Borley case in August 1931 when Mary Braithwaite (the sister of Sir John Braithwaite) wrote to him, complaining that the sensational haunting was turning the village into **"almost like a plague spot"** [2]. Her brother subsequently sent Salter a report asserting that the rector's wife, Marianne Foyster, was obviously faking the phenomena [3, 4].

On **9 October 1931**, Salter visited Borley Rectory with Sir George Whitehouse [5, 6]. Upon meeting the Rev. Lionel Foyster, Salter concluded the rector lacked worldly wisdom and was entirely dominated by his wife [6]. Aware that rival ghost hunter Harry Price was scheduled to visit the Rectory a few days later, Salter **deliberately attempted to persuade Foyster to sever all links with Price** and the National Laboratory of Psychical Research, offering the SPR's services instead [5-7]. He urged Foyster to at least make Price sign a legal agreement blocking any publicity [5, 6]. 

When Foyster refused to break his prior commitment to Price, Salter ominously warned him, **"You'll regret it"** [6, 8]. Undeterred, Salter then tried to persuade the Rectory's patrons, the Misses Bull, to block Price's visit [9]. Miss Ethel Bull made no comment at the time but later admitted she **"thought he had a cheek to ask"** [9, 10]. 

This attempt to poach the investigation sparked heavy ethical criticism. John L. Randall later labeled Salter's actions a **"clear example of unprofessional conduct,"** comparing it to a medical practitioner trying to steal a colleague's patients, especially since Price had been working on the Borley case since 1929 [7, 11]. While Salter's defenders argued he was merely trying to prevent undue publicity, critics countered that the SPR was **"not a psychic police force"** and that Salter had absolutely no right to interfere [7, 11].

**The Confidential "Schiller Letter"**
On **11 November 1931**, Salter wrote a letter marked "CONFIDENTIAL" to Dr. E.C.S. Schiller regarding his afternoon visit to Borley. In it, he admitted he saw no phenomena but gossiped about two recent "fiascos" at the house. One was an August séance that left Mrs. Foyster having "something like a fit," and the other was Harry Price's October visit, which Salter gleefully noted had **"created a most unfavorable impression"** [12].

**Spearheading the "Borley Report" (Post-1948)**
Following Harry Price's death in 1948 and the subsequent publication of Charles Sutton's allegations of fraud, it was **Salter who pressed his SPR colleagues to launch a rigorous re-investigation into Borley** [13]. Guided by Salter's longstanding antipathy toward Price, the objective of what became the highly skeptical *Borley Report* was seemingly **not to investigate the truth of the haunting, but specifically to discredit Harry Price** [14]. Salter waved off concerns that exposing Price might damage the credibility of psychical research, confidently asserting that the report would be a **"bestseller and a credit to all"** [15].

During this period, Salter also engaged in selective damage control. When Mabel Smith (widow of the 1929 rector) wrote a letter to the *Daily Mail* in May 1948 dismissing the ghosts, Salter rushed to publish his own letter insisting the SPR was never called to investigate Borley in 1929 and was "in no way responsible for the excessive publicity" [16]. He then personally contacted Mrs. Smith to gather further anti-Price testimony [16].

**Contradictions and Reactions to Criticism**
Salter's hostility made him highly defensive against anyone who questioned the SPR's findings. When researcher Robert J. Hastings wrote to Salter over Christmas 1956 to point out that the *Borley Report* was heavily biased—noting that every single mistake made by the authors conveniently "tended in a direction unfavourable to Price"—an unsettled Salter wrote to his colleague Mollie Goldney that he regarded Hastings **"with some suspicion"** [17]. 

A final, glaring contradiction in Salter's record is found in his 1961 book, *Zoar*. In it, he caustically derided Price's 1937–38 observer tenancy, stating that Borley was invaded by groups **"whose hunger for sensation much exceeded, in most cases, their competence as investigators"** [18]. Yet, as the Borley sources point out, it was Salter himself who had **expressly turned down the opportunity to officially investigate Borley on behalf of the SPR** when the highly active Foyster phenomena were occurring [18].
