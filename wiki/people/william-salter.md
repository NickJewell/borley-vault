# William H. Salter (9 March 1880 – 21 July 1969)

Born in London; called to the bar in 1905. President of the [[spr|Society for Psychical Research]] 1947–48; Honorary Treasurer 1920–31; Honorary Secretary 1924–48. MBE 1918. Introduced to psychical research by his wife, the psychologist and psychic Helen Verrall, whom he married in 1915 [2]. The man who instigated the formal SPR examination of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s Borley investigation.

Described by peers as possessing a "rock-like integrity" and bearing an "odd resemblance to an extremely intelligent and respectable white Aberdeen terrier" [1, 2]. [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] found him "overbearing, over-conscious of the importance of the SPR and violently anti-Price and also anti-Borley."

## Early Opposition to Price

As early as **9 October 1931** — seventeen years before the SPR inquiry — Salter visited [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] with [[sir-george-whitehouse|Sir George Whitehouse]] during the height of the Foyster disturbances [5, 6]. Having concluded that [[lionel-foyster|the rector]] lacked worldly wisdom and was entirely dominated by his wife, he **deliberately attempted to persuade Foyster to sever all links with Price** and the National Laboratory of Psychical Research, offering the SPR's services instead [5–7]. He also urged Foyster to make Price sign a legal agreement blocking any publicity. When Foyster refused, Salter ominously warned him: **"You'll regret it"** [6, 8].

Undeterred, Salter then tried to persuade the Rectory's patrons — the Misses Bull — to block Price's visit. Miss [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]] made no comment at the time but later admitted she "thought he had a cheek to ask" [9, 10]. He also visited [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]] at [[chilton-lodge]] attempting to persuade her and her sisters to "have nothing to do with Price." He tried to get [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] to sever his link with Price. Both refused.

John Randall later described this as "a clear example of unprofessional conduct... the SPR is not a psychic police force."

On **11 November 1931**, Salter wrote a letter marked "CONFIDENTIAL" to Dr. E.C.S. Schiller regarding his Borley visit. In it, he gossiped about two recent "fiascos": an August séance that left [[marianne-foyster|Mrs. Foyster]] having "something like a fit," and [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s October visit, which Salter gleefully noted had "created a most unfavorable impression" [12].

## Instigating the Borley Report

Following [[harry-price|Price]]'s death in March 1948, Salter (then SPR President) was informed by journalist Charles Sutton's 1948 *Inky Way Annual* piece that Price had allegedly been caught faking phenomena at Borley. He used this as the pretext to commission a formal investigation, guided by his longstanding antipathy toward Price. He recruited [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] and [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen Goldney]] — both of whom had personal history with Price — and later introduced [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] to the project.

Salter waved off concerns that exposing Price might damage the credibility of psychical research, confidently asserting the report would be a "bestseller and a credit to all" [15]. When [[mabel-smith|Mabel Smith]] wrote a letter to the *Daily Mail* in May 1948 dismissing the ghosts, Salter rushed to publish his own letter insisting the SPR was never called to investigate Borley in 1929 and was "in no way responsible for the excessive publicity," then personally contacted Mrs. Smith to gather further anti-Price testimony [16].

The resulting [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] (1956) was, in the view of its critics, Salter's vehicle for settling a longstanding personal grievance.

## Contradictions and Reactions to Criticism

When researcher [[robert-hastings|Robert J. Hastings]] wrote to Salter over Christmas 1956 to point out that the Borley Report was heavily biased — noting that every single mistake made by the authors conveniently "tended in a direction unfavourable to Price" — an unsettled Salter wrote to his colleague [[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]] that he regarded Hastings "with some suspicion" [17].

## Verdict

Salter commented in his book *Zoar* (1961) that during Price's tenancy Borley "was invaded week by week by groups whose hunger for sensation much exceeded, in most cases, their competence as investigators" [18]. Critics pointed out that it was Salter himself who had **expressly turned down the opportunity to officially investigate Borley on behalf of the SPR** when the highly active Foyster phenomena were occurring — leaving a void that Price filled [18].
