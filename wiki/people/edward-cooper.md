Groom-gardener at [[borley-rectory]] from April 1916 to March 1920, living with his wife in the cottage adjoining the Rectory. One of the most important independent witnesses in the entire Borley record. Price interviewed him twice — in 1929 and again in March 1939 with Rev. Henning — and noted: "They do not vary by a hairsbreadth."

## Phenomena Witnessed

**"The padding dog" (1916–1919)**: For more than three years, Cooper and his wife were disturbed almost every night by pattering sounds from the adjacent room — "just as if a large dog or small child had jumped from a height and was padding round the room." They grew accustomed to it. Then one night after the usual padding began, there was "a terrific crash as if all the china on the kitchen dresser had fallen to the floor simultaneously." Immediate investigation: not a thing was displaced or broken. The padding was never heard again.

**Black shape (early morning, 1919)**: "In the form of a little man," running round their bedroom in the grey pre-dawn light. Vanished before either could act.

**Nun sightings**: The Coopers saw the figure crossing the courtyard multiple times from their cottage window. On the most striking occasion, they observed her step onto the loose cover of a ground-level inspection chamber. The cover was known to rattle and clank when walked on — the figure stepped on it with no sound whatsoever. Cooper made inquiries the following morning; no one in the neighbourhood had seen a woman matching the description.

**The phantom coach** — the most detailed account in the record: On a bright moonlight night, Cooper saw approaching lights across the meadow by the church. As they neared he realised they were headlamps on "a large, old-fashioned black coach" sweeping rapidly toward the Rectory. He saw:
- Two horses with *glittering harness*
- Two figures in high top hats on the coachman's box
- Duration: approximately 30 seconds of clear visibility
- *No sound whatsoever*

He shouted to his wife; she arrived at the window just as the coach swept into the farmyard and disappeared. Cooper noted the road outside the Rectory sounds hollow under heavy vehicles — possibly a tunnel runs beneath it.

## Reliability

Price emphasises Cooper's value as a witness: a "sturdy yeoman type, intelligent, hardworking, frank, and downright." His two accounts (1929 and 1939) were consistent to the hairsbreadth. He had no interest in psychical research and no apparent motive to fabricate.

After the Coopers left the Rectory cottage in March 1920, Cooper experienced "no phenomena of any kind, anywhere."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Edward Cooper** was employed as a groom, gardener, and coachman for the Bull family at Borley Rectory [1, 2]. He and his wife resided in the Rectory Cottage (the stable-coachhouse) with their adolescent daughter, Lily, from April 1916 until March 1920 [1-4]. Described as a sturdy, "sensible and rather religious" yeoman, Cooper and his wife provided some of the most enduring firsthand testimonies of the early hauntings [5, 6]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, quotes, and specific contradictions regarding his experiences:

**The "Padding Dog" and the Imaginary Crash (1916–1919)**
Shortly after moving into the cottage, the Coopers were disturbed almost nightly by the sound of a "padding dog" pacing in the room above or adjoining their bedroom [1, 2, 7]. This phenomenon continued for about three years until it culminated in a "tremendous crash," sounding as though a heavy tray of crockery had been smashed to the floor [3, 8]. Mr. Cooper immediately lit a candle and searched the kitchen, but found absolutely nothing disturbed [3, 8]. Following this dramatic climax, the padding noises ceased entirely [2, 8]. 

**The Phantom Nun and the Inspection Cover (Autumn 1916)**
One evening at dusk, Cooper observed a figure dressed as a "Sister of Mercy" leaving the back entrance of the Rectory [7, 9, 10]. He followed the figure at a distance of about six feet—one account notes he was accompanied by the Rev. Harry Bull [11]. As she crossed the courtyard, the apparition stepped directly onto a loose metal inspection cover that normally produced a loud, metallic clank; however, she made absolutely no sound [10]. The figure completely vanished upon reaching the roadway [7, 9]. 

**The "Black Shape" (1919)**
Early one morning near dawn, the couple awoke to see a "black shape" darting around their bedroom [3, 10]. While Mrs. Cooper thought it resembled a dog, Mr. Cooper described it as taking the form of a "little man" [2, 3, 10]. As Cooper jumped out of bed to investigate, the form vanished [10].

**The Phantom Coach Sighting and the Price Contradiction**
Cooper’s most famous experience occurred on a bright, moonlit night when he looked out of his window and witnessed an old-fashioned coach drawn by two horses, complete with glittering harness and lit headlamps, sweeping across the grounds in complete silence [12-14]. He watched the vehicle for about thirty seconds before it disappeared [14]. 

This sighting became the subject of a major contradiction regarding Harry Price's reporting. In his books, Price claimed that Cooper saw the coach from his front bedroom window, that it was moving *towards* the Rectory, and that it was driven by two figures in high top-hats [13, 15]. However, when investigator Peter Underwood and Brigadier C.A.L. Brownlow interviewed Cooper in August 1954, Cooper revealed that Price had got the story **"a little muddled in the book"** [16, 17]. Cooper clarified that he actually saw the coach from a *rear* window, that it was hurtling in the *opposite* direction (away from the Rectory), and that it was driven by only a single, dim figure on the box, not headless or top-hatted [16, 17]. 

**Later Life and Media Appearances**
The Coopers left Borley in March 1920 when the remaining Bull family relocated, moving to a villa named "Werneth" in Broom Street, Great Cornard, Sudbury [18, 19]. They never experienced any further paranormal phenomena for the rest of their lives [18, 20]. 

Harry Price interviewed Cooper twice, first on June 13, 1929, and again nearly ten years later on March 29, 1939; Price noted that Cooper's account never varied by a hairsbreadth during these sessions [6, 21]. The Coopers also publicly recounted their experiences on the BBC radio broadcast *The Haunted Rectory* in June 1947 [21-23]. Despite Price's errors in publishing his story, Cooper maintained his testimony to the end of his life, insisting he had genuinely seen the coach on that moonlit night [16, 17].
