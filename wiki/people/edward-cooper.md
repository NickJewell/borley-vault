# Edward Cooper

Groom-gardener and coachman for the [[henry-bull|Bull]] family at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] from April 1916 to March 1920. He and his wife — and their adolescent daughter Lily — resided in the Rectory Cottage (the stable-coachhouse) adjoining the Rectory. One of the most important independent witnesses in the entire Borley record. Described by [[harry-price|Price]] as a "sturdy yeoman type, intelligent, hardworking, frank, and downright," and as "sensible and rather religious" [5, 6]. Price interviewed him twice — in June 1929 and again on 29 March 1939 with Rev. Henning — and noted: "They do not vary by a hairsbreadth."

## The "Padding Dog" (1916–1919)

Shortly after moving into the cottage, the Coopers were disturbed almost every night by pattering sounds from the adjacent room — "just as if a large dog or small child had jumped from a height and was padding round the room." This phenomenon continued for about three years until it culminated in a "tremendous crash" as though a heavy tray of crockery had been smashed to the floor [3, 8]. Cooper immediately lit a candle and searched the kitchen — not a thing was displaced or broken [3, 8]. Following this dramatic climax, the padding noises ceased entirely [2, 8].

## Black Shape (early morning, 1919)

Early one morning near dawn, the couple awoke to see a black shape darting around their bedroom [3, 10]. While Mrs Cooper thought it resembled a dog, Mr Cooper described it as taking the form of a "little man" [2, 3, 10]. As Cooper jumped out of bed, the form vanished [10].

## Nun Sightings and the Inspection Cover (Autumn 1916)

On one evening at dusk, Cooper observed a figure dressed as a "Sister of Mercy" leaving the back entrance of the Rectory [7, 9, 10]. He followed the figure at a distance of about six feet — one account notes he was accompanied by the [[harry-bull|Rev. Harry Bull]] [11]. As she crossed the courtyard, the apparition stepped directly onto a loose metal inspection cover that normally produced a loud, metallic clank; however, she made absolutely no sound [10]. The figure completely vanished upon reaching the roadway [7, 9].

The Coopers saw the figure crossing the courtyard multiple times from their cottage window. Cooper made inquiries the following morning; no one in the neighbourhood had seen a woman matching the description.

## The Phantom Coach

The most detailed coach account in the Borley record. On a bright moonlight night, Cooper looked out of his window and witnessed an old-fashioned coach drawn by two horses, complete with glittering harness and lit headlamps, sweeping across the grounds in complete silence [12–14]. He watched the vehicle for about thirty seconds before it disappeared [14]. He shouted to his wife; she arrived at the window just as the coach swept into the farmyard and disappeared. Cooper noted the road outside the Rectory sounds hollow under heavy vehicles — possibly a tunnel runs beneath it.

> **Contradiction:** In his books, Price claimed that Cooper saw the coach from his front bedroom window, that it was moving *towards* the Rectory, and that it was driven by two figures in high top-hats [13, 15]. However, when Peter Underwood and Brigadier C.A.L. Brownlow interviewed Cooper in August 1954, Cooper revealed that Price had got the story "a little muddled in the book" [16, 17]. Cooper clarified that he actually saw the coach from a *rear* window, that it was hurtling in the *opposite* direction (away from the Rectory), and that it was driven by only a single, dim figure on the box — not headless or top-hatted [16, 17].

## Reliability

After the Coopers left the Rectory cottage in March 1920, when the remaining Bull family relocated, they moved to a villa named "Werneth" in Broom Street, Great Cornard, Sudbury [18, 19]. Cooper experienced "no phenomena of any kind, anywhere" for the rest of his life [18, 20]. Harry Price's two interviews (1929 and 1939) were separated by nearly a decade, yet Price noted that Cooper's account never varied by a hairsbreadth [6, 21].

The Coopers also publicly recounted their experiences on the BBC radio broadcast *The Haunted Rectory* in June 1947 [21–23]. Despite Price's errors in publishing his story, Cooper maintained his testimony to the end of his life, insisting he had genuinely seen the coach on that moonlit night [16, 17].
