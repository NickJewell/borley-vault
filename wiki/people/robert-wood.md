# Robert Wood

Author of *The Widow of Borley* (c. late 1980s/early 1990s), the only full-length biography of [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]]. The most aggressively skeptical of all major Borley books.

## Background

Wood grew up in **Acton, Suffolk**, a few miles from Borley, and recalls childhood excursions to "see the ghosts" — though he later stated firmly that he never saw a ghost at Borley and concluded that no one else should have either. This local upbringing gives him a perspective no other Borley author shares: direct personal knowledge of the regional folklore tradition independent of the Price-inflated legend. He noted that Acton had an identical death-coach legend (attached to ruined Acton Place, former home of miser Robert Jennens), demonstrating the Borley coach was standard regional folklore rather than unique phenomena. He also knew La Crosse, Wisconsin — and other American towns where Marianne settled — personally.

## *The Widow of Borley*

**Scope:** A biography of [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] from birth (1899) to the mid-1980s when she was still alive in La Crosse, Wisconsin. The book heavily utilised and was essentially an abridged version of an unpublished, highly libellous five-volume manuscript titled *Marianne Foyster of Borley Rectory*, originally compiled in 1958 by the prominent Borley skeptic [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]]. Wood himself admitted that researching the project was exceedingly difficult, as Marianne Foyster was still living in the United States and wanted nothing more to do with Borley, and had deliberately kept her adopted son ([[vincent-oneil|Vincent O'Neil]]) entirely ignorant of her past.

**Thesis:** The Borley haunting was "based on nothing more than unscrupulous sensation-seeking on the one hand and credulity on the other." In a letter to [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] dated 22 March 1992, Wood explicitly stated: "I am very sceptical about the alleged haunting of Borley." He dismissed [[harry-price|Harry Price]] as an "astute and agreeable old fraudster" who "never believed that Borley was haunted" but saw there was money to be made. Marianne was a pathological liar; Foyster was a sexual deviant who was a willing accomplice rather than a passive dupe.

**Unique contributions:**
- The only source to document the **insurance company's documented repudiation** of Gregson's fire claim in precise language: "We repudiated his impudent claim for 'accidental loss by fire' — £7,356 — pleading bluntly that he had fired the place himself" (lawyer William Crocker and loss adjuster Col. Cuthbert Buckle)
- Full details of Marianne's birth (5 Guy Wood Cottages, Romiley, Cheshire; real name Mary Anne Emily Rebecca Shaw)
- Wood's thesis on **Foyster's sexual psychology** — not previously stated in print
- **[[willy-palmer|Willy Palmer]]** identified as Marianne's village confederate who rigged the collapsing shelf
- **Ian Shaw's account** that d'Arles told him Marianne was "a sexual maniac" and that both faked the effects
- [[eileen-garrett|Eileen Garrett]]'s interview notes including: Marianne admitted killing Foyster "had often been in her mind, but... he was too cunning for anything like that to happen to him"

**Critical limitations:**
- No serious treatment of Bull-period phenomena or post-fire sightings
- Inadequate engagement with [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard Whitehouse's]] contemporary notes and his own rebuttal of SPR charges
- The "Willy Palmer" claim (via Ian Shaw's testimony) is not independently corroborated
- Sexual psychology of Foyster is speculative and polemically stated
- Ian Shaw was a hostile source whose evidence must be treated with caution

## Allegations Against the Foysters

Wood's book is most notable for its incredibly hostile psychological assessments:

- **Marianne Foyster**: Wood believed there was "compelling evidence to suggest that Marianne's claims were wholly fraudulent." He echoed [[trevor-hall|Hall]]'s accusation that Marianne actually murdered [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] in 1945 (possibly by putting a pillow over his face) so she would be free to marry her American GI lover.
- **Lionel Foyster**: Wood depicted Lionel as a mentally and physically sick "pervert," a voyeur, and a willing accomplice in his wife's bigamy and infidelities, suggesting he had been sent to the Canadian backwoods early in his career to cover up indiscretions with underage girls. Wood concluded that Lionel Foyster "had as peculiar a sexuality as his wife."
- **The Misses Bull**: Wood characterised them as "malicious and vindictive old women" whose village gossip formed the basis for the séance phenomena.

## Contradictions and Backlash

- **The Ghost Club Incident**: Prior to publication, [[peter-underwood|Underwood]] had invited Wood to give a talk to the Ghost Club Society. After reading an advance copy, Underwood abruptly withdrew the invitation. He wrote to Wood: "I do not recognise the pictures you paint of these people. There are many mistakes and omissions in the book."
- **The "Last Widow" Contradiction**: [[claudine-mathias|Claudine Mathias]] criticised Wood's title as "misleading and rather illogical," pointing out that she, not Marianne Foyster, was technically the last widow of Borley. Mathias also fiercely contested Wood's characterisation of [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]], noting that Ethel was charming and kind.
- **Historical Inaccuracies**: Wood falsely claimed that [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] was not a Cambridge graduate, when in fact he was. Other researchers noted that Wood's determination to pour scorn on the Borley saga led him to make massive assumptions "not based on firm foundations."

## Assessment

Wood's biographical research — particularly on Marianne's early life, her American career, and the documented insurance fraud — adds genuine new material. His framework for understanding Foyster as complicit (rather than duped) aligns with what Marianne told Owen and Mitchell in 1978. However, his outright dismissal of all phenomena as fraud does not survive contact with the New Horizons Foundation's conclusion that "Borley is unique in its complications and admixture of truth and fraud."
