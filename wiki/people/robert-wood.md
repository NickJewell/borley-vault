# Robert Wood

Author of *The Widow of Borley* (c. late 1980s/early 1990s), the only full-length biography of [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]]. The most aggressively skeptical of all major Borley books.

## Background

Wood grew up in **Acton, Suffolk**, a few miles from Borley, and recalls childhood excursions to "see the ghosts." This gives him a perspective no other Borley author shares: direct personal knowledge of the local folklore tradition independent of the Price-inflated legend. He noted that Acton had an identical death-coach legend (attached to ruined Acton Place, former home of miser Robert Jennens), demonstrating the Borley coach was standard regional folklore rather than unique phenomena.

He also knew La Crosse, Wisconsin — and other American towns where Marianne settled — personally.

## *The Widow of Borley*

**Scope:** A biography of Marianne from birth (1899) to the mid-1980s when she was still alive in La Crosse, Wisconsin. Draws on Marianne's own testimony (via the Swanson interviews), Ian Shaw's account, the SPR files, Price's HPL archive, and Wood's own research including conversations with people who knew Marianne in America.

**Thesis:** The Borley haunting was "based on nothing more than unscrupulous sensation-seeking on the one hand and credulity on the other." Marianne was a pathological liar; Foyster was a sexual deviant who was a willing accomplice rather than a passive dupe.

**Unique contributions:**
- The only source to document the **insurance company's documented repudiation** of Gregson's fire claim in precise language: "We repudiated his impudent claim for 'accidental loss by fire' — £7,356 — pleading bluntly that he had fired the place himself" (lawyer William Crocker and loss adjuster Col. Cuthbert Buckle)
- Full details of Marianne's birth (5 Guy Wood Cottages, Romiley, Cheshire; real name Mary Anne Emily Rebecca Shaw)
- Wood's thesis on **Foyster's sexual psychology** — not previously stated in print
- **Willy Palmer** identified as Marianne's village confederate who rigged the collapsing shelf
- **Ian Shaw's account** that d'Arles told him Marianne was "a sexual maniac" and that both faked the effects
- Eileen Garrett's interview notes including: Marianne admitted killing Foyster "had often been in her mind, but... he was too cunning for anything like that to happen to him"

**Critical limitations:**
- No serious treatment of Bull-period phenomena or post-fire sightings
- Inadequate engagement with Dom Richard Whitehouse's contemporary notes and his own rebuttal of SPR charges
- The "Willy Palmer" claim (via Ian Shaw's testimony) is not independently corroborated
- Sexual psychology of Foyster is speculative and polemically stated
- Ian Shaw was a hostile source whose evidence must be treated with caution

## Assessment

Wood's biographical research — particularly on Marianne's early life, her American career, and the documented insurance fraud — adds genuine new material. His framework for understanding Foyster as complicit (rather than duped) aligns with what Marianne told Owen and Mitchell in 1978. His characterization of d'Arles as the "Rasputin of the Rectory" and his tracing of the Wimbledon flower-shop as the pivot of the relationship are well-supported.

However, his outright dismissal of all phenomena as fraud does not survive contact with the New Horizons Foundation's conclusion that "Borley is unique in its complications and admixture of truth and fraud."
