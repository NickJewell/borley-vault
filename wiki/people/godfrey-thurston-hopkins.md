# Godfrey Thurston Hopkins

Photographer working for the magazine *Picture Post*. Dispatched to Borley with reporter [[kenneth-allsop|Kenneth Allsop]] in **late 1954** to investigate the Borley haunting for a feature article [2, 3].

## The Ghost Photograph

During the visit, Hopkins used a Rolleiflex camera to photograph the northeast entrance to the former rectory driveway — the specific spot where [[fred-cartwright|Fred Cartwright]] had claimed to see [[the-nun|the phantom nun]] on four occasions in 1927, and which was marked by one of the four tall driveway gateposts and a short section of picket fencing [2, 3]. Hopkins took the photograph on a clear, sunny mid-afternoon with good visibility, specifically intending to emphasise the outline of the gatepost against the clear sky [1, 2].

When the film was developed, the photograph revealed a "wispy, black and cloud-like shape" or "dark shadow" hovering just inside the Rectory gateway — appearing to be superimposed upon or standing in front of the wooden stakes of the fence [1, 3, 4]. Hopkins stated:
- It was mid-afternoon with good visibility
- He was emphasising the gatepost against the clear sky
- The dark shadow was **not visible in the viewfinder**
- He could not account for its appearance

He ultimately suggested the dark shadow he had captured was the apparition of the Borley Nun [1].

*Picture Post*'s editorial staff and photographic experts were mystified but, after careful scrutiny, established that the negative was genuine and had not been tampered with [3, 4]. A second photographer was sent to Borley with instructions to take a picture from the exact same position; the resulting photograph showed only the normal gatepost and fencing [3, 4].

Both photographs were published in *Picture Post* on **January 1, 1955**, under the provocative headline **"Is this the Borley Rectory ghost?"** [1, 3–5]. The feature article also included interviews with local residents, including Ethel and Alfred Bull, the Coopers, and the Hennings [5]. The photograph gained widespread international attention, including a syndicated version published in Australia's *Sydney Morning Herald* [5], and was later reprinted in *Two Worlds* on January 28, 1956 [6].

## Scepticism and Comparisons

Despite Hopkins's suggestion that he had photographed the famous phantom nun, the image has been heavily criticised. Neutral observers reviewing the photograph concluded that the anomaly was nothing more than an "indistinct black shape, which could in fact be anything" [1].

The authors of *The Final Analysis* note a peculiar coincidence involving Hopkins's equipment: he was not the only person to experience mechanical or photographic anomalies at that exact gate. Seven years earlier, in March 1947, [[peter-hilton-rowe|Dr. Peter Hilton-Rowe]] had attempted to take a reading at the same gatepost, only to have his light meter completely malfunction and jam, only resuming normal operation once he moved away from the spot [7]. This is consistent with the [[ley-lines-at-borley|ley-lines hypothesis]] proposed by Babbs and Mathias.
