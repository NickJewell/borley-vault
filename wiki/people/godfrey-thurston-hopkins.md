# Godfrey Thurston Hopkins

Photographer working for the magazine *Picture Post*. Dispatched to Borley with reporter Kenneth Allsop in late 1954 to investigate the Borley haunting for an article.

## The Ghost Photograph

Hopkins attempted to photograph one of the two gatepost entrances to the former rectory driveway — the specific spot where [[fred-cartwright|Fred Cartwright]] had claimed to see the [[the-nun|phantom nun]] on four occasions in 1927. The intention was to capture the gatepost and picket fencing against the clear sky.

When the film was developed, the photograph showed **a wispy, shadowy outline of a figure** superimposed upon or standing in front of the picket fencing stakes — not visible in the viewfinder when the picture was taken. Hopkins stated:
- It was mid-afternoon with good visibility
- He was emphasising the gatepost against the clear sky
- The dark shadow was **not visible in the viewfinder**
- He could not account for its appearance

*Picture Post*'s photographic department confirmed the negative was genuine and had not been tampered with. A second photographer was sent to take a picture from exactly the same position — it showed only the gatepost and fencing.

*Picture Post* published both pictures in their issue dated **1 January 1955** under the headline "Is this the Borley Rectory ghost?"

The authors note this may be another example of mechanical/photographic equipment behaving anomalously at Borley, consistent with the [[ley-lines-at-borley|ley-lines hypothesis]]. They compare it to Dr C. Hilton-Rowe's light meter malfunctioning at exactly the same gate in March 1947.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Godfrey Thurston Hopkins** was a photographer for the popular (now defunct) magazine *Picture Post* [1, 2]. He holds a specific place in the Borley Rectory narrative as the creator of one of the most controversial and debated photographs of an alleged apparition at the site. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, dates, and contradictions regarding his involvement:

**The Late 1954 Visit and Photograph**
Towards the end of **1954**, the managers of *Picture Post* decided to concentrate their attention on the Borley Rectory story [2]. Hopkins was dispatched to the village alongside the noted broadcaster and reporter Kenneth Allsop to research a feature article [3]. 

During the visit, Hopkins used a Rolleiflex camera to take a picture of the northeast entrance to the Rectory site [3]. This location featured one of the four tall driveway gateposts and a short section of picket fencing—the precise spot where the journeyman carpenter Fred Cartwright and others claimed to have seen the phantom nun [2, 3]. Hopkins took the photograph on a clear, sunny mid-afternoon with good visibility, specifically intending to emphasize the outline of the gatepost and the fencing against the clear sky [1, 2]. 

**The Anomaly and Hopkins's Reaction**
When Hopkins's film was developed, the picture revealed a "wispy, black and cloud-like shape" or "dark shadow" hovering just inside the Rectory gateway [1, 3]. The shadowy figure appeared to be superimposed upon or standing in front of the wooden stakes of the fence [4]. 

Hopkins was completely unaware of anything untoward when he pressed the shutter [1]. Confronted with the developed image, he stated definitively that the dark mass had not been visible in his viewfinder and he was at a loss to provide a rational explanation for why it appeared [2]. He ultimately suggested that the dark shadow he had captured was the apparition of the Borley Nun [1]. 

**Investigation and Publication (January 1, 1955)**
The editorial staff and photographic experts at *Picture Post* were mystified by the image, but after subjecting the negative to careful scrutiny, they established that it was perfectly genuine and had not been tampered with [3, 4]. To test the location, the magazine sent a second photographer to Borley with instructions to take a picture from the exact same position; the resulting second photograph showed only the normal gatepost and fencing [3, 4].

Both photographs were published in *Picture Post* on **January 1, 1955** [1, 3-5]. Hopkins's photograph was much enlarged and printed under the provocative caption: **"Is this the Borley Rectory ghost?"** (also cited as "Is this the Borley ghost?") [3, 4]. The feature article also included interviews with local residents, including Ethel and Alfred Bull, the Coopers, and the Hennings [5]. 

The photograph gained widespread international attention. A syndicated version was published in Australia's *Sydney Morning Herald* [5], and it was later reprinted in the publication *Two Worlds* on January 28, 1956 [6]. 

**Skepticism and Comparisons**
Despite Hopkins's suggestion that he had photographed the famous phantom nun, the image has been heavily criticized. Neutral observers reviewing the photograph concluded that the anomaly was nothing more than an "indistinct black shape, which could in fact be anything" [1]. 

In the broader context of the haunting, historians note a peculiar coincidence involving Hopkins's equipment: he was not the only person to experience mechanical or photographic anomalies at that exact gate [7]. Seven years earlier, in March 1947, Dr. Peter Hilton-Rowe had attempted to take a reading at the same gatepost, only to have his light meter completely malfunction and jam, only resuming normal operation once he moved away from the spot [7].
