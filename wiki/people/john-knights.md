# John Knights (formerly John Fisher)

Boy adopted by [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] in August 1935 as **John Fisher**, passed off as her son by [[henry-fisher|Johnny Fisher]]; abandoned by Marianne in 1946 and subsequently adopted by [[letty-knights|Letty Knights]].

## Origins

John was born **Timothy Eves** in Birmingham on **14 July 1935**, to an unmarried shorthand typist [1]. [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] adopted him from the Church of England Adoption Society in August 1935, renaming him **John Henry Francis Fisher** (though she disliked the name and called him "Johnnie") [5]. She was at the time living a double life in Ipswich, engaged in a bigamous marriage with the mentally unstable commercial traveller [[henry-fisher|Henry Francis Fisher]] [2, 3]. Marianne passed the adopted baby off as Fisher's biological son, sending him a telegram that read, **"It's a boy!"** [1, 4]. John was baptised at St Pancras' Roman Catholic Church in Ipswich on 22 September 1935 [5].

## Childhood and Abuse at Rendlesham

John spent his early childhood during the war years at [[dairy-cottage-rendlesham|Dairy Cottage in Rendlesham]] alongside his adopted sister Astrid (born Mary Lex) and an older adopted girl, Adelaide [6, 7]. His childhood was marked by neglect, abuse, and strict Catholic instruction [8, 9].

- Marianne forbade the children from calling her "Mother" or "Mummy"; she insisted on being addressed exclusively as **"Morny"** [7, 10].
- Marianne treated the children with sadistic cruelty, frequently beating them and punishing misbehaviour by holding their heads in buckets of cold water for dangerously long periods [10, 11]. John recalled her threatening: **"If you don't behave yourself, I'll send you to Johnny Fisher"** [7].
- John got on well with the bedridden [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]], whom the children called **"Fafa"** [7, 10]. He spent time reading to him, writing his letters, and feeding him from an invalid cup [10, 12].
- John later revealed to his adoptive mother that "Morny" had "strange soldiers" billeted on her every night during the war, and that due to a lack of beds, the children **"always slept with mother"** [10].

## The Abandonment and Second Adoption

Sometime between Easter and Whitsun in 1946, when John was ten, Marianne took him to the cinema and introduced him to a local woman named Letty Knights [13, 14]. She told John he would be spending the Whitsun half-term with Mrs Knights — then casually said goodbye and vanished, fleeing to America to join her new American G.I. husband, Robert O'Neil [13–15]. John was left with absolutely nothing but the clothes he stood up in [13, 14]. Letty Knights legally adopted him, changing his surname to **Knights** [14, 15].

## The Priesthood and Severing Ties with Astrid

John was brought up strictly in the Roman Catholic faith and began training for the priesthood [9, 16]. However, he suffered a great emotional crisis when he discovered that he and his sister [[astrid-fisher|Astrid Fisher]] were both adopted and not blood relations [9, 16]. His seminary superiors advised that it would be inappropriate to maintain a relationship with a young woman who was not his "real sister" [16]. Anguished, John wrote Astrid a letter stating they could no longer communicate [16]. He ultimately did not complete his training; he left, married, and raised a family of his own [16].

## Significance: The Wallace Tower Connection

John Knights was the critical intermediary who enabled [[wallace-tower|Wallace Tower]] to find his long-lost sister [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide]] in the 1970s: when Wallace visited Woodbridge looking for Adelaide, John was able to provide her last known English address, leading eventually to the 1974 Tower family reunion. John also later confirmed to Kevin Taylor ([[astrid-fisher|Astrid Fisher]]'s son) some of the details of life with Marianne at Rendlesham.

## The BBC Documentary and Reunion

John appeared in a 1994 BBC2 documentary series called *Picture This* [17, 18]. [[astrid-fisher|Astrid]]'s son Kevin Taylor had been searching for his mother (who had emigrated to Australia) and placed an ad in the *East Anglian Daily Times* [9, 18]. John answered the ad [9]. Astrid was flown to the UK and she and John were tearfully reunited in his kitchen — John finally able to hug her and apologise for the letter he had written decades earlier [16, 19].

In 1992, John received a letter from America informing him that Marianne was gravely ill and in a coma in a Utah hospital [19]. Despite the severe abuse and abandonment he had suffered, John sent a short message to be read to her: **"To Morny. From all over here, love and forgiveness"** [19]. A nun at the hospital read the message to Marianne, and a distinct look of relief and happiness came over her face. As John reflected: **"I just feel she felt that, 'John has forgiven me for what I've done to him — I can die at peace'"** [19]. Because no one else in the world knew her by the name "Morny," Marianne recognised instantly that the message of forgiveness was from the son she had abused and abandoned [19].
