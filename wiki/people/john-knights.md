# John Knights (formerly John Fisher)

Boy adopted by [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] in August 1935 as **John Fisher**, passed off as her son by [[henry-fisher|Johnny Fisher]]; abandoned by Marianne in 1946 and subsequently adopted by [[letty-knights|Letty Knights]].

## Origins

John was born **Timothy Eves** in Birmingham on 14 July 1935, to an unmarried shorthand typist. [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] adopted him through the Church of England Adoption Society, renaming him John Fisher. She told her husband [[henry-fisher|Johnny Fisher]] that she was pregnant and then presented him with the baby when he returned from visiting family, saying "It's a boy!" Fisher was thrilled.

## Life at Rendlesham

John spent his wartime childhood in difficult circumstances — first in Borley, then at various addresses as the family moved repeatedly. He got on well with the bedridden [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]], spending time reading to him and feeding him from an invalid cup. He suffered from Marianne's fierce temper and was not told she was not his mother. He was not allowed to call her "Mother" or "Mummy" — only "Morny."

John was trained for the priesthood (funded partly by Marianne) but did not complete his training. He went on to marry and have a family.

## The Abandonment and Adoption

In spring 1946, Marianne left John with [[letty-knights|Letty Knights]] "for a couple of days," then vanished. Letty legally adopted him; he took her surname and became **John Knights**.

## Significance

John Knights was the critical intermediary who enabled [[wallace-tower|Wallace Tower]] to find his long-lost sister [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide]] in the 1970s: when Wallace visited Woodbridge looking for Adelaide, John was able to provide her last known English address, leading eventually to the 1974 Tower family reunion. John also later confirmed to Kevin Taylor (Astrid Fisher's son) some of the details of life with Marianne at Rendlesham — including Marianne's cruelty to the children and the "strange soldiers" who "slept with mother."

## The BBC Documentary

John appeared in the BBC2 documentary *Haunted* (1994), movingly describing his separation from [[astrid-fisher|Astrid Fisher]] (Marianne's other adopted daughter) and his guilt at having written her a letter ending their contact. The reunion of John and Astrid in the documentary was its emotional climax.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**John Knights (formerly John Fisher)** is a pivotal, tragic figure in the later biographical history of Borley Rectory’s most controversial resident, Marianne Foyster. His life story reveals the dark domestic reality behind the "haunted" Rectory and the complex web of aliases and bigamy surrounding Marianne's later years. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following specific biographical details, events, quotes, and moments of emotional closure regarding his life:

### **Birth and First Adoption**
*   **Original Identity:** He was born **Timothy Eves** in Birmingham on July 14, 1935, to an unmarried shorthand typist [1]. 
*   **The Fisher Subterfuge:** In August 1935, Marianne Foyster adopted the newborn boy from the Church of England Adoption Society [2]. At the time, she was living a double life in Ipswich, engaged in a bigamous marriage with a mentally unstable commercial traveller named Henry Francis Fisher [2, 3]. 
*   **A Faked Pregnancy:** Marianne passed the adopted baby off as Henry Fisher's biological son, sending Fisher a telegram that read, **"It's a boy!"** [1, 4]. 
*   **New Identity:** The child was renamed **John Henry Francis Fisher** (though Marianne disliked the name and called him "Johnnie") [5]. He was baptised at St Pancras' Roman Catholic Church in Ipswich on September 22, 1935 [5]. 

### **Childhood and Abuse at Rendlesham**
John spent his early childhood during the war years at Dairy Cottage in Rendlesham alongside his adopted sister Astrid (born Mary Lex) and an older adopted girl, Adelaide [6, 7]. His childhood was marked by neglect, abuse, and strict Catholic instruction [8, 9].
*   **"Morny":** Marianne forbade the children from calling her "Mother" or "Mummy"; she insisted on being addressed exclusively as **"Morny"** [7, 10]. 
*   **Abuse and Threats:** Marianne treated the children with sadistic cruelty, frequently beating them and punishing misbehaviour by holding their heads in buckets of cold water for dangerously long periods [10, 11]. John recalled her threatening him by saying, **"If you don't behave yourself, I'll send you to Johnny Fisher"** [7]. 
*   **Refuge with "Fafa":** John found his only refuge in the room of the elderly, crippled Rev. Lionel Foyster, whom the children called **"Fafa"** [7, 10]. John enjoyed the bedridden clergyman's company, spending time reading to him, writing his letters, and feeding him from an invalid cup [10, 12]. 
*   **Marianne's Affairs:** John later revealed to his adoptive mother that "Morny" had "strange soldiers" billeted on her every night during the war, and that due to a lack of beds, the children **"always slept with mother"** [10]. 

### **Abandonment and Second Adoption**
*   **The Cinema Trip:** Sometime between Easter and Whitsun in 1946, when John was ten years old, Marianne took him and a friend to the cinema [13]. Afterwards, she introduced him to a local woman named Letty Knights [13, 14]. 
*   **Abandonment:** Marianne told John he would be spending the Whitsun half-term with Mrs. Knights [13]. She then casually said goodbye and vanished, fleeing to America to join her new American G.I. husband, Robert O'Neil [13-15].
*   **A New Life:** John was left with absolutely nothing but the clothes he stood up in [13, 14]. When Letty Knights realized Marianne had abandoned the boy and fled the country, she legally adopted him, changing his surname to **Knights** [14, 15]. 

### **The Priesthood and Severing Ties with Astrid**
John was brought up strictly in the Roman Catholic faith and subsequently began training for the priesthood (which, according to one source, was actually funded by Marianne) [9, 16]. 
*   However, John suffered a great emotional crisis when he discovered that he and his sister Astrid were both adopted and not blood relations [9, 16]. 
*   He was advised by his seminary superiors that it would be inappropriate for him to maintain a relationship with a young woman who was not his "real sister" [16]. Anguished, John wrote Astrid a letter stating they could no longer communicate [16]. 
*   John ultimately did not finish his seminary training; he left, married, and raised a family of his own [16]. 

### **Reunion and Forgiveness**
John's later years were marked by profound emotional closure regarding both his sister and his abusive adoptive mother.
*   **The 1994 BBC Reunion:** John Knights was featured in a 1994 BBC2 documentary series called *Picture This* [17, 18]. Astrid's son, Kevin Taylor, had been searching for his mother (who had emigrated to Australia) and placed an ad in the *East Anglian Daily Times* [9, 18]. John Knights answered the ad [9]. Astrid was flown to the UK, and she and John were tearfully reunited in his kitchen [16, 19]. John was finally able to hug her and apologize for the letter he had written decades earlier [16, 19]. 
*   **Forgiving Marianne:** In 1992, John received a letter from America informing him that Marianne was gravely ill and in a coma in a Utah hospital [19]. Despite the severe abuse and abandonment he had suffered, John sent a short message to be read to her: **"To Morny. From all over here, love and forgiveness"** [19]. 
*   **The "Morny" Address:** The specific use of the "Morny" address was essential for Marianne's emotional closure. John noted that when a nun at the hospital read the message to her, a distinct look of relief and happiness came over Marianne's face [19]. Because no one else in the world knew her by the name "Morny", Marianne recognized instantly that the message of forgiveness was from the son she had abused and abandoned [19]. John reflected, **"I just feel she felt that, 'John has forgiven me for what I've done to him - I can die at peace'"** [19].
