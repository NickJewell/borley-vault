# Grant Vallender

A young psychical researcher who visited Borley on Saturday 8 June 1968, taking photographs and talking to local residents.

## Visit

Vallender called at Foxearth Rectory where he met Canon [[canon-leslie-pennell|J. H. L. Pennell]] and talked for about half an hour. He then walked to Borley via the cart track to Brook Hall, which he found "astonishingly like the vanished Borley Rectory." He spent the afternoon photographing the site and spoke with [[mr-and-mrs-arbon|Mr Arbon]], who had been caretaker during [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s tenancy, and with [[payne-family|Mrs E. E. Payne]] of Borley Place.

At 6 pm, sitting in the churchyard by the wall of the tithe barn, he witnessed what he described as "a typical poltergeist phenomenon":

> "He was standing in the churchyard by the wall of the tithe-barn; when he looked up, he noticed a large pebble travelling over the roof. It hit the roof and fell into the churchyard. He immediately rushed round to the other side of the tithe barn but found no one there."

The angle of the pebble's trajectory indicated that the throwing force must have been very close to the far side of the barn — yet there was no one there when he investigated. Vallender spent the rest of the night in the churchyard but noted nothing further of interest.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Grant Vallender** was a young psychical researcher who conducted a focused, overnight investigation of the Borley site over the weekend of **June 8–9, 1968** [1, 2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, quotes, and details regarding his visit:

**Arrival and First Impressions**
Vallender began his trip by visiting the Rectory at Foxearth, where he spoke with the Reverend Canon J. H. L. Pennell for about half an hour [2]. From there, he walked to Borley via a cart track leading to Brook Hall, a building he noted looked **"quite astonishingly like the vanished Borley Rectory"** [2]. Upon arriving at Borley itself, he was immediately struck by its **"serenity and quiet,"** and he spent his afternoon taking photographs and exploring the area [2].

**Interviews with Historic Witnesses**
During the afternoon of June 8, Vallender considered himself lucky to track down and interview two significant local residents who had firsthand knowledge of the Rectory's history:
*   **Lionel Arbon:** Vallender spoke with Mr. Arbon, who had lived in the Rectory Cottage and acted as the site's caretaker during Harry Price's 1937–1938 tenancy [1, 2]. Arbon told Vallender that he remembered the famous investigator well, describing Price as **"a good fellow"** [2].
*   **Mrs. E. E. Payne:** Later in the afternoon, he interviewed Mrs. Payne of Borley Place [1, 2]. She recounted her own experiences, including witnessing the strange lights in the Rectory windows during the incumbency of the Rev. Guy Eric Smith [2]. Regarding the highly controversial Foyster period, she told Vallender that she thought Marianne Foyster was a very **"funny"** woman, and felt certain that Marianne was personally responsible for the famous "wine-into-ink" trick that occurred when Harry Price visited the Foysters in 1931 [2]. Like Mr. Arbon, Mrs. Payne also affirmed to Vallender that she believed Harry Price was an honest man [2].

**The Tithe-Barn Sighting (7:00 p.m.)**
At 6:00 p.m., Vallender took up a position in Borley churchyard [3]. About an hour later, around **7:00 p.m.**, while standing by the wall of the tithe-barn, he experienced what he explicitly described as a **"typical poltergeist phenomenon"** [3]. 

Looking up, Vallender observed a large pebble travelling over the roof of the barn; it struck the roof and fell down into the churchyard [3]. He immediately rushed around to the other side of the barn to catch whoever had thrown the stone, but found absolutely no one there [3]. He noted that the angle of the pebble's trajectory indicated that the throwing force must have been located very close to the opposite side of the barn, making the lack of a culprit highly unusual [3]. 

Following this stone-throwing incident, Vallender spent the remainder of the night keeping vigil in the churchyard, but recorded that nothing else of interest happened [3].
