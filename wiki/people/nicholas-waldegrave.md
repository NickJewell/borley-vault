# Nicholas Waldegrave (c.1550–1621)

Second son of Sir [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Edward Waldegrave]] (died in the Tower 1561) and Frances Neville. Inherited the Borley property from his father, and from him all subsequent Borley Waldegraves are descended. He is the direct ancestor of the Waldegrave line that held the Borley Manor until the death of [[philip-waldegrave-last|Philip Waldegrave]] in 1720–21.

## Life and Holdings

Nicholas was born between 1550 and 1561 (his father died when Nicholas was an infant or child). He died on June 19, 1621, possessed of:
- The Manor of Borley Hall
- Ten acres (probably woodland) in Bulmer
- An unspecified share in the church and Manor of "Langhooo" (Langenhoo)

## Significance

Nicholas is the link between the famous Sir Edward Waldegrave of the tomb in [[borley-church|Borley Church]] and the seventeenth-century Borley Waldegraves implicated in the [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] narrative. His eldest son Philip Waldegrave married twice; Philip's eldest son John had only one son Philip, listed as a recusant in 1715 and last of the direct Borley Waldegrave line.

[[georgina-dawson|Mrs. Georgina Dawson]]'s 20,000-word dossier on the Waldegrave family traced this descent and established the biographical details reproduced by [[harry-price|Price]] in Chapter XII. Nicholas is the root of the local branch of the family; the "senior" Waldegrave family (lords of the Chewton estate in Somerset) was a separate branch.

See also [[waldegrave-family]] and [[sir-edward-waldegrave]].

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

Based on the provided Borley sources, **Nicholas Waldegrave** (c. 1550–1621) was the patriarch of the Borley branch of the powerful Waldegrave family. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific biographical details, property holdings, events, and textual contradictions regarding his life:

**Biographical Details and Marriage**
*   **Birth and Lineage:** He was born between 1550 and 1561, the year his prominent Catholic father, Sir Edward Waldegrave, died a prisoner in the Tower of London [1]. He was Sir Edward's second son [1, 2]. 
*   **Marriage:** In 1583, he married Catherine Browne of Wellhall, Essex [3, 4]. 
*   **Death:** He died on June 19, 1621 [1]. 

**Properties and Wills**
Nicholas was a wealthy landowner whose acquisitions established a long-standing historical link between two allegedly haunted Essex parishes.
*   Upon his father's death, Nicholas inherited the Manor of Borley Hall, ten acres of woodland in nearby Bulmer, and a share in the church and manor of Langenhoe [1, 2]. 
*   His 1583 marriage to Catherine Browne further cemented his ownership of Langenhoe, as Catherine and her sister Jane had inherited the Langenhoe estate that same year [3]. 
*   Historical records of his Last Will and Testament, dated 1603 and 1615, specifically mention the **"lease of rectory and parsonage of Borley with the mansion house"** [5]. 

**Descendants**
All subsequent Borley Waldegraves were descended from Nicholas [1, 2]. 
*   His eldest son and heir was Philip Waldegrave [1, 2]. 
*   He had at least two daughters who, adhering to the family's staunch Catholic faith, left England to become nuns in exile on the Continent. His daughter Barbara (born 1603) entered a Brussels community and took the name Sister Mary Apollonia, while her sister Jeronima joined the nuns of Ghent [4, 6, 7]. 

**Contradictions in the Sources**
The texts contain two notable contradictions regarding Nicholas's title and his relationship to Sir Edward:
*   **The "Sir" Title:** He is frequently referred to throughout the texts as **"Sir Nicholas Waldegrave of Borley"** [2, 6, 7]. However, local historian Georgina Dawson explicitly noted in her research: **"Nicholas Waldegrave, the second son, sometimes called Sir Nicholas, but I am not certain that this is correct"** [1].
*   **Son vs. Grandson:** While the vast majority of historical references in the text correctly identify Nicholas as the "second son" of Sir Edward Waldegrave [1, 2], one anomalous passage discussing his daughter Barbara contradictorily refers to him as **"Sir Edward's grandson Nicholas"** [8].

**Relevance to the Borley Legends**
Nicholas Waldegrave's life connects to the later Borley Rectory ghost stories in two specific ways:
*   **The Langenhoe Hauntings:** Nicholas's acquisition of Langenhoe in 1583 established the historical and patronal link between Borley and Langenhoe [3]. Centuries later, the two parishes were combined, and both Borley Church and Langenhoe Church became the sites of strikingly similar hauntings, including phantom choir-singing and ghostly female apparitions [3, 9].
*   **The Murder Theory:** When Dr. W. J. Phythian-Adams formulated his famous theory that the Borley ghost was a French novice named Marie Lairre who had been strangled by a member of the Waldegrave family, he concluded that the tragedy did not involve the senior Chewton branch of the family. Instead, he asserted it **"must have had to do with some unknown member of the Borley Waldegraves, the descendants of that Nicholas who died in 1621"** [10].
