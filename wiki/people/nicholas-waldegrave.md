# Nicholas Waldegrave (c.1550–1621)

Second son of Sir [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Edward Waldegrave]] (died in the Tower 1561) and Frances Neville. He was born between 1550 and 1561 — the year his prominent Catholic father died a prisoner in the Tower. Inherited the Borley property from his father, and from him all subsequent Borley Waldegraves are descended. He is the direct ancestor of the Waldegrave line that held the Borley Manor until the death of [[philip-waldegrave-last|Philip Waldegrave]] in 1720–21.

## Life, Marriage, and Holdings

In 1583, Nicholas married **Catherine Browne of Wellhall, Essex**. He died on June 19, 1621, possessed of:

- The Manor of Borley Hall
- Ten acres (probably woodland) in Bulmer
- An unspecified share in the church and Manor of "Langhooo" (Langenhoe)

Nicholas's 1583 marriage to Catherine Browne further cemented his ownership of Langenhoe, as Catherine and her sister Jane had inherited the Langenhoe estate that same year. Historical records of his Last Will and Testament, dated 1603 and 1615, specifically mention the "lease of rectory and parsonage of Borley with the mansion house."

## Descendants

All subsequent Borley Waldegraves descended from Nicholas. His eldest son and heir was Philip Waldegrave. He had at least two daughters who, adhering to the family's staunch Catholic faith, left England to become nuns in exile on the Continent: his daughter **Barbara** (born 1603) entered a Brussels community and took the name Sister Mary Apollonia, while her sister **[[jeronima-waldegrave|Jeronima]]** joined the nuns of Ghent.

## Contradictions in the Sources

> **Contradiction:** Nicholas is frequently referred to throughout the texts as "Sir Nicholas Waldegrave of Borley." However, local historian [[georgina-dawson|Georgina Dawson]] explicitly noted in her research: "Nicholas Waldegrave, the second son, sometimes called Sir Nicholas, but I am not certain that this is correct."

> **Contradiction:** While the vast majority of historical references correctly identify Nicholas as the "second son" of Sir Edward Waldegrave, one anomalous passage discussing his daughter Barbara contradictorily refers to him as "Sir Edward's grandson Nicholas."

## Relevance to the Borley Legends

Nicholas connects to the later Borley Rectory ghost stories in two specific ways:

**The Langenhoe Hauntings:** His acquisition of Langenhoe in 1583 established the historical and patronal link between Borley and Langenhoe. Centuries later, the two parishes were combined, and both [[borley-church|Borley Church]] and Langenhoe Church became the sites of strikingly similar hauntings — phantom choir-singing and ghostly female apparitions.

**The Murder Theory:** When [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] formulated his famous theory that the Borley ghost was a French novice named [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] who had been strangled by a member of the Waldegrave family, he concluded that the tragedy "must have had to do with some unknown member of the Borley Waldegraves, the descendants of that Nicholas who died in 1621."

## Significance

[[georgina-dawson|Mrs Georgina Dawson]]'s 20,000-word dossier on the Waldegrave family traced this descent and established the biographical details reproduced by [[harry-price|Price]] in Chapter XII. Nicholas is the root of the local branch of the family; the "senior" Waldegrave family (lords of the Chewton estate in Somerset) was a separate branch.
