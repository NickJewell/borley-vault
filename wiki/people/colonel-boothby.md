# Colonel Boothby

Husband of [[constance-brackenbury|Constance Brackenbury]], [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]]'s stepdaughter by his wife [[ivy-brackenbury|Ivy Brackenbury]]. Constance married Boothby at eighteen; the Colonel was described as "considerably older than herself." He subsequently died at Cheltenham. Following his death, Constance moved to South Africa, where she died in 1970.

No further biographical details about Colonel Boothby appear in *Ghosts of Borley*.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Colonel Boothby** (also recorded in one chronology as **Capt. Francis S.E. Boothy**) was the husband of Constance Brackenbury, who was the stepdaughter of the Rev. Henry "Harry" Foyster Bull [1-3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, contradictions, and biographical details regarding him:

**Marriage and the Age Contradiction**
He married Constance Brackenbury at **All Saints Church, St Johns Wood, London, on February 21, 1938** [2]. A lesser-known biographical detail noted in the sources is that **Colonel Boothby was "considerably older" than his bride** [3]. 

However, there is a glaring mathematical and chronological contradiction in the texts regarding his wife's age at the time of their union. One source explicitly claims that **Constance "married, at eighteen, a Colonel Boothby"** [3]. Yet, the timeline provided by the texts makes this impossible: Constance was the daughter of Ivy Brackenbury from a previous marriage before Ivy married Harry Bull on September 12, 1911 [2, 4]. Therefore, if Constance was born prior to 1911, she would have been at least 27 years old when she married Boothby in 1938, not eighteen. 

**Death and Posthumous Connection to Borley**
Colonel Boothby **died in Cheltenham**, predeceasing his wife [3]. 

He had no direct involvement with the Borley hauntings during his life, but following his death, his widow (referred to then as Mrs. Boothby) was tracked down and interviewed by investigator Norah Walrond at the Ellenborough Hotel in Cheltenham in 1945 [1, 3]. During this interview, his widow provided the complete, mundane explanation for the famous "skull in the cupboard" that had supposedly terrorized the subsequent Smith incumbency at Borley Rectory [1]. After his death, his widow moved to South Africa, where she resided until her death in 1970 [3].
