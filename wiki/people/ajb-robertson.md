# A.J.B. Robertson

**Dr. Andrew J. B. Robertson (d. 1987)** — MA, PhD in physical chemistry, St John's College, Cambridge [2–4]. After the war he was appointed Assistant Lecturer at King's College, London (1950), progressing to Lecturer (1951–56), Reader (1956–81), and Professor of Chemistry (1970–81), before retiring as Emeritus Professor in Sevenoaks, Kent [2, 5]. A long-standing Council Member of the [[spr|Society for Psychical Research]]. After reading *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940), he formed the Cambridge Commission of inquiry into the Borley hauntings and led it throughout its five-year life (1939–1944).

## The Cambridge Commission

Robertson convened the Commission after the fire; investigations ran from Easter 1939 to July 1944, covering 25 separate nights and involving 58 persons in total — almost all Cambridge undergraduates, nearly all deeply sceptical on arrival [6–9]. Robertson prepared a comprehensive summary report for [[harry-price|Harry Price]] from observers' individual protocols, which formed the basis of Chapter IX of Price's 1946 book *The End of Borley Rectory* [9, 13, 14].

The Commission faced significant disadvantages: the Rectory was a ruin, not occupied, with no Base Room comfort or heating; phenomena were acknowledged to be weakening over the years; visits were infrequent (months between some) and personnel changed constantly. Robertson himself attended only 8 of 25 visits [15, 16]. Despite this, roughly one-third of observers reported events they considered paranormal, one-third reported events that might be naturally explained, and the remaining third reported nothing unusual [6, 10].

## Key Findings

**Auditory phenomena**: Most frequently occurring class. Footsteps traversing corridors or rooms were distinguished from static knockings. Footsteps were usually heard when investigators were stationary in the sewing-room, separated by one wall from the sound; this regularity was noted as difficult to ascribe to random causes. Knockings often came 5–10 minutes after requests to "any entity" to manifest — this responsiveness to prompting was the most evidentially interesting feature.

**Robertson's personal visits included:**

- **Visit 1 (Easter 1939):** Noted the floor heavily strewn with rubble, making quiet movement impossible; observed nothing unusual [8].
- **Visit 2 (October 31, 1941):** Heavy footsteps on pantry stairs, both observers certain; a dark outline moving from moonlight to shadow; a luminous patch on wall for c.1 second. Robertson noted: "We do not consider that great weight should be attached to this observation" [12, 17–19].
- **Visit 3 (December 20, 1941):** Eighteen knocks in a regular double-knock pattern repeated three times — Robertson and [[enj-angelbeck|Angelbeck]] heard it simultaneously; did not resemble any mechanical or structural cause [12, 20, 21].
- **Visit 4 (February 27, 1942):** Three regular knocks from front door; rumblings as of furniture moved upstairs; three regular footsteps. Also: a luminous flash seen independently by J.P. Grantham (not told of the earlier luminous patch seen in Visit 2) [22, 23].
- **Visit 6 (September 22, 1942):** Measured, steady footsteps across a ceiling; bumps and dragging sounds; clock ticking heard primarily by [[je-lankester|J.E. Lankester]], whose mother (in Ipswich) also awoke to a clock she could not explain in her room — Robertson notes this suggests the two were *en rapport*.
- **Visit 9 (March 21, 1943):** Five observers in the garden heard the stamping of a horse in the road outside the summer-house on two occasions. None of the five knew the coach-and-horses tradition. Simultaneous perception rules out individual hallucination [27, 28].
- **Visit 14 (June 19, 1943):** Temperature readings on the Cold Spot showed an 11°F difference between an enclosed thermometer and an adjacent exposed thermometer. Robertson and colleague E.D. Low applied Grasshof's criterion and the heat-conduction equation to test whether heat removal at 0.006 cal/cm³/sec could account for the reading. The maths is consistent.
- **Visit 19 (December 3, 1943):** Four whistles sounding human; heard by all observers; probable origin near the back staircase.
- **Visit 25 (July 22–23, 1944):** A hoax was staged by four members (P.H. Lord with disguise, etc.); however, after the hoaxers retired at 3 a.m., the five legitimate investigators in the summer-house heard faint raps and established communication via a code (one knock for yes). The entity claimed to be a nun, died c.1250, glad the buildings were gone, wanted a minister but not Presbyterian, had communicated with Miss Glanville via Planchette. The rapping answered questions intelligibly for about 45 minutes; the hoaxers deny having produced it.

## Scientific Assessment

Robertson concluded that any argument for paranormal activity at Borley had to rely mainly on the **auditory phenomena** — specifically, regular footsteps and intelligent knocking — because their statistical probability of occurring by chance was "extremely small" [13, 31, 32]. His statistical analysis of the knock-pattern probabilities (triple knocks arising from independent random events would be of order 10⁻⁷) supports an intelligence-guided origin for at least some auditory phenomena. He contributed an essay, *"The Poltergeist Problem: A Physical View"*, to Price's 1945 book *Poltergeist Over England*, speculating that the famous [[mysterious-footsteps|cold spot]] at the Rectory might have acted as an energy store to fuel poltergeist activity [2, 33].

Robertson maintained orthodox scientific caution, openly acknowledging the power of suggestion: "It is, however, probably not difficult to be mistaken with such fleeting impressions, especially when observed in a supposedly haunted ruin" [31]. His full protocols, with graphs, tables, and sketches, were deposited in the Harry Price Library at the University of London.

## Robertson's Conclusion

"There appears to be something at the Rectory which cannot be at all easily explained away." He noted phenomena were "fragmentary versions" of those reported in Price's book — as if the haunting was declining, as observers and Price both remarked independently.

## Criticisms and Defence

The highly critical 1956 SPR *Borley Report* (Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) argued that the auditory phenomena were likely the natural sounds of the ruined, weather-exposed building disintegrating, and that the observers were mere undergraduates prone to levity and pranks [4, 34, 35]. Critics highlighted Visit 25, when students secretly arrived to fake an apparition of the nun.

However, Robertson's integrity was proven by his deliberate inclusion of the undergraduate hoax in his final report, and he had explicitly noted that phenomena rarely occurred while observers were actually moving around the rubble [4, 15, 30].

Robertson never wavered in his belief that genuine phenomena occurred at the site. At a packed SPR meeting in 1975, when the elderly [[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]] delivered a lecture fiercely dismissing the haunting, Robertson stood up to challenge her. Speaking with "great authority and precision," he publicly rejected her absolute scepticism, firmly maintaining his position that at least some of the phenomena reported by his students defied any normal explanation [5, 37].
