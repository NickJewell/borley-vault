# A.J.B. Robertson

Andrew F.B. Robertson, M.A., of St John's College, Cambridge. Honours degree in chemistry, specialising in physical chemistry. Interested in psychical research for many years. After reading *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940), he formed the Cambridge Commission of inquiry into the Borley hauntings and led it throughout its five-year life (1939–1944).

## The Cambridge Commission

Robertson convened the Commission after the fire; investigations ran from Easter 1939 to July 1944, covering 25 separate nights and involving 58 persons in total — almost all Cambridge undergraduates, nearly all deeply sceptical on arrival. Robertson prepared a comprehensive summary report for [[harry-price|Harry Price]] from observers' individual protocols.

The Commission faced significant disadvantages: the Rectory was a ruin, not occupied, with no Base Room comfort or heating; phenomena were acknowledged to be weakening over the years; visits were infrequent (months between some) and personnel changed constantly (Robertson himself attended only 8 of 25 visits). Despite this, 22 of 58 persons reported something they thought either curious or strange.

## Key Findings

**Auditory phenomena**: Most frequently occurring class. Footsteps traversing corridors or rooms were distinguished from static knockings. Footsteps were usually heard when investigators were stationary in the sewing-room, separated by one wall from the sound; this regularity was noted as difficult to ascribe to random causes. Knockings often came 5–10 minutes after requests to "any entity" to manifest — this responsiveness to prompting was the most evidentially interesting feature.

**Specific highlights**:
- Visit 2 (October 31, 1941): Heavy footsteps on pantry stairs, both observers certain; a dark outline moving from moonlight to shadow, not a shadow of either investigator; luminous patch on wall for c.1 second.
- Visit 3 (December 20, 1941): Eighteen knocks in a regular double-knock pattern repeated three times — Robertson and Angelbeck heard it simultaneously; did not resemble any mechanical or structural cause.
- Visit 4 (February 27, 1942): Three regular knocks from front door; rumblings as of furniture moved upstairs; three regular footsteps. Also: a luminous flash seen independently by J.P. Grantham (not told of the earlier luminous patch seen in Visit 2).
- Visit 6 (September 22, 1942): Measured, steady footsteps across a ceiling; bumps and dragging sounds; clock ticking heard primarily by J.E. Lankester, whose mother (in Ipswich) also awoke to a clock she could not explain in her room — Robertson notes this suggests the two were *en rapport*.
- Visit 9 (March 21, 1943): Five observers in the garden heard the stamping of a horse in the road outside the summer-house on two occasions. None of the five knew the coach-and-horses tradition. Simultaneous perception rules out individual hallucination.
- Visit 14 (June 19, 1943): Temperature readings on the Cold Spot showed an 11°F difference between an enclosed thermometer and an adjacent exposed thermometer — the Cold Spot apparently 11°F colder than surrounding air. Robertson and colleague E.D. Low applied Grasshof's criterion and the heat-conduction equation to test whether heat removal at 0.006 cal/cm³/sec could account for the reading. The maths is consistent.
- Visit 19 (December 3, 1943): Four whistles (Visits 19), sounding human; heard by all observers; probable origin near the back staircase.
- Visit 25 (July 22–23, 1944): A hoax was staged by four members (P.H. Lord with disguise, etc.); however, after the hoaxers retired at 3 a.m., the five legitimate investigators in the summer-house heard faint raps and established communication via a code (one knock for yes). The entity claimed to be a nun, died c.1250, glad the buildings were gone, wanted a minister but not Presbyterian, had communicated with Miss Glanville via Planchette. The rapping answered questions intelligibly for about 45 minutes; the hoaxers deny having produced it.

## Robertson's Conclusion

"There appears to be something at the Rectory which cannot be at all easily explained away." He notes phenomena were "fragmentary versions" of those reported in Price's book — as if the haunting was declining, as observers and Price both remarked independently. His statistical analysis of the knock-pattern probabilities (triple knocks arising from independent random events would be of order 10⁻⁷) supports an intelligence-guided origin for at least some auditory phenomena. His full protocols, with graphs, tables, and sketches, were deposited in the Harry Price Library at the University of London.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Dr. Andrew J. B. Robertson (d. 1987)** was a distinguished physical chemist, an academic, and a long-standing Council Member of the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) who played a major role in investigating Borley Rectory after it was destroyed by fire in 1939 [1, 2]. 

**Academic and Professional Background**
Educated at St John’s College, Cambridge, where he earned his MA and PhD in physical chemistry, Robertson became interested in poltergeist phenomena as a young scientist [2-4]. His career was highly successful: he was appointed Assistant Lecturer in the Department of Chemistry at King's College, London in May 1950, progressing to Lecturer (1951–56), Reader (1956–81), and finally Professor of Chemistry (1970–81), before retiring as Emeritus Professor and living his final years in Sevenoaks, Kent [2, 5]. 

**The Cambridge Commission**
Between the Easter vacation of 1939 and the final demolition of the Rectory ruins in 1944, Robertson organized and led a protracted investigation that became known as the **"Cambridge Commission"** [6-8]. He recruited a rota of 58 observers, mostly Cambridge undergraduates, who made 25 separate overnight visits to the ruins [6, 9]. The investigators approached the case with considerable skepticism and utilized scientific equipment, including protected thermometers, to monitor the environment [10-12].

Robertson’s meticulous record-keeping culminated in his authoring Chapter IX of Harry Price's 1946 book, *The End of Borley Rectory*, which summarized the Commission's findings [9, 13, 14]. According to his analysis, roughly one-third of his observers reported nothing unusual, one-third reported events that might be naturally explained, and the remaining third reported phenomena they considered paranormal [6, 10]. 

**Robertson's Personal Visits and Observations**
Due to his academic commitments, Robertson was only personally present for eight of the twenty-five visits [15, 16]. His detailed reports from these specific visits include:
*   **Visit 1 (Easter 1939):** Accompanied by two friends shortly after the fire, he noted that the floor was heavily strewn with rubble, making quiet movement impossible, but observed nothing unusual [8].
*   **Visit 2 (31 Oct - 1 Nov 1941):** At 12:45 a.m., Robertson personally witnessed a luminous patch about a foot square appear on a wall near the sewing room. He noted it lasted a second and could not have been moonlight, though he added: "We do not consider that great weight should be attached to this observation." Footsteps were also heard [12, 17-19].
*   **Visit 3 (20 Dec 1941):** Robertson and E.N.J. Angelbeck heard a series of eighteen regular knocks—described as sounding like a small hand-pump—after directly calling out to the "entities" and Harry Bull [12, 20, 21]. *(Note: Some source passages confusingly date this visit to 12 Nov 1941 [22], but the detailed Commission log confirms it as 20 Dec [12, 21]).*
*   **Visit 4 (27-28 Feb 1942):** The observers produced regular knocks on a door, asked the "entities" to reply with three knocks, and shortly after heard three heavy, regular knocks from the front door [22, 23]. 
*   **Visit 5 (28-29 July 1942):** A vigil held on the traditional anniversary of the phantom nun's appearance yielded no phenomena [24, 25].
*   **Visit 7 (22-23 Dec 1942):** Uneventful [26, 27].
*   **Visit 9 (21-22 March 1943):** Robertson and his party heard the distinct sound of a horse stamping its hooves outside the summer-house, despite no animals being present. Later, Robertson and another observer heard shuffling footsteps in the kitchen passage [27, 28].
*   **Visit 24 (9 June 1944):** Uneventful [29, 30].

**Scientific Assessment and Published Theories**
Robertson concluded that any argument for paranormal activity at Borley had to rely mainly on the **auditory phenomena**—specifically, regular footsteps and intelligent knocking—because their statistical probability of occurring by sheer chance (especially when answering questions) was "extremely small" [13, 31, 32]. He also contributed an essay titled *"The Poltergeist Problem: A Physical View"* to Price's 1945 book *Poltergeist Over England*, where he speculated that the famous "cold spot" at the Rectory might have acted as an energy store to fuel poltergeist activity [2, 33].

Despite these findings, Robertson maintained an orthodox scientific caution. He openly acknowledged the power of suggestion, noting: **"It is, however, probably not difficult to be mistaken with such fleeting impressions, especially when observed in a supposedly haunted ruin"** [31]. 

**Criticisms and Contradictions**
The highly critical 1956 SPR *Borley Report* authored by Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall sought to diminish the Cambridge Commission's findings. The critics argued that:
*   The auditory phenomena were likely just the natural sounds of the ruined, weather-exposed building disintegrating and rubble falling [4, 34, 35]. 
*   The observers were mere undergraduates prone to levity and pranks. The critics highlighted Visit 25 (22-23 July 1944), when a group of students secretly arrived to fake an apparition of the nun to scare the official observers [15, 30]. 

However, supporters of Borley point out that Robertson's integrity was proven by his deliberate inclusion of the undergraduate hoax in his final report, and that he had explicitly noted that phenomena rarely occurred while observers were actually moving around the rubble [4, 15, 30].

**Final Stance on Borley**
Dr. Robertson never wavered in his belief that genuine phenomena occurred at the site. He told investigator Peter Underwood on several occasions that **"there was something at the Rectory which cannot at all easily be explained away"** [36]. 

This conviction was put on public display at a packed SPR meeting in 1975. When the elderly Mollie Goldney delivered a lecture fiercely dismissing the haunting and attacking Harry Price's integrity, Robertson—then living in retirement—stood up to challenge her. Speaking with "great authority and precision," he publicly rejected her absolute skepticism, firmly maintaining his position that at least some of the phenomena reported by his students defied any normal explanation [5, 37].
