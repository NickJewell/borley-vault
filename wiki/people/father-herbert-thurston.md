# Father Herbert Thurston, S.J. (1857–1939)

Jesuit priest and respected authority on spiritualism and the paranormal, consulted by [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] before his first visits to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in 1931. Though highly esteemed as a Catholic intellectual authority, some orthodox Catholics feared that his attitude toward the paranormal was **"too sympathetic to be compatible with his calling"** — the texts go so far as to describe him as a "spiritualist" [3]. He had published extensively on spiritualism and paranormal phenomena from a Catholic perspective.

## Influence on Edwin Whitehouse (May 1931)

In May 1931, before Edwin Whitehouse travelled to Suffolk to investigate the Borley hauntings, he consulted Father Thurston in London [1, 3]. Thurston was impressed by the phenomena described in a letter written by [[lady-whitehouse|Lady Whitehouse]] [3]. He advised Edwin:

1. To **say prayers** at the house
2. To **bless it with holy water**
3. But to be warned that "in almost all the recorded cases of poltergeist phenomena, this had had little effect" [3]

This measured but engaged response — acknowledging the phenomena as real while tempering expectations of exorcism — is consistent with Thurston's published view that poltergeist phenomena are genuine but resist purely spiritual remedies.

Following this consultation, Whitehouse proceeded to make some thirty visits to Borley Rectory between June and December 1931 during the height of the [[foyster-phenomena|Foyster disturbances]] [1].

## Debunking the "Walled-Up Nun" Legend

Thurston's historical writings were utilised by researchers to dismantle the core Borley legend of the bricked-up nun. He had carefully analysed the historical evidence for the immurement of religious figures who broke their vows of chastity and concluded that such punishments never actually happened [4]. In the June 1892 issue of the Catholic journal *The Month*, he summarised alleged cases and concluded: **"Perhaps such charges are to be found, but I can only say that I have not yet heard of a case, nor met any controversialist who pretended to quote one"** [4]. Returning to the subject in January 1894, Thurston attacked the author Rider Haggard for printing a sensational scene in his novel *Montezuma's Daughter* where a nun is immured alive in a coffin-shaped niche [4].

## Posthumous Publication (1953)

Fourteen years after his death, Thurston's book *Ghosts and Poltergeists* (London, 1953) was published [2]. The book included full details of a 1929 Indian poltergeist case involving the Pillay household, which researchers noted shared distinct similarities with the Borley haunting [2]. Thurston had gone to considerable trouble to confirm the facts of this Indian case, personally consulting a Salesian missionary who had visited the troubled house [2].

## Significance

Thurston's consultation was one of several clerical interventions sought by the Foysters and their associates. The pattern of Anglo-Catholic and Roman Catholic clergy being drawn to Borley — Edwin, [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard]] in his later monk persona, [[arthur-sellwood|Arthur Sellwood]], [[bernard-smith-rector|Bernard Smith]], and eventually [[father-john-wright|Father Wright]] who said the Requiem Mass for Marie Lairre — reflects the Catholic character of the haunting as Price and others perceived it.
