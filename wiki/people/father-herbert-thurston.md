# Father Herbert Thurston, S.J.

Jesuit priest and respected authority on spiritualism and the paranormal, consulted by [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] before his first visits to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in 1931.

## Background

Thurston was a Jesuit priest who was highly esteemed in Catholic intellectual circles but whose attitude to the paranormal was considered by some orthodox Catholics to be too sympathetic to be compatible with his calling. He had published extensively on spiritualism and paranormal phenomena from a Catholic perspective.

## Role at Borley

When [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] received [[lady-whitehouse|Lady Whitehouse]]'s letter describing the extraordinary events at Borley and decided to visit the Rectory, he first consulted Father Thurston in London. Thurston was impressed by the phenomena described in the letter and advised Edwin:

1. To **say prayers** at the house
2. To **bless it with holy water**
3. But to be warned that "in almost all the recorded cases of poltergeist phenomena, this had had little effect"

This measured but engaged response — acknowledging the phenomena as real while tempering expectations of exorcism — is consistent with Thurston's published view that poltergeist phenomena are genuine but resist purely spiritual remedies.

## Significance

Thurston's consultation was one of several clerical interventions sought by the Foysters and their associates. The pattern of Anglo-Catholic and Roman Catholic clergy being drawn to Borley — Edwin, [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard]] in his later monk persona, [[arthur-sellwood|Arthur Sellwood]], [[bernard-smith-rector|Bernard Smith]], and eventually Father Wright who said the Requiem Mass for Marie Lairre — reflects the Catholic character of the haunting as Price and others perceived it.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Father Herbert J. Thurston, S.J.** (1857–1939) was a well-known Jesuit priest and Catholic authority who wrote extensively on the subjects of poltergeists and the supernatural [1, 2]. A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, quotes, and details regarding his indirect but notable connections to the Borley case:

**Lesser-Known Biographical Details**
While highly esteemed as an authority, some orthodox Catholics feared that Thurston's attitude toward the paranormal was **"too sympathetic to be compatible with his calling,"** with the texts going so far as to describe the Jesuit priest as a "spiritualist" [3]. 

**Influence on Edwin Whitehouse (May 1931)**
In May 1931, before Edwin Whitehouse traveled to Suffolk to investigate the Borley hauntings, he consulted Father Thurston in London [1, 3]. Thurston was impressed by the phenomena described in a letter written by Lady Whitehouse [3]. **He advised Edwin to say prayers at the Rectory and bless it with holy water, although he warned the young man that "in almost all the recorded cases of poltergeist phenomena, this had had little effect"** [3]. Following this consultation with Thurston, Whitehouse proceeded to make some thirty visits to Borley Rectory between June and December 1931 during the height of the Foyster disturbances [1].

**Debunking the "Walled-Up Nun" Legend**
Thurston’s historical writings were utilized by researchers to actively dismantle the core Borley legend of the bricked-up nun. He had carefully analyzed the historical evidence for the immurement of religious figures who broke their vows of chastity (such as the remains found at Coldingham Abbey) and concluded that such punishments never actually happened [4]. In the June 1892 issue of the Catholic journal *The Month*, he summarized alleged cases and concluded: **"Perhaps such charges are to be found, but I can only say that I have not yet heard of a case, nor met any controversialist who pretended to quote one"** [4]. Returning to the subject in January 1894, Thurston attacked the author Rider Haggard for printing a sensational scene in his novel *Montezuma's Daughter* where a nun is immured alive in a coffin-shaped niche [4].

**Posthumous Publication (1953)**
Fourteen years after his death, Thurston's book *Ghosts and Poltergeists* (London, 1953) was published [2]. The book included full details of a 1929 Indian poltergeist case involving the Pillay household, which researchers noted shared distinct similarities with the Borley haunting [2]. Thurston had gone to considerable trouble to confirm the facts of this Indian case, personally consulting a Salesian missionary who had visited the troubled house [2].
