# Samuel Seal

Army sergeant (also recorded in some sources as **Samuel Seals**) originally from **Bures**, approximately seven miles from Borley [1, 2]. During the Second World War he served at a searchlight battery at **Belchamp Walter**, near Borley. His nighttime encounter near the ruins of Borley Rectory in 1942 provides one of the case's most dramatic accounts of the legendary [[phantom-coach|phantom coach]] phenomenon.

## Testimony (1942)

Seal's experience occurred late one dark night in 1942 while he was returning to his camp on his bicycle, having been on leave visiting his wife's home [2, 3].

While en route, a policeman stopped him to check his lamp and warned him: **"I shouldn't go that way if I were you. I should go through Foxearth"** [3]. Unaware of Borley's haunted reputation, Seal ignored the advice [1, 3].

Halfway up the hill toward the [[borley-rectory|Rectory site]] and [[borley-church|Borley Church]], he saw **"two lights coming round the corner... coming at a devil of a speed"** [1, 3]. Hearing no engine noise, he initially assumed it was someone coasting downhill in a motor car [1, 3]. When the lights drew to within about a hundred yards, they suddenly **"switched across the road"** [1, 2]. Behind the lights, Seal caught a glimpse of a distinct "dark shape" and concluded that a vehicle or carriage had swept straight into the Rectory's driveway [1–3].

Reaching the driveway entrance seconds later, Seal found the gates completely closed, with the tall grass by the gate standing undisturbed [2, 3]. There was no sign of any vehicle in the forecourt of the ruins [1, 2]. While investigating the hedgerow near the ruined tower, he was startled by a terrifying **"yelping scream"** [3]. Seal later stated the scream frightened him so badly he dared swear his hair **"must have stood on end and it must have left my head and left it a blooming yard"** [3]. He immediately got back on his bicycle and fled.

Upon arrival at camp, a fellow gunner named Bill Williams noticed his distress and asked, **"What's the matter with you, Serg. Have you seen a ghost?"** Seal replied: **"Talk about seeing one, I've heard one too"** [3].

Seal was adamant that at the time of the incident he had no prior knowledge of Borley Rectory or its haunted reputation; he only learned of the legends when he related his experience to his fellow soldiers [1–3].

## Interviews and Recordings

Because of his spectacular account, Seal became a sought-after witness for Borley researchers:
- He relayed his experience to Harry Price and his colleagues in **1945** [2].
- He was personally interviewed by Peter Underwood and Tom Brown during their visit to Borley on **May 17–18, 1947** [4, 5].
- On **Tuesday, June 17, 1947**, Seal participated in a BBC radio recording session at Borley alongside other notable witnesses, including Mr. and Mrs. Edward Cooper and Miss Ethel Bull [6]. His account was subsequently featured in the BBC broadcast *The Haunted Rectory* later that month [3].

## Textual Contradictions

> **Contradiction:** The sources are inconsistent with his surname, referring to him interchangeably as "Samuel Seal" [2, 6] and "Samuel Seals" [1, 4]. Additionally, in a transcript of the BBC radio documentary recordings, the speaker recounting his story is bizarrely labelled "MRS. SEAL" — immediately betrayed as a typographical error by the speaker's first-person account of being a male Sergeant returning from his "wife's home" and being addressed by a fellow soldier as "Serg" [3].

## Significance

This is an independently witnessed light phenomenon at the [[borley-rectory|Rectory site]] in 1942, three years after the [[fire-1939|fire]], from a witness with no prior knowledge of the Borley haunting. The lights behaved as the [[phantom-coach|phantom coach lights]] are described — appearing from the direction of the church, crossing the road, entering the former Rectory grounds — and the witness was shocked enough to flee.
