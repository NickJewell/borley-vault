# Samuel Seal

Sergeant with the searchlight battery stationed at Belchamp Water during World War II. Witnessed a phantom light phenomenon near the Borley Rectory site in 1942, recorded in the 1947 BBC broadcast.

## Testimony (1942)

Returning from leave after midnight, Seal was cycling toward Borley. A policeman stopped him to check his lamp and advised him not to go through Borley: "I shouldn't go that way if I were you. I should go through Foxearth." Seal ignored the advice.

Halfway up the hill toward the Rectory site, he saw "two lights coming round the corner... coming at a devil of a speed" — he assumed it was a car coasting downhill. As the lights drew to within about 100 yards, "they switched across the road... as though they just shot across the road and there was a dark shape following them." He assumed they had turned into the Rectory garden gate — but on investigation, "there was no gate that had been opened as the grass was still up by the gate." He investigated the hedgeside and was just against the burnt-out Rectory tower when "there was a yelping scream" — he fled by bicycle.

His report back at the battery: "Talk about seeing one... I've heard one too." He had never heard anything about the Rectory haunting.

## Significance

This is an independently-witnessed light phenomenon at the [[borley-rectory|Rectory site]] in 1942, three years after the fire, from a witness with no prior knowledge of the Borley haunting. The lights behaved as the phantom coach lights are described — appearing from the direction of the church, crossing the road, entering the former Rectory grounds — and the witness was shocked enough to flee.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Samuel Seal** (frequently recorded in the sources as **Samuel Seals**) was an army sergeant whose nighttime encounter near the ruins of Borley Rectory during the Second World War provides one of the case's most dramatic accounts of the legendary "phantom coach" phenomenon. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, facts, dates, quotes, and textual contradictions regarding his involvement:

**Biographical Details and Military Service**
*   Samuel Seal originally hailed from **Bures**, a village about seven miles from Borley [1, 2].
*   During the Second World War (specifically in **1942**), he served as a Sergeant stationed at a searchlight battery at **Belchamp Walter**, located near Borley [1-3].

**The 1942 "Phantom Coach" Encounter**
Seal's famous experience occurred late one dark night in 1942 while he was returning to his camp at Belchamp Walter on his bicycle, having been on leave visiting his wife's home [2, 3]. 
*   **The Policeman's Warning:** While en route, Seal was stopped by a policeman who checked his lamp. When Seal mentioned he was heading back to camp via Borley, the policeman warned him: **"I shouldn't go that way if I were you. I should go through Foxearth"** [3]. Unaware of Borley's reputation, Seal ignored the advice [1, 3]. 
*   **The Approaching Lights:** Halfway up the hill leading towards Borley Church and the burnt-out Rectory, Seal saw two lights coming around the corner towards him **"at a devil of a speed"** [1, 3]. Because he heard absolutely no engine noise, he initially assumed it was someone coasting down the hill in a motor car [1, 3]. 
*   **The Disappearance:** When the lights were about a hundred yards away, they suddenly **"switched across the road"** [1, 2]. Behind the lights, Seal caught a glimpse of a distinct "dark shape" and concluded that a vehicle or carriage had swept straight into the Rectory's driveway [1-3]. 
*   **The Closed Gates and the Scream:** A few seconds later, Seal reached the driveway entrance but found the gates completely closed, with the tall grass by the gate standing undisturbed [2, 3]. There was no sign of any vehicle in the forecourt of the ruins [1, 2]. While investigating the hedgerow near the ruined tower of the Rectory, Seal was startled by a terrifying **"yelping scream"** [3]. Seal later stated that the scream frightened him so badly he dared swear his hair **"must have stood on end and it must have left my head and left it a blooming yard"** [3]. 

**Aftermath and Prior Knowledge**
*   Terrified, Seal immediately got back on his bicycle and fled to his camp [3]. Upon arrival, a fellow gunner named Bill Williams noticed his distress and asked, **"What's the matter with you, Serg. Have you seen a ghost?"** Seal replied, **"Talk about seeing one, I've heard one too"** [3]. 
*   Seal was absolutely adamant that at the time of the incident, he had no prior knowledge of Borley Rectory or its haunted reputation; he only learned of the legends when he related his experience to his fellow soldiers [1-3]. 

**Interviews and Recordings**
Because of his spectacular account, Seal became a sought-after witness for Borley researchers:
*   He relayed his experience to Harry Price and his colleagues in **1945** [2].
*   He was personally interviewed by investigator Peter Underwood and Tom Brown during their visit to Borley on **May 17–18, 1947** [4, 5]. 
*   On **Tuesday, June 17, 1947**, Seal participated in a BBC radio recording session at Borley alongside other notable witnesses, such as Mr. and Mrs. Edward Cooper and Miss Ethel Bull [6]. His account was subsequently featured in the BBC broadcast *The Haunted Rectory* later that month [3]. 

**Textual Contradictions and Anomalies**
*   **Name Variations:** The sources are inconsistent with his surname, referring to him interchangeably as **"Samuel Seal"** [2, 6] and **"Samuel Seals"** [1, 4].
*   **The BBC Transcript Error:** In a transcript of the 1942 BBC radio documentary recordings provided in the texts, the speaker recounting Samuel Seal's story is bizarrely labeled as **"MRS. SEAL"** [3]. However, the text immediately betrays this as a typographical or transcript error, as the speaker gives a first-person account of being a male Sergeant returning from his "wife's home" and being addressed by a fellow soldier as "Serg" [3].
