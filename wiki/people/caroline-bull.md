# Caroline Bull (née Foyster)

Wife of [[henry-bull|Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]]; mother of his thirteen children; mistress of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] for its first three decades. Her family background connects the Foyster and Bull clerical dynasties.

## Family Background

Caroline was the daughter of **Henry Samuel Foyster**, rector of All Saints Church, Hastings — see [[henry-samuel-foyster]]. Her father and [[rev-edward-bull-pentlow|Rev. Edward Bull of Pentlow]] were close friends, with their families frequently visiting each other. Caroline's brother **George Alfred Foyster** (see [[rev-george-alfred-foyster]]) was a contemporary of [[henry-bull|Henry Dawson Bull]], and it was during a stay with the Foysters in Hastings that Henry became acquainted with Caroline.

This family friendship meant that when Henry Bull married Caroline in 1862, he was also making [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] — George's son — a distant cousin, setting up the family connection by which Lionel would eventually succeed him as rector of Borley thirty years after his death.

## Marriage and Family at Borley

Caroline and Henry married on 11 February 1862 and moved to Borley on 20 February, after a honeymoon in Tunbridge Wells. She had brought a considerable dowry to the marriage, including property in central London, which helped fund the construction of the new [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

For sixteen years Caroline was almost continually pregnant. By 1879 she had borne thirteen children: Harry (born March 1863 at Pentlow, the future rector), Dodie ([[dodie-bull|Caroline Sarah]], b. 1864), Freda ([[freda-bull|Freda]]), Alfred, Basil, [[ethel-bull|Ethel]], Mabel (a daughter, distinct from [[mabel-smith|Mabel Smith]]), [[gerald-bull|Gerald]], Constance, Hubert, Emily, Elsie, and Kathleen. One child, Cyril, died in infancy in 1877. She was supported by a succession of nannies, nursemaids, and a Swiss governess, **Marie Rolt** ([[maria-rolf]]).

## Death and Legacy

Caroline Bull died on 13 April 1914 at the age of seventy-seven. The funeral five days later was attended by ten of her children. After her death, Harry's sisters took over her parish duties and ran the house — until Harry's marriage to [[ivy-brackenbury|Ivy Brackenbury]] in 1911 had already displaced them from the Rectory.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Caroline Sarah Bull (née Foyster)** was the wife of the Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull (the clergyman who built Borley Rectory in 1863) and the matriarch of the expansive Bull family, bearing him fourteen children [1, 2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, and lesser-known biographical details regarding her life:

**Early Life, Marriage, and the "Simple-Minded" Rumor**
Caroline came from the genteel Foyster family of Hastings, an ecclesiastical dynasty, and was already related to Henry Dawson Ellis Bull prior to their marriage [2, 3]. The 1871 census recorded her birthplace as Harrow Weald, Sussex, though modern researchers note this was an error, as Harrow Weald is located on the north-west outskirts of London [3]. 

A lesser-known and rather delicate biographical detail recorded in the sources is that Caroline was reliably considered by some to be "slightly simple-minded" [3]. Prior to her marriage, she was employed for a time as a resident helper at Gestingthorpe Rectory, which is where she gave birth to her first child, Henry Foyster "Harry" Bull, in March 1863 [3]. She and Henry Dawson Ellis Bull were married on February 11, 1862 [4]. 

**Life at Borley Rectory**
For decades, Caroline managed the massive Rectory household and was actively involved in parish duties. While her husband instructed the village boys in Sunday School, Caroline took charge of teaching the girls, a duty that was later passed down to her eldest daughter, Freda [5]. 

In her later years, local resident Alfred Finch remembered her as a frail, very small, and highly aged woman [6]. A fond local memory involved her participating in an Easter tradition: she would sit in a chair by the green baize door in the Rectory hall—requiring physical assistance to get in and out of her seat due to her frailty—and dispense hot cross buns from a basket to the village children [6]. 

**Sighting the Phantom Nun**
Caroline Bull is recorded as one of the firsthand witnesses to Borley's most famous ghost. According to the 86-year-old brother of the Rev. Lionel Foyster, "old Mrs. Bull" mentioned at breakfast one morning that she had seen the apparition of the ghost nun in the garden the previous evening [7, 8]. Her husband replied that he was not at all surprised, noting that the previous day had been **July 28th**—the date the nun was traditionally reputed to appear [8, 9]. 

**The 1887 Will Codicil**
A curious legal contradiction or mystery surrounds Caroline's inheritance. The Rev. Henry Bull’s original will, dated March 22, 1877, bequeathed the contents of the Rectory and £500 to Caroline, alongside the right to continue living in the house [10]. However, a codicil added on June 5, 1887, revoked her outright ownership of the home's contents, demoting her inheritance to merely the *use* of the contents for the remainder of her life [10]. Borley researchers Edward Babbs and Claudine Mathias theorize that this codicil operated to her distinct disadvantage and may have been the result of an undisclosed family disagreement or the lingering belief that she was simple-minded [11]. 

**Death and Funeral**
Caroline outlived her husband by twenty-two years, dying on **April 13, 1914**, at the age of 77 [12, 13]. Unlike her husband and her son Harry (who both died in the Rectory's infamous Blue Room), Caroline passed away at Borley Place, the house across the road where she had been living with Harry and his new wife, Ivy [14, 15]. 

Her funeral took place on April 18, 1914 [13]. Her coffin was carried from the Rectory and followed in procession by ten of her surviving children, which the sources note included her "shamed son, Hubert" [13]. 

**The 1937 Séance Communications**
Caroline Bull briefly re-enters the Borley narrative posthumously during the intensive planchette séances conducted by Harry Price's observers in 1937. During a session in the Rectory library on October 25, 1937, an entity communicated with the sitters identifying itself as "Jane Foyster" who had married Henry Dawson Bull [16]. The entity confirmed that she was his only wife and her real name was indeed Caroline Sarah, explaining that her husband had affectionately called her "Jane" and that her mother had given her the unusual nickname "Caldebec" [16, 17].
