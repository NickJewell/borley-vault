# Catherine Foster (of Acton)

An Acton woman — in the parish immediately across the Stour from [[borley-rectory]] — who poisoned her husband with arsenic in November 1846 and was tried and publicly executed. [[andrew-clarke]] identifies her case as one of the real local crimes absorbed into the mythology that the Bull family wove into the Borley haunting legend.

## The crime and execution

Clarke describes her as "a simple-minded woman who poisoned her husband with arsenic in November 1846. Her trial and public execution was a local sensation."

No further biographical detail is given, but the event's scale — a public execution in the local area — would have been a major event in the community's living memory throughout the mid-Victorian period when the Bull family were establishing themselves at Borley.

## Role in the Borley legend

Clarke's analysis in essay 11 places Catherine Foster alongside the rape case of [[rev-john-foster-foxearth]] and the Maria Marten Red Barn Murder at nearby Polstead as elements of a broader regional folklore of violent secrets. The Bull sisters, Clarke argues, "processed" these into the Borley narrative:

> *There was also the murder by poisoning committed by Catherine Foster in the next parish, Acton. She was a simple-minded woman who poisoned her husband with arsenic in November 1846. Her trial and public execution was a local sensation. There were also shades of Maria Marten and the Red Barn Murder, in the nearby parish of Polstead. All was processed into a fantastic soup by the inventive Bull girls and poor Mrs. Smith fell for it all.*
> — *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, essay 11

The specific relevance is to the "Sugar of Lead" (lead acetate) bottle found in the Rectory when the Smiths arrived, which [[mabel-smith]] briefly believed might be evidence of poisoning. Clarke's point is that the bulls' stories of murder-by-poisoning had deep local roots in real criminal cases, which made them more plausible to newcomers.

## Significance

Catherine Foster is part of Clarke's larger argument that the Borley haunting legend did not arise from nothing but was assembled from real and semi-real local events, legends, and scandals. Her case supplied a template for poisoning narratives at a rectory, available in local memory to anyone in the Stour Valley communities.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Catherine Foster** was a nineteenth-century resident of Acton, a parish neighbouring Borley. While she never lived at Borley Rectory itself, her infamous and gruesome crime became a significant part of the local folklore that helped inspire the Rectory's legendary reputation. 

A brief reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts and connections regarding her case:

**The Crime and Execution (1846)**
Described in the sources as a **"simple-minded woman,"** Catherine Foster murdered her husband by poisoning him with arsenic in **November 1846** [1]. The case culminated in her trial and public execution, an event that became a massive **"local sensation"** [1]. 

**Influence on the Borley Legends**
Catherine Foster's story—alongside other notorious local scandals like the 1871 rape case involving the Rev. John Foster of Foxearth and the famous Red Barn Murder in Polstead—became woven into the dark history of the area [1]. 

According to the texts, these sensational true crimes were **"processed into a fantastic soup by the inventive Bull girls"** (the daughters of Borley's original rector, Henry Dawson Ellis Bull) [1]. The Bull sisters passed these whispered secrets and tales of murder down to the wife of the next rector, Mabel Smith, who **"fell for it all"** [1]. Mrs. Smith subsequently used these exaggerated local legends as the inspiration for her own fictionalized manuscript, *Murder at the Parsonage* [1]. 

The sources conclude that the genuine local horror of Catherine Foster's execution contributed heavily to the atmosphere of morbidity surrounding the area, and that subsequent paranormal investigators, such as the Glanvilles, were also taken in by the fantastic legends that sprouted from these real historical crimes [1].
