# Catherine Foster (of Acton)

A resident of Acton — the parish immediately across the Stour from [[borley-rectory]] — who poisoned her husband with arsenic in November 1846 and was tried and publicly executed. [[andrew-clarke]] identifies her case as one of the real local crimes absorbed into the mythology that the Bull family wove into the Borley haunting legend.

## The Crime and Execution

Described in the sources as a **"simple-minded woman,"** Catherine Foster murdered her husband by poisoning him with arsenic in **November 1846** [1]. The case culminated in her trial and public execution, an event that became a massive **"local sensation"** [1]. No further biographical detail about Foster is given beyond Clarke's brief account.

## Role in the Borley Legend

Clarke's analysis in essay 11 places Catherine Foster alongside the rape case of [[rev-john-foster-foxearth|Rev. John Foster of Foxearth]] and the Maria Marten Red Barn Murder at nearby [[polstead-haunting|Polstead]] as elements of a broader regional folklore of violent secrets. These sensational true crimes were, according to Clarke, **"processed into a fantastic soup by the inventive Bull girls"** (the daughters of Borley's original rector, Henry Dawson Ellis Bull) [1]. The Bull sisters passed these whispered stories down to the wife of the next rector, [[mabel-smith|Mabel Smith]], who **"fell for it all"** [1]. Mrs. Smith subsequently used these exaggerated local legends as inspiration for her own fictionalized manuscript, *[[murder-at-the-parsonage|Murder at the Parsonage]]* [1].

The specific relevance to Borley is the "Sugar of Lead" (lead acetate) bottle found in the Rectory when the Smiths arrived, which Mrs. Smith briefly believed might be evidence of poisoning. Clarke's point is that the Bulls' stories of murder-by-poisoning had deep local roots in real criminal cases, which made them more plausible to newcomers.

> *There was also the murder by poisoning committed by Catherine Foster in the next parish, Acton. She was a simple-minded woman who poisoned her husband with arsenic in November 1846. Her trial and public execution was a local sensation. There were also shades of Maria Marten and the Red Barn Murder, in the nearby parish of Polstead. All was processed into a fantastic soup by the inventive Bull girls and poor Mrs. Smith fell for it all.*
> — *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, essay 11

## Significance

Catherine Foster is part of Clarke's larger argument that the Borley haunting legend was assembled from real and semi-real local events, legends, and scandals — not conjured from nothing. Her case supplied a template for poisoning narratives at a rectory, available in local memory to anyone in the Stour Valley communities.
