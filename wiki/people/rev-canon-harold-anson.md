# Rev. Canon Harold Anson, M.A.

Master of the Temple, London. Writing on **29 October 1940**, shortly after the publication of *The Most Haunted House in England*, Anson posed two questions to [[harry-price|Price]] — and a third historical challenge — that proved to have significant implications for the future direction of the Borley investigation.

## The Three Queries

**1. Why had no one opened the Nun's grave?**

Anson's first challenge, articulated in a direct quote:

> **"I have been wondering very much why no one ever tried to open up the nun's grave—it seems to me to have been the obvious thing to do, as an experiment—and why no Mass was celebrated."** [1]

Price responded that Planchette and table-tipping messages were incredibly contradictory regarding the grave's location (suggesting it was "in the garden," "under the house," "near the greenhouse," etc.), so they simply did not know where to dig [1].

**2. Why was no Mass ever said for the Nun?**

Anson found it remarkable that no priest had said a Requiem Mass for the soul of [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]], as the Planchette and wall-messages had repeatedly requested. Price explained the difficulties: the property belonged to Anglican ecclesiastical authorities who would have resented a Catholic Requiem, and efforts to find a willing priest to say it in the Rectory had failed. [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard Whitehouse, O.S.B.]] had also tried and failed. Several Requiem Masses were eventually said elsewhere — at Arundel and at other locations.

**3. The Historical Implausibility of a 17th-Century English Nunnery**

Anson also used his historical and theological knowledge to point out a major flaw in the timeline of the "Marie Lairre" legend, which suggested the nun had lived in an English convent in 1667:

> **"I wonder also how it is possible to imagine a nunnery existing in England in the seventeenth century. Except for the small Anglican community at Little Gidding, in Charles I's reign, there were no religious houses in England, either Roman or Anglican, between 1558 and the nineteenth century, unless I am much mistaken."** [3]

Price conceded that Canon Anson's historical fact was absolutely correct. To circumvent this glaring anachronism, Price fell back on [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]]'s theory, arguing that [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] was a *French* nun who had been inveigled into coming to England from a convent in Le Havre before being murdered at Borley [3].

## Significance

Anson's queries, arriving in October 1940, are part of the context of external intellectual pressure that eventually drove Price toward the August 1943 excavation. Combined with Canon Phythian-Adams's January 1941 letter offering to decode the [[wall-writings|wall-writings]], these early reader responses to *The Most Haunted House in England* pushed Price from documentation toward active physical investigation.

The Mass question was eventually answered: the Rev. [[father-john-wright|Father John Wright, of S. Philip Neri, Arundel]] said the first documented Requiem Mass for the Borley Nun on **30 October 1943**.
