# Rev. Canon Harold Anson, M.A.

Master of the Temple, London. Writing on October 29, 1940, shortly after the publication of *The Most Haunted House in England*, Anson posed two questions to [[harry-price|Price]] that proved to have significant implications for the future direction of the Borley investigation.

## The Two Queries

**1. Why was no Mass ever said for the Nun?**

Anson found it remarkable that no priest had said a Requiem Mass for the soul of [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]], as the Planchette and wall-messages had repeatedly requested. Price explained the difficulties: the property belonged to Anglican ecclesiastical authorities who would have resented a Catholic Requiem, and efforts to find a willing priest to say it in the Rectory had failed. [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard Whitehouse, O.S.B.]] had also tried and failed. Several Requiem Masses were eventually said elsewhere — at Arundel and at other locations.

**2. Why had no one opened the Nun's grave?**

Anson wrote: "I have been wondering very much why no one ever tried to open up the nun's grave—it seems to me to have been the obvious thing to do, as an experiment." Price explained the problem: they had been given many contradictory Planchette instructions about where the remains were buried, and the property then belonged to Queen Anne's Bounty who would not permit disfiguring excavation.

## Significance

Anson's queries, arriving in October 1940, are part of the context of external intellectual pressure that eventually drove Price toward the August 1943 excavation. Combined with [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]]'s January 1941 letter offering to decode the wall-writings, these early reader responses to *The Most Haunted House in England* pushed Price from documentation toward active physical investigation.

The Mass question was eventually answered: the Rev. [[father-john-wright|Father John Wright, of S. Philip Neri, Arundel]] said the first documented Requiem Mass for the Borley Nun on October 30, 1943.
