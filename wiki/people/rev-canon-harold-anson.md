# Rev. Canon Harold Anson, M.A.

Master of the Temple, London. Writing on October 29, 1940, shortly after the publication of *The Most Haunted House in England*, Anson posed two questions to [[harry-price|Price]] that proved to have significant implications for the future direction of the Borley investigation.

## The Two Queries

**1. Why was no Mass ever said for the Nun?**

Anson found it remarkable that no priest had said a Requiem Mass for the soul of [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]], as the Planchette and wall-messages had repeatedly requested. Price explained the difficulties: the property belonged to Anglican ecclesiastical authorities who would have resented a Catholic Requiem, and efforts to find a willing priest to say it in the Rectory had failed. [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard Whitehouse, O.S.B.]] had also tried and failed. Several Requiem Masses were eventually said elsewhere — at Arundel and at other locations.

**2. Why had no one opened the Nun's grave?**

Anson wrote: "I have been wondering very much why no one ever tried to open up the nun's grave—it seems to me to have been the obvious thing to do, as an experiment." Price explained the problem: they had been given many contradictory Planchette instructions about where the remains were buried, and the property then belonged to Queen Anne's Bounty who would not permit disfiguring excavation.

## Significance

Anson's queries, arriving in October 1940, are part of the context of external intellectual pressure that eventually drove Price toward the August 1943 excavation. Combined with [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]]'s January 1941 letter offering to decode the wall-writings, these early reader responses to *The Most Haunted House in England* pushed Price from documentation toward active physical investigation.

The Mass question was eventually answered: the Rev. [[father-john-wright|Father John Wright, of S. Philip Neri, Arundel]] said the first documented Requiem Mass for the Borley Nun on October 30, 1943.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Rev. Canon Harold Anson, M.A.**, who held the prestigious title of **Master of the Temple**, appears in the Borley Rectory sources as a correspondent of Harry Price. Although he did not visit the Rectory or participate in the active investigation, he played a notable role by raising sharp, logical, and historically grounded questions regarding the famous "ghost nun" legend.

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific dates, quotes, and queries regarding his involvement:

**The October 1940 Letter and Queries**
Following the publication of Harry Price's first book, *The Most Haunted House in England*, Canon Anson wrote to Price on **October 29, 1940**, to question several practical and historical aspects of the nun's story [1]. He challenged the narrative on three specific fronts:

**1. The Unopened Grave and the Unsaid Mass**
Canon Anson was puzzled as to why researchers had not taken direct, physical or spiritual action based on the messages supposedly received from the nun (who had requested masses and a proper burial). He posed the following direct quote: 
> **"I have been wondering very much why no one ever tried to open up the nun’s grave—it seems to me to have been the obvious thing to do, as an experiment—and why no Mass was celebrated."** [1]

*Price's Response:* Price responded that planchette and table-tipping messages were incredibly contradictory regarding the grave's location (suggesting it was "in the garden," "under the house," "near the greenhouse," etc.), so they simply did not know where to dig [1]. Regarding the Mass, Price explained that it was difficult to find a Roman Catholic priest willing to celebrate a Requiem Mass in an Anglican Rectory without the permission of the Anglican ecclesiastical authorities [2].

**2. The Historical Implausibility of a 17th-Century English Nunnery**
Canon Anson also used his historical and theological knowledge to point out a major flaw in the timeline of the "Marie Lairre" legend, which suggested the nun had lived in an English convent in 1667. He provided the following specific quote outlining this historical contradiction:
> **"I wonder also how it is possible to imagine a nunnery existing in England in the seventeenth century. Except for the small Anglican community at Little Gidding, in Charles I’s reign, there were no religious houses in England, either Roman or Anglican, between 1558 and the nineteenth century, unless I am much mistaken."** [3]

*Price's Response:* Price conceded that Canon Anson's historical fact was absolutely correct. To circumvent this glaring anachronism, Price fell back on Canon W.J. Phythian-Adams's theory, arguing that Marie Lairre was a *French* nun who had been inveigled into coming to England from a convent in Le Havre before being murdered at Borley [3]. 

The provided Borley sources contain no further biographical details or records of Canon Anson's involvement beyond this specific 1940 correspondence [1-3].
