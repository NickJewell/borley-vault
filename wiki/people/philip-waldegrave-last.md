# Philip Waldegrave (d. 1720–21)

The last of the Borley branch of the Waldegrave family. Son of John Waldegrave (eldest son of Nicholas's son Philip). Listed as a recusant (Catholic refusing to attend Anglican services) in 1715 — confirming that the Borley Waldegraves remained steadfastly Catholic into the early eighteenth century. He died c.1720–21 without male heirs.

## End of the Borley Waldegraves

On Philip's death, the Borley estates passed to **James, Lord Waldegrave** — later made an Earl. This transfers the Borley holdings from the local branch of the Waldegrave family (resident at Borley Hall on the Stour) to the senior branch (based at Chewton in Somerset).

From this point the Waldegraves were absentee owners of the Borley estates; the direct personal connection of a resident Waldegrave lord to the village ended with Philip's death.

## Significance

Philip's death in 1720–21 concludes the period during which an on-site Waldegrave presence at Borley could potentially have concealed the burial of [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] under the Rectory cellar. By 1720, the alleged murder had occurred fifty-three years earlier; any living witnesses would be dead. The transfer to the distant Chewton branch meant that local Borley Waldegrave memory of any crime would effectively have terminated here.

[[georgina-dawson|Mrs. Dawson]]'s research, as quoted by [[harry-price|Price]], traces the complete Borley descent from [[nicholas-waldegrave|Nicholas]] through to Philip, establishing the family's continuous Catholic recusancy throughout the period relevant to the [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] narrative.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Philip Waldegrave** (d. 1720–21) is recorded in the sources as the final descendant of the Borley branch of the powerful Waldegrave family [1, 2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific biographical details, dates, and connections to the Borley Rectory legends:

**Lineage and Background**
*   **Family Tree:** He was the only son of John Waldegrave [1, 3]. His father, John, was the eldest son (by his first wife) of the *first* Philip Waldegrave, who was in turn the eldest son of Sir Nicholas Waldegrave of Borley [1, 3]. 
*   **Religion:** Like his ancestors, Philip remained a **staunch Roman Catholic** [4]. The sources explicitly note that he was officially **"listed as a recusant in 1715"** [1].

**Church Patronage and Appointments**
As the Lord of the Manor, Philip held the right to appoint the rectors of Borley Church. The sources record his involvement in two specific appointments:
*   **1660:** He appointed William Playne as Rector of Borley [5]. However, this appointment was made *per curatores* (through guardians or curators), which led historian Georgina Dawson to deduce that Philip was likely **"not of age"** at this time [5]. 
*   **1709:** Following the death of the incumbent Thomas Muriell, Philip again exercised his right as patron, this time appointing Robert Goodwin **"in the regular way"** [5].

**Connections to the "Ghost Nun" Theories**
Because Philip's life spanned the late seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries, he is frequently mentioned in modern theories attempting to identify the ghost nun of Borley Rectory:
*   **The Arabella Waldegrave Theory:** Historian Georgina Dawson theorized that the phantom nun was Philip's kinswoman, Arabella Waldegrave (born 1687), who vanished from the historical record and was supposedly murdered at Borley as a spy [4, 6]. However, Dawson herself noted a major flaw in this theory: because Philip Waldegrave was a devout Catholic, it is highly unlikely he would have allowed his Catholic kinswoman to be quietly murdered on his estate without raising a hue and cry [4]. 
*   **The Mary Lycett Theory:** In Mary Lycett's highly romanticized (and entirely theoretical) 1952 account conflating Marie Lairre and Arabella, a **"Philip, Lord Waldegrave of Borley"** is identified as the father of Henry Waldegrave [7]. In this fictionalized scenario, Henry secretly marries the nun, hides her at Borley, and later brutally strangles her when his father, Lord Waldegrave, discovers the marriage and banishes him [8, 9]. 

**Death and the End of the Borley Line**
Philip Waldegrave died in **1720 (or 1720–21)** [2, 3]. With his passing, the Borley branch of the Waldegraves officially came to an end [1]. Following his death, the Borley estates passed to **James, Lord Waldegrave**, who subsequently became the 1st Earl Waldegrave in 1729 [1, 3].
