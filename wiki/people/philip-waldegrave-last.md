# Philip Waldegrave (d. 1720–21)

The last of the Borley branch of the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]]. Son of John Waldegrave — himself the eldest son (by his first wife) of the first Philip Waldegrave, who was in turn the eldest son of Sir [[nicholas-waldegrave|Nicholas Waldegrave]] of Borley [1, 3]. Listed as a recusant (Catholic refusing to attend Anglican services) in 1715 — confirming that the Borley Waldegraves remained steadfastly Catholic into the early eighteenth century [1]. He died c. 1720–21 without male heirs.

## Church Patronage

As Lord of the Manor, Philip held the right to appoint the rectors of Borley Church. He was involved in two specific appointments:

- **1660:** He appointed William Playne as Rector of Borley [5]. This appointment was made *per curatores* (through guardians or curators), leading historian [[georgina-dawson|Georgina Dawson]] to deduce that Philip was likely **"not of age"** at this time [5].
- **1709:** Following the death of the incumbent Thomas Muriell, Philip again exercised his patronage, appointing Robert Goodwin **"in the regular way"** [5].

## End of the Borley Waldegraves

On Philip's death, the Borley estates passed to **James, Lord Waldegrave** — subsequently made the 1st Earl Waldegrave in 1729 [1, 3]. This transferred the Borley holdings from the local branch of the Waldegrave family (resident at Borley Hall on the Stour) to the senior branch (based at Chewton in Somerset). From this point the Waldegraves were absentee owners of the Borley estates; the direct personal connection of a resident Waldegrave lord to the village ended with Philip's death.

## Connections to the Ghost Nun Theories

Because Philip's life spanned the late seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries, he is mentioned in modern theories attempting to identify [[the-nun|the ghost nun of Borley Rectory]].

**The Arabella Waldegrave Theory:** Historian [[georgina-dawson|Georgina Dawson]] theorised that the phantom nun was Philip's kinswoman, [[arabella-waldegrave|Arabella Waldegrave]] (born 1687), who vanished from the historical record and was supposedly murdered at Borley as a spy [4, 6]. However, Dawson herself noted a major flaw: because Philip Waldegrave was a devout Catholic, it is highly unlikely he would have allowed his Catholic kinswoman to be quietly murdered on his estate without raising a hue and cry [4].

**The Mary Lycett Theory:** In Mary Lycett's highly romanticised 1952 account conflating [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] and Arabella, a **"Philip, Lord Waldegrave of Borley"** is identified as the father of Henry Waldegrave [7]. In this entirely theoretical scenario, Henry secretly marries the nun, hides her at Borley, and later strangles her when his father discovers the marriage [8, 9].

## Significance

Philip's death in 1720–21 concludes the period during which an on-site Waldegrave presence at Borley could potentially have concealed the burial of [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] under the Rectory cellar. By 1720, the alleged murder had occurred fifty-three years earlier; any living witnesses would be dead. The transfer to the distant Chewton branch meant that local Borley Waldegrave memory of any crime would effectively have terminated here.

[[georgina-dawson|Mrs Dawson]]'s research, as quoted by [[harry-price|Price]], traces the complete Borley descent from [[nicholas-waldegrave|Nicholas]] through to Philip, establishing the family's continuous Catholic recusancy throughout the period relevant to the [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] narrative.
