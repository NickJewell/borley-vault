# Rev. Stephen Luskin

Austin Friar monk of [[Clare Priory]], Suffolk, who — twenty-seven years after the Dissolution of his monastery — reappeared as Rector of Borley in 1565. Ivan Banks identifies him as one of the most significant historical figures connecting the monastery legend, the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegraves]], and the Borley site.

## Background at Clare Priory

Clare Priory was an Augustinian (Austin Friars) house in Clare, Suffolk, dissolved on November 29, 1538. One year before the final dissolution, on December 20, 1537, Luskin obtained a dispensation from Archbishop Thomas Cranmer to continue wearing his monk's habit beneath the robes of a priest — at a cost of £4. At that time it was already illegal to wear a monk's or nun's habit in public.

Banks notes that Luskin clearly hoped for a reversal of the Dissolution. As the Rev. M. J. Loughran pointed out to Banks, "Luskin could at least claim that he had never stopped wearing his habit, even if unseen under the cloak of a Protestant priest."

The order did not return to Clare for four hundred years: the Austin Friars re-established themselves there in 1953, after the Second World War.

## Arrival at Borley

In 1565, four years after the death of [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Sir Edward Waldegrave]], Luskin succeeded the Rev. William Cooper as Rector of Borley — the very post Cooper had held at the time of Waldegrave's arrest for heresy in 1561. Banks raises the question of what Luskin had been doing in the twenty-seven years between leaving Clare and arriving at Borley, and why he chose this particular living above others in the district.

The living of Borley was, of course, in the gift of the Waldegraves, who were longstanding Catholic sympathizers. Banks suggests that Luskin and the Waldegraves may have shared matters of common cause during those interim decades.

## Significance to the Borley Story

Banks identifies several connections:

1. **The monastery controversy**: The existence of an Austin Friar connection to Borley — via Luskin — helps explain persistent local beliefs about a monastic presence at the site. At least one Waldegrave from a previous generation was known to have been a member of the Augustinian order.

2. **The Waldegrave nuns**: About forty years after Luskin's arrival at Borley, the wife of Sir Nicholas Waldegrave (Luskin's patron's successor) bore a daughter, Barbara Waldegrave — who became [[barbara-waldegrave|Sister Apollonia]] of the Brussels English Benedictine nuns in exile. Banks suggests Luskin may have had a role in facilitating Waldegrave family members' departure to French and Flemish religious houses.

3. **The nun story**: Banks raises the possibility that Luskin, as a one-time Catholic monk with monastic contacts, may have assisted in conveying members of the Waldegrave family to safety in France — potentially connecting him to whatever event underlies the ghost nun tradition.

> **Banks's words:** "As Rector of Borley, whose living was, of course, in the gift of the Waldegraves, and as a one-time Catholic monk, it seems not impossible that had he been asked to assist in the removal of any members of the Waldegrave family to the safety of France, he might well have acceded to such a request."

## The Name Confusion

Banks further notes that "Luskin" is remarkably close to "Luskyn" — which is the spelling of Rev. Stephen Luskyn in the record of Borley rectors. He is listed in some sources as "Stephen Luskin" and in others as "Stephen Luskyn"; Banks treats both spellings as the same person.
