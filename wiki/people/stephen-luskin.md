# Rev. Stephen Luskin

Also recorded in the parish lists as **Steven Luskyn**. Austin Friar monk of [[clare-priory|Clare Priory]], Suffolk, who — twenty-seven years after the Dissolution of his monastery — reappeared as Rector of Borley in 1565 (or 1566, per the formal rectors list [3, 5]). [[ivan-banks|Ivan Banks]] identifies him as one of the most significant historical figures connecting the monastery legend, the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegraves]], and the Borley site.

## Background at Clare Priory

Clare Priory was an Augustinian (Austin Friars) house in Clare, Suffolk, dissolved on November 29, 1538. One year before the final dissolution, on **December 20, 1537**, Luskin obtained a dispensation from Archbishop Thomas Cranmer to continue wearing his monk's habit secretly beneath the robes of a Protestant priest — at a specific cost of **£4.00** [2]. At that time it was already illegal to wear a monk's or nun's habit in public.

Banks notes that Luskin clearly hoped for a reversal of the Dissolution. As the Rev. M. J. Loughran pointed out to Banks: **"Luskin could at least claim that he had never stopped wearing his habit, even if unseen under the cloak of a Protestant priest!"** [2]. The order did not return to [[clare-priory|Clare]] for four hundred years, re-establishing themselves there in 1953.

## Arrival at Borley

For twenty-seven years following the dissolution of Clare Priory, Luskin's life is unaccounted for in the sources [3, 4]. He unexpectedly reappears when he is appointed the **Rector of Borley**, succeeding the Rev. William Cooper — who had been the incumbent during the arrest of Borley's Catholic Lord of the Manor, [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Sir Edward Waldegrave]], in 1561 [3].

The living of Borley was entirely in the gift of the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegraves]], who were longstanding Catholic sympathisers secretly trying to restore a Catholic monarch to the throne [4]. Banks raises the question of what Luskin had been doing in the twenty-seven years between leaving Clare and arriving at Borley, and why he chose this particular living above others in the district. He suggests that Luskin and the Waldegraves may have shared matters of common cause during those interim decades.

## Significance to the Borley Story

Banks identifies several connections:

1. **The monastery controversy**: The existence of an Austin Friar connection to Borley — via Luskin — helps explain persistent local beliefs about a monastic presence at the site. At least one Waldegrave from a previous generation was known to have been a member of the Augustinian order.

2. **The Waldegrave nuns**: About forty years after Luskin's arrival at Borley, the wife of Sir Nicholas Waldegrave (Luskin's patron's successor) bore a daughter, [[barbara-waldegrave|Barbara Waldegrave]] — who became Sister Apollonia of the Brussels English Benedictine nuns in exile. Banks suggests Luskin may have had a role in facilitating Waldegrave family members' departure to French and Flemish religious houses.

3. **The nun story**: Banks raises the possibility that Luskin, as a one-time Catholic monk with monastic contacts, may have assisted in conveying members of the Waldegrave family to safety in France — potentially connecting him to whatever event underlies the ghost nun tradition:

> **Banks's words:** "As Rector of Borley, whose living was, of course, in the gift of the Waldegraves, and as a one-time Catholic monk, it seems not impossible that had he been asked to assist in the removal of any members of the Waldegrave family to the safety of France, he might well have acceded to such a request."
