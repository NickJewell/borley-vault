# Reverend Harry Foyster Bull (1862–1927)

Son of [[henry-bull]]; held the living at Borley for thirty-five years (1892–1927). The most psychically active of the Bull rectors — openly interested in paranormal phenomena and a prolific witness.

## Birth and Early Life

He was born on **24 January 1862** in Gestingthorpe, Essex [1, 2]. A lesser-known biographical fact is that **he was almost certainly born out of wedlock**, as his parents (the Rev. [[henry-bull|Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] and Caroline Sarah Foyster) did not marry until November 1862 [1, 3-5]. Educated at Felstead School, Pembroke College (Cambridge), and Exeter College (Oxford) [1, 6], his path to the priesthood was not entirely smooth.

## Character and Appearance

Described by contemporaries as a **"puckish, lovable man"** [11, 12] — also "one of the most normal men one could meet" and "a typical squire-parson, a lovable and easy-going man with a great sense of humour." He was a tall, athletic, dark-haired man who wore a heavy 'Kitchener' moustache, mutton-chop whiskers, and an exceptionally deep clerical collar [13, 14]. He enjoyed boxing and hunting [13, 15]. A doctor friend of his told [[peter-underwood]] that Harry "could hail a spectre as easily as most people can hail a friend."

He suffered from a bizarre form of narcolepsy or sleeping sickness. His Oxford friend, P. Shaw Jeffrey, recalled: **"He was an extraordinary man; he was always asleep. Nine times out of ten he never turned up to meals at the Rectory"** [16, 17]. A massive animal lover, **he kept between 20 and 34 cats at a time, knew them all by name, and had them follow him around the rectory "like the Pied Piper"** [12, 14, 18].

## Ordination Records (from Durham University Archives)

[[ivan-banks|Ivan Banks]] obtained Harry Bull's ordination assessments from the Auckland Castle Episcopal and Ordination Registers (1881–89) via Durham University. Comments on his work included:
- "Weak!"
- "Only Sufficient!"
- "On Isaiah… Fair Matter, but form weak!"
- "Style Weak, but Fair Matter!" (Durham Diocesan Records 1887–94)

He was informed he must bring his work up to standard before being ordained at Advent 1887. During his curacy at Westoe St Thomas (County Durham, 1886–89), he apparently fell out with the incumbent Rev. Morris over "High Church" practices.

## Marriage and the Will

He remained a bachelor until the age of 49. On 12 September 1911, he married **[[ivy-brackenbury|Ivy Johnson Brackenbury]]** — a 29-year-old nurse described as "ostensibly a widow" — at an Anglo-Catholic church in Holborn, London, not at Borley [1, 19, 20]. [[ivan-banks|Ivan Banks]] notes the Glanville Locked Book records that her previous husband was still alive at the time of her marriage to Harry.

**This marriage sparked a vicious, lifelong family feud** [21-23]:
- The marriage took place against opposition from other Bull family members
- Ivy was described as domineering; her insistence on moving into the Rectory after Harry's mother's death created friction with the Bull sisters
- Harry is alleged to have struck Ivy's daughter by her first marriage (a Catholic) — R.J. Hastings reported this in the 1969 report
- Harry's sisters had to move out of the Rectory in 1920 when Harry and Ivy moved in
- When Harry died, his will revealed he had left his entire £6,643 estate in trust to Ivy, leaving his sisters nothing [24, 25]

Harry's stepdaughter (Ivy's daughter, a Catholic) may have been the source of the French Revolution Miraculous Medal found at the Rectory in 1929 — she could have hidden it from Harry to avoid friction over her faith.

## Paranormal Experiences

Harry Bull reported more paranormal encounters than any other single witness in the Bull period. He enthusiastically embraced the supernatural — as his friend J. Osborne Harley stated, **"He could hail a spectre as easily as most people can hail a friend"** [11, 14, 26]. He regularly spoke to his parishioners about his ghosts and held nocturnal vigils in the rectory's octagonal summer-house between 10:00 p.m. and 3:00 a.m. to watch for [[the-nun]] [27, 28]:

- [[the-nun]] — saw her multiple times; freely discussed this with visitors
- [[phantom-coach]] — saw the ghostly coach drawn by two horses, headless coachman, on multiple occasions; also *heard* it without seeing it
- [[headless-man]] — claimed to see a headless man emerge from behind fruit bushes in the garden (see contradiction below)
- Little old man in the garden identical in features to former gardener "Old Amos," allegedly dead for 200 years — vanished when approached [29, 30]
- Mysterious sounds entering via the back door (recurring from soon after 1892)

## The "Headless Man" Contradiction

One of the most famous Borley legends claims that Harry Bull was in the garden with his retriever, Juvenal, when he saw the legs of a man behind some fruit trees. According to [[harry-price|Harry Price]], when the figure cleared the trees, **Bull saw it was a headless man**, who then walked through a locked gate and vanished [31-33].

However, investigators Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall later discovered the original 1929 notes taken by Price's secretary, which revealed a blatant contradiction. **The contemporary notes merely stated that Harry Bull saw a man's legs, "thought poacher," followed him with his dog, and watched the legs disappear through a shut gate.** The dramatic "headless" detail was a sensational embellishment added by Price when he published his book ten years later [32, 34-37].

## The Moth-Ball Prediction

In 1922, Harry Bull confided to J. Harley that he had "personal communications with spirits" [38, 39]. He made a specific promise regarding his own eventual demise: **"If I can return after my death, I shall do something really different, something unusual so that you know it is me – like throwing mothballs about; yes, if mothballs are thrown after I die, you'll know it's me!"** [11, 12]. Moth-balls were reportedly thrown inside the Rectory on at least one occasion during the Smith incumbency, and investigators visiting in 1929 were greeted by a shower of moth-balls [11, 43].

## Death

Harry died in the [[blue-room]] on **9 June 1927** from chronic bronchitis and cancer [14, 40-42]. His wife Ivy died at Hastings in 1955.

## Posthumous Apparitions and Murder Rumours

Harry Bull's death birthed a new layer of Borley mythology:

**The murder rumour:** Because his sisters were furious about being cut out of his will, **they maliciously spread the fabricated rumour that his wife, Ivy, had murdered him by poisoning him with "sugar of lead"** [21, 44, 45]. During a 1929 séance attended by the sisters and [[harry-price|Harry Price]], a tapping mirror purporting to be Harry Bull's spirit "confirmed" that he had been murdered by his wife and was unhappy about money [46-49].

**The smoking jacket ghost:** During the highly active Foyster incumbency (1930–1935), [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] claimed to see Harry Bull's apparition repeatedly on the stairs and landings. He was distinctly described as **wearing a peculiar plum-coloured dressing gown or smoking jacket** and often carrying a manuscript or scroll, which his sisters eagerly interpreted as him searching for a lost will [25, 50, 53, 54].

## Séance Contacts (October 1937)

Multiple table-tipping sessions in October 1937 produced entities apparently claiming to be Harry Bull:
- The entity confirmed Harry died in the [[blue-room]] (Q.187–188)
- To "Did you die naturally?" — "No" (Q.189)
- To "Were you poisoned by your wife?" — "Yes" (Q.192)
- The entity also stated his "misfortune" involved "a wife" (Q.185–186)

[[ivan-banks|Ivan Banks]] concludes these séance contacts are not proof of poisoning but reflect an atmosphere of domestic unhappiness that pervaded the Rectory after 1911. He speculates Miss Brackenbury may have discovered the bottle of sugar of lead (possibly connected to the [[kate-boreham]] episode) and hidden it, or may herself have been a suspect. Banks does not assert Harry was actually poisoned.
