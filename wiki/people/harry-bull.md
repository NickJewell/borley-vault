# Reverend Harry Foyster Bull (d. 9 June 1927)

Son of [[henry-bull]]; held the living at Borley for thirty-five years (1892–1927). The most psychically active of the Bull rectors — openly interested in paranormal phenomena and a prolific witness.

## Character

Described by a regular army officer as "one of the most normal men one could meet." Also described as "a typical squire-parson, a lovable and easy-going man with a great sense of humour." A doctor friend of his told [[peter-underwood]] that Harry "could hail a spectre as easily as most people can hail a friend."

## Paranormal Experiences

Harry Bull reported more paranormal encounters than any other single witness in the Bull period:

- [[the-nun]] — saw her multiple times; freely discussed this with visitors
- [[phantom-coach]] — saw the ghostly coach drawn by two horses, headless coachman, on multiple occasions; also *heard* it without seeing it
- [[headless-man]] — saw a headless figure emerge from behind fruit bushes in the garden
- Little old man in the garden — identical in features to a former gardener; vanished when approached
- Mysterious sounds entering via the back door (recurring from soon after 1892)

He would spend hours (10 pm to 3 am) in the octagonal summer-house facing [[nuns-walk]], watching for the Nun.

## The Moth-Ball Prediction

Harry Bull told Mr Harley that if, after his death, he was dissatisfied with his successor, he would "make his displeasure felt in some unusual way — such as throwing moth-balls about." Moth-balls were reportedly thrown inside the Rectory on at least one occasion during a subsequent incumbency.

## Ordination Records (from Durham University Archives)

[[Ivan Banks]] obtained Harry Bull's ordination assessments from the Auckland Castle Episcopal and Ordination Registers (1881–89) via Durham University. Comments on his work included:
- "Weak!"
- "Only Sufficient!"
- "On Isaiah... Fair Matter, but form weak!"
- "Style Weak, but Fair Matter!" (Durham Diocesan Records 1887–94)

He was informed he must bring his work up to standard before being ordained at Advent 1887. During his curacy at Westoe St Thomas (County Durham, 1886–89), he apparently fell out with the incumbent Rev. Morris over "High Church" practices.

## Marriage and Later Years

Married **Ivy Brackenbury** at St Alban's, Holborn (not at Borley) in 1911. [[Ivan Banks]] notes tensions in this arrangement:
- The marriage took place against opposition from other Bull family members
- Ivy was described as "ostensibly a widow" though the Glanville Locked Book records her previous husband was still alive at the time of her marriage to Harry
- Harry is alleged to have struck Ivy's daughter by her first marriage (a Catholic) — R.J. Hastings reported this in the 1969 report
- Banks speculates Ivy was a domineering wife whose insistence on moving into the Rectory after Harry's mother's death created friction with the Bull sisters
- Harry's **will** effectively left everything to Ivy, with nothing for his sisters — who had to move out of the Rectory in 1920 when Harry and Ivy moved in

Harry's stepdaughter (Ivy's daughter, a Catholic) may have been the source of the French Revolution Miraculous Medal found at the Rectory in 1929 — she could have hidden it from Harry to avoid friction over her faith.

Harry died in the [[blue-room]] on June 9, 1927, from cancer. His wife Ivy died at Hastings in 1955.

## Séance Contacts (October 1937)

Multiple table-tipping sessions in October 1937 produced entities apparently claiming to be Harry Bull:
- The entity confirmed Harry died in the Blue Room (Q.187–188)
- To "Did you die naturally?" — "No" (Q.189)
- To "Were you poisoned by your wife?" — "Yes" (Q.192)
- The entity also stated his "misfortune" involved "a wife" (Q.185–186)

[[Ivan Banks]] concludes these séance contacts are not proof of poisoning but reflect an atmosphere of domestic unhappiness that pervaded the Rectory after 1911. He speculates Miss Brackenbury may have discovered the bottle of sugar of lead (possibly connected to the [[kate-boreham]] episode) and hidden it, or may herself have been a suspect. Banks does not assert Harry was actually poisoned.

Harry's remark to Mr. Harley (1922) that after his death he would announce his discontent through "mechanical disturbances" — such as breaking glass or throwing moth-balls — is noted by Banks as consistent with the poltergeist activity that followed his death in 1927.

## Key Witnesses to His Testimony

Rev. Harry Carpenter, Rev. W. G. Stote, Sir George and Lady Whitehouse, Mr J. Harley (of Cavendish), Rev. Clive Luget (of Middleton), Mr Farrance (local parishioner).
