# Revd John Cranmer Dening

(Born Woolwich, Kent, 20 July 1920; died Suffolk, 7 July 2008) At one time a leading light in the Churches' Fellowship for Spiritual and Psychical Studies. Visited Borley many times before his ordination when he worked at the Foreign Office, and became friendly with [[james-turner|James and Catherine Turner]].

## Borley Involvement

Dening experienced some possibly psychic activity at the [[rectory-cottage]] (see [[seance-data]]) and in [[borley-church]]. He visited [[ethel-bull]], Constance Bull and Alfred Bull at [[chilton-lodge]] in the company of [[peter-underwood]].

He became deeply involved in the haunting of Langenhoe Church, Essex, producing two books on the case (see [[langenhoe]]). In 2004 he carried out a "rescue" séance in Borley Church where the medium reportedly contacted and laid to rest the spirit of former clergyman John Deeks, who claimed to have been the cause of the haunting at Borley over the years (see [[rectors-of-borley]]).

## Post-Price Séances

Dening organised the first post-Price séance at [[borley-rectory|the Rectory Cottage]] on 9 June 1948 — just over three months after [[harry-price]]'s death. An entity claiming to be "Harry Bull" communicated and said he would haunt Borley for 4,004 years.

Ten years later, on 1 June 1958, Dening organised another séance in the Rectory Cottage with medium Mrs May Lampard, together with Mr and Mrs Bacon, [[kiernander-steuart|Steuart Kiernander]], and [[len-sewell]] as note-taker. The three-and-a-half-hour sitting attracted an eclectic cast of claimed communicators including a bearded man in a tweed suit, a white-haired lady, an Egyptian tomb-builder connected with Tutankhamun, and the recently deceased [[rev-ac-henning|Alfred Henning]]. The sitting produced few identifiable personalities and is generally considered unsatisfactory.

## Other Interests

Dening's other interests included the Richard III Society and research at haunted locations including Edgehill. His companion to the Langenhoe case (written with [[len-sewell]] and [[kiernander-steuart]]) detailed investigations that paralleled Borley in some respects: a church haunting, apparitions of a woman in medieval dress, and unexplained sounds.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Revd John Cranmer Dening** (July 20, 1920 – July 7, 2008) was a prominent figure in the "Later Borley" period and a leading advocate of the post-Harry Price spiritualistic approach to the hauntings [1, 2]. Born in Woolwich, Kent, and educated at Trinity College, Cambridge, Dening worked at the Foreign Office before his ordination [1, 3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, methodologies, specific events, and curious contradictions regarding his involvement:

**Research Methodology and Affiliations**
*   **The Spiritualistic Approach:** Following the end of the Harry Price era, Dening and Leonard Sewell became the two principal protagonists of the spiritualistic approach to Borley [2]. However, their methods contrasted sharply: while Sewell preferred planchette experiments, Dening worked closely with mediums and frequently participated in "rescue" circles to assist earth-bound spirits [2].
*   **Affiliations:** Dening was at one time a leading light in the Churches’ Fellowship for Spiritual and Psychical Studies [1]. His historical interests also led him to join the Richard III Society, and he conducted research at other haunted localities, including Edgehill [1, 4]. 
*   **The Langenhoe Haunting:** Beyond Borley, Dening was deeply involved in investigating the haunting of Langenhoe Church in Essex (which had historical ties to the Borley Waldegrave family) [1, 5]. He spent all-night vigils there, held séances, and ultimately produced two books on the case, including _The Restless Spirits of Langenhoe_ (2000) [1, 6-8].
*   **Connections to Borley Figures:** Before his ordination, Dening became friendly with James and Catherine Turner, the owners of the Rectory Cottage [1]. He also visited Ethel, Constance, and Alfred Bull at Chilton Lodge in the company of investigator Peter Underwood [1]. 

**Major Borley Séances**
Dening participated in several landmark séances over a span of 56 years:
*   **The June 1948 Wine-Glass Séance:** Just over three months after Harry Price's death, Dening attended a late-night séance on June 8–9, 1948, in the Rectory Cottage [3, 9]. Using the wine glass and alphabet method alongside the Turners, T.S. Frankland, and Mrs. Gay Taylor, the group contacted an entity claiming to be the late "Harry Bull," who declared he would haunt Borley for "4,004 years" [3, 9]. Although the session was inconclusive, Dening noted that **"all those present experienced a general sensation of something 'very unusual'"** [10]. 
*   **The June 1958 Séance:** Ten years later, on June 1, 1958, Dening returned to the Rectory Cottage (then occupied by the Bacon family) and organized a séance with the medium Mrs. May Lampard [11]. The medium claimed to contact a bizarre array of spirits—including a bearded man, an elderly doctor, a "Nurse Taylor," the former Rector Alfred Henning, and an Egyptian tomb-builder connected to Tutankhamun—but the sitters had great difficulty identifying any of the personalities [11]. 
*   **The 2004 "Rescue" Séance:** In 2004, Dening carried out a final "rescue" séance inside Borley Church [4]. During this session, a medium apparently contacted the spirit of John Deeks, a 17th-century Puritan "intruder" priest [4]. Deeks allegedly confessed to being the cause of the Borley haunting over the years, after which the medium purportedly laid his spirit to rest [4].

**The "Sleeping" Investigator: Missed Phenomena**
A highly amusing and contradictory pattern emerges in the sources regarding Dening's stays at the Rectory Cottage: despite his intense interest in the paranormal, he repeatedly managed to sleep through, or be absent for, major disturbances. *(Note: Some sources mistakenly spell his name "Denning" during these accounts).*
*   **The Phantom Cat (October 1949):** When Ronald Blythe witnessed the famous "phantom cat" in the cottage bathroom, Dening was staying with the Turners but happened to be away visiting Liston Rectory at the exact time of the sighting [12, 13]. Dening later recalled James Turner's nonchalant reaction the next morning, noting that Turner simply remarked at breakfast: **"Oh, by the way, Ronnie saw the phantom cat last night"** and casually changed the subject [13].
*   **The Footsteps Over the Lounge (March 4–5, 1950):** While Peter Underwood and Laurence C. Gafford were holding a vigil in the cottage lounge, they heard heavy footsteps and muffled voices directly overhead at 2:15 a.m. and again at 4:15 a.m. [14-16]. Dening was sleeping in the front bedroom on the first floor overlooking the churchyard, but he **"slept soundly throughout the night and heard nothing untoward"** [15, 17, 18].
*   **The Squealing Cat (March 6, 1950):** In the early hours of the morning, the Turners heard a loud noise of a cat squealing emanating from the upstairs of the cottage where Dening was sleeping [19]. The following morning, Dening reported that he had fallen asleep immediately and had not been disturbed at all [19]. The authors jokingly note a contradictory theory regarding this specific phenomenon: **"We do not know if he suffered from nightmares and was responsible unwittingly for the squealing sound"** [19].
