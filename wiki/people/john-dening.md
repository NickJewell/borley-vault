# Revd John Cranmer Dening

(Born Woolwich, Kent, 20 July 1920; died Suffolk, 7 July 2008) Educated at Trinity College, Cambridge; worked at the Foreign Office before his ordination [1, 3]. At one time a leading light in the Churches' Fellowship for Spiritual and Psychical Studies. Visited Borley many times before his ordination and became friendly with [[james-turner|James and Catherine Turner]].

## Research Methodology and Affiliations

Following the end of the Harry Price era, Dening and Leonard Sewell became the two principal protagonists of the spiritualistic approach to Borley [2]. Their methods contrasted sharply: while Sewell preferred planchette experiments, Dening worked closely with mediums and frequently participated in "rescue" circles to assist earth-bound spirits [2]. Dening also joined the Richard III Society and conducted research at other haunted localities, including Edgehill [1, 4].

He experienced some possibly psychic activity at the [[rectory-cottage|Rectory Cottage]] and in [[borley-church|Borley Church]], and visited [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]], Constance Bull and Alfred Bull at [[chilton-lodge|Chilton Lodge]] in the company of [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]].

## The Langenhoe Haunting

Beyond Borley, Dening was deeply involved in investigating the haunting of Langenhoe Church in Essex (which had historical ties to the Borley [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]]) [1, 5]. He spent all-night vigils there, held séances, and ultimately produced two books on the case, including *The Restless Spirits of Langenhoe* (2000), co-authored with [[len-sewell|Leonard Sewell]] and [[steuart-kiernander|Steuart Kiernander]] [1, 6–8].

## Major Borley Séances

Dening participated in several landmark séances over a span of 56 years:

**The June 1948 Wine-Glass Séance:** Just over three months after [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s death, Dening attended a late-night séance on 8–9 June 1948 in the Rectory Cottage — the first post-Price séance at the site [3, 9]. Using the wine glass and alphabet method alongside the Turners, T. S. Frankland, and Mrs. Gay Taylor, the group contacted an entity claiming to be the late "Harry Bull," who declared he would haunt Borley for "4,004 years" [3, 9]. Dening noted that "all those present experienced a general sensation of something 'very unusual'" [10].

**The June 1958 Séance:** Ten years later, on 1 June 1958, Dening returned to the Rectory Cottage (then occupied by the Bacon family) and organised a séance with the medium Mrs. May Lampard [11]. The medium claimed to contact a bizarre array of spirits — including a bearded man in a tweed suit, a white-haired lady, a "Nurse Taylor," the recently deceased [[rev-ac-henning|Alfred Henning]], and an Egyptian tomb-builder connected to Tutankhamun — but the sitters had great difficulty identifying any of the personalities [11]. The sitting is generally considered unsatisfactory.

**The 2004 "Rescue" Séance:** In 2004, Dening carried out a final "rescue" séance inside Borley Church [4]. During this session, a medium apparently contacted the spirit of [[john-deeks|John Deeks]], a 17th-century Puritan "intruder" priest, who allegedly confessed to being the cause of the Borley haunting over the years, after which the medium purportedly laid his spirit to rest [4].

## The "Sleeping" Investigator

A striking and somewhat ironic pattern emerges in the sources regarding Dening's stays at the Rectory Cottage: despite his intense interest in the paranormal, he repeatedly managed to sleep through, or be absent during, major disturbances. *(Some sources mistakenly spell his name "Denning" in these accounts.)*

- **The Phantom Cat (October 1949):** When [[ronald-blythe|Ronald Blythe]] witnessed the famous phantom cat in the cottage bathroom, Dening was staying with the Turners but happened to be away visiting [[liston-rectory|Liston Rectory]] at the exact time of the sighting [12, 13]. Dening later recalled James Turner's nonchalant reaction: Turner simply remarked at breakfast, "Oh, by the way, Ronnie saw the phantom cat last night," and casually changed the subject [13].
- **The Footsteps Over the Lounge (4–5 March 1950):** While Peter Underwood and Laurence Gafford were holding a vigil in the cottage lounge and heard heavy footsteps and muffled voices directly overhead at 2:15 a.m. and 4:15 a.m. [14–16], Dening was sleeping in the front bedroom on the first floor overlooking the churchyard and **"slept soundly throughout the night and heard nothing untoward"** [15, 17, 18].
- **The Squealing Cat (6 March 1950):** The Turners heard a loud noise of a cat squealing emanating from the upstairs of the cottage where Dening was sleeping [19]. The following morning, Dening reported he had fallen asleep immediately and had not been disturbed. The authors note with some amusement: **"We do not know if he suffered from nightmares and was responsible unwittingly for the squealing sound"** [19].
