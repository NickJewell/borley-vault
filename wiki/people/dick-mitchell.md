# Dick Mitchell

Seventeen-year-old son of a Yorkshireman who, along with his parents, moved into the [[rectory-cottage|Rectory Cottage]] when it became available at the start of the Foyster tenancy in 1930 [1]. A key helper and incidental witness to poltergeist phenomena at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

## Name Contradiction

There is a notable contradiction in the sources regarding his first name. He is formally introduced in the Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]]'s manuscript as "Dick" [1] and referred to as such in several anecdotes [3, 4], but the Rector also refers to him as "Jack Mitchell the boy from our cottage" in another entry [2]. Decades later, during her 1958 interview with Robert Swanson, [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] also referred to him as "Jack, a young fellow" [5].

## Involvement with the Phenomena

Dick frequently performed odd jobs in and around the Rectory for the Foysters [2, 3], and during the intense poltergeist period of early 1931 he was a regular presence. The Foysters noted that paranormal manifestations seldom occurred when the "non-psychic" Mitchell family was in the house [3], yet Dick was personally targeted on at least two occasions:

- A stone was thrown near him, though he incorrectly assumed the Foysters' toddler, [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide]], had thrown it [2, 3].
- Shortly after a thorough house exorcism and fumigation, another stone was thrown at Dick while he was working in the Rectory [2, 3]. This time, he apparently suspected [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] of throwing it, prompting her to remark indignantly, "as if I should want to throw stones at him" [3].

The Rev. Foyster offered a unique explanation: noting that the "Goblins" had no spite against the Mitchells, Lionel reasoned that because Dick was about the same height as himself, the invisible entities "may possibly have mistaken him for me" in the darkness [2, 3].

Dick was also a firsthand witness to the aftermath of a particularly violent night at the Rectory. When [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] entered the kitchen one morning and discovered it looking "as if a band of hooligans had been round," with the heavy table turned upside down and the store cupboard contents scattered across the room, Dick subsequently helped her put it straight [4]. Marianne worried about what he must be thinking, noting he "probably [thought] that you and I had had a rare old scuffle last night" [4].

## Suspicions Regarding the Wall Writings

Decades after leaving Borley, [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] firmly denied having anything to do with the famous ghostly messages scrawled on the Rectory walls. When investigator Robert Swanson pressed her in 1958 on who else could have faked them, Marianne specifically pointed to the cottage boy as a prime suspect: "There were a lot of people around. There was Edwin [Whitehouse]. There were tenants over at the cottage, the Mitchells, at that time. They had a boy, Jack, a young fellow" [5, 6].

## Departure

When the Mitchells gave up the tenancy of the Rectory Cottage, the Foysters quickly missed having an energetic young man like Dick to help with maintenance and gardening. This prompted Lionel to place an advertisement in *The Times* personals — the advertisement that brought [[francois-darles|Francois D'Arles]] to Borley.
