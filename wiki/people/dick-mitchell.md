# Dick Mitchell

Seventeen-year-old son of the Mitchell family, tenants of [[rectory-cottage|Rectory Cottage]] during the early Foyster period; a key helper and incidental witness to poltergeist phenomena at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

## Background

Mr and Mrs Mitchell and their son Dick moved into the Rectory Cottage when it became available at the start of the Foyster tenancy in 1930. They helped the Foysters with unpacking and odd jobs, and Dick in particular became a reliable worker around the house and grounds.

## Involvement with the Phenomena

During the intense poltergeist period of early 1931, Dick was a regular presence at the Rectory and experienced the phenomena directly:

- He was working in the house when a stone was thrown at him. [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] insisted this could not have been [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide]], as she had suggested.
- On the morning of 9 March, when a plumber came to thaw frozen pipes, Dick was also doing odd jobs; Marianne found a log "bowling along outside" in the kitchen passage.
- A separate incident confirmed by Dick happened when stones were thrown at him while he worked in the house during the period of Arthur Sellwood's attempted exorcism.

His willingness to work in the house despite these incidents, and his corroboration of individual phenomena, made him a small but credible witness from outside the Foyster household.

## Departure

When the Mitchells gave up the tenancy of the Rectory Cottage, the Foysters quickly missed having an energetic young man like Dick to help with maintenance and gardening. This prompted Lionel to place an advertisement in *The Times* personals — the advertisement that brought [[francois-darles|Francois D'Arles]] to Borley.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Dick Mitchell** was the seventeen-year-old son of a Yorkshireman who, along with his parents, became a tenant of the Borley Rectory cottage shortly after the Rev. Lionel Foyster and his wife Marianne moved into the main house [1]. He frequently performed odd jobs in and around the Rectory for the Foysters [2, 3].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, and contradictions regarding his time at the Rectory:

**The Name Contradiction**
There is a notable contradiction in the sources regarding his first name. While he is formally introduced in the Rev. Lionel Foyster's manuscript as "Dick" [1] and referred to as such in several anecdotes [3, 4], the Rector also refers to him as **"Jack Mitchell the boy from our cottage"** in another entry [2]. Decades later, during her 1958 interview with Robert Swanson, Marianne Foyster also referred to him as **"Jack, a young fellow"** [5]. 

**Target of Poltergeist Activity and Foyster's Theory**
Despite the Foysters noting that paranormal manifestations seldom occurred when the "non-psychic" Mitchell family was in the house [3], Dick was personally targeted by the alleged poltergeist on at least two occasions:
*   On a Monday, a stone was thrown near him, though he incorrectly assumed the Foysters' toddler, Adelaide, had thrown it [2, 3]. 
*   Shortly after a thorough house exorcism and fumigation, another stone was thrown at Dick while he was working in the Rectory [2, 3]. This time, he apparently suspected Marianne Foyster of throwing it, prompting her to remark indignantly, **"as if I should want to throw stones at him"** [3].

The Rev. Foyster offered a unique explanation for why the entities targeted the cottage boy. Noting that the "Goblins" had no spite against the Mitchells, Lionel reasoned that because Dick was about the same height as himself, the invisible entities **"may possibly have mistaken him for me"** in the darkness, as the Rector was usually the primary target of their animosity [2, 3].

**The Wrecked Kitchen**
Dick was a firsthand witness to the immediate aftermath of a particularly violent night at the Rectory. When Marianne entered the kitchen one morning, she discovered it looking **"as if a band of hooligans had been round,"** with the heavy table turned upside down and the contents of the store cupboard scattered broadcast across the room [4]. When Dick came to the kitchen door and saw the chaos, Marianne worried about what he must be thinking, noting he **"probably \[thought] that you and I had had a rare old scuffle last night"** [4]. Dick subsequently helped Marianne put the kitchen straight [4].

**Suspicions Regarding the Wall Writings**
Decades after leaving Borley, Marianne Foyster firmly denied having anything to do with the famous ghostly messages scrawled on the Rectory walls. When investigator Robert Swanson pressed her in 1958 on who else could have faked them, Marianne specifically pointed to the cottage boy as a prime suspect, noting: **"There were a lot of people around. There was Edwin \[Whitehouse\]. There were tenants over at the cottage, the Mitchells, at that time. They had a boy, Jack, a young fellow"** [5, 6].
