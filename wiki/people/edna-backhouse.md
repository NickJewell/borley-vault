# Edna Backhouse

Headmistress of the Church of England Primary School at nearby Foxearth, remembered by Claudine Mathias. She reported an unusual paranormal experience at Borley Church in the autumn of 1947.

## Character

Edna Backhouse was, in CM's description, the old-fashioned type of authoritarian teacher who had her favourites and unfavourites. She is noted — with some asperity — for having refused to allow the [[rev-lanfranc-mathias|Rev Lanfranc Mathias]] to give voluntary religious instruction at what was technically a Church of England school. Despite her strict manner, the authors regard her as neither a fool nor a liar.

## The Borley Church Experience (~1947)

Miss Backhouse visited Borley Church to see the recently restored stone altar (the *mensa*), which the [[rev-ac-henning|Rev Henning]] had worked to reinstate in its original position. As she walked towards the altar she felt conscious of a "presence" near her, then heard footsteps following her.

When she turned and walked away from the altar, she again heard the footsteps following. The sound ceased when she left the church. She noted the following:
- The aisle was carpeted
- She was certain no one else was present in the building
- The footsteps were **uneven**, as if the person responsible walked with a limp

The authors note that in his last years, [[henry-bull|the Rev Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] had a pronounced limp. He died in 1892, aged only 59. However, they are careful not to over-speculate about identification.

## Significance

The Backhouse account contributes to the body of evidence for paranormal footstep phenomena in [[borley-church]], adding the specific detail of the asymmetric gait — a feature not found in most other footstep reports.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Miss Edna Backhouse** was a local school teacher whose inclusion in the Borley case rests on a specific paranormal encounter she experienced inside Borley Church. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, events, and minor contradictions regarding her:

**Biographical Details and Personality**
The texts record a minor geographical contradiction regarding her residence or position, describing her both as a "school teacher from Sudbury" and as the headmistress of the Church of England Primary School in the nearby village of Foxearth [1, 2]. 

Claudine Mathias (co-author of *Borley Rectory: The Final Analysis* and wife of the later Borley rector, the Rev. Lanfranc Mathias) remembered Miss Backhouse personally, though "not with any excessive degree of affection" [2]. She described Backhouse as a "formidable" woman and the "old type of authoritarian teacher with her favourite and unfavourite pupils" [2, 3]. Furthermore, she demonstrated a surprising level of "pettiness" by barring the Rev. Lanfranc Mathias from giving voluntary religious instruction at the school, despite it ostensibly being a Church of England institution [2].

**The Borley Church Encounter (Autumn 1947)**
Miss Backhouse's paranormal experience occurred sometime between August 22 and October 15, 1947 [1]. She had visited Borley Church specifically to look at the recently restored stone altar (which the Rev. Alfred Henning referred to as the *mensa*) [3]. 

As she entered the empty church and walked down the carpeted aisle toward the altar, she became acutely conscious of a "presence" near her, followed by the distinct sound of footsteps tracking her from the porch door to the chancel step [1, 3]. When she turned around and walked away from the altar, she heard the footsteps following her back [3]. The sounds only ceased when she physically exited the building [3]. She was absolutely convinced that no one else was inside the church with her at the time [3]. 

**The "Limping" Footsteps**
A highly specific and notable detail of her account is her description of the phantom sounds. She reported that the footsteps pacing behind her were strictly **"uneven, as if whoever was responsible for them walked with a limp"** [3]. 

**Credibility**
Despite her severe reputation, the authors of *The Final Analysis* maintain her credibility as a witness. They argue that while villagers might agree she was "far too inflexible in matters of discipline," this character trait **"does not mean that she was a fool or a liar,"** leaving her account as a piece of serious testimony for individuals to evaluate [3].
