# Alexander Campbell

Editor of the *Daily Mirror* who initiated Harry Price's involvement in the Borley case in June 1929.

## Role in the Borley Story

On Tuesday 11 June 1929, Campbell telephoned the offices of [[national-laboratory|Price's National Laboratory of Psychical Research]] and asked for [[harry-price|Harry Price]] by name. A *Mirror* journalist and photographer were already at Borley investigating strange occurrences reported by [[rev-eric-smith|Rev. Eric Smith]] and his wife. Campbell asked Price if he could help "unravel the mystery" and would he take up the case?

Price, reached at his paper-supplier employer Edward Saunders & Son in Cannon Street, agreed immediately. He was "grateful for the tip-off" and "known for his desire for a good story above all else." The next day, 12 June 1929, Price and his secretary [[lucie-kaye|Lucie Kaye]] drove to Borley, beginning the decade-long association between Price and the Rectory.

## Significance

Campbell's call was the single act that transformed [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] from a locally-known curiosity into a national tabloid story. The *Daily Mirror*'s choice to contact Price's populist National Laboratory rather than the [[spr-investigation|Society for Psychical Research]] had lasting consequences — it fed Price's need for publicity and sowed the seeds of the professional rivalry that would eventually produce the critical [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] of 1956.

> O'Connor notes the irony: the Smiths had written to the *Mirror* hoping for help getting a transfer to another parish, not realising what they had unleashed.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

Based on the Borley sources, **Alexander Campbell** was the editor of the *Daily Mirror* in 1929 and played a brief but historically crucial role as the catalyst for Harry Price's involvement with Borley Rectory. 

A thorough reference profile of his involvement includes the following specific details, dates, and events:

**The Smiths' Letter and the News Scoop (June 1929)**
Around **June 7 or 8, 1929**, the Rev. Guy Eric Smith and his wife, Mabel, wrote a letter to the *Daily Mirror* requesting the details of a suitably qualified society or organization that could help them find answers to the unusual happenings they had been experiencing at Borley Rectory [1]. **"Sensing a news scoop," Campbell immediately dispatched journalist Vernon C. Wall and a staff photographer** to Borley to interview the Smiths and report on the ghostly events [1]. Wall arrived at the Rectory on June 9 [1]. 

**Recruiting Harry Price (June 11, 1929)**
Realizing the potential of the story after Wall filed his initial reports, Campbell decided to bring in a psychic expert [2, 3]. On **Tuesday, June 11, 1929**, Campbell telephoned the offices of the National Laboratory for Psychical Research, asking to speak to its founder and director, Harry Price [3]. 

When Price's secretary informed Campbell that Price was "lunching at a friend’s house," she provided the telephone number where he could be reached (the offices of Edward Saunders & Son, where Price worked as a paper salesman) [3]. 

Campbell rang the number and spoke directly to Price. According to the sources, **Campbell "excitedly explained" that a *Mirror* journalist and photographer were at that moment investigating some remarkable occurrences** at a rectory in Essex [3]. He asked Price if he could help in **"unravelling the mystery"** and explicitly asked, **"Would he take up the case?"** [3]. Price, eager for a good story, accepted the invitation [3].

**Historical Impact**
Though Campbell's personal involvement was limited to these initial editorial decisions, his journalistic instinct to send a reporter and subsequently recruit Harry Price inadvertently transformed Borley Rectory from a localized village legend into a national talking point in tabloid print [4]. By connecting the bewildered Smiths with the media and Harry Price, Campbell set in motion the events that would establish Borley as "the most haunted house in England" [4].
