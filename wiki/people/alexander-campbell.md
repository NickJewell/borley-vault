# Alexander Campbell

Editor of the *Daily Mirror* who initiated [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s involvement in the Borley case in June 1929.

## The Smiths' Letter and the News Scoop

Around **June 7 or 8, 1929**, the Rev. [[rev-eric-smith|Guy Eric Smith]] and his wife, [[mabel-smith|Mabel]], wrote a letter to the *Daily Mirror* requesting the details of a suitably qualified organisation that could help them find answers to the unusual happenings at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] [1]. Sensing a news scoop, Campbell immediately dispatched journalist Vernon C. Wall and a staff photographer to Borley to interview the Smiths and report on the ghostly events [1]. Wall arrived at the Rectory on June 9 [1].

> O'Connor notes the irony: the Smiths had written to the *Mirror* hoping for help getting a transfer to another parish, not realising what they had unleashed.

## Recruiting Harry Price (June 11, 1929)

Realising the potential of the story after Wall filed his initial reports, Campbell decided to bring in a psychic expert. On **Tuesday, June 11, 1929**, he telephoned the offices of the [[national-laboratory|National Laboratory for Psychical Research]], asking to speak to its director, Harry Price [3]. When Price's secretary informed Campbell that Price was at a friend's house, she provided the telephone number where he could be reached (the offices of Edward Saunders & Son, where Price worked as a paper salesman) [3].

Campbell rang the number and spoke directly to Price, "excitedly explaining" that a *Mirror* journalist and photographer were at that moment investigating remarkable occurrences at a rectory in Essex [3]. He asked Price if he could help in "unravelling the mystery" and explicitly asked, "Would he take up the case?" [3]. Price, "grateful for the tip-off" and "known for his desire for a good story above all else," agreed immediately [3]. The next day, June 12, Price and his secretary [[lucie-kaye|Lucie Kaye]] drove to Borley, beginning the decade-long association between Price and the Rectory.

## Significance

Campbell's call was the single act that transformed Borley Rectory from a locally-known curiosity into a national tabloid story. The *Daily Mirror*'s choice to contact Price's populist National Laboratory rather than the [[spr-investigation|Society for Psychical Research]] had lasting consequences — it fed Price's need for publicity and sowed the seeds of the professional rivalry that would eventually produce the critical [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] of 1956.
