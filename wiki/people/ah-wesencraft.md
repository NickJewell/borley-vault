# A. H. Wesencraft

Curator of the **Harry Price Library** at the University of London from 1956 (succeeding Dr. H.P. Pafford). Cared for Price's extensive collection of books and files from that year onward. First met Harry Price in 1937, when Price was negotiating the gift of his library to the University.

## Views on Borley (Letter to Banks, June 10, 1985)

Wesencraft provided [[Ivan Banks]] a written assessment:

- Price was "sincere, devoted to his work" and liked talking about it to anyone who cared to listen
- After studying the Borley dossier, a case "could be made against Price on the grounds of exaggeration and possible distortion of evidence" — specifically, a letter exists in which Price admits Mrs Foyster was probably faking some phenomena, not mentioned in his books
- The [[the-borley-report|1956 SPR report]] was "very much a case for the prosecution and needed a suitable rejoinder" — provided by the Hastings report
- Met Robert Hastings who worked in the HPL; became convinced "the Borley story cannot be dismissed as a journalistic stunt"
- James Turner (Rectory Cottage 1947–50) "assured me there was definitely something very mysterious about the place"
- Wesencraft himself had visited Borley many times and noted "the extraordinary stillness which pervades the location" — though he never experienced psychic manifestations

## On the Locked Book

Wesencraft expressed doubts to [[Ivan Banks]] about [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]]'s claim to ownership of the Locked Book and his right to sell it for over £1,000. The then-curator (Dr. Pafford) had stated in 1959 that there was "a strong suspicion that the Locked Book was removed from the library 'under debatable circumstances'."

Wesencraft confirmed to Banks that Price had "an extensive knowledge of the paper-making business" — supporting Banks's theory that Price's Shropshire paper-trade links were real rather than invented.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Alan Henry Wesencraft (1912–2007)** was a highly respected Reference Librarian who served for forty-two years as the Curator of the Harry Price Library of Magical Literature at the University of London [1]. Described as an able and knowledgeable archivist, anyone who researched or wrote about Borley Rectory was indebted to him [1]. 

**Early Encounters with Harry Price**
Wesencraft first met Harry Price in 1937, while Price was negotiating the acceptance of his magnificent library by the University of London [2]. While waiting in the university corridor to see Mr. Rye, the Librarian, Price would frequently talk to the young Wesencraft about his work. Wesencraft later recalled that Price spoke with a "boyish enthusiasm," striking him as sincere, devoted, and eager to share his ventures with anyone who would listen [2, 3]. During these brief encounters, Price also mentioned that he considered *Psychic News* to be by far the best of the Spiritualist journals [2]. 

Wesencraft lost touch with the University during World War II, serving two years in full-time Civil Defence and five years in the Army [4]. Because Price died in 1948, Wesencraft never met him again after the war [4]. 

**Curator of the Harry Price Library**
In 1956, Wesencraft was officially appointed Curator of the Harry Price Library [4, 5]. Delving into the enormous Borley dossier, he realized that a legitimate case could be made against Price for exaggeration and possible distortion of evidence—for instance, Price admitted in private letters that he suspected Marianne Foyster of faking phenomena, a detail he suppressed in his books [4]. 

Despite recognizing Price's flaws, Wesencraft firmly believed that the Borley story "cannot be dismissed as a journalistic stunt" [6]. He viewed the highly critical 1956 Society for Psychical Research (SPR) *Borley Report* as very much a "case for the prosecution" that desperately needed a rejoinder, which was ultimately provided by Robert J. Hastings's 1969 report [4]. Wesencraft frequently discussed the haunting with former rectory cottage resident James Turner and investigator Peter Underwood, agreeing with them that there was definitively something mysterious about the site [6]. 

**The Conflict with Trevor Hall**
Wesencraft became deeply critical of SPR investigator Trevor H. Hall, who co-authored the 1956 *Borley Report*. In 1956, the University Librarian, Dr. Pafford, approached Wesencraft regarding a disturbing incident: Hall had borrowed Sidney Glanville’s famous *Locked Book* (a crucial compilation of Borley evidence) from the library and refused point-blank to return it, baselessly claiming it did not belong to the University [5]. Advised by the University solicitor, Pafford dropped the matter, and Hall subsequently sold the *Locked Book* to an American collector for £1,200 [7]. Wesencraft bluntly stated that Hall had stolen it [8]. Wesencraft also noted that Hall used a similar devious ruse to obtain the typescript of Lionel Foyster’s *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*, selling it to America for £700 [7].

Wesencraft felt that Sidney Glanville, already in bad health, had fallen "easy prey to Hall's bulldog tactics" and "insistent browbeating" [9]. When Hall later provided a microfilm copy of the *Locked Book* to the University, Wesencraft deliberately returned it to him, reasoning that it would not be available to students and researchers [10]. In a scathing 2000 letter to Vincent O'Neil (Marianne Foyster's adopted son), Wesencraft summarized his assessment of Hall: "I don’t know why [he] behaved so badly. He was a supreme egotist, inflated to the breaking point with self-importance and a determination to get his name and exploits at all costs before the public" [11]. Hall's 1978 publication of *Search for Harry Price* ultimately alienated Wesencraft permanently [12].

**Debunking the Louis Mayerling Hoax**
Wesencraft's unparalleled knowledge of the archives was instrumental in exposing the fraudulent book *We Faked the Ghosts of Borley Rectory*, written by Louis Mayerling (George Carter) in 2000. Wesencraft confirmed that in his forty-two years as curator, he had almost certainly met and conversed with every serious investigator of the Borley mystery, and he had exhaustively examined the files of Harry Price, Mrs. C. Ryan Baines, Eric Dingwall, and Trevor Hall [13, 14]. He definitively stated that there was never a single mention of a Louis Mayerling or a George Carter in any of those records, proving the book was a complete fabrication [13, 14].

**Personal Experiences and Later Life**
Wesencraft visited Borley many times over the years. He freely admitted to succumbing to the location's "charm" and "spell," though he noted he never personally experienced any psychic manifestations there, apart from an "extraordinary stillness which pervades the location" [15, 16]. 

He was an active participant in historical discussions of the case, engaging in lengthy correspondence with student Richard Lee-Van den Daele to aid his thesis on Price's works [17]. On January 18, 1981, Wesencraft spoke at a Ghost Club Special Meeting celebrating the centenary of Harry Price's birth [15, 18]. Later that year, on September 7, he addressed the Ghost Club at the Sesame Club, exhibiting items of special interest from the library [15]. 

Wesencraft wrote the Introduction for Edward Babbs's 2003 book, *Borley Rectory – The Final Analysis* [15, 19]. He was a close friend of Peter Underwood and was slated to write the Foreword for Underwood's book *Borley Postscript*. Sadly, just as sample chapters were sent to him for this task, Wesencraft was admitted to Glenfield Hospital in late November 2007 and died a few days later on December 3, 2007, at the age of 95 [1, 20].
