# V. C. Wall

**Vernon C. Wall** — reporter for the *Daily Mirror* whose series of articles in June 1929 effectively broke the story of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] to the British public, transforming a local legend into a national sensation [1]. He was the first investigator on the scene, arriving before [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and publishing his reports independently.

## First on the Scene

In early June 1929, the Rev. [[rev-eric-smith|Guy]] and [[mabel-smith|Mabel Smith]] wrote to the *Daily Mirror* asking to be put in touch with a psychical research society [2]. Sensing a news scoop, editor Alexander Campbell immediately dispatched Wall and a staff photographer to Borley; they arrived on **9 June 1929** [1, 2]. Wall's first article, "Ghost Visits to a Rectory," appeared on **10 June** [1]. Over the course of a week, he published six articles (10, 11, 12, 14, 15, and 17 June), chronicling tales of "headless coachmen and a nun" and dragging footsteps [1, 3].

Price was telephoned by the same editor on Tuesday, 11 June and arrived on Wednesday, 12 June — by which point Wall's reports were already in print. This sequence matters: Wall's coverage is independent of Price. His reports cannot have been shaped by Price's influence because Price had not yet visited.

## What Wall Reported

Wall's first dispatch reported: headless coachmen, a nun, an old-time coach drawn by two bay horses, and dragging footsteps in empty rooms. Specific incidents:

- [[rev-eric-smith|Rev. Eric Smith]] sat alone in a room hearing footsteps and swung a hockey stick at the source — it passed through empty air while the footsteps continued
- A servant girl brought from London left after two days having seen a nun in the wood
- A maid saw an old-fashioned coach on the lawn; brown horses; also saw a nun leaning over a gate
- The late [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] reportedly heard a coach driven by headless men while walking outside the Rectory at night

Wall also recorded the local legend as he found it among villagers — predating Price entirely.

## Wall's Personal Vigil and Reported Experiences

Wall claimed to have personally witnessed a **"mysterious light"** shining from an upstairs window in a disused wing of the house [4, 5]. After verifying with the Rector that no one was inside with a lamp, Wall deemed the appearance something "which simply cannot be explained" [5].

> **Contradiction:** Mabel Smith later claimed to have discovered the light was merely the reflection of passing trains [6, 7].

On the evening of 10 June, Wall and his photographer had a "terrible shock" when they "distinctly saw a white figure flitting about in the gloom" of the garden [8]. Upon closer inspection, they discovered the "apparition" was simply the Smiths' maid, [[mary-pearson|Mary Pearson]], engaging in a prank [8]. Wall honestly reported this anticlimactic event in his 11 June article, headlined "Midnight Apparition That Proved to Be a Maid" [8]. When [[harry-price|Harry Price]] later wrote *The Most Haunted House in England*, he deliberately omitted this debunking detail when summarising Wall's 11 June article — a suppression later heavily criticised by skeptical researchers [8].

## Investigation with Harry Price

Following Wall's initial reports, the *Mirror* contacted [[harry-price|Harry Price]], who arrived on 12 June. During this joint investigation, Wall was central to several major events:

- **The Nun's Walk Sighting:** At dusk on 12 June, Wall and Price kept watch near the large summer-house. Wall reported seeing a dark, shadowy figure moving along the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] towards the stream, writing: "**something certainly moved along the path on the other side of the lawn, and although I immediately ran across to investigate, it had vanished when I reached the spot**" [9, 10].
- **The Vase vs. Candlestick Contradiction:** Later that evening, a glass object was violently thrown down the stairs in the dark [11, 12]. In his 14 June article, Wall explicitly identified the smashed object as a **"red vase"** and defended Price against any suspicion of fraud, writing: **"Mr Price was the only person behind me and he could not have thrown the vase at such an angle to pass my head and hit the stove below"** [11]. This created a textual contradiction, as Price and [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] later insisted in their books that the object was a **"red glass candlestick"** [11, 13, 14].
- **The Blue Room Séance:** At 1:00 a.m. on 13 June, Wall participated in a séance in the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] where a mirror began producing answering "taps" [15, 16]. It was Wall who suggested that Price should ask if the communicating entity was the late [[harry-bull|Rev. Harry Bull]]: "I had hardly finished the name when three hurried taps came on the mirror, which meant an emphatic 'yes'" [17, 18].

## The Aftermath and Disappearance

Wall's sensational reporting caused chaos in the quiet village. By the end of the week, he filed his final story, "Shy 'Ghost' of Borley Rectory — 'Rowdy' Visitors," detailing how his articles had prompted an invasion of sightseers, motor-coach tours, and trespassers, forcing the Rector to call the police [19–21].

Following this chaotic week, **Wall never returned to Borley**, and no further accounts of his experiences were published [22]. He was not interviewed when the [[spr|Society for Psychical Research]] reopened the Borley case in the 1950s. [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] also attempted to track him down to obtain follow-up testimony but was entirely unsuccessful, leaving a permanent void regarding the firsthand observations of Price's dramatic first visit [22].

In a dramatised stage play script written about the hauntings by Donald Sinclair, Vernon C. Wall was fictionalised into a character named "Ted Burton, a young American newspaperman," serving a Watson-like role to explain the technical aspects of ghost-hunting to the audience [23].

## Significance

Wall's status as a pre-Price independent witness is central to the evidential debate. His reports confirm:
1. Villagers already knew and feared the haunting before Price arrived
2. The Nun, the coach, the footsteps, and the light anomaly were all reported by non-Price parties
3. [[rev-eric-smith|Eric Smith]] was willing to participate in informal tests despite not believing in ghosts
