# V. C. Wall

Journalist, staff reporter for an unnamed London daily newspaper. The first investigator on the scene at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in June 1929 — arriving before [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and publishing his reports independently.

## First on the Scene

Wall was sent to Borley over the weekend of 7–9 June 1929. His dispatches ran in his paper from Monday, 9 June 1929. Price was telephoned by the same editor on Tuesday, 11 June and arrived on Wednesday, 12 June — by which point Wall's reports were already in print.

This sequence matters: Wall's coverage is independent of Price. His reports cannot have been shaped by Price's influence because Price had not yet visited.

## What Wall Reported

Wall's first dispatch reported: headless coachmen, a nun, an old-time coach drawn by two bay horses, and dragging footsteps in empty rooms. Specific incidents:

- [[rev-eric-smith|Rev. Eric Smith]] sat alone in the room hearing footsteps and swung a hockey stick at the source — it passed through empty air while the footsteps continued
- A servant girl brought from London left after two days having seen a nun in the wood
- A maid saw an old-fashioned coach on the lawn; brown horses; also saw a nun leaning over a gate
- The late [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] reportedly heard a coach driven by headless men while walking outside the Rectory at night

Wall also recorded the local legend as he found it among villagers — predating Price entirely.

## Wall's Personal Vigil

Wall and a photographer kept watch in the wood. They observed a mysterious light shining through a window of a locked, disused wing; on investigation the room was empty and dark, though the light remained visible from outside. Rev. Smith placed a comparison lamp in a second window; the mystery light disappeared as they approached while Smith's lamp remained.

## Significance

Wall's status as a pre-Price independent witness is central to the evidential debate. His reports confirm:
1. Villagers already knew and feared the haunting before Price arrived
2. The Nun, the coach, the footsteps, and the light anomaly were all reported by non-Price parties
3. Eric Smith was willing to participate in informal tests despite not believing in ghosts

Price later reproduced Wall's complete dispatches verbatim in *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940) — see [[mhh-ch02]].
