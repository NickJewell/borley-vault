# H.F. Russell

Businessman; Acting Chairman and Manager in England and for all of Europe and the British Empire of a major cable company; connected with several other cable companies; educated in Rome, Florence, Brussels, and Louvain; spoke several languages. His son described him as "not in the habit of falling about."

## Thrown by a Poltergeist

On November 12, 1941, Russell visited [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] with two sons (then respectively Wing-Commander and Squadron-Leader in the RAF), prompted by reading *The Most Haunted House in England*. His sons entered the ruins ahead of him. As Russell was following, "despite my attempts to keep the vertical, I was dashed to the ground. I felt an unseen, unknown power trying to throw me down, in which it succeeded, and I landed in a pool of mud which necessitated the sending of some clothes to the cleaners."

The incident occurred near the entrance to the former stables — nearly opposite the main gate on the road to Borley Green. Russell described the sensation as "a force 'ejecting' me, as with the injunction 'Get out!'" He was certain of the location when Price sent him a plan.

[[harry-price|Price]] notes this is virtually unique in the Borley annals — ghosts rarely throw people. He compares it to a case recorded by Rev. Joseph Glanvill at Woodbridge, Suffolk (a few miles from Borley), where both a rector and a Dutch lieutenant were thrown into the middle of the street by an invisible force, the rector with a broken knee.

The day of the visit: November 12, 1941. Russell's letter to Price: November 15, 1941; follow-up with location details: December 7, 1941.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

Based on the Borley sources, **H. F. Russell** was a distinguished businessman who became the subject of one of the most unique and violent physical encounters in the history of the Borley Rectory hauntings. 

*(Note: He should not be confused with J.H. Russell, an Oxford undergraduate who investigated the Rectory in 1944, or Captain Russell, who purchased part of the Rectory grounds in 1946).*

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following biographical details, events, specific quotes, and correspondence regarding H. F. Russell's experience:

**Lesser-Known Biographical Details**
*   **Residence and Career:** He resided at **73 London Road, Chelmsford** [1]. He was a highly successful businessman who served as the Acting Chairman and Manager for a famous cable company, overseeing operations in England, the whole of Europe (except France), and the entire British Empire (except Canada) [1]. 
*   **Education and Family:** He was a highly educated man who had studied mainly in Rome, Florence, Brussels, and Louvain, and spoke several languages fluently [1]. He was the father of three sons, all of whom served as officers in the Royal Air Force [2]. 

**The Poltergeist Attack (November 12, 1941)**
Harry Price considered Russell's experience to be entirely unique in the annals of Borley because poltergeists are rarely reported to physically attack or harm people, especially in broad daylight [1]. 

On **Wednesday, November 12, 1941**, Russell visited the ruined Borley Rectory accompanied by two of his RAF officer sons (a Wing-Commander and a Squadron-Leader) [2-4]. While his sons went ahead and entered the ruined house, Russell followed about twenty yards behind them [3, 4]. 

Suddenly, Russell was seized by an invisible entity from behind and hurled face-down into a puddle of dirty water [5]. In a letter to Harry Price dated **November 15, 1941**, Russell described the startling daylight attack with the following specific quote:
> **"I was suddenly seized (so I imagine) and, despite my attempts to keep the vertical, was dashed to the ground. I felt an unseen, unknown power trying to throw me down, in which it succeeded, and I landed in a pool of mud which necessitated the sending of some clothes to the cleaners. I am not in the habit of falling about."** [3]

**Location of the Incident and the "Injunction"**
Harry Price sent Russell a plan of the Rectory and its grounds to pinpoint exactly where the physical assault occurred. In a follow-up letter on **December 7, 1941**, Russell clarified the location and the distinct psychological impression that accompanied the physical force:
> **"I was so flabbergasted by the event that it may have shaken me out of my usual calm... it was, I think, to the right of the plan you sent me and more in the entrance side of my incident, as I had the feeling that some force was ‘ejecting’ me, as with the injunction 'Get out!'"** [2]

The sources record slight variations in how researchers later described this exact spot: Russell himself referred to it as being **"more in the entrance to the farm"** [2, 6], while Harry Price noted it was near the entrance to the stable grounds, nearly opposite the main gate leading to Borley Green [7]. Later researcher Ivan Banks described the location as being **"near the gateway between the back of the Rectory and the stable cottage"** [5].
