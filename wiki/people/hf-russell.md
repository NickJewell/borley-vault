# H.F. Russell

Businessman; Acting Chairman and Manager in England and for all of Europe and the British Empire of a major cable company; also connected with several other cable companies. Educated mainly in Rome, Florence, Brussels, and Louvain; spoke several languages fluently. Resided at **73 London Road, Chelmsford** [1]. Father of three sons, all of whom served as officers in the Royal Air Force [2].

*(Note: Not to be confused with [[jh-russell-oxford|J. H. Russell]], the Oxford undergraduate who investigated the Rectory in 1944, or Captain Russell, who purchased part of the Rectory grounds in 1946.)*

## Thrown by a Poltergeist (November 12, 1941)

[[harry-price|Price]] considers Russell's experience to be **entirely unique** in the annals of Borley, because poltergeists are rarely reported to physically attack or harm people — especially in broad daylight [1].

On **Wednesday, November 12, 1941**, Russell visited the ruined [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] with two of his RAF officer sons (a Wing-Commander and a Squadron-Leader), prompted by reading *The Most Haunted House in England* [2–4]. His sons entered the ruins ahead of him. As Russell followed, roughly twenty yards behind:

> **"I was suddenly seized (so I imagine) and, despite my attempts to keep the vertical, was dashed to the ground. I felt an unseen, unknown power trying to throw me down, in which it succeeded, and I landed in a pool of mud which necessitated the sending of some clothes to the cleaners. I am not in the habit of falling about."** [3]

Russell wrote this letter to Price on **November 15, 1941**; he followed up with location details on **December 7, 1941**, after Price sent him a plan of the Rectory grounds:

> **"I was so flabbergasted by the event that it may have shaken me out of my usual calm... it was, I think, to the right of the plan you sent me and more in the entrance side of my incident, as I had the feeling that some force was 'ejecting' me, as with the injunction 'Get out!'"** [2]

The incident occurred near the entrance to the former stables — nearly opposite the main gate on the road to Borley Green. There are slight variations among researchers about the precise location: Russell himself referred to it as being "more in the entrance to the farm" [2, 6]; Price noted it was near the entrance to the stable grounds; [[ivan-banks|Ivan Banks]] described it as **"near the gateway between the back of the Rectory and the stable cottage"** [5].

Price compares this incident to a case recorded by Rev. Joseph Glanvill at Woodbridge, Suffolk (a few miles from Borley), where both a rector and a Dutch lieutenant were thrown into the middle of the street by an invisible force, the rector with a broken knee.

His son described him as "not in the habit of falling about" — corroborating Price's point that this was not an instance of clumsiness or accident.
