# Letty Knights

Roman Catholic woman in **Woodbridge**, Suffolk, who met [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] at St Thomas's Church and was ultimately left to adopt **[[john-knights|John Knights]]** (formerly John Fisher) when Marianne abandoned him in 1946.

## Meeting Marianne

Letty Knights first got to know Marianne at the Roman Catholic church in Woodbridge. She understood Marianne to be a "cultured and well-bred woman" who lived in Rendlesham with her husband Johnny Fisher and her father (in reality the ageing [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]], whom Marianne passed off as her father-in-law and later as her father). Letty thought Fisher "slightly mental" but a good helper with the bedridden old man.

## The Abandonment

In April or May 1946, [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] asked Letty if she would look after the boy **John** for a couple of days over the Whit weekend while she went to Ireland to deal with "her father's affairs." Letty agreed and took John in. When several more days passed with no word, she went to Marianne's house on Deben Avenue: it was empty. A letter eventually arrived, apparently posted from a ship, enclosing John's ration book but offering no explanation.

Letty consulted a solicitor and the police. She discovered that Marianne had gone to Canada and then America, deliberately abandoning her adopted children. Extradition was not applicable for abandoning children. Letty legally adopted the boy, who was now calling himself **John Knights**.

## Long Correspondence

Despite everything, Letty continued to correspond warmly with Marianne over the following decades, signing her letters "With fondest love." She kept Marianne informed of John's progress — including his ambitions for the priesthood. When investigators later traced Marianne's whereabouts via the Woodbridge area, it was from Letty's knowledge of the address in Rendlesham that they began their search.

In 1973, it was **John Knights** who gave [[wallace-tower|Wallace Tower]] his sister Adelaide's last known address in England when Wallace visited looking for her — the information that eventually led to Adelaide's reunion with her family.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Letty Knights** (often referred to as Mrs. Knights) was a grey-haired, bespectacled woman from Woodbridge who played a dramatic and emotional role in the aftermath of Marianne Foyster's life in England [1]. She met Marianne (then using the surname Fisher) at **St Thomas’s Roman Catholic church in Woodbridge** [1]. 

**Initial Impressions of the Fisher Household**
Letty Knights initially believed Marianne was a cultured, well-bred, only child of a Danish mother, who had been raised as a strict Catholic [1]. She believed Marianne lived with her retired Anglican clergyman father and her husband, Johnny Fisher, whom Letty considered "slightly mental" but nevertheless helpful to his father-in-law [1]. 

**The 1946 Abandonment of John Fisher**
Letty Knights' primary historical significance in the Borley literature centers around her taking in Marianne's adopted son, John [2]. After **Easter 1946**, Marianne visited Letty to ask a favour: she claimed she needed to travel to Ireland for a few days over the Whit weekend (half term) to deal with her late father’s affairs [2]. She asked Letty to care for the ten-year-old John, promising she would take her baby, Peter, with her [2]. Letty agreed, and the following week, Marianne dropped John off, saying a quick goodbye and leaving **"just as if she was going to post a letter"** [2, 3]. 

When several days passed with no word, a worried Letty took John to Marianne’s house at Deben Avenue in Martlesham, only to discover the house empty and the family vanished [4, 5]. Letty immediately went to the police [4]. The very next day, she received a letter from Marianne, posted from a ship, which contained John’s ration book but offered absolutely no explanation [4]. Letty consulted a solicitor who traced Marianne to Canada near the US border, but police advised her that child abandonment was a misdemeanor not subject to extradition [4]. Left with the boy—who had been abandoned with **"only the clothes he stood up in"**—Letty wrote to Somerset House for his birth certificate and legally adopted him herself [2, 4]. It was only through this abandonment to Letty's care that John finally learned he was adopted [6].

**Revelations of Abuse**
After taking him in, John confided in Letty about the life he had endured at the hands of his adoptive mother [7]. He recounted sadistic cruelty, explaining that Marianne would punish the children by holding their heads in buckets of cold water [7]. He also revealed that Marianne had "strange soldiers" billeted with her every night, leading Letty to the firm conclusion that Marianne was a prostitute [7]. An investigator noted that Letty was in tears recounting this, expressing deep worry for the baby that "cruel woman" had taken with her [7]. 

**A Major Post-War Contradiction**
The Borley sources record a striking contradiction regarding Letty Knights' later relationship with Marianne. When the investigator H.E. Pratt interviewed Letty in the 1950s, she explicitly told him that **she had "never seen or heard from Marianne since" the 1946 abandonment** [2]. However, the texts later reveal that **Letty actually continued to correspond with Marianne for years**, exchanging news and photographs of John and Vincent (the baby Peter) [8]. Despite her earlier tearful condemnation of Marianne's cruelty, Letty signed these letters **"With fondest love"** and even shared the news that John wanted to train for the priesthood, to which a thrilled Marianne managed to send money to help with his seminary education [7, 8].
