# Letty Knights

Roman Catholic woman in **Woodbridge**, Suffolk, who met [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] at St Thomas's Roman Catholic church in Woodbridge and was ultimately left to adopt **[[john-knights|John Knights]]** (formerly John Fisher) when Marianne abandoned him in 1946. A grey-haired, bespectacled woman, she is described as a caring and emotionally perceptive figure in the post-Borley biographical history.

## Meeting Marianne

Letty Knights initially believed Marianne to be a "cultured and well-bred woman" — a strict Catholic and only child of a Danish mother — who lived in Rendlesham with her husband Johnny Fisher and her father (in reality the ageing [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]], whom Marianne passed off as her father-in-law and later as her father). Letty thought Fisher "slightly mental" but a good helper with the bedridden old man.

## The 1946 Abandonment

After Easter 1946, Marianne visited Letty to ask a favour: she claimed she needed to travel to Ireland for a few days over the Whit weekend to deal with her late father's affairs, asking Letty to care for the ten-year-old [[john-knights|John]] and promising to take her baby, Peter, with her. Letty agreed. The following week, Marianne dropped John off, saying a quick goodbye and leaving **"just as if she was going to post a letter."**

When several days passed with no word, a worried Letty went to Marianne's house at Deben Avenue in Martlesham — only to discover the house empty and the family vanished. Letty immediately went to the police. The very next day she received a letter from Marianne, posted from a ship, which contained John's ration book but offered absolutely no explanation. Letty consulted a solicitor who traced Marianne to Canada near the US border, but police advised her that child abandonment was a misdemeanour not subject to extradition. Left with the boy — who had been abandoned with "only the clothes he stood up in" — Letty wrote to Somerset House for his birth certificate and legally adopted him herself. It was only through this abandonment that John finally learned he was adopted.

## Revelations of Abuse

After taking John in, he confided in Letty about the life he had endured at the hands of his adoptive mother. He recounted sadistic cruelty, explaining that Marianne would punish the children by holding their heads in buckets of cold water. He also revealed that Marianne had "strange soldiers" billeted with her every night, leading Letty to the firm conclusion that Marianne was a prostitute. An investigator noted that Letty was in tears recounting this, expressing deep worry for the baby that "cruel woman" had taken with her.

## Long Correspondence and a Post-War Contradiction

Despite everything, Letty continued to correspond warmly with Marianne over the following decades, signing her letters "With fondest love." She kept Marianne informed of John's progress — including his ambitions for the priesthood, to which a thrilled Marianne managed to send money to help with his seminary education.

> **Contradiction:** When the investigator H. E. Pratt interviewed Letty in the 1950s, she explicitly told him that she had "never seen or heard from Marianne since" the 1946 abandonment. However, the sources later reveal that Letty actually continued to correspond with Marianne for years, exchanging news and photographs of John and Vincent (the baby Peter).

When investigators later traced Marianne's whereabouts via the Woodbridge area, it was from Letty's knowledge of the address in Rendlesham that they began their search.
