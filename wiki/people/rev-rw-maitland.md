# Rev. R. W. Maitland

Vicar of Darsham, Suffolk. Published an "explanatory theory" of the Borley hauntings in the journal *Light* on January 23, 1941. His theory — the "psychic saturation" or "absorbed power" hypothesis — attributes the hauntings primarily to the accumulated psychic energy of the Bull family over nearly seventy years.

## The Psychic Saturation Theory

Maitland begins from [[harry-price|Price]]'s own suggestion that the house might be "saturated with emanations" of past occupants, and develops it substantially. His thesis rests on the fact that [[borley-rectory]] was brand new when [[henry-bull|Rev. Henry Bull]] moved in — the house had no pre-existing psychic history. But Henry Bull was himself "a psychic" and filled the house with his family's psychic power. A large psychic family (fourteen or seventeen children) lived in the Rectory for nearly seventy years, continuously infusing it with psychic energy, which accumulated over time.

In Maitland's own words:

> *"My theory is this; the house was built and immediately lived in — this is an important point — by a man who undoubtedly was a psychic. Very likely, his family, a large one, inherited his powers — some certainly did..."*

He argued that the building became saturated with their emanations, but crucially that this residual psychic power had no consciousness of its own:

> *"It is not the 'persisting remnants of egos' or any intelligence of itself. It remained for those living in the house to use that power as their minds or subconscious minds desired."*

On this reading, the later inhabitants — particularly [[marianne-foyster|Mrs. Foyster]], whom Maitland regards as "a very potent" Sensitive — did not merely witness phenomena; they *produced* them, drawing unconsciously on the reservoir of accumulated psychic power stored in the house. The nun, the coach, and the sounds are not independent entities but "veridical" experiences: "there is an external origin of an internal vision." Something outside the percipient *causes* them to see such things — but that something is the house's stored psychic charge, not a separate spirit.

## The Flying Objects Problem

Maitland acknowledges the problem: this framework struggles to account for physically objective phenomena — the bottles, the apported coat, the gold ring. He argues the same "psychic power" that produced visions could also produce telekinetic effects, citing the analogy of stage demonstrations of telekinesis.

## Assessment

[[mr-w-harrison|Mr. W. Harrison]] replied to Maitland in *Light* (April 24, 1941), arguing that Maitland's theory was inconsistent with the facts: Borley's apparitions were of figures from periods predating the Bull family, often unknown to the witnesses and later verified from historical records — not subjective creations of the percipients' imaginations.

> **Contradiction:** The debate between Maitland and Harrison represents a clean statement of the two main interpretive poles: "place memory" (Maitland) vs. "independent surviving entities" (Harrison).
