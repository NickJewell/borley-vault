# Prof. John Gerald Taylor

(Born 1931) Professor of Mathematics at King's College, University of London. Author of several books including *Black Holes: The End of the Universe* (1973) and *Science and the Supernatural* (1980). Interested in scientifically recording paranormal activity.

## Borley Church Investigation

Taylor visited [[borley-church]] and conducted experiments with a technical assistant. Their equipment included a magnetometer — a machine to record the strength and direction of magnetic fields — and other sophisticated apparatus.

The visit was not very rewarding overall. They did experience "sensations of coldness and unease that they could not explain" and some unexpected sounds of movement they thought might be due to movement of the church foundations. At the end they decided that no psychic force or presence manifested itself, but felt that more work of this type could well be rewarding.

He took part in an hour-long radio programme with [[peter-underwood]] (see Chronology, 31 July 1976). A full account of the Borley visit is included in Underwood's *Hauntings* (see [[borley-bibliography|Borley Bibliography]]).

## Significance

Taylor's investigation represents the kind of systematic scientific approach that [[anthony-cornell]] had proposed in 1954 (before his investigation was cancelled). The magnetometer work at Borley Church is consistent with the [[ley-lines-at-borley|ley-lines hypothesis]] explored by [[edward-babbs]] in the *Final Analysis* — both approaches attempt to find measurable physical correlates for the reported phenomena. Taylor found no positive results, but his own observation that "more work of this type could be rewarding" is significant coming from a Professor of Mathematics.

**Prof. John Gerald Taylor** (born 1931) was a Professor of Mathematics at King’s College, University of London, who harbored a strong interest in the scientific recording of paranormal activity [1]. He was also the author of several books, including *_Black Holes: The End of the Universe_* (1973) and *_Science and the Supernatural_* (1980) [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following specific events, dates, and conclusions regarding his involvement in the case:

**The June 1976 Investigation**
In **early June 1976**, Professor Taylor and a technical research assistant visited Borley Church to conduct sound-recording and other scientific experiments [1, 2]. To assess the environment, they brought sophisticated apparatus, most notably a **magnetometer**, which was used to record the strength and direction of magnetic fields inside the church [1]. 

**Results and Experiences**
While the investigation was ultimately deemed "not very rewarding," Taylor and his assistant did record a few anomalies [1]. During their time in the church, they experienced sudden **"sensations of coldness and unease"** that they were unable to explain [1]. They also heard some **"unexpected sounds of movement,"** though they rationally theorized that these noises might simply be due to the shifting of the church's physical foundations [1]. 

At the end of the investigation, Taylor and his assistant decided that **"no psychic force or presence manifested itself"** during their time there, but they nonetheless felt that **"more work of this type could well be rewarding"** [1]. A complete account of his visit was later documented in Peter Underwood's book _Hauntings_ [1].

**The BBC Broadcast**
Shortly after his site visit, Taylor participated in a one-hour BBC World Service radio programme entitled *_Ghosts_*, which was broadcast on **July 31, 1976** (and repeated on August 1) [1, 3]. During this programme, Taylor specifically discussed the recording experiments he had carried out at Borley, appearing alongside Peter Underwood, the S.P.R. President Prof. Arthur Ellison, and Dr. Jonathan Miller [3].
