# John May (Bury St Edmunds)

An investigator from Bury St Edmunds who had been familiar with the tales of the Borley ghosts for many years [1]. May is notable in the Borley literature for experiencing both dramatic poltergeist activity and auditory phenomena during site visits in 1946 and 1947, which he reported in detail to [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]]. He is referred to in later texts as "the late John May," indicating he died before the publication of some contemporary source volumes [2].

## July 1946 — Levitating Stick

On a hot, sultry July afternoon in 1946, May cycled to Borley from Redgrave. He stood in the shade of the tree where [[the-nun|the Nun]] was said to materialise, then moved into a sunlit patch near some tall bushes. There he became aware of "a cold wave of air" — not a breeze, but "the sticky, damp, raw cold, that one would expect to meet on entering a deep underground tomb." Then:

> "Without warning, and very suddenly... a stout stick rose seemingly unaided from the ground and waved itself about six inches from my nose. It waved backwards and forwards for several inches, then soared away in an arc and fell some distance away among the shrubs."

May saw absolutely no one on or near the Rectory grounds to account for the movement [1]. Reacting to the flying stick, he recited a specific prayer of exorcism from an old manuscript he had found in Rome during the Second World War, which he noted he had used successfully there [1–3]:

> **"Oh unquiet spirit, who at thy release from the contagion of the flesh choosest to remain earthbound and haunt this spot, go thy way, rejoicing that the prayers of the faithful shall follow thee, that thou mayest enjoy everlasting rest and peace, and at the end mayest find thy rightful place at the Throne of Grace, Amen."** [3]

May noted that this impromptu exorcism had "no noticeable effect at Borley," and the unexplained incidents on the site continued unabated [2, 3].

## May 1947 — Earlier Letter

An earlier letter from May (dated April 1950, recounting his visit to the Borley site in early May 1947) described the exorcism prayer and the stick incident from the previous year. This report was sent to Peter Underwood after Underwood had made contact with May.

## September 1947 — Night Vigil at the Church

Because May felt a "ghost might be extremely difficult to see in the hard light of day," he returned to Borley on **15 September 1947** and parked his bicycle against the stone wall of the churchyard at 10:30 p.m. [4, 5]. Wearing crêpe-soled shoes to remain quiet, he sat in the church porch to wait [4]. He noted the warm, windless night, the dim outlines of the yew trees, and the occasional hoot of an owl; he briefly dozed off at 11:43 p.m. [4].

He was jerked awake at **12:16 a.m.** by the distinct sound of the churchyard gate latch clicking [4]. May then heard a sequence of impossible sounds: footsteps passing along the path, pausing near a yew bush, then continuing — though there was no figure to be seen. "I sensed someone passing me, there was a chilliness in the air, and I felt a slight pressure. Whatever it was, I knew and felt that it was essentially EVIL." [5]

He then heard what sounded like a key in the lock, the creak of door hinges, the door close — and then soft, atonal chords from the organ. The music continued until he left at 2:30 a.m.; he never heard the door open again [5]. On a subsequent visit to examine the organ, May remarked: "rather a mean instrument for such an important visitor as a ghost."
