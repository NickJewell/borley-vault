# John May (Bury St Edmunds)

Of Bury St Edmunds. May was an unconventional figure who claimed some experience in exorcising unquiet spirits and had been familiar with the Borley hauntings for many years. He made several recorded visits to the Borley site in 1946 and 1947.

## July 1946 — Levitating Stick

On a hot, sultry July afternoon in 1946, May cycled to Borley from Redgrave. He stood in the shade of the tree where the [[the-nun|Nun]] was said to materialise. Moving into a sunlit patch near some tall bushes, he became aware of "a cold wave of air" — not a breeze, but "the sticky, damp, raw cold, that one would expect to meet on entering a deep underground tomb." Then:

> "Without warning, and very suddenly... a stout stick rose seemingly unaided from the ground and waved itself about six inches from my nose. It waved backwards and forwards for several inches, then soared away in an arc and fell some distance away among the shrubs."

May then said a prayer of exorcism he described as taken from an old manuscript found in Rome during the war, asking the "unquiet spirit" to depart in peace.

## September 1947 — Night Vigil at the Church

On 15 September 1947, May spent the night at [[borley-church|Borley Church]] porch. From his own account:

> At 12:16 am he heard the churchyard gate latch click. Footsteps came up the path, paused near a yew bush, and continued — but there was no figure to be seen. "I sensed someone passing me, there was a chilliness in the air, and I felt a slight pressure. Whatever it was, I knew and felt that it was essentially EVIL."

He then heard the sound of a key in the lock, the creak of door hinges, the door close — and then soft, atonal chords from the organ. The music continued until he left at 2:30 am; he never heard the door open again. On a subsequent visit to the church he examined the organ: "rather a mean instrument for such an important visitor as a ghost."

## Earlier Visit — May 1947

An earlier letter from May (dated April 1950) described visiting the Borley site in early May 1947. This report, recounting the exorcism prayer and the stick incident from the previous year, was sent to Peter Underwood after Underwood had made contact with May.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**John May** was an investigator and resident of Bury St Edmunds who had been familiar with the tales of the Borley ghosts for many years [1]. He is notable in the Borley literature for corresponding with Peter Underwood and providing detailed, first-hand reports of both dramatic poltergeist activity and auditory phenomena he experienced during site visits in 1946 and 1947. He is referred to in later texts as "the late John May," indicating he passed away prior to the publication of some of the contemporary source volumes [2].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific events, dates, quotes, and lesser-known details regarding his involvement:

**The July 1946 Visit and the "Stout Stick"**
During a visit to the Rectory site on a hot summer day in **July 1946**, May reported experiencing a sudden, chilling physical manifestation [1, 2]. While looking at the Rectory site, he was hit by a "cold wave of air" [1]. Simultaneously, he witnessed a stout stick rise seemingly unaided from the ground [1]. The stick waved backwards and forwards in front of his face before soaring away in an arc and falling to the ground some distance away [1]. May noted that he saw absolutely no one on or near the Rectory grounds to account for the movement [1].

**The Roman Exorcism Prayer**
Reacting to the flying stick, May decided to perform an exorcism [1, 2]. He recited a specific prayer taken from an old manuscript that he had found in Rome, Italy, during the Second World War (which he noted he had used successfully there) [1-3]. The specific quote of the prayer he used at Borley was:
> **"Oh unquiet spirit, who at thy release from the contagion of the flesh choosest to remain earthbound and haunt this spot, go thy way, rejoicing that the prayers of the faithful shall follow thee, that thou mayest enjoy everlasting rest and peace, and at the end mayest find thy rightful place at the Throne of Grace, Amen."** [3]

Unfortunately, May noted that this impromptu exorcism had absolutely "no noticeable effect at Borley," and the unexplained incidents on the site continued unabated [2, 3].

**The Night Vigil of September 15–16, 1947**
Because May felt that a **"ghost might be extremely difficult to see in the hard light of day,"** he decided to return to Borley to conduct a night-time vigil [4]. He arrived on **September 15, 1947**, and parked his bicycle against the stone wall of the churchyard at 10:30 p.m. [4, 5]. Wearing crêpe-soled shoes to remain quiet, he sat down on a seat in the church porch to wait [4]. 

In a detailed report sent to Peter Underwood (in April 1950), May described the atmosphere of the warm, windless night, noting the dim outlines of the yew trees, the rustling of nesting birds, and the occasional hoot of an owl [1, 4]. He recorded looking at his watch at 11:43 p.m. before briefly dozing off [4].

He was suddenly jerked awake at **12:16 a.m.** by the distinct sound of the churchyard gate latch clicking [4]. May then reported hearing a sequence of impossible sounds: he heard footsteps passing along the path, the sound of the church door unlocking and opening, and finally, soft music playing from the church organ [5]. Despite hearing this progression of noises, May saw absolutely no one there to account for them [5].
