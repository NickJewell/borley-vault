# Ian Geoffrey William Shaw (Greenwood) (19 April 1915 – 1986)

Born Larne, Northern Ireland; died Aylesbury, Buckinghamshire, 1986. [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster's]] natural son by her first marriage to [[harold-greenwood|Harold Gifford Greenwood]]. His bitterness towards his mother became a major source of material for [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]]'s investigation of the Foyster incumbency — and hence for the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]].

## Origins and Concealed Parentage

Ian was born on **April 19, 1915**, near Larne in Northern Ireland [1, 2]. He was the product of Marianne's first marriage to a young clerk named [[harold-greenwood|Harold Gifford Greenwood]] [3, 4]. Only six weeks after Ian's birth, his father abandoned the family and disappeared, eventually being rumoured to have gone to Australia or New Zealand [2].

To conceal Marianne's early, failed marriage, Ian was raised by his grandparents [[annie-shaw|William and Annie Shaw]] and passed off to the world as Marianne's younger brother [5, 6]. He used his mother's maiden name, Shaw, for his entire life [6].

> **Contradiction:** A major contradiction exists regarding whether the Rev. Lionel Foyster knew Ian's true identity. In a 1958 interview, Marianne insisted that Lionel "never did" know Ian was his stepson [7, 8]. However, in 1978, she contradicted herself, claiming Lionel was "completely aware of her marital status" and the existence of her baby before they married [8, 9].

Marianne sailed for Canada in 1922 to marry [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]]; Ian stayed behind, raised by the Shaws.

## Time in Canada (1925–1930)

In 1925, Ian was sent to join Lionel and Marianne in [[salmonhurst-new-brunswick|Salmonhurst, New Brunswick]], Canada, ostensibly because the air would be good for his asthma [7, 10]. Lionel funded the boy's education, and Ian proved to be an excellent student, winning a scholarship to a strict local school [11, 12].

While in Canada, Ian inadvertently exposed one of his mother's grandiose fabrications. While Lionel was away in England, Marianne began receiving expensive gifts from a Major [[major-forbes-mitchell|Forbes-Mitchell]] after introducing herself as the aristocratic "Mrs. von Kiergraff" [13]. The deception collapsed when Ian smeared gun-oil on his jacket while inspecting the Major's gun collection; the Major found a letter in the pocket bearing Ian's real name and address and reported Marianne to the police [13].

When the Foysters returned to England to take the living at Borley in 1930, Ian was left behind in Canada to finish his schooling [11, 14].

## Residence at Borley Rectory (Early 1933)

Ian arrived at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in January 1933, staying for approximately three months [11, 15]. Aged eighteen, he was described as high-spirited, Irish, and "full of fun" [16]. Marianne recalled that he "led the world a merry chase," singing at the top of his lungs and hunting for buried treasure in the Rectory gardens [16, 17]. During his treasure hunts, Ian discovered the remains of a tunnel that went under the road by the churchyard and traced it to the cellar; the entrance conveniently caved in shortly after [16, 17].

He did not get on well with the ailing, elderly Lionel Foyster [15, 18]. Ian frequently clashed with the Rector, ringing church bells and behaving too boisterously for the sick man to tolerate [18].

## The Bell Wires and Poltergeist Deceptions

Ian's most significant contribution to the physical evidence of the Borley haunting was his discovery of a string concealed in the ivy in the Rectory courtyard, specifically outside the "knife and boot room" [15, 16]. This string passed into the house and was attached to exposed bell-wires; when pulled, it rang all the servant bells simultaneously, creating a classic "poltergeist" effect [20, 21]. Ian told [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] that when he proudly showed his mother the string, Marianne sharply told him to "shut up" [22]. Marianne later admitted Ian found the wire, but insisted she simply meant nothing by the remark, asserting it was just a broken wire [23, 24].

Ian also later confessed to investigators that he had participated in the haunting himself, pulling the bells "for a lark" with the full knowledge of both Marianne and Lionel [25]. On one occasion, Ian and Marianne teamed up to play a prank on [[francois-darles|François d'Arles]]: they constructed a crude cardboard torso, draped it in a sheet painted with luminous paint, and rigged it on pulleys to successfully terrify the lodger [23–25].

## Relationship with François d'Arles

Because Ian spoke French, he became good friends with the Foysters' lodger, [[francois-darles|François d'Arles]] (Frank Pearless) [15]. According to Ian, d'Arles openly confessed to the teenager that he and Marianne were faking the poltergeist phenomena, and that they had used the visit of the [[marks-tey-spiritualist-circle|Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle]] as an excuse to stop the haunting because things were getting out of hand [15, 27]. Furthermore, d'Arles told Ian that his mother was a "sexual maniac" [15, 28]. Ian also learned that d'Arles used the secret knowledge that Ian was Marianne's son (not her brother) to blackmail Marianne into continuing their sexual affair [29].

Following his stay at Borley, Ian moved with Marianne and d'Arles to Wimbledon, where the trio lived in a flat above the *[[jonquille-et-cie|Jonquille et Cie]]* flower shop [11, 30].

## Later Life and Marriages

Disliking London, Ian returned to Northern Ireland after about eighteen months to live with his grandparents [18, 31]. He worked as a builder and married, though this first marriage ended in divorce [11]. In 1942, he returned to London to work on bomb-site clearance during World War II [32]. Following the war, on **December 11, 1946**, he married 27-year-old Margaret Kearney [11].

Ian died in Aylesbury, Buckinghamshire, in **1986** [1, 33]. He kept his mother's secrets so well hidden from his own family that, at the time of his death, his widow Margaret still believed William and Annie Shaw were Ian's biological parents, completely unaware of his connection to Borley Rectory [34].

## Role in the SPR Investigation

Following the publication of the highly sceptical 1956 *Borley Report*, Ian contacted [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] [35, 36]. Ian harboured a deep resentment towards his mother for abandoning him as a baby, regarding her with "loathing and contempt," and willingly provided Hall with highly damaging information [32, 37]. He told Hall that **"the whole business was founded on a mass of falsehoods and deception"** [36]. He accused Marianne and d'Arles of faking the phenomena and deliberately persecuting the ailing Rector [38].

Despite his cooperation with the investigators, Ian wrote to Marianne in America to warn her that Hall and the SPR were tracking her down [36, 39, 40]. A desperate Marianne wrote back:

> **"Kid, for the love of God, help me. Tell me - do you think if I wrote to the people and told them I didn't do it and that all this about my so called love affairs is nothing to do with ghosts... they would stop persecuting me? What shall I do. Tell me, Ian."** [41]

In her panic, she even confessed: **"Shall I commit suicide? I have contemplated it so often."** [42]

> **Caveat:** "A great deal of Shaw's recollections (much of which are uncorroborated) may have been coloured by his personal feelings for his mother whom he clearly resented; also Hall's reporting of these recollections are similarly coloured by his apathy towards the paranormal and his desire to totally debunk the Borley haunting." (The Companion)

Marianne's later adopted son, [[vincent-oneil|Vince O'Neil]], noted that Ian believed his mother's handwriting did not actually match the famous Borley wall writings [43].
