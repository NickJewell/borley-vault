# Ian Geoffrey William Shaw (Greenwood) (19 April 1915 – 1986)

Born Larne, Northern Ireland; died Aylesbury, Buckinghamshire, 1986. [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster's]] natural son by her first marriage. His bitterness towards his mother became a major source of material for [[trevor-hall]]'s investigation of the Foyster incumbency — and hence for the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]].

## Origins

Marianne married Harold Gifford Greenwood at Sheriff's warrant (Scotland) 8 June 1914, and again at St Anne's Cathedral, Belfast, 12 November 1914, when she was 15 (giving her age as 17). Ian was born 19 April 1915. Six weeks after the birth, Greenwood deserted the family under pressure from his mother. Ian was raised by Marianne's parents William and Annie Shaw, and used the Shaw surname for the rest of his life. Marianne sailed for Canada in 1922 to marry [[lionel-foyster]]; Ian stayed behind.

Ian joined the Foysters in Canada in 1925 for seven years of schooling. He rejoined Marianne at [[borley-rectory]] in late 1932 for a three-month stay. He then moved to Wimbledon with Marianne and [[francois-darles|d'Arles]], living above the flower shop (see [[jonquille-et-cie]]). After the shop failed he returned to Northern Ireland, married twice.

## Role in the Borley Controversy

In 1956, shortly after the SPR published the Borley Report, Ian made contact with [[trevor-hall]] and offered information about his mother. He had "clearly hated his mother for 'abandoning him as a baby'" (the Companion's assessment) and used this opportunity to attack her. He met Hall at the Waldorf Hotel in London and the information flowed freely.

Ian told Hall:
- Details of Marianne's relationships in Canada and at Borley (including her affair with d'Arles)
- That he had himself played tricks on d'Arles at Borley — including creating a makeshift phantom from a bed sheet and rigging bell wires to ring
- That Marianne and d'Arles had faked phenomena, using the visit of the [[marks-tey-circle|Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle]] to bring the "haunting" to a convenient end
- His suspicion that d'Arles was the father of the adopted baby [[john-emery|John Emery]] who died at Borley

Hall used Ian's material to locate Marianne in America, ultimately leading to the [[robert-swanson|Swanson interviews]] and Hall's unpublished *Marianne Foyster of Borley Rectory* manuscript.

> **Caveat:** "A great deal of Shaw's recollections (much of which are uncorroborated) may have been coloured by his personal feelings for his mother whom he clearly resented; also Hall's reporting of these recollections are similarly coloured by his apathy towards the paranormal and his desire to totally debunk the Borley haunting." (The Companion)

## Irony

Ian's widow Margaret was still under the impression when he died in 1986 that William and Annie Shaw were Ian's parents. He had kept the whole secret — including his connection to Borley Rectory and his role in supplying the SPR's investigators — from his own family.

He warned Marianne in a letter (May 1956) that Hall intended to contact her. Her reply was poignant: "I am grateful to you beyond belief for your kindness to me and I am not a bad woman. I have been a driven woman."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Ian Geoffrey William Shaw (Greenwood)** is a crucial, if highly biased, behind-the-scenes witness in the Borley Rectory saga. As the natural son of Marianne Foyster, his presence at the Rectory and his subsequent adult testimonies to the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) provided some of the most damaging allegations against his mother regarding the fraudulent nature of the "Foyster phenomena." 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, events, quotes, and controversies regarding his life and involvement:

**Early Life and Concealed Parentage**
Ian was born on April 19, 1915, near Larne in Northern Ireland [1, 2]. He was the product of Marianne’s first marriage to a young clerk named Harold Gifford Greenwood [3, 4]. Only six weeks after Ian’s birth, his father abandoned the family, eventually disappearing to Australia or New Zealand [2]. 

To conceal Marianne’s early, failed marriage, Ian was raised by his grandparents (William and Annie Shaw) and passed off to the world as Marianne's younger brother [5, 6]. He went by his mother's maiden name, Shaw, for his entire life [6]. A major contradiction exists regarding whether Marianne's subsequent husband, the Rev. Lionel Foyster, knew Ian’s true identity. In 1958, Marianne insisted that Lionel "never did" know Ian was his stepson [7, 8]. However, in 1978, she contradicted herself, claiming Lionel was "completely aware of her marital status" and the existence of her baby before they married [8, 9].

**Time in Canada and the "Gun-Oil" Incident**
In 1925, Ian was sent to join Lionel and Marianne in Salmonhurst, New Brunswick, Canada, ostensibly because the air would be good for his asthma [7, 10]. Lionel Foyster funded the boy's education, and Ian proved to be an excellent student, winning a scholarship to a strict local school [11, 12]. 

While in Canada, Ian inadvertently exposed one of his mother's grandiose fabrications. While Lionel was away in England, Marianne began receiving expensive gifts from a Major Forbes-Mitchell after introducing herself as the aristocratic "Mrs. von Kiergraff" [13]. The deception collapsed when Ian smeared gun-oil on his jacket while inspecting the Major's gun collection [13]. The gallant Major insisted on having the jacket cleaned, found a letter in the pocket bearing Ian's real name and address, and reported Marianne to the police, ending the relationship in scandal [13]. 

When the Foysters returned to England to take the living at Borley in 1930, Ian was left behind in Canada to finish his schooling [11, 14]. 

**Residence at Borley Rectory (Early 1933)**
Ian finally arrived at Borley Rectory in January 1933, staying for approximately three months [11, 15]. At eighteen years old, he was described as high-spirited, Irish, and "full of fun" [16]. Marianne later recalled that he "led the world a merry chase," singing at the top of his lungs and hunting for buried treasure in the Rectory gardens [16, 17]. During his treasure hunts, Ian discovered the remains of a tunnel that went under the road by the churchyard and traced it to the cellar; the entrance conveniently caved in shortly after [16, 17].

He did not get on well with the ailing, elderly Lionel Foyster [15, 18]. Ian frequently clashed with the Rector, ringing church bells and behaving too boisterously for the sick man to tolerate [18]. 

**The Bell Wires and Poltergeist Deceptions**
Ian's most significant contribution to the physical evidence of the Borley haunting was his discovery of a string concealed in the ivy in the Rectory courtyard [19-21]. This string passed into the house and was attached to exposed bell-wires; when pulled, it rang all the servant bells simultaneously, creating a classic "poltergeist" effect [20, 21]. When Ian proudly showed his mother the string, he claimed Marianne sharply told him to "shut up" [22]. Marianne later admitted Ian found the wire, but insisted she simply meant nothing by the remark, asserting it was just a broken wire in the "knife and boot room" [23, 24].

Ian also later confessed to investigators that he had participated in the haunting himself, pulling the bells "for a lark" with the full knowledge of both Marianne and Lionel [25]. He also played minor practical jokes on his mother, running in and out of doors to startle her [26].

**Relationship with François d'Arles**
Because Ian spoke French, he became good friends with the Foysters' lodger, François d'Arles (Frank Pearless) [15]. According to Ian, d'Arles openly confessed to the teenager that he and Marianne were faking the poltergeist phenomena [15, 27]. Furthermore, d'Arles told Ian that his mother was a "sexual maniac" [15, 28]. Ian also learned that d'Arles used the secret knowledge that Ian was Marianne's son (not her brother) to blackmail Marianne into continuing their sexual affair [29]. 

Following his stay at Borley, Ian moved with Marianne and d'Arles to Wimbledon, where the trio lived in a flat above the *Jonquille et Cie* flower shop [11, 30]. 

**Later Life and Marriages**
Disliking London, Ian returned to Northern Ireland after about eighteen months to live with his grandparents [18, 31]. He worked as a builder and married, though this first marriage ended in divorce [11]. In 1942, he returned to London to work on bomb-site clearance during World War II [32]. Following the war, on December 11, 1946, he married 27-year-old Margaret Kearney [11]. 

Ian died in Aylesbury, Buckinghamshire, in 1986 [1, 33]. He kept his mother's secrets so well hidden from his own family that, at the time of his death, his widow Margaret still believed William and Annie Shaw were Ian's biological parents, completely unaware of his connection to Borley Rectory [34].

**Role in the S.P.R. Investigation and Quotes**
Following the publication of the highly skeptical 1956 *Borley Report*, Ian contacted investigator Trevor Hall [35, 36]. Ian harbored a deep resentment towards his mother for abandoning him as a baby, regarding her with "loathing and contempt," and he willingly provided Hall with highly damaging information to assassinate her character [32, 37]. He told Hall that **"the whole business was founded on a mass of falsehoods and deception"** [36]. He accused Marianne and d'Arles of faking the phenomena and deliberately persecuting the ailing Rector [38]. 

Despite his cooperation with the investigators, Ian wrote to Marianne in America to warn her that Trevor Hall and the S.P.R. were tracking her down [36, 39, 40]. A desperate Marianne wrote back, begging her son for advice:
> **"Kid, for the love of God, help me. Tell me - do you think if I wrote to the people and told them I didn't do it and that all this about my so called love affairs is nothing to do with ghosts... they would stop persecuting me? What shall I do. Tell me, Ian."** [41]

In her panic over the impending exposure, she even confessed to Ian: **"Shall I commit suicide? I have contemplated it so often."** [42]. 

*Note: Because Ian's testimony was fueled by an intense personal hatred of his mother, researchers note that his uncorroborated claims about her character and the faking of the phenomena must be treated with significant caution [10, 37].*
