# Mrs Mabel Smith

Wife of [[rev-eric-smith|Rev. G. Eric Smith]]; resident at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] October 1928 – July 1929. The source of one of the most glaring testimony contradictions in the entire case.

## 1929 Testimony (during residency)

- Reported "sibilant whisperings," mysterious lights, shadowy figures, keys moving, bells — indicating she found events genuinely frightening
- Prepared an "excellent supper" for Price and described him warmly (per Price)
- Her husband wrote to Price and Glanville for years afterwards in friendly terms; a 1937 letter invites Price to visit

## 1945 Testimony (*Church Times* letter)

Signed a letter stating: "neither my husband nor myself believed the house haunted by anything else but rats and local superstition." Said they left because of the building's dilapidated condition, not anything supernatural.

## 1949 Signed Statement to the SPR (spr-iv)

The SPR investigators ([[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], [[trevor-hall]]) visited Mrs Smith on 1 July 1949. Her signed statement — prepared from notes, sent back for corrections, and signed again — provides far greater detail than her press letters. Key elements:

**All phenomena occurred only when Price was present:** "I cannot explain these happenings while Mr Price was present; but such things had never occurred before he appeared, and we knew he was an expert conjurer and had our suspicions as well as being puzzled." When [[lucie-kaye]] visited alone, nothing paranormal occurred at all.

**The water-to-ink trick:** At a dinner with other guests, a glass of water changed to ink "while Mr Price was sitting next to the user of the glass." A guest believed Price had done it — "but remarked 'He wasn't a man they would like to offend' and no such suggestion was made openly." This same trick occurred at the Foysters' in October 1931 — the only two occasions it ever happened at Borley, both when Price was present and beside the glass.

**Pebbles thrown at her:** "Once when Mr Price was in a room with me, little pebbles whizzed past me suddenly. I was astonished. Such a phenomenon had never happened before, nor anything like it. So I could not help thinking perhaps Mr Price had thrown them."

**The red cross and pencil:** "We did not consider it a joke when we found a large red cross had been drawn on our bedroom door; especially as a red pencil was seen next day in the vicinity of Mr Price."

**Mary Pearson's admission:** [[mary-pearson|Mary]] told Mrs Smith openly after Price had gone: "It was that man threw that coin — so I threw some sugar." Mary had herself produced some "phenomena" in competition with what she suspected Price of doing.

**The séance taps:** "Certainly knocks came from the mirror, but I often wish I had examined it closer. I think that 'tricks' were being played all around us."

**The Smiths did not believe in the haunting:** "I have no reason at all to think Borley was haunted."

**On Price's book:** "My impression on reading the volume was utter astonishment at the clever mixture of legend, truth, phantasy, and disregard of the intellects of intelligent people; the facts are so twisted."

**Title suggestion:** "I think the title should read 'The Most Maligned House in England'."

> **Contradiction:** [[ethel-bull]] wrote in 1954: "All nonsense Mrs Smith making out they were not frightened, she used to shriek with fright."

## Nuance in the 1929 Correspondence

The SPR noted that Mrs Smith's letters during 1929–30, while at the rectory and immediately after, do not perfectly match her 1949 testimony. Mr Smith wrote "Borley is undoubtedly haunted, though we have tried to dispossess ourselves of the idea" (November 1929). Mrs Smith described the rectory as "creepy." In 1937 they eagerly visited the rectory again with Glanville and participated in a planchette séance. The SPR argues this reflects a genuine psychological ambivalence at the time — their final settled view (Borley not haunted) only crystallised later — rather than making Mrs Smith's 1949 evidence unreliable.

## Possible Motives for the Reversal

- Price refused to help her publish her novel *Murder at the Parsonage* (based on the Borley hauntings)
- She may have wished to end what she saw as "dangerous" publicity
- Belief that Price was atoning in the afterlife and needed her help to stop the story
- General psychological suppression of unpleasant memories (the authors cite psychoanalytic theory)

## Significance

The SPR used her 1945–49 testimony as key evidence against Price. The authors argue her reversal makes her 1945–49 testimony unreliable — and that her 1929 behaviour (including keeping the "ticking mirror" as a souvenir, and attempting to write a novel about the events) is incompatible with her later claim that nothing happened.
