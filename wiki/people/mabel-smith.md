# Mrs Mabel Smith

Wife of [[rev-eric-smith|Rev. G. Eric Smith]]; resident at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] October 1928 – July 1929. The source of one of the most glaring testimony contradictions in the entire case.

**Sarah Helen Mabel Smith (née Hart)** was born in England in 1885 and worked as a shorthand typist [1, 2]. She was the eldest daughter of Montague Hart, a railway clerk from Isleworth, Middlesex; although born in England, her mother and grandmother were from an Indian "Raj" family [1, 2]. She married the Eurasian civil servant Guy Eric Smith in Calcutta on November 1, 1924 [1, 3, 4]. She suffered a serious illness in India, prompting their return to England and her husband's decision to take Holy Orders [3, 5, 6]. Contemporaries offered contrasting views of her character: SPR investigator [[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]] described her in 1949 as a "middle-aged, practical, sensible woman" [7], while [[ethel-bull|Miss Ethel Bull]] disliked her and stated she "seemed frightened of shadows" and would "shriek with fright" at Borley [1, 8, 9]. She was a deeply religious woman with a "keen sense of sin" who suffered from poor health and nerves [10–12].

## 1929 Testimony (during residency)

The Smiths found the Rectory in a shocking state of disrepair, lacking basic amenities like indoor piped water, gas, and electricity, and infested with rats [13–16]. Despite this, Mabel was directly involved in several of the site's most famous phenomena:

- **The Skull in the Cupboard:** While cleaning out a cupboard in the library, Mabel discovered a brown-paper parcel tied with string. Unwrapping it, she found the perfectly preserved skull of a young woman, which her husband subsequently buried in the churchyard [17–21]
- **The Shadowy Figure:** She reported frequently seeing a dark, shadowy figure leaning over one of the drive gates; whenever she approached it, the figure vanished [22–25]
- **The Lighted Window:** Returning from church after dark, she repeatedly noticed the window of the upstairs schoolroom (Room 11) brightly illuminated. She pointed this out to neighbours (including Mrs Payne and the church choir) to prove it was happening, yet upon unlocking and entering the room, they always found it in total darkness [26–29]
- Reported "sibilant whisperings," mysterious lights, shadowy figures, keys moving, bells — indicating she found events genuinely frightening
- Prepared an "excellent supper" for Price and described him warmly (per Price)
- Her husband wrote to Price and [[sidney-glanville|Glanville]] for years afterwards in friendly terms; a 1937 letter invites Price to visit

## The Media and *Murder at the Parsonage*

Because local superstition hindered parish activities, the Smiths wrote to the *Daily Mirror* in June 1929 asking for the address of a psychical research society, inadvertently sparking a national media frenzy that brought reporter V. C. Wall and investigator [[harry-price|Harry Price]] to their door [26, 30–32].

Lesser-known evidence, however, suggests Mabel had additional motives. She told a local acquaintance she was "going to make Borley hum!" [33]. Inspired by village gossip and the legends of the Bull family, she was writing a fictional supernatural thriller titled ***[[murder-at-the-parsonage|Murder at the Parsonage]]*** [21, 34–36]. Skeptics suggest she deliberately invited press attention to generate publicity for her novel, and her later hostility toward [[harry-price|Harry Price]] stemmed from his refusal or failure to help her get the manuscript published by a major publishing house [36–38].

## 1945 Testimony (*Church Times* letter)

After abandoning the Rectory in July 1929 due to the lack of amenities and the nuisance of sightseers [39, 40], the Smiths maintained a cordial correspondence with Price for years, continually affirming their belief that the house was haunted [41, 42]. Following her husband's death in 1940, however, Mabel underwent an extraordinary and complete reversal of opinion.

On October 19, 1945, she published a letter in *The Church Times* — bizarrely signing it with her deceased husband's name, "G. Eric-Smith," and misplacing Borley in Sussex. She stated: **"I would like to state definitely that neither my husband nor myself believed the house haunted by anything else but rats and local superstition. We left the rectory because of its broken-down condition, but certainly found nothing to fear there."** [43–47]

She reiterated this denial in a letter to the *Daily Mail* on May 26, 1949 [48–50].

## 1949 Signed Statement to the SPR (spr-iv)

The SPR investigators ([[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], [[trevor-hall]]) visited Mrs Smith on 1 July 1949. Her signed statement — prepared from notes, sent back for corrections, and signed again — provides far greater detail than her press letters. During an extensive SPR interview, she emphatically claimed that 99% to 100% of the Borley phenomena were "normally produced" by rats, mice, practical jokes, and village gossip [7, 51]. She directly accused [[harry-price|Harry Price]] of faking phenomena to generate publicity, noting that the severe [[poltergeist-activity|poltergeist]] activity only began upon his arrival [52, 53]. Key elements:

**All phenomena occurred only when Price was present:** "I cannot explain these happenings while Mr Price was present; but such things had never occurred before he appeared, and we knew he was an expert conjurer and had our suspicions as well as being puzzled." When [[lucie-kaye]] visited alone, nothing paranormal occurred at all.

**The [[wine-into-ink-trick|water-to-ink trick]]:** At a dinner with other guests, a glass of water changed to ink "while Mr Price was sitting next to the user of the glass." A guest believed Price had done it — "but remarked 'He wasn't a man they would like to offend' and no such suggestion was made openly."

**Pebbles thrown at her:** "Once when Mr Price was in a room with me, little pebbles whizzed past me suddenly. I was astonished. Such a phenomenon had never happened before, nor anything like it. So I could not help thinking perhaps Mr Price had thrown them."

**The red cross and pencil:** "We did not consider it a joke when we found a large red cross had been drawn on our bedroom door; especially as a red pencil was seen next day in the vicinity of Mr Price."

**Mary Pearson's admission:** Mary told Mrs Smith openly after Price had gone: "It was that man threw that coin — so I threw some sugar." Mary had herself produced some "phenomena" in competition with what she suspected Price of doing. She also claimed her maid admitted to throwing sugar to mimic Price throwing coins [54, 55].

**The séance taps:** "Certainly knocks came from the mirror, but I often wish I had examined it closer. I think that 'tricks' were being played all around us."

**The Smiths did not believe in the haunting:** "I have no reason at all to think Borley was haunted."

**On Price's book:** "My impression on reading the volume was utter astonishment at the clever mixture of legend, truth, phantasy, and disregard of the intellects of intelligent people; the facts are so twisted."

**Title suggestion:** "I think the title should read 'The Most Maligned House in England'."

> **Contradiction:** [[ethel-bull]] wrote in 1954: "All nonsense Mrs Smith making out they were not frightened, she used to shriek with fright."

## Contradictions and "Selective Memory"

The SPR investigators ultimately relied heavily on Mabel Smith's 1949 testimony to discredit [[harry-price|Harry Price]], but her claims contained massive, documented contradictions [56, 57]:

1. **The [[wine-into-ink-trick|Wine-into-Ink]] Trick:** Mabel claimed in 1949 that during a dinner at Borley with [[harry-price|Harry Price]] in 1929, the water in a guest's glass turned to ink after the guest suggested a "clever man" could fake the phenomena [58–60]. However, historical records prove this specific event happened two years later, on October 13, 1931, during the *Foyster* incumbency [61–67]. Mabel had completely appropriated a later story to attack Price.

2. **Burning Price's Book:** She told investigators that she was so disgusted by Price's 1940 book *The Most Haunted House in England* that she threw the copies he sent her into the fire without reading them [68]. Yet surviving letters from September 1940 show she wrote to Price thanking him for the book, stating she was "awfully thrilled" and had "laughed a lot over the witty remarks" [35, 69].

3. **Forgetting Key Investigators:** She claimed she had no memory whatsoever of investigator [[sidney-glanville]] [70, 71]. This was impossible: Glanville had visited the Smiths in Kent in 1937, interviewed them for hours, exchanged numerous letters with Mabel herself, and the Smiths had even accompanied him on an overnight séance trip back to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in 1938 [69–76]. She similarly "forgot" a visit from Edwin Whitehouse [71].

## Nuance in the 1929 Correspondence

The SPR noted that Mrs Smith's letters during 1929–30, while at the rectory and immediately after, do not perfectly match her 1949 testimony. [[rev-eric-smith|Mr Smith]] wrote "Borley is undoubtedly haunted, though we have tried to dispossess ourselves of the idea" (November 1929). Mrs Smith described the rectory as "creepy." In 1937 they eagerly visited the rectory again with [[sidney-glanville|Glanville]] and participated in a planchette séance. The SPR argues this reflects a genuine psychological ambivalence at the time — their final settled view (Borley not haunted) only crystallised later — rather than making Mrs Smith's 1949 evidence unreliable.

## Possible Motives for the Reversal

- Price refused to help her publish her novel *[[murder-at-the-parsonage|Murder at the Parsonage]]* (based on the Borley hauntings)
- She may have wished to end what she saw as "dangerous" publicity
- Belief that Price was atoning in the afterlife and needed her help to stop the story
- General psychological suppression of unpleasant memories (the SPR authors cite psychoanalytic theory)

## Notable Quotes

- On reading Price's book: **"My impression on reading the volume was utter astonishment at the clever mixture of legend, truth, phantasy, and disregard of the intellects of intelligent people; the facts are so twisted... I cannot think how so many country people were bewitched into giving such testimonies... Throw one stone into the water and the ripples widen!"** [77, 78]
- On the Rectory's fame: **"I think the title should read 'The Most Maligned House in England'."** [68]
- On the mundane causes of the haunting: **"I have seen a big rat sitting on its haunches licking its paws, so how can people say there were no rats?????"** [79]
- On her own regrets: **"I do not think I should re-open this closed book of the past in my life, but my beloved husband and Mr. Price are constantly influencing my sub-conscious mind and thus resuscitating Borley."** [80, 81]

## Significance

The SPR used her 1945–49 testimony as key evidence against Price. The authors argue her reversal makes her 1945–49 testimony unreliable — and that her 1929 behaviour (including keeping the "ticking mirror" as a souvenir, and attempting to write a novel about the events) is incompatible with her later claim that nothing happened.
