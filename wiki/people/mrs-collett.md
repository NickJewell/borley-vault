# Mrs Collett

Lady diviner called in by the Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|A. C. Henning]] to attempt to locate the missing church plate of [[borley-church|Borley Church]] — believed to have been hidden or buried during the Reformation. Her visit predates the August 1943 excavation, but its results were only revealed to [[harry-price|Harry Price]] over dinner on the evening of August 17, after the day's digging had concluded.

## The Quest for the Church Plate

Henning was aware of local legends suggesting that lost church plate belonging to Borley Church was buried somewhere on the Rectory grounds, and he hoped Mrs Collett could use her divining skills to locate it. Price himself admitted the evidence she provided was "not very impressive" as regards the plate — it was never found.

## The Hazel-Twig Test

During her investigation, Mrs Collett was taken down to explore the various compartments of the Rectory's cellars. While walking through the subterranean area she suddenly stopped near the site of the "well-tank" (the rectangular trough whose location had been indicated by the [[wall-writings|wall-message]] "Well-Tank-Bottom-Me"). At that specific spot her traditional **hazel twig became violently agitated and snapped in two** directly over the area.

That area was precisely where, on August 17, 1943, the human remains, pendants, and other objects were subsequently found.

## Evidential Significance

Price is careful to note that he **did not know of Mrs Collett's experiment until after the first day's digging was complete** — Henning revealed it over dinner that evening. Price documented his ignorance with the following direct quote: "I must emphasize that I did not know of these experiments until after we had concluded our first day's digging; so that Mrs. Collett's experiment did not influence us as to where we should dig."

This removes any possibility that her result influenced the excavators' choice of location, which was independently made on the basis of [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]]'s analysis of the wall-writings. That two entirely independent methods of location — Phythian-Adams's textual analysis and Collett's dowsing exercise — converged on the same spot adds a layer of independent confirmation, whatever view one takes of the reliability of dowsing.

Price makes no strong claims for or against the validity of dowsing; he presents Collett's result as a coincidence worth recording.

## A Second Diviner: Mrs Parker

While Mrs Collett inadvertently pinpointed the location of the hidden human remains, her failure to find the actual church plate led Henning to continue his dowsing experiments. In **May 1947**, he invited a second female diviner to the site: **Mrs Parker** of Long Melford, who specialised in dowsing for metals. Mrs Parker experienced a similarly violent reaction with her own twig — it bent over with such force that it could hardly be forced back up — and her twig was reportedly teleported across the site on multiple occasions. However, her target was an enormous walnut tree in the garden, and subsequent deep excavations at her chosen spot yielded nothing of interest.
