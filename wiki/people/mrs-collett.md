# Mrs Collett

Lady diviner called in by the Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|A. C. Henning]] to attempt to locate the missing church plate of Borley Church — believed to have been hidden or buried during the Reformation. Her visit predates the August 1943 excavation, but its results were only revealed to [[harry-price|Harry Price]] over dinner on the evening of August 17, after the day's digging had concluded.

## The Hazel-Twig Test

During her visit to the Borley cellars, Mrs Collett explored the various compartments with her traditional hazel-twig divining rod. When she reached a specific area near the site of the "well-tank" (the rectangular trough whose location had been indicated by the [[wall-writings|wall-message]] "Well-Tank-Bottom-Me"), her twig **"became violently agitated and snapped in two"** exactly over the area.

That area was precisely where, on August 17, 1943, the human remains, pendants, and other objects were subsequently found.

## Evidential Significance

Price is careful to note that he **did not know of Mrs Collett's experiment until after the first day's digging was complete**. This removes any possibility that her result influenced the excavators' choice of location — the choice was independently made on the basis of Canon Phythian-Adams's analysis of the wall-writings.

That two entirely independent methods of location — Phythian-Adams's textual analysis of the wall-writings, and Collett's dowsing exercise — converged on the same spot adds a layer of independent confirmation, whatever view one takes of the reliability of dowsing.

Price makes no strong claims for or against the validity of dowsing; he presents Collett's result as a coincidence worth recording, noting that the church plate itself was never found.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mrs. Collett** was a "lady diviner" whose brief but striking involvement in the Borley Rectory saga occurred during the site's ruined period, prior to the major cellar excavations of August 1943. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following specific details, events, and quotes regarding her experience, as well as an interesting parallel with another local dowser:

**The Quest for the Church Plate**
Mrs. Collett was specifically called in by the **Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning** (the rector of Borley-cum-Liston). Like other investigators, Henning was aware of local legends suggesting that lost or missing church plate belonging to Borley Church was buried somewhere on the Rectory grounds, and he hoped Mrs. Collett could use her divining skills to locate it [1, 2].

**The Cellar Incident and the Snapped Twig**
During her investigation, Mrs. Collett was taken down to explore the various compartments of the Rectory's cellars [1]. 
*   While walking through the subterranean area, she **"suddenly stopped near the site of the well-tank"** [1]. 
*   At this specific spot, she experienced a dramatic physical reaction: her **"traditional hazel twig became violently agitated and snapped in two"** [1]. 
*   Significantly, this spot exactly marked the area where investigator Harry Price would subsequently unearth fragmentary human remains (a jawbone and pieces of a skull) alongside religious medallions during his own excavations [1, 3].

**Harry Price's Defensive Quote**
Because Mrs. Collett's twig had snapped over the precise location where Price struck his famous "discoveries," Price was highly conscious of how skeptics might interpret the sequence of events. 
*   Price only learned of Mrs. Collett’s prior visit from the Rev. Henning over dinner at Liston Rectory on the evening of **August 17, 1943**—which happened to be immediately after Price and his team had concluded their first day of digging in the cellars [1, 4]. 
*   To preempt any accusations that he had simply dug where the diviner had already pointed (which would diminish the "psychic" claims that his digging was guided by planchette messages), Price firmly documented his ignorance of her visit with the following direct quote: **"I must emphasize that I did not know of these experiments until after we had concluded our first day’s digging; so that Mrs. Collett’s experiment did not influence us as to where we should dig"** [1].

**Lesser-Known Context: A Second Diviner**
While Mrs. Collett successfully (though inadvertently) pinpointed the hidden human remains in the cellar, her failure to find the actual church plate led the Rev. Henning to continue his dowsing experiments. 
*   The sources record that several years later, in **May 1947**, Henning invited a second female diviner to the site: **Mrs. Parker** of Long Melford, who specialized in dowsing for metals [2, 5]. 
*   Mrs. Parker experienced a similarly violent reaction with her own twig (it bent over with such force that it could hardly be forced back up) and her twig was repeatedly teleported across the site [2, 5]. However, unlike Mrs. Collett's cellar discovery, Mrs. Parker's target was an enormous walnut tree in the garden, and subsequent deep excavations at her chosen spot yielded absolutely nothing of interest [5-7].
