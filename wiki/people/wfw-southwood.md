# W. F. W. Southwood

W. F. W. Southwood was a member of the [[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]] investigation of the Borley ruins. His name appears in [[ajb-robertson|Robertson]]'s report as having witnessed nothing of interest at Borley during his visit. However, in November 1946 Southwood wrote to [[harry-price|Price]] to say that in the small hours of 7 June 1944, while in an upstairs room of the burnt-out rectory, he had seen what appeared to him to be the **skeleton of a horse** — a sight he could not approach because of the partially destroyed floor. His account was not included in his original Commission report.

The SPR investigators cited Southwood's letter as an example of the "extraordinary testimony" that filled Price's later Borley files — material driven by the mechanism of suggestion, expectancy, and the desire not to have made an unremarkable visit to "the most haunted house in England":

> "In the small hours of the night of 7 June 1944 he saw in an upstairs room of the burnt-out rectory what appeared to him to be the skeleton of a horse, which he could not approach because of the partially destroyed floor."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**W. F. W. Southwood** (also recorded in one chronological entry as **W. E. Southwood**) was an investigator who participated in the Borley Rectory vigils as a member of the **Cambridge Commission**, a group of predominantly Cambridge undergraduate students who observed the ruined site between 1939 and 1944 [1-3].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following specific details and a notable contradiction regarding his experiences:

**The June 1944 Visit and the Official Report**
*   Southwood visited the Rectory site as part of the Cambridge Commission's **Visit No. 23**, which took place on **5–6 June 1944** [2]. 
*   He was accompanied on this investigation by C. H. Talbot and H. L. Thompson [2]. 
*   According to the official report submitted for this visit, **"no phenomena"** were reported, and Southwood was officially recorded as having seen nothing of interest at Borley [2-4].

**The Contradiction and the "Horse Skeleton"**
Following the publication of the official findings, Southwood evidently felt that the report did his experience **"rather less than justice"** [4]. 
*   On **16 November 1946**, Southwood wrote a personal letter to ghost hunter Harry Price to correct the record [3, 4]. 
*   In direct contradiction to the official report, Southwood claimed in his letter that during the small hours of the night of **7 June 1944** (notably a day after the officially recorded dates of Visit 23), he had experienced a bizarre visual anomaly [2, 4]. 
*   He stated that he looked into an upstairs room of the burnt-out Rectory and saw what appeared to him to be the **"skeleton of a horse"** [3, 4].
*   He explained that he was entirely unable to approach the skeleton to investigate further because the floor of the ruined building was partially destroyed [4].
