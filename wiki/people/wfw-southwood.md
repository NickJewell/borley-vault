# W. F. W. Southwood

Also recorded in one chronological entry as **W. E. Southwood** [1–3]. An investigator who participated in the Borley Rectory vigils as a member of the [[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]], a group of predominantly Cambridge undergraduate students who observed the ruined site between 1939 and 1944.

## The June 1944 Visit

Southwood visited the Rectory site as part of the Cambridge Commission's **Visit No. 23**, which took place on **5–6 June 1944** [2]. He was accompanied by C. H. Talbot and H. L. Thompson [2]. According to the official report submitted for this visit, **"no phenomena"** were reported, and Southwood was officially recorded as having seen nothing of interest at Borley [2–4].

## The "Horse Skeleton" Letter

Following the publication of the official findings, Southwood evidently felt that the report did his experience "rather less than justice" [4]. On **16 November 1946**, he wrote a personal letter to [[harry-price|Harry Price]] to correct the record [3, 4].

In direct contradiction to the official report, Southwood claimed that during the small hours of the night of **7 June 1944** (notably a day after the officially recorded dates of Visit 23), he had looked into an upstairs room of the burnt-out Rectory and seen what appeared to him to be the **"skeleton of a horse"** [3, 4]. He explained that he was entirely unable to approach this skeleton to investigate further because the floor of the ruined building was partially destroyed [4].

> "In the small hours of the night of 7 June 1944 he saw in an upstairs room of the burnt-out rectory what appeared to him to be the skeleton of a horse, which he could not approach because of the partially destroyed floor."

The [[spr|SPR]] investigators cited Southwood's letter as an example of the "extraordinary testimony" that filled [[harry-price|Price]]'s later Borley files — material driven by the mechanism of suggestion, expectancy, and the desire not to have made an unremarkable visit to "the most haunted house in England."
