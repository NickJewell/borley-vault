# Thomas Muriell

Rector of Borley 1681–1709 (died 1709). Like his predecessor [[william-playne|William Playne]], Muriell was transferred to Langenhoo (another Waldegrave living) in 1681, though he was "still officially at Borley" in 1700. His work at Borley was done by Robert Gooding, who was appointed to officiate there in 1700 by one John Fanshaw, though Gooding "had no seat" there.

## Non-Residency Pattern

[[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] notes that both Playne's departure (1662) and Muriell's arrangement (1681 onward) resulted in the Borley Rectory being effectively unoccupied for extended periods:

> *"From 1662 to some unknown date at the end of the eighteenth or the beginning of the nineteenth century the Rectory at Borley was either untenanted or occupied by someone other than the rector."*

The Rectory's value eventually fell to **nil** in the *King's Books*, and by 1808–09 it was assessed at only £9 — suggesting prolonged uninhabitability. This decay over the century-plus period of non-residency is consistent with the Rectory having been abandoned rather than maintained.

Phythian-Adams reads the successive Waldegrave-patronage transfers to Langenhoo — first Playne, then Muriell — as potentially indicative of deliberate intent to keep the Borley Rectory unoccupied following the murder of [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] in 1667. If the remains were buried under the cellar floor, a permanently absent rector would ensure they were never discovered.

> **Note:** The non-residential plurality arrangement was legally normal in the seventeenth century. Phythian-Adams's sinister interpretation of these transfers is speculative, though the structural argument is coherent.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Thomas Muriell** (frequently recorded in the sources with varying spellings such as **Murrell** or **Muriel**) was a seventeenth-century clergyman whose tenure as the Rector of Borley plays a specific role in one of the most prominent historical theories regarding the Rectory's hauntings. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical facts, dates, anomalies, and theoretical connections regarding his incumbency:

**Biographical Details and Dates**
*   **Academic Degree:** He held a Master of Arts (M.A.) degree [1, 2].
*   **Induction:** The formal lists of Borley Rectors record his induction in **1680**, succeeding the Rev. William Playne [1-3]. 
*   **Death:** Muriell died in **1709**, at which point he was succeeded "in the regular way" by Robert Goodwin (or Gooding) [2-4].

**The Move to Langenhoo (Langenhoe)**
The most defining feature of Muriell's time as Borley's rector is that he does not appear to have actually lived there. 
*   Following the exact pattern of his predecessor, William Playne, Muriell was transferred to the parish of **Langenhoo in 1681**, just a year after his induction at Borley [4]. 
*   Langenhoo was another incumbency whose patronage was in the gift of the powerful Catholic Waldegrave family [4, 5].
*   Despite this physical transfer, Muriell remained "still officially at Borley" in the year 1700 [4]. 

**Anomalies in Patronage and Duties**
The sources note a bizarre ecclesiastical contradiction that occurred during Muriell's official incumbency. Even though Muriell was still officially the Rector of Borley in 1700, the patronage of the living had inexplicably fallen into the hands of a man named **John Fanshaw** [4]. For reasons unknown, Fanshaw appointed another clergyman, **Robert Gooding**, to "officiate" at Borley in 1700, even though Gooding had "no seat" there [4].

**Significance to the "Borley Nun" Theory**
Muriell's long absence from the parish is a cornerstone of the famous "Marie Lairre" murder theory proposed by Dr. W. J. Phythian-Adams (Canon of Carlisle). 
*   Phythian-Adams pointed out that because both Playne and Muriell were transferred to Langenhoo by the Waldegraves, the Rectory at Borley was left entirely untenanted (or occupied by someone other than the rector) for a space of "some fifty years, if not more" [4, 6]. 
*   The Canon viewed this as a "singular thing" and suggested it had a highly sinister significance: an empty, isolated house allowed a member of the Waldegrave family to secretly house a young French novice there [6]. 
*   Because Muriell was living miles away at Langenhoo, the murderer was able to strangle the girl and bury her body just three feet under the cellar floor in total secrecy, without any risk of the parish priest discovering the crime or the grave [6, 7].
