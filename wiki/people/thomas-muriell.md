# Thomas Muriell

Also recorded in the sources with varying spellings: **Murrell** or **Muriel**. Rector of Borley, formally inducted in **1680** (or 1681, per some lists [1–3]), succeeding the Rev. [[william-playne|William Playne]]. He held a Master of Arts (M.A.) degree [1, 2]. He died in **1709**, at which point he was succeeded by Robert Goodwin (or Gooding) [2–4].

## Non-Residency Pattern

Like his predecessor [[william-playne|William Playne]], Muriell was transferred to the parish of **[[langenhoe|Langenhoo]] in 1681**, just a year after his induction at Borley [4] — another incumbency whose patronage was in the gift of the powerful Catholic [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]] [4, 5]. Despite this physical transfer, Muriell remained "still officially at Borley" in the year 1700 [4].

A bizarre ecclesiastical anomaly occurred during his official incumbency: even though Muriell was still officially the Rector of Borley in 1700, the patronage of the living had inexplicably fallen into the hands of a man named **John Fanshaw** [4]. For reasons unknown, Fanshaw appointed another clergyman, **Robert Gooding**, to "officiate" at Borley in 1700, even though Gooding had "no seat" there [4].

[[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] notes that both Playne's departure (1662) and Muriell's arrangement (1681 onward) resulted in the Borley Rectory being effectively unoccupied for extended periods:

> *"From 1662 to some unknown date at the end of the eighteenth or the beginning of the nineteenth century the Rectory at Borley was either untenanted or occupied by someone other than the rector."*

The Rectory's value eventually fell to **nil** in the *King's Books*, and by 1808–09 it was assessed at only £9 — suggesting prolonged uninhabitability consistent with the Rectory having been abandoned rather than maintained.

## Significance to the "Borley Nun" Theory

Muriell's long absence from the parish is a cornerstone of the famous "[[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]" murder theory proposed by [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]]. The Canon viewed the successive Waldegrave-patronage transfers to Langenhoo — first Playne, then Muriell — as potentially indicative of deliberate intent to keep the Borley Rectory unoccupied following the alleged murder of Marie Lairre in 1667. If the remains were buried under the cellar floor, a permanently absent rector would ensure they were never discovered. Because Muriell was living miles away at Langenhoo, the murderer was able to bury the body just three feet under the cellar floor in total secrecy, without any risk of the parish priest discovering the crime or the grave [6, 7].

> **Note:** The non-residential plurality arrangement was legally normal in the seventeenth century. Phythian-Adams's sinister interpretation of these transfers is speculative, though the structural argument is coherent.
