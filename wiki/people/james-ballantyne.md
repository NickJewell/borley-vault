# James Ballantyne

[[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s chauffeur (or, according to some sources, the chauffeur employed by [[miss-may-walker|Miss May Walker]]) who drove the party to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] on 13 October 1931 for Price's first visit during the [[lionel-foyster|Foyster]] incumbency. Price described him as a **"dour, unexcitable old Scotsman"** whom a hundred black hands could not move [3].

The party comprised Price, [[mrs-henry-richards|Mrs Henry Richards]], [[kathleen-goldney|Mrs K. M. Goldney]], Miss May Walker, and Ballantyne, all of whom stayed at the Bull Hotel in [[long-melford|Long Melford]] during the visit.

## Character and Disposition

Ballantyne had no real interest in ghosts or the investigation itself. Rather than joining Price and the council members on their tour of the Rectory, he preferred to sit downstairs and read [4]. Later in his career, [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] interviewed Ballantyne, citing the chauffeur as one of the many direct witnesses who provided him with "a tremendous insight" into the Borley mystery [5].

## The "Grisly Black Hand" Incident

While the rest of the party was exploring the house on the evening of October 13, Ballantyne ensconced himself on a chair by the kitchen fire to quietly read his evening newspaper [6]. Looking up, his eyes travelled toward the kitchen door near the dresser, where he was surprised to see a **"grisly black hand" move slowly up and down between the door and the jamb** [6]. The manifestation occurred twice, and whatever was attached to the hand remained completely hidden behind the door [6].

Despite the startling nature of the apparition, Ballantyne remained entirely unfazed. Because he knew their mission concerned a haunted house and had heard the Rectory's history on the drive down from London, he assumed the black hand was "just part of the business" and simply went back to reading his newspaper [3, 4].

When the investigators later questioned him about the event and asked if he had been afraid, Ballantyne flatly replied, **"No"** [3]. During that same evening, he also heard the loud smash of a glass bottle falling in the house, but remained completely unbothered, summarising his experiences with the specific quote that he knew **"it was them ghosts larking about"** [3].
