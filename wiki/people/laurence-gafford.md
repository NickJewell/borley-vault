# Laurence C. Gafford

Of Stevenage, Hertfordshire. A persistent post-fire visitor to Borley who compiled detailed reports of his experiences and assisted Peter Underwood in his investigations. He made at least two documented visits.

## 4 August 1949 — Night Visit with Sugg

Gafford and [[james-sugg|James M. Sugg]] obtained permission from [[james-turner|James Turner]] to spend a night on the Rectory site. They arrived at 10:15 pm and parked on the Rectory drive. At about 11:15 pm they heard "light, brisk footsteps" coming down the lane outside the cottage, followed by a sound like a bicycle freewheeling downhill. When Gafford lowered the car window to look, the squeaking mechanism drowned the footsteps — and when it stopped both footsteps and bicycle sound had ceased, with no figure visible in the moonlit lane. Gafford subsequently discovered that a similar incident (bicycle freewheeling, but in that case *uphill*, followed by an empty lane) had been reported by a previous visitor.

## 4–5 March 1950 — Overnight Vigil with Underwood

Gafford and Peter Underwood spent a night in the Turner's lounge at the cottage. Electrical and other controls were placed around the church and the site. At 2:15 am, both men simultaneously heard heavy footsteps and muffled voices from overhead — precisely where the Turners were sleeping. They checked the controls and returned to the lounge; the sounds were heard again. At 4:15 am the same thing occurred a third time, each episode lasting about twenty seconds before ceasing.

Next morning Mr and Mrs Turner assured them they had slept soundly and had not woken up. When told of the sounds, they "were not surprised" and mentioned that the Coopers had also reported inexplicable whisperings from the same corner of that room.

> "There was certainly no motive whatsoever for the Turners to mislead the two investigators, and it would have been a tiresome and aimless procedure for them to get up three times during the night just for that purpose." — Underwood & Tabori

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Laurence C. Gafford** was an investigator who visited the Borley Rectory site during the "Later Borley" period. A lesser-known biographical detail recorded in the sources is that he lived in Stevenage, Hertfordshire, where he **constructed an interesting model of Borley Rectory** at his home [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific events, dates, quotes, and a chronological contradiction regarding his involvement:

**The August 4, 1949 Visit and the Phantom Bicycle**
Gafford visited the Rectory site with James M. Sugg, having deliberately refrained from making detailed inquiries about the haunting beforehand [2]. After getting permission from the owner James Turner to spend the night on the site, they arrived at **10:15 p.m.** and parked their car on the drive behind the Turners' cottage, approximately outside where the Rectory's Sewing Room used to be [2]. 

At **11:15 p.m.**, while Gafford and Sugg were sitting inside the car talking with James and Cathy Turner, all four of them distinctly heard **"light, brisk footsteps"** approaching down the lane, accompanied by the sound of a bicycle freewheeling downhill [3]. However, **when Gafford lowered the car window to investigate, all the sounds abruptly and completely ceased**, and a subsequent search revealed no one in the area to account for the noises [3]. 

**The March 4–5, 1950 Vigil and the Tunnel Discovery**
Gafford returned to Borley to spend the night with the investigator Peter Underwood in the Turners' cottage lounge [1, 4]. During this visit, Gafford assisted Underwood with a series of photographic experiments that convinced both men **"beyond any shadow of a doubt"** that tunnels or hollows existed beneath the road between the church and the Rectory site [5]. 

**The "Disturbed Night" and a Timeline Contradiction**
After the Turners retired to bed, Gafford and Underwood began an indoor vigil at **1:00 a.m.** [1, 4, 5]. During the early morning hours, both men simultaneously heard **"heavy footsteps and muffled voices"** (or mutterings) coming from the Turners' bedroom directly overhead [1, 4, 5]. Assuming a natural cause for the noise, Gafford specifically commented that the Turners were having a **"disturbed night"** [1, 4, 5]. These noises lasted for about twenty seconds and ceased shortly after the men became aware of them [1, 4, 5]. The heavy footsteps and mutterings occurred again at **4:15 a.m.** under similar circumstances [1, 4, 5]. The next morning, however, the Turners surprised the investigators by assuring them that they had both slept completely soundly until rising [1]. The sources note that neither investigator ever heard similar sounds at the cottage before or after this specific visit [1].

The Borley texts present a specific timeline contradiction regarding this March 1950 vigil. While all accounts agree that the second set of overhead footsteps occurred at 4:15 a.m., they conflict on the timing of the first instance. Some accounts state the first heavy footsteps and muffled voices were heard at **2:15 a.m.** [1, 4], while another account by Peter Underwood explicitly records the time of this initial disturbance as **3:15 a.m.** [5].
