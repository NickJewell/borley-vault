# Canon William John Phythian-Adams (27 May 1888 – 20 February 1967)

Canon of Carlisle Cathedral; archaeologist; soldier; polymath. The man whose theoretical analysis of the Borley wall writings directly led to [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s 1943 excavations and the discovery of human bones in the Rectory cellars.

## Background

Son of the cleric Rev. E.C. Phythian-Adams; educated at Marlborough and Corpus Christi College, Oxford (First Class Honours in Classics and Literae Humaniores; MA 1914; DD from Oxford 1935). Served in WWI attaining Lieutenant-Colonel; MC (1917) and DSO (1918). Before his ordination, he had a distinguished career as an archaeologist — serving as **Assistant Director of the British School of Archaeology in Jerusalem** (now the Kenyon Institute) and Keeper of the Museums to the Palestine administration [3]. He directed excavations at Askelon and collaborated with Professor John Garstang in Northern Syria, the Sudan, and Palestine [3]. Ordained 1925–26; Residentiary Canon of Carlisle 1932–1958; Chaplain to King George VI (1933–52) and Queen Elizabeth II (1953 onwards); Editor of the *Church Quarterly Review* 1940–45.

## Entry into the Borley Case

During Christmas 1940, while recovering from flu in bed, Phythian-Adams read a public library copy of [[mhh|*The Most Haunted House in England*]] cover to cover [2, 5]. On January 8, 1941, he sent Price a lengthy analytical essay — "The Borley Haunt" — which Price later described as "brilliant" and as the direct cause of his decision to excavate. Price wrote: "I am quite certain I should not have attempted another work on the Rectory had it not been for [the Canon's] great interest in the case."

## The Phythian-Adams Analysis

Drawing on his archaeological expertise and experience deciphering hieroglyphics, the Canon conducted a meticulous analysis of the Rectory's wall-writings and planchette scripts [2, 6]. He hypothesised that the hauntings centred on a **"crime of a peculiarly distressing nature"** which had impregnated the site with psychic energy [7], and proposed that the famous phantom nun was **[[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]**, a French Roman Catholic novice from Le Havre, enticed to England and allegedly murdered c.1667.

**Key discoveries in the wall-writings**:

1. **'Trompée'**: He was the first person to read the French word *trompée* ("deceived" or "betrayed," feminine form) clearly legible in the main Marianne message (Plate IX) [6, 12]. It is the best-written word in all the wall-messages, in the flowing hand of someone writing in their own language. This confirmed the entity was French. Price and all previous readers had missed it entirely.

2. **"Well-Tank-Bottom-Me"**: He re-read the principal message — previously rendered by Price as the meaningless "Marianne At Get Help Entant Bottom Me" — and decoded it as: "Marianne, Get Help. Well... Tank... Bottom Me" — a direct instruction to search beneath the cellar's filled-in well-tank [11]. The remains were eventually found there.

3. **'Répond ici'**: In the wall-writing previously read as "Light In... Write Prayer and O...", he detected the French words *Répond ici* ("reply here") — another indication of the entity writing in her own language.

**Causation theory**: All the physical indicators (coat apport = woman; French Revolutionary badge = French; Confirmation medallion = Catholic; coach = 17th century or later; wedding ring apports = a marriage or failed marriage; "Old Amos" = 17th century gardener) together point to a French Catholic woman brought to Borley by a Waldegrave and murdered at the Rectory site. He also theorised that the ghost had used a French-English dictionary — which had inexplicably vanished and reappeared during a visitor's stay in 1885 — to study English words so she could write her appeals [13, 14].

He connected this to the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave]] tombs in [[borley-church|Borley Church]], noting that the Waldegraves as patrons for 300 years would have had access to what was then the chaplain's house on the Rectory site.

## Impact: The 1943 Excavations

Following the Canon's specific instructions to dig at the location of the "well tank," Price organised the August 1943 cellar excavations that unearthed a woman's skull fragment and jawbone, alongside Catholic medallions [11, 19-21]. See [[bones-excavation]].

## Second Essay (1946, EBR Ch. 16)

After learning of the excavation results, Phythian-Adams wrote a second essay for *The End of Borley Rectory*:

1. **Scene of the murder**: The remains found 3 feet under the cellar floor indicate a cellar (and therefore a house) existed at this site before the murder c.1667. The cellars contain ancient two-inch bricks (pre-1863). A building was here — almost certainly the older rectory.

2. **The empty rectory**: From 1662 onward, the rector was effectively absent from Borley — both Rev. Playne (1662) and Rev. Muriell (1681) were moved to Langenhoe, also in Waldegrave patronage. For approximately 50 years, the Borley rectory was untenanted. A crime there could go undetected.

3. **Arabella Waldegrave dismissed**: Mrs [[georgina-dawson|Dawson]]'s suggestion that [[arabella-waldegrave|Arabella Waldegrave]] (born 1687) was the nun is demolished — Arabella was educated at Pontoise, an English convent in France; her education was entirely English, and she could not be the author of messages including *trompée* and *Répond ici*.

4. **Poltergeist theory**: "All Poltergeist phenomena are of human origin, and they are connected with the life (and death) history of the particular personality. Exorcism failed at Borley because what was wanted was not the exorcism of an evil spirit but the quieting of a miserable mind."

As for the intense poltergeist activity during the 1930s, Phythian-Adams concluded that the highly-strung [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] acted unconsciously as a strong "Poltergeist-focus," channelling the trapped psychic energy of the murder [10, 15].

## Contradictions and Later Assessment

> **Contradiction:** When [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] interviewed the Canon in a Carlisle hotel in the early 1950s for the [[spr|SPR]] inquiry, Phythian-Adams reportedly declared that his contributions to Price's book were made **"very much with his tongue in his cheek"** [23], calling it "just a bit of fun that he'd conjured while ill in bed that Christmas" and expressing surprise that Price took his hypotheses seriously enough to publish them [23]. Yet other passages indicate the Canon retained genuine belief in the haunting and in Price's integrity, and was highly critical of Hall's post-mortem attack on Price, arguing there was no justification for the SPR to publish a report that would only upset Price's widow [23, 24]. The chapter Hall wrote about their meeting contains no quotations from any statement Phythian-Adams made — relying instead on published statements — implying the canon did not support Hall's conclusions.

His analysis was described by [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard Whitehouse]] as "ingenious, but not scientific." The [[the-borley-report|SPR investigators]] tried to use the excavations as evidence of Price's fraud, arguing Price had no genuine belief in the theory and used it only to justify a second book. The Companion's analysis shows this characterisation is unfair: Price genuinely could not organise the dig earlier due to wartime labour shortages.

Phythian-Adams wrote 3,000 words of commentary on a graphological analysis of the wall-writings for the unpublished *Borley Symposium*, before passing his extensive Borley correspondence to [[peter-underwood]] upon retirement in 1958 [25, 26]. He retired to Onslow village near Guildford and died there in February 1967. He and his wife visited Underwood at his home on several occasions; they exchanged "scores of letters."
