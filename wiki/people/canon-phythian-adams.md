# Canon William John Phythian-Adams (27 May 1888 – 20 February 1967)

Canon of Carlisle Cathedral; archaeologist; soldier; polymath. The man whose theoretical analysis of the Borley wall writings directly led to Harry Price's 1943 excavations and the discovery of human bones in the Rectory cellars.

## Background

Son of a clergyman; educated at Marlborough and Corpus Christi College, Oxford (First Class Honours in Classics and Literae Humaniores, 1909–11; MA 1914; DD from Oxford 1935). Served in WWI attaining Lieutenant-Colonel; MC (1917) and DSO (1918). Career in Middle Eastern archaeology — Assistant Director of the British School of Archaeology at Jerusalem, directed excavations in Syria and Palestine. Ordained 1925–26; Residentiary Canon of Carlisle 1932–1958; Chaplain to the King 1933–52 and to the Queen 1953 onwards. Editor of the *Church Quarterly Review* 1940–45.

## The Phythian-Adams Analysis (January 1941)

During Christmas 1940, he read *The Most Haunted House in England* cover to cover. In January 1941 he sent [[harry-price|Harry Price]] a long letter-essay — "The Borley Haunt" — which Price later described as "brilliant" and as the direct cause of his decision to excavate.

**Key discoveries in the wall-writings**:

1. **'Trompée'**: Phythian-Adams was the first person to read the French word *trompée* ("deceived," feminine) clearly legible in the main Marianne message (Plate IX). It is the best-written word in all the wall-messages — written in the flowing hand of someone using their own language. This confirmed the entity was French. Price and all previous readers had missed it entirely.

2. **"Well-Tank-Bottom-Me"**: He re-read the principal message — previously rendered by Price as the meaningless "Marianne At Get Help Entant Bottom Me" — and decoded it as: "Marianne, Get Help. Well... Tank... Bottom Me." This was a direct instruction to search beneath the cellar's filled-in well-tank. The remains were eventually found there.

3. **French word in second message**: In the wall-writing previously read as "Light In... Write Prayer and O...", he detected the French word *Répond ici* ("reply here") — another indication of the entity writing in her own language.

**Causation theory**: Phythian-Adams argued that all the physical indicators (coat apport = woman; French Revolutionary badge = French; Confirmation medallion = Catholic; coach = 17th century or later; wedding ring apports = a marriage or failed marriage; "Old Amos" = 17th century gardener) together point to a French Catholic woman brought to Borley by a Waldegrave and murdered at the Rectory site. The Planchette name "Mary Lairre" and the date 1667 are consistent with this reconstruction.

He connected this to the Waldegrave tombs in [[borley-church|Borley Church]] — noting that the Waldegraves, as patrons for 300 years, would have had access to what was then the chaplain's house or rectory on the Rectory site.

Price: "I am quite certain I should not have attempted another work on the Rectory had it not been for [the Canon's] great interest in the case."

## Second Essay (1946, EBR Ch. 16)

After learning of the August 1943 excavation results, Phythian-Adams wrote a second essay for *The End of Borley Rectory*:

1. **Scene of the murder**: The remains were found 3 feet under the cellar floor — this means a cellar (and therefore a house) existed at this site before the murder c.1667. The cellars contain ancient two-inch bricks (pre-1863). A building was here. It was almost certainly the older rectory.

2. **The empty rectory**: From 1662 onward, the rector was effectively absent from Borley — both Rev. Playne (1662) and Rev. Muriell (1681) were moved to Langenhoo, also in Waldegrave patronage. For c.50 years, the Borley rectory was untenanted. A crime there could go undetected; ensuring the house stayed empty afterward was straightforward for the Waldegrave patron.

3. **Arabella Waldegrave dismissed**: Mrs [[georgina-dawson|Dawson]]'s suggestion that Arabella Waldegrave (born 1687) was the nun is demolished: Arabella was educated at Pontoise, an English convent in France — her education was entirely English. She could not be the author of wall-messages including 'trompée' and 'Répond ici'. Return to Marie Lairre.

4. **Poltergeist theory**: "All Poltergeist phenomena are of human origin, and they are connected with the life (and death) history of the particular personality. Exorcism failed at Borley because what was wanted was not the exorcism of an evil spirit but the quieting of a miserable mind."

## Assessment

His analysis was described by [[dom-richard-whitehouse]] (as Edwin became) as "ingenious, but not scientific." The [[the-borley-report|SPR investigators]] tried to use the excavations as evidence of Price's fraud — arguing Price had no genuine belief in the theory and only used it to justify a profitable second book. The Companion's detailed analysis shows this characterisation is unfair: Price genuinely could not organise the dig earlier due to wartime labour shortages and the difficulty of getting accommodation near Borley.

When [[trevor-hall]] visited the canon in Carlisle as part of the SPR inquiry, the chapter he subsequently wrote in the Borley Report contains no quotations from any statement made by Phythian-Adams at their meeting, relying instead on published statements — implying the canon did not support Hall's conclusions.

Phythian-Adams retired to Onslow village near Guildford and died there in February 1967. He and his wife visited Peter Underwood at his home on several occasions; they exchanged "scores of letters."
