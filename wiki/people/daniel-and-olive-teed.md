# Daniel and Olive Teed

Daniel and Olive Teed (née Cox) were the owners of the house at Princess Street, Amherst, Nova Scotia, in which the [[great-amherst-mystery|Great Amherst Mystery]] poltergeist phenomena occurred in 1878–79. Olive was the older sister of [[esther-cox|Esther Cox]], who was living with them as a lodger when the disturbances began.

## Significance

The Teed family name is directly relevant to the Borley story because [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]] used the pseudonym "Teed" for a character in his manuscript *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House* (written 1932–34) — "Teed" being, the SPR investigators observe, "the unusual name" of Esther Cox's hosts. This strongly suggests Foyster was familiar with Walter Hubbell's account of the Amherst case, which he could easily have encountered during his incumbency at Sackville, Nova Scotia (1928–30), five miles from Amherst.

The SPR report treats this pseudonym as a significant clue:

> "It does not seem unreasonable to postulate that the Foysters would be familiar with the story of Esther Cox and the manifestations at Amherst, and that one or both of them may have read one of the many editions of Mr. Hubbell's book. This suspicion is heightened by the curious fact that in manufacturing pseudonyms for the real characters in his *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*, Mr. Foyster called one member of a party of spiritualists 'Mr. Teed'."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Daniel and Olive Teed** were not residents of Borley, but they are recorded in the Borley sources due to their central role in the "Great Amherst Mystery"—a famous 19th-century Canadian poltergeist case that investigators believe directly inspired the phenomena at Borley Rectory during the Foyster incumbency. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following facts, biographical details, and connections regarding the Teeds:

**Biographical Details and the Amherst Household**
Daniel Teed was a 35-year-old shoemaker who worked as a foreman at the Amherst Boot and Shoe Factory in Amherst, Nova Scotia [1, 2]. He is described in the texts as a **"God-fearing and hard-working"** man [1]. He and his wife, Olive, had two small sons [1]. 

The family resided at **Number 6, Princess Street** in Amherst, which was a bright, clean, yellow-painted wooden house featuring four ground-floor rooms, an entrance hall, and four bedrooms on the first floor [1]. The Teed household was quite large; in addition to Daniel, Olive, and their sons, the home accommodated Daniel's brother John Teed, as well as Olive’s siblings: her brother William and her two unmarried sisters, Jean (aged 21) and the "dark and sullen" Esther Cox (aged 18) [1, 2]. The entire family were devout and active members of the Wesleyan Methodist Church [1, 2]. 

**The Amherst Mystery (1878–1879)**
Between September 1878 and July 1879, the Teeds' home became the site of one of the most famous poltergeist cases in the world [1]. The violent phenomena focused entirely on 18-year-old Esther Cox following an attempted sexual assault on her by a local man [2]. 

The Teeds' house was plagued by small outbreaks of fire (which were always extinguished before causing major damage), bedclothes being violently stripped from beds by an unseen force, and scratching noises [3, 4]. The most terrifying phenomenon was the appearance of chilling words scratched deep into the plaster of the wall above Esther's bed: **"Esther Cox, you are mine to kill"** [4]. The sensational events attracted hordes of visitors and journalists to the Teeds' home, though the happenings eventually ceased as inexplicably as they had begun [4, 5]. 

**The Borley Connection and the "Teed" Pseudonym**
The Teeds and the Amherst Mystery are of paramount importance to the Borley Rectory story because researchers discovered "many and striking" similarities between the events in the Teeds' house and the phenomena reported at Borley Rectory by the Rev. Lionel Foyster and his wife Marianne between 1930 and 1932 [6]. The Borley phenomena perfectly mirrored Amherst, including the young age of the female focus, the small outbreaks of fire, the disturbances of bedclothes, and, most notably, the appearance of sensational messages scrawled on the walls [3, 6]. 

Investigators highlighted a highly suspicious geographical link: immediately before moving to Borley in 1930, the Foysters had lived in Sackville, New Brunswick, which is only about five miles away from Amherst [7]. 

The most damning clue connecting the Foysters to the Teed family emerged in a fictionalized account of the Borley hauntings that the Rev. Foyster wrote, titled *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House* [7]. In this manuscript, Foyster disguised the real participants using pseudonyms [7]. When describing a séance held at Borley by a visiting spiritualist circle, Foyster named the medium "Mr. Thompson" and gave the spiritualist interrogator the highly unusual name of **"Mr. Teed"** [8]. In Foyster's account, it was "Teed's" job to draw out information from the spirit, leading to the following quoted exchange when the ghost asked for a drink:
> **"Friend, you have no body to put it into..."** replied Teed [8].

Because "Teed" was the real surname of Esther Cox's sister and brother-in-law in whose house the famous haunting took place, psychical researchers used Foyster's use of this specific pseudonym to argue that he was intimately aware of the Amherst case, suggesting the Foysters used the events at the Teeds' house as a blueprint to manufacture or embellish the haunting at Borley Rectory [7, 8].
