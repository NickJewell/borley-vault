# Daniel and Olive Teed

Daniel and Olive Teed (née Cox) were the owners of the house at Number 6, Princess Street, [[amherst-nova-scotia|Amherst, Nova Scotia]], in which the [[great-amherst-mystery|Great Amherst Mystery]] poltergeist phenomena occurred in 1878–79. Olive was the older sister of [[esther-cox|Esther Cox]], who was living with them as a lodger when the disturbances began.

## The Teed Household

Daniel Teed was a 35-year-old shoemaker who worked as a foreman at the Amherst Boot and Shoe Factory [1, 2]. He is described as a "God-fearing and hard-working" man [1]. He and Olive had two small sons [1]. The family home was a bright, clean, yellow-painted wooden house with four ground-floor rooms, an entrance hall, and four bedrooms on the first floor [1]. In addition to Daniel, Olive, and their sons, the household accommodated Daniel's brother John Teed, Olive's brother William, and Olive's two unmarried sisters: Jean (aged 21) and the "dark and sullen" [[esther-cox|Esther Cox]] (aged 18) [1, 2]. The entire family were devout Wesleyan Methodists [1, 2].

## The Amherst Mystery (1878–1879)

Between September 1878 and July 1879, the Teeds' home became the site of one of the most famous poltergeist cases in the world [1]. The violent phenomena focused entirely on 18-year-old [[esther-cox|Esther Cox]] following an attempted sexual assault on her by a local man [2]. The house was plagued by small outbreaks of fire (always extinguished before causing major damage), bedclothes violently stripped from beds by an unseen force, and scratching noises [3, 4]. The most terrifying phenomenon was the appearance of words scratched deep into the plaster of the wall above Esther's bed: "Esther Cox, you are mine to kill" [4]. The sensational events attracted hordes of visitors and journalists to the Teeds' home [4, 5].

The case was later chronicled by [[walter-hubbell|Walter Hubbell]] in a widely read commercial book.

## The Borley Connection and the "Teed" Pseudonym

The Teeds and the Amherst Mystery are directly relevant to the Borley story because researchers identified "many and striking" similarities between the events in the Teeds' house and the phenomena reported at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] by the Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] and his wife [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] between 1930 and 1932 [6]. The Borley phenomena allegedly mirrored Amherst, including the young age of the female focus, the small outbreaks of fire, the disturbances of bedclothes, and — most notably — the appearance of sensational messages scrawled on the walls [3, 6].

Investigators highlighted a suspicious geographical link: immediately before moving to Borley in 1930, the Foysters had lived in [[sackville-new-brunswick|Sackville, New Brunswick]], which is only about five miles from Amherst [7].

The most damning clue connecting the Foysters to the Teed family is found in [[lionel-foyster|Foyster]]'s fictionalized account of the Borley hauntings, [[foyster-manuscripts|*Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*]]. In this manuscript, Foyster disguised the real participants using pseudonyms. When describing a séance held at Borley by a visiting spiritualist circle, he named the medium "Mr. Thompson" and gave the spiritualist interrogator the highly unusual name of **"Mr. Teed"** [8]. In Foyster's account, "Teed's" job was to draw out information from the spirit:

> **"Friend, you have no body to put it into..."** replied Teed [8].

The SPR report treats this pseudonym as a significant clue:

> "It does not seem unreasonable to postulate that the Foysters would be familiar with the story of Esther Cox and the manifestations at Amherst, and that one or both of them may have read one of the many editions of Mr. Hubbell's book. This suspicion is heightened by the curious fact that in manufacturing pseudonyms for the real characters in his *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*, Mr. Foyster called one member of a party of spiritualists 'Mr. Teed'."

Because "Teed" was the real surname of Esther Cox's sister and brother-in-law, psychical researchers used Foyster's adoption of this specific pseudonym to argue that he was intimately aware of the Amherst case — suggesting the Foysters used events at the Teeds' house as a blueprint to manufacture or embellish the haunting at Borley Rectory [7, 8].
