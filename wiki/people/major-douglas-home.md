# Major the Hon. Henry Douglas-Home

**Major the Hon. Henry Montagu Douglas-Home** (born 21 November 1907; died 1980) was an official observer at Borley Rectory during the [[harry-price|Price]] tenancy (1937–38) and one of the more careful and sceptical investigators. He is primarily remembered for openly accusing [[harry-price|Harry Price]] of faking phenomena. His notes were not seen by Price, and were only made available to the [[spr-investigation|SPR investigators]] after his death.

He was the son of the 13th Earl of Home, the elder brother of the playwright William Douglas-Home, and the younger brother of Alec Douglas-Home, who served as Conservative Prime Minister from October 1963 to October 1964 [1]. He was married to Lady Margaret Spencer; during his time at Borley in 1937 his wife was expecting their first child, and this limited his availability for the investigation [3]. Their son, Charles Cospatrick Douglas-Home — later a journalist on *The Times* — was born on 1 September 1937 [3].

## Background

Douglas-Home visited Borley Rectory on **21 July 1937**, accompanied by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and [[sj-de-lotbiniere|Mr S. J. de Lotbinière]] of the BBC [2, 4]. He paid two further visits, but both were blank visits with no phenomena reported [3]. Price apparently trusted him enough to allow the visit before he had signed the *Haunted House Declaration Form*, which he did not sign officially until the end of July [2].

## The Pencil Marks Observation

Douglas-Home allegedly observed that new pencil markings on the rectory walls appeared consistently only when Price was at the rear of the touring party. He wrote [7]:

> "On all our other tours I manoeuvred myself to the rear directly we entered a room. He was therefore in the beam of my torch. We never found another mark."

This observation parallels [[lord-charles-hope]]'s earlier 1929 experience, when a piece of linoleum reportedly appeared on stairs after Hope noticed Price had been at the rear.

> **Contradiction:** [[robert-hastings|Hastings]] proved that de Lotbinière's official log for that July 1937 visit recorded five tours (8:15 p.m., 9:15 p.m., 11:00 p.m., 1:10 a.m., and 5:15 a.m.), with fresh marks found on the first four. Only on the final 5:15 a.m. tour did the marks stop appearing [12]. Hastings pointed out that at 5:15 a.m. in late July it would have been broad daylight, making the claim that Douglas-Home kept Price "in the beam of my torch" physically impossible [12].

## The Cellophane Paper

During the joint visit with Price and de Lotbinière, a rustling noise was heard in the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] which Price attributed to paranormal activity. Douglas-Home compared it to the sound of Miss Bull's gown ascending the stairs [8, 10]. He recalled that while his heart was "fluttering like a bird," Price "never altered an iota in breathing" [10], and deduced that Price had secretly fed cellophane into a hot stove in the hallway to fake the sound. On the drive home, he reportedly peeped into Price's suitcase and found "a long roll of cellophane paper with a jagged edge" [8, 11].

> **Contradiction:** [[robert-hastings|Hastings]] and other researchers pointed out that this theory was physically impossible: Price would have had to silently rip cellophane, secretly open a stove "giving out a considerable heat," stuff the material inside, hide the roll, and run upstairs — all unobserved [13]. Cellophane shrinks and burns almost instantly in heat, making a prolonged rustling noise impossible [13]. When confronted with these impossibilities by the SPR in 1953, Douglas-Home was forced to backtrack, admitting: "I fear my explanations for the 'noise' are nil but doubtless a large crumpled sheet of cellophane could and did produce it somehow... I must confess I have no idea how it was done" [14].

## The Cottage Acoustic Effect

Douglas-Home documented the acoustic properties of the rectory courtyard more thoroughly than any other observer:

- The [[mr-and-mrs-arbon|Arbons']] footsteps crossing the courtyard were "clearly audible in *each* room of the house"
- Arbon's footsteps "sounded as if they were in the corridor of the house"
- The hand-pump in the covered passage produced "thumps, groans-galore"
- "Words spoken outside the pantry by cottage, which at its nearest part was only some 18 feet off, were strongly heard in the Base and other rooms"

This observation largely explains the [[mysterious-footsteps|mysterious footsteps]] that impressed official observers and was entirely omitted by Price in his published reports.

## The Adelaide Wall Writings

Among the many wall writings never fully catalogued by Price was the word "Adelaide" on the kitchen wall — the name of the Foysters' adopted child — "in similar writing" to the [[wall-writings|Marianne messages]], and at the same height [6]. When Douglas-Home raised the matter, the housekeeper replied: "Adelaide was a terror for scribbling — she used to write on every wall — and I spent hours washing them out." Price "brushed aside the question" and never mentioned the Adelaide writings in his published books [6].

## His Final Assessment

In correspondence with [[lord-charles-hope|Lord Charles Hope]] in August 1949, Douglas-Home accused Price of faking phenomena and referred to him by "a number of highly derogatory names" [8]. He submitted the following statement to the SPR in August 1953:

> "I have no objection to any of my observations on Borley Rectory being used... in fact I would welcome such action as I have always felt that Harry Price by his complete disregard for the truth in this matter did... psychical research a grave disservice." [9]

The SPR, however, found his specific fraud allegations undermined by chronological and physical impossibilities, and regarded his testimony with caution.
