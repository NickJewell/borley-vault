# Major the Hon. Henry Douglas-Home

Official observer at Borley Rectory during the Price tenancy (1937–38). One of the more careful and sceptical observers, who left detailed notes on his investigations and who ultimately concluded that [[harry-price]] had been producing phenomena fraudulently. His notes were not seen by Price.

## Background

Henry Douglas-Home visited Borley Rectory in 1937, including a joint visit with Price and Mr S. de Lotbinière of the BBC. His observations were written up in detailed notes and correspondence with [[lord-charles-hope]], eventually made available to the SPR investigators after his death.

## The Pencil Marks Observation

Douglas-Home observed that new pencil markings on the rectory walls appeared consistently only when Price was at the rear of the party making the tour of inspection:

> "On all our other tours I manoeuvred myself to the rear directly we entered a room. He was therefore in the beam of my torch. We never found another mark."

This observation parallels [[lord-charles-hope]]'s earlier 1929 experience when a piece of linoleum appeared on stairs after Hope noticed Price had been in the rear.

## The Cellophane Paper

During a joint visit with Price and Mr de Lotbinière, a rustling noise was heard in the Blue Room which Price attributed to paranormal activity. Douglas-Home observed that the sound was "very similar to that caused by the unrolling and crinkling of cellophane paper." On the journey home in the car, "he managed to peep into Price's suitcase and there was a long roll of cellophane paper with a jagged edge."

## The Cottage Acoustic Effect

Douglas-Home documented the acoustic properties of the rectory courtyard more thoroughly than any other observer:

- The Arbons' footsteps crossing the courtyard were "clearly audible in *each* room of the house"
- Arbon's footsteps "sounded as if they were in the corridor of the house"
- The hand-pump in the covered passage produced "thumps, groans-galore"
- "Words spoken outside the pantry by cottage, which at its nearest part was only some 18 feet off, were strongly heard in the Base and other rooms"

This observation largely explains the "mysterious footsteps" that impressed official observers and was entirely omitted by Price in his published reports.

## His Final Assessment

In correspondence with Lord Charles Hope (August 1949) Douglas-Home gave his view that phenomena were produced by Price and used "a number of highly derogatory names" to describe him. He wrote to the SPR in August 1953:

> "I have no objection to any of my observations on Borley Rectory being used... in fact I would welcome such action as I have always felt that Harry Price by his complete disregard for the truth in this matter did... psychical research a grave disservice."

## Notes on the Observer Period

From his unpublished notes Douglas-Home recorded an observation omitted by Price: that among the many wall writings at the rectory (never all catalogued by Price) was the word "Adelaide" on the kitchen wall — the name of the Foysters' adopted child — "in similar writing" to the Marianne messages, at the same height. Price "brushed aside the question" when Douglas-Home raised it. The housekeeper told him that "Adelaide was a terror for scribbling — she used to write on every wall — and I spent hours washing them out."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Major the Hon. Henry Montagu Douglas-Home** was a highly connected official observer during Harry Price's tenancy at Borley Rectory. He is primarily recorded in the sources as one of the few investigators who openly accused Harry Price of faking phenomena. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, investigations, specific allegations, and contradictions regarding his involvement:

**Biographical Details**
*   **Family:** Born on **November 21, 1907** (died 1980), he was the son of the 13th Earl of Home [1]. He was the elder brother of the playwright William Douglas-Home and the younger brother of Alec Douglas-Home, who served as the Conservative UK Prime Minister from October 1963 to October 1964 [1, 2].
*   **Personal Life:** He was married to Lady Margaret Spencer, and during the time of the Price tenancy in 1937, his wife was expecting. Their son, Charles Cospatrick Douglas-Home (who later became a journalist on *The Times*), was born on September 1, 1937 [3]. Because his wife was expecting, Douglas-Home could not be as actively involved in the investigation as he might have otherwise been [3].

**The July 21, 1937 Visit and Observations**
Douglas-Home visited Borley Rectory for the first time on **July 21, 1937**, accompanied by Harry Price and a BBC executive, Mr. S.J. de Lotbinière [2, 4]. He paid two further visits to the Rectory, but both were "blank visits" with no phenomena reported [3]. Notably, Price apparently trusted Douglas-Home enough to allow him to visit without signing the *Haunted House Declaration Form*, which he did not officially sign until the end of July [2]. 

During his time at the Rectory, Douglas-Home proposed non-paranormal explanations for several mysteries:
*   **Amplified Noises:** He asserted that the Rectory's acoustics amplified normal sounds. He stated that the caretaker, Mr. Arbon, operating the handpump in the courtyard late at night produced **"thumps, groans-galore,"** and noted that **"Arbon's footsteps... sounded as if they were in the corridor of the house,"** being clearly audible in every room [5].
*   **The "Adelaide" Wall Writings:** Douglas-Home found the name "Adelaide" scribbled on the kitchen wall at the exact same height as the famous "Marianne" messages. When he asked the housekeeper about it, she replied: **"Adelaide was a terror for scribbling—she used to write on every wall—& I spent hours washing them out"** [6]. When Douglas-Home confronted Price with this evidence of normal human agency, Price brushed the question aside and never mentioned the Adelaide writings in his books [6].

**Accusations of Fraud Against Harry Price**
In August 1949, long after Price's death, Douglas-Home submitted a statement to Lord Charles Hope accusing Price of faking phenomena during their 1937 visit [7]. Douglas-Home held a highly hostile view of Price, referring to him by **"a number of highly derogatory names"** [8] and submitting this specific quote regarding his feelings on the matter in 1953:
> **"I have no objection to any of my observations on Borley Rectory being used... in fact I would welcome such action as I have always felt that Harry Price by his complete disregard for the truth in this matter did... psychical research a grave disservice"** [9].

His accusations centered on two specific incidents:
1.  **Faking Pencil Marks:** Douglas-Home claimed that during tours of the house, fresh pencil marks only appeared when Price was at the back of the group. He wrote: **"on all our other tours I manoeuvred myself to the rear directly we entered a room. He [Price] was, therefore, in the beam of my torch. We never found another mark"** [7]. 
2.  **The Cellophane / Rustling Noise:** While sitting closely next to Price in the Blue Room, the party heard a loud rustling sound from below that Douglas-Home likened to **"Miss Bull's gown ascending the stairs"** [8, 10]. Douglas-Home recalled: **"while my heart was fluttering like a bird—I swear that Harry (who normally jumped a yard at anything unexpected) never altered an iota in breathing"** [10]. He deduced that Price had faked the noise by secretly shoving a piece of cellophane into the flue of a hot stove in the hallway [8]. He became so convinced of this that, on the drive back to London while Price was sleeping, he snooped into Price's bag and claimed to have found **"a long roll of cellophane paper with a jagged edge"** [8, 11]. 

**Contradictions and Rebuttals by Investigators**
When the investigator Robert J. Hastings systematically examined the SPR's critical *Borley Report*, he completely dismantled Douglas-Home's allegations, exposing severe contradictions and impossibilities in his memory:
*   **The Daylight Contradiction:** Hastings proved that the official log kept by de Lotbinière in 1937 completely contradicted Douglas-Home's 1949 memory of the pencil marks. The log recorded five tours (8:15 p.m., 9:15 p.m., 11:00 p.m., 1:10 a.m., and 5:15 a.m.). Fresh marks were found on the first four. Only on the final 5:15 a.m. tour did the marks stop appearing [12]. Hastings pointed out the absurdity of Douglas-Home claiming he kept Price in the **"beam of my torch"** during this specific tour, because at 5:15 a.m. in late July, it would have been broad daylight [12].
*   **The Cellophane Impossibility:** Hastings and other researchers pointed out that Douglas-Home's cellophane theory defied physical logistics. To fake the noise, Price would have had to silently rip cellophane from a roll, secretly open the door of a stove that was "giving out a considerable heat," stuff the cellophane inside, hide the roll, and run upstairs to sit next to Douglas-Home without anyone noticing [13]. Furthermore, cellophane shrinks and burns almost instantly in heat, making a prolonged rustling noise impossible [13]. 
*   **A Retraction:** When confronted with these logistical impossibilities by the SPR in 1953, Douglas-Home was forced to backtrack on his firm accusation, admitting in a letter: **"I fear my explanations for the 'noise' are nil but doubtless a large crumpled sheet of cellophane could and did produce it somehow... I must confess I have no idea how it was done"** [14].
