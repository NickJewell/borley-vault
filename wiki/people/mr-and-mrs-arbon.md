# Mr and Mrs Arbon (Lionel and Winifred Arbon)

**Lionel Arbon** was a steam-roller driver; **Winifred Arbon** (née Pearson) was the daughter of [[annie-pearson|Annie Pearson]], who worked as daily woman at the Rectory during the Foyster period and lived at Place Farm Cottage across the road. Tenants of the [[rectory-cottage|Rectory cottage]] from February 1936 onwards, the Arbons served as key caretakers and key-holders for [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] throughout [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s entire tenancy (June 1937 – May 1938).

## Role During Price's Tenancy

When Price and his Oxford graduate friend [[ellic-howe|Ellic Howe]] set up the Base Room on 2 June 1937, they visited Mr and Mrs Arbon to arrange the custody of one set of Rectory keys. The Arbons agreed to hand the keys only to authorised observers presenting a letter of introduction from Price, and under no other circumstances.

Major H. Douglas-Home noted that Mr and Mrs Arbon used to work the hand-pump in the courtyard very late at night, causing "groans and thumps galore" — a detail cited by critics of the case as a possible mundane explanation for some of the auditory phenomena reported by official observers. Their footsteps were also said to be clearly audible in each room of the house.

## Post-Tenancy Contact

When Price and [[geoffrey-motion|Geoffrey Motion]] made their final visit on 19 May 1938 to collect his belongings, they "had a word with the Arbons" and learned that efforts were being made to sell the Rectory. Mr Arbon remained in the cottage area for some time thereafter; when [[grant-vallender|Grant Vallender]] visited Borley on 8 June 1968, he was able to talk with an Mr Arbon "who had been Rectory caretaker and lived in the cottage during Price's tenancy." Arbon told Vallender that Price was "a good fellow," and remembered him well.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mr. Lionel Arbon and his wife Winifred** were the tenants of Borley Rectory Cottage from February 1936 to December 1938 [1-3]. Despite their close proximity to the supposedly haunted house, they were a pragmatic couple whose mundane daily activities inadvertently contributed to several "ghostly" sightings and whose involvement exposed critical flaws in Harry Price's investigation.

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, events, quotes, and controversies regarding their time at Borley:

**Biographical Details**
*   **Occupations and Background:** Lionel Arbon was a steam-roller driver whose job frequently required him to be away from home for extended periods [2, 4]. Winifred Arbon was the daughter of Mrs. Pearson, a local Borley resident who had served as a maid during the Smith incumbency [2]. 
*   **Spelling Discrepancies:** In the reports submitted by Price's observers, the couple's surname was frequently misspelled as "Arbor" or "Arbin" [1, 5].
*   **Immunity to Phenomena:** Unlike their predecessors in the cottage (such as the Coopers, who claimed to see phantom coaches and dark shapes), the Arbons never experienced any paranormal phenomena during their residency [2-4].

**Role as Caretakers During the Price Tenancy (1937–1938)**
When Harry Price rented the empty Rectory for his year-long investigation, he met with the Arbons on June 2, 1937, and formally appointed them as caretakers and keyholders [2, 6]. 
*   **Duties:** They were instructed to grant access only to authorized observers who presented a letter of introduction or whose arrival Price had notified them of in advance [2, 6]. Additionally, for a small tip, Mr. Arbon agreed to regularly fill the Rectory’s attic water cistern using the pump mechanism in the adjacent courtyard [2].
*   **Frustration with the Investigation:** Although initially cooperative, Mr. Arbon eventually came to regard the constant stream of ghost-hunters as an unwelcome nuisance, and he wrote a letter to Price on September 19, 1937, explicitly telling him so [7]. 

**Contradictions and "Manufactured" Phenomena**
The Arbons' presence heavily compromised the strict control conditions Price claimed to have established. Skeptics, particularly the authors of the 1956 SPR *Borley Report*, used the Arbons' actions to explain away several major phenomena:

*   **Weakness in Key Control and Suspicions of Fraud:** Observer D. Adcock reported in August 1937 that "the caretaker gives up his keys readily to anyone," a major lapse in security [7, 8]. Another observer, J.W. Burden, went further in a letter to Price on December 21, 1937, suspecting that Mr. Arbon might be deliberately faking phenomena for financial gain: *"I would hesitate to place some of the manifestations beyond Arbin [sic] because he might easily realise that ‘Phenomena appearing strong’ would mean more observers and consequently more tips..."* [5, 9].
*   **Mistaken Identity as the "Dark Figure":** On multiple occasions, overzealous investigators mistook Mr. Arbon for a ghost. On the evening of August 7–8, 1937, D. Adcock's party saw a dark figure cross the Nun's Walk; upon investigating with a torch, they found it was merely Mr. Arbon having "a look round to see if there was anybody about" [10]. This deeply annoyed Adcock, who demanded Price instruct his caretaker to stop wandering the grounds during vigils [10]. A nearly identical incident occurred during the visit of C.S. Taylor on January 8–9, 1938, when a "dark figure" seen at 5:25 p.m. again turned out to be Arbon [8, 10, 11].
*   **Acoustic Misunderstandings (Footsteps and Groans):** Because the Rectory’s architecture caused courtyard noises to echo loudly inside, the Arbons' daily routines were repeatedly logged as poltergeist activity [12, 13]. Major Douglas-Home stated that the Arbons often worked the handpump late at night, producing "thumps, groans-galore," and that "Arbon’s footsteps... sounded as if they were in the corridor of the house" [14]. On July 19, 1937, observers Bailey and Wintour investigated "ghostly footsteps," only to discover it was Mrs. Arbon walking in the courtyard [15]. In December 1937, J.W. Burden also attributed shuffling footsteps and talking to the Arbons [15, 16].
*   **The Window Lights:** Mr. Arbon’s habit of leaving a lantern burning in the courtyard while working the pump was suggested as a mundane explanation for the famous "mysterious lights" seen in the Rectory windows, though the Rev. A.C. Henning argued that some of the reported lights were too bright to be mere lantern reflections [12, 17].

**Testimony Regarding the 1939 Fire**
The Arbons vacated the cottage in December 1938 when Captain W.H. Gregson purchased the property, relocating down the road to Brook Hall Lane [2, 18]. However, Lionel Arbon provided crucial contemporary testimony to Harry Price regarding the devastating fire of February 27, 1939. 
*   In a hurried note sent on February 28, Arbon confirmed the fire started at 11:00 p.m. [19]. 
*   On March 5, he sent a detailed letter offering a damning assessment of Gregson's actions during the blaze: *"Capt. Gregson over turned a lamp, he ran to the pump for water but the fire got his master and he lost his head, he never called for any help. He went off in his car to fetch a fire engine. Mr Pearson saw the fire from his bedroom and went to the Rectory and could not make anyone hear... He went and called up Mr Payne at the farm who phoned for the fire brigade."* [19].

**Later Life and Opinions**
Despite his earlier complaints about the investigators being a nuisance, Lionel Arbon retained a surprisingly fond memory of Harry Price. When interviewed decades later (June 8–9, 1968) by researcher Grant Vallender, Arbon stated that he remembered the investigator well and described Price affectionately as "a good fellow" [7, 20, 21].
