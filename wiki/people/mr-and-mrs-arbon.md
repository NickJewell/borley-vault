# Mr and Mrs Arbon (Lionel and Winifred Arbon)

**Lionel Arbon** was a steam-roller driver whose job frequently required him to be away from home for extended periods [2, 4]. **Winifred Arbon** (née Pearson) was the daughter of [[annie-pearson|Annie Pearson]], who worked as a daily woman at the Rectory during the Foyster period and lived at Place Farm Cottage across the road [2]. Their surname was frequently misspelled in observers' reports as "Arbor" or "Arbin" [1, 5].

Tenants of [[rectory-cottage|Rectory cottage]] from **February 1936 onwards**, the Arbons served as key caretakers and key-holders for [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] throughout [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s entire tenancy (June 1937 – May 1938). Unlike their predecessors in the cottage — such as the [[edward-cooper|Coopers]], who claimed to see phantom coaches and dark shapes — the Arbons never experienced any paranormal phenomena during their residency [2–4].

## Role During Price's Tenancy

When Price and his friend [[ellic-howe|Ellic Howe]] set up the [[base-room|Base Room]] on 2 June 1937, they visited the Arbons and formally appointed them as caretakers and key-holders [2, 6]. They were instructed to grant access only to authorised observers who presented a letter of introduction or whose arrival Price had notified them of in advance [2, 6]. Additionally, for a small tip, Mr Arbon agreed to regularly fill the Rectory's attic water cistern using the pump mechanism in the adjacent courtyard [2].

Although initially cooperative, Mr Arbon eventually came to regard the constant stream of ghost-hunters as an unwelcome nuisance, and wrote a letter to Price on **19 September 1937** explicitly telling him so [7].

## Contradictions and "Manufactured" Phenomena

The Arbons' presence heavily compromised the strict control conditions Price claimed to have established. Sceptics, particularly the authors of the 1956 SPR *Borley Report*, used the Arbons' activities to explain away several major phenomena:

**Weakness in key control:** Observer D. Adcock reported in August 1937 that "the caretaker gives up his keys readily to anyone" — a major lapse in security [7, 8]. Observer J. W. Burden went further in a letter to Price on 21 December 1937, suspecting that Mr Arbon might be deliberately faking phenomena for financial gain: *"I would hesitate to place some of the manifestations beyond Arbin [sic] because he might easily realise that 'Phenomena appearing strong' would mean more observers and consequently more tips..."* [5, 9].

**Mistaken identity as the "dark figure":** On multiple occasions, investigators mistook Mr Arbon for a ghost. On the evening of 7–8 August 1937, D. Adcock's party saw a dark figure cross the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]]; upon investigating with a torch, they found it was merely Arbon "having a look round to see if there was anybody about" [10]. Adcock demanded Price instruct his caretaker to stop wandering the grounds during vigils [10]. A nearly identical incident occurred during [[gs-taylor|C. S. Taylor's]] visit on 8–9 January 1938, when a "dark figure" seen at 5:25 p.m. again turned out to be Arbon [8, 10, 11].

**Acoustic misunderstandings:** Because the Rectory's architecture caused courtyard noises to echo loudly inside, the Arbons' daily routines were repeatedly logged as [[poltergeist-activity|poltergeist activity]] [12, 13]. [[major-douglas-home|Major Douglas-Home]] stated that the Arbons often worked the handpump late at night, producing **"thumps, groans-galore,"** and that "Arbon's footsteps... sounded as if they were in the corridor of the house" [14]. On 19 July 1937, observers Bailey and Wintour investigated "ghostly footsteps," only to discover it was Mrs Arbon walking in the courtyard [15]. In December 1937, Burden also attributed shuffling footsteps and talking to the Arbons [15, 16].

**The window lights:** Mr Arbon's habit of leaving a lantern burning in the courtyard while working the pump was suggested as a mundane explanation for the famous [[window-lights|mysterious lights]] seen in the Rectory windows, though [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Henning]] argued that some of the reported lights were too bright to be mere lantern reflections [12, 17].

## Testimony Regarding the 1939 Fire

The Arbons vacated the cottage in December 1938 when [[captain-gregson|Captain W. H. Gregson]] purchased the property, relocating down the road to Brook Hall Lane [2, 18]. Lionel Arbon subsequently provided crucial contemporary testimony to Price about the devastating [[fire-1939|fire of 27 February 1939]]:

- In a hurried note sent on 28 February, Arbon confirmed the fire started at 11:00 p.m. [19]
- On 5 March, he sent a detailed letter offering a damning assessment of Gregson's actions: *"Capt. Gregson over turned a lamp, he ran to the pump for water but the fire got his master and he lost his head, he never called for any help. He went off in his car to fetch a fire engine. Mr Pearson saw the fire from his bedroom and went to the Rectory and could not make anyone hear... He went and called up Mr Payne at the farm who phoned for the fire brigade."* [19]

## Later Life and Opinions

Despite his earlier complaints about the investigators being a nuisance, Lionel Arbon retained a fond memory of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]. When interviewed decades later (**8–9 June 1968**) by researcher [[grant-vallender|Grant Vallender]], he stated that he remembered the investigator well and described Price affectionately as **"a good fellow"** [7, 20, 21].
