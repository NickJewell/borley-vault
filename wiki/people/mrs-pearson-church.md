# Mrs Pearson (Annie Pearson)

Full name **Mrs Annie Pearson**. A prominent and long-standing local figure in the Borley saga who lived across the road from the Rectory site at Place Farm Cottage (also described as a cottage just past [[borley-church|Borley Place]]). She was the **aunt of [[mary-pearson|Mary Pearson]]** (the Smiths' maid who witnessed the phantom coach) and the **mother of Winifred Arbon**, who resided in the [[rectory-cottage|Rectory Cottage]] with her husband Lionel during [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s 1937–1938 investigation.

Mrs Pearson served in multiple capacities over many years: as a daily scrubbing woman for the [[lionel-foyster|Foyster]] family from October 1930, as the holder of a spare set of Rectory keys for Price's observers, and as **churchwarden and caretaker for Borley Church**. The person who looked after the sanctuary lamp — distinct from [[mary-pearson|Mary Pearson]] — she reported a series of unexplained incidents to the Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|A. C. Henning]] in spring 1942.

## Scepticism About the Haunting

Despite her close proximity to the Rectory and her central role in the church, **Mrs Pearson and her husband were highly sceptical of the hauntings**. When interviewed by SPR investigators Mollie Goldney and [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]], she took a "dim view" of the entire Borley story, insisting that in all her years living opposite the site she and her husband had never experienced anything paranormal. She believed the Rev. Guy and [[mabel-smith|Mabel Smith]] vacated the Rectory simply because it was far too big and expensive to run. Regarding the Foyster period, she suspected that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] was desperate to escape her dull life at Borley and manufactured much of the "phenomena" herself. She also dismissed the mysterious [[wall-writings|wall writings]], complaining that the Foysters' adopted daughter, Adelaide, had a "horror for scribbling."

Harry Price's critics noted that he completely omitted Mrs Pearson from his writings, never interviewing her despite her daily presence at the Rectory over many years.

## The Sanctuary Lamp Incidents (Spring 1942)

The sanctuary lamp was kept burning near the Tabernacle on the altar where the Sacrament was reserved. Mrs Pearson lit the small wick each morning and put it out at night. For approximately a fortnight in spring 1942, the **wick was frequently moved** during the night in a locked church.

Attempts to prevent this: she placed a psalter over the lamp glass after putting it out and locking the church for the night. Next morning the psalter was on the floor. She then placed a book-cover over the lamp — also removed on two occasions. After this, nothing further happened.

Henning communicated these incidents to Price in a letter of **April 16, 1942**:

> *"I suggested putting a book or cover of some kind over the lamp glass. She put a psalter over, after putting the light out, and then locked up for the night. She was very surprised next morning to find the book on the floor, especially as both doors were locked and no one could possibly have got in during the night."*

Henning initially suggested a mouse might explain the phenomenon, then retreated from this explanation. Mrs Pearson kept a lookout for birds or bats that might have caused the disturbance but never found any inside the church. She and another local, Mrs Gooch, also found the altar lamp inexplicably smashed on two separate occasions.

## Other Church Phenomena

- **The Moving Registers:** Mrs Pearson and Mrs Gooch noticed that a church register was being moved repeatedly. To test this, they hid the book before locking the building for the night, only to discover the next morning that it had been taken from its hiding place and moved several yards. Other heavy objects were also moved inside the locked church during the night.
- **The Phantom Bell (April 24, 1949):** On Low Sunday, Mrs Pearson and her husband distinctly heard the church bell ring at 8:00 a.m. Believing Henning was there for communion, she did not unlock the church at her usual time. When Henning arrived at 6:30 p.m. for evensong, he was surprised to find the church locked; Mrs Pearson told him, "But you were there at eight o'clock, both my husband and I heard the bell!" Henning had actually been at Liston all morning, making this incident particularly impressive given the Pearsons' usual disbelief in the supernatural.

## Contradictions in the Sources

> **Contradiction:** While most sources state the Pearsons lived at Place Farm Cottage (or a cottage just past Borley Place), one chronological entry contradicts this by placing "Mr and Mrs Pearson in the Rectory Cottage" when they heard the church bell ringing in April 1949.

> **Contradiction:** Marianne Foyster claimed to have seen an apparition of an old man in the garden and described his plum-coloured smoking jacket to Mrs Pearson. One account notes that upon hearing this description Mrs Pearson's face turned "a kind of greenish white" because she recognised the figure as the late [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]]. However, in a 1958 interview, Marianne offered a contradictory version, stating that Mrs Pearson (spelled "Pierson" in the transcript) simply replied "I don't know" in a broad Suffolk accent.

## Significance

The incidents are consistent with the long record of minor phenomena in [[borley-church|Borley Church]] — displaced objects, heard footsteps, organ music — and with the Borley Rectory pattern of interference with specifically religious objects. The significance of several church incidents is heightened precisely by Mrs Pearson's habitual scepticism: she was not a credulous witness.
