# Mrs Pearson (church caretaker)

The person who looked after the sanctuary lamp in [[borley-church|Borley Church]] — distinct from [[mary-pearson|Mary Pearson]], the Smiths' maid who witnessed the phantom coach. She reported a series of unexplained incidents to the Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|A. C. Henning]] in the spring of 1942.

## The Sanctuary Lamp Incidents

The sanctuary lamp was kept burning near the Tabernacle on the altar where the Sacrament was reserved. Mrs Pearson lit the small wick each morning and put it out at night. For approximately a fortnight in spring 1942, the **wick was frequently moved** during the night in a locked church.

Attempts to prevent this: she placed a psalter over the lamp glass after putting it out and locking the church for the night. Next morning the psalter was on the floor. She then placed a book-cover over the lamp — also removed on two occasions. After this, nothing further happened.

Henning communicated these incidents to Price in a letter of April 16, 1942:

> *"I suggested putting a book or cover of some kind over the lamp glass. She put a psalter over, after putting the light out, and then locked up for the night. She was very surprised next morning to find the book on the floor, especially as both doors were locked and no one could possibly have got in during the night."*

Henning initially suggested a mouse might explain the phenomenon, then retreated from this explanation: "we don't quite see how a bird or bat could remove the book and cover — at least, it seems unlikely to me." He had not found a bird in the church for some time.

## Significance

The incidents are consistent with the long record of minor phenomena in [[borley-church|Borley Church]] — displaced objects, heard footsteps, organ music — and with the Borley Rectory pattern of interference with specifically religious objects (holy water, relics, the Foysters' candlesticks). Price regards the church as an independent locus of psychic activity, separate from but parallel to the Rectory. See [[borley-church]] for related phenomena.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mrs. Annie Pearson** was a prominent local figure in the Borley saga who lived across the road from the Rectory site at Place Farm Cottage (also described as being near the church, just past Borley Place) [1], [2]. She was the **aunt of Mary Pearson**, the teenage maid who witnessed phenomena during the Smith incumbency, and the **mother of Winifred Arbon**, who resided in the Rectory Cottage with her husband Lionel during Harry Price's 1937–1938 investigation [3], [2]. Over the years, Mrs. Pearson served in multiple capacities, working as a **daily scrubbing woman for the Foyster family** beginning in October 1930, holding a spare set of Rectory keys for Harry Price's observers when the Arbons were away, and acting as the **churchwarden and caretaker for Borley Church** [3], [4], [2].

**Skepticism and Opinions on the Haunting**
Despite her close proximity to the Rectory and her involvement in the church, **Mrs. Pearson and her husband were highly skeptical of the hauntings** [1]. When interviewed by Society for Psychical Research (SPR) investigators Mollie Goldney and Eric Dingwall, she took a "dim view" of the entire Borley story, insisting that in all her years living opposite the site, she and her husband had never experienced anything paranormal [5]. She firmly believed that the Rev. Guy and Mabel Smith vacated the Rectory simply because it was far too big and expensive to run [5]. Regarding the intense poltergeist activity during the Foyster incumbency, **she suspected that Marianne Foyster was desperate to escape her dull life at Borley and thus manufactured much of the "phenomena" herself** [5]. Mrs. Pearson also dismissed the mysterious wall writings, complaining that the Foysters' adopted daughter, Adelaide, had a "horror for scribbling" [6]. Additionally, Harry Price’s critics noted that he completely omitted Mrs. Pearson from his writings, never interviewing her despite her daily presence at the Rectory over many years [7].

**Experiences with Church Phenomena**
Despite her staunch skepticism, Mrs. Pearson was central to several inexplicable events in her capacity as churchwarden:
*   **The Sanctuary Lamp (April 1942):** Mrs. Pearson was responsible for lighting the church's sanctuary lamp every morning and extinguishing it at night [8], [9]. For about a fortnight, she found the wick had been frequently moved [9]. The Rev. A.C. Henning suggested placing a psalter or book cover over the lamp glass; however, after she did this and locked the church for the night, the book was found on the floor the next morning [9]. Mrs. Pearson kept a lookout for birds or bats that might have caused the disturbance, but never saw any inside the church [9]. She and another local, Mrs. Gooch, also found the altar lamp inexplicably smashed on two separate occasions [4].
*   **The Moving Registers:** Mrs. Pearson and Mrs. Gooch noticed that a church register was being moved repeatedly [4]. To test this, they hid the book before locking the building for the night, only to discover the next morning that the book had been taken from its hiding place and moved several yards [4]. She was also aware of other heavy objects being moved inside the locked church during the night [10].
*   **The Phantom Bell (April 24, 1949):** On Low Sunday, Mrs. Pearson and her husband distinctly heard the church bell ring at 8:00 a.m. [1]. Believing the Rev. Henning was there for communion, she did not unlock the church at her usual time of 9:00 or 10:00 a.m. [1]. When Henning arrived at 6:30 p.m. for evensong, he was surprised to find the church locked; Mrs. Pearson told him, "But you were there at eight o'clock, both my husband and I heard the bell!" [1]. Henning had actually been at Liston all morning, making this incident particularly impressive given the Pearsons' usual disbelief in the supernatural [1].

**Contradictions in the Sources**
The texts reveal a few contradictions regarding Mrs. Pearson:
*   **Residence Discrepancy:** While most sources state the Pearsons lived at Place Farm Cottage (or a cottage just past Borley Place), one chronological entry contradicts this by claiming that "Mr and Mrs Pearson in the Rectory Cottage" heard the church bell ringing in April 1949 [11], [1], [2]. 
*   **Reaction to the Ghost:** Marianne Foyster claimed to have seen an apparition of an old man in the garden and described his plum-coloured smoking jacket to Mrs. Pearson [12]. One account notes that upon hearing this description, **Mrs. Pearson's face turned "a kind of greenish white"** because she recognized the figure as the late Harry Bull [12]. However, in a later 1958 interview, Marianne offered a contradictory version of this exchange, stating that Mrs. Pearson (spelled "Pierson" in the transcript) simply replied "I don't know" in a broad Suffolk accent [13].
