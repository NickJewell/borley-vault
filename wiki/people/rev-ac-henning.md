# Reverend Alfred Clifford Henning (d. 14 January 1955)

Rector of Borley with Liston (the two livings having been amalgamated), inducted March 13, 1936; served until his death on 14 January 1955. Did not reside at Borley Rectory — lived at Liston Rectory — but was the key collaborating figure for [[harry-price|Price's]] tenancy and all subsequent work. The most continuous clerical presence in the later history of the case.

## Enabling Price's Tenancy

When Price asked Miss Ethel Bull who to approach about renting the Rectory, she directed him to Henning (May 4, 1937). Henning's reply (May 7, 1937) immediately offered to lease Price the Rectory and invited him to lunch to discuss it. At the May 19 lunch — Price describes the warmth of Liston Rectory in sharp contrast with "the deathly coldness of Borley Rectory" — the terms were agreed: **£30 per year, Henning paying the rates**. Price received the keys and became the first lay tenant since the Rectory was built in 1862.

Henning was generous throughout: he allowed Price full access, cooperated with the observers, and attended the Rectory personally many times during the tenancy.

## Active Role in the Observer Program (1937–38)

Henning participated as a witness and co-investigator, not merely as a cooperating landlord:
- Called at the Rectory on July 16, 1937, with his wife and Miss Reid; Miss Reid experienced sudden intense cold at the "cold spot" outside the Blue Room (independently confirming two earlier witnesses)
- On July 16, he joined [[mark-kerr-pearse]] and Kerr-Pearse's own friends for an informal table-rapping session: "a most extraordinary thumping sound was heard as though coming from the kitchen, and slowly moving down passage towards them" — ceased when they stood up
- On July 21, attended with Kerr-Pearse; new pencil marks were found that evening

His gardener [[herbert-mayes]] confirmed on July 16, 1937 that the "strange coat" was not present in the Rectory during his June 1 inspection — establishing the tight window within which it appeared.

## Role in the Investigation

- Cooperated with Price throughout the tenancy (1937–38) and the 1943 excavations
- Was present at the 1943 excavation when human remains were found
- Officiated at the burial of the bone fragments in [[liston-rectory|Liston churchyard]], 29 May 1945
- Conducted his own divining experiments to find lost church plate (nothing found)
- Wrote *Haunted Borley* (1949) — a booklet on the case, now rare
- Organized the "Psychic Fête" at the ruins on June 21, 1939 for church restoration funds

## Paranormal Experiences

At [[borley-church]] in particular:
- Heard mysterious footsteps approaching the church on several occasions
- Heard organ music on the church path with Mrs Walrond (October 1947) — both rushed in to find the church empty and silent
- Experienced objects moved in the locked church (sanctuary lamp book found on floor)
- Three incidents in 1949: loud knocks near the Waldegrave monument during a catechism class; the church bell ringing when no one was there on Low Sunday; difficulty locking the church door ("as if someone was holding the lock back")
- The strange light in Room 7 of the empty Rectory: reported by Henning and his wife among others

## Character

"Quiet-spoken, gentle in manner and appearance." His wife was "hospitable, much-travelled, and well-educated, a person of integrity." Both were convinced of the authenticity of the Borley haunting. Died suddenly on 14 January 1955.

## *Haunted Borley* Booklet — Content (brfa-ch05)

Babbs and Mathias obtained a copy of the now-rare booklet. Of its ten short chapters, only three concern the alleged haunting; the rest provide context. Key first-hand phenomena described by Henning:

- **Footsteps in the rectory with Kerr-Pearse:** locked and sealed house, then sound of a door opening and footsteps along the stone-flagged kitchen passage with a swishing sound (as of long trailing garments) coming toward the study — ceased immediately on investigation; nobody there
- **The rotten wood incident:** Kerr-Pearse showed him a piece of rotten wood that had appeared overnight in the sewing room near the fireplace; no soot on it; Henning checked the chimney flue and found the soot undisturbed
- **The women's coats mystery:** his wife found a tattered woman's jacket on a bedroom door; asked Herbert Mayes to burn it. Then Price found a different blue jacket in the Blue Room. Mayes confirmed he had burned the first one; the second was a completely different garment
- **Herbert Mayes's phantom horses:** March 1939, Mayes cycling home up Hall Lane heard horses trotting; pulled onto verge, shone lamp — nothing visible; the sound of hooves passed by and continued down toward the Stour. Mayes reluctant to discuss it for fear of ridicule
- **Alice Reid's cold spot:** July 1937, Reid felt suddenly very cold on the landing outside the Blue Room; moved away and felt warmer; returned to same spot and experienced extreme cold again. Thermometer experiments at this spot later recorded up to 11°F variance
- **Fresh wall markings under observation:** Price's group toured the house, ringed all existing marks, returned to base room, then re-toured — fresh marks had appeared in the 30-minute interval despite no one leaving the locked room
- **Church: children locked in:** Sunday school children heard footsteps, key turned in the lock from outside — but no one was there when the children were released; footsteps were heard approaching before the key turned, but no departing footsteps
- **Church: footsteps with Price, 4 June 1947:** while Price, Mrs Henning and Henning examined the restored altar stone, birds screeched; then footsteps heard in the porch; no one there, no time to hide
- **Church: organ music with Mrs Walrond, 15 October 1947:** both heard "proper music" from outside, rushed in to find church empty and organ locked. Corroborated by Jephcott (September 1947) and later by Maud Stearn (1955) and Thomas Musk (1987)

Henning died unexpectedly on 14 January 1955 due to "a spell of exceptionally cold weather." His widow continued to lecture about the haunting. CM knew Mrs Henning and found her "a truthful and down-to-earth person."

Note: When the SPR report (*The Haunting of Borley Rectory*) was leaked to a journalist in late 1954, Mrs Henning said: "My husband and I think that this investigation is a dastardly attack on Mr Price when he is no longer here to defend himself."
