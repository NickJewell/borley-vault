# Reverend Alfred Clifford Henning (1888–1955)

Rector of Borley with Liston (the two livings having been amalgamated), inducted **13 March 1936**; served until his death on **14 January 1955**. Born in Forest Hill, London, he was the son of a clergyman [1, 2]. Ordained as a deacon at St Germains, Truro, in 1911, and as a priest at Lincoln in 1919 [1, 3, 4]. He worked in eight different parishes before Borley, including a curacy in South Norwood, Middlesex [1, 2, 4]. He married his wife **Annette Eva** (a B.A. of London University) in 1934; they had two sons, Richard (born 1935) and Stephen [2, 3].

Henning did not reside at Borley Rectory — he lived at Liston Rectory — but was the key collaborating figure for [[harry-price|Price's]] tenancy and all subsequent work: the most continuous clerical presence in the later history of the case.

## Character

"Quiet-spoken, gentle in manner and appearance." An Anglo-Catholic (High Church) who wore a square black biretta, rarely wore lay clothes, and introduced incense to his services, which upset some of the local agricultural workers [6]. His wife was described as "hospitable, much-travelled, and well-educated, a person of integrity." Both were convinced of the authenticity of the Borley haunting. He was glad to leave his previous urban parish of 4,000 residents, complaining that only about twenty people attended his services [3].

## Residence at Borley Place

Induced in March 1936, the Hennings originally stayed with the [[payne-family|Payne family]] at Borley Place for nine months while awaiting permanent accommodation [6–8]. A subsequent dispute over a right of way soured the relationship, and the Paynes took little further interest in church affairs during Henning's incumbency [6–8]. Appalled by the immense size and lack of amenities (no electricity or running water) of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], the Hennings moved into the much more comfortable Liston Rectory in December 1936 [2, 9–11].

## Enabling Price's Tenancy

When [[harry-price|Price]] asked Miss Ethel Bull who to approach about renting the Rectory, she directed him to Henning (May 4, 1937). Henning's reply (May 7, 1937) immediately offered to lease Price the Rectory and invited him to lunch to discuss it. At the May 19 lunch — Price describes the warmth of Liston Rectory in sharp contrast with "the deathly coldness of Borley Rectory" — the terms were agreed: **£30 per year, Henning paying the rates** [11, 15]. Price received the keys and became the first lay tenant since the Rectory was built in 1862. Henning was generous throughout: he allowed Price full access, cooperated with the observers, and attended the Rectory personally many times during the tenancy.

He essentially acted as an unpaid estate agent for the Church Commissioners, eventually warning the final buyer, [[captain-gregson|Capt. William Hart Gregson]]: **"I think I have to warn you, Capt Gregson, that this house is supposed to be very haunted"** [16].

## Active Role in the Observer Program (1937–38)

Henning participated as a witness and co-investigator, not merely as a cooperating landlord:
- Called at the Rectory on July 16, 1937, with his wife and Miss Reid; Miss Reid experienced sudden intense cold at the "cold spot" outside the Blue Room (independently confirming two earlier witnesses)
- On July 16, he joined [[mark-kerr-pearse]] and Kerr-Pearse's own friends for an informal table-rapping session: "a most extraordinary thumping sound was heard as though coming from the kitchen, and slowly moving down passage towards them" — ceased when they stood up
- On July 21, attended with Kerr-Pearse; new pencil marks were found that evening

His gardener [[herbert-mayes]] confirmed on July 16, 1937 that the "strange coat" was not present in the Rectory during his June 1 inspection — establishing the tight window within which it appeared.

## The Excavations and Relics

Henning provided local labour (his gardener, Jackson) and personally assisted with pick and shovel when Price excavated the Rectory cellars in **August 1943** [22–25]. He and Price later gave the discovered bone fragments (alleged to be the nun "[[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]") a Christian burial in Liston churchyard on **29 May 1945** [26–29].

While searching for a crypt in [[borley-church|Borley Church]] in August 1943, Henning found the pre-Reformation altar stone (*mensa*) lying upside down on sand [22, 30, 31]. In May 1947, while preparing to restore the altar, he discovered the intact vault of the [[rev-humphrey-burrough|Rev. Humphrey Burrough]] (rector 1722–1757) containing three skeletons in water [32, 33].

On **21 June 1939**, Henning co-organised a "Psychic Fête" with Price in the burnt-out ruins of the Rectory to raise funds for Borley Church [17, 18].

Though interested in clearing evil spirits by invocation, Henning refused requests to hold a specific Requiem Mass for the phantom nun because he felt her identity was too shadowy. Instead, on **All Souls' Day 1947**, he conducted a general Requiem Mass in the churchyard for any unquiet spirits [34].

## Paranormal Experiences

Henning experienced several phenomena first-hand, and became reportedly obsessed with the hauntings — some parishioners felt to the point of neglecting his duties [6].

**Footsteps in the Rectory with Kerr-Pearse:** In July 1937, inside the locked and sealed Rectory with [[mark-kerr-pearse|Mark Kerr-Pearse]], Henning heard footsteps and a swishing sound as of long trailing garments in the passage. Flinging the door open, they found the passage completely empty [35].

**Fresh wall markings under observation:** Price's group toured the house, ringed all existing marks, returned to the base room, then re-toured — fresh marks had appeared in the 30-minute interval despite no one leaving the locked room.

**The rotten wood incident:** Kerr-Pearse showed him a piece of rotten wood that had appeared overnight in the sewing room near the fireplace; no soot on it; Henning checked the chimney flue and found the soot undisturbed.

**Herbert Mayes's phantom horses:** March 1939, his gardener [[herbert-mayes|Herbert Mayes]] cycling home up Hall Lane heard horses trotting; pulled onto verge, shone lamp — nothing visible; the sound of hooves passed by and continued down toward the Stour. Mayes was reluctant to discuss it for fear of ridicule.

**Borley Church Footsteps:** On **4 June 1947**, Henning, his wife, and Price heard a sudden screeching of birds followed by distinct footsteps entering the church porch; Henning checked immediately but found no one [36, 37]. The exact same phenomenon occurred three days later when Henning was with two Cambridge students [38].

**Phantom Organ Music:** On **15 October 1947**, Henning and novelist Norah Walrond (Burke) were walking up the church path when both clearly heard the church organ playing (like a voluntary). They rushed inside to find the church empty and the organ locked [38–41]. Henning subsequently tested the theory that leftover wind in the organ caused the sounds, but found the air escaped immediately and produced no music [42].

**The Door Lock:** In the autumn of 1949, Henning struggled to lock the church door, stating: **"It was just as if someone was holding the lock back"** [43, 44].

**The Waldegrave Tomb:** On **20 March 1949**, while teaching a catechism class, Henning said the words "Christ is here," which was immediately followed by three loud, unexplained blows near the [[waldegrave-monument|Waldegrave monument]] [45–47].

**Sunday school children locked in:** Children heard footsteps, then the key turned in the lock from outside — but no one was there when the children were released; footsteps were heard approaching before the key turned, but no departing footsteps.

**Planchette:** On 30 October 1937 and 20–21 November 1937, Henning operated a planchette with [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]], receiving messages referencing the nun "Marie Lairre" [48, 49] and claiming a jewelled cup/chalice was buried in the garden [50].

## *Haunted Borley* Booklet

In **1949**, Henning wrote a 56-page booklet titled *Haunted Borley*, with a foreword by the novelist Elisabeth Goudge. The book was typed and edited by [[james-turner|James Turner]], on the strict condition that Henning omitted the Turners' names and personal experiences [19–21].

## Contradictions and the SPR Report

When objects (like a psalter placed over a sanctuary lamp) were mysteriously moved in Borley Church in 1942, Henning demonstrated occasional scepticism, writing to Price suggesting the culprit might simply be a mouse caught in the church [51, 52].

When [[mabel-smith|Mrs Mabel Smith]] wrote to the *Church Times* in 1945 denying she and her husband ever believed Borley was haunted, Henning was shocked. He forwarded the letter to Price, writing: **"I was astounded to read the enclosed letter from Mrs. Smith in the Church Times. There are still people in Borley who remember Mrs. Smith showing them the mystery light and then taking them into the Rectory and finding no light in the room."** He added that he suspected they were **"desperately anxious to have their names in print"** [53–56].

When the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) authors Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall prepared their highly critical [[spr-investigation|*Borley Report*]] (1956) attacking Price, they notably failed to interview the Hennings [12]. In response, Mrs Annette Henning fiercely defended the deceased Harry Price, calling the report **"a hateful book"** and stating: **"My husband and I think that this investigation is a dastardly attack on Mr Price when he is no longer here to defend himself"** [12, 58].

> **Contradiction:** One source attributes to Henning a 1959 statement expressing disbelief that a Black Mass had been performed near the Rectory, concluding instead: **"There seems, however, to be some foundation for the suggestion of witchcraft"** [57]. As Henning died in January 1955, this 1959 attribution is an apparent chronological error within the sources.

## Death

Henning died suddenly on **14 January 1955**, falling dead in his garage during a spell of exceptionally cold weather [1, 12–14]. His widow continued to lecture about the haunting.
