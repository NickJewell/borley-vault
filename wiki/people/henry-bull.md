# Reverend Henry Dawson Ellis Bull MA (d. 1892)

First rector of Borley; builder of [[borley-rectory]]. Father of thirteen baptized children (including [[harry-bull]] and [[ethel-bull]]); not fourteen as commonly stated.

## Birth and Early Career

Sources offer conflicting dates for his birth: some state 23 November 1831 [4], while others record his birth year as 1833 [1, 5, 6]. He was born at [[pentlow]] to the Rev. Edward Bull and Elizabeth Hoddson [5, 6]. Breaking his family's Cambridge tradition, he was educated at Wadham College, Oxford, where he was a popular undergraduate, an excellent oarsman, and a talented amateur boxer [2, 7, 8].

Before Borley, he served as a curate at Holy Trinity Church in Ely from 1858 to 1860 [2, 9]. Church records there reveal he performed his first baptism on May 1, 1858, his first burial on June 17, 1858, and officiated only a single wedding on November 7, 1859 [10]. He preached his first sermon at [[pentlow]] on Sunday, 6 June 1858, inspired by the Book of Luke: **"Except ye repent, ye shall all likewise perish"** [9], confiding in his personal diary: **"May God give me the grace both to know and to preach his holy word"** [9].

## Marriage and the Building of the Rectory

He married Caroline Sarah Foyster (a sister of a fellow clergyman from Hastings). Sources contradict the exact date of their wedding: one specifies 11 February 1862 [11], while another states November 1862 [12]. They had thirteen or fourteen children together, though [[borley-church|Borley parish registers]] only record thirteen baptisms [13, 14].

Inducted to the living of Borley in 1862, Bull lived in nearby [[borley-place|Borley Place]] while he commissioned a massive new parsonage on the site of the former [[herringham-rectory|Herringham rectory]] [12, 15]. He hired the architect John Johnson and the builder Thomas Farrow, completing the red-brick High Victorian Gothic mansion in 1863 for an estimated £3,000 [16]. He later added a large new wing in 1875–1876 [17].

**Architectural quirks:** He purchased a large marble fireplace from the 1851 Great Exhibition for the dining room, which featured carvings of monks' heads [18, 19]. He notoriously bricked up one of the dining room windows; while legend claimed he did this because a phantom nun kept staring in at the family while they ate, more practical theorists suggest he simply wanted privacy from the adjacent road [18, 20]. Researchers note he could not have done it to avoid the Window Tax, as that was repealed in 1851, twelve years before the house was built [21].

## Life at Borley

Inducted to the living in 1862, Henry Bull held it for thirty years. He and his wife knew the legend of [[the-nun]] and reportedly built the large garden pavilion partly for the purpose of watching for her appearances. Under his watch the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] acquired its name.

A classic Victorian "squarson" (squire-parson), he was a man of considerable wealth, influence, and eccentricity [2, 3]. He was a tall, broad-shouldered man of immense physical strength, described as having a strong, thoughtful, and somewhat "hard" face, clean-shaven except for mutton-chop whiskers [22, 23]. He famously referred to himself as a **"hedge parson"** and only wore his canonicals for Sunday morning service [24]. An avid sportsman and member of the Suffolk and Essex Hunt, he kept horses and frequently rode to hounds [22, 25]. A favourite pastime was changing into his ordinary clothes, lying on the floor of the drawing room or library, and potting rabbits on the lawn through the French windows using a single-shot rook rifle [3, 22, 24, 26].

"Freda" does not appear in the register and may have been a nickname. Banks notes Henry Foyster Bull's baptism record lists his birthplace as Guestingthorpe in the 1871 and 1881 census, though he is usually said to have been born at Borley Place.

## Paranormal Beliefs

Bull was well aware of the local ghost legends. He built a large, octagonal summer-house in the garden, allegedly for the express purpose of sitting and watching the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] for the ghost [27-29]. When his wife Caroline reported seeing the phantom nun on the lawn around 1890, he nonchalantly replied that **"he would not be at all surprised if she had for the previous day had been July 28th, the 'nun's day'"** [30].

Several phenomena were reported during his incumbency (accounts gathered much later, from his daughter [[ethel-bull]] and others):
- All twenty bells pealing simultaneously, without explanation
- One daughter slapped by an invisible presence
- [[ethel-bull]] saw a dark figure of an old man in a tall hat beside her bed
- His wife reportedly saw [[the-nun]] on at least one occasion

## Death

Bull died in [[borley-rectory|the Rectory's]] famous [[blue-room]]. His date of death is given by some sources as 2 May 1892 [31, 32], and by others as 7 May 1892 [33, 34]. He was buried in [[borley-church|Borley churchyard]]. Succeeded by his son [[harry-bull]] after thirty years' incumbency.

By the time he died at age 59 he had gone completely blind [32] — relatively young even by Victorian standards.

## Cause of Death — Syphilis

[[ivan-banks|Ivan Banks]] obtained Henry Dawson Bull's death certificate and found the cause of death recorded as **Locomotor Ataxia**, which medical dictionaries define as "a manifestation of tertiary syphilis" and "a degenerative disease of the spinal cord… allied to general paralysis of the insane" [32, 35, 36]. This medical record lends significant weight to persistent local rumours that he was a philanderer who was overly familiar with maidservants and fathered illegitimate children with village girls [37, 38]. No treatment for tertiary syphilis existed in 1892. See [[kate-boreham]] for Banks's theory about an affair and its consequences.

## The Katie Boreham Connection

Banks argues that [[kate-boreham|Kate Boreham]] was employed as a maid at the Rectory and became pregnant by Henry. Kate's first child was baptized at Borley on June 4, 1876 — the same year as her marriage to Walter Boreham on February 19, 1876 — suggesting possible premarital conception. A half-empty bottle of "sugar of lead" (lead acetate, usable as an abortifacient) was reportedly found in a Rectory cupboard after his death. Banks believes Kate died in 1888 as a result of trying to terminate a second pregnancy by Henry, and that her removal in the Bull family carriage (driven by coachman [[joseph-tyler|Joseph Tyler]]) accounts for the [[phantom-coach]] phenomenon.

## The Will

Bull's will, dated 22 March 1877, contained a suspicious detail: the gross value of his personal estate was listed at only £3,132 [42]. Given his affluent lifestyle, domestic staff, and massive home, researchers suggest he must have kept the bulk of his considerable wealth out of probate via trusts [43]. A later codicil (5 June 1887) also downgraded his wife's inheritance from outright ownership of the house's contents to merely having the "use" of them, suggesting a potential rift in the marriage [42, 43].

## Séance Identity

At table-tipping sessions in October 1937, an entity identified itself as Henry Dawson Bull after giving the nickname "**Carlos**" (Q.24–25; confirmed). The same séance confirmed that the entity was distressed about something that happened in the kitchen involving a servant girl and a baby (Q.26–32: "Did a servant girl die in your presence? Yes. Did she die a natural death? No. Was there a baby? Yes"). During the 1937 Glanville planchette séances, this entity claiming to be Henry Dawson Bull alleged responsibility for a "misfortune" in the Rectory kitchen involving a maid named [[kate-boreham|Katie Boreham]] [39-41], indicating she had died an unnatural death there while giving birth to a child [39, 40].
