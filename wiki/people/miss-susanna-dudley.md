# Miss Susanna Dudley

Of Stradishall Manor, near Newmarket. On Sunday 20 February 1949, Dudley visited Borley accompanied by three companions — [[miss-audrey-sawrey-cookson|Miss Audrey Sawrey-Cookson]], [[sub-lt-mciever|Sub-Lt Alastair McIver, RN]], and [[sub-lt-crossley|Sub-Lt Jonathan Crossley, RN]] — hoping to catch a glimpse of the [[the-nun|Borley Nun]]. They were disappointed on that count, but had an encounter of a different kind.

## The Strange Smell

Seeking shelter from the cold, the group crossed the road to inspect [[borley-church|Borley Church]]. Halfway up the church path, they "walked straight into" an intense smell. Dudley's account:

> "What struck us — or what made us think it was an odd smell — was that Jonathan Crossley could not smell it at all. It was terribly strong — so strong that the rest of us had to walk over and breathe clean air — but Jonathan could not smell it. Yet his sense of smell was (before and after) as good as ours."

She described it as:

> "fearfully sickly, very very heavy and clinging and sweet, perhaps with a touch of disinfectant... Personally, the thought that passed through my mind was that it smelt as I imagine balm — an embalmed body — would smell!"

The smell was sharply localised — "in a kind of bank" — about three yards long, with clearly defined edges where one walked into and out of it suddenly. Crucially, it persisted in the same location after the group inspected the church and returned ten minutes later, despite a "fairly good wind blowing at the time." The group searched for open graves, flowers, fertilizer, or any other explanation; it was February, ruling out most botanical sources.

Dudley noted it was in precisely "one place and one only, nowhere else either along the road or in the old Rectory grounds."

## Significance

The most remarkable element of this encounter is Sub-Lt Crossley's complete inability to smell what the other three found overwhelming — an anomaly that resists normal explanation (the smell was not a common allergen, and his sense of smell was verified to be functional).

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Miss Susanna Dudley** (recorded as a resident of **Stradishall Manor, near Newmarket**) was a visitor to the Borley area whose primary contribution to the case records centers on a highly subjective and localized olfactory phenomenon she and her friends experienced in 1949 [1, 2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific details, dates, quotes, and a notable media contradiction regarding her experience:

**The Visit of February 20, 1949**
On **Sunday, February 20, 1949**, Miss Dudley traveled to Borley with three friends: Miss Audrey Sawrey-Cookson, Sub-Lieutenant Alastair McIver, RN, and Sub-Lieutenant Jonathan Crossley, RN [1, 3]. 
*   Their primary objective had been to catch a glimpse of the famous Borley "Nun" [1]. 
*   After waiting unsuccessfully and feeling chilly in the wind, the group sought shelter by crossing the road to inspect Borley Church [1]. 

**The "Embalmed Body" Odor**
As the group walked halfway up the church path, they suddenly walked into a strange, overpowering smell [2]. Miss Dudley provided a detailed written report of the incident, describing the scent and the varied reactions of her party:
*   **Description of the Smell:** She described the odor as **"fearfully sickly, very very heavy and clinging and sweet, perhaps with a touch of disinfectant"** [2]. She explicitly noted, **"the thought that passed through my mind was that it smelt as I imagine balm—an embalmed body—would smell!"** [2].
*   **Subjectivity of the Phenomenon:** The scent provoked widely different reactions. Miss Sawrey-Cookson thought it smelled like incense, while Sub-Lt. McIver called it the most disgusting thing he had ever smelled [2]. Curiously, **Jonathan Crossley could not smell it at all** and insisted it was merely their imagination, despite having a perfectly normal sense of smell before and after the event [2].
*   **Physical Characteristics:** Dudley noted the smell was confined to a highly localized "patch" about three yards long, forming a kind of invisible bank that one walked into and out of suddenly, without it fading [2]. 
*   **Lack of Normal Explanations:** Despite a "fairly good wind blowing," the smell remained in the exact same location as strong as ever when they returned ten minutes later [2]. The group searched the area for natural causes (such as open graves, pigs, fertilizer, or flowering bushes) but found nothing that could account for it in the cold February weather [2].

**Media Coverage and Contradictions**
The group's experience was subsequently picked up by the national press, leading to a specific geographical contradiction in the Borley records:
*   On **May 23, 1949**, a light-hearted, perhaps tongue-in-cheek article was published in the *Daily Mail* under the headline **"Whiff of Evil at Borley"** [4].
*   The newspaper quoted Miss Dudley as saying: **"The 'wall' was of some sickly sweet perfume so powerful as to be absolutely revolting... It was an evil smell"** [4]. 
*   **The Contradiction:** The *Daily Mail* reported that the two naval officers and the young women experienced this "wall of perfume" **within the ruins of the Rectory** [4]. This directly contradicts Miss Dudley's own detailed written report, which firmly places the location of the smell as being **"halfway up the church path,"** distinctly noting that it was *not* anywhere in the old Rectory grounds [2]. This discrepancy demonstrates how the popular press often altered or sensationalized facts to fit the established narrative of the "haunted rectory" ruins.
