# Miss Susanna Dudley

Of **Stradishall Manor, near Newmarket**. On Sunday **20 February 1949**, Dudley visited Borley accompanied by three companions — [[miss-audrey-sawrey-cookson|Miss Audrey Sawrey-Cookson]], [[sub-lt-mciever|Sub-Lt Alastair McIver, RN]], and [[sub-lt-crossley|Sub-Lt Jonathan Crossley, RN]] — hoping to catch a glimpse of the [[the-nun|Borley Nun]]. They were disappointed on that count, but encountered a different kind of phenomenon.

## The Strange Smell

Seeking shelter from the cold and wind, the group crossed the road to inspect [[borley-church|Borley Church]]. Halfway up the church path, they "walked straight into" an intense smell. Dudley's own written report described it in detail:

> "What struck us — or what made us think it was an odd smell — was that Jonathan Crossley could not smell it at all. It was terribly strong — so strong that the rest of us had to walk over and breathe clean air — but Jonathan could not smell it. Yet his sense of smell was (before and after) as good as ours." [2]

She described the scent itself as:

> "fearfully sickly, very very heavy and clinging and sweet, perhaps with a touch of disinfectant... Personally, the thought that passed through my mind was that it smelt as I imagine balm — an embalmed body — would smell!" [2]

The smell was sharply localised — "in a kind of bank" — about three yards long, with clearly defined edges where one walked into and out of it suddenly. It persisted in the same location when the group returned ten minutes later, despite "a fairly good wind blowing at the time." The group searched for open graves, flowers, fertilizer, or any other explanation; it was February, ruling out most botanical sources. Dudley noted it was in "one place and one only, nowhere else either along the road or in the old Rectory grounds" [2].

The three witnesses described the smell very differently: [[miss-audrey-sawrey-cookson|Miss Sawrey-Cookson]] thought it smelled like incense; Sub-Lt McIver called it the most disgusting thing he had ever smelled.

## Media Coverage and Contradiction

The group's experience was subsequently reported in the press, creating a geographical discrepancy:

- On **23 May 1949**, the *Daily Mail* published an article headlined "Whiff of Evil at Borley," quoting Dudley as saying: "The 'wall' was of some sickly sweet perfume so powerful as to be absolutely revolting... It was an evil smell" [4].

> **Contradiction:** The *Daily Mail* reported that the experience occurred **within the ruins of the Rectory** [4]. This directly contradicts Dudley's own detailed written report, which firmly places the location **halfway up the church path**, explicitly noting it was *not* anywhere in the old Rectory grounds [2]. This discrepancy illustrates how the popular press often altered facts to fit the established "haunted rectory" narrative.

## Significance

The most remarkable element of this encounter is [[sub-lt-crossley|Sub-Lt Crossley's]] complete inability to smell what the other three found overwhelming — an anomaly that resists normal explanation (the smell was not a common allergen, and his sense of smell was verified to be functional).
