# Josef Kraus ("Marion")

Czechoslovak vaudeville telepathist and psychometrist tested by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] at the National Laboratory of Psychical Research over several years. Kraus performed under the stage name "Marion." Price assessed that he "possesses some extraordinary faculties which, however, may not be paranormal."

## Psychometry of the Touchwood Apport

Kraus psychometrized a piece of touchwood (petrified, hard, and dry) that had spontaneously appeared in the grate of the sewing-room at Borley Rectory during a visit by [[mark-kerr-pearse|M. Kerr-Pearse]] on July 18, 1937. The object had been placed in the room as a controlled item; it materialised during the observational period.

Kraus's psychometric impressions of the touchwood are reproduced by Price and analysed at length by [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] in Chapter X of *The End of Borley Rectory*. The key passage:

> *"It went to different places. I do not know which place to say at the outset… It is a sort of old-fashioned building with small buildings adjoining. A small island."*

Phythian-Adams interprets the "small island" as potentially Iona, Holy Island, or Glastonbury — suggesting the touchwood was once associated with a sacred relic held by a Catholic family. The "Marion" impression then shifts to describe "a very tall man … a public man who played a big part in public life … He died a lonely man … disappointed with life and his work. People in the Church were enemies to him."

Phythian-Adams reads this as a description of Sir Edward Waldegrave — M.P., Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster, imprisoned in the Tower for saying Mass, died 1561 — and concludes that "Marion" was effectively identifying the apport as having a Waldegrave association.

## Assessment

Price makes clear that he does not claim Kraus's impressions are definitely paranormal; the "dominant idea" / unconscious muscular action explanation applies to psychometry as much as to Planchette. But the convergence of Kraus's description of a tall, publicly powerful, church-opposed, "disappointed" man with the historical figure of Sir [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Edward Waldegrave]] was, Price and Phythian-Adams argued, too specific to dismiss entirely.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Josef Kraus** (also recorded as Joseph Kraus) was a Czechoslovakian performer who worked under the stage name **"Marion"** [1, 2]. In the Borley sources, he is identified as a well-known vaudeville telepathist, psychometrist, and hyperaesthete [1, 2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following biographical details, specific events, quotes, and conclusions regarding his involvement in the Borley Rectory investigation:

**Scientific Testing and Background**
Harry Price noted that Marion possessed **"extraordinary faculties which, however, may not be paranormal"** [1]. Nevertheless, Price considered him to be brilliantly successful as a hyperaesthete [2]. Prior to the Borley investigation, Price and S. G. Soal had conducted many experiments with Marion under laboratory test conditions for several years, and both researchers were deeply impressed with his work and his "excellent results" [1, 2].

**The November 9, 1938 Psychometry Experiment**
Marion's sole involvement in the Borley case centers on a psychometry experiment conducted on **November 9, 1938** [2-4]. Harry Price's lead observer, Sidney H. Glanville, had suggested that Marion be allowed to psychometrize the **"touchwood apport"**—a mysterious three-inch square of rotten wood that observer Mark Kerr-Pearse had found appearing inexplicably on the hearth of the Rectory's Sewing Room in July 1937 [2, 3]. 

Price was absent from this séance, leaving Glanville to conduct the inquiry [2]. The sources emphasize the strict conditions of the test: **Marion had never met Glanville, knew nothing of the Rectory's history beyond what he might have read in the press, was unaware of any connection between Glanville and Price, and was not told that the piece of rotten wood had any connection to Borley** [2]. During the initial phase of the séance, Glanville did not speak at all [2].

**Marion's Quotes and Impressions**
Upon handling the wood, Marion provided a confused narrative about the object, claiming it was part of a relic or treasure brought from "another continent" that "went to different places" [5]. He described a setting as **"a sort of old-fashioned building with small buildings adjoining. A small island"** [5]. 

Sensing intense historical conflict attached to the object, Marion stated:
> **"There is no peace or rest. There is a sort of feud or quarrel between two families—two sorts of ideas. They are not enemies, but have a quarrel between each other, not for something but for ideas or ideals. . . . Their lives did not touch"** [6].

He then described one of the object's past owners with this specific quote:
> **"One of the last owners was a very tall man, he is dead. He was a public man who played a big part in public life. . . . He worked to a certain point and became very disappointed—died a lonely man and not appreciated. They tried to blame him for many things and, during the last few years of his life, he became very queer. He was disappointed with life and his work."** [7]

Following this statement, Glanville provided Marion with some general information about the Rectory and its history [7]. Marion then added that the object had **"a general association with a church. But people in the church were enemies to him."** [7]

**Interpretations and Contradictions**
Marion's impressions were seized upon by Canon W. J. Phythian-Adams, who believed the telepathist was accurately sensing the "turbulent chaos of the Reformation" [6]. Phythian-Adams argued that Marion's description of a disappointed public man who had "enemies in the Church" perfectly matched the Tudor Catholic Sir Edward Waldegrave, a former lord of Borley who was imprisoned in the Tower of London for his loyalty to the "Old Religion" [6, 8].

Despite this enthusiastic interpretation, the sources present a major contradiction regarding the overall success of the experiment:
*   Harry Price admitted that he did not reproduce all of Marion's statements in his books because **"a great deal of it was very wide of the mark"** (though Price maintained there was "more than a germ of truth" to some utterances) [9].
*   Other historical summaries in the texts flatly dismiss the séance, stating that **"most of his predictions are wide of the mark"** and that as a whole, the results of Marion's experiment were **"inaccurate"** [3, 4].
