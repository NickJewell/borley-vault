# Josef Kraus ("Marion")

(Also recorded as Joseph Kraus) Czechoslovak vaudeville telepathist, psychometrist, and hyperaesthete who performed under the stage name **"Marion"** [1, 2]. Tested by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] at the [[national-laboratory|National Laboratory of Psychical Research]] over several years, in collaboration with S. G. Soal [1, 2]. Price assessed that Kraus "possesses some extraordinary faculties which, however, may not be paranormal," but considered him brilliantly successful as a hyperaesthete [1, 2].

## The Psychometry Experiment (9 November 1938)

Kraus's sole involvement in the Borley case centres on a psychometry experiment conducted on **9 November 1938** [2–4]. [[sidney-glanville|Sidney H. Glanville]], [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s lead observer, suggested that Marion be allowed to psychometrize the **"touchwood apport"** — a mysterious three-inch square of rotten wood that observer [[mark-kerr-pearse|Mark Kerr-Pearse]] had found appearing inexplicably on the hearth of the Rectory's Sewing Room in July 1937 [2, 3].

Price was absent from this séance, leaving Glanville to conduct the inquiry [2]. The conditions were strictly controlled: **Marion had never met Glanville, knew nothing of the Rectory's history beyond what he might have read in the press, was unaware of any connection between Glanville and Price, and was not told that the piece of rotten wood had any connection to Borley** [2]. During the initial phase, Glanville did not speak at all [2].

## Marion's Impressions

Upon handling the wood, Kraus provided a confused narrative, claiming it was part of a relic or treasure brought from "another continent" that "went to different places" [5]. He described a setting as **"a sort of old-fashioned building with small buildings adjoining. A small island"** [5].

Sensing intense historical conflict attached to the object, Marion stated:

> **"There is no peace or rest. There is a sort of feud or quarrel between two families—two sorts of ideas. They are not enemies, but have a quarrel between each other, not for something but for ideas or ideals. . . . Their lives did not touch."** [6]

He then described one of the object's past owners:

> **"One of the last owners was a very tall man, he is dead. He was a public man who played a big part in public life. . . . He worked to a certain point and became very disappointed—died a lonely man and not appreciated. They tried to blame him for many things and, during the last few years of his life, he became very queer. He was disappointed with life and his work."** [7]

After Glanville provided some general information about the Rectory and its history, Marion added that the object had **"a general association with a church. But people in the church were enemies to him."** [7]

## Interpretations

[[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] seized upon these impressions, arguing that Marion was accurately sensing the "turbulent chaos of the Reformation" [6]. He interpreted the description of a disappointed, publicly powerful man who had "enemies in the Church" as matching the Tudor Catholic [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Sir Edward Waldegrave]] — M.P., Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster, imprisoned in the Tower for saying Mass, died 1561 — effectively identifying the touchwood apport as having a Waldegrave association. Phythian-Adams interpreted the "small island" as potentially Iona, Holy Island, or Glastonbury, suggesting the touchwood was once associated with a Catholic relic.

> **Contradiction:** Price admitted that he did not reproduce all of Marion's statements in his books because **"a great deal of it was very wide of the mark"** (though Price maintained there was "more than a germ of truth" to some utterances) [9]. Other historical summaries in the texts flatly dismiss the séance, stating that **"most of his predictions are wide of the mark"** and that the results were **"inaccurate"** overall [3, 4].

Price makes clear he does not claim Kraus's impressions are definitely paranormal; the "dominant idea" / unconscious muscular action explanation applies to psychometry as much as to Planchette. But the convergence of Kraus's description with the historical figure of Sir Edward Waldegrave was, Price and Phythian-Adams argued, too specific to dismiss entirely.
