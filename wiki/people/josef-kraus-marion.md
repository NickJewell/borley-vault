# Josef Kraus ("Marion")

Czechoslovak vaudeville telepathist and psychometrist tested by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] at the National Laboratory of Psychical Research over several years. Kraus performed under the stage name "Marion." Price assessed that he "possesses some extraordinary faculties which, however, may not be paranormal."

## Psychometry of the Touchwood Apport

Kraus psychometrized a piece of touchwood (petrified, hard, and dry) that had spontaneously appeared in the grate of the sewing-room at Borley Rectory during a visit by [[mark-kerr-pearse|M. Kerr-Pearse]] on July 18, 1937. The object had been placed in the room as a controlled item; it materialised during the observational period.

Kraus's psychometric impressions of the touchwood are reproduced by Price and analysed at length by [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] in Chapter X of *The End of Borley Rectory*. The key passage:

> *"It went to different places. I do not know which place to say at the outset… It is a sort of old-fashioned building with small buildings adjoining. A small island."*

Phythian-Adams interprets the "small island" as potentially Iona, Holy Island, or Glastonbury — suggesting the touchwood was once associated with a sacred relic held by a Catholic family. The "Marion" impression then shifts to describe "a very tall man … a public man who played a big part in public life … He died a lonely man … disappointed with life and his work. People in the Church were enemies to him."

Phythian-Adams reads this as a description of Sir Edward Waldegrave — M.P., Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster, imprisoned in the Tower for saying Mass, died 1561 — and concludes that "Marion" was effectively identifying the apport as having a Waldegrave association.

## Assessment

Price makes clear that he does not claim Kraus's impressions are definitely paranormal; the "dominant idea" / unconscious muscular action explanation applies to psychometry as much as to Planchette. But the convergence of Kraus's description of a tall, publicly powerful, church-opposed, "disappointed" man with the historical figure of Sir [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Edward Waldegrave]] was, Price and Phythian-Adams argued, too specific to dismiss entirely.
