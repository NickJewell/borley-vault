# Rev. C. Sinclair

Rector of Chale, Isle of Wight, from 1940. Although he never visited Borley Rectory or participated in its investigation, Sinclair played a crucial indirect role in the Borley saga. His published account of a "haunting" at his own rectory directly provoked [[mabel-smith|Mrs Mabel Smith]] to write her famous 1945 public letter dismissing the Borley phenomena.

## Move to Chale Rectory (1940)

Rev. Sinclair and his wife moved into Chale Rectory on the Isle of Wight in 1940 [1, 2]. Upon their arrival, they were warned by local residents and former maids that the property was haunted [1, 2]. Local legends claimed that a phantom carriage and pair was frequently heard, though never seen, driving into the yard at midnight [1, 2].

## The Chale Rectory Phenomena

The Sinclairs soon found themselves the victims of "disturbances" that bore a striking resemblance to the classic phenomena reported at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]]:

- They experienced "cold spots" and heard stealthy footsteps on the landings [1, 2]
- They heard creaks, clangings, grinding, and jarring sounds [1, 2]
- Household objects and preserves were mysteriously knocked off shelves [1, 2]
- Their pet cat and dog maintained mysterious "vigils" outside the closed doors of empty rooms [1, 2]
- They even discovered mysterious pencil-scribblings on the walls — though this was quickly established to be the mundane work of a sub-tenant's child [1, 2]

Regarding the phantom coach, Sinclair noted that they clearly heard jingling harness and a **"rather stagey horses-hooves noise of a somewhat 'coconutty' quality"** [1, 2].

## Resolution and the "Rats" Explanation

Rather than calling in psychical researchers, Sinclair called in pest-control men. Two men arrived and placed little heaps of powder/poison around the house "with ancillary mutterings" [1, 2], returning three times at intervals of three days [1]. After the poison was laid, the curious noises ceased entirely [1, 2]. From then on, his standard reply to anyone inquiring about the Chale Rectory ghost was the single word: **"rats"** [1, 2].

## Impact on the Borley Saga (October 1945)

On **28 September 1945**, the *Church Times* published a review of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s book *Poltergeist Over England*, which included a long chapter on [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] [1, 2]. In the following issue (early October 1945), Sinclair wrote a letter to the *Church Times* detailing his highly similar experiences at Chale Rectory and explicitly stating that his "ghosts" were simply rats [1, 2].

Sinclair's letter was read by [[mabel-smith|Mrs Mabel Smith]] (the widow of the Rev. G. Eric Smith, who had resided at Borley in 1928–1929). Seemingly inspired by the remarkable similarities between Chale and Borley, Mrs Smith wrote her own letter to the *Church Times*, published on **19 October 1945** [3–5], publicly declaring for the first time — borrowing Sinclair's exact mundane explanation: **"I would like to state definitely that neither my husband nor myself believed the house haunted by anything else but rats and local superstition"** [3, 5].

[[peter-underwood|Underwood and Tabori]] cited Sinclair's letter as a striking parallel to the Borley case, noting: "Perhaps Mrs Smith did notice this resemblance and concluded that the 'phenomena' at Borley were due at least to a considerable extent to the same causes as at Chale." They pointedly questioned whether "rats can produce 'creaks, clangings, grinding and jarring sounds' unless they possess remarkable onomatopoeic talents."
