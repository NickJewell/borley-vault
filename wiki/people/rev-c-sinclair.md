# Rev. C. Sinclair

Rector of Chale, Isle of Wight, from 1940. Sinclair wrote a letter to the *Church Times* in October 1945, responding to an editorial about Harry Price's *Poltergeist Over England*, in which he described phenomena at Chale Rectory remarkably similar to those reported at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

## Chale Rectory Phenomena

Sinclair and his wife arrived at Chale in 1940. Former maids in the rectory claimed that strange things had happened there, including the driving into the yard at midnight of a carriage and pair — heard but not seen. The Sinclairs were "all agog on arrival for the manifestations, nor were they to be disappointed":

> "...they heard jingling as of harness, creaks, clangings, grinding and jarring sounds, and a rather stagey horses-hooves noise of a somewhat 'coconutty' quality."

Other disturbances included preserves and objects being knocked off shelves, stealthy footsteps on landings, "cold spots," and mysterious "vigils" of the cat and dog outside closed doors of empty rooms. Even pencil-scribbling was discovered — subsequently established as the work of a sub-tenant's child. Two people eventually arrived and conducted some kind of ritual with powders and "ancillary mutterings," returning three times at three-day intervals. After their final visit, "no more curious noises were heard," and thereafter Sinclair's reply to inquiries was simply: "rats."

## Significance

Underwood and Tabori cite Sinclair's letter as a striking parallel to the Borley case, noting: "Perhaps Mrs Smith did notice this resemblance and concluded that the 'phenomena' at Borley were due at least to a considerable extent to the same causes as at Chale." [[mabel-smith|Mrs Mabel Smith]] herself wrote to the *Church Times* the following week denying any haunting at Borley, citing "rats and local superstition." Underwood and Tabori pointedly questioned whether "rats can produce 'creaks, clangings, grinding and jarring sounds' unless they possess remarkable onomatopoeic talents."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Reverend C. Sinclair** was the Rector of Chale on the Isle of Wight. Although he never visited Borley Rectory or participated in its investigation, he played a crucial indirect role in the Borley saga. His published account of a "haunting" at his own rectory directly provoked Mrs. Mabel Smith to write her famous 1945 public letter dismissing the Borley phenomena. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific details, events, dates, and quotes regarding his tangential but highly impactful role in the Borley story:

**The Move to Chale Rectory (1940)**
*   Rev. Sinclair and his wife moved into Chale Rectory on the Isle of Wight in 1940 [1, 2]. 
*   Upon their arrival, they were warned by local residents and former maids that the property was haunted [1, 2]. 
*   Local legends claimed that a phantom carriage and pair was frequently heard, though never seen, driving into the yard at midnight [1, 2]. 

**The Chale Rectory "Phenomena"**
The Sinclairs soon found themselves the victims of "disturbances" that bore a striking resemblance to the classic phenomena reported at Borley Rectory:
*   They experienced "cold spots" and heard stealthy footsteps on the landings [1, 2]. 
*   They heard creaks, clangings, grinding, and jarring sounds [1, 2]. 
*   Household objects and preserves were mysteriously knocked off shelves [1, 2]. 
*   Their pet cat and dog maintained mysterious "vigils" outside the closed doors of empty rooms [1, 2]. 
*   They even discovered mysterious pencil-scribblings on the walls, though this was quickly established to be the mundane work of a sub-tenant's child [1, 2]. 
*   **Direct Quote:** Regarding the phantom coach, Rev. Sinclair noted that they clearly heard jingling harness and a **"rather stagey horses-hooves noise of a somewhat 'coconutty' quality"** [1, 2]. 

**Resolution and the "Rats" Quote**
Rather than calling in psychical researchers, Rev. Sinclair called in pest-control men. 
*   Two men arrived and placed little heaps of powder/poison around the house "with ancillary mutterings" [1, 2]. They returned three times at intervals of three days [1]. 
*   After the poison was laid, the curious noises ceased entirely [1, 2]. 
*   Rev. Sinclair concluded that his poltergeist activity was not supernatural at all. From then on, his standard reply to anyone inquiring about the Chale Rectory ghost was the single word: **"rats"** [1, 2]. 

**Impact on the Borley Saga (October 1945)**
*   On September 28, 1945, the *Church Times* published a review of Harry Price's book *Poltergeist Over England*, which included a long chapter on Borley Rectory [1, 2]. 
*   In the following issue (early October 1945), Rev. Sinclair wrote a letter to the *Church Times* detailing his highly similar experiences at Chale Rectory and explicitly stating that his "ghosts" were simply rats [1, 2]. 
*   Rev. Sinclair's letter was read by Mrs. Mabel Smith (the widow of the Rev. G. Eric Smith, who had resided at Borley in 1928–1929). Seemingly inspired by the remarkable similarities between Chale and Borley, Mrs. Smith wrote her own bombshell letter to the *Church Times*, published on October 19, 1945 [3-5]. 
*   Borrowing Rev. Sinclair's exact mundane explanation, Mrs. Smith publicly declared for the first time: **"I would like to state definitely that neither my husband nor myself believed the house haunted by anything else but rats and local superstition"** [3, 5]. 

The sources contain no further biographical details or records of Rev. C. Sinclair's life beyond this 1945 correspondence.
