# Mrs C. C. Baines

Mrs C. C. Baines was a member of the Society for Psychical Research who played a small but evidentially important role in the SPR investigators' inquiry into the Borley medals.

In January 1951, Mrs Baines wrote to [[lucie-kaye|Mrs Meeker (Lucie Kaye)]], Harry Price's former secretary, specifically to ask her about the St Ignatius medal that had "appeared" at Borley Rectory on 5 July 1929. Mrs Meeker replied on 24 January 1951:

> "It is the only medal or medallion I remember appearing in the early days and quite undoubtedly this is the one that 'appeared' on the same night as the keys."

This was crucial testimony for the SPR investigators, who were building their case that the St Ignatius medal was the only medal to appear in 1929 — and that the two different medals (a Roman Catholic confirmation medallion and a French Revolution badge) which Price described in *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940) as appearing that night were never in evidence at Borley at all, and were introduced retrospectively to support the "French Roman Catholic Nun" legend that emerged from the 1937 planchette séances.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mrs. Cecil Ryan Baines** (frequently referred to in the texts as **Mrs. C. C. Baines** or **Mrs. C. Ryan Baines**) was a dedicated psychical researcher and a devout Roman Catholic whose contributions to the Borley Rectory saga centered entirely on her extensive historical research into the Catholic elements of the haunting.

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, specific dates, events, quotes, and strong opinions regarding her involvement:

**Biographical Details and Associations**
*   **Affiliations:** She was a member of Harry Price's **Ghost Club (Member Number 371)** and knew Price well [1]. 
*   **Residence:** She resided at **The Lodge, Lincombe Lane, Boar’s Hill, Oxford** in 1940 [2], and later lived with her husband, **Lt.-Cmdr Baines**, in a flat at **Dolphin Square, London**, where she once dined with investigator Peter Underwood [1].
*   **Relationship with Harry Price:** Price respected her enough to make her his first choice as his **Literary Executor**, a prestigious position that she ultimately declined [1]. 
*   **Legacy:** She amassed a copious amount of Borley-related correspondence and papers. Upon her death, her books and papers were left to her appointed Literary Executors, **Tony Cornell and Dr. Alan Gauld**, and were later passed to **Alan Wesencraft** (the former Curator of the Harry Price Library) [1]. Her files were thoroughly examined by Wesencraft, who confirmed they contained no mention of the later Borley hoaxer Louis Mayerling (George Carter) [3].

**The "Marie Lairre" Investigations and Catholic Theories**
Mrs. Baines’s primary interest was in the planchette scripts that claimed the Borley ghost was a murdered 17th-century French nun named Marie Lairre [1, 2]. She conducted extensive continental research to establish the historical plausibility of a French nun being in England at that time:
*   In a letter to Harry Price on **October 23, 1940**, she argued that her research gave the séance fragments the "ring of truth" [2]. She pointed out that **the Restoration of Charles II in 1660 relaxed penal laws against Catholics**, leading to close communication between exiled Catholics and Englishmen who had educated their children in monasteries abroad [2]. 
*   She speculated that a member of the powerful and Catholic **Waldegrave family** might have abducted Mary Lairre, brought her to Borley, and strangled her, and that "the coach (which by that time in England would have been well established) drove off empty" [2].
*   She also offered a unique architectural theory for the haunting, suggesting that the ghost nun persistently peered through the Rectory's dining-room window because the **Italian marble mantelpiece inside was adorned with busts of two evil-looking monks** [4]. 

**The "Borley Symposium" and Unpublished Book**
Mrs. Baines had planned to write a comprehensive book on her Catholic researches regarding Borley, which was reported to be well on its way to completion at the time of her death [1]. 
*   She was slated to contribute her findings to a projected book called the **"Borley Symposium,"** which was being compiled by Paul Tabori [5].
*   However, she fiercely protected her work. In a letter to Peter Underwood on **December 22, 1948**, she complained that Tabori was "hedging" and trying to dictate her output based on publishers' requirements. She provided the defiant quote: **"I retorted that no publisher would dictate to me what was wanted; he would be lucky to get what I proposed to give"** [5]. Ultimately, she never completed her part for the symposium [5]. 

**Key Events and Direct Quotes**
Mrs. Baines was known for her outspoken nature and sharp critiques of other Borley researchers and related media:
*   **On Trevor Hall:** She was a fierce critic of Trevor Hall’s skeptical investigations. She explicitly condemned his report on the life of Marianne Foyster, describing it as **"the vilest form of dirt-digging possible"** [1].
*   **On the Upton Sinclair Film:** Writing to Canon W. John Phythian-Adams on **May 19, 1948**, she dismissed a proposed Hollywood film script about Borley Rectory written by Upton Sinclair, stating: **"with all the will in the world no one could make the script into a Hollywood production"** [6].
*   **On the Door Keys Incident:** In a **September 26, 1949** letter to Canon Phythian-Adams, she relayed a story from Price's secretary, Lucy Kaye, about a terrific clatter in the hall that resulted in all the Rectory's door keys being found in a twisted heap with a Catholic medal on top. Mrs. Baines considered this to be **"one of the most striking incidents in the whole story"** [7].
*   **The "Dodie" Bull Diary:** In **November 1970**, the investigator Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth lent Mrs. Baines the original diary of Caroline "Dodie" Bull. Mrs. Baines meticulously made a typescript copy of the extracts, and possibly a complete long-hand copy, preserving a vital piece of the Bull family history [8].
*   **Support for Croom-Hollingsworth:** During the 1980s, Croom-Hollingsworth claimed in a letter that Mrs. Baines had highly endorsed his modern Borley investigations, allegedly telling him: **"I think your investigation is greater than Harry Price’s"** [9]. 
*   **Correspondence with Mrs. Henning:** Between 1957 and 1964, Mrs. Baines maintained a regular correspondence with Annette Henning (the widow of the Rev. A. C. Henning). In these letters, Mrs. Henning staunchly defended Harry Price to Mrs. Baines, insisting he was too weak to have faked the 1943 bone excavations [10].
