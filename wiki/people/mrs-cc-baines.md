# Mrs C. C. Baines

Mrs C. C. Baines (also recorded as **Mrs Cecil Ryan Baines** or **Mrs C. Ryan Baines**) was a dedicated psychical researcher, a devout Roman Catholic, and a Member of Harry Price's [[ghost-club]] (Number 371). She resided at The Lodge, Lincombe Lane, Boar's Hill, Oxford in 1940; later she lived with her husband, **Lt.-Cmdr Baines**, in a flat at Dolphin Square, London, where she once dined with investigator [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]].

[[harry-price|Harry Price]] respected her enough to make her his first choice as his **Literary Executor** — a position she declined after examining his files and finding them "horrifying." She described Price's habit of including "rubbish" for its "sensational value" and concluded: "It was perhaps... the best thing for him to have died when he did."

In January 1951, Mrs Baines wrote to [[lucie-kaye|Mrs Meeker (Lucie Kaye)]], Harry Price's former secretary, specifically to ask her about the St Ignatius medal that had "appeared" at Borley Rectory on 5 July 1929. Mrs Meeker replied on 24 January 1951:

> "It is the only medal or medallion I remember appearing in the early days and quite undoubtedly this is the one that 'appeared' on the same night as the keys."

This was crucial testimony for the SPR investigators, who were building their case that the St Ignatius medal was the only medal to appear in 1929 — and that the two different medals (a Roman Catholic confirmation medallion and a French Revolution badge) which Price described in *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940) as appearing that night were never in evidence at Borley at all, and were introduced retrospectively to support the "French Roman Catholic Nun" legend that emerged from the 1937 planchette séances.

## The "Marie Lairre" Investigations and Catholic Theories

Mrs Baines's primary interest was in the planchette scripts that claimed the Borley ghost was a murdered 17th-century French nun named [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]. She conducted extensive continental research to establish the historical plausibility of a French nun being in England at that time:

- In a letter to Harry Price on **October 23, 1940**, she argued that her research gave the séance fragments the "ring of truth." She pointed out that the Restoration of Charles II in 1660 relaxed penal laws against Catholics, leading to close communication between exiled Catholics and Englishmen who had educated their children in monasteries abroad.
- She speculated that a member of the powerful and Catholic [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]] might have abducted Marie Lairre, brought her to Borley, and strangled her, adding that "the coach (which by that time in England would have been well established) drove off empty."
- She also offered a unique architectural theory, suggesting the ghost nun persistently peered through the Rectory's dining-room window because the Italian marble mantelpiece inside was adorned with busts of two evil-looking monks.

## The Borley Symposium and Unpublished Book

Mrs Baines had planned to write a comprehensive book on her Catholic researches regarding Borley, reported to be well on its way to completion at the time of her death. She was slated to contribute her findings to a projected book called the **"Borley Symposium"**, compiled by [[paul-tabori|Paul Tabori]]. In a letter to Underwood on **December 22, 1948**, she complained that Tabori was "hedging" and trying to dictate her output based on publishers' requirements: "I retorted that no publisher would dictate to me what was wanted; he would be lucky to get what I proposed to give." Ultimately she never completed her part.

A first draft of her unpublished manuscript survived and came into the possession of researcher Edward Babbs. Upon her death, her books and papers were left to her Literary Executors **Tony Cornell and Dr. [[alan-gauld|Alan Gauld]]**, and were later passed to **[[ah-wesencraft|Alan Wesencraft]]** (former Curator of the Harry Price Library). Wesencraft confirmed they contained no mention of the later Borley hoaxer Louis Mayerling (George Carter).

## Character, Opinions, and Key Correspondence

Mrs Baines was known for her outspoken nature and sharp critiques:

- **On Trevor Hall:** She was a fierce critic of [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]]'s skeptical investigations, explicitly condemning his report on the life of [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] as "the vilest form of dirt-digging possible."
- **On the Upton Sinclair Film:** Writing to [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon W. John Phythian-Adams]] on **May 19, 1948**, she dismissed a proposed Hollywood film script about Borley written by Upton Sinclair: "with all the will in the world no one could make the script into a Hollywood production."
- **The Door Keys Incident:** In a **September 26, 1949** letter to Canon Phythian-Adams, she relayed [[lucie-kaye|Lucie Kaye]]'s story about all the Rectory's door keys being found in a twisted heap with a Catholic medal on top, calling it "one of the most striking incidents in the whole story."
- **The Dodie Bull Diary:** In **November 1970**, investigator Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth lent Mrs Baines the original diary of Caroline "Dodie" Bull. She meticulously made a typescript copy of the extracts, and possibly a complete long-hand copy.
- **Correspondence with Mrs Henning:** Between 1957 and 1964, she maintained a regular correspondence with [[mrs-henning|Annette Henning]], who staunchly defended Price and insisted he was too weak to have faked the 1943 bone excavations.
