# Geoffrey Shaw

Older brother of [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]]; son of [[william-steele-shaw|William Steele Shaw]] and [[annie-shaw|Annie Elizabeth Shaw]]. Born 1897 — two years before Marianne.

## Known Facts

Geoffrey Shaw was the first child of the Shaw family. He visited Marianne in Canada at Salmonhurst Rectory before [[ian-greenwood|Ian Greenwood Shaw]] did, suggesting the family maintained contact across the Atlantic. Marianne confirmed this in her 1958 interview with Swanson:

> "My older brother Geoffrey had been out there to visit and then Ian came because he suffered from asthma and it was thought that the air out there would be good for him."

Beyond this, Robert Wood's account provides no further detail about Geoffrey's life or role.

## Context

Geoffrey plays a minor but confirming role in the Borley narrative: his visit to Salmonhurst establishes that the Shaw family actively engaged with Marianne's Canadian household, and that [[ian-greenwood|Ian]] was sent partly for health reasons — information Wood uses to moderate Ian's more hostile testimony about Marianne.

**Biographical Details**
Geoffrey Shaw was born in **1897** [1]. He was the eldest child and only brother of Marianne Foyster (who was born two years later in 1899 as Mary Anne Emily Rebecca Shaw) [1, 2]. Their parents were William Steele Shaw, a school teacher and private tutor, and Annie Elizabeth Shaw (née Woodyatt) [1, 2].

**The 1914 Belfast Marriage**
Geoffrey Shaw's only specific recorded involvement in the events surrounding the Borley narrative occurred on **November 12, 1914** [2, 3]. On this date, his fifteen-year-old sister Marianne married a twenty-one-year-old clerk named Harold Gifford Greenwood at St Anne’s Cathedral in Belfast [2, 3]. Geoffrey, alongside his mother, **acted as an official witness to the ceremony** [2, 3]. At this wedding, Marianne falsely gave her age as seventeen, a deception that Geoffrey and his mother must have been aware of as witnesses [2, 3].
