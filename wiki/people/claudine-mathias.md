# Claudine Mathias

Co-author of *Borley Rectory: The Final Analysis* (with [[edward-babbs]]). She is the widow of the Rev [[rev-lanfranc-mathias]], who was the last rector of Borley and Liston (1955–1967). As such, she lived at Liston Rectory for 12½ years and knew personally many of the key witnesses in the case.

## Personal Knowledge of Witnesses

Her direct acquaintance gives her testimony a unique status among Borley researchers:

- **[[ethel-bull]]** — met at the "luncheon" at Four Swans Hotel, Sudbury, before Lanfranc's induction; found her a "bird-like" elderly lady, "kind" and "not at all cantankerous." Ethel interrupted Alfred Bull when he tried to ask about "the Borley gh...?" — she never claimed the haunting was a hoax.
- **[[lady-whitehouse]]** — knew her personally; found her "very sensible and shrewd" and not easily fooled.
- **[[dr-margaret-abernethy]]** — their family GP at Long Melford; "blunt, no-nonsense Scotswoman."
- **Alfred and [[alfred-finch|Margaret Finch]]** — longtime Borley residents living near the Green.
- **Nancy Goodale (née Fisher)** — daughter of Liston rector; close friend.
- **Kathleen King (née Finch)** — Kathleen remembered by CM through parish connections.
- **Ursula Pennell** — wife of [[canon-leslie-pennell]] of Foxearth; knew her well.

CM never personally experienced paranormal phenomena at Borley, which she attributes to her busy life as a country parson's wife and mother of three, and to uncertainty about her own psychic sensitivity.

## Mrs Smith's Letter

CM considers it possible that the 1945 letter to *The Church Times* signed "G Eric-Smith" was written by an imposter. The letter placed Borley in Sussex (not Essex), signed in the name of a man who had been dead for five years, and misspelled the signature — inconsistent with a sensible, practical woman in middle age.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Claudine Mathias** was the wife of the Rev. Edward Lanfranc Morgan Mathias, the final rector to oversee the combined parishes of Borley and Liston, and she later co-authored the book *Borley Rectory – The Final Analysis* with Edward Babbs [1, 2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, relationships, and observations regarding her connection to the case:

**Marriage and Life at Liston**
Claudine married the Rev. Edward Lanfranc Morgan Mathias, M.A., on July 3, 1946 [3]. A year later they had a son, followed by a daughter three years after that [3]. 

Her husband was inducted to the living of Borley and Liston on **July 28, 1955**—a date Claudine noted with irony ("28 July (of all dates)"), as it is the traditional anniversary of the phantom nun's appearances [3, 4]. He remained in the post until 1967 [2, 5]. The family resided in Liston (first in the old rectory, and later in the new one), where Claudine came to know the people of both parishes intimately [2].

A lesser-known biographical detail is that the family did not own a car during this period; if Claudine needed to go to Borley, she had to cycle or walk up the long hill separating the two communities [6]. 

**Lack of Personal Paranormal Experiences**
When asked if she personally experienced anything paranormal at Borley, Claudine explicitly stated: **"the short answer is that I have not"** [6]. 

However, she emphasized that this did not mean she disbelieved in the paranormal [6]. She explained that she was simply kept incredibly busy as a parson's wife and the mother of three schoolchildren, managing a large Georgian house [6]. She added that she never went to Borley for the specific purpose of long-term observation, noting pragmatically that while some people are psychically aware, others "would not see a ghost if it was standing right in front of them" [6].

**Interactions with Key Borley Witnesses**
Because of her position, Claudine had unique firsthand access to some of the most famous figures in the Borley drama:
*   **The Bull Family:** Before her husband accepted the living, they were invited to "luncheon" at The Four Swans Hotel in Sudbury by Miss Ethel Bull and her brother Alfred (who was still the patron of the living) [7]. Knowing they were being "put under the microscope" to assess their suitability, Claudine sensed the word "luncheon" implied strict correctness; she wore a hat and gloves and recalled, **"in the event I was proved right to do so"** [7]. Knowing Ethel Bull personally, Claudine firmly defended her against later critics, stating she did not regard Ethel as being dishonest or possessing a hyperactive imagination [8].
*   **Lady Whitehouse:** Claudine also knew Lady Florence Whitehouse well [9]. Defending Lady Whitehouse's reports of poltergeist phenomena (such as spontaneous fires during the Foyster incumbency), Claudine dismissed Robert Wood's suggestion that Marianne Foyster had tricked the Whitehouses [9]. Claudine remembered Lady Whitehouse as **"a very sensible and shrewd person who would not have been fooled easily by any trickery"** [9].

**Publications**
Drawing on her intimate knowledge of the parish, its people, and the Rectory's history, Claudine later collaborated with local researcher Edward Babbs to write *Borley Rectory – The Final Analysis* [2, 10]. The book (published around 2001/2003) utilized personal interviews with local residents to provide a balanced assessment of the haunting, concluding that at least some of the phenomena were genuine, despite the presence of exaggeration and trickery [11, 12].
