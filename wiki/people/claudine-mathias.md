# Claudine Mathias

Co-author of *Borley Rectory: The Final Analysis* (with [[edward-babbs]]). She is the widow of the [[rev-lanfranc-mathias|Rev. Edward Lanfranc Morgan Mathias]], who was the last rector of Borley and Liston (1955–1967). As such, she lived at Liston Rectory for 12½ years and knew personally many of the key witnesses in the case.

## Marriage and Life at Liston

Claudine married Lanfranc Mathias on July 3, 1946 [3]. A year later they had a son, followed by a daughter three years after that [3]. Her husband was inducted to the living of Borley and Liston on **[[july-28th|July 28, 1955]]** — a date Claudine noted with irony ("28 July (of all dates)"), as it is the traditional anniversary of the phantom nun's appearances [3, 4]. He remained in the post until 1967 [2, 5].

The family resided in Liston (first in the old rectory, and later in the new one). A lesser-known detail is that the family did not own a car during this period; if Claudine needed to go to Borley, she had to cycle or walk up the long hill separating the two communities [6].

## Lack of Personal Paranormal Experiences

When asked if she personally experienced anything paranormal at Borley, Claudine explicitly stated: **"the short answer is that I have not"** [6]. She emphasised that this did not mean she disbelieved in the paranormal, explaining that she was simply kept incredibly busy as a parson's wife and the mother of three schoolchildren managing a large Georgian house [6]. She added pragmatically that while some people are psychically aware, others "would not see a ghost if it was standing right in front of them" [6].

## Personal Knowledge of Witnesses

Her direct acquaintance gives her testimony a unique status among Borley researchers:

- **[[ethel-bull]]** — met at "luncheon" at Four Swans Hotel, Sudbury, before Lanfranc's induction; found her "bird-like," "kind," and "not at all cantankerous." Knowing Ethel Bull personally, Claudine firmly defended her against later critics, stating she did not regard Ethel as dishonest or possessing a hyperactive imagination [7, 8]. Ethel interrupted Alfred Bull when he tried to ask about "the Borley gh...?" — she never claimed the haunting was a hoax.
- **[[lady-whitehouse]]** — knew her personally; found her **"a very sensible and shrewd person who would not have been fooled easily by any trickery"** [9], dismissing Robert Wood's suggestion that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] had tricked the Whitehouses [9].
- **[[dr-margaret-abernethy]]** — their family GP at Long Melford; "blunt, no-nonsense Scotswoman."
- **Alfred and [[alfred-finch|Margaret Finch]]** — longtime Borley residents living near the Green.
- **Nancy Goodale (née Fisher)** — daughter of Liston rector; close friend.
- **Kathleen King (née Finch)** — remembered through parish connections.
- **[[ursula-pennell]]** — wife of [[canon-leslie-pennell|Canon Pennell]] of Foxearth; knew her well.

## Mrs Smith's Letter

CM considers it possible that the 1945 letter to *The Church Times* signed "G Eric-Smith" was written by an imposter. The letter placed Borley in Sussex (not Essex), was signed in the name of a man who had been dead for five years, and misspelled the signature — inconsistent with a sensible, practical woman in middle age.

## Publications

Drawing on her intimate knowledge of the parish, its people, and the Rectory's history, Claudine later collaborated with local researcher [[edward-babbs|Edward Babbs]] to write *Borley Rectory – The Final Analysis*, published around 2001–2003 [2, 10]. The book utilised personal interviews with local residents to provide a balanced assessment of the haunting, concluding that at least some of the phenomena were genuine despite the presence of exaggeration and trickery [11, 12].
