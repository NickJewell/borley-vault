# Sir William Charles Crocker

(1886–1973) Renowned solicitor and former President of the Law Society, 1953. Dubbed "the Sherlock Holmes of Insurance," he came to prominence in the 1933 case of Leopold Harris, an insurance assessor convicted of arson — a film based on the case, *The Fire Raisers*, was released the following year.

## Borley Investigation

Crocker's involvement with Borley came through another fire case. Together with loss adjuster Colonel Cuthbert Buckle, he investigated the insurance claim submitted by [[captain-gregson]] following the fire that destroyed [[borley-rectory]] on the night of 27/28 February 1939.

Unhappy with Gregson's story as to how the fire started, Crocker and Buckle became suspicious of his claim for accidental loss totalling £7,356. Gregson had bought the Rectory for £500 with a mortgage totalling £600, then insured it and its contents for £10,000. The insurance company refused to pay out, stating that the opinion of its advisors was that Capt. Gregson had fired the place himself. The case was eventually resolved out of court for the much smaller sum of £750.

Sir William included an account of his involvement with Borley in *Far From Humdrum — A Lawyer's Life* (Hutchinson, 1967).

## Significance

Crocker's documented investigation and the insurance company's stated position that Gregson fired the property himself provides the most authoritative external evidence for the deliberate-arson theory. Unlike Anthony Hart Gregson's verbal testimony (which said his father torched it) and [[alan-gregson]]'s letters (which said it was accidental), Crocker and Buckle's professional legal and insurance opinion represents a formal institutional finding. However, as [[robert-wood]] noted, the settlement of £750 out of court does not constitute a proven finding of arson — it represents a compromise that was more economical than litigation, not a judicial determination.

> **Contradiction:** [[alan-gregson]]'s letters from Australia (1985) maintain the fire was accidental. [[anthony-gregson]] told [[richard-lee-van-den-daele]] his father "torched it for the insurance." The insurance company's advisors concluded Gregson fired it himself. The truth remains unresolved; see the main entry on [[captain-gregson]].

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Sir William Charles Crocker** (1886–1973) was a renowned solicitor who served as the President of the Law Society in 1953 [1]. Dubbed **"the Sherlock Holmes of Insurance"**, he first gained national prominence during the 1933 case of Leopold Harris, an insurance assessor who was convicted of arson—a famous case that inspired the 1934 film *The Fire Raisers* [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals that Crocker was brought into the Borley story to investigate the highly suspicious circumstances surrounding the destruction of the Rectory on the night of February 27–28, 1939 [1]. 

**The Fire Investigation and Capt. Gregson**
Acting as the lawyer for the insurance company, Crocker worked alongside the "redoubtable" loss adjuster Col. Cuthbert Buckle to examine the claim submitted by the Rectory's final owner, Capt. W. H. Gregson [1, 2]. 
*   Crocker and Buckle immediately distrusted Gregson’s account of how the fire started and took a very dim view of the financial background of his ownership [3, 4]. They noted that Gregson had purchased the Rectory for a mere £500 (raising a £600 mortgage to do so), but had subsequently insured the building and its contents for the vastly inflated sum of £10,000 [3, 4]. 
*   The two investigators carried out a careful examination of the wreckage, which even included putting the fire debris through sieves for close scrutiny [4]. 

**The "Impudent" Claim and the Settlement**
As a result of their investigation, Crocker and Buckle repudiated Gregson's claim for £7,356 for "accidental loss by fire" [2]. They bluntly informed the Captain that the insurance company was refusing to pay out because it was their advisors' firm opinion that **he had deliberately "fired the place himself"** [2, 3, 5, 6]. 
*   Despite this firm stance, the case was eventually resolved out of court on a "nuisance basis" for the much smaller sum of £750 plus costs [3, 5, 6]. 
*   Crocker justified this payout by reasoning that it was far less than the legal costs the insurance company would have incurred by debunking Gregson in court, an exercise he noted would only have achieved **"an empty judgment against this man of straw"** [5, 7].

**Memoirs and Opinions on Borley**
In 1967, Sir William published his memoirs, *Far From Humdrum – A Lawyer’s Life*, devoting Chapter 23 to his side of the Borley Rectory episode [3, 4]. 
*   He held a decidedly "jaundiced view" of the Rectory's haunted reputation [8].
*   He was equally unimpressed by the building itself, bluntly describing Borley Rectory as being an example of the worst that the **"bad taste of 1863 could produce"** [9].

**The Harry Price "Bunk" Quote**
Beyond his investigation of the fire, Crocker is highly notable in the Borley literature for publishing a damaging anecdote regarding the ghost hunter Harry Price, whom Crocker also knew from Price's exposure of the fraudulent Scottish medium Helen Duncan [10]. In his memoirs, Crocker alleged that Price—disgusted and contemptuous that the public was often unwilling to accept his debunking of fake mediums—once declared: **"People don’t want debunk - they prefer Bunk! Alright, I'll give them Bunk"** [11]. Skeptical critics have subsequently used this quote to infer that Price then deliberately set out to concoct the Borley hauntings as a massive, profitable fraud [11].
