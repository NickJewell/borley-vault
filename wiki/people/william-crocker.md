# Sir William Charles Crocker (1886–1973)

Renowned solicitor and former President of the Law Society, 1953. Dubbed **"the Sherlock Holmes of Insurance"**, he first gained national prominence during the 1933 case of Leopold Harris, an insurance assessor convicted of arson — a film based on the case, *The Fire Raisers*, was released the following year [1].

## Borley Investigation

Crocker's involvement with Borley came through another fire case. Together with the "redoubtable" loss adjuster Colonel Cuthbert Buckle, he acted as lawyer for the insurance company investigating the claim submitted by [[captain-gregson|Captain W. H. Gregson]] following the fire that destroyed [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] on the night of **27/28 February 1939** [1, 2].

Crocker and Buckle immediately distrusted Gregson's account of how the fire started. They noted that Gregson had purchased the Rectory for a mere £500 (raising a £600 mortgage to do so), but had subsequently insured the building and its contents for the vastly inflated sum of **£10,000** [3, 4]. Their careful examination of the wreckage included putting fire debris through sieves for close scrutiny [4].

## The "Impudent" Claim and the Settlement

As a result of their investigation, Crocker and Buckle repudiated Gregson's claim for **£7,356** for "accidental loss by fire." They informed the Captain that the insurance company was refusing to pay out because it was their advisors' firm opinion that he had deliberately **"fired the place himself"** [2, 3, 5, 6]. The case was eventually resolved out of court for the much smaller sum of **£750** plus costs [3, 5, 6]. Crocker justified this by reasoning that it was far less than the legal costs the insurance company would have incurred, noting it would only have achieved "an empty judgment against this man of straw" [5, 7].

> **Contradiction:** [[alan-gregson]]'s letters from Australia (1985) maintain the fire was accidental. Anthony Hart Gregson told Richard Lee Van Den Daele his father "torched it for the insurance." The insurance company's advisors concluded Gregson fired it himself. The truth remains unresolved; see the main entry on [[captain-gregson]].

## Memoirs and Opinions on Borley

In 1967, Sir William published his memoirs, *Far From Humdrum — A Lawyer's Life* (Hutchinson, 1967), devoting Chapter 23 to his side of the Borley episode [3, 4]. He held a decidedly "jaundiced view" of the Rectory's haunted reputation [8], bluntly describing Borley Rectory as being an example of the worst that the **"bad taste of 1863 could produce"** [9].

Beyond his investigation of the fire, Crocker is highly notable in the Borley literature for publishing a damaging anecdote regarding [[harry-price|Harry Price]], whom Crocker also knew from Price's exposure of the fraudulent Scottish medium Helen Duncan [10]. In his memoirs, Crocker alleged that Price — disgusted that the public was often unwilling to accept his debunking of fake mediums — once declared: **"People don't want debunk — they prefer Bunk! Alright, I'll give them Bunk"** [11]. Sceptical critics have subsequently used this quote to infer that Price then deliberately set out to concoct the Borley hauntings as a massive, profitable fraud.

## Significance

Crocker's documented investigation and the insurance company's stated position that Gregson fired the property himself provides the most authoritative external evidence for the deliberate-arson theory. As [[robert-wood|Robert Wood]] noted, the settlement of £750 out of court does not constitute a proven finding of arson — it represents a compromise that was more economical than litigation, not a judicial determination.
