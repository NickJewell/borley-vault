# Captain W. H. Gregson (William Hart Gregson, b. 1879)

Final owner of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] before its destruction. Purchased c. November 1938 for £500 (though [[alan-gregson|Alan Gregson]]'s 1985 letters give the figure as £350); insured the property and effects for £10,000. Renamed the building "Borley Priory." Came to [[harry-price|Price's]] attention through Price's November 1938 BBC broadcast.

## Background

Originally from Rusland Valley in the north of England, where his father was a local preacher and architect's apprentice. Gregson served as a Lieutenant in the Royal Engineers during the First World War [1–3]. He had a distinguished and traumatic war record at the Battle of the Somme: he supplied water under heavy shell-fire to frontline Somme trenches from 2 July 1916 onward for three weeks, during which two-thirds of his men were killed. In August 1916, he survived a severe gas attack, having removed his gas mask to call out orders to get his men to safety [3, 4]. He married [[rita-pattard-gregson|Rita Pattard]] (a Guernsey farmer's daughter) in 1920, settling on Canvey Island where he became the largest single landowner by the mid-1920s [5]. They divorced in 1934. By the late 1930s he was operating as an "Estate Management Consultant in Rural Development" [6].

### Fraud Conviction (1923)

In November 1923, Gregson became the first person since the 1875 inauguration of the Land Registry to be tried at the Essex assizes for fraudulently attempting to procure an entry and forging title deeds — he had coloured a triangular Canvey Island plot pink to claim it as his own [7, 8]. The jury found him "guilty of uttering" but without intent to defraud; the judge released him with the warning that he had been "pretty lucky" [8].

### Fascist Sympathizer

By 1935, while living in Maldon, Essex, Gregson had become an associate of Alexander Raven Thomson (British Union of Fascists policy director) and was appointed the BUF's **political agent for the Maldon Division** [2, 9]. He participated in the "tithe war" — the BUF's campaign to support beleaguered Essex and Suffolk farmers against Church tithe collection. [[richard-lee-van-den-daele|Richard Lee-Van den Daele]] later documented this Fascist affiliation. (hbrs-ch15)

## How Gregson Found Price

He heard Price's BBC broadcast on haunted houses (1 November 1938) — which described Borley without naming it — and wrote in identifying himself as the new owner. Price was struck by the coincidence that Gregson should reveal himself through exactly the channel by which Price first heard of Borley (via a newspaper editor in 1929).

## Purchase of Borley Rectory

After Harry Price's tenancy ended, Gregson purchased the Rectory, the stable cottage, and about 3.5 acres of grounds, financing it with a £600 mortgage [11–13]. Because of a clause in the sale contract to avoid confusion, he officially renamed the property **"Borley Priory"** [14–16]. Gregson moved into the adjacent stable cottage with his two sons, Alan (aged 18) and Anthony (aged 14), using the main Rectory to temporarily store his large library, furniture, and belongings [12, 15, 17]. The family never slept or ate in the main rectory.

## Pre-Fire Phenomena

During this short period, Gregson reported several bizarre occurrences to Harry Price.

**The well hatch**: He placed a heavy hundredweight cover over an open cellar well to protect his sons. Despite locking the cellar, the cover was inexplicably thrown off and found some distance away on the floor [18, 22].

**The disappearing dogs** (two separate animals):
- His black cocker spaniel, named Peter, was taken into the courtyard at night. Gregson heard footsteps over the wooden trapdoor; the dog "shrieked, and tore away, still shrieking, and we have not seen or heard of him since" [18–20].
- A second cocker spaniel puppy was purchased to replace the first and behaved exactly as its predecessor — whining and shrieking before bolting, never to return [20].

> **Contradiction:** The Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|A.C. Henning]]'s wife countered that the first dog was seen the next day having a fit in the village and was shot, while the second was run over by a car [18, 21].

**The glass of water**: Alan placed a nearly-full glass on the hall table. Next morning it was found shattered in four or five pieces, quite dry, with no trace of moisture on the polished mahogany table or floor. The house had been securely locked; no one had entered [24, 25].

**The escaping toad**: Gregson found a toad in the cellar and tied it securely in a cloth bag with two tight knots. A minute later the toad was gone — "the knots were still tied tightly, and there was no visible means of escape" [19, 23].

**Strange footprints in snow**: Coming down one morning, Gregson found a series of formless impressions tracking diagonally across the main lawn in one direction only, disappearing at the wall by the [[nuns-walk]]. The prints resembled neither human nor animal tracks [19, 26, 27]. Alan Gregson's later account describes these as iron-shod wheel tracks from the front of the rectory, down the lawn past the cedar tree, apparently "lifting off" at the shrubbery — corroborating the [[phantom-coach]] phenomenon, as no physical access for anything larger than a wheelbarrow led to that area.

**Daytime footsteps in the kitchen wing**: "So definite and real were they that I called to my son, whom I supposed had gone in and was walking about. But both the sons were in the cottage, and the doors had not even been unlocked."

## The Fire (February 27, 1939, at midnight)

At exactly midnight on 27 February 1939 — eleven months to the day after a planchette entity named [[sunex-amures|"Sunex Amures"]] threatened to burn the house down "over the hall" — the Rectory caught fire and was gutted [28–30].

Gregson was sorting a pile of books in the main hall by oil lamp when the pile reportedly "fell over onto a lamp and upsetting it" — though he noted the books were "stacked quite carefully, and in ordinary commonsense they should have remained in their stack without falling over at all." The burning oil spread among the packages; within minutes the hall was ablaze. He sent his sons to Borley Green to telephone the Sudbury Fire Brigade, then rushed to rouse them from the cottage. The Sudbury Fire Brigade arrived at c.12:15 a.m. The fire gutted the entire upper storey; the roof over the hall collapsed first [17, 31–34].

Gregson's insurance claim was subsequently rejected and settled out of court for £750 and costs.

## The Fire Night — Apparitions

During the blaze:
- A police constable asked Gregson "who were the lady and gentleman who had just preceded me in passing over the courtyard." Gregson replied there was no woman on the premises [19, 35, 36].
- Two figures were seen at upper windows during the fire — one appearing to be a girl, the other "formless" — by several villagers.
- Two other witnesses saw "figures moving amongst the flames, near the [[blue-room]] window."

## The Insurance Fraud Allegations

Although Gregson purchased the property for £500 (or £350 by Alan's account), he immediately insured the Rectory and its contents for £10,000 [11, 13]. Following the fire, he filed a claim for £7,356 for "accidental loss" [11, 37].

The insurance company enlisted lawyer Sir [[william-crocker|William Charles Crocker]] and loss adjuster Col. Cuthbert Buckle to investigate [11, 37]. Suspecting arson, they sifted the debris through quarter-inch metal sieves [38, 39]. The investigation uncovered damning discrepancies: Gregson claimed the loss of a £50 coin collection, but the sifted ashes yielded exactly one Victorian farthing [39, 40]. Furthermore, a chest of drawers claimed to contain highly-priced, initialled underwear survived the fire; the tightly packed garments were found to be "darned, worn out and fit only for the rag bag" [39, 40].

The insurers repudiated what they called an **"impudent claim,"** stating bluntly that Gregson had **"fired the place himself"** [41, 42]. To avoid the costs of fighting a "man of straw" in court, the insurance company settled out of court for the reduced sum of £750 and costs [11, 41, 43]. Crocker included this account in his memoir *Far From Humdrum* (1967). This documented repudiation is the strongest single piece of evidence for the arson interpretation.

## BBC Broadcast (April 15, 1939)

Gregson spoke on the BBC's *In Town To-night* programme, describing his pre-fire experiences and the apparitions seen in the flames, receiving a fee of 3 guineas [47, 51, 52].

## Post-Fire Exploitation and Sale

Following the fire, Gregson unapologetically attempted to monetise the ruins:
- He charged ghost-hunters admission to the site, keeping a Visitors Book and allegedly accepting £5 from a group of Polish officers for the privilege of staying overnight [47–49].
- He proposed organising commercial motor-coach trips to the ruins, printing brochures to impress coach companies [50].
- On 21 June 1939, he co-hosted the [[psychic-fete-1939|Psychic Fête]] in the ruins with Harry Price to raise funds for the local church, personally conducting guided tours [53–55].
- He offered to sell the ruins to Price for £1,100 (or £1,190) as a "memorial to scientific psychical research" [56–58].

Gregson gave Price "carte blanche" to excavate the cellars in August 1943 ([[bones-excavation]]) and was personally interested in the project. He was present on August 18 when the pendants were found. He presented Price with the large bronze yard-bell as a souvenir — it now hangs in Price's Pulborough garden. Gregson eventually sold the shell to a local contractor, Mr. Woods, reportedly for £560, for demolition [59, 60].

## Contradictions Between Gregson's Sons

Decades later, Gregson's two sons offered entirely contradictory accounts of the fire.

**[[anthony-gregson|Anthony Hart Gregson]]** (traced to Cultus Lake, British Columbia): flatly stated that his father bought the Rectory as a real estate venture and "when this didn't pan out, he torched it for the insurance" [44]. He describes his father as essentially a "man of straw."

**[[alan-gregson|Alan Gregson]]** (Tasmania, writing from Australia in 1985): denies the arson allegation, calling his father "an upright citizen" and claiming his brother suffered from "overexposure to James Cagney." Alan insists the falling books knocking over the oil lamp was a genuine accident [10, 17, 34, 45]. His 1985 letters also clarify that the family never slept or ate in the main rectory — they lived in the flat above the coach-house. Skeptics note, however, that Gregson sending both boys to fetch the fire brigade conveniently left him alone to ensure the fire took hold [45, 46]. The captain also wrote to Price in January 1940 saying he and his sons would like to return to Borley — hardly the behaviour of someone with a guilty conscience about the fire.

> **Contradiction:** [[ivan-banks|Banks]] (1994) concludes that Allan, sent off-premises to phone for help, had no reason to distrust his father's account, while Anthony apparently had inside knowledge and stated the fire was deliberate.

Babbs and Mathias conclude: "the issue of the fire is not as simple as [Wood] would have us believe; the reality is that the matter is clouded in doubts and uncertainties" — and they reject the arson allegation.

**Note on purchase price:** Banks gives the purchase price as £500 (the figure quoted to Price); the brfa source (citing Alan Gregson's 1985 letters) gives £350. This discrepancy is unresolved.

## Later Life

Gregson emigrated to Tasmania with his son Alan in 1947. Anthony stowed away on a ship and ended up in Canada. Alan was later convicted of bullion theft in the Northwest Territories (1957) and returned to Yellowknife to serve two years. Captain Gregson died in Burnie, Tasmania, on 27 July 1957.
