# Captain W. H. Gregson (William Hart Gregson, b. 1879)

Final owner of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] before its destruction. Purchased c. November 1938 for £500; insured the property and effects for £10,000. Renamed the building "Borley Priory." Came to [[harry-price|Price's]] attention through Price's November 1938 BBC broadcast.

## Background (per O'Connor, hbrs-ch15)

Born in Lancashire; father an architectural apprentice. Distinguished WWI record with the Royal Engineers: supplied water under heavy shell-fire to frontline Somme trenches (2 July 1916 onward) for three weeks; two-thirds of his men were killed. In August 1916, survived a gas attack without his gas mask (he was calling out orders to get his men to safety). Married Rita Pattard (Guernsey farmer's daughter) 1920; largest single landowner on Canvey Island by mid-1920s.

### Fraud Conviction (1923)

November 1923, Essex assizes: charged with fraudulently altering title deeds for a triangular Canvey Island plot (had coloured the plot pink to indicate ownership). Jury found him "guilty of uttering" but without intent to defraud; released with the judge's warning that he had been "pretty lucky."

### Fascist Sympathizer

By 1935, Gregson had become an associate of Alexander Raven Thomson (British Union of Fascists policy director) and was appointed the BUF's **political agent for the Maldon Division**. He participated in the "tithe war" — the BUF's campaign to support beleaguered Essex and Suffolk farmers against Church tithe collection. (hbrs-ch15)

## How Gregson Found Price

He heard Price's BBC broadcast on haunted houses (1 November 1938) and wrote in identifying himself as the new owner.

## How Gregson Found Price

He heard Price's BBC broadcast on haunted houses (November 1, 1938) — which described Borley without naming it — and wrote in. Price was struck by the coincidence that Gregson should reveal himself through exactly the channel by which Price first heard of Borley (via a newspaper editor in 1929).

## Pre-Fire Phenomena

Gregson moved to the Rectory cottage in early December 1938 with his two sons Alan (18) and Andrew (14), while gradually furnishing the main house. During this short period:

**The well hatch**: He placed a heavy cover over the open cellar well to protect his sons. Next morning, the cover had been "thrown off, and lay some distance away on the floor." The house had been securely locked; no one had entered.

**The disappearing dogs** (two separate animals):
- His black cocker spaniel, taken into the courtyard at night: Gregson heard footsteps over the wooden trapdoor; the dog "shrieked, and tore away, still shrieking, and we have not seen or heard of him since."
- A second cocker spaniel puppy, purchased to replace the first: "Going through the courtyard one day with the animal, it behaved exactly as its predecessor did. It saw something that Captain Gregson could not see, began whining and shrieking — and bolted. It has not been seen since."

**The glass of water**: Alan placed a nearly-full glass on the hall table. Next morning: found "in four or five pieces, quite dry, and not a trace of moisture either on the table (which was a polished mahogany one) nor on the floor beneath." Place definitively locked; no one had entered.

**The escaping toad**: Found a toad in the cellar; "making a bag out of a piece of cloth, placed it inside. He took the four corners of the cloth and tied them into two knots, very tightly, making it impossible for the toad to escape." A minute later: toad gone. "The knots were still tied tightly, and there was no visible means of escape."

**Strange footprints in snow**: Coming down one morning, found a series of impressions going diagonally across the main lawn in one direction only, disappearing at the wall by the [[nuns-walk]]. "Not a bit like imprints that could possibly have been made by a human foot. Nor were they like the imprints of any animal with which he was acquainted." Mystery never solved.

**Daytime footsteps in the kitchen wing**: "So definite and real were they that I called to my son, whom I supposed had gone in and was walking about. But both the sons were in the cottage, and the doors had not even been unlocked."

## The Fire (February 27, 1939, at midnight)

Gregson was sorting a pile of books in the main hall by oil lamp. The pile "fell over onto a lamp and upsetting it" — though he noted the books were "stacked quite carefully, and in ordinary commonsense they should have remained in their stack without falling over at all." The burning oil spread among the packages; within minutes the hall was ablaze. He rushed to the cottage to rouse his sons. The Sudbury Fire Brigade arrived at c.12:15 a.m. The fire gutted the entire upper storey; the roof over the hall was the first to collapse.

Gregson's insurance claim was rejected; settled out of court for £750 and costs.

## The Fire Night — Apparitions

During the blaze:
- A police constable asked Gregson "who were the lady and gentleman who had just preceded me in passing over the courtyard." Gregson: there was no woman on the premises.
- Gregson later described two figures as "cloaked"; two figures were seen at upper windows during the fire — one appearing to be a girl, the other "formless" — by several villagers.
- Two other witnesses saw "figures moving amongst the flames, near the Blue Room window."

## BBC Broadcast (April 15, 1939)

Gregson spoke on the BBC's "In Town To-night" programme, describing his pre-fire experiences and the apparitions seen in the flames.

## Companion Additional Details (brc-ii-gh)

**Death**: Died Burnie, Tasmania, 27 July 1957.

**Insurance fraud investigation**: Loss adjuster Col. Cuthbert Buckle and insurance company lawyer **William Crocker** (later Sir William Charles Crocker) investigated Gregson's insurance claim for £7,356 (building purchased for £500, insured for £10,000). Their professional judgment, as quoted by Robert Wood in *The Widow of Borley*: *"We repudiated his impudent claim for 'accidental loss by fire' — £7,356 — pleading bluntly that he had fired the place himself."* Eventually settled out of court for £750. Crocker included this in his memoir *Far From Humdrum* (1967). This documented repudiation — the insurance company's own statement — is the strongest single piece of evidence for the arson interpretation.

**Fascism**: [[richard-lee-van-den-daele|Richard Lee-Van den Daele]] discovered that Gregson, while living at Maldon, Essex in the years before buying the Rectory, was an area organiser for the British Union of Fascists — Oswald Mosley's Blackshirts.

**Sons' testimonies conflict**: Anthony Hart Gregson said his father bought the Rectory as a real estate venture and torched it for the insurance money. Alan Hart Gregson described his father as "an upright citizen." In 1947 Gregson and Alan left for Tasmania; Anthony stowed away on a ship and ended up in Canada. Alan was later convicted of bullion theft in the Northwest Territories (1957) and returned to Yellowknife to serve two years.

## End of the Rectory (ebr-ch14, ebr-ch19)

Gregson gave Price "carte blanche" to excavate the cellars in August 1943 and was personally interested in the project. He was present on August 18 when the pendants were found. He presented Price with the large bronze yard-bell as a souvenir — it now hangs in Price's Pulborough garden.

Gregson attempted to sell the ruins for £1,190 (September 1943), thinking a rich American might buy them as a "memorial to scientific psychical research." They eventually sold to a local man, reportedly for £560. Gregson subsequently went abroad (to Tasmania).

## Alan Gregson's Letters (1985) — New Evidence

[[alan-gregson|Alan Gregson]] (Captain Gregson's son, writing from Australia in 1985) provided two letters with previously unpublished details:

- Purchase price was **£350**, not £500 as reported elsewhere — the family should know what they paid
- The family **never slept or ate in the main rectory** — they lived in the flat above the coach-house
- **The fire was accidental:** the captain stood an oil lamp on a table or box while rummaging for a book; a stack of books "suddenly keeled over onto the lamp, overturning it, breaking the glass and spilling kerosene which then caught alight." The boys were sent to Borley Green to telephone the Sudbury fire brigade
- **Carriage tracks in snow:** iron-shod wheel tracks ran from the front of the rectory, down the lawn past the cedar tree, and appeared to "lift-off at the shrubbery." No physical access for anything larger than a wheelbarrow led to that area — confirming the [[phantom-coach]] phenomenon
- **The dogs:** Alan confirms both dogs (first: Peter, a spaniel-retriever cross; second: a young replacement) panicked in the courtyard and ran away, never recovered
- The captain wrote to Price in January 1940 saying he and his sons would like to return to Borley — hardly the behaviour of someone with a guilty conscience about the fire
- Alan did not personally witness any apparitions in or around the fire night

Babbs and Mathias conclude: "the issue of the fire is not as simple as [Wood] would have us believe; the reality is that the matter is clouded in doubts and uncertainties." They reject the arson allegation.

## Banks's Investigation — Two Sons, Two Stories (enbr-ch14)

[[Ivan Banks]] (1994) obtained both sons' accounts through researcher Richard Lee van den Daele, and they directly contradict each other:

**[[anthony-gregson|Anthony Hart Gregson]]** (traced to Cultus Lake, British Columbia): flatly stated in a letter that his father bought the Rectory as a real estate venture and "when this didn't pan out, he torched it for the insurance." Describes his father as essentially a "man of straw."

**[[alan-gregson|Allan Gregson]]** (Tasmania): says fire was accidental — books fell on lamp; fire brigade called immediately by both boys.

**Banks's conclusion:** The contradiction itself is revealing. Allan, sent off-premises to phone for help, had no reason to distrust his father's account. Anthony, who apparently had inside knowledge, stated it was deliberate. Banks concludes Gregson did start the fire deliberately, using both sons' absence as cover.

**Note on purchase price:** Banks gives the purchase price as £500 (the figure quoted to Price); the brfa source (citing Alan Gregson's 1985 letters) gives £350. This discrepancy across sources is unresolved.
