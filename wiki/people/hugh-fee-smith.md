# Hugh E. Fee-Smith

Friend of [[dr-hf-bellamy|Dr. H. F. Bellamy]] and [[fc-bellamy|Francis C. Bellamy]]; accompanied them on both Borley visits (October 1937 and May 1938). Experienced tactual phenomena on both occasions, listed in Appendix D under "Tactual Phenomena ('Touchings')."

## Tactual Experiences

**October 1937**: Not described in detail in the surviving report extracts.

**May 1938**: While standing with the Bellamy party outside the large cupboards in the corridor between the hall and the garden-door side passage:

> "Our friend we took down had a distinct feeling that someone had ruffled his hair lightly."

Two hours later the party returned to the same spot:

> "Fee-Smith had the sensation of someone touching his face."

Francis Bellamy notes in his covering letter: "Was it not through this garden door that the nun sometimes was seen to enter?" — the passage where the tactual phenomena occurred was the approach route from the area of the Nun's Walk and drive gate.

Fee-Smith's experiences are a subset of the Borley "touching" phenomenon, which includes [[mark-kerr-pearse]] having his coat pulled twice and [[mrs-margaret-wilson|Mrs. Mansbridge]] (correctly: Mrs. F. A. Mansbridge) having her coat belt lifted and dropped.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Hugh E. Fee-Smith** (sometimes referred to as H. E. Fee-Smith) was one of Harry Price’s "official observers" who participated in the 1937–1938 tenancy investigation of Borley Rectory [1]. He conducted his investigations in the company of his friends, Dr. Henry F. Bellamy and Dr. Bellamy's son, Francis C. Bellamy [2, 3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific events, dates, quotes, and controversies regarding his involvement:

**First Visit and the "Bell Alarm" Incident (October 16–17, 1937)**
During the weekend of October 16–17, 1937, Fee-Smith and the Bellamys conducted an observational vigil at the Rectory [3]. The team had rigged a simple electrical contact-breaker connected to a bell alarm and placed it under a pile of books on the mantelpiece of the dining room [3]. The device was designed so that if a book was disturbed, the circuit would close and ring the bell [3].

At **12:50 a.m.** on October 17, while the men were resting upstairs in the Blue Room, the bell began ringing and continued for about a minute before ceasing abruptly [4]. When Fee-Smith and his colleagues investigated, they found the books had been completely displaced, with the bottom book pushed entirely off the contact breaker [4]. The observers were utterly unable to account for this physical phenomenon [4, 5]. During this same visit, the observers also noted two fresh pencil marks and heard a faint knocking coming from the Base Room [2].

*   **Contradiction and Skepticism:** The authors of the highly critical 1956 *Borley Report* pointed out a significant omission in Harry Price's published account of this event [5]. In the original report furnished to Price, the investigators noted that a cat had entered the house the evening before the disturbance [5]. Although the observers reported ejecting the cat at **6:20 p.m.**, the S.P.R. critics argued it was entirely possible the animal had found its way back in (as the room's door was unlatched) and disturbed the books—a crucial detail Price deliberately left out of his book, *The Most Haunted House in England* [5]. 

**Second Visit and Tactile Phenomena (May 26–27, 1938)**
Fee-Smith returned to Borley Rectory with the Bellamys for a second observation period on **May 26–27, 1938** [6]. It was during this visit that he experienced startling tactile manifestations while standing outside the two large cupboards in the short corridor leading to the side garden door [7].
*   At about **7:00 p.m.**, Fee-Smith had a distinct feeling that someone had lightly ruffled his hair [7].
*   At about **10:00 p.m.**, taking up the exact same position in the dark, Fee-Smith had the clear sensation of someone touching his face [7].

In the official report submitted to Harry Price, Francis Bellamy pondered the significance of the location of Fee-Smith's experiences, providing the specific quote: **"Was it not through this garden door that the nun sometimes was seen to enter?"** [7]. 

Additionally, at about **1:00 a.m.** during this same visit, the party discovered and marked a fresh pencil line in Room No. 7, which was situated next to the Blue Room [7].
