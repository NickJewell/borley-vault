# Zoë / Sister Catherine Labouré (1806–1876)

Born Zoë Labouré at Fain-les-Moûtiers, Côte-d'Or, France in 1806; died December 31, 1876, aged seventy. She is the originator of the **"Miraculous Medal"** — the copper pendant found three feet underground beneath the Borley Rectory cellar floor in August 1943 and identified by [[father-mj-moriarty|Father Moriarty]] at Westminster Cathedral.

## Early Life

Zoë Labouré grew up in a large devout farming family of seven sons and three daughters [3]. Her mother died when she was only eight, prompting the child to resolve "that henceforth Our Lady might be her Mother" [3]. Following her first Communion at age twelve, she had to take charge of managing the family household; by eighteen her education had been so neglected that she could "hardly read or write" [4]. From earliest childhood she fasted on Fridays and Saturdays and attended early Mass daily, famously **"kneeling without support on the cold pavement, even in the frosts of winter"** [5]. A charming lesser-known detail records that the 700 to 800 pigeons kept on the family farm loved her so much that they **"came when she appeared... circling round her in the form of a crown"** [5].

## The Strange Dream and Call to the Church

When she was eighteen, Zoë had a profound dream while sleeping in her village church in which an aged priest called to her [4]. Later, while visiting her sister-in-law in Châtillon-sur-Seine, she saw a portrait of St. Vincent de Paul and instantly recognised him as the priest from her dream, interpreting this as a divine sign [4]. It took her six years to persuade her father to allow her to join the Sisters of Charity, but she was finally received into the novitiate at the Mother House in the Rue du Bac, Paris, in April 1830 [6].

## Life and Visions

Zoë entered the novitiate of the Sisters of Charity in Paris (Rue du Bac) in April 1830. That same year she experienced three visions of the Virgin Mary:

1. **April 25, 1830**: A vision of St Vincent's heart above his shrine, seen for three consecutive days. Her prudent confessor, Father Aladel, fearing deception, ordered her to dismiss the visions from her mind and tell no one [6].
2. **July 18, 1830**: The Virgin appeared at her bedside, led by a child of about five dressed in white, who brought her to the chapel, where a beautiful lady in a yellow dress and blue cloak spoke to her about future suffering ("the streets of Paris will run with blood" — fulfilled at the Commune) [7].
3. **November 27, 1830**: The Virgin appeared again, this time instructing Catherine to have a medal struck according to the model she was shown.

The design specified: the Virgin Mary standing on a globe, rays of light from her hands falling on earth, surrounded by the words: **"O Mary, Conceived Without Sin, Pray for Us Who Have Recourse to Thee."** The reverse: the letter M, a cross, two hearts (one crowned with thorns, one pierced with a sword), surrounded by twelve stars. Medals struck to this design were first produced in Paris in 1832 and their distribution became widespread throughout the Catholic world, particularly in France.

## Death, Incorruption, and Beatification

Sister Catherine Labouré died on December 31, 1876, aged seventy (the sources note she died exactly thirteen years after Borley Rectory was built [11]). As part of the process to declare her a saint, her body was exhumed by ecclesiastical authorities and found to be **"incorrupt and most wonderfully preserved"** [10]. During the exhumation, two elderly women who had been in her care as little children immediately recognised her [2, 10]. On May 28, 1933, she was beatified by Pope Pius XI at St. Peter's Basilica in Rome — a ceremony attended by kings, princes, state presidents, and 3,000 Sisters of Charity [2, 10, 12]. Today her incorrupt remains rest in the Chapel of the Mother House, Rue du Bac, Paris [12].

## Connection to Borley

The copper pendant found at Borley was identified as a specimen of the Miraculous Medal — a Catholic devotional object struck in France, typically worn by nuns and devout Catholics. [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] and Price regard its discovery under the cellar floor as consistent with the narrative of a French Catholic woman (nun or novice) at Borley in the seventeenth century — though Price's alternative explanation is that it was lost by Italian workmen ("Mercur") during the Rectory's construction in 1863.

The coincidence noted by Price: on August 7, 1943, a correspondent wrote to him about the "Blessed Catherine Labouré"; eleven days later, a Miraculous Medal with her design was found at Borley. He acknowledges this may be pure coincidence, but notes "everything connected with Borley is strange."
