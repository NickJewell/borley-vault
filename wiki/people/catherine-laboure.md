# Zoë / Sister Catherine Labouré (1806–1876)

Born Zoë Labouré at Fain-les-Moûtiers, Côte-d'Or, France in 1806; died December 31, 1876 aged seventy. She is the originator of the **"Miraculous Medal"** — the copper pendant found three feet underground beneath the Borley Rectory cellar floor in August 1943 and identified by [[father-mj-moriarty|Father Moriarty]] at Westminster Cathedral.

## Life and Visions

Zoë entered the novitiate of the Sisters of Charity in Paris (Rue du Bac) in April 1830. That same year she experienced three visions of the Virgin Mary:

1. **April 25, 1830**: A vision of St Vincent's heart above his shrine (three days before the translation of his relics)
2. **July 18, 1830**: The Virgin appeared at her bedside, led by a child of about five dressed in white, and spoke to her about future suffering ("the streets of Paris will run with blood" — fulfilled at the Commune)
3. **November 27, 1830**: The Virgin appeared again, described in detail, and told Catherine to have a medal struck according to the model she was shown

The design specified by the Virgin: the Virgin Mary standing on a globe, rays of light from her hands falling on earth, surrounded by the words: **"O Mary, Conceived Without Sin, Pray for Us Who Have Recourse to Thee."** The reverse: the letter M, a cross, two hearts (one crowned with thorns, one pierced with a sword), surrounded by twelve stars.

Medals struck to this design were first produced in Paris in 1832. Their distribution became widespread throughout the Catholic world, particularly in France.

## Beatification

Catherine Labouré died on December 31, 1876, aged seventy. On beatification in May 1933, her coffin was opened and her body found **incorrupt and wonderfully preserved**. She was beatified at St Peter's, Rome, on May 28, 1933 by Pope Pius XI.

## Connection to Borley

The copper pendant found at Borley was identified as a specimen of the Miraculous Medal — a Catholic devotional object struck in France, typically worn by nuns and devout Catholics. [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] and [[harry-price|Price]] regard its discovery under the cellar floor as consistent with the narrative of a French Catholic woman (nun or novice) at Borley in the seventeenth century — though Price's alternative explanation is that it was lost by Italian workmen ("Mercur") during the Rectory's construction in 1863.

The coincidence noted by Price: on August 7, 1943, a correspondent wrote to him about the "Blessed Catherine Labouré"; eleven days later, a Miraculous Medal with her design was found at Borley. He acknowledges this may be pure coincidence, but notes "everything connected with Borley is strange."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Zoë Labouré**, later known as **Sister Catherine** and eventually **Blessed (Saint) Catherine Labouré** (1806–1876), enters the Borley Rectory narrative due to the discovery of a copper "Miraculous Medal" bearing her iconography, which was unearthed by investigator Harry Price in the Rectory cellars in August 1943 [1, 2].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources provides the following biographical details, visions, and post-mortem marvels surrounding her life:

**Early Life and Lesser-Known Biographical Details**
*   **Childhood and Family:** Zoë Labouré was born in 1806 in the small French village of Fain-les-Moûtiers, located in the Côte-d'Or department [3]. She was raised by simple, devout Catholic parents in a large farming family of seven sons and three daughters [3]. 
*   **Loss of Her Mother:** Her mother died when Zoë was only eight years old, prompting the child to resolve "that henceforth Our Lady might be her Mother" [3].
*   **Household Duties:** Following her first Communion at age twelve, Zoë had to take charge of managing the family household, cooking, and carrying food to the field workers, as her eldest sister had left to become a Sister of Charity [3]. By the time she was eighteen, her education had been so neglected that she could "hardly read or write" [4].
*   **Extreme Piety:** From her earliest childhood, she fasted on Fridays and Saturdays, and attended early Mass daily, famously **"kneeling without support on the cold pavement, even in the frosts of winter"** [5].
*   **Affinity with Animals:** A charming lesser-known detail records that she was incredibly fond of pets, especially the 700 to 800 pigeons kept on the farm. It was noted that the birds loved her so much that they **"came when she appeared... circling round her in the form of a crown"** [5].

**The Strange Dream and Call to the Church**
When she was eighteen, Zoë had a profound dream while sleeping in her village church [4]. She dreamed of an aged priest saying Mass; when he called to her, she shrank back in fright [4]. The priest later appeared to her again in the dream while she was visiting a sick person, telling her: **"My daughter, you do well to take care of the sick. You shrink from me now, but one day you will be eager to come to me. God has great designs in your regard"** [4]. 

Later, while visiting her sister-in-law in Châtillon-sur-Seine, she saw a portrait of St. Vincent de Paul and instantly recognized him as the aged priest from her dream, interpreting this as a divine sign that she was meant to become a Sister of Charity [4]. It took her six years to persuade her father to allow her to join, but she was finally received into the novitiate at the Mother House in the Rue du Bac, Paris, in April 1830 [6].

**Visions and the "Miraculous Medal"**
Immediately upon entering the novitiate, Sister Catherine (as she was now known) experienced a series of heavenly visions:
*   **Vision of the Heart:** On April 25, 1830, during the translation of the relics of St. Vincent de Paul, she claimed to see his heart suspended above his shrine for three consecutive days [6]. Her prudent confessor, Father Aladel, fearing deception, ordered her to dismiss the visions from her mind and tell no one [6]. 
*   **The First Apparition of the Virgin Mary:** On the night of July 18, 1830, she was awakened by a radiant, beautiful child of about five years old, dressed in white, who told her: **"Come to the Chapel, the Blessed Virgin is waiting for you"** [7]. In the candlelit chapel, she heard a sound like rustling silk and saw a beautiful lady enter wearing a yellow dress and a blue cloak [7].
*   **The Medallion:** In total, the 24-year-old nun experienced three apparitions of the Virgin Mary in 1830, during which she was instructed to have a medallion struck recording the visions [8, 9]. This became the famous "Miraculous Medal" intended to bring spiritual support to its wearers [1, 8]. 

**Death, Incorruption, and Beatification**
Sister Catherine Labouré died on December 31, 1876, at the age of seventy [2, 10]. (The sources note she died exactly thirteen years after Borley Rectory was built [11]).

Decades later, as part of the process to declare her a saint, her body was exhumed by ecclesiastical authorities [2, 8]. Her corpse was found to be **"incorrupt and most wonderfully preserved"** [10]. The only signs of discoloration were where her cornette (habit) touched her face and hand, and this was easily washed away, leaving her body exactly as it was when she died [10]. During the exhumation, two elderly women who had been in her care as little children immediately recognized her [2, 10].

On May 28, 1933, she was officially beatified by Pope Pius XI at St. Peter's Basilica in Rome, a massive ceremony attended by kings, princes, state presidents, and 3,000 Sisters of Charity [2, 10, 12]. Today, her incorrupt remains rest in the Chapel of the Mother House in the Rue du Bac, Paris [12].
