# Mrs Cecil Ryan Baines

A Member of the Harry Price [[ghost-club]], Number 371. She knew [[harry-price]] well and was his choice as Literary Executor — a position she declined.

## Borley Research

Deeply interested in the possible Roman Catholic connections at Borley, Baines carried out extensive investigations into the possibility of [[marie-lairre]] being a French novice or nun, perhaps murdered at Borley, and into the reported Catholic establishment at nearby Bures and the involvement of other Roman Catholics. She planned a book on her researches, reported to be well on its way to completion at the time of her death, but it was never published.

Much of her copious correspondence with [[peter-underwood]] and [[canon-phythian-adams]] survives in Underwood's possession. She left her books and papers to [[alan-gauld]] and [[cornell-anthony|Tony Cornell]], who were appointed her Literary Executors; her Borley papers were later passed to [[ah-wesencraft|Alan Wesencraft]].

## Role in Borley Symposium

Baines was a contributor to the projected [[borley-symposium]], responsible for the historical Roman Catholic side of the case. In the event, she never completed her contribution, citing the need for considerable research on the Continent.

## Character and Controversy

She was an outspoken critic of [[trevor-hall]], describing his report into the life of [[marianne-foyster]] as "the vilest form of dirt-digging possible." She described aspects of Hall's treatment of witnesses as "a hateful book." Peter Underwood noted he dined with her and her husband Lt.-Cmdr Baines at their flat at Dolphin Square.

She also communicated with [[canon-phythian-adams]] about Borley and passed on important information — including a key letter from [[lucie-kaye]] about the key-and-medal incident on the night of 27 June 1929.

## Key Correspondence

Writing to Canon Phythian-Adams on 26 September 1949, Baines described Lucy Kaye's first-hand account of a shower of door keys found in the hall at Borley, topped by a medal, calling it "one of the most striking incidents in the whole story."

She also identified inconsistencies in the accusations against [[charles-sutton]] and drew Peter Underwood's attention to material that [[robert-hastings]] later incorporated into the [[hastings-report|Hastings Report]].
