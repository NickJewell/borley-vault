# Mrs Cecil Ryan Baines

Also recorded as **Mrs C. C. Baines** and **Mrs C. Ryan Baines**. A dedicated psychical researcher, a devout Roman Catholic, a Member of the Society for Psychical Research (SPR), and a member of Harry Price's [[ghost-club]] (Number 371). In 1940 she resided at The Lodge, Lincombe Lane, Boar's Hill, Oxford; later she lived with her husband, **Lt.-Cmdr Baines**, in a flat at Dolphin Square, London, where she once dined with investigator [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]].

[[harry-price|Harry Price]] respected her enough to make her his first choice as his **Literary Executor** after his death in 1948. After examining his files she was "horrified," discovered he had "wilfully misrepresented the facts" by including "rubbish" for its "sensational value," and withdrew from the project entirely, declining the executorship and concluding: "It was perhaps... the best thing for him to have died when he did." (She had known nothing of Borley until reading press reports of the 1939 fire and had met Price only twice.)

## Borley Research

Deeply interested in the possible Roman Catholic connections at Borley, Baines carried out extensive investigations into the possibility of [[marie-lairre]] being a French novice or nun murdered at Borley, and into the Catholic establishment at nearby [[bures-en-bray|Bures]] and the involvement of the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]].

In a letter to Harry Price on **October 23, 1940**, she argued her research gave the séance fragments the "ring of truth." She pointed out that the Restoration of Charles II in 1660 relaxed penal laws against Catholics, leading to close communication between exiled Catholics and Englishmen who had educated their children in monasteries abroad. She speculated that a Catholic Waldegrave might have abducted Marie Lairre, brought her to Borley, and strangled her — and that "the coach (which by that time in England would have been well established) drove off empty." She also offered a unique architectural theory: the ghost nun persistently peered through the Rectory's dining-room window because the Italian marble mantelpiece inside was adorned with busts of two evil-looking monks.

## Role in the Borley Symposium

Baines was a contributor to the projected [[borley-dossier|Borley Symposium]], responsible for the historical Roman Catholic side of the case. She had planned a comprehensive book on her researches, reported to be well on its way to completion at the time of her death, but it was never published. A first draft survived and came into the possession of researcher Edward Babbs. In a letter to [[peter-underwood|Underwood]] on **December 22, 1948**, she complained that [[paul-tabori|Tabori]] was "hedging" and trying to dictate her output based on publishers' requirements: "I retorted that no publisher would dictate to me what was wanted; he would be lucky to get what I proposed to give." Ultimately, she never completed her part.

## Character and Controversy

She was an outspoken critic of [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]], describing his report into the life of [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] as "the vilest form of dirt-digging possible." Writing to [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon W. John Phythian-Adams]] on **May 19, 1948**, she dismissed a proposed Hollywood film script about Borley written by Upton Sinclair: "with all the will in the world no one could make the script into a Hollywood production."

She became extremely sceptical of investigator Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth; however, Croom-Hollingsworth claimed in a letter that she had highly endorsed his investigations, allegedly telling him: "I think your investigation is greater than Harry Price's." In **November 1970**, Croom-Hollingsworth lent her the original 1885 diary of Caroline "Dodie" Bull, from which she meticulously made a typescript copy, preserving a vital piece of the Bull family history.

Between 1957 and 1964 she maintained a regular correspondence with [[mrs-henning|Annette Henning]] (the widow of Rev. Henning), who staunchly defended Price and insisted he was too physically weak to have fraudulently planted the excavated bones in 1943.

## Key Correspondence

Writing to [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] on **26 September 1949**, Baines described [[lucie-kaye|Lucie Kaye]]'s first-hand account of all the Rectory's door keys being found in the hall in a twisted heap topped by a medal, calling it "one of the most striking incidents in the whole story."

She also identified inconsistencies in the accusations against [[charles-sutton|Charles Sutton]] and drew Underwood's attention to material that [[robert-hastings|Robert Hastings]] later incorporated into the [[hastings-report|Hastings Report]].

## Legacy

Much of her copious correspondence with [[peter-underwood|Underwood]] and [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] survives in Underwood's possession. She left her books and papers to [[alan-gauld|Alan Gauld]] and [[anthony-cornell|Tony Cornell]], who were appointed her Literary Executors; her Borley papers were later passed to [[ah-wesencraft|Alan Wesencraft]]. Wesencraft confirmed they contained no mention of the later Borley hoaxer [[louis-mayerling|Louis Mayerling]] (George Carter).
