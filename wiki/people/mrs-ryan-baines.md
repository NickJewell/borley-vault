# Mrs Cecil Ryan Baines

A Member of the Harry Price [[ghost-club]], Number 371. She knew [[harry-price]] well and was his choice as Literary Executor — a position she declined.

## Borley Research

Deeply interested in the possible Roman Catholic connections at Borley, Baines carried out extensive investigations into the possibility of [[marie-lairre]] being a French novice or nun, perhaps murdered at Borley, and into the reported Catholic establishment at nearby Bures and the involvement of other Roman Catholics. She planned a book on her researches, reported to be well on its way to completion at the time of her death, but it was never published.

Much of her copious correspondence with [[peter-underwood]] and [[canon-phythian-adams]] survives in Underwood's possession. She left her books and papers to [[alan-gauld]] and [[cornell-anthony|Tony Cornell]], who were appointed her Literary Executors; her Borley papers were later passed to [[ah-wesencraft|Alan Wesencraft]].

## Role in Borley Symposium

Baines was a contributor to the projected [[borley-symposium]], responsible for the historical Roman Catholic side of the case. In the event, she never completed her contribution, citing the need for considerable research on the Continent.

## Character and Controversy

She was an outspoken critic of [[trevor-hall]], describing his report into the life of [[marianne-foyster]] as "the vilest form of dirt-digging possible." She described aspects of Hall's treatment of witnesses as "a hateful book." Peter Underwood noted he dined with her and her husband Lt.-Cmdr Baines at their flat at Dolphin Square.

She also communicated with [[canon-phythian-adams]] about Borley and passed on important information — including a key letter from [[lucie-kaye]] about the key-and-medal incident on the night of 27 June 1929.

## Key Correspondence

Writing to Canon Phythian-Adams on 26 September 1949, Baines described Lucy Kaye's first-hand account of a shower of door keys found in the hall at Borley, topped by a medal, calling it "one of the most striking incidents in the whole story."

She also identified inconsistencies in the accusations against [[charles-sutton]] and drew Peter Underwood's attention to material that [[robert-hastings]] later incorporated into the [[hastings-report|Hastings Report]].

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mrs. Cecil Ryan Baines** (frequently referred to in the texts as **Mrs. C. C. Baines** or **Mrs. C. Ryan Baines**) was a dedicated psychical researcher, a member of the Society for Psychical Research (S.P.R.), and a devout Roman Catholic [1, 2]. Her contributions to the Borley Rectory saga centered heavily on extensive historical research into the Catholic elements of the haunting. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, specific dates, events, quotes, and strong opinions regarding her involvement:

**Biographical Details and Associations**
*   **Affiliations:** She was a member of Harry Price's **Ghost Club (Member Number 371)** [1]. 
*   **Residence:** In 1940 she resided at **The Lodge, Lincombe Lane, Boar’s Hill, Oxford** [1, 3]. Later, she lived with her husband, **Lt.-Cmdr Baines**, in a flat at **Dolphin Square, London**, where she once dined with investigator Peter Underwood [1].
*   **Legacy:** She amassed a copious amount of Borley-related correspondence and papers [1]. She had planned to write a comprehensive book on her Catholic researches, which was well on its way to completion at the time of her death [1]. A first draft of this unpublished manuscript survived and came into the possession of researcher Edward Babbs [4]. Upon her death, her books and papers were left to her appointed Literary Executors, **Tony Cornell and Dr. Alan Gauld**, and were later passed to **Alan Wesencraft** (former Curator of the Harry Price Library) [1].

**The "Marie Lairre" Investigations and Catholic Theories**
Mrs. Baines’s primary interest was in the planchette scripts claiming the Borley ghost was a murdered 17th-century French nun named Marie Lairre [1]. She conducted continental research to establish the historical plausibility of a French nun being in England at that time:
*   In a letter to Harry Price on **October 23, 1940**, she argued the séance fragments had the **"ring of truth"** [3, 5]. She pointed out that **the Restoration of Charles II in 1660 relaxed penal laws against Catholics**, leading to close communication between exiled Catholics and Englishmen who educated their children in monasteries abroad [3]. 
*   She speculated that a Catholic member of the powerful **Waldegrave family** might have abducted Mary Lairre, brought her to Borley, and strangled her, and that **"the coach (which by that time in England would have been well established) drove off empty"** [3].
*   She also offered a unique architectural theory, suggesting the ghost nun persistently peered through the Rectory's dining-room window because the **Italian marble mantelpiece inside was adorned with busts of two evil-looking monks** [6]. 

**Relationship with Harry Price and the Literary Executorship**
Though Price respected her enough to make her his first choice as his **Literary Executor** after his death in 1948, her ultimate assessment of him was damning [1, 7]. 
*   Mrs. Baines had known nothing of Borley until reading press reports of the 1939 fire, and had only met Price twice [7].
*   When Price's trustees formally approached her to edit his unfinished third book (*Borley III*), she consulted the material held in his files and was **"horrified"** [8]. 
*   She discovered that Price had **"wilfully misrepresented the facts,"** including "rubbish" simply because it possessed **"sensational value"** [8]. She felt he lacked the patience and experience for checking facts to establish the truth [7]. 
*   Declining the executorship and withdrawing from the project entirely, she delivered the stinging verdict: **"It was perhaps... the best thing for him to have died when he did"** [8].

**The "Borley Symposium"**
Mrs. Baines was subsequently slated to contribute her historical findings to a projected book called the **"Borley Symposium,"** compiled by Paul Tabori [9, 10].
*   She fiercely protected her work. In a letter to Peter Underwood on **December 22, 1948**, she complained that Tabori was "hedging" by trying to dictate her output based on publishers' requirements [11]. 
*   She provided the defiant quote: **"I retorted that no publisher would dictate to me what was wanted; he would be lucky to get what I proposed to give"** [11]. Ultimately, she never completed her part [11].

**Key Events and Direct Quotes on Other Figures**
Mrs. Baines was known for her outspoken nature and sharp critiques of other Borley researchers:
*   **On Trevor Hall:** She was a fierce critic of Trevor Hall’s skeptical investigations. She explicitly condemned his report on the life of Marianne Foyster as **"the vilest form of dirt-digging possible"** [1].
*   **On Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth:** She became extremely skeptical of this modern investigator [12]. However, Croom-Hollingsworth claimed in a letter that Mrs. Baines had highly endorsed his Borley investigations, allegedly telling him: **"I think your investigation is greater than Harry Price’s"** [13]. In **November 1970**, Croom-Hollingsworth lent her the original 1885 diary of Caroline "Dodie" Bull, from which she meticulously made a typescript copy and possibly a long-hand copy [14].
*   **On the Upton Sinclair Film:** Writing to Canon W. John Phythian-Adams on **May 19, 1948**, she dismissed a proposed Hollywood film script about Borley written by Upton Sinclair, stating: **"with all the will in the world no one could make the script into a Hollywood production"** [15].
*   **On the Door Keys Incident:** In a **September 26, 1949** letter to Canon Phythian-Adams, she relayed a story from Price's secretary, Lucie Kaye, about a terrific clatter in the hall resulting in all the Rectory's door keys being found in a twisted heap with a Catholic medal on top. Mrs. Baines considered this to be **"one of the most striking incidents in the whole story"** [16].
*   **Correspondence with Mrs. Henning:** Between 1957 and 1964, Mrs. Baines maintained a regular correspondence with Annette Henning (the widow of the Rev. A. C. Henning). In these letters, Mrs. Henning staunchly defended Harry Price to Mrs. Baines, insisting he was too physically weak to have fraudulently planted the excavated bones in 1943 [17].
