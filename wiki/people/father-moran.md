# Father Moran

Roman Catholic parish priest based in **Sudbury** during the Foyster period; asked by [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] to say a Mass at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], but declined.

## The Refused Request

Following the novena that Edwin and [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] prayed together on the Rectory landing in June 1931, and after fresh [[wall-writings|wall-writing]] appeared directing them to *"Get Light, Mass, Prayers,"* Edwin became convinced that a Roman Catholic Mass should be held in the Rectory itself. He asked Father Moran, the local Catholic priest in Sudbury, if he would come to Borley to hold a Mass and offer prayers in the house to ward off the disturbances [2, 3].

Father Moran refused. According to interviews given years later by Marianne Foyster, the priest **"had too much sense for this kind of foolishness and ignored it in a diplomatic way"** [3]. She noted that Father Moran did not encourage the idea because he likely realised that Edwin was **"not exactly himself"** [3]. Edwin was known to be in a highly emotional state at the time — the result of his unstable mental state following his breakdown and incomplete treatment at Bowdon House — and "gave people rather a rough trip," leading the pragmatic priest to dismiss the young man's supernatural claims diplomatically [1, 3]. The Mass was never held at the Rectory during the Foyster period.

## Significance

Father Moran's refusal is a small but telling moment. It suggests that the Catholic clergy in the area, while not unsympathetic to the idea of a distressed spirit seeking requiem, were wary of the specific dynamic at play — particularly Edwin's emotional vulnerability and [[dom-richard-whitehouse|his religious obsessiveness]]. The Mass that was eventually said for the soul of "Marie Lairre" did not take place until 30 October 1943, when [[father-john-wright|Father Wright]] of St Philip Neri's in Arundel said the Requiem.
