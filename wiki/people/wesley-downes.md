# Wesley H. Downes

Publisher, lay investigator, and ghost writer who visited Borley many times, sometimes spending a night there. A long-time member and area representative of the [[ghost-club|Ghost Club Society]] [2]. He was a friend of [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] and Dr. Peter Hilton-Rowe, and ran **Wesley's Publications**, which produced the quarterly magazine ***Essex Ghosts and Hauntings*** containing periodical articles on Borley [2].

## Publications

Downes authored the 60-page ***The Ghosts of Borley*** (1993) as part of the Wesley's Essex Collection series [2, 3]. The first thirty pages were devoted to the Borley haunting, while the rest covered other haunted sites along the Essex and Suffolk border (such as Polstead and Acton) [3]. The book provided a condensed account of the traditional Borley story alongside brief accounts of modern investigations in Borley Church during the 1970s and 1980s, and uniquely featured the **"veiled bride" photograph** taken by Geoff and Anthea Holland in the churchyard [3].

## Borley Involvement

Downes held notes of various investigations by local psychic research groups, including details of their access to the [[borley-church-crypt|church crypt]]. He retained notes suggesting that on a number of occasions when the crypt was entered, **the lead Waldegrave coffins were found inexplicably displaced** even though they had been previously straightened up, before the authorities sealed the crypt entrance [5].

## The Campsey Ashe Vigil

Downes and fellow investigator Paul Moss-Kemp went to St John the Baptist Church at [[campsey-ashe]] on the anniversary of [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]]'s death and held a vigil in the churchyard. Around midnight, the two men claimed to see mist or smoke rise directly from Foyster's grave, move horizontally, rise vertically, and then drift across the churchyard until it passed straight through the door of the closed and locked church [1]. When [[tony-broughall|Tony Broughall]] later asked what they did: **"We ran for our lives!"** [1].

## Gruesome and Lesser-Known Legends

In his writings, Downes recorded several disturbing local tales beyond the traditional Borley lore:

- **The Cromwellian Crucifixion:** Downes documented a gruesome variation of the classic monk and nun theory, dating it to between 1655 and 1658 [6, 7]. According to this report, a Catholic priest (named Dominic or Enoch) fell in love with a nun. Following a "terrible tragedy," the nun was murdered [7]. The priest was then nailed to a cross and crucified inside [[borley-church|Borley Church]] by Oliver Cromwell's soldiers. While still half-dead, the soldiers cut him down and hung him upside down in a nearby well, leaving his body to fall into the water three days later [6, 7].
- **The "Embodiment of Evil":** Downes also recorded a story about a 17th-century cottage near Borley Lodge that held the reputation of being haunted by a "Thing" described as an **"embodiment of evil,"** though local residents refused to discuss the matter [7].

## Archival Role

Paul Moss-Kemp sent his personal notes, titled 'About My Investigations,' directly to Downes [4], making Downes an important informal archivist of local investigative activity in the 1980s and 1990s.
