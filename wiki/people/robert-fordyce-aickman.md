# Robert Fordyce Aickman

Writer and investigator. Visited [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] (ruins) multiple times in July–August 1943, motivated by "solely scientific interest." His party on the first visit included a Press Officer of the Dominions Office, the Chief Executive Secretary of *Vry Nederland* (organ of the Netherlands Government), a young Czech scientist, and his wife E.R. Gregorson.

## Visits (1943)

**First visit (July 24)**: 9:15 p.m. to 11 p.m. Only notable incident: a loud crash from inside a cupboard to the west of the drawing-room door, with dust rising in the passage. "On a later occasion when a considerable wind was blowing [we] did not hear any disturbance at all similar." The evening was perfectly still.

**Second visit (July 31 – August 1)**: Spent the night during a thunderstorm of exceptional intensity — Long Melford's electrical supply was knocked out. Nothing happened during the storm. After the storm, at 12:30 a.m.: "a sharp crack, as of a pebble thrown inside an adjoining room." At 1:30 a.m. on inspection: a wooden floor rectangle in the scullery had been lifted — in place on the 24th, now behind the scullery door. On a third visit the same day (August 1): Miss George was struck by a pebble.

Aickman reported that the pseudo-Gothic summer-house at the far end of the garden had been demolished by the military, who had excavated "on the site of the summer-house" for no obvious reason.

> **Note:** Robert Fordyce Aickman (1914–1981) was later known as an important writer of "strange tales" and co-founder of the Inland Waterways Association. His participation in Borley investigations at age 29 is consistent with his lifelong interest in the uncanny.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Robert Fordyce Aickman** was an award-winning author of supernatural fiction and a dedicated psychical researcher whose involvement with the Borley Rectory case provided both corroborating evidence of phenomena and crucial sceptical insights into the wartime investigations. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, events, dates, quotes, and contradictions regarding his life and involvement:

**Biographical Details**
*   **Birth and Death:** Born in London on **27 June 1914**, he died on **26 February 1981** [1].
*   **Literary Lineage and Career:** He was the grandson of the Victorian novelist Richard Marsh, who penned the popular supernatural thriller *The Beetle* (published the same year as Stoker’s *Dracula*) [1]. Aickman himself wrote some of the most original ghost stories of the late twentieth century, including the classic tale *Ringing the Changes*, and won the World Fantasy Award in America in 1978 [1]. 
*   **Other Achievements:** Outside of literature and the paranormal, he was a friend of Sir Peter Scott and the founder of the Inland Waterways Association in 1946 [1].
*   **Psychical Research:** Aickman was deeply interested in psychical research, serving as a member of the Ghost Club Society for many years and remaining a friend of investigator Peter Underwood for nearly four decades [1]. 

**Relationship with Harry Price**
Aickman respected Harry Price’s work, particularly his original approach to the Borley haunting, though he was not blind to his flaws [1]. Aickman once famously quipped that Price’s **"love of public speaking was only equalled by his badness as a public speaker"** [1]. 

**The August 1950 Article and a Contradiction**
In August/September 1950, Aickman published an article entitled *Postscript to Harry Price* in the *London Mystery Magazine* detailing his Borley visits [1, 2]. In it, he highlighted a curious contradiction regarding Price's secretive methods: Aickman noted that although he was in close communication with Price during the summer of 1943, **Price said absolutely nothing to him about secretly visiting the site almost immediately before and after Aickman's own visits to carry out excavations in the cellars** [2].

**The July/August 1943 Borley Investigations**
On 9 July 1943, Aickman wrote to Price asking for permission to visit the ruined Rectory with a few friends, stating they were governed **"solely by scientific interest"** [3]. He subsequently made the following visits:
*   **24 July 1943 (9:15 p.m. to 1:00 a.m.):** Aickman visited with his wife (E. R. Gregorson) and Miss Mary George (a Press Officer of the Dominions Office). During this visit, a loud crashing noise was heard [3, 4].
*   **31 July – 1 August 1943 (10:00 p.m. to 6:50 a.m.):** The party returned, joined by a Czech scientist, Mr. L. W. J. Jelinek [5, 6]. They experienced a long thunderstorm of **"exceptional intensity for England,"** making auditory observations difficult [6]. Despite a great sense of unease and a loud noise, Aickman and Jelinek watched the Nun's Walk in the dark for 45 minutes and saw nothing [5, 6]. They settled in "No. 1 Room," rotating watches while the others slept [6]. 
*   **1 August 1943 (Daytime):** Returning to the site later that day, Miss George was struck by a pebble [5, 7]. 

*Field Discoveries:* Aickman reported back to Price that the military authorities had **"completely demolished"** the pseudo-Gothic summer-house to excavate an earthwork directly on its site, and that the famous cats' cemetery was completely invisible due to a dense growth of brambles [5, 7]. 

**The 1953 SPR Interview and the "Polish Officers" Controversy**
Aickman's testimony later proved highly damaging to some of Harry Price's wartime Borley claims. On **14 January 1953**, Aickman was interviewed at the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) by Dr. Eric Dingwall and Kathleen Goldney [8]. He revealed two highly suspicious incidents from his 1943 visits:
1.  **The £5 Fee:** While at the Rectory ruins, Aickman was approached by an unknown person asking for payment for the privilege of spending the night there. When Aickman demurred, he was told that **"substantial fees were being paid"** and that a group of Polish officers had recently paid £5 for access [8, 9].
2.  **The Drunken Revellers:** During one of his night vigils, a hilarious, noisy group of people who gave the impression they had consumed **"a good deal to drink"** approached the Rectory and entered the cottage at 1:30 a.m. [8, 9]. Aickman understood that the owner, Captain Gregson, was leasing or lending the cottage to friends for weekends [8, 9].

The SPR investigators used Aickman's testimony to suggest that the spectacular phenomena reported by the Polish officers at this time were likely an elaborate hoax fuelled by alcohol and financial exploitation [8, 9].

**Later Borley Involvements**
*   Aickman later proposed a venture to buy the Rectory ruins outright so the site could be studied on a regular basis, using the Rectory Cottage as a centre of operations to continue Price's work, but the plan came to nothing [1].
*   He later attended a 100-slide lecture on the case given by Peter Underwood, which Aickman praised as **"the finest appraisal of the Borley haunting"** he had ever heard [10].
