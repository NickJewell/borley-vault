# Robert Fordyce Aickman

(27 June 1914 – 26 February 1981) Writer, psychical researcher, and co-founder of the Inland Waterways Association (1946). The grandson of the Victorian novelist Richard Marsh, who penned the popular supernatural thriller *The Beetle*, Aickman wrote some of the most original ghost stories of the late twentieth century — including the classic tale *Ringing the Changes* — and won the World Fantasy Award in 1978. He was a long-standing member of the [[ghost-club|Ghost Club Society]] and a close friend of investigator [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] for nearly four decades.

Aickman visited [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] (ruins) multiple times in July–August 1943, motivated by "solely scientific interest." He respected [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s work, though once famously quipped that Price's "love of public speaking was only equalled by his badness as a public speaker."

## Visits (1943)

**First visit (24 July 1943, 9:15 p.m. to 1:00 a.m.)**: Aickman visited with his wife (E. R. Gregorson) and Miss Mary George, a Press Officer of the Dominions Office. The only notable incident was a loud crash from inside a cupboard to the west of the drawing-room door, with dust rising in the passage: "On a later occasion when a considerable wind was blowing [we] did not hear any disturbance at all similar." The evening was perfectly still.

**Second visit (31 July – 1 August 1943, 10:00 p.m. to 6:50 a.m.)**: The party returned, joined by a Czech scientist, Mr. L. W. J. Jelinek. They experienced a thunderstorm of exceptional intensity — Long Melford's electrical supply was knocked out. Aickman and Jelinek watched the Nun's Walk in the dark for 45 minutes and saw nothing. After the storm, at 12:30 a.m., they heard a sharp crack as of a pebble thrown inside an adjoining room. At 1:30 a.m.: a wooden floor rectangle in the scullery had been lifted — in place on the 24th, now found behind the scullery door. Later the same day (1 August), daytime visit: Miss George was struck by a pebble.

Aickman reported that the pseudo-Gothic summer-house at the far end of the garden had been completely demolished by the military, who had excavated directly on its site, and that the famous cats' cemetery was completely invisible under a dense growth of brambles.

## The Secretive Price: A Contradiction

In August/September 1950, Aickman published an article entitled *Postscript to Harry Price* in the *London Mystery Magazine*. In it he highlighted a curious contradiction: although he was in close communication with Price during the summer of 1943, Price said nothing to him about secretly visiting the site almost immediately before and after Aickman's own visits to carry out excavations in the cellars [2].

## The 1953 SPR Interview and the "Polish Officers" Controversy

On 14 January 1953, Aickman was interviewed at the Society for Psychical Research by Dr [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] and [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen Goldney]]. He revealed two highly suspicious incidents from his 1943 visits:

1. **The £5 Fee**: While at the Rectory ruins, Aickman was approached by an unknown person asking for payment for the privilege of spending the night there. When he demurred, he was told that "substantial fees were being paid" and that a group of Polish officers had recently paid £5 for access [8].
2. **The Drunken Revellers**: During one of his night vigils, a noisy group who gave the impression they had consumed "a good deal to drink" approached the Rectory and entered the cottage at 1:30 a.m. Aickman understood that [[captain-gregson|Captain Gregson]] was leasing the cottage to friends for weekends [8].

The SPR investigators used Aickman's testimony to suggest that spectacular phenomena reported by Polish officers at this time were likely an elaborate hoax fuelled by alcohol and financial exploitation.

## Later Involvements

Aickman later proposed a venture to buy the Rectory ruins outright so the site could be studied on a regular basis, using the Rectory Cottage as a centre of operations to continue Price's work, but the plan came to nothing. He later attended a 100-slide lecture on the case given by [[peter-underwood|Underwood]], which he praised as "the finest appraisal of the Borley haunting" he had ever heard.

> **Note:** Robert Fordyce Aickman (1914–1981) was later known as an important writer of "strange tales" and co-founder of the Inland Waterways Association. His participation in Borley investigations at age 29 is consistent with his lifelong interest in the uncanny.
