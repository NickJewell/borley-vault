# P. H. Lord

P. H. Lord was a Cambridge undergraduate who participated in the [[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]] investigation of the Borley ruins, and who staged a successful practical joke demonstrating how easily observers could be deceived.

## The Fake Apparition (22 July 1944)

On 22 July 1944, Lord and three other undergraduates with "a sense of humour" secretly travelled to Borley with the deliberate aim of producing fake "phenomena" to test the five investigators already there as members of the Commission. They staged a performance of fake poltergeist manifestations plus a fake "nun" apparition on the lawn — P. H. Lord himself playing the apparition role "with appropriate disguises." The investigators initially missed the apparition because they had retired to the summer-house to sleep, finding the ruins demolished. However, the fake poltergeist sounds eventually drew their attention, and Lord's silent movements and sudden disappearance (he was illuminated with a torch) led them to believe the apparition was genuine. The Commission's official report recorded:

> "The silent movements of the apparition and its sudden disappearance (it was illuminated with a torch) led the investigators to think it was genuine."

## The Light Incident (30 April 1944)

Significantly, P. H. Lord was also one of the three investigators supposedly sleeping inside the ruins on 30 April 1944 when a light was observed appearing twice in rectory windows from outside — an incident the Commission's report described as one of the more difficult phenomena "to explain away." The SPR investigators noted the "somewhat significant coincidence" that the investigator who successfully impersonated the nun in July was one of the inside witnesses for the unexplained light in April.

## Significance

The fake apparition episode was included in the Commission's own report (EBR, p. 167), which the SPR investigators saw as evidence of Robertson's honesty of purpose. They also noted, however, that it "provides in itself abundant proof" that if practical jokes were being played on one visit, they may have been played on other visits without detection.
