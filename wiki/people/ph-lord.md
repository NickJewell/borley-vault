# P. H. Lord

P. H. Lord was a Cambridge undergraduate who participated in the [[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]] investigation of the Borley ruins, and who staged a successful practical joke demonstrating how easily observers could be deceived.

## The Fake Apparition (22 July 1944)

On 22 July 1944, Lord and three other undergraduates with "a sense of humour" secretly travelled to Borley with the deliberate aim of producing fake "phenomena" to test the five investigators already there as members of the Commission. They staged a performance of fake poltergeist manifestations plus a fake "nun" apparition on the lawn — P. H. Lord himself playing the apparition role "with appropriate disguises." The investigators initially missed the apparition because they had retired to the summer-house to sleep, finding the ruins demolished. However, the fake poltergeist sounds eventually drew their attention, and Lord's silent movements and sudden disappearance (he was illuminated with a torch) led them to believe the apparition was genuine. The Commission's official report recorded:

> "The silent movements of the apparition and its sudden disappearance (it was illuminated with a torch) led the investigators to think it was genuine."

## The Light Incident (30 April 1944)

Significantly, P. H. Lord was also one of the three investigators supposedly sleeping inside the ruins on 30 April 1944 when a light was observed appearing twice in rectory windows from outside — an incident the Commission's report described as one of the more difficult phenomena "to explain away." The SPR investigators noted the "somewhat significant coincidence" that the investigator who successfully impersonated the nun in July was one of the inside witnesses for the unexplained light in April.

## Significance

The fake apparition episode was included in the Commission's own report (EBR, p. 167), which the SPR investigators saw as evidence of Robertson's honesty of purpose. They also noted, however, that it "provides in itself abundant proof" that if practical jokes were being played on one visit, they may have been played on other visits without detection.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**P. H. Lord** was a University of Cambridge undergraduate who served as an investigator for the "Cambridge Commission," a student group led by Dr. Andrew J. B. Robertson that conducted a series of observational visits to the ruins of Borley Rectory between 1939 and 1944 [1, 2]. Lord's involvement is primarily remembered for a practical joke that subsequently cast doubt on the validity of the Commission's findings.

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following specific events, dates, quotes, and controversies regarding his involvement:

**Official Observational Visits**
Lord formally participated in two official overnight vigils at the Rectory site during the spring of 1944, a period when the fire-damaged building was undergoing rapid demolition:
*   **Visit No. 21 (March 20–21, 1944):** Lord investigated alongside A. G. Phillips and R. V. Weaving. The demolition was well underway, and no phenomena were reported [3].
*   **Visit No. 22 (April 30–May 1, 1944):** Lord returned with C. J. Lethbridge, T. Sullivan, R. G. Watkinson, and H. P. Lethbridge. By this time, only the kitchen wing remained standing. During this visit, a mysterious light was reportedly seen from the garden shining in the upstairs rooms where the observers were sleeping [3].

**The July 1944 Practical Joke**
Lord’s most famous contribution to the Borley saga occurred during **Visit No. 25 on the night of July 22–23, 1944**. 
*   By this date, the Rectory was completely demolished, and a team of five official investigators (P. Brennan, P. Brown, C. J. Lethbridge, R. G. Watkinson, and D. Williams) were using the large summer-house as their base [4]. 
*   Lord, accompanied by three other Cambridge students, arrived at the site in secret to play a prank on their colleagues by staging a performance of poltergeist manifestations and an apparition [2, 4]. 
*   **The Disguise and Quote:** Wearing "appropriate disguises," Lord personally impersonated the famous ghost nun. Dr. Robertson detailed the farce in his official report, providing the following specific quote: **"The investigators, on arriving and finding the Rectory non-existent, retired to the summer-house to sleep: they therefore completely failed to notice several traverses of the lawn by an ‘apparition’. However, by some Poltergeist 'manifestations', the attention of the investigators was drawn to the ‘apparition’, consisting actually of P. H. Lord with appropriate disguises"** [2]. 
*   When the investigators illuminated the figure with a torch, Lord's silent movements and sudden disappearance led them to believe they had witnessed a genuine paranormal event [2]. 

**Skepticism and Contradictions**
While Dr. Robertson included the prank in his report as proof of his commission's honesty, the skeptical authors of the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) *Borley Report* (Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) used P. H. Lord's antics to severely undermine the Commission's other evidence. 
*   The SPR authors pointed out a highly suspicious contradiction regarding the "Light in the Bedroom" phenomenon recorded on April 30, 1944. Dr. Robertson had singled out this light as one of the few visual appearances that was **"difficult to explain away"** [2].
*   However, the skeptics noted that **P. H. Lord was one of the three investigators who was supposedly asleep inside that exact room when the light was seen from the outside** [2]. 
*   The authors argued that Dr. Robertson had completely overlooked this "significant coincidence," concluding that if Lord was willing to stage an elaborate hoax as the "nun" in July, it was highly likely that he was also secretly responsible for manufacturing the mysterious bedroom light three months earlier [2].
