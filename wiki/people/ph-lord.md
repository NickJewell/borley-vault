# P. H. Lord

A University of Cambridge undergraduate who served as an investigator for the [[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]] — a student group led by Dr Andrew J. B. Robertson that conducted observational visits to the ruins of Borley Rectory between 1939 and 1944 [1, 2]. Lord's involvement is primarily remembered for a practical joke that subsequently cast doubt on the validity of the Commission's findings.

## Official Observational Visits

Lord formally participated in two official overnight vigils at the Rectory site during the spring of 1944, when the fire-damaged building was undergoing rapid demolition:

- **Visit No. 21 (20–21 March 1944):** Lord investigated alongside A. G. Phillips and R. V. Weaving. The demolition was well underway, and no phenomena were reported [3].
- **Visit No. 22 (30 April–1 May 1944):** Lord returned with C. J. Lethbridge, T. Sullivan, R. G. Watkinson, and H. P. Lethbridge. Only the kitchen wing remained standing. During this visit, a mysterious light was reportedly seen from the garden shining in the upstairs rooms where the observers were sleeping [3].

## The Fake Apparition (22 July 1944)

Lord's most famous contribution to the Borley saga occurred on **Visit No. 25, the night of 22–23 July 1944**. By this date the Rectory was completely demolished, and a team of five official investigators (P. Brennan, P. Brown, C. J. Lethbridge, R. G. Watkinson, and D. Williams) were using the large summer-house as their base [4].

Lord, accompanied by three other Cambridge students, arrived at the site in secret to play a prank on their colleagues by staging a performance of poltergeist manifestations and an apparition [2, 4]. Wearing "appropriate disguises," Lord personally impersonated [[the-nun|the famous ghost nun]]. Dr Robertson detailed the farce in his official report:

> **"The investigators, on arriving and finding the Rectory non-existent, retired to the summer-house to sleep: they therefore completely failed to notice several traverses of the lawn by an 'apparition'. However, by some Poltergeist 'manifestations', the attention of the investigators was drawn to the 'apparition', consisting actually of P. H. Lord with appropriate disguises."** [2]

When the investigators illuminated the figure with a torch, Lord's silent movements and sudden disappearance led them to believe they had witnessed a genuine paranormal event [2].

## The Light Incident (30 April 1944)

Significantly, P. H. Lord was also one of the three investigators supposedly sleeping inside the ruins on 30 April 1944 when a light was observed appearing twice in Rectory windows from outside — an incident the Commission's report described as one of the more difficult phenomena "to explain away." The SPR investigators noted the "somewhat significant coincidence" that the investigator who successfully impersonated the nun in July was one of the inside witnesses for the unexplained light in April.

The authors of the [[spr-investigation|SPR *Borley Report*]] (Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) pointed out this highly suspicious coincidence: Dr Robertson had completely overlooked it, and the authors argued that if Lord was willing to stage an elaborate hoax as the "nun" in July, it was highly likely that he was also secretly responsible for manufacturing the mysterious bedroom light three months earlier [2].

## Significance

The fake apparition episode was included in the Commission's own report (EBR, p. 167), which the SPR investigators saw as evidence of [[ajb-robertson|Robertson]]'s honesty of purpose. They also noted, however, that it "provides in itself abundant proof" that if practical jokes were being played on one visit, they may have been played on other visits without detection.
