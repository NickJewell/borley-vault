# Rev John Foster MA (Rector of Foxearth)

The Rector of Foxearth — the parish immediately adjacent to [[borley-rectory]] — who was the subject of a rape-case scandal in 1871 that was closely followed by the Bull family. [[andrew-clarke]] identifies the gossip surrounding Foster as the probable origin of the "Katie Boreham" poisoning narrative that became embedded in the Borley legend.

## The 1871 scandal

Clarke cites the *Haverhill Echo*, 5 December 1871, as documenting a rape case against Foster. The Bull family, as his immediate clerical neighbours in the Stour Valley, would have been fully aware of the case.

## Connection to the Borley legend

Clarke's argument in essay 11 is that the Bull girls assembled the Borley haunting legend piecemeal from local stories, scandals, and folklore. The Foster case provided a ready template for narratives of clerical impropriety and dark secrets in a rectory setting:

> *There was the famous rape case against the vicar of the neighbouring parish, Rev John Foster, M.A., Rector of Foxearth, (Haverhill Echo, December 5th 1871.). The Bull family were particularly close to the Fosters, and featured amongst the mourners when they eventually died. The ridiculous story of 'Katie Boreham' seems to have derived from the gossip about him.*
> — *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, essay 11

[[kate-boreham]] — the cook who died in 1888 whose bones were later supposed to be buried in the Borley cellars — is identified by Clarke as a figure whose story was shaped by pre-existing gossip about clerical scandal in the Foxearth-Borley community. Ivan Banks's investigation of the Boreham case, by contrast, focused on lead-acetate poisoning theories.

## Wider pattern

Clarke places Foster alongside [[catherine-foster-acton]] (arsenic poisoner, 1846), and the Maria Marten Red Barn Murder in neighbouring Polstead, as elements of a wider county folklore of violent crime and dark secrets that the Bull sisters wove into the Borley myth. The proximity and family closeness of the Fosters to the Bulls made their scandal especially available as raw material.

> **Note:** The exact nature of the 1871 case and Foster's fate are not described in Clarke's account beyond the single *Haverhill Echo* citation. He was separate from the later Rector of Foxearth, [[rev-bassett]], who served during the Foyster period.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Reverend John Foster, M.A.**, served as the Vicar and Rector of the neighboring parish of Foxearth during the latter half of the 19th century [1]. Though he did not reside at Borley Rectory, his scandalous private life significantly influenced the grim local folklore that eventually attached itself to the "haunted" house [1].

A reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific details regarding his life and his connection to the Borley saga:

**The 1871 Rape Case**
*   Rev. Foster was the center of a **"famous rape case"** that was publicized in the local press, specifically being reported in the *Haverhill Echo* on **December 5, 1871** [1]. 

**Relationship with the Bull Family**
*   Despite this scandal, the prominent Bull family of Borley Rectory maintained a strong relationship with the Foxearth clergyman. The texts note that the Bulls were **"particularly close to the Fosters, and featured amongst the mourners when they eventually died"** [1].

**Influence on the Borley Rectory Legends**
*   **The "Katie Boreham" Myth:** The whispered secrets and local gossip surrounding Rev. Foster's rape case directly bled into the ghost stories associated with Borley Rectory [1]. The sources state that the **"ridiculous story of 'Katie Boreham'"**—a persistent local legend concerning a servant girl who allegedly died tragically or was murdered to cover up a scandalous pregnancy at Borley Rectory—**"seems to have derived from the gossip about him"** [1].
*   **'Murder at the Parsonage':** The dark tales of rape and murder originating from neighboring parishes like Foxearth fueled the imaginations of the younger Bull sisters [1]. When they relayed these whispered secrets to Mrs. Mabel Smith (the wife of the Rev. G. Eric Smith, who resided at Borley Rectory from 1928 to 1929), the scandalous lore directly inspired Mrs. Smith to write a fictionalized account of these events titled **'Murder at the Parsonage'** [1]. 

The provided Borley sources contain no further biographical details or records regarding Rev. John Foster's life beyond this specific 1871 scandal and its impact on the Borley legend [1].
