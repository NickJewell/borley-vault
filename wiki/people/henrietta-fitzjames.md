# Henrietta FitzJames (1670–1730)

Natural daughter of King James II and his mistress [[arabella-churchill|Arabella Churchill]]; wife of Henry, first Baron [[henry-lord-waldegrave|Waldegrave]]; mother of [[arabella-waldegrave|Arabella Waldegrave]].

## Background

Henrietta was born in 1670, the natural (illegitimate) daughter of King James II and Arabella Churchill — the sister of John Churchill, first Duke of Marlborough [1, 2]. Price notes that this lineage makes Winston Churchill a collateral descendant of the family [1]. She married Henry Waldegrave, a staunch Catholic and loyalist peer elevated to the peerage by James II; they had three children:

- **James (1684–1741)**, who later became the first Earl Waldegrave
- **Henry**, who died a bachelor in 1792
- **Arabella Waldegrave**, born in 1687, whose later disappearance into a French convent made her a primary candidate for the identity of the Borley phantom nun [1, 3]

Because the Waldegraves were fiercely loyal Catholics, the family followed the deposed King James II into exile in France in 1688, when Arabella was just a baby [3, 4].

## Espionage

Henrietta is documented to have become a spy for the British Government — working against the Stuart Pretenders she had nominally served. According to the extensive historical researches of [[georgina-dawson|Mrs. Georgina Dawson]], she was **expelled from the French Court in 1695** on charges of spying against her own father, King James II [6, 7]. Dawson's research indicates that Henrietta was subsequently sent to a convent, from which she successfully escaped, returning to England to actively oppose the Stuart cause. She died in 1730, aged about sixty.

## Sensational Borley Theories

Henrietta's daughter Arabella seemingly vanished from the records of the French convent where she was being educated, and twentieth-century paranormal theorists developed contradictory legends attempting to link both women to the Borley ghosts:

- **The Murder Legend:** Wesley Downes promoted a theory that in **1730**, Henrietta murdered her own daughter Arabella because they were working as spies for opposing factions (Henrietta for the government, Arabella for the Stuarts) [5, 8, 9]. Downes claimed both women were nuns at the time, and that Henrietta was subsequently murdered by Nicholas Waldegrave — suggesting their reunited spirits returned to haunt Borley church [8, 9].
- **The Hanoverian Blackmail Legend:** In the 1950s, [[mary-lycett|Mary Lycett]] proposed a highly romanticised version wherein Arabella was actually the famous Borley phantom "Marie Lairre" [4, 10]. Lycett theorised that Henrietta had become an active spy for the Hanoverian party because she was **"embittered by poverty and the fact of James II's neglect to recognize her claim to relationship"** [11]. In this version, Henrietta intercepted her escaping daughter in France and blackmailed her, forcing Arabella to carry "secret documents" to London before she would help her [11, 12].

## The Historical Reality

The sensational Borley legends surrounding Henrietta and the supposed murder of her daughter were ultimately proven to be baseless fabrications. The Countess Mary Waldegrave later clarified the true historical record: Arabella had not vanished or been murdered in a spy-thriller scenario [13, 14]. She had simply become a nun in Paris and **maintained a regular, peaceful correspondence with her mother, Henrietta**, as well as with her brother, Lord James Waldegrave, while he was serving as Ambassador to the Court in Paris [14].

## Relevance to Borley

Her documented espionage career is the basis for [[georgina-dawson|Mrs. Dawson]]'s suggestion that her daughter [[arabella-waldegrave|Arabella]] similarly became a spy and may have been murdered at Borley. The argument by analogy has its limits — [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] rejected the Arabella theory on other grounds — but Henrietta's own history establishes that political intrigue within the Waldegrave family was real, not invented.
