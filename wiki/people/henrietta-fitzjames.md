# Henrietta FitzJames (1670–1730)

Natural daughter of King James II and his mistress [[arabella-churchill|Arabella Churchill]]; wife of Henry, first Baron [[henry-lord-waldegrave|Waldegrave]]; mother of [[arabella-waldegrave|Arabella Waldegrave]].

## Background

Henrietta was born in 1670, one of James's illegitimate children by Arabella Churchill. When James II fled England in 1688 she accompanied the exiled court to France. She married Henry Waldegrave, a staunch Catholic and loyalist peer; they had two sons and two daughters, of whom Arabella was the most historically notable.

## Espionage

Henrietta is documented to have become a spy for the British Government — working against the Stuart Pretenders she had nominally served. She was **expelled from the French Court in 1695** on this charge, sent to a convent, and subsequently escaped to England. She continued to oppose the Stuart cause and died in 1730.

## Relevance to Borley

Her documented espionage career is the basis for [[georgina-dawson|Mrs. Dawson]]'s suggestion that her daughter [[arabella-waldegrave|Arabella]] similarly became a spy and may have been murdered at Borley. The argument by analogy has its limits — [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] rejected the Arabella theory on other grounds — but Henrietta's own history establishes that political intrigue within the Waldegrave family was real, not invented.

Her mother [[arabella-churchill|Arabella Churchill]]'s brother became John Churchill, first Duke of Marlborough — the great soldier and ancestor of [[harry-price|Winston Churchill]]'s collateral line. Price notes this connection explicitly.

Henrietta died in 1730, aged about sixty. Her mother-in-law [[arabella-waldegrave|Arabella Waldegrave's grandmother]] — the elder Arabella Churchill — also died in 1730, aged about eighty.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Henrietta FitzJames (1670–1730)**, also known as Lady Waldegrave, is a historical figure whose life was posthumously woven into the sensational legends surrounding the Borley Rectory hauntings. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, espionage allegations, and contradictions regarding her role in the legends:

**Biographical Details and Royal Lineage**
Henrietta FitzJames was the **natural daughter of King James II and his mistress Arabella Churchill** (the sister of John Churchill, Duke of Marlborough) [1, 2]. She was married to Henry, the first Lord Waldegrave (Baron Waldegrave of Chewton), who was elevated to the peerage by King James II [1]. 

The couple had three children: two sons, James (the first Earl Waldegrave, 1684–1741) and Henry (who died a bachelor in 1792), and a daughter named Arabella Waldegrave, born in 1687 [1, 3]. Because the Waldegraves were fiercely loyal Catholics, **the family followed the deposed King James II into exile in France in 1688**, when Henrietta's daughter Arabella was just a baby [3, 4]. 

**Espionage Allegations and Exile**
According to the extensive historical researches of Mrs. Georgina Dawson, Henrietta’s life in France took a dangerous turn when she **became a spy for the British Government** [5, 6]. As a result of her activities, she was expelled from the French Court in Paris in 1695 on charges of spying against her own father, King James II [6, 7]. Dawson's research indicates that Henrietta was subsequently sent to a convent, from which she successfully escaped, returning to England to actively oppose the Stuart Pretenders [6]. 

**Sensational Borley Theories and Contradictions**
Because Henrietta's daughter, Arabella, seemingly vanished from the records of the French convent where she was being educated, twentieth-century paranormal theorists developed highly dramatic, contradictory legends attempting to link Henrietta and her daughter to the Borley ghosts:
*   **The Murder Legend:** In his book *The Ghosts of Borley*, Wesley Downes promoted a theory that in **1730**, Henrietta murdered her own daughter Arabella because they were working as spies for opposing factions (Henrietta for the government, and Arabella for the Stuarts) [5, 8, 9]. Downes claimed both women were nuns at the time, and that Henrietta was subsequently murdered by Nicholas Waldegrave—suggesting that their reunited spirits returned to haunt Borley church [8, 9].
*   **The Hanoverian Blackmail Legend:** In the 1950s, a theorist named Mary Lycett proposed a highly romanticized version of events wherein Arabella was actually the famous Borley phantom, "Marie Lairre" [4, 10]. Lycett theorized that Henrietta had become an active spy for the Hanoverian party because she was **"embittered by poverty and the fact of James II's neglect to recognize her claim to relationship"** [11]. In this version, Henrietta intercepted her escaping daughter in France and blackmailed her, forcing Arabella to carry "secret documents" to London before she would help her [11, 12].

**The Historical Reality**
The sensational Borley legends surrounding Henrietta and the supposed murder of her daughter were ultimately proven to be baseless fabrications. The Countess Mary Waldegrave later clarified the true historical record: Arabella had not vanished or been murdered in a spy-thriller scenario [13, 14]. She had simply become a nun in Paris and **maintained a regular, peaceful correspondence with her mother, Henrietta**, as well as with her brother, Lord James Waldegrave, while he was serving as an Ambassador to the Court in Paris [14].
