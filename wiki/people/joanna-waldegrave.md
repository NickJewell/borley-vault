# Joanna Waldegrave / Sister Theodosia

A Waldegrave woman from the Borley branch of the family who became a Brussels Benedictine nun and was provisionally identified by Ivan Banks as a possible candidate for the ghost nun of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] — before his research definitively eliminated her.

## Background

Joanna Waldegrave was a great-granddaughter of the heavily persecuted Tudor Catholic [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Sir Edward Waldegrave]] [1]. Like several other women in her family, she pursued a religious life in exile on the Continent. She entered the Brussels English Benedictine community in **1663**, at the age of fifteen, and was professed a nun three years later in **1666** [2]. Upon taking her vows, she assumed the religious name **Sister Theodosia** [3]. She was highly successful in her vocation, eventually rising to the prominent position of Abbess of the Brussels Benedictines [3, 4].

## Connection to the Borley Séances

During the late 1930s, [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s investigators conducted table-tipping and planchette séances that purportedly contacted the spirit of a murdered 19-year-old French novice named "[[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]," who claimed she was killed at Borley on 17 May 1667, by a Waldegrave [2, 5]. The date of Joanna's profession — 1666 — caught Banks's attention, because in 1667 Joanna would have been exactly **nineteen years old**, perfectly matching the age and timeline given by the séance entity [5].

Banks identified several further intriguing coincidences:

- During the séances, the planchette produced the names "Janen" and "Jeane Waldegrave" [5]. Banks asked: could the entity have been attempting to convey "Joanna"?
- Examining the planchette writing reproduced in *The End of Borley Rectory* (p. 226), Banks suggested the letter taken to be "L" at the start of "Lairre" could be a "J," making the word "Joanne" or "Joanna" [5].
- Some investigators hypothesised that "Lairre" was a phonetic misinterpretation of "Lier" (a town in Belgium), suggesting the entity was providing a confused mix of her baptismal and religious names — perhaps "Joanna Waldegrave cum Sister Marie du Lier" [3, 6].

> "Was it perhaps the name Joanna that was meant to be conveyed to the sitters at those sessions? On top of all this, the Borley nun, or Marie Lairre as the case may be, is supposed to have been 19 years old at the time of her murder, if murder it was. In 1667, Joanna Waldegrave would have been 19 years of age!"

## Elimination

Banks's research into the Brussels archives definitively resolved the question. The official chronicles of the Brussels Benedictine order confirm that Sister Theodosia (Joanna Waldegrave) was not murdered in 1667 [3]. Instead, she lived a long and successful life within the church, served as Abbess, and died in **1719** — fifty-two years after the supposed murder of Marie Lairre [1, 3]. Her documented survival well into the 18th century rules her out entirely as a murder victim.

Banks also investigated whether "Lairre" might be a corruption of "Lier" (Lierre), a Belgian town with Carmelite English nuns, but established that the Lier Carmelites and the Brussels Benedictines never communicated with each other, further closing off that line of enquiry [3, 6]. Banks then refocused attention on her near-contemporary, [[barbara-waldegrave|Barbara Waldegrave (Sister Apollonia)]], who did die young — in 1638, aged about 36.
