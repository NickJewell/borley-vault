# Mrs Newman

(née unknown; married surname Newman). Cook employed by the [[henry-bull|Bull]] family at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] from early 1924 until the death of the Rev. Harry Bull in 1927 — a period of three and a half years. Interviewed by [[harry-price|Price]] on May 30, 1945, at Rectory Cottages, Liston. Described by Price as "very intelligent and willing to relate her psychic adventures."

## Phenomena

Newman's sleeping quarters was a large room over the kitchen (Room 3 on Plan III), with a window overlooking the courtyard. The room had two doors — one to the bathroom passage, one to the "pear room" (where pears and fruit were stored). She locked both doors every night before retiring.

**Clothes disturbance**: A curtain divided her room in half. Her clothes hung on wall-pegs on the window side. On several occasions, just as light was beginning to appear through the window, she was awakened and could see through the semi-transparent curtain the silhouettes of her garments being disturbed and moved back and forth — as if "something was examining her belongings." She never discovered what moved them; there was no draught.

**Door-unlocking**: The door to the pear room was found unlocked and standing open "morning after morning," despite being locked and the key left inside the room on the window side. Price compares this to [[mark-kerr-pearse|Kerr-Pearse]]'s experience of being locked in the Base Room by something that remained in the room (October 26, 1937). Newman eventually removed the key after locking the pear room door, and from that point the door was not interfered with. The bathroom passage door and its key were never disturbed.

Whatever was opening her door "must have been inside the room while she was undressing" — as Price notes, echoing the long history of Borley's door and lock phenomena during all periods of occupation.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mrs. W. Newman** was a former servant at Borley Rectory whose experiences provided investigators with first-hand testimony of paranormal activity occurring during the latter years of the Bull family's residency. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, specific events, dates, quotes, and controversies regarding her involvement:

**Biographical Details and Employment**
*   **Role and Tenure:** As a young girl, she was employed as a cook by the Bull family. She worked at the Rectory for three and a half years, from the beginning of **1924 until the death of the Rev. Harry Bull in 1927** [1, 2].
*   **1945 Interview:** Eighteen years after she left the Rectory, she was residing at Rectory Cottages in the nearby village of Liston. The Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning informed investigator Harry Price of her past employment and experiences, leading Price to formally interview her on **May 30, 1945** [1]. Price personally found her to be **"very intelligent and willing to relate her psychic adventures"** [1]. 

**The "Pear Room" and the Unlocking Door**
Mrs. Newman's primary paranormal experience centered on a persistent, localized poltergeist disturbance in her sleeping quarters. 
*   **The Room Layout:** She slept in a large bedroom situated over the kitchen (designated as Room No. 3 on Price's plans), which featured a window overlooking the courtyard [1]. The room had two doors: one led to the bathroom passage, and the other opened into Room No. 2, which Mrs. Newman distinctly referred to as the **"pear room"** (because it was used to store pears and other fruit)—a local domestic term that was entirely new to Harry Price [1].
*   **The Interference:** Every night before retiring to bed, Mrs. Newman would carefully lock both of these doors, leaving the keys inside the locks [1, 2]. Despite this precaution, morning after morning she would wake to find the door leading to the Pear Room unlocked and standing wide open [2]. 
*   **The Resolution:** The only other access to her room was the courtyard window situated many feet above the ground, which remained completely untouched and was often closed during these nocturnal disturbances [2]. After enduring this repeatedly, she finally managed to stop the interference by locking the Pear Room door and **physically removing the key from the lock** [2]. 
*   **The "Presence":** Chronological records of the haunting describe Mrs. Newman's overall experience during this 1924–1927 period as encountering a **"presence"** within her bedroom [3]. Harry Price deduced that whatever was responsible for turning the key and opening the door **"must have been inside the room while she was undressing"** [4]. 

**Contradictions and the SPR Report**
Mrs. Newman's testimony serves as a specific point of contention between defenders of the Borley haunting and the skeptical investigators of the Society for Psychical Research (SPR). 
*   When the SPR authors (Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) published their highly critical *Borley Report* in 1956, attempting to debunk the haunting and Harry Price's methods, they **entirely omitted Mrs. Newman's testimony** [2]. 
*   Defenders of the case point out that the SPR authors **did not question Mrs. Newman** (among many other available witnesses), ignoring evidence of physical phenomena that occurred years before Harry Price or Marianne Foyster ever arrived at the Rectory [2].
