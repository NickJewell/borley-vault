# Mrs Newman

Also recorded as **Mrs W. Newman**. Cook employed by the [[henry-bull|Bull]] family at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] from early 1924 until the death of the Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] in 1927 — a period of three and a half years. By 1945 she was residing at Rectory Cottages in the nearby village of Liston. Interviewed by [[harry-price|Price]] on **May 30, 1945** — informed of her by [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Henning]] — and described by Price as "very intelligent and willing to relate her psychic adventures."

## The "Pear Room" and the Unlocking Door

Newman's sleeping quarters was a large room over the kitchen (Room 3 on Plan III), with a window overlooking the courtyard. The room had two doors — one to the bathroom passage, one to the "pear room" (where pears and fruit were stored; a local domestic term entirely new to Price). She locked both doors every night before retiring, leaving the keys inside the locks.

Despite this precaution, morning after morning she would wake to find the door to the Pear Room unlocked and standing wide open. The only other access to her room was the courtyard window situated many feet above the ground, which remained completely untouched.

After enduring this repeatedly, she finally managed to stop the interference by locking the Pear Room door and **physically removing the key from the lock**. From that point, the door was not interfered with. The bathroom passage door and its key were never disturbed.

Whatever was opening her door "must have been inside the room while she was undressing" — as Price notes, echoing the long history of Borley's door and lock phenomena during all periods of occupation. Price compares this to [[mark-kerr-pearse|Kerr-Pearse]]'s experience of being locked in the Base Room by something that remained in the room (October 26, 1937).

## Clothes Disturbance

A curtain divided her room in half. Her clothes hung on wall-pegs on the window side. On several occasions, just as light was beginning to appear through the window, she was awakened and could see through the semi-transparent curtain the silhouettes of her garments being disturbed and moved back and forth — as if "something was examining her belongings." She never discovered what moved them; there was no draught.

Chronological records of the haunting describe Mrs Newman's overall experience during this period as encountering a **"presence"** within her bedroom.

## Omission from the SPR Report

Mrs Newman's testimony is a specific point of contention between defenders of the Borley haunting and the SPR investigators. When Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall published their highly critical *Borley Report* in 1956, they **entirely omitted Mrs Newman's testimony** without explanation — and did not interview her. Defenders point out that she provides evidence of physical phenomena occurring years before [[harry-price|Harry Price]] or [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] ever arrived at the Rectory.
