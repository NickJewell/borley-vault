# Ethel Bull (1868–1961)

Daughter of [[henry-bull]]; known within the family as "Kitty." Her full baptismal name was Ethel Mary Bull, baptized 20 January 1868 [3]. One of the most important long-term witnesses in the Borley case. Her testimony spans from the 1880s to the 1950s — over fifty years — and remained substantially consistent throughout, though with important contradictions noted below. Interviewed multiple times by Price (June 1929, March 1939) and by [[sidney-glanville]] (June 1938). Described by acquaintances as a small, bird-like woman who wore enormous hats and gloves [5], she was the acknowledged centre and spokesperson for the Bull family regarding the hauntings.

## Paranormal Experiences

**Nightly footsteps**: Every night at approximately 10:30 p.m., footsteps were heard passing their bedroom door and along the landing, stopping outside the night nurseries — always followed by exactly three taps. Heard at least one hundred times over years. (Independently confirmed by nursemaid Mrs E. Byford, who worked at the Rectory in 1886.)

**The old man in the bedroom**: Woke one night to find "an old man in dark, old-fashioned clothes, wearing a tall hat, standing by her bed." She later told [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] in 1953 that Price had exaggerated: she had only seen the man **once**, though she felt someone sit on her bed "once or twice" [12, 22–24]. Price had published that she saw this figure "many times."

**The rapping door**: After the tall hat incident, Ethel shared a room with her sister Mabel. Every night between 9:00 p.m. and 10:00 p.m. they would hear three raps on their bedroom door — they never called out because they knew no one was there [25, 26].

**Tall, dark man in the passage**: Saw a tall, dark man standing beside her in one of the upper passages; he vanished before she could recover from surprise [27, 28].

**All 20 bells ringing simultaneously**: On one occasion while in the house, all the bells pealed at once; investigation found no explanation.

**Face-slapping**: A sister sleeping in a smaller bedroom was awakened by something slapping her face.

**Sounds of entering**: After their father's death, when only an invalid mother and one daughter were at home, sounds as of someone entering the back door — investigation always found no one.

**Rushing water**: Heard the sound of rushing water with no explanation.

## The July 28, 1900 Four-Sister Sighting

The canonical Borley event. Returning from a garden party in daylight with sisters [[freda-bull|Freda]] and [[mabel-bull|Mabel]], the three entered the garden at the postern gate and immediately saw a female figure with bowed head, dressed entirely in black as a nun, slowly gliding along the [[nuns-walk]]. All three were terrified. Sister [[elsie-bull|Elsie]], fetched from the house, said "A ghost? Oh, what nonsense, I'll go and speak to it!" — ran across the lawn; the figure stopped, turned, showed "an expression of intense grief" — Elsie froze and it vanished.

Ethel's own memory: **"Certainly, I was scared stiff!"** [9].

> **Contradiction — the nun's face:** In Harry Price's books, the nun was said to have "an expression of intense grief on her face." However, Ethel explicitly contradicted this in a 1950 SPR interview, stating she saw a woman bent over in a flowing black robe and **"could not see the face"** [12, 13]. The "intense grief" detail therefore rests on Elsie's account alone.

> **Contradiction — prior knowledge:** Ethel repeatedly insisted the July 1900 incident was the first time she had ever seen the nun [14, 15]. However, a local resident named Mrs Poore claimed to have seen an old pencil diary kept by Ethel containing entries such as **"we saw the nun in the garden today,"** which would contradict her timeline [16, 17].

**November 1900**: Saw the nun again simultaneously with the cook, leaning over the garden gate.

**A cousin** also saw the nun during a visit at the same period.

## Coffins in the Church Crypt

Reports that old coffins in the [[borley-church-crypt|Borley Church crypt]] were found displaced from their positions on several occasions — no explanation ever found.

> **Contradiction — the crypt:** Price claimed Ethel told him that lead coffins had moved mysteriously of their own accord. Ethel later denied this to the SPR, insisting she knew nothing of a crypt [49]. Yet investigator [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth|Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth]] later claimed he possessed evidence that Ethel not only knew about the crypt but had actually been down inside it [50, 51].

## Interactions with Harry Price and the SPR

Ethel actively encouraged [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s investigations. On 29 September 1931, she and a sister visited Price in London to ask him to return to Borley to investigate the intense phenomena surrounding [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] [29–31]. When SPR's W.H. Salter visited Ethel to pressure her into severing ties with Price, she told [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] that she **"thought he had a cheek to ask"** [32–34].

Despite her loyalty, Ethel grew frustrated with Price's journalistic embellishments. In 1953 she complained to the SPR that Price frequently "gingered up" her testimony and was annoyed that he **"never gave her the opportunity of approving the section of MHH dealing with her testimony"** [15, 35]. She told Underwood that some of Price's written accounts "were not entirely accurate" regarding the 1900 sighting — specifically that the light was poor, the distance about a hundred yards, and no white colouring was discernible.

## Clarke's Assessment

[[andrew-clarke]] provides additional critical context from Ethel's later interviews (slbr-04, slbr-11):

- In 1953 (SPR interview) and 1965, Ethel complained that Price had "misrepresented her" and "gingered up" her testimony: a single apparition seen at twilight became "many times"; the 1900 sighting's detail about the face showing "intense grief" was impossible — it was seen in poor light and no face was visible.
- The 1900 sighting was at twilight (9pm in July, approximately one hour after sunset), not in daylight as Price implied.
- Ethel became "the butt of jokes whenever she returned to Borley" as a result of Price's sensationalized version: the villagers jeered at her for believing in "fairies."
- The "headless man" story: Ethel's original note to Price described a poacher seen through fruit trees; by Price's second book it had become a headless phantom vanishing in the vegetable garden. Ethel's original description did not include the word "headless."
- The Foxearth Historical Society's research: Clarke notes that despite fifteen years of newspaper searches from 1780, no haunting was reported at Borley before 1929 — suggesting the pre-1900 family legends were not publicly circulating.

## The Blue Room Séance and the "Jumping Soap"

During Harry Price's first visit on 13 June 1929, Ethel participated in an impromptu late-night séance in the [[blue-room|Blue Room]]. According to Price and the *Daily Mirror*, the sitting climaxed when a cake of soap leapt from a washstand and smashed into a jug [36, 37].

> **Contradiction:** Ethel totally contradicted this account, telling Peter Underwood: **"We were none of us in the room when the piece of soap jumped out of its washstand"** [38, 39].

## The Campaign Against Ivy Brackenbury

Ethel and her sisters harboured a bitter, lifelong hatred for their brother [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]]'s widow, [[ivy-brackenbury|Ivy Brackenbury]] [40]. During a 25 June 1938 interview with [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]], Ethel regaled the investigator with malicious slander about Ivy — claiming she had been left at the altar by a previous husband, that her marriage to Harry was miserable, and openly accusing Ivy of murdering Harry, claiming the sisters had found a half-filled bottle of "sugar of lead" (poison) in the cellar [41–43].

## Opinions of Marianne Foyster and Mabel Smith

Ethel despised [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]], describing her to investigators as **"mad as a hatter"** and **"a little beast"** who did "some very disreputable things" [44, 45]. When [[mabel-smith|Mabel Smith]] wrote to the press in 1945 claiming she had never believed the house was haunted, Ethel furiously retorted: **"All nonsense, she used to shriek with fright"** [46, 47].

## Later Life and Death

Ethel and her brother Alfred Bull moved from Chilton Lodge, Great Cornard, and were the last surviving patron-family members. In 1935, the electoral roll lists Ethel as patron of the living, with her brother Alfred as official patron. Alfred Bull died 17 March 1956. [[claudine-mathias|CM]] met Ethel and Alfred at the Four Swans Hotel, Sudbury, before [[rev-lanfranc-mathias|Lanfranc's]] induction in 1955. CM describes Ethel as "a small, bird-like lady... very kind to us... anything but a cantankerous old spinster." When Alfred began to ask about "the Borley gh...?", Ethel interrupted: "Shush! They don't know anything about it!" — notably not claiming the haunting was a hoax; only that the incoming rector had not been briefed on it.

Ethel's voice was immortalized when she recounted her 1900 sighting for the 1947 BBC Radio broadcast, *The Haunted Rectory* [25]. She also became the godmother of [[esme-lawton|Esme Lawton]] following Canon Lawton's 1933 stay at Borley [48].

Ethel remained steadfast in her belief that her childhood home was haunted until her death at the age of 93 on 29 April 1961 [5, 52]. Defending her lifelong testimony to the end:

> **"What would be the use of an old lady like me, waiting to meet her Maker, telling a lot of fairy stories?"** [53]
