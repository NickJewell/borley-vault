# Ethel Bull (1868–1961)

Daughter of [[henry-bull]]; one of the most important long-term witnesses in the Borley case. Her testimony spans from the 1880s to the 1950s — over fifty years — and remained consistent throughout. Interviewed multiple times by Price (June 1929, March 1939) and by [[sidney-glanville]] (June 1938).

## Paranormal Experiences

**Childhood phenomena at the Rectory**:

- **Nightly footsteps**: Every night at approximately 10:30 p.m., footsteps were heard passing their bedroom door and along the landing, stopping outside the night nurseries — always followed by exactly three taps. Heard at least one hundred times over years. (Independently confirmed by nursemaid Mrs. E. Byford, who worked at the Rectory in 1886.)
- **The old man in the bedroom**: Woke one night to find "an old man in dark, old-fashioned clothes, wearing a tall hat, standing by her bed." Seen on multiple occasions; once sitting on the edge of the bed.
- **Tall, dark man in the passage**: Saw a tall, dark man standing beside her in one of the upper passages; he vanished before she recovered from surprise.
- **All 20 bells ringing simultaneously**: On one occasion while in the house, all the bells pealed at once; investigation found no explanation.
- **Face-slapping**: A sister sleeping in a smaller bedroom was awakened by something slapping her face.
- **Sounds of entering**: After [[henry-bull|their father's]] death, when only an invalid mother and one daughter were at home, sounds as of someone entering the back door — investigation always found no one.
- **Rushing water**: Heard the sound of rushing water with no explanation.

**The July 28, 1900 four-sister sighting** — the canonical Borley event:
Returning from a garden party in daylight with sisters Freda and Mabel, the three entered the garden at the postern gate and immediately saw a female figure with bowed head, dressed entirely in black as a nun, slowly gliding along the [[nuns-walk]]. All three were terrified. Sister Elsie, fetched from the house, said "A ghost? Oh, what nonsense, I'll go and speak to it!" — ran across the lawn; the figure stopped, turned, showed "an expression of intense grief" — Elsie froze and it vanished. [[harry-price]] cites this as one of the strongest instances of objective apparition: four simultaneous witnesses, solid figure, definite vanishing.

**November 1900**: Saw the nun again simultaneously with the cook, leaning over the garden gate.

**A cousin**: Also saw the nun during a visit at the same period.

**As a source for her brother Harry's experiences**: Relayed Harry Bull's accounts of the headless man, "Old Amos" the gardener's ghost on the lawn, and the phantom coach and headless coachman.

**Coffins in the church crypt**: Reports that old coffins in the Borley church crypt were found displaced from their positions on several occasions — no explanation ever found.

## As a Source

[[peter-underwood]] and [[paul-tabori]] visited Ethel Bull multiple times at Chilton Lodge, Great Cornard (the Bull family's later home). They found her testimony credible: she maintained "identical, consistent stories of the experience for over fifty years."

In a 1954 letter she wrote that she "quite expects" her parents "knew about the nun but the children were never told."

Told [[peter-underwood]] that some of Price's written accounts "were not entirely accurate" regarding the 1900 sighting — specifically that the light was poor, the distance about a hundred yards, and no white colouring was discernible.

Despite everything she had witnessed as a child, Ethel said: "nothing would now induce her to sleep at the Rectory."

## Clarke's Assessment (slbr-04, slbr-11)

Andrew Clarke provides additional context from Ethel's later interviews:

- In 1953 (SPR interview) and 1965, Ethel complained that Price had "misrepresented her" and "gingered up" her testimony: a single apparition seen at twilight became "many times"; the 1900 sighting's detail about the face showing "intense grief" was impossible — it was seen in poor light and no face was visible.
- The 1900 sighting was at twilight (9pm in July, approximately one hour after sunset), not in daylight as Price implied.
- Ethel became "the butt of jokes whenever she returned to Borley" as a result of Price's sensationalized version: the villagers jeered at her for believing in "fairies."
- The "headless man" story (slbr-04): Ethel's original note to Price described a poacher seen through fruit trees; by Price's second book it had become a headless phantom vanishing in the vegetable garden. Ethel's original description did not include the word "headless."
- The Foxearth Historical Society's research (slbr-11): [[andrew-clarke|Clarke]] notes that despite 15 years of newspaper searches from 1780, no haunting was reported at Borley before 1929 — suggesting the pre-1900 family legends were not publicly circulating.

## Later Life and Death

Ethel and her brother Alfred Bull moved from Chilton Lodge, Great Cornard, and were the last surviving patron-family members. Alfred Bull died **17 March 1956**. Ethel died **29 April 1961, aged 93**.

[[claudine-mathias|CM]] met Ethel and Alfred at the Four Swans Hotel, Sudbury, for a "luncheon" before [[rev-lanfranc-mathias|Lanfranc's]] induction in 1955. CM describes Ethel as "a small, bird-like lady... very kind to us... anything but a cantankerous old spinster" — directly contradicting Robert Wood's assessment. When Alfred began to ask about "the Borley gh...?", Ethel interrupted: "Shush! They don't know anything about it!" — notably not claiming the haunting was a hoax; only that the incoming rector had not been briefed on it.

The 1935 electoral roll confirms Ethel was still living in the Borley district at that time. She is listed as the 1935 electoral roll patron of the living, with her brother Alfred as official patron.
