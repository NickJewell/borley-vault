# Charles Sutton

Journalist on the staff of the *Daily Mail*. The first person to make a public accusation of fraud against [[harry-price]] in connection with Borley Rectory.

## The 1929 Visit and Accusation

Sutton visited Borley Rectory in late July 1929, accompanying Price and his secretary [[lucie-kaye]] on a night-time investigation. This was Price's last visit to the rectory for approximately eight years. According to Sutton's account:

> "Many things happened the night I spent in the famous Borley Rectory with Harry Price and one of his colleagues, including one uncomfortable moment when a large pebble hit me on the head."

> "After much noisy phenomena I seized Harry and found his pockets full of bricks and pebbles. This was one 'phenomenon' he could not explain, so I rushed to the nearest village to 'phone the *Daily Mail* with my story, but after a conference with the lawyer my story was killed. The News Editor said: 'Bad luck, old man, but there were two of them and only one of you.'"

The law of libel prevented publication during Price's lifetime.

## The Inky Way Annual (c. 1949)

After Price's death in March 1948, Sutton published his account in the *Inky Way Annual* in an article entitled *The Meditations of Charles Sutton*. He described finding stones in Price's pockets during their tour and being hit on the head by a pebble. He had previously attempted to phone the story to the *Daily Mail* but been blocked by legal advice.

## Signed Statement to the SPR

Sutton provided a full signed statement to the SPR investigators ([[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], [[trevor-hall]]) in June 1949, furnishing far greater detail than the published paragraphs. The statement describes:

- The order of the party during the tour: Miss Kaye first, Sutton, then Price at the rear
- Pebble-throwing in multiple rooms with Price always behind the others
- A pebble hitting Sutton's hat with considerable force
- His proposal to reverse order so that Price walked in front — which Price argued against
- The final incident at which Price's pockets were searched and found to contain bricks and pebbles

## Corroboration

The accusation is corroborated by multiple independent sources:

- **[[lord-charles-hope]]'s contemporary notes (July 1929):** Written at the time of the visits, these record that Miss Kaye told Hope about the incident — that Sutton had "accused \[Price\] to his face and made him turn out his pockets in which there were several similar stones" — and that Hope was advised by Miss Kaye to see Sutton himself. Hope interviewed Sutton in his flat; Sutton showed him what he had seen
- **The pattern of Price's subsequent behaviour:** His abrupt loss of interest in Borley from late July 1929 — after years of calling it "the best authenticated case of haunting in psychical research" — is consistent with his knowledge that Sutton had caught him and that Hope shared the suspicion
- **The Battersea Poltergeist case (1928):** Sixteen months before Borley, Price visited a poltergeist case in Battersea; the family told SPR investigators that objects "appeared" and moved only when Price was present, and that they "complained things always seemed to happen when he was there"

## Lucie Kaye's Denial

[[lucie-kaye]] told the SPR investigators she had "no memory whatever" of any stone or pebble episode and denied the accusation entirely. She offered the explanation that Price "attracts poltergeist disturbances." The SPR noted that the same remark was independently reported by both Mrs Smith and Miss Kaye about different occasions.

## Significance

The SPR describes Sutton's accusation as "the only openly published accusation of fraud against Price" and the combined weight of Sutton's statement, Hope's contemporary notes, and Douglas-Home's independent 1937 evidence as constituting "a disquieting picture."
