# Charles Sutton

Charles W. Sutton was a prominent London journalist on the staff of the *Daily Mail* [1–3] — described by contemporaries as "the best police court sub in Fleet Street" [6]. He is the first person to make a public accusation of fraud against [[harry-price]] in connection with [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], and the only one to have done so in print during his own lifetime.

## Biographical Background

Before his involvement with Borley, Sutton had a remarkably adventurous career as a veteran reporter. His exploits included nearly drowning while covering Amelia Earhart's first transatlantic flight, throwing a wealthy American out of his cabin on the *Berengaria*, and being apprehended as a cat burglar [6]. In late 1938, he suffered a severe accident when a horse rolled over him, breaking his pelvis, which laid him up for months [7, 8]. He was later approached by researchers working on the 1973 book *The Ghosts of Borley* but declined to assist, claiming he was writing his own memoirs and "did not wish to spike his own guns" [3]. No record of those memoirs being published exists [3].

## The 1929 Visit and Accusation

Sutton visited Borley Rectory on the evening of 25 July 1929, accompanying Price and his secretary [[lucie-kaye]] on a night-time investigation. This was Price's last visit to the Rectory for approximately eight years.

> **Contradiction:** Sutton later claimed in an official statement that Price telephoned and invited him [10], whereas Miss Kaye firmly maintained that Sutton urgently requested to be taken there because he was sailing from Southampton to New York on 27 July and wanted to see the haunted house before leaving [3, 11].

According to Sutton's account, suspicious events began almost immediately upon arrival at dusk — a window broke and Price inexplicably disappeared for several minutes [12]. As the trio toured the darkened ground floor with a hurricane lamp, pebbles and stones seemingly flew across the rooms, and Sutton was hit on the hat by a pebble [13]. Upstairs in the [[blue-room]], Sutton claimed to hear noises that sounded like "Harry Price making a very bad attempt at a ventriloquial act" [13].

Sutton alleged that as they left the room, he heard the swish of an arm, followed by a large stone or half-brick crashing down the stairs [14]. Convinced Price was the culprit:

> "Many things happened the night I spent in the famous Borley Rectory with Harry Price and one of his colleagues, including one uncomfortable moment when a large pebble hit me on the head."

> "After much noisy phenomena I seized Harry and found his pockets full of bricks and pebbles. This was one 'phenomenon' he could not explain, so I rushed to the nearest village to 'phone the *Daily Mail* with my story, but after a conference with the lawyer my story was killed. The News Editor said: 'Bad luck, old man, but there were two of them and only one of you.'"

The law of libel prevented publication during Price's lifetime.

## The Inky Way Annual (December 1948)

After Price's death in March 1948, Sutton published his account in *The Meditations of Charles Sutton*, a short article in the *Inky Way Annual (Book 2)* — a publication sold in aid of press charities [2, 19, 20]. This explosive claim became the primary catalyst for the highly sceptical *Borley Report* published by the Society for Psychical Research in 1956 [20, 21].

## Signed Statement to the SPR

Sutton subsequently provided a full signed statement for the SPR files in 1950 [10, 22], furnishing far greater detail than the published paragraphs. The statement describes:

- The order of the party during the tour: Miss Kaye first, Sutton, then Price at the rear
- Pebble-throwing in multiple rooms with Price always behind the others
- A pebble hitting Sutton's hat with considerable force
- His proposal to reverse order so that Price walked in front — which Price argued against
- The final incident at which Price's pockets were searched and found to contain bricks and pebbles

## Corroboration

The accusation is corroborated by multiple independent sources:

- **[[lord-charles-hope]]'s contemporary notes (July 1929):** Written at the time of the visits, these record that Miss Kaye told Hope about the incident — that Sutton had "accused \[Price\] to his face and made him turn out his pockets in which there were several similar stones" — and that Hope was advised by Miss Kaye to see Sutton himself. Hope interviewed Sutton in his flat; Sutton showed him what he had seen.
- **The pattern of Price's subsequent behaviour:** His abrupt loss of interest in Borley from late July 1929 — after years of calling it "the best authenticated case of haunting in psychical research" — is consistent with his knowledge that Sutton had caught him and that Hope shared the suspicion.
- **The [[battersea-poltergeist-1927|Battersea Poltergeist]] case (1928):** Sixteen months before Borley, Price visited a poltergeist case in Battersea; the family told SPR investigators that objects "appeared" and moved only when Price was present, and that they "complained things always seemed to happen when he was there."

## Lucie Kaye's Denial

[[lucie-kaye]] told the SPR investigators she had "no memory whatever" of any stone or pebble episode and denied the accusation entirely. She offered the explanation that Price "attracts poltergeist disturbances." She also stated that rather than Sutton rushing to a telephone, the three of them retreated to the nearby Bull Inn, where they sat in the lounge, had drinks, and chatted amicably for hours [11, 29]. The SPR noted that the remark about attracting poltergeist disturbances was independently reported by both Mrs Smith and Miss Kaye about different occasions.

## Contradictions and Scrutiny

Sutton's allegations were subjected to severe scrutiny in [[robert-hastings|Robert J. Hastings]]' 1969 *Examination of the 'Borley Report'*, which highlighted numerous weaknesses in Sutton's claims [21, 23]. During a detailed interview with Dr. Alan Gauld in June 1966, Sutton made several significant admissions:

- **He never saw Price throw any stones**, nor did he pick up or visually examine the pebbles that supposedly fell; he admitted they "were not supposed to be" pebbles but just sounded like them [24, 25].
- **He admitted he did not visually confirm the objects in Price's pockets were stones**, having only felt them from the outside, and he acknowledged that concealing a half-brick in a coat pocket would have been highly difficult [26–28].
- He conceded that the "ventriloquism" he reported might actually have been natural acoustic effects of the house [27].

## Post-Incident Relationship with Price

Perhaps the most damaging contradiction to Sutton's claim of a dramatic "exposure" is that he remained on excellent and friendly terms with Price for years afterward [30, 31]. Despite claiming to have caught Price in blatant fraud, Price subsequently invited Sutton to attend five highly restricted séances with the medium [[rudi-schneider]] [11, 31]. In November 1931, Price explicitly recommended Sutton to another psychical investigator as a friendly Press contact [32], and [[lucie-kaye]] recorded having lunch with Sutton in Fleet Street as late as 1940 [33] — actions widely considered inconceivable if Sutton had genuinely caught Price cheating in 1929 [34, 35].

## Significance

The SPR describes Sutton's accusation as "the only openly published accusation of fraud against Price" and the combined weight of Sutton's statement, Hope's contemporary notes, and [[major-douglas-home|Douglas-Home]]'s independent 1937 evidence as constituting "a disquieting picture."
