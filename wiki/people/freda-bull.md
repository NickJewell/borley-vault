# Freda Bull

**Winifred Margaret "Freda" Bull** (1865–1943) was the third child and second daughter of the Rev. [[henry-bull|Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] and Caroline Sarah Foyster [1]. She lived at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] for much of her life and is best known as one of the principal witnesses to the famous 1900 daylight sighting of the "phantom nun."

She is named alongside Isabel Bull and "Mary H. Gladwin" as one of the daughters who gave full accounts of life and phenomena at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] to [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]].

## Name and Role in the Household

> **Note:** While universally referred to as "Freda" in the Borley literature, the Borley Parish Church registers record her baptism on **March 17, 1865** under the name **"Winifred Margaret Bull"**. Researchers conclude that "Freda" must have been an additional Christian name or a moniker adopted unofficially by the family [2, 3].

A minor contradiction appears in a local poem written by Mildred and May Gardiner in the early 20th century, which incorrectly states: *"Freda was the eldest and Ethel coming next"*. In reality, her sister Caroline (Dodie) was the eldest daughter [4–6].

[[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] recalled that Freda effectively functioned as the housekeeper for the large Bull family: Freda **"had charge of the servants, money, and made all the arrangements"** [7]. In their younger years, Freda and Dodie were also responsible for providing a basic education to the younger girls in the family [8].

## Events from Dodie's 1885 Diary

Freda features prominently in her sister Dodie's surviving 1885 diary, which reveals a lively household.

- **The P. Shaw Jeffrey Rivalry:** When the Oxford undergraduate P. Shaw Jeffrey came to stay in June 1885, Dodie developed a fierce crush on him and viewed Freda as a romantic rival, explicitly writing: **"Freda had better not thwart me for I mean to have him"** [10].
- **Freda's 20th Birthday:** The diary records her birthday party on **January 11, 1885**, an event that prompted Dodie to lament, **"What a crop of old maids are growing up"** [11].

## The July 28, 1900 Nun Sighting

Freda's most significant contribution to the Borley legend occurred on the evening of **July 28, 1900**. She, along with her sisters [[ethel-bull|Ethel]] and [[mabel-bull|Mabel]], was returning from a garden party in Sudbury [13, 14]. As they entered the drive gate, all three women distinctly saw a dark, nun-like figure with a bowed head gliding along the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]]. The sisters were terrified, and one ran into the house to fetch an older sister to witness the apparition before it vanished [14, 15].

Freda was almost certainly one of the four sisters present at this landmark sighting. Three sisters returning from the garden party entered by the lower gate and glimpsed a dark female figure gliding along the Nun's Walk; a fourth was fetched from the house. All four remained consistent in describing the figure for the rest of their lives.

The survivors later gathered at [[chilton-lodge|Chilton Lodge]], Great Cornard, where [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] interviewed them multiple times. "These meetings," Underwood and Tabori recorded, "made it difficult to dismiss the 1900 'incident' as mere hallucination."

## Interactions with Harry Price (June 1929)

On the evening of **June 12, 1929** — Price's very first day investigating Borley — Freda and [[ethel-bull|Ethel]] arrived at the Rectory from [[chilton-lodge|Chilton Lodge]] and were interviewed by Price [16]. A lesser-known detail from this visit survives in notes taken by Price's secretary, [[lucie-kaye|Lucy Kaye]], who specifically distinguished the two sisters by describing Ethel as "the talkative one" and Freda simply as **"the little old one"** [17]. Following this interview, Freda remained at the Rectory until the early hours of the morning to participate in the famous Blue Room mirror-tapping séance [18].

## Later Life

Like her sisters, Freda settled at [[chilton-lodge|Chilton Lodge]] after leaving the Rectory. She predeceased the last surviving Bull daughter, Constance, who died in 1963.
