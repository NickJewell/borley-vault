# Freda Bull

One of the fourteen children of the Reverend [[henry-bull|Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]], and a sister of [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]], [[isabel-bull|Isabel Bull]], [[walter-bull|Walter Bull]], and [[gerald-bull|Gerald Bull]].

Freda Bull is named alongside Isabel Bull and "Mary H. Gladwin" as one of the daughters who gave full accounts of life and phenomena at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] to [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]]. As related by Ethel Bull, these three women provided detailed testimony about the haunting during their father's thirty-year incumbency.

## 1900 Nun Sighting

Freda was almost certainly one of the four sisters present at the landmark July 1900 [[the-nun|Nun]] sighting. Three sisters returning from a garden party entered by the lower gate and glimpsed a dark female figure gliding along the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]]; a fourth was fetched from the house. All four remained consistent in describing the figure for the rest of their lives.

The survivors gathered at [[chilton-lodge|Chilton Lodge]], Great Cornard, where Peter Underwood interviewed them multiple times. "These meetings," Underwood and Tabori recorded, "made it difficult to dismiss the 1900 'incident' as mere hallucination."

## Later Life

Like her sisters, Freda settled at Chilton Lodge after leaving the Rectory. She predeceased the last surviving Bull daughter, Constance, who died in 1963.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Winifred Margaret "Freda" Bull** (1865–1943) was the third child and second daughter of the Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull and Caroline Sarah Foyster [1]. She lived at Borley Rectory for much of her life and is best known as one of the principal witnesses to the famous 1900 daylight sighting of the "phantom nun."

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following specific facts, events, quotes, and contradictions regarding her life:

**Name Contradictions and Lesser-Known Biographical Details**
*   **The Baptismal Record Anomaly:** While universally referred to as "Freda" in the Borley literature, the Borley Parish Church registers contain no mention of that name. Instead, the register records her baptism on **March 17, 1865**, under the name **"Winifred Margaret Bull"**. Researchers conclude that "Freda" must have been an additional Christian name or a moniker adopted unofficially by the family [2, 3].
*   **The Gardiner Poem Error:** A minor contradiction exists in a local poem written in the early 20th century by Mildred and May Gardiner, which incorrectly states: *"Freda was the eldest and Ethel coming next"*. In reality, her sister Caroline (Dodie) was the eldest daughter [4-6]. 
*   **Role in the Household:** Marianne Foyster recalled that Freda effectively functioned as the housekeeper for the large Bull family. Marianne noted that Freda **"had charge of the servants, money, and made all the arrangements"** [7]. In their younger years, Freda and Dodie were also responsible for providing a basic education to the younger girls in the family [8]. In later life, following the death of Dodie in 1937, it was recorded that Freda was **"working at the family business"** while her siblings resided in the area [9].

**Events from Dodie’s 1885 Diary**
Freda features prominently in her sister Dodie’s surviving 1885 diary, which reveals a lively household and instances of sibling rivalry. 
*   **The P. Shaw Jeffrey Rivalry:** When the Oxford undergraduate P. Shaw Jeffrey came to stay at the Rectory in June 1885, Dodie developed a fierce crush on him and viewed Freda as a romantic rival, explicitly writing: **"Freda had better not thwart me for I mean to have him"** [10]. 
*   **Social Life:** The diary records Freda's 20th birthday party on **January 11, 1885**, an event that prompted Dodie to lament, **"What a crop of old maids are growing up"** [11]. In another entry, Dodie notes that she and Freda walked to Long Melford together to borrow a high comb for their mother's mantilla ahead of a local ball [12].

**The July 28, 1900 Sighting**
Freda's most significant contribution to the Borley legend occurred on the evening of **July 28, 1900**. She, along with her sisters Ethel and Mabel, was returning from a garden party in Sudbury [13, 14]. As they entered the drive gate, all three women distinctly saw a dark, nun-like figure with a bowed head gliding along the Nun's Walk. The sisters were terrified, and one ran into the house to fetch an older sister to witness the apparition before it vanished [14, 15]. 

**Interactions with Harry Price (June 1929)**
Freda, alongside Ethel, formally introduced the Bull family's supernatural claims to Harry Price. On the evening of **June 12, 1929** (Price's very first day investigating Borley), Freda and Ethel arrived at the Rectory from their home at Chilton Lodge and were interviewed by Price [16]. 

A lesser-known, somewhat humorous detail from this visit survives in the original notes taken by Price's secretary, Lucy Kaye. While evaluating the witnesses, Miss Kaye specifically distinguished the two sisters by describing Ethel as "the talkative one" and Freda simply as **"the little old one"** [17]. Following this interview, Freda remained at the Rectory until the early hours of the morning to participate in the famous Blue Room mirror-tapping séance [18].
