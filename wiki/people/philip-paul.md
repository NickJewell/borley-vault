# Philip Paul

A London journalist specialising in occult matters, a ghost hunter, and an author who considered himself to be continuing [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s investigations at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] [1, 2]. He was also an associated member of the [[ghost-club]] [3]. Active at Borley primarily through digging and excavation on the Rectory site, 1954–57.

## Ghost Club Involvement

Philip Paul was active for a time with [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]], Percival Seward, Cyril Wilkinson, Leonard Kingston and Dr C. Nicholson in reviving the [[ghost-club]] in 1954, but friction quickly developed and Paul left the Club.

## Media Appearances

Paul was adept at generating publicity for his work at Borley. On **25 March 1953** he published an article entitled **"Ghosts Still Walk at Borley"** in *The Star* newspaper [1, 2]. On **12 April 1953** he appeared on the popular BBC television programme *What's My Line* [1, 2], during which he displayed tracings of the [[wall-writings|Borley wall-writings]], alleging that one of the messages had led directly to the discovery of human remains in the cellars, thereby proving the existence of ghosts [1].

In the 1950s he also recounted the **"case of the chiming clock"** to David Piper of the *Sunday Graphic* — a visitor to the [[rectory-cottage|Rectory Cottage]] sitting room kept awake by a mournful ticking clock until midnight, when she heard someone tiptoe in and stop it: **"No human being had entered her room... and that clock had not been working for more than two years"** [11].

## Excavations (1954–1956)

Paul's most significant physical contribution to the Borley saga was a series of excavations he led on the former Rectory site between **October 1954 and August 1956** [2]. Before beginning, he consulted [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]], who provided a sketch map of reputed tunnel locations [4, 5]. Paul had four specific objectives: to find the rest of the phantom nun's skeleton, to locate an ancient tunnel, to confirm the Rectory grounds were once part of a plague pit, and to find the lost plate of [[borley-church|Borley Church]] [4, 6]. He was assisted by [[len-sewell|Leonard Sewell]], [[steuart-kiernander|Steuart Kiernander]], Leonard Kingston, and [[john-dening|the Rev. John C. Dening]] [4, 6].

In October 1954, Paul spent a week searching for tunnels, uncovering a dome near [[borley-place|Borley Place]] that proved only to be a well cover [5]. He supervised the digging of three holes on the Rectory site, one of which reached four feet below the old cellar floor into undisturbed ground, but found only large animal bones and rubble [6]. In July 1955, his team unearthed a block of pre-1772 two-inch bricks, establishing the existence of a much earlier building on the site [7, 8].

In August 1956, Paul introduced a mechanical digger to the site [7, 9]. Critics observed that this was a highly inappropriate tool for the task, risking the destruction of the fragile bones and valuable metalware Paul was hoping to find [10]. Ultimately, nothing of great moment was discovered, and none of his four main objectives were achieved [4]. The sources note that with **"the departure of Philip Paul, the mechanical digger and the publicity that he seemed to enjoy, things became less active at Borley"** [4].

## The Tunnel Discovery

Philip Paul's mechanical digger work at the Borley site came to an end in 1956, and it was [[len-sewell|Len Sewell]] who was on site in September 1957 when the long-sought [[tunnels|Tudor-brick tunnel]] under the road between [[borley-place]] and the former Rectory grounds was accidentally cut through by water-main contractors.

## Defence of Harry Price and Views on the Haunting

Paul was a staunch believer in the Borley phenomena and a fierce defender of Harry Price against the highly critical 1956 *Borley Report* (authored by Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) [12, 13]. He pointed out that even the title of the report (*The Haunting of Borley Rectory*) was in complete opposition to its sceptical contents [12], and attacked the authors' portrayal of Price:

> **"The prestidigitation of Price pictured in this unhappy work with such venomous vigour must have been the most ruthless of rogues and the luckiest of liars to have had such aids and accomplices in his Borley manipulations ... is it reasonably credible that a psychist, with a lifetime's reputation for the ruthless exposure of fraud, would jeopardize, at the peak of his career, all that had gone before for no more purpose than to 'stage' a poltergeist?"** [14]

In his book *Some Unseen Power*, Paul wrote that **"even were the whole of Price's work at Borley to be utterly and finally dismissed, the case for paranormalities having occurred there would remain unscathed."** He maintained that "there was firm evidence" that [[the-nun|the phantom nun]] was seen in 1885, 1900, 1916–19, and 1927, and claimed to have "gathered reliable testimonies" of new sightings in **1949, 1953, and 1955** [13].

> **Contradiction:** Different sources within the Borley corpus contradictorily date the publication of *Some Unseen Power: Diary of a Ghost-Hunter* to both **1958** [4] and **1985** [3, 15].
