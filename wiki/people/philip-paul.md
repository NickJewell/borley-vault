# Philip Paul

A clerk and journalist turned psychic investigator who was Vice-President of the old Spiritualist Association of Great Britain. Active at Borley primarily through digging and excavation on the Rectory site, 1954–57.

## Ghost Club Involvement

Philip Paul was active for a time with [[peter-underwood]], Percival Seward, Cyril Wilkinson, Leonard Kingston and Dr C. Nicholson in reviving the [[ghost-club]] in 1954, but friction quickly developed and Paul left the Club.

## Excavations

Paul organised and led excavations on the former [[borley-rectory|Rectory]] site over a five-year period (see [[bones-excavation|Bones and Excavations]]). His work continued the digging started by the Turners and others after World War II. He was assisted by [[len-sewell]], [[steuart-kiernander|Steuart Kiernander]], Leonard Kingston and [[john-dening]], who continued digging after Paul became convinced there was nothing further of interest. None of his four original objectives were achieved: finding the rest of the nun's skeleton, locating an ancient tunnel, confirming a plague pit, or finding lost church plate.

In his book *Some Unseen Power* (Robert Hale, 1985), Paul wrote that "even were the whole of Price's work at Borley to be utterly and finally dismissed, the case for paranormalities having occurred there would remain unscathed." He maintained that "there was firm evidence that the 'nun' phantom was seen in 1885, 1900, 1916–19 and 1927" and claimed to have "gathered reliable testimonies to sightings of the same or a comparable apparition in 1949, 1953 and 1955."

## The Tunnel Discovery

Philip Paul's mechanical digger work at the Borley site came to an end in 1956, and it was [[len-sewell]] who was on site in September 1957 when the long-sought [[tunnels|Tudor-brick tunnel]] under the road between [[borley-place]] and the former Rectory grounds was accidentally cut through by water-main contractors.

## Publications

*Some Unseen Power: Diary of a Ghost-Hunter* (Robert Hale, 1985); for his involvement with the Ghost Club see Rosemary Ellen Guiley, *The Encyclopedia of Ghosts and Spirits* (2000).

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Philip Paul** was a London journalist specializing in occult matters, a ghost hunter, and an author who considered himself to be continuing Harry Price's investigations at Borley Rectory [1, 2]. He was also an associated member of the Ghost Club [3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, specific dates, events, quotes, and textual contradictions regarding his involvement:

**The Borley Excavations (1954–1956)**
Paul’s most significant physical contribution to the Borley saga was a series of excavations he led on the Rectory site between **October 1954 and August 1956** [2]. 
*   **Objectives:** Before beginning, Paul consulted investigator Peter Underwood, who provided a sketch map of reputed tunnel locations [4, 5]. Paul had four specific objectives: to find the rest of the phantom "Nun's" skeleton, to locate an ancient tunnel, to confirm the Rectory grounds were once part of a plague pit, and to find the lost plate of Borley Church [4, 6]. He was assisted by Leonard Sewell, Leonard G. Rayner, Steuart Kiernander, and the Rev. John C. Dening [4, 6].
*   **Initial Digging (1954–1955):** In October 1954, Paul spent a week searching for tunnels, uncovering a dome near Borley Place that proved only to be a well cover [5]. He supervised the digging of three holes on the Rectory site, one of which reached four feet below the old cellar floor into undisturbed ground, but found only large animal bones and rubble [6]. In July 1955, his team unearthed a block of pre-1772 two-inch bricks, establishing the existence of a much earlier building on the site [7, 8].
*   **The Mechanical Digger (1956):** In August 1956, Paul appealed for help and introduced a mechanical digger to the site [7, 9]. While this allowed rapid movement of rubble, critics observed that it was a highly inappropriate tool for the task, risking the destruction of the fragile bones and valuable metalware Paul was hoping to find [10].
*   **Abandonment:** Ultimately, nothing of great moment was discovered, and none of his four main objectives were achieved [4]. Paul concluded there was no point in continuing the project [10]. The sources note that with **"the departure of Philip Paul, the mechanical digger and the publicity that he seemed to enjoy, things became less active at Borley"** [4].

**Media Appearances and the "Chiming Clock"**
Paul was adept at generating publicity for his work at Borley.
*   On **25 March 1953**, he published an article entitled **"Ghosts Still Walk at Borley"** in *The Star* newspaper [1, 2].
*   On **12 April 1953**, he appeared on the popular BBC television programme ***What's My Line*** [1, 2]. During the broadcast, he displayed tracings of the Borley wall-writings, alleging that one of the messages had led directly to the discovery of human remains in the cellars, thereby proving the existence of ghosts [1]. 
*   In the 1950s, he recounted the **"case of the chiming clock"** to David Piper of the *Sunday Graphic* [11]. He described a visitor staying in the Rectory Cottage sitting room who was kept awake by a mournful ticking clock until midnight, when she heard someone tiptoe in and stop it. Paul provided the quote: **"No human being had entered her room... and that clock had not been working for more than two years"** [11]. He noted that these newer, "friendly" ghosts did not cause anyone trouble [11].

**Defense of Harry Price and Views on the Haunting**
Paul was a staunch believer in the Borley phenomena and a fierce defender of Harry Price against the highly critical 1956 *Borley Report* (authored by Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) [12, 13]. 
*   **On the SPR Report:** He pointed out that even the title of the *Borley Report* (*The Haunting of Borley Rectory*) was in complete opposition to its skeptical contents [12]. Attacking the authors' portrayal of Price as a fraudster, Paul wrote: **"The prestidigitation of Price pictured in this unhappy work with such venomous vigour must have been the most ruthless of rogues and the luckiest of liars to have had such aids and accomplices in his Borley manipulations ... is it reasonably credible that a psychist, with a lifetime's reputation for the ruthless exposure of fraud, would jeopardize, at the peak of his career, all that had gone before for no more purpose than to 'stage' a poltergeist?"** [14].
*   **On the Haunting:** He firmly believed the location was haunted, arguing that **"even were the whole of Price’s work at Borley to be utterly and finally dismissed, the case for paranormalities having occurred there would remain unscathed"** [13]. He maintained there was "firm evidence" that the phantom nun was seen in 1885, 1900, 1916–19, and 1927, and claimed he had gathered reliable testimonies of new sightings in **1949, 1953, and 1955** [13].

**Contradictions in the Sources**
The texts contain a notable chronological contradiction regarding Paul's own book about his experiences, ***Some Unseen Power: Diary of a Ghost-Hunter***. Different sources within the Borley corpus contradictorily date the publication of this book to both **1958** [4] and **1985** [3, 15].
