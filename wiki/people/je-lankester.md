# J. E. Lankester

Cambridge University student; member of the [[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]] investigating the Borley ruins. Lankester was part of the group that visited the site on September 22, 1942 (Visit 6), and was the principal witness of a striking incident involving a phantom clock.

## The Phantom Clock

Throughout the night of September 22–23, 1942, Lankester heard the ticking of a clock. The ticking was heard only occasionally by the other observers (J. C. Brown and R. A. Brown). As [[ajb-robertson|A. J. B. Robertson]]'s report notes, there was no clock on the premises.

What made this incident particularly striking was a coincidence revealed afterward: Lankester's mother, in Ipswich, woke during the same night at about midnight, "puzzled by the sound of a clock. There was no clock in the room and none in the house that could be heard there."

She had, at about the same time, **thought of her son** and wondered whether he too was being disturbed by the same thing. Price comments:

> *"Mother and son were en rapport, but the exciting cause is not known."*

The case is unusual in the Borley record for involving a possible telepathic connection between a witness at the site and a person many miles away. It also stands as one of the few Borley phenomena that might be categorised as psychic rather than Poltergeist in character.

## Other Experiences

On the same night, the group also heard measured footsteps crossing the ceiling (a room above them) — heavy and steady — followed by bumps and sounds of something being dragged, then silence, then a renewal. Price regards the footsteps as probably paranormal. The room from which they appeared to come was in a dangerous condition, making a human origin difficult. [[ajb-robertson]] reports these as "probably psychic phenomena."
