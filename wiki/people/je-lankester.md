# J. E. Lankester

Cambridge University student and member of the [[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]] investigating the Borley ruins. Lankester participated in two wartime observational vigils, the first of which produced one of the most unusual incidents in the entire Borley record — a possible telepathic connection between Lankester at the site and his mother many miles away in Ipswich.

## Visit No. 6 — Footsteps and the Phantom Clock (22–23 September 1942)

Lankester's most notable experiences occurred during the Commission's sixth official visit overnight on 22–23 September 1942, conducted alongside fellow observers J. C. Brown and R. A. Brown [1, 2]. Because a door leading from the kitchen passage to the upper stairs had been nailed shut, the group was unable to access the first floor and established their base in the ground-floor sewing-room [1, 2].

- At about 11:00 p.m., the party heard several sharp cracks [2].
- At around 11:30 p.m., the observers heard distinctly heavy, **"measured and steady footsteps"** crossing the ceiling of the room directly above them [2].
- The footsteps were followed by bumps and the sound of something being dragged across the floor. The sounds paused, restarted for several minutes, and ceased abruptly, with a slight renewal occurring at dawn [2].
- Before leaving at dawn, they also heard two sharp cracks from the dining-room [4].

The team noted that it would be extraordinarily difficult for anyone to move with such a measured tread in that specific ruined room, although they honestly acknowledged there was no definitive proof the noises were not produced by a living person [2]. Nevertheless, the observers concluded that the footsteps could probably be considered a **"psychic phenomenon"** [4].

## "The Strange Story of a Clock" (Telepathic Incident)

Throughout the night of 22–23 September 1942, Lankester principally — though the other observers also heard it occasionally — reported hearing the sporadic ticking of a clock [1, 4]. There was no clock on the premises.

What made this incident particularly striking was a coincidence revealed afterward: Lankester's mother, in Ipswich, woke during the same night at about midnight, "puzzled by the sound of a clock. There was no clock in the room and none in the house that could be heard there." [4] She had, at about the same time, **thought of her son** and wondered whether he too was being disturbed by the same thing.

Harry Price documented this as an *en rapport* (telepathic) connection, theorising that Mrs Lankester was subconsciously fearing her son was in danger inside the "haunted house" [3]. Price comments:

> *"Mother and son were en rapport, but the exciting cause is not known."*

The case is unusual in the Borley record for involving a possible telepathic connection between a witness at the site and a person many miles away, and stands as one of the few Borley phenomena that might be categorised as psychic rather than poltergeist in character.

## Visit No. 17 — No Phenomena (17–18 July 1943)

Lankester returned to Borley for the Commission's seventeenth visit overnight on 17–18 July 1943, accompanied by R. A. Brown [5]. Unlike his highly eventful first vigil, the official report for this observation period noted that absolutely no phenomena were experienced [5].
