# J. E. Lankester

Cambridge University student; member of the [[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]] investigating the Borley ruins. Lankester was part of the group that visited the site on September 22, 1942 (Visit 6), and was the principal witness of a striking incident involving a phantom clock.

## The Phantom Clock

Throughout the night of September 22–23, 1942, Lankester heard the ticking of a clock. The ticking was heard only occasionally by the other observers (J. C. Brown and R. A. Brown). As [[ajb-robertson|A. J. B. Robertson]]'s report notes, there was no clock on the premises.

What made this incident particularly striking was a coincidence revealed afterward: Lankester's mother, in Ipswich, woke during the same night at about midnight, "puzzled by the sound of a clock. There was no clock in the room and none in the house that could be heard there."

She had, at about the same time, **thought of her son** and wondered whether he too was being disturbed by the same thing. Price comments:

> *"Mother and son were en rapport, but the exciting cause is not known."*

The case is unusual in the Borley record for involving a possible telepathic connection between a witness at the site and a person many miles away. It also stands as one of the few Borley phenomena that might be categorised as psychic rather than Poltergeist in character.

## Other Experiences

On the same night, the group also heard measured footsteps crossing the ceiling (a room above them) — heavy and steady — followed by bumps and sounds of something being dragged, then silence, then a renewal. Price regards the footsteps as probably paranormal. The room from which they appeared to come was in a dangerous condition, making a human origin difficult. [[ajb-robertson]] reports these as "probably psychic phenomena."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**J. E. Lankester** was a student investigator and a member of the **"Cambridge Commission,"** a group of university undergraduates that conducted a series of wartime observational vigils at the ruined Borley Rectory [1-3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following specific dates, events, and a notable telepathic incident regarding his involvement:

**Visit No. 6 and the Footsteps (September 22–23, 1942)**
Lankester's most notable experiences at the Rectory occurred during the Cambridge Commission's sixth official visit overnight on **September 22–23, 1942**, which he conducted alongside fellow observers J. C. Brown and R. A. Brown [1, 2]. Because a door leading from the kitchen passage to the upper stairs had been nailed shut, the group was unable to access the first floor and established their base in the ground-floor **sewing-room** [1, 2]. 

*   At about 11:00 p.m., the party heard several sharp cracks [2]. 
*   At around 11:30 p.m., the observers heard distinctly heavy, **"measured and steady footsteps"** crossing the ceiling of the room directly above them [2]. 
*   The footsteps were followed by bumps and the sound of something being dragged across the floor. The sounds paused, restarted for several minutes, and ceased abruptly, with a slight renewal occurring at dawn [2]. 
*   Before leaving at dawn, they also heard two sharp cracks from the dining-room [4].

The team noted that it would be extraordinarily difficult for anyone to move with such a measured tread in that specific ruined room, although they honestly acknowledged there was no definitive proof the noises were not produced by a living person [2]. Nevertheless, the observers concluded that the footsteps could probably be considered a **"psychic phenomenon"** [4].

**"The Strange Story of a Clock" (Telepathic Incident)**
Lankester is best known in the Borley literature for a bizarre, seemingly telepathic event that occurred during this same September 1942 vigil. Throughout the night, Lankester principally (though the other observers also heard it occasionally) reported hearing the sporadic ticking of a clock [1, 4]. 

Remarkably, **his mother, Mrs. Lankester, who was located many miles away in Ipswich, was awakened at about midnight that same night by the sound of a clock ticking in her room—where no clock existed** [4]. Even more strikingly, she specifically thought of her son at that exact moment and wondered if he too was being disturbed by a ticking clock [3, 4]. Harry Price documented this as an *en rapport* (telepathic) connection between mother and son, theorizing that Mrs. Lankester was subconsciously fearing her son was in danger inside the "haunted house" [3]. 

**Visit No. 17 (July 17–18, 1943)**
Lankester returned to Borley for the Commission's seventeenth visit overnight on **July 17–18, 1943**, accompanied by R. A. Brown [5]. Unlike his highly eventful first vigil, the official report for this observation period noted that absolutely no phenomena were experienced [5].
