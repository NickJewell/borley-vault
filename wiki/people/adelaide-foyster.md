Born **Barbara Alice Tower** on **20 March 1928** in West Sackville, New Brunswick, Canada; baptised 3 March 1929. Youngest of seven children of Seward Tower and Alice Tower. Her mother died of a brain tumour in January 1929, followed only a month later by her father, who fractured his skull falling from a load of marsh hay [2]. The orphaned infant was subsequently adopted by [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Algernon Foyster]] and his young wife [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]], who renamed her **Adelaide** after Lionel's own mother [1–3]. Adopted formally in 1930, she was approximately 2 years 7 months old when the family arrived at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] on October 16, 1930.

In a bizarre display of control, Marianne raised Adelaide to believe she was an only child, deliberately concealing the existence of her six biological siblings in North America [4]. Marianne later told the growing girl that Lionel was her adoptive father but that Marianne herself was actually Lionel's daughter — making them "half-sisters" [5]. Consequently, Adelaide was instructed to call her adoptive mother "Marianne" instead of "Mum" [5].

## Life at Borley Rectory (1930–1935)

At Borley, Adelaide's upbringing was remarkably strict. The villagers found the Foysters' parenting style eccentric; Marianne insisted on dressing Adelaide in overalls, which was common in Canada but considered highly improper by the rural English locals [5, 9]. Because the Rectory was isolated, Adelaide was a lonely child whose only initial companions were a puppy and a kitten named Romp [5]. Eventually, the Foysters brought in a young boy, François d'Arles Jr. (the son of Marianne's lover, [[francois-darles|Frank Pearless]]), to act as her playmate [10, 11].

Adelaide was present for many of the most famous poltergeist events of the Foyster incumbency, experiencing phenomena from her very first day in the house when she, alongside her parents, allegedly heard footsteps and a voice calling "Marianne" [7, 12].

## Phenomena Witnessed

**The bell-ringing (October 13–14, 1931)**: During Price's second sealed-house night at the Rectory, a bell rang from Adelaide's room. Price had previously noted that the bell wire had been broken off close to the ceiling — making it physically impossible for a 3½-year-old to pull it. The room's seals were not disturbed. This is one of the most controlled instances of spontaneous bell-ringing in the record. Price nonetheless directly accused the toddler of faking the phenomenon, stating, "It couldn't have been Mrs. Foyster so it must have been Adelaide. She is suspect now" [24–26].

**"A nasty thing by curtain"**: Sometime in October–November 1931, Adelaide was sent to rest in Room 3 (over the kitchen), a room in which a shadowy apparition had been reported. She came downstairs with a bruise under her eye. When asked how she got it, she said: "A nasty thing by curtain in my room gave it me" [13–16]. No adult witnessed this; but the same apparition was reported by other people in Room 3. On another occasion, she and young François were found cowering near the dining-room door during a violent poltergeist outbreak, telling adults, "We don't like so many 'fings falling about" [15, 17, 18].

**The wall-writing accusation (disputed)**: Adelaide was accused of producing the famous [[wall-writings|Borley wall-writings]]. An investigator interviewing a former housekeeper was told that Adelaide was "a terror for scribbling — she used to write on every wall" and drew little lines of doll's houses [27–29]. However, this directly contradicts Lionel Foyster and investigator Mollie Goldney, who both noted that at the time the writings appeared, Adelaide was only three years old, "very backward," and entirely incapable of writing complex words or sentences [29, 30]. (disputed)

**The arson accusations**: Miss Evelyn Gordon, who ran a nearby children's playgroup, reported Adelaide to the NSPCC in 1933 over concerns of neglect; the investigation found conditions poor but no "technical cruelty." Gordon claimed the five-year-old would build small fires out of pillows in the bedrooms and talked constantly of "having to burn down the Rectory" [31, 32]. Sceptics point out the improbability of a toddler orchestrating such acts without adults keeping matches away from her [33].

**The poltergeist-focus theory**: Researchers [[iris-owen|Iris Owen]] and [[paulene-mitchell|Pauline Mitchell]] later theorised that Adelaide's profound emotional confusion — having lost her biological parents, been separated from her siblings, moved to a strange country, and immersed in ghost stories — made her a classic subconscious trigger for genuine poltergeist phenomena [34].

## Mastoiditis Crisis (1933)

Adelaide fell dangerously ill with mastoiditis around 1934; taken to Great Ormond Street Hospital, she underwent two operations with little hope offered for her recovery [19, 20]. Marianne secretly slipped a relic of St. John Vianney (the Curé d'Ars) into the child's bandages; within days, her ear miraculously healed, astonishing her doctors and reinforcing Lionel Foyster's belief in divine and supernatural intervention [20, 21].

## Abandonment and Later Life

After Lionel Foyster's health failed and the family left Borley in 1935, Adelaide briefly lived with Marianne, Lionel, and Marianne's bigamous husband, [[henry-fisher|Henry Fisher]], in Ipswich [35, 36]. However, Marianne soon placed Adelaide in a convent in Braintree run by the Sisters of the Immaculate Conception [37, 38]. By 1940, Marianne had largely abandoned her, placing her in an orphanage and severing all contact [3, 39].

Adelaide moved to Manchester in 1944, where she trained and worked as a nurse [40]. In 1950, she married a diesel engineer named Gordon Cartwright, becoming **Adelaide Cartwright** [40].

## Reunion with the Tower Family (1974)

For decades, Adelaide believed she was an only child with no blood relatives. In 1973, her biological brother Wallace Tower, after years of searching, traced her via Borley investigator [[alan-roper|Alan Roper]], eventually obtaining her married name and telephone number. In November 1974, Adelaide received a phone call from Wallace, and that Christmas she flew to Rhode Island to meet all five surviving siblings — completing what she described as **"the most marvellous reunion of a lifetime"** after 46 years apart [41, 42].

## The Foyster Bible

In 1994, Adelaide wrote to [[vincent-oneil|Vincent O'Neil]] requesting the return of Lionel's 1537 Bible, which Marianne had taken to America and bequeathed to Vince. Signing herself "formerly Adelaide Foyster," she argued it had "no historical interest in the USA and should never have left England" [43]. Vince relinquished it; the Bible was returned to Adelaide [40].

Despite her central placement in the Borley Rectory legend, Adelaide maintained a grounded perspective on her childhood. In letters written in 1994 and 1995, she revealed that she had spent most of her youth away at boarding schools and possessed only "vague memories" of Borley [44]. She candidly admitted that she didn't even know the house was supposedly haunted until she read books about it later in life [44].

## Companion Note (brc-ii-ef)

As of 2008, Adelaide was the last living member of the Foyster household at Borley. Now resident in northern England under a different name. Her grandson told Paul Adams that she recollected seeing the apparition of a weeping nun on the Rectory lawn, and experiencing writing appearing on her bedroom wall at night — experiences consistent with the Foyster-period record, though from a very young child whose testimony must be treated carefully.
