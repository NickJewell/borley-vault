Born **Barbara Alice Tower** on **20 March 1928** in West Sackville, New Brunswick, Canada; baptised 3 March 1929. Youngest of seven children of Seward Tower (killed in an agricultural accident) and Alice (died in childbirth). The Foysters, then in Sackville, agreed to take the infant; renamed her Adelaide after Lionel's mother. Adopted formally in 1930. The Foysters, then in Sackville, agreed to take the infant. 

Child adopted by [[lionel-foyster]] and [[marianne-foyster]], aged approximately 2 years 7 months when the family arrived at [[borley-rectory]] on October 16, 1930. She was present throughout the Foyster period and witnessed phenomena at a very young age.

## Phenomena Witnessed

**The bell-ringing (October 13–14, 1931)**: During Price's second sealed-house night at the Rectory, a bell rang from Adelaide's room. Price had previously noted that the bell wire had been broken off close to the ceiling — making it physically impossible for a 3½-year-old to pull it. The room's seals were not disturbed. This is one of the most controlled instances of spontaneous bell-ringing in the record.

**"A nasty thing by curtain"**: Sometime in October–November 1931, Adelaide was sent to rest in Room 3 (over the kitchen), a room in which a shadowy apparition had been reported. She came downstairs with a bruise under her eye. When asked how she got it, she said: "A nasty thing by curtain in my room gave it me." No adult witnessed this; but the same apparition was reported by other people in Room 3. (Foyster diary: "This same apparition said to have been seen also early one morning, entering our bedroom.")

**Physical harm**: The Foyster period record notes that Adelaide was injured on at least one occasion: [[lionel-foyster]]'s diary records "Adelaide Foyster injured by a 'nasty thing'" in October–November 1931.

## Significance

Adelaide is notable as one of very few child witnesses in the record, and the youngest. The "nasty thing by curtain" account — a child's straightforward description of an encounter that left a physical mark — is cited by Price as evidence that the phenomena were not purely adult projection or expectation.

## Later Life (per O'Connor, hbrs-ch18, hbrs-ch20)

Adelaide had a "very unhappy" childhood at Borley and didn't want to discuss it. She was told by Marianne that she was an only child — her blood siblings in Canada (Wallace, Edna, Fred, Royal, Merton Tower) were not mentioned.

**Mastoiditis crisis (1933):** fell dangerously ill; taken to Great Ormond Street Hospital. Two operations; near-death. The St John Vianney relic was placed in her bandages; she recovered unexpectedly. Evelyn Gordon (children's playgroup, Borley Lodge) reported Adelaide to the NSPCC in 1933 over concerns of neglect; the investigation found conditions poor but no "technical cruelty."

**Behavioural issues:** Adelaide began scribbling on walls and building small fires out of pillows in the bedrooms. She would talk of "having to burn down the Rectory." Not considered typical child behaviour. (hbrs-ch18)

**Left the Rectory 1935;** began nursing training in Manchester from 1944. Married Gordon Cartwright (diesel engineer) in 1950.

**Reunion with Tower family (November 1974):** Her brother Wallace Tower, after years of searching, traced her via phone. When he rang her in Manchester, Adelaide was speechless. She flew to Rhode Island for Christmas 1974 and was reunited with all surviving siblings after 46 years. "It was not until we set foot at Kennedy Airport... that we knew it was not a fairy story." It was, she said, "the most marvellous reunion of a lifetime." (hbrs-ch20)

**The Foyster Bible:** In 1994, Adelaide (now Mrs Cartwright) wrote to [[vincent-oneil|Vincent O'Neil]] requesting the return of Lionel's 1537 Bible, which Marianne had taken to America. Vince relinquished it; the Bible was returned to Adelaide.

## Companion Note (brc-ii-ef)

As of 2008, Adelaide was the last living member of the Foyster household at Borley. Now resident in northern England under a different name. Her grandson told Paul Adams that she recollected seeing the apparition of a weeping nun on the Rectory lawn, and experiencing writing appearing on her bedroom wall at night — experiences consistent with the Foyster-period record, though from a very young child whose testimony must be treated carefully.
