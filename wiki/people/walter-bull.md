# Walter Bull

Son of [[henry-bull]] (the builder-Rector) and brother of [[harry-bull]]. Born at [[borley-rectory]]. Reportedly spent much of his life at sea, giving him few opportunities to witness phenomena directly, though he lived at Chilton Lodge, Sudbury with his sisters later in life. Interviewed by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] at Chilton Lodge on **29 March 1939**.

## The Identity Contradiction

The sources contain a significant chronological contradiction regarding Walter Bull's life and death:

> **Contradiction:** Several historical researchers point out that "Walter" was actually the middle name of **Basil Walter Bull**, who according to these records left the Rectory for Canada in 1885, became an American citizen, volunteered for the Canadian Expeditionary Force, and **died in Belgium in 1917** after being wounded by a high-explosive shell during World War I [1–4]. Because of this, several informants who knew the Bull family well could not recall a living "Walter" in later years [2]. In direct contradiction to the 1917 death date, Harry Price explicitly claimed to have **interviewed Walter Bull at Chilton Lodge on 29 March 1939**, alongside Sidney Glanville [5, 6]. Price noted that Glanville had also previously met Walter when he called at the Rectory on 20 November 1937 [6]. To explain why Walter was not well-remembered locally, Price claimed Walter had "spent most of his life at sea" [2, 7].

## Phenomena Witnessed

**Footsteps in the lane**: Walter Bull's primary testimony — and an important one: although he "never saw anything unusual in the Rectory during the time he was living there" (noises were frequent but not disturbing enough to excite comment), he had a consistent outdoor experience:

> When walking up the lane and approaching the Rectory, "he, at least fifty times, heard footsteps following him." He always turned round, but never saw anyone, and could not account for them [7, 9].

This testimony matches [[harry-bull]]'s own description of hearing a "great clatter of hoofs" on the same hollow lane while returning to the Rectory, and Herbert Mayes's 1939 experience of phantom horses on the same stretch of road. The lane outside the Rectory was consistently associated with unexplained sounds.

Walter Bull was present at the Rectory when his father [[henry-bull]] died, but was away when his brother [[harry-bull]] died.

## Extreme Scepticism

Walter was a staunch sceptic regarding the haunted reputation of his childhood home. In his manuscript *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*, the Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] ruefully described Walter as being **"entirely unbelieving"** [8, 9]. Walter emphatically maintained that **"nothing abnormal ever happened in the rectory when the Bulls lived there"** [4, 8, 9]. He also reportedly visited the Foysters on many occasions at the Rectory during the 1930s and was noted as being **"very unimpressed with the integrity of the rector's 'wife'"** [2].

**The Bricked-Up Window:** The authors of the [[the-borley-report|*Borley Report*]] noted that Walter Bull provided the entirely mundane explanation for the famous bricked-up dining room window: rather than being sealed to prevent the phantom nun from staring at the family, Walter stated it was sealed **"because people passing along the road could see the Bull family having their meals"** [10]. Critics point out that [[harry-price|Harry Price]] was told this explanation but deliberately omitted Walter as the authoritative source in his books to maintain the mystery.

## The Dr. Alexander Anecdote

A lesser-known detail found in [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]]'s [[locked-book|*Locked Book*]] records Walter Bull mentioning a "Doctor Alexander." According to Walter, this physician had visited the Rectory during the Rev. Henry Bull's time and subsequently declared he would **"never enter the place again"** [11]. Researchers have speculated whether the doctor was referring to the haunting, or to some scandalous family secret he had been called upon to attend.

## Significance

A conservative witness who claims minimal direct experience — which makes his fifty-odd footstep encounters the more striking. His testimony contributes to the pattern of outdoor phenomena concentrated on the lane between the Rectory gates.
