Son of [[henry-bull]] (the builder-Rector) and brother of [[harry-bull]]. Born at [[borley-rectory]]. Spent much of his life at sea, giving him few opportunities to witness phenomena directly, though he lived at Chilton Lodge, Sudbury with his sisters later in life. Interviewed by [[harry-price]] and [[sidney-glanville]] at Chilton Lodge on March 29, 1939.

## Phenomena Witnessed

**Footsteps in the lane**: Walter Bull's primary testimony — and an important one: although he "never saw anything unusual in the Rectory during the time he was living there" (noises were frequent but not disturbing enough to excite comment), he did have a consistent outdoor experience:

> When walking up the lane and approaching the Rectory, "he, at least fifty times, heard footsteps following him." He always turned round, but never saw anyone, and could not account for them.

This testimony matches [[harry-bull]]'s own description of hearing a "great clatter of hoofs" on the same hollow lane while returning to the Rectory, and [[herbert-mayes]]'s 1939 experience of phantom horses on the same stretch of road. The lane outside the Rectory was consistently associated with unexplained sounds.

Walter Bull was present at the Rectory when his father [[henry-bull]] died, but was away when his brother [[harry-bull]] died.

## Significance

A conservative witness who claims minimal direct experience — which makes his fifty-odd footstep encounters the more striking. His testimony contributes to the pattern of outdoor phenomena concentrated on the lane between the Rectory gates.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Walter Bull** is recorded in the Borley sources as one of the sons of the Rev. Henry D. E. Bull, but his presence in the texts creates one of the most glaring historical contradictions in the entire Borley Rectory saga. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, specific events, quotes, and the major discrepancy regarding his existence:

**The Identity Contradiction**
The sources contain a massive chronological contradiction regarding Walter Bull's life and death:
*   **The WWI Casualty:** Several historical researchers point out that "Walter" was actually the middle name of **Basil Walter Bull**. According to these records, Basil Walter left the Rectory for Canada in 1885, became an American citizen, volunteered for the Canadian Expeditionary Force, and **died in Belgium in 1917 after being wounded by a high-explosive shell** during World War I [1-4]. Because of this, several informants who knew the Bull family well could not recall a living "Walter" in later years [2].
*   **Price's 1939 Interview:** In direct contradiction to the 1917 death date, ghost hunter Harry Price explicitly claimed to have **interviewed Walter Bull at Chilton Lodge, Sudbury on March 29, 1939**, alongside Sidney Glanville [5, 6]. Price noted that Glanville had also previously met Walter when he called at the Rectory on November 20, 1937 [6]. 
*   To explain why Walter was not well-remembered locally, Price claimed that Walter had **"spent most of his life at sea"** [2, 7]. 

**Biographical Details (According to Price and Foyster)**
*   Walter was born at Borley Rectory [7].
*   He was at home when his father, Henry Bull, died in 1892, but he was away at sea when his brother, Harry Bull, died in 1927 [7].
*   He is reported to have visited the Rev. Lionel Foyster and his wife Marianne on many occasions at the Rectory during the 1930s, and was noted as being **"very unimpressed with the integrity of the rector’s 'wife'"** [2]. 

**Extreme Skepticism and Quotes**
Walter was a staunch skeptic regarding the haunted reputation of his childhood home. 
*   In his manuscript *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*, the Rev. Lionel Foyster ruefully described Walter as being **"entirely unbelieving"** [8, 9]. 
*   Walter emphatically maintained that **"nothing abnormal ever happened in the rectory when the Bulls lived there"** [4, 8, 9]. 
*   **The Bricked-Up Window:** The authors of the skeptical *Borley Report* noted that Walter Bull provided the entirely mundane explanation for the famous bricked-up dining room window. Rather than being bricked up to prevent the phantom nun from staring at the family, Walter stated it was sealed **"because people passing along the road could see the Bull family having their meals"**. Critics point out that Harry Price was told this explanation, but deliberately omitted Walter as the authoritative source in his books to maintain the mystery [10].

**The Phantom Footsteps**
Despite his absolute denial of any indoor hauntings, Walter did admit to experiencing one outdoor phenomenon. He stated that when walking up the lane and approaching the Rectory, **he heard footsteps following him on "at least fifty times"** [7, 9]. He noted that he always turned round, but never saw anyone, and could never rationally account for the sounds [7]. 

**The Dr. Alexander Anecdote**
A lesser-known detail found in Sidney Glanville's *Locked Book* records Walter Bull mentioning a "Doctor Alexander." According to Walter, this physician had visited the Rectory during the Rev. Henry Bull's time and subsequently declared that he would **"never enter the place again."** Researchers have speculated whether the doctor was referring to the haunting, or to some scandalous family secret he had been called upon to attend [11].
