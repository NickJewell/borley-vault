# Father Hayden, S.J.

Jesuit priest. In 1892, he spent nine nights at Ballechin House, Perthshire — disturbed by extraordinary phenomena every night except the last — and his account, related to [[lord-bute|Lord Bute]] at a chance meeting, triggered the systematic investigation of Ballechin.

## Nine Nights at Ballechin (July 1892)

Hayden occupied Ballechin (then furnished but the Steuarts not in residence) from July 14–23, 1892. He was disturbed every night except the last, sleeping in two different rooms. Phenomena included:

- **Loud inexplicable noises** each night — including "sounds like continuous explosions of petards" occurring between his bed and the ceiling
- **"Battering"** sounds at the room doors as if something were falling heavily against them — he described it as "like a calf or big dog falling against the door"
- **Screams and raps** throughout the building
- Noises followed him from room to room

He sprinkled the rooms with holy water and recited the *Visita quaesumus* prayer for the divine protection of a house. He also had a Roman Catholic Archbishop attempt exorcism. Both failed to stop the disturbances. Only on the ninth night — when he moved to a different room in the house — did he sleep undisturbed.

After leaving, Hayden spoke of his experiences to almost no one for a year. Then, in August 1893, he met by chance a former governess to the Steuart family, who *without any prompting* said she had left her employment because so many people complained of queer noises in the house. The noises had occurred in the identical bedrooms Hayden had occupied.

## Meeting with Lord Bute

In 1892 Hayden related his Ballechin experiences to [[lord-bute|Lord Bute]], who stored the information. When further reports accumulated in 1896 from the Spanish family's butler, Bute decided to investigate. Hayden's account was thus the seed of the entire Ballechin investigation.

[[harry-price|Price]] draws the explicit parallel: it was a Roman Catholic priest ([[rev-eric-smith|the Rev. G. Eric Smith]]) who reported Borley to a psychical researcher ([[harry-price|Price]]); it was a Roman Catholic priest (Father Hayden) who reported Ballechin to a psychical researcher ([[lord-bute|Lord Bute]]). The two cases begin with the same institutional pathway.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Father Hayden, S.J.** is not directly connected to the haunting of Borley Rectory itself; rather, he features in the Borley sources as a central figure in the famous haunting of Ballechin House in Scotland [1]. Psychical researcher Harry Price drew a direct comparison between the Ballechin and Borley cases in his writings, specifically noting that **in both hauntings it was a priest who originally reported the phenomena to a psychical researcher**—the Rev. G. Eric Smith at Borley (to Harry Price), and Father Hayden at Ballechin (to John, third Marquess of Bute) [1, 2].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, quotes, and details regarding his experiences:

**The Ballechin House Haunting (July 1892)**
In 1892, the psychical researcher Lord Bute met Father Hayden, who related an extraordinary account of his recent stay at Ballechin House, which was completely furnished but unoccupied by its owners at the time [1]. Father Hayden visited the estate from **Thursday, July 14, to Saturday, July 23, 1892**, and experienced terrifying phenomena [1]. 

He reported hearing screams and raps, and distinctly described hearing **"loud noises like continuous explosions of petards"** between his bed and the ceiling [3]. Moving from room to room offered no relief, as the disturbances seemed to follow him [3]. In an attempt to banish the entities, **Father Hayden sprinkled the rooms with holy water and recited the *Visita quaesumus***—a prayer seeking divine protection for a house and its inhabitants—but these efforts entirely failed to stop the disturbances [3].

**Father Hayden's Testimony**
Father Hayden recounted his experiences to Lord Bute in a detailed statement:
> **"I slept at Ballechin for nine nights, or rather one night, because I was disturbed by very queer and extraordinary noises every night except the last, which I spent in Mr Steuart’s dressing-room"** [1]. 

He explained that while occupying two different rooms on the right of the landing, he was terrified on two or three nights by **"a sound of somebody or something falling against the door outside,"** which he consistently described as sounding as if **"a calf or a big dog"** had fallen against the wood [1]. He concluded his testimony with a frank admission regarding his fear: 
> **"One of the oddest things in my case, in connexion with the house, is that it appeared to me (a) that somebody was relieved by my departure; (b) that nothing could induce me to pass another night there, at all events alone, though in other respects I do not think I am a coward"** [1].

**The 1893 Corroboration**
In **August 1893**, Father Hayden had an accidental encounter with a young woman who had served as a governess for the Steuart family at Ballechin in 1881 [4]. Without Father Hayden ever mentioning that he had visited the estate, the former governess volunteered that she had quit her job there because she became frightened after so many people complained of queer noises [4]. Upon further questioning, **Father Hayden discovered that the noises the governess described had occurred in the exact same bedrooms he had occupied** during his harrowing stay [4].
