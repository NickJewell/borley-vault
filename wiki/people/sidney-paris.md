# Sidney A. Paris

Civil engineer of 62 Cornwall Road, Bishopston, Bristol. Writing on April 25, 1941, Paris provided a detailed Catholic theological analysis of the Borley hauntings, drawing on the *Philosophia Perennis* (Aristotelian-Thomist tradition) and distinguishing analytically between the nun apparition and the Poltergeist phenomena.

## Theological Framework

Paris argues that the human being has a spiritual soul which (absent divine annihilation) is indestructible, establishing the *possibility* of discarnate souls. For the existence of specific spirits (good, evil, and the geography of afterlife states), he turns to Revelation and Church teaching.

His key distinction:
- **The nun**: Probably a personality — "a being having intelligence and will" — either a human soul or a good/evil spirit. The visible shape is "tenuous matter borrowed perhaps from the observers"
- **The Poltergeists**: Not human entities, but possibly (following Father Aidan Elrington, O.P.) spirits of a non-human order — or perhaps monks from the supposed priory who suffered during the Reformation and retained feelings of revenge

His proposed general narrative: a crime **"selecting persons out of different historic periods and making them 'earth-bound' at this spot"** [1] — the nun (1667?), the coachman (seventeenth-century), and the Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] (1927) as a chain of psychic imprinting at the same location.

## The Mass Argument

Paris suggested that a Mass on the spot might be the "condition of her release" — an exact parallel to what the entities themselves had been requesting. He proposed this analysis in April 1941, nearly two and a half years before [[father-john-wright|Father Wright]] said the first documented Requiem Mass in October 1943 and before the 1945 reburial of [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]].

Price quotes Paris's letter without direct comment, but its emphasis on the Mass as the proper resolution is consistent with the eventual course Price took.
