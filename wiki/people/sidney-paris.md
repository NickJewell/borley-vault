# Sidney A. Paris

Civil engineer of 62 Cornwall Road, Bishopston, Bristol. Writing on April 25, 1941, Paris provided a detailed Catholic theological analysis of the Borley hauntings, drawing on the *Philosophia Perennis* (Aristotelian-Thomist tradition) and distinguishing analytically between the nun apparition and the Poltergeist phenomena.

## Theological Framework

Paris argues that the human being has a spiritual soul which (absent divine annihilation) is indestructible. This establishes the *possibility* of discarnate souls. For the existence of specific spirits (good, evil, and the geography of afterlife states), he turns to Revelation and Church teaching.

His key distinction:
- The **nun**: Probably a personality — "a being having intelligence and will" — either a human soul or a good/evil spirit. The visible shape is "tenuous matter borrowed perhaps from the observers"
- The **Poltergeists**: Not human entities, but possibly (following Father [[father-aidan-elrington|Aidan Elrington, O.P.]]) spirits of a non-human order — or perhaps monks from the supposed priory who suffered during the Reformation and retained feelings of revenge

His proposed general narrative: a crime "selecting persons out of different historic periods and making them 'earth-bound' at this spot" — the nun (1667?), the coachman (seventeenth-century), and the Rev. Harry Bull (1927) as a chain of psychic imprinting at the same location.

## The Mass Argument

Paris suggested that a Mass on the spot might be the "condition of her release" — an exact parallel to what the entities themselves had been requesting. He proposed this analysis in April 1941, nearly two and a half years before [[father-john-wright|Father Wright]] said the first documented Requiem Mass in October 1943 and before the 1945 reburial.

Price quotes Paris's letter without direct comment, but its emphasis on the Mass as the proper resolution is consistent with the eventual course Price took.

*   Paris cataloged what he viewed as the **"main ghostly actors"** at Borley into five distinct categories: **"1. The nun. 2. The monk. 3. The coach and headless man. 4. The Rev. Harry Bull. 5. Poltergeists, etc."** [1].
*   **Theory:** His primary contribution was a hypothesis regarding how these seemingly disparate apparitions were connected. He proposed the idea of a **"chain development,"** theorizing: **"There seems to be some cause selecting persons out of different historic periods and making them ‘earth-bound’ at this spot"** [1]. To illustrate his point, he noted that the entity of the nun, the coachman, and the Rev. Harry Bull all originated from completely different eras in history [1].
