# Colonel J. Wroblewski

Officer Commanding the Polish Signal Training Centre; senior-most military officer present at the first of the two Polish Army investigations of the Borley ruins in June 1943.

## First Night (June 28–29, 1943)

Wroblewski was part of the four-man party that included [[gb-nawrocki|Lt. G. B. Nawrocki]], [[lt-kujawa|Lt. W. Kujawa]], and Second Lt. [[lt-ligaszewski|A. Ligaszewski]]. As O.C. of the Signal Training Centre, his presence indicates these were officers of senior rank investigating on their own initiative, not junior enlisted men. *(Note: Wroblewski was present only for this first night of the investigation, and is not listed among the party that returned a month later on July 28 [2].)*

The party arrived at 8:00 p.m. and spent the night at the ruins until 5:15 a.m. The official timeline records the following specific events:

- **8:00–8:15 p.m.:** The party arrived and entered the Rectory to begin their investigation [1].
- **9:05 p.m.:** While the party was on the first floor, the kitchen door below them opened and shut with a great noise, despite the ground floor being empty [3].
- **9:10 p.m.:** The party decided to spend the night in the ground-floor dining room, improvising a makeshift table from a half-burnt drawer and building seats from bricks gathered in the garden [3].
- **10:00 p.m.:** Wroblewski and the group took up a position in the summer-house to **wait for the appearance of the phantom nun** [4]. (Lt. Nawrocki claimed to see a black shadow moving on the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] [4].)
- **1:00 a.m.:** Seated in complete darkness in the dining room, the party heard scratching sounds from the corner near the window. The report notes that despite their efforts, the group **"cannot enter into conversation with the 'scratching ghost'"** [4].
- **2:40 a.m.:** While conversing, Lt. Kujawa warned the others to be quiet "because a brick could easily be thrown from the ceiling upon our heads." Immediately after he spoke, **half a brick plummeted from the ceiling and landed directly in the centre of their makeshift table** with a massive crash [5].
- **5:15 a.m.:** The party departed [5].

Wroblewski is listed as one of the four signatories of the protocol submitted by Nawrocki to [[harry-price|Price]] — a report written in what Price describes as "perfect English." His rank as Colonel adds to the credibility of the investigation: as a senior military officer accustomed to evaluating conditions and recording events accurately, his endorsement of Nawrocki's protocol carries weight.

## Contradictions and Scepticism

> **Contradiction:** Price published the Polish officers' report in *The End of Borley Rectory*, championing their testimony as being of the "highest importance" to prove the Rectory was still actively haunted even after burning down [6]. However, the authors of the 1956 [[spr|SPR]] *Borley Report* **bluntly dismissed the Polish investigation as an "elaborate hoax"**, stating **"Frankly, we cannot take this account seriously"** [7, 8]. The SPR critics argued it was incredibly suspicious that Wroblewski's party managed to experience a "cross-section of virtually the whole of the more sensational traditional phenomena" in just a few hours — especially given that the Cambridge Commission undergraduates were investigating the ruins during the exact same period and had experienced virtually nothing across 25 separate visits [8, 9].
