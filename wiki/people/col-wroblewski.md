# Colonel J. Wroblewski

Officer Commanding the Polish Signal Training Centre; senior-most military officer present at the first of the two Polish Army Medical Corps investigations of the Borley ruins in June 1943.

## First Night (June 28–29, 1943)

Wroblewski was part of the four-man party that included [[lt-nawrocki|Lt. G. B. Nawrocki]], [[lt-kujawa|Lt. W. Kujawa]], and Second Lt. [[lt-ligaszewski|A. Ligaszewski]]. As O.C. of the Signal Training Centre, his presence indicates that these were officers of senior rank investigating on their own initiative, not junior enlisted men.

The party arrived at 8:00 p.m. and spent the night at the ruins until 5:15 a.m. The reported phenomena during this visit included stones thrown at Nawrocki and Kujawa in the kitchen passage, a door opening and shutting by itself on the first floor while no one was downstairs, scratching heard in the dining-room, and at 2:40 a.m. a half-brick thrown from the ceiling directly onto the impromptu table.

Wroblewski is listed as one of the four signatories of the protocol submitted by Nawrocki to [[harry-price|Price]] — a report written in what Price describes as "perfect English."

His rank as Colonel adds to the credibility of the investigation: as a senior military officer accustomed to evaluating conditions and recording events accurately, his implicit endorsement of Nawrocki's protocol carries weight.

See [[lt-nawrocki]] for the full documented account of both nights.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Colonel J. Wroblewski** was a military officer who served as the **Officer Commanding (O.C.) of the Polish Signal Training Centre** during the Second World War [1]. In the Borley sources, he is recorded exclusively for his participation in the famous "Polish Investigation" of the Rectory ruins in the summer of 1943. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following facts, events, and contradictions regarding his involvement:

**The Investigation of June 28–29, 1943**
Colonel Wroblewski visited the gutted ruins of Borley Rectory for a single overnight vigil on **June 28–29, 1943**, accompanied by three other Polish officers: Lieut. W. Kujawa, Second Lieut. A. Ligaszewski, and Lieut. G. B. Nawrocki (a doctor in the Polish Army Medical Corps who authored the official report of the visit) [1]. *(Note: Wroblewski was present only for this first night of the investigation, and is not listed among the party that returned a month later on July 28 [2]).* 

During his night at the Rectory, Wroblewski was present for a highly concentrated series of alleged paranormal events. The official timeline of his visit records the following specific details:
*   **8:00 p.m. to 8:15 p.m.:** The party arrived at the site, looked outside the ruins, and then Wroblewski and the rest of the group entered the Rectory to begin their investigation [1]. 
*   **9:05 p.m.:** While Wroblewski and the party were on the first floor, the kitchen door below them was heard to open and shut with a great noise, despite the ground floor being empty [3].
*   **9:10 p.m.:** The party decided to spend the night in the ground-floor dining room. Here, they **improvised a makeshift table using a half-burnt drawer from the pantry and built seats out of bricks** they gathered from the garden [3].
*   **10:00 p.m.:** Wroblewski and the group took up a position in the summer-house specifically to **wait for the appearance of the phantom "nun"** [4]. (While there, Lieut. Nawrocki claimed to see a black shadow moving on the Nun's Walk [4]).
*   **1:00 a.m.:** Having returned to the dining room to sit in "complete darkness," the party heard scratching sounds coming from the corner of the room near the window. The report notes that despite their efforts, **the group "cannot enter into conversation with the 'scratching ghost'"** [4].
*   **2:40 a.m.:** While conversing, Lieut. Kujawa warned the others to be quiet "because a brick could easily be thrown from the ceiling upon our heads." Immediately after he spoke, **half a brick plummeted from the ceiling and landed directly in the centre of their makeshift table** with a massive crash [5]. 
*   **5:15 a.m.:** Wroblewski and the party departed the Rectory [5].

**Contradictions and Skepticism**
The events witnessed by Colonel Wroblewski and his colleagues became a major point of contention between Harry Price and his critics. Price published the Polish officers' report in his book *The End of Borley Rectory*, championing their testimony as being of the "highest importance" to prove the Rectory was still actively haunted even after burning down [6]. 

However, the authors of the highly critical 1956 Society for Psychical Research (SPR) *Borley Report* **bluntly dismissed the Polish investigation as an "elaborate hoax"** and stated, **"Frankly, we cannot take this account seriously"** [7, 8]. 

The SPR critics argued it was incredibly suspicious that Wroblewski's party managed to experience a "cross-section of virtually the whole of the more sensational traditional phenomena" (including stone-throwing, door-slamming, scratching, and shadowy figures) in just a few hours, especially considering that the Cambridge Commission undergraduates were investigating the ruins during the exact same period and had experienced virtually nothing across 25 separate visits [8, 9].
