# Vincent O'Neil (1945–)

Also known as "The Son of Borley." Born **Peter Richard Howlett** on or about **9 October 1945** in Ipswich, England, to Kate Howlett of Framlingham — a woman who had worked at Helmingham School. His biological father's identity was never confirmed; Kate had an affair with an American serviceman, and her husband agreed to reconcile only on the condition that the child be disposed of or aborted [2, 3].

> **Contradiction:** Most sources and chronological entries record his birth date as 9 October 1945; one specific narrative text places it on 8 October 1945 [1–4].

He was adopted by [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] (then Mrs. Robert O'Neil) at approximately one month old. Marianne falsely informed her new American husband, Robert O'Neil from Minnesota, that he was the father. The boy was baptised **Robert Vincent O'Neil Jr.** on **11 July 1946** at St Mary's Church in Axminster, Devon [5, 6]. In August 1946, Marianne emigrated to the United States with the baby, somehow bypassing US military bureaucracy without a birth certificate for the child [9, 11]. He grew up in America — in North Dakota, Wisconsin, Minnesota — entirely unaware of his connection to the Borley legend.

## Early Life in America

Vincent's early life in America was unstable, spent in towns like [[la-crosse-wisconsin|La Crosse]] and Bangor in Wisconsin, [[hokah-minnesota|Hokah]] in Minnesota, and [[jamestown-north-dakota|Jamestown]] and [[fargo-north-dakota|Fargo]] in North Dakota [2, 12, 13]. His adoptive father's alcoholism and violence led to a temporary placement in the La Crosse Home for Children in early 1951, for which his adoptive father paid nothing [7–9].

Rather than discussing ghosts, Marianne instilled in him a deep fear of Satan, whom she referred to as **"Tantrabobous"** [11]. As a rebellious teenager who stole a car, he was sent to live with Mormon friends in Utah to avoid a correctional center — a move that later prompted Robert Wood to describe "Vernon O'Neil" as "a member of a religious sect in America" [1, 9]. He eventually married and started a family in Utah [16, 17].

## Discovery of Identity

Marianne died **18 December 1992** without telling him. On **8 September 1994**, while doing Internet genealogy research, O'Neil posted a public request for help [13]. On **22 September 1994**, English researcher Nick Rowland contacted him, revealing his true birth name and his mother's notorious connection to Borley Rectory [7, 14, 15]. Rowland famously advised him: **"Please, before you read further, would you sit down with a cup of coffee and take a deep breath"** and warned that "Your mother was one of the most amazing women of her time and also became one of the most notorious."

Until that day, O'Neil had no idea Marianne had lived at Borley Rectory, that she was the most controversial figure in the most famous haunting in England, or that she had been married to anyone before Robert O'Neil. "She never talked about anything supernormal. In the 47 years I knew her, I never heard about or witnessed any psychic phenomena of any kind."

During this same period, "heir-hunters" from Wisconsin were desperately trying to track Vincent down to prevent his mother's pension from dissolving. They succeeded only after unmasking a fanciful alias Marianne had used to register at a school district in Bangor, Wisconsin, in 1950: "Ena Howlett O'Neil" [21]. The fund had grown to roughly $3,000, netting Vincent over $2,000 after fees — which helped fund his 1997 trip to England [19].

## The 1997 Pilgrimage to England

Determined to uncover his past, Vincent planned a trip to England between **17 and 26 September 1997** [16, 22], additionally funded by a £250 contribution from *The Fortean Times* [23, 24].

During the trip, guided by researcher [[stewart-p-evans|Stewart P. Evans]], Vincent located his biological cousin Peter Frederick Long and discovered he had been born in his sister's house rather than a military transit camp [20, 21]. On **23 September 1997**, he met his biological brother Billy at the Three Tuns Inn, learning his older brother John had died in 1994, and that he had a younger brother named Philip — the exact same name Vincent had unknowingly given his own youngest son [22–24]. He also visited the grave of [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] at [[campsey-ashe|Campsey Ashe]], placing Marianne's possessions — medals, rosary, scapular, her ashes — and asking Lionel for a sign about whether Marianne had killed him. Nothing happened; his tape recording showed nothing unusual.

He met several prominent researchers, including [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]], [[ah-wesencraft|Alan Wesencraft]], and [[guy-lyon-playfair|Guy Lyon Playfair]] [22, 24–27]. Despite all this, Vincent recorded that he was ultimately disappointed by the pilgrimage: he received a somewhat cold reception from his biological relatives, his mother's spirit failed to appear, and he experienced absolutely no paranormal phenomena at Borley [12, 26, 27].

## Research and Publications

From 1994 onward, O'Neil devoted himself to researching the Borley story, corresponding with and interviewing many surviving witnesses and researchers. He established the **borleyrectory.com** website in 1994 and founded the internet-based **Borley Ghost Society**, a group whose members eventually included Uri Geller and Colin Wilson [3, 22, 28].

Books authored:
- *Borley Rectory: The Ghosts That Will Not Die* (2001) — this book is a primary source for this wiki
- *Fifteen Months in the Most Haunted House in England* — combining [[lionel-foyster|Lionel's]] and Marianne's accounts
- *The Most Haunted Woman in England* — Marianne's American life
- *Son of Borley — The Mysterious Search for My Identity*
- *Things My Mother Tried to Teach Me* — Marianne's poems and sayings

**On the wall writings:** Vincent strongly disagreed with researchers who claimed [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] faked the famous [[wall-writings|Borley wall writings]], explicitly stating: **"The more I compare the wall writings to my mother's letters, the more convinced I become she was not responsible"** [28].

**Debunking Louis Mayerling:** When George Carter (alias Louis Mayerling) published the notorious 2000 hoax book *We Faked the Ghosts of Borley Rectory*, Vincent vigorously repudiated him: **"I no longer believe that Mayerling knew my mother, and if he met her at all, it was only for a brief period of time... The time spent with my mother happened only in his imagination. Unfortunately, he has lived with these false ideas so long, he probably now believes them to be true. There is no credible evidence to support his claims"** [29, 31, 32].

## The 2004 Withdrawal

On **30 November 2004**, Vincent abruptly pulled *borleyrectory.com* from the internet and disbanded the Borley Ghost Society [31–35]. He cited the ongoing disturbances and vandalism caused by amateur ghost hunters in Borley village as his primary reason, declaring that he no longer wished to promote the case [33]. Concurrently, he shelved his project to publish [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]]'s original novel manuscript and declined to be involved in the compilation of [[brc|*The Borley Rectory Companion*]] [34].

## Assessment of Borley

Despite his mother's later claims that she faked much of the phenomena, and despite his own fruitless visits, O'Neil remained convinced of the core mystery: **"My conclusion was that something happened at Borley. How much happened is opened for debate, but something strange did happen there"** [30, 36, 37].
