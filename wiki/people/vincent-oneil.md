# Vincent O'Neil (1945–)

Born **Peter Richard** on October 9, 1945, in Ipswich, England, to Kate (born 1906; died 1980) — a woman who had worked at Helmingham School and whom his biological cousin described as "never so happy as during those two years." His biological father's identity was never confirmed.

He was adopted by [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] (then Mrs. Robert O'Neil) at approximately one month old. In 1946, Marianne emigrated to America with her new husband, American G.I. **Robert O'Neil** from Minnesota, bringing Vincent with her. He grew up in America — in North Dakota, Wisconsin, Minnesota — entirely unaware of his connection to the Borley legend.

Also known as "The Son of Borley."

## Discovery of Identity

On September 22, 1994, while doing Internet genealogy research, O'Neil mentioned a letter he had found in Marianne's dresser from Peter Underwood asking about "the Reverend Lionel Foyster and Borley Rectory." His contact Nick Rowland identified the connection and wrote: "Please, before you read further, would you sit down with a cup of coffee and take a deep breath."

Until that day, O'Neil had no idea Marianne had lived at Borley Rectory, that she was the most controversial figure in the most famous haunting in England, or that she had been married to anyone before Robert O'Neil. "She never talked about anything supernormal. In the 47 years I knew her, I never heard about or witnessed any psychic phenomena of any kind."

Marianne died December 18, 1992, without telling him.

## Research and Publications

From 1994 onward, O'Neil devoted himself to researching the Borley story. He corresponded with and interviewed many surviving witnesses and researchers; received unpublished materials from Iris Owen, Alan Wesencraft, Stewart Evans, and others. Visited England in September 1997.

Books authored:
- *Fifteen Months in the Most Haunted House in England* — combining Lionel's and Marianne's accounts
- *The Most Haunted Woman in England* — Marianne's American life
- *The Ghosts That Will Not Die* — this book; the web-published source for this wiki
- *Son of Borley — The Mysterious Search for My Identity*
- *Things My Mother Tried to Teach Me* — Marianne's poems and sayings

The web site borleyrectory.com was established by O'Neil in 1994. "When I started my research into Borley in 1994, there were only three web sites that mentioned the haunting."

## 1997 England Visit

Visited Borley Church on September 21, 1997 — attended Sunday services. Observed that the Waldegrave tomb dominated the interior and that the building was extremely cold. The wardens, Colonel and Mrs. Dorie, were cautious after recent vandalism. He was unable to play the organ or spend time alone in the building.

At [[lionel-foyster|Lionel's]] grave at Campse Ash, O'Neil placed Marianne's possessions (medals, rosary, scapular, her ashes) and asked Lionel for a sign about whether Marianne had killed him. Nothing happened. His tape recording showed nothing unusual.

Met his biological cousin **Peter Frederick Long** at his birthplace (54B Roseview Avenue, Ipswich). Met half-brother **Billy** and his wife Gwen briefly. His youngest brother Philip — who knew nothing of his existence — was not contacted.

## Assessment of Borley

O'Neil concludes: "I believe *something* happened at Borley." While nothing happened during his own visits, he finds the weight of testimony from over a century of witnesses too substantial to dismiss entirely. He accepts Owen/Mitchell's finding that most Foyster-period phenomena were fabricated or explained, while acknowledging a genuine residue.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

As detailed in our previous discussion under the misnomer "Vernon O'Neil," Vincent O'Neil (born Peter Richard Howlett) is the adopted son of Marianne Foyster. The expanded Borley sources—particularly his own 2001 autobiographical writings in *Borley Rectory: The Ghosts That Will Not Die*—offer a much deeper, highly personal look into his life, his biological roots, and his journey to uncover his mother's notorious past.

A thorough reference profile drawn from all the sources reveals the following specific facts, dates, quotes, and lesser-known details:

**Birth, Adoption, and Date Contradictions**
*   **True Identity:** He was born Peter Richard Howlett in Ipswich [1]. His biological mother, Kate Howlett of Framlingham, had an affair with an American serviceman [2]. Because her husband demanded she abort or dispose of the child as a condition of their reconciliation, Marianne took the baby in November 1945 [2, 3]. 
*   **Contradiction in Birth Date:** Most sources and chronological entries record his birth date as **9 October 1945** [1, 3, 4]. However, one specific narrative text places his birth on **8 October 1945** [2]. 
*   **Baptism and Emigration:** He was baptised as Robert Vincent O'Neil Jr. on 11 July 1946 at St Mary’s Church in Axminster, Devon, before emigrating to America with Marianne in August 1946 [5, 6]. 

**Early Life in America and "Tantrabobous"**
*   **Childhood Instability:** Growing up in America (including Hokah, Minnesota, and La Crosse, Wisconsin), Vincent's early life was marred by his adoptive father's alcoholism and violence, leading to a temporary placement in the La Crosse Home for Children in 1951 [7-9]. As a rebellious teenager who stole a car, he was sent to live with Mormon friends in Utah to avoid a correctional center [9]. 
*   **Fear of the Occult:** Vincent grew up entirely unaware of Borley Rectory [10]. Instead of discussing ghosts, Marianne instilled in him a deep fear of Satan, whom she referred to as **"Tantrabobous"** [11]. She taught him that Satan caused his adoptive father's alcoholism, and she would cross herself whenever violent or abusive language was used in the home [11].

**The 1994 Discovery**
Following Marianne's death on 18 December 1992 (her ashes were scattered in the mountains of Utah), Vincent began searching for his roots [12]. 
*   On **8 September 1994**, he posted a genealogical request on the internet [13]. 
*   On **22 September 1994**, English researcher Nick Rowland replied, famously advising him: **"Please, before you read further, would you sit down with a cup of coffee and take a deep breath"** [14]. Rowland revealed Vincent's true birth name and urged him to read Robert Wood's book, *The Widow of Borley*, warning him: **"Your mother was one of the most amazing women of her time and also became one of the most notorious. This part may not be pleasant for you"** [15].

**The 1997 Pilgrimage to England**
Determined to uncover his past, Vincent planned a trip to England between 17 and 26 September 1997 [16].
*   **"Ena Howlett O'Neil" and the Funding:** The trip was miraculously funded by his mother from beyond the grave. In 1950, Marianne had briefly taught at a school in Bangor, Wisconsin, under the fanciful alias "Ena Howlett O'Neil" [17, 18]. She had paid just over $50 into a retirement fund and forgotten it. Decades later, "heir-hunters" tracked Vincent down to prevent the pension from dissolving; the fund had grown to roughly $3,000, netting Vincent over $2,000 after fees [19]. The trip was additionally funded by a £250 contribution from *The Fortean Times* [19].
*   **Finding His Biological Family:** Guided by researcher Stewart P. Evans, Vincent located his biological cousin, Peter Frederick Long, and discovered he had been born in his sister's house, not a military transit camp [20, 21]. He deduced he had been named "Peter Richard" after this cousin [21]. 
*   **Meeting His Brother:** On 23 September 1997, Vincent met his biological brother Billy at the Three Tuns Inn [22]. He learned his older brother John had died in 1994, and that he had a younger brother named Philip (the exact same name Vincent had unknowingly given his own youngest son) [23]. Seeing photos of his biological mother, Kate, Vincent deduced he had inherited his chronic depression from her [22]. He also learned Kate's husband had died of coronary thrombosis; eerily, Vincent himself was scheduled to receive a pacemaker in 1999 [24].
*   **The SPR and Disappointment:** During the trip, he visited the Society for Psychical Research to search for a rumored report by Mollie Goldney accusing his mother of killing Lionel Foyster [25]. Ultimately, Vincent recorded that he was deeply disappointed by his pilgrimage: he received a somewhat cold reception from his biological relatives, his mother's spirit failed to appear to comfort him, and he experienced absolutely no paranormal phenomena at Borley or at Lionel's grave at Campsey Ashe [12, 26].

**Archival Work, Opinions, and Publications**
Embracing the title "The Son of Borley", Vincent became a central figure in modern Borley research [10].
*   **Publications:** He published several works including *Fifteen Months in the Most Haunted House in England* (combining Lionel and Marianne's accounts), *The Most Haunted Woman in England*, *The Ghosts That Will Not Die* (2001), and a book of his mother's poems and sayings titled *Things My Mother Tried to Teach Me* [27]. 
*   **Stance on the Wall Writings:** Vincent strongly disagreed with researchers who claimed Marianne faked the famous Borley wall writings. He explicitly stated: **"The more I compare the wall writings to my mother's letters, the more convinced I become she was not responsible"** [28].
*   **Debunking Louis Mayerling:** When George Carter (alias Louis Mayerling) published a hoax book in 2000 claiming he had helped fake the haunting, Vincent flatly rejected it, stating: **"I no longer believe that Mayerling knew my mother... The time spent with my mother happened only in his imagination... There is no credible evidence to support his claims"** [29].
*   **Final Conclusions:** Vincent maintained a balanced skepticism, concluding: **"My conclusion was that something happened at Borley. How much happened is opened for debate, but something strange did happen there"** [30].

**The 2004 Withdrawal**
On 30 November 2004, Vincent abruptly pulled *borleyrectory.com* from the internet and disbanded his Borley Ghost Society [31, 32]. He cited the continuous disturbances and vandalism caused by amateur ghost hunters in Borley village as his reason [31]. Concurrently, he shelved his project to publish Lionel Foyster's original novel manuscript and declined to be involved in the compilation of *The Borley Rectory Companion* [33].
