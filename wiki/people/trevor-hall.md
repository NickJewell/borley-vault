# Trevor Henry Hall (28 May 1910 – 8 March 1991)

Born Wakefield; died of pneumonia aged 80. Chartered surveyor, book collector, Sherlock Holmes enthusiast, and the most determined opponent of the Borley legend. Co-author of the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] (1956). Described by [[alan-wesencraft|Alan Wesencraft]] as "a supreme egotist, inflated to the breaking point with self-importance and a determination to get his name and exploits at all costs before the public."

## Background

Educated at Wakefield School; qualified as a chartered surveyor 1932; FRICS 1940. Career at V. Stanley Walker & Sons in Leeds until retirement 1982; served as Justice of the Peace from 1959; President, Huddersfield and Bradford Building Society, 1972–74; President, Leeds Library.

His interest in conjuring and debunking stems from his father, who as an antidote to a talk by Conan Doyle on Spiritualism, took the young Hall to a Maskelyne magic show. Hall became a life-long collector of conjuring books. His first publications (1945–47) were conjuring books.

### The False Cambridge Degree

Hall claimed in *Who's Who* to be a graduate of Trinity College, Cambridge. **This is false.** He never attended Trinity College or was matriculated by the University. Leeds University awarded him a PhD (1970) for a thesis on old English conjuring books, and an MA for bibliography — but his Cambridge entry is spurious. This was established by **John Randall** and **Ivan Banks** through their own research. He also falsely claimed in print (1985) to have been made Honorary Vice-President of the Magic Circle — the current (2007) President confirmed he held no such office, despite being a member for forty years.

## Role in the Borley Report

Hall met [[eric-dingwall]] at the Magic Circle in 1949; Dingwall introduced him to [[kathleen-goldney]] in November 1951; Hall joined the SPR and took on the Borley investigation. He wrote approximately two-thirds of the resulting *Borley Report* (1956).

His chapters examined:
- The Bull family and early history
- The Foyster incumbency (his main focus)
- "Later Borley" after the fire
- Background and legends

To gather material he travelled extensively: he interviewed [[mabel-smith]], [[mary-pearson]], [[henry-lawton|Canon Lawton]], [[edith-dytor|Edith Wildgoose]], and most critically, [[sidney-glanville]] — from whom he extracted the [[locked-book|Locked Book]]. He also found and cultivated [[ian-greenwood]], Marianne's estranged son, as a source.

## The Locked Book Affair

During Hall's visits to the dying Glanville at Fittleworth in 1953, he persistently pressed Glanville to give him the Locked Book. On 27 July 1953, Glanville — "not thinking clearly" and in very poor health — inscribed a dedication to Hall and handed it over. The University of London (which owned it) formally protested in a 1959 memo. Wesencraft states unambiguously that Hall *stole* it. Hall subsequently **sold the original to an American dealer for £1,200.** (Underwood confirms this sum in bps-09; earlier sources cited £1,000.) See [[locked-book]].

## Investigation of Marianne Foyster

Hall spent over five years investigating [[marianne-foyster|Marianne's]] background. He commissioned a private detective ([[robert-swanson|Robert Swanson]]) funded by [[eileen-garrett|Eileen Garrett]] of the Parapsychology Foundation to interview Marianne in America. He used [[ian-greenwood|Ian Shaw's]] bitter testimony against his mother as a primary source.

His unpublished five-volume typescript *Marianne Foyster of Borley Rectory* (~600 pages, completed 1958) accused Marianne of: manufacturing the Foyster phenomena; murdering Lionel Foyster by suffocation; and the bigamous marriage to [[henry-fisher|Henry Fisher]]. He could not publish it while Marianne was alive. She died 18 December 1992 — one year after Hall.

## Further Career and Character

Hall wrote books attacking prominent Victorian paranormal investigators: *The Strange Case of Edmund Gurney* (1964); *New Light on Old Ghosts* (1965); *The Spiritualists* (1962, accusing Crookes of an affair with Florence Cook). His 1978 *Search for Harry Price* was described by John Randall as "infused with unmitigated malice." Even Dingwall felt Hall "went too far" and broke off the friendship.

He used his position at Leeds Library to **threaten a student's degree** when she intended to publish an account of the Leeds Library haunting that contradicted his sceptical article on the subject.

A Sherlock Holmes devotee, he wrote four books on the detective and his final published work was *The Last Case of Sherlock Holmes* (1986).

## Personal Verdict

[[alan-wesencraft|Wesencraft]] (2000): "I don't know why [he] behaved so badly. He was a supreme egotist, inflated to the breaking point with self-importance."

An American conjuring collector (1993): "Trevor Hall had two reputations: one as a scholar, the other as a scoundrel."

[[nandor-fodor|Nandor Fodor]]: The Borley Report represented "never before in the history of psychical research... such a scandalous piece of writing."

## Unpublished Satanism Research

Despite his rationalist profile, Hall privately accumulated evidence of possible Satanic activity connected to Borley — correspondence from Mrs. Guy Taylor about satanic séances; Henning's investigation of witchcraft in the Stour valley. He never published this material. See [[satanism-at-borley]].

## Randall's Assessment (bps-10)

John Randall (who wrote Price's *Dictionary of National Biography* entry) profiled Hall as "an estate agent, local magistrate, amateur conjurer and writer of popular books on Sherlock Holmes and Dorothy L. Sayers" who "seems to have conceived an almost fanatical hatred for the famous ghost-hunter" despite never having met or corresponded with Price. Randall credits Hall's participation with giving the Borley Report its "uncompromisingly hostile tone."

Underwood on Hall (bps-09): quotes from *A Study in Scarlet* — "His ignorance was as remarkable as his knowledge." He never met Hall in person despite "reams of correspondence."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Trevor Henry Hall** (1910–1991) was a British chartered surveyor, amateur conjurer, writer, and one of the most obsessive, fierce, and controversial critics of the Borley Rectory hauntings. He is best known as a co-author of the highly sceptical 1956 Society for Psychical Research (SPR) publication, *The Haunting of Borley Rectory* (commonly known as *The Borley Report*), which sought to entirely dismantle the haunting's credibility and destroy the reputation of ghost hunter Harry Price [1-3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, contradictions, specific actions, and quotes regarding his involvement:

**Biographical Details and Professional Status**
*   **Early Life and Career:** Born on **May 28, 1910**, and educated at Wakefield School, Hall qualified as a chartered surveyor, becoming a Fellow of the Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors on December 9, 1940 [3, 4]. After military service during World War II, he became a senior partner at the Leeds firm V. Stanley Walker & Sons, eventually retiring in 1982 [4]. 
*   **Public Office:** He served as a Justice of the Peace in Leeds in 1959, was President of the Leeds Library, and acted as President of the Huddersfield and Bradford Building Society from 1972 to 1974 [4].
*   **Origins in Magic:** Hall's interest in the esoteric began in childhood when his father introduced him to Arthur Conan Doyle at a psychic bookshop [5]. To provide an "immediate antidote" to Doyle's Spiritualism, his father took him to a magic show by the Maskelyne brothers, which sparked Hall's lifelong passion for conjuring and his desire to debunk the paranormal [5]. He published several books on card magic in the 1940s, including *The Testament of R.W. Hull* (1945) and *Reading is Believing* (1947) [6]. 
*   **Death:** He died of pneumonia on **March 8, 1991**, aged 80 [7, 8].

**Contradictions and False Academic Claims**
Despite dedicating much of his life to exposing the alleged frauds of psychical researchers, **Hall repeatedly fabricated his own academic and professional credentials** [9, 10]. 
*   He claimed in his *Who's Who* entry to hold a Master of Arts (MA) degree from Trinity College, Cambridge, but subsequent investigations by researchers John Randall and Ivan Banks proved he had never attended the college or matriculated there [9]. 
*   He applied for a Doctor of Letters (Litt D) from Leeds University but was rejected [11]. 
*   He also falsely stated in print in 1985 that he had been made an Honorary Vice-President of the Magic Circle of London; the President of the Magic Circle later confirmed Hall never held any office with them [10]. 
*   As an American collector of conjuring books summarized in 1993: **"Trevor Hall had two reputations: one as a scholar, the other as a scoundrel"** [12].

**The Borley Report and the Campaign Against Harry Price**
Hall's involvement with Borley began after he met Dr. Eric J. Dingwall at a Magic Circle lecture in 1949 [6, 13]. Dingwall invited Hall to join the SPR and collaborate with him and Kathleen "Mollie" Goldney on a reinvestigation of Harry Price's Borley files [6]. 
*   Hall wrote four of the eight chapters of the *Borley Report*, focusing heavily on the Bull family, the Foyster incumbency, and "Later Borley" [2, 14]. 
*   Hall's hostility toward Price culminated in his 1978 solo book, *Search for Harry Price* [15, 16]. John L. Randall of the SPR described this book as being **"infused with unmitigated malice"** [16], while Alan Wesencraft, curator of the Harry Price Library, described Hall as a **"supreme egotist, inflated to the breaking point with self-importance and a determination to get his name and exploits at all costs before the public"** [12, 17]. 

**The Obsessive Pursuit of Marianne Foyster**
During the 1950s, Hall became obsessed with the private and sexual life of Marianne Foyster, treating it as the key to debunking the poltergeist phenomena reported during her time at the Rectory [18]. 
*   **The Private Investigator:** Discovering that Marianne had emigrated to the United States, Hall enlisted the help of medium Eileen Garrett's Parapsychology Foundation in New York to hire an American private investigator, A. Robert Swanson, to track her down [19, 20]. Hall specifically recommended Swanson for his **"masculine good looks,"** believing this would exploit Marianne's alleged weakness for men [20]. 
*   **The Suicide Threat:** When Hall contacted Marianne through her estranged son, Ian Shaw, she panicked, denied any involvement in faking the hauntings, and threatened suicide. Hall wrote back assuring her he meant no harm and hid the fact that he had already dispatched Swanson to track her down in Jamestown, North Dakota [21, 22]. 
*   **The Unpublished Manuscript:** Hall's digging resulted in an unpublished five-volume, 600-page manuscript titled *Marianne Foyster of Borley Rectory: A Biographical, Psychological and Psychical Investigation* [23, 24]. He was unable to publish it during Marianne's lifetime due to the libelous nature of his accusations—which included bigamy, adultery, and the claim that she murdered her husband Lionel [23, 25-27]. Mrs. C.C. Ryan Baines, a fellow researcher, condemned Hall's obsessive manuscript as **"the vilest form of dirt-digging possible"** [28]. Decades later, author Robert Wood used Hall's unpublished manuscript as the foundation for his highly critical 1992 book, *The Widow of Borley* [29, 30].

**Theft of Artifacts and Intimidation**
Hall's aggressive methods extended to the questionable acquisition of Borley's most important primary documents and the intimidation of witnesses.
*   **The Locked Book:** Hall befriended Sidney Glanville (Price's chief observer) and persuaded the ailing man to give him the original *Locked Book*—the primary ledger of Borley phenomena (inscribed to Hall on July 27, 1953) [31, 32]. Despite the University of London Library maintaining that the book was their property, Hall flatly refused to return it and subsequently sold it to an American magic dealer for £1,000 (or £1,200) [33-35]. 
*   **The Foyster Manuscript:** Using a similarly devious ruse, Hall acquired the original typescript of the Rev. Lionel Foyster’s *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House* from the Harry Price collection and sold it to an American dealer for £700 [35-37]. 
*   **Intimidating a Student:** While serving on the management committee of the Leeds Library in the 1960s, Hall published an essay debunking the library's ghost. When a female student at Leeds University later claimed to have seen the apparition and planned to write about it, Hall threatened to use his influence with the university authorities to block her degree, silencing her completely [38].

**Other Investigations and Later Writings**
Awarded the Perrott Studentship in Psychical Research at Trinity College (1954–1956) to study ESP, Hall never published his results [39]. Instead, he dedicated his writing to destroying the reputations of dead psychical researchers who could not sue for libel [15]. In *The Spiritualists* (1962), he scandalously alleged that the eminent Victorian scientist Sir William Crookes and the medium Florence Cook were secret lovers [40]. He also wrote several self-indulgent books indulging his love for Sherlock Holmes, including *The Last Case of Sherlock Holmes* (1986) [5, 41].
