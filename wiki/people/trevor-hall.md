# Trevor Henry Hall (28 May 1910 – 8 March 1991)

Born Wakefield; died of pneumonia aged 80. Chartered surveyor, book collector, Sherlock Holmes enthusiast, and the most determined opponent of the Borley legend. Co-author of the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] (1956). Described by [[ah-wesencraft|Alan Wesencraft]] as "a supreme egotist, inflated to the breaking point with self-importance and a determination to get his name and exploits at all costs before the public."

## Background

Educated at Wakefield School; qualified as a chartered surveyor 1932; FRICS 1940. Career at V. Stanley Walker & Sons in Leeds until retirement 1982; served as Justice of the Peace from 1959; President, Huddersfield and Bradford Building Society, 1972–74; President, Leeds Library.

His interest in conjuring and debunking stems from his father, who — as an antidote to a talk by Conan Doyle on Spiritualism — took the young Hall to a Maskelyne magic show, sparking a lifelong passion for conjuring and debunking [5]. His first publications (1945–47) were conjuring books, including *The Testament of R.W. Hull* (1945) and *Reading is Believing* (1947).

### The False Cambridge Degree

Hall claimed in *Who's Who* to hold a Master of Arts degree from Trinity College, Cambridge. **This is false.** Subsequent investigations by **John Randall** and **[[ivan-banks|Ivan Banks]]** proved he had never attended Trinity College or matriculated at the University [9]. Leeds University awarded him a PhD (1970) for a thesis on old English conjuring books — he applied for a Doctor of Letters but was rejected [11]. He also falsely stated in print in 1985 that he had been made Honorary Vice-President of the Magic Circle; the current (2007) President confirmed he held no such office, despite being a member for forty years [10].

As an American conjuring book collector summarised in 1993: **"Trevor Hall had two reputations: one as a scholar, the other as a scoundrel"** [12].

## Role in the Borley Report

Hall met [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] at the Magic Circle in 1949; Dingwall introduced him to [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen Goldney]] in November 1951; Hall joined the SPR and took on the Borley investigation [6, 13]. He wrote approximately four of the eight chapters of the resulting [[the-borley-report|*Borley Report*]] (1956), focusing on the Bull family and early history, the Foyster incumbency (his main focus), "Later Borley" after the fire, and background and legends [2, 14].

To gather material he travelled extensively: he interviewed [[mabel-smith|Mabel Smith]], [[mary-pearson|Mary Pearson]], [[henry-lawton|Canon Lawton]], [[edith-wildgoose|Edith Wildgoose]], and most critically, [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] — from whom he extracted the [[locked-book|Locked Book]]. He also found and cultivated [[ian-greenwood|Ian Greenwood]], Marianne's estranged son, as a source.

## The Locked Book Affair

During Hall's visits to the dying Glanville at Fittleworth in 1953, he persistently pressed Glanville to give him the Locked Book. On 27 July 1953, Glanville — "not thinking clearly" and in very poor health — inscribed a dedication to Hall and handed it over. The University of London (which owned it) formally protested in a 1959 memo. Wesencraft states unambiguously that Hall *stole* it. Hall subsequently **sold the original to an American dealer for £1,200** (Underwood confirms this sum in bps-09; earlier sources cited £1,000) [33–35]. Using a similarly devious ruse, Hall also acquired the original typescript of the Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]]'s *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House* from the Harry Price collection and sold it to an American dealer for £700 [35–37].

See [[locked-book]].

## Investigation of Marianne Foyster

Hall spent over five years investigating [[marianne-foyster|Marianne's]] background. He enlisted the help of [[eileen-garrett|Eileen Garrett]]'s Parapsychology Foundation in New York to hire an American private investigator, **[[robert-swanson|A. Robert Swanson]]**, to track her down [19, 20]. Hall specifically recommended Swanson for his "masculine good looks," believing this would exploit Marianne's alleged weakness for men [20].

When Hall contacted Marianne through her estranged son [[ian-greenwood|Ian Shaw]], she panicked, denied any involvement in faking the hauntings, and threatened suicide. Hall wrote back assuring her he meant no harm — while having already dispatched Swanson to track her down in [[jamestown-north-dakota|Jamestown, North Dakota]] [21, 22].

His unpublished five-volume, ~600-page typescript *Marianne Foyster of Borley Rectory: A Biographical, Psychological and Psychical Investigation* accused Marianne of: manufacturing the Foyster phenomena; murdering [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] by suffocation; and a bigamous marriage to [[henry-fisher|Henry Fisher]] [23–27]. He could not publish it while Marianne was alive. She died 18 December 1992 — one year after Hall. Mrs. C.C. Ryan Baines, a fellow researcher, condemned Hall's obsessive manuscript as "the vilest form of dirt-digging possible" [28]. Author [[robert-wood|Robert Wood]] later used the manuscript as the foundation for his 1992 book, *The Widow of Borley* [29, 30].

## Further Career and Character

Hall wrote books attacking prominent Victorian paranormal investigators: *The Strange Case of Edmund Gurney* (1964); *New Light on Old Ghosts* (1965); *The Spiritualists* (1962, alleging that Sir William Crookes had an affair with Florence Cook) [40]. His 1978 *Search for Harry Price* was described by John Randall as "infused with unmitigated malice" [16]. Even Dingwall felt Hall "went too far" and broke off the friendship.

He used his position at Leeds Library to **threaten a student's degree** when she intended to publish an account of the Leeds Library haunting that contradicted his sceptical article on the subject [38].

A Sherlock Holmes devotee, he wrote four books on the detective; his final published work was *The Last Case of Sherlock Holmes* (1986) [5, 41].

## Unpublished Satanism Research

Despite his rationalist profile, Hall privately accumulated evidence of possible Satanic activity connected to Borley — correspondence from Mrs. Guy Taylor about satanic séances; Henning's investigation of witchcraft in the Stour valley. He never published this material. See [[satanism-at-borley]].

## Randall's Assessment (bps-10)

John Randall (who wrote Price's *Dictionary of National Biography* entry) profiled Hall as "an estate agent, local magistrate, amateur conjurer and writer of popular books on Sherlock Holmes and Dorothy L. Sayers" who "seems to have conceived an almost fanatical hatred for the famous ghost-hunter" despite never having met or corresponded with Price. Randall credits Hall's participation with giving the Borley Report its "uncompromisingly hostile tone."

Underwood on Hall (bps-09): quotes from *A Study in Scarlet* — "His ignorance was as remarkable as his knowledge." He never met Hall in person despite "reams of correspondence."

## Personal Verdict

[[ah-wesencraft|Wesencraft]] (2000): "I don't know why [he] behaved so badly. He was a supreme egotist, inflated to the breaking point with self-importance."

[[nandor-fodor|Nandor Fodor]]: The Borley Report represented "never before in the history of psychical research... such a scandalous piece of writing."
