# Trevor Henry Hall (28 May 1910 – 8 March 1991)

Born Wakefield; died of pneumonia aged 80. Chartered surveyor, book collector, Sherlock Holmes enthusiast, and the most determined opponent of the Borley legend. Co-author of the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] (1956). Described by [[alan-wesencraft|Alan Wesencraft]] as "a supreme egotist, inflated to the breaking point with self-importance and a determination to get his name and exploits at all costs before the public."

## Background

Educated at Wakefield School; qualified as a chartered surveyor 1932; FRICS 1940. Career at V. Stanley Walker & Sons in Leeds until retirement 1982; served as Justice of the Peace from 1959; President, Huddersfield and Bradford Building Society, 1972–74; President, Leeds Library.

His interest in conjuring and debunking stems from his father, who as an antidote to a talk by Conan Doyle on Spiritualism, took the young Hall to a Maskelyne magic show. Hall became a life-long collector of conjuring books. His first publications (1945–47) were conjuring books.

### The False Cambridge Degree

Hall claimed in *Who's Who* to be a graduate of Trinity College, Cambridge. **This is false.** He never attended Trinity College or was matriculated by the University. Leeds University awarded him a PhD (1970) for a thesis on old English conjuring books, and an MA for bibliography — but his Cambridge entry is spurious. This was established by **John Randall** and **Ivan Banks** through their own research. He also falsely claimed in print (1985) to have been made Honorary Vice-President of the Magic Circle — the current (2007) President confirmed he held no such office, despite being a member for forty years.

## Role in the Borley Report

Hall met [[eric-dingwall]] at the Magic Circle in 1949; Dingwall introduced him to [[kathleen-goldney]] in November 1951; Hall joined the SPR and took on the Borley investigation. He wrote approximately two-thirds of the resulting *Borley Report* (1956).

His chapters examined:
- The Bull family and early history
- The Foyster incumbency (his main focus)
- "Later Borley" after the fire
- Background and legends

To gather material he travelled extensively: he interviewed [[mabel-smith]], [[mary-pearson]], [[henry-lawton|Canon Lawton]], [[edith-dytor|Edith Wildgoose]], and most critically, [[sidney-glanville]] — from whom he extracted the [[locked-book|Locked Book]]. He also found and cultivated [[ian-greenwood]], Marianne's estranged son, as a source.

## The Locked Book Affair

During Hall's visits to the dying Glanville at Fittleworth in 1953, he persistently pressed Glanville to give him the Locked Book. On 27 July 1953, Glanville — "not thinking clearly" and in very poor health — inscribed a dedication to Hall and handed it over. The University of London (which owned it) formally protested in a 1959 memo. Wesencraft states unambiguously that Hall *stole* it. Hall subsequently **sold the original to an American dealer for £1,200.** (Underwood confirms this sum in bps-09; earlier sources cited £1,000.) See [[locked-book]].

## Investigation of Marianne Foyster

Hall spent over five years investigating [[marianne-foyster|Marianne's]] background. He commissioned a private detective ([[robert-swanson|Robert Swanson]]) funded by [[eileen-garrett|Eileen Garrett]] of the Parapsychology Foundation to interview Marianne in America. He used [[ian-greenwood|Ian Shaw's]] bitter testimony against his mother as a primary source.

His unpublished five-volume typescript *Marianne Foyster of Borley Rectory* (~600 pages, completed 1958) accused Marianne of: manufacturing the Foyster phenomena; murdering Lionel Foyster by suffocation; and the bigamous marriage to [[henry-fisher|Henry Fisher]]. He could not publish it while Marianne was alive. She died 18 December 1992 — one year after Hall.

## Further Career and Character

Hall wrote books attacking prominent Victorian paranormal investigators: *The Strange Case of Edmund Gurney* (1964); *New Light on Old Ghosts* (1965); *The Spiritualists* (1962, accusing Crookes of an affair with Florence Cook). His 1978 *Search for Harry Price* was described by John Randall as "infused with unmitigated malice." Even Dingwall felt Hall "went too far" and broke off the friendship.

He used his position at Leeds Library to **threaten a student's degree** when she intended to publish an account of the Leeds Library haunting that contradicted his sceptical article on the subject.

A Sherlock Holmes devotee, he wrote four books on the detective and his final published work was *The Last Case of Sherlock Holmes* (1986).

## Personal Verdict

[[alan-wesencraft|Wesencraft]] (2000): "I don't know why [he] behaved so badly. He was a supreme egotist, inflated to the breaking point with self-importance."

An American conjuring collector (1993): "Trevor Hall had two reputations: one as a scholar, the other as a scoundrel."

[[nandor-fodor|Nandor Fodor]]: The Borley Report represented "never before in the history of psychical research... such a scandalous piece of writing."

## Unpublished Satanism Research

Despite his rationalist profile, Hall privately accumulated evidence of possible Satanic activity connected to Borley — correspondence from Mrs. Guy Taylor about satanic séances; Henning's investigation of witchcraft in the Stour valley. He never published this material. See [[satanism-at-borley]].

## Randall's Assessment (bps-10)

John Randall (who wrote Price's *Dictionary of National Biography* entry) profiled Hall as "an estate agent, local magistrate, amateur conjurer and writer of popular books on Sherlock Holmes and Dorothy L. Sayers" who "seems to have conceived an almost fanatical hatred for the famous ghost-hunter" despite never having met or corresponded with Price. Randall credits Hall's participation with giving the Borley Report its "uncompromisingly hostile tone."

Underwood on Hall (bps-09): quotes from *A Study in Scarlet* — "His ignorance was as remarkable as his knowledge." He never met Hall in person despite "reams of correspondence."
