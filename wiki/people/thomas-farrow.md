# Thomas Farrow

Builder and surveyor based in Bury St Edmunds who constructed [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in 1862–63 to designs by architect [[john-johnson-architect|John Johnson]].

## The Rectory Contract

Farrow consulted Johnson's plans at the old [[herringham-rectory|Herringham Rectory]] and submitted an estimate of **£3,000**, which was accepted by [[henry-bull|Rev. Henry Dawson Bull]]. With Bull and his wife moving to [[pentlow|Pentlow]] Rectory for the duration of the build, the Georgian Herringham Rectory was demolished in the summer of 1862, and Farrow's team — bricklayers, stonemasons, carpenters, scaffolders, plasterers and decorators — began work on the new building.

## Construction Details

Farrow directed his carpenters, John and Henry, to take **extra care with the carpentry** throughout the building to eradicate draughts, given the chilling easterly winds on the Borley ridge. This resulted in tightly-laid floorboards, securely tacked door panels, and window frames with no allowances for movement. As a consequence, the woodwork throughout would creak and groan whenever there was a temperature change — structural noise that many later residents and investigators would interpret as paranormal activity.

The carpenters scratched their **names on the rafters in the attics** so that future generations might acknowledge their work. When [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and [[lucie-kaye|Lucie Kaye]] explored the attics on their first visit in June 1929, they found these signatures.

## The Builders' Omen

While work on the Rectory was underway, on 5 August 1862, a group of Farrow's builders went bathing in the River Stour after a hot summer day. Among them was **[[john-whyard|John Whyard]]**, a seventeen-year-old bricklayer's labourer. Whyard drowned despite the rescue attempts of carpenter John Mathoby. At the inquest in Long Melford, the jury returned a verdict of accidental drowning. Superstitious locals muttered that the boy's death was a bad omen for the new rectory — an association that would prove remarkably durable in local legend.

## Legacy

The red brick Farrow used, along with the tight carpentry and the subsidence-prone clay foundations, defined the physical character of the Rectory. When the building was demolished in 1944, its red bricks were salvaged and much of the material was crushed into hardcore for American airbase runways. [[harry-price|Harry Price]] noted in *The End of Borley Rectory* that the walls had been laid by Farrow "just eighty-two years earlier."
