# Thomas Farrow

Builder and surveyor based in the Suffolk market town of **Bury St Edmunds** [1] who constructed [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in 1862–63 to designs by architect [[john-johnson-architect|John Johnson]].

## The Rectory Contract

At the **beginning of June 1861**, architect John Johnson put out a tender in the local press seeking builders to demolish the old Georgian rectory at Borley and construct a new one for the [[henry-bull|Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] [1]. Farrow consulted Johnson's plans at the old [[herringham-rectory|Herringham Rectory]] and submitted an estimate of **£3,000**, which was accepted [1]. With Bull and his wife moving to [[pentlow|Pentlow]] Rectory for the duration of the build, the Georgian Herringham Rectory was demolished. Under Farrow's oversight, the actual construction of the new Borley Rectory began in the **summer of 1862**, utilising a large team of **"bricklayers, stonemasons, carpenters, scaffolders, plasterers and decorators"** [1].

## Construction Details

Farrow directed his carpenters, John and Henry, to take **extra care with the carpentry** throughout the building to eradicate draughts, given the chilling easterly winds on the Borley ridge. This resulted in tightly-laid floorboards, securely tacked door panels, and window frames with no allowances for movement. As a consequence, the woodwork throughout would creak and groan whenever there was a temperature change — structural noise that many later residents and investigators would interpret as paranormal activity.

The carpenters scratched their **names on the rafters in the attics** so that future generations might acknowledge their work. When [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and [[lucie-kaye|Lucie Kaye]] explored the attics on their first visit in June 1929, they found these signatures.

## The Builders' Omen

While work on the Rectory was underway, on **5 August 1862**, a group of Farrow's builders went bathing in the River Stour after a hot summer day. Among them was **[[john-whyard|John Whyard]]**, a seventeen-year-old bricklayer's labourer. Whyard drowned despite the rescue attempts of carpenter John Mathoby. At the inquest in Long Melford, the jury returned a verdict of accidental drowning. Superstitious locals muttered that the boy's death was a bad omen for the new rectory — an association that would prove remarkably durable in local legend.

## Legacy

The red brick Farrow used, along with the tight carpentry and the subsidence-prone clay foundations, defined the physical character of the Rectory for its entire existence. [[harry-price|Harry Price]] noted in *The End of Borley Rectory* that the walls had been laid by Farrow "just eighty-two years earlier." When the building was demolished in 1944, its red bricks were salvaged and much of the material was crushed into hardcore for American airbase runways.
