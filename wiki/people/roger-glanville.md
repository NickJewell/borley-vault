# Roger Herbert Glanville

(Born 2 April 1914; died May 2008) Squadron Leader in the RAF during the Second World War; electronics specialist by profession. Only son of [[sidney-glanville]]. By 1987 he was living in Henley-on-Thames [1, 2].

## Borley Involvement

Roger Glanville signed the "Haunted House Declaration Form" on June 4, 1937, and accompanied his father on the very first observational vigil of the Price tenancy on June 19–20, 1937 [3–5]. He observed afterwards that it was "the coldest and most silent" house he had ever experienced. Over the course of the tenancy he assisted [[sidney-glanville|Glanville senior]] in research, plan-making, and interviews, occasionally bringing along RAF officer friends such as [[alan-cuthbert|Alan J. Cuthbert]] [1, 6].

Roger is the sleeping observer appearing in one of the most iconic photographs of the Price tenancy — first published in *MHH* in 1940 — showing him resting on the camp bed in the [[base-room]] (the former library), a picture taken by his father [7–9].

## Paranormal Experiences

Roger was present for several of the most notable phenomena reported during the Price tenancy:

- **The Muffled Footsteps (October 1937):** During the early hours of October 25 (or October 28, depending on the source), while [[alan-cuthbert|Alan Cuthbert]] slept soundly on a camp bed in the [[base-room]], Roger, his father, and Mark Kerr-Pearse sat in the dark on the upstairs landing. They all clearly heard **"heavy muffled footsteps walk across the hall, just under us,"** accompanied by the sounds of a body moving [10–13]. (multiple witnesses)
- **The [[swinging-blind-incident|"Swinging Blind" Incident]] (November 20, 1937):** While in the [[base-room]] with his father, Capt. H. G. Harrison, and Dr. H. F. Bellamy, the group observed the edges of a lowered window blind waving irregularly with a **"palpitating action."** Convinced there was no natural draught, they blew cigarette smoke at the gap between the blind and the window frame; the smoke did not disperse, ruling out a breeze [6, 11, 14, 15].
- **A Sensation of Being Watched:** During a table-tipping séance held in the kitchen passage on October 23, 1937, the sitters felt an ice-cold draught, and Roger specifically reported a distinct sensation of being watched [16, 17].

## Séances

Roger Glanville was present at some of the planchette and table-tipping séances, including sittings that produced the supposed identity of the nun ([[marie-lairre]]), the circumstances of the death of the maid [[kate-boreham]], and the name of [[sunex-amures]] who apparently foretold the disastrous Rectory fire.

On **March 27, 1938**, Roger and his sister [[helen-glanville]] held a planchette séance at Streatham. An entity calling itself "[[sunex-amures]]" communicated a threat to burn the Rectory down that night at nine o'clock and claimed that bones of a murdered victim would be found under the ruins. While nothing happened that specific night, the Rectory was gutted by the [[fire-1939|mysterious fire]] exactly eleven months later, on February 27, 1939 [21–26].

Roger was also a sitter during several other Streatham planchette sessions in December 1937, which produced specific instructions to search the filled-in cellar well for "writing" and "bones" [27–31]. In a blind-test session on January 8, 1938, he silently wrote questions on paper while [[helen-glanville]] and [[alan-cuthbert]] acted as sitters — an entity identifying itself as "Jeanne Waldegrave" communicated [21, 22].

## Physical Excavations at Borley

Roger actively participated in manual excavations at the Rectory site during two different periods:

- **The [[cats-cemetery|Cats' Cemetery]] (August 1937):** Finding that a portion of the [[cats-cemetery|cats' cemetery]] at the end of the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] had been mysteriously disturbed during an observational period, Roger and his father dug over the whole area on August 23–24. They uncovered a number of large bones they believed belonged to horses or oxen, plus a few they could not identify [8, 32–34].
- **The Cellar Dig (August 1943):** Following [[harry-price|Harry Price's]] highly publicised discovery of human remains in the cellar on August 17, 1943, Roger and his father travelled to Borley on August 20 to spend a full day digging in the cellars (see [[bones-excavation|Excavations]]). They returned on August 30, 1943, to assist Price and Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|Henning]] in a final official day of digging, though no further discoveries were made [8, 37–41].

## Post-Price and the Locked Book Controversy

After the war Roger lived at Henley-on-Thames. [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] visited [[sidney-glanville]] after Price's death, and Glanville mentioned on several occasions that Kerr-Pearse had been to visit or that they had met — Roger was presumably present on some occasions.

Following his father's death, the ownership of [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville's]] meticulously compiled [[locked-book|Locked Book of Private Information]] became a subject of bitter dispute between the University of London and skeptical author [[trevor-hall]]. While Dr. Pafford at the University of London asserted that the book belonged to the Harry Price Library and that Glanville had merely borrowed it, Roger firmly contradicted this. He maintained that his father's gift of the volume to [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] was intentionally arranged during a meeting at the Glanville home, and that ownership rested with his father [2, 42]. Like [[sidney-glanville|his father]], Roger was devastated by [[trevor-hall|Hall's]] treatment of Sidney and the handling of the controversy.
