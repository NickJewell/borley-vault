# Roger Herbert Glanville

(Born 2 April 1914; died May 2008) Squadron Leader in the RAF during the Second World War; electronics specialist by profession. Only son of [[sidney-glanville]].

## Borley Involvement

Roger Glanville spent many hours at [[borley-rectory]] with his father during the [[price-tenancy]], sometimes accompanied by officer friends from the RAF (such as Alan J. Cuthbert). He assisted his father in research, plan-making, interviews, etc. He accompanied Glanville senior on the first observation period on 19 June 1937, observing afterwards that it was "the coldest and most silent" house he had ever experienced.

## Séances

Roger Glanville was present at some of the planchette and table-tipping séances (see [[seance-data]]), including sittings that produced the supposed identity of the nun ([[marie-lairre]]), the circumstances of the death of the maid [[kate-boreham]], and the name of [[sunex-amures]] who apparently foretold the disastrous Rectory fire.

Finding that a portion of the [[cats-cemetery|cats' cemetery]] had been disturbed during an observational period in August 1937, Roger and his father dug over the whole area and discovered large bones that had never belonged to any cat.

Roger Glanville also assisted in the cellar digging at Borley in August 1943 (see [[bones-excavation|Excavations]]). He is the sleeping observer appearing in one of the most iconic photographs of the Price tenancy — first published in *MHH* in 1940 — showing him resting on the camp bed in the [[base-room]].

## Post-Price

After the war, Roger lived at Henley-on-Thames. Peter Underwood visited Sidney Glanville after Price's death and Glanville mentioned on several occasions that Kerr-Pearse had been to visit him or that they had met — Roger was presumably present on some occasions. Like his father, Roger was devastated by [[trevor-hall]]'s treatment of Sidney and the handling of the [[locked-book]].

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Roger Herbert Glanville** (born April 2, 1914; died May 2008) was a dedicated official observer during Harry Price’s investigation of Borley Rectory and the only son of Price's chief investigator, Sidney H. Glanville [1]. An electronics specialist by profession, he also served as a Squadron Leader in the Royal Air Force (RAF) during the Second World War [1]. By 1987, he was recorded as living in Henley-on-Thames [1, 2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, dates, and lesser-known details about his involvement in the case:

**Early Involvement and The Price Tenancy (1937–1938)**
*   **First Tour of Duty:** Roger signed the "Haunted House Declaration Form" on June 4, 1937, and accompanied his father on the very first observational vigil of the Price tenancy on June 19–20, 1937 [3-5]. Following this initial stay, Roger remarked that the Rectory was **"the coldest and most silent"** house he had ever experienced [1].
*   **Observation Companions:** Over the course of the tenancy, Roger spent many hours at Borley assisting his father with research, interviews, and plan-making, occasionally bringing along RAF officer friends, such as Alan J. Cuthbert [1, 6].
*   **The "Sleeping Observer" Photograph:** Roger is the actual subject of the iconic "sleeping observer" photograph taken by his father in the Rectory's Base Room (the former library), an image that was prominently published in Harry Price’s *The Most Haunted House in England* [7-9]. 

**Key Paranormal Experiences**
Roger was present for several of the most notable phenomena reported during the Price tenancy:
*   **The Muffled Footsteps (October 1937):** During the early hours of October 25 (or October 28 depending on the specific source timeline), while Alan Cuthbert slept soundly on a camp bed in the Base Room, Roger, his father, and Mark Kerr-Pearse sat in the dark on the upstairs landing. They all clearly heard **"heavy muffled footsteps walk across the hall, just under us,"** accompanied by the sounds of a body moving [10-13]. 
*   **The "Swinging Blind" Incident (November 20, 1937):** While in the Base Room with his father, Capt. H. G. Harrison, and Dr. H. F. Bellamy, the group observed the edges of a lowered window blind waving irregularly with a **"palpitating action."** Convinced there was no natural draught, they blew cigarette smoke at the gap between the blind and the window frame; the smoke did not disperse, ruling out a breeze [6, 11, 14, 15].
*   **A Sensation of Being Watched:** During a table-tipping séance held in the kitchen passage on October 23, 1937, the sitters felt an ice-cold draught, and Roger specifically reported a distinct sensation of being watched [16, 17].

**Séance and Planchette Data**
Roger played a major role in obtaining some of the most famous séance "data" of the Borley haunting:
*   He participated in multiple table-tipping and planchette sessions at the Rectory alongside his father, Mark Kerr-Pearse, and Alan Cuthbert [8, 18-20].
*   **The "Sunex Amures" Prophecy:** On March 27, 1938, at the Glanville family home in Streatham, Roger and his sister Helen operated a planchette board. An entity calling itself "Sunex Amures" communicated a threat to **burn the Rectory down that night at nine o'clock** and claimed that bones of a murdered victim would be found under the ruins. While nothing happened that specific night, the Rectory was gutted by a mysterious fire exactly eleven months later, on February 27, 1939 [21-26].
*   Roger was also a sitter during several other Streatham planchette sessions in December 1937, which produced specific instructions to search the filled-in cellar well for "writing" and "bones" [27-31].

**Physical Excavations at Borley**
Roger actively participated in manual excavations at the Rectory site during two different periods:
*   **The Cats' Cemetery (August 1937):** After Sidney Glanville discovered that a section of the cats' cemetery at the end of the Nun's Walk had been mysteriously disturbed by an unknown intruder, Roger and his father thoroughly re-dug the area on August 23–24. They uncovered a number of large bones they believed belonged to horses or oxen, plus a few they could not identify [8, 32-34].
*   **The Cellar Dig (August 1943):** Following Harry Price’s highly publicized discovery of human remains in the cellar on August 17, 1943, Roger and his father traveled to Borley on August 20 to spend a full day digging in the cellars [35, 36]. They returned on August 30, 1943, to assist Price and the Rev. Henning in a final official day of digging, though no further discoveries were made [8, 37-41]. 

**Contradiction and The "Locked Book" Controversy**
Following his father's death, the ownership of Sidney Glanville's meticulously compiled *Locked Book of Private Information* became a subject of bitter dispute between the University of London and skeptical author Trevor H. Hall. While Dr. Pafford at the University of London asserted that the book belonged to the Harry Price Library and that Glanville had merely borrowed it, Roger Glanville contradicted this. Roger firmly believed that the ownership of the *Locked Book* rested with his father and maintained that his father's gift of the volume to Trevor Hall was intentionally arranged during a meeting at the Glanville home [2, 42].
