# John Clucas Cannell

(Often referred to as "Jack" Cannell) A London journalist on the staff of the *Daily Sketch*, popularly known in Fleet Street as **"the spook man"** due to his frequent coverage of ghost stories, including the [[battersea-poltergeist-1927|Battersea poltergeist]] [2]. An amateur conjuror and Vice-President of the Magician's Club; author of *The Secrets of Houdini* (1931) [1, 2]. At the time of his Borley visit he was approximately 33 years old [1].

## Publications and Articles

Cannell authored *When Fleet Street Calls* (published by Jarrold, 1932), which featured a lengthy chapter titled "The Haunted Rectory" detailing a first-hand visit to the house [1, 3]. Although he did not explicitly name Borley Rectory, identification was unmistakable — he referenced "a former rector, the Revd Harry Bull" and [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s visits [1]. His description of acknowledged events and the reactions of the participants makes this a firsthand and almost contemporary account, predating the publication of [[mhh-pref|MHH]] by eight years.

Cannell was also responsible for writing a sensational article about Borley titled **"Rectory Tale of Terror!"** which appeared in the *Daily Sketch* on **6 February 1932**, likely using information supplied to him by Harry Price [1, 4]. [[guy-lestrange|Guy l'Estrange]] read Cannell's London press accounts and used them as the reason for requesting an invitation to visit Borley in January 1932.

## Visit to the Rectory

Cannell paid a visit to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] late one evening during the [[foyster-incumbency|Foyster incumbency]], arriving unannounced by taxi [2, 3]. He came equipped with a remarkable array of paraphernalia intended to either assess the ghosts or expose hoaxers: **a wooden brick to wedge doors open, special ink to apply to door handles, screws to secure the windows, tin tacks to scatter on the floor to defeat intruders, and a small pistol secreted in his pocket** [2].

The door was opened by [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]], who was initially reluctant to discuss disturbances [2, 3]. The Foysters had seen his articles and insisted they wanted no more publicity; [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] pointed out that the *Daily Sketch* had even published a photograph of the house [5]. Eventually invited into the Drawing Room, Cannell noted a stark contrast between the couple: the aged Rector was tall, solemn, scholarly, and stood "strangely silent" during the visit, while Marianne struck him as an **"alert, self-confident and well-balanced young woman"** — "lively and shrewd" [5, 6, 7].

Marianne brought up the significant age difference between herself and her husband, telling Cannell directly: **"There are people who say that I am hysterical and that I have been doing these things."** When Cannell asked if it was suggested she had been **"giving herself black eyes and throwing bricks about,"** she shrugged resignedly, pointing out that the manifestations had been occurring in the Rectory long before she and her husband moved in [6, 8]. Cannell found this quite compelling; he noted that Marianne's replies were so "vigorous" and "quick-witted" that the silent Rector was twice forced to mildly rebuke his wife [8].

Cannell pleaded to stay the night but Lionel Foyster shook his head and refused, stating in a low voice: **"Things are quiet in this house now, and I believe they will remain so"** [7]. Cannell lingered as long as politely possible before finally taking a taxi to Long Melford [8].

## Conclusions

Reflecting on the Borley phenomena, Cannell theorised that the events might be caused by structural defects, a brilliant hoaxer, or vibrations resonating from past tragedy [7]. Ultimately he admitted he could not decide on the true origin of the disturbances, but summarised his position:

> **"One fact does emerge with certainty – that people of the highest reputation and integrity have testified that these things have happened in the Rectory. Whatever the cause of them may be, the happenings at the Rectory have provided what I consider to be the world's best ghost story."** [7, 9]

## Significance

The *Daily Sketch* article of 6 February 1932 is one of the earliest detailed press accounts of the Foyster-period phenomena, published while the Foysters were still in residence. Its existence, and the fact that *When Fleet Street Calls* appeared in 1932 with a chapter on Borley, forms part of the newspaper coverage that [[andrew-clarke|Andrew Clarke]] later identified as crucial to understanding how the Borley legend built on itself.
