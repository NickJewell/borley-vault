# John Clucas Cannell

A London journalist, Vice-President of the Magician's Club and author of *The Secrets of Houdini* (1931). On the staff of the *Daily Sketch*.

## Borley Involvement

Cannell knew [[harry-price]] and wrote a sensationalist article on Borley entitled "Rectory Tale of Terror" (probably from information supplied by Price), published in February 1932 during the period that the Foysters were at the Rectory experiencing much [[foyster-phenomena|phenomena]].

He also wrote *When Fleet Street Calls* (1932), which includes a chapter "The Haunted Rectory." Although Borley is not named, identification is clear: Cannell mentions "a former rector, the Revd Harry Bull" and details of [[harry-price]]'s visits. His description of acknowledged events and the reactions of the participants makes this a firsthand and almost contemporary account — importantly, it predates the publication of [[mhh|MHH]] by eight years.

## Visit to the Rectory

Cannell visited [[borley-rectory]] late one night during the [[foyster-incumbency]]. The door was opened by [[marianne-foyster]] who was initially reluctant to discuss disturbances but eventually invited Cannell into the Drawing Room. He noticed a strong contrast between the rector and his wife: Foyster "tall and solemn and of a scholarly appearance" while Marianne was "lively and shrewd."

Marianne told Cannell directly that she knew people were suggesting she was responsible for the phenomena: "There are people who say that I am hysterical and that I have been doing these things." When Cannell asked if it was suggested she had been "giving herself black eyes and throwing bricks about," she shrugged resignedly. Cannell found this quite absurd given the manifestations had been reported for years before she arrived. He offered to stay the night but the rector declined.

Summing up, Cannell admitted he could not decide on the origin of the disturbances, but stated: "One fact does emerge with certainty — that people of the highest reputation and integrity have testified that these things have happened in the Rectory."

## Significance

The *Daily Sketch* article of February 1932 (described by the BRC as "Rectory Tale of Terror") is one of the earliest detailed press accounts of the Foyster-period phenomena, published while the Foysters were still in residence. Its existence, and the fact that *When Fleet Street Calls* appeared in 1932 with a chapter on Borley, forms part of the [[newspaper-articles|newspaper coverage]] that [[andrew-clarke]] later identified as crucial to understanding how the Borley legend built on itself.

[[guy-lestrange]] read Cannell's London press accounts and used them as the reason for requesting an invitation to visit Borley in January 1932.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**John Clucas Cannell** (often referred to as "Jack" Cannell) was a 33-year-old London journalist who worked on the staff of the *Daily Sketch* [1, 2]. He was popularly known in Fleet Street as "the spook man" due to his frequent coverage of ghost stories, including the Battersea poltergeist [2]. In addition to his journalism, he was an amateur conjuror and served as Vice-President of the Magician’s Club [1, 2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following specific details regarding his involvement in the Borley Rectory case:

**Publications and Articles**
Cannell authored the book *The Secrets of Houdini* (1931) and *When Fleet Street Calls* (published by Jarrold in 1932) [1, 3]. The latter book featured a lengthy chapter titled "The Haunted Rectory," which detailed a first-hand visit to the house [1, 3]. Although he did not explicitly name Borley Rectory in the book, the identity of the house was unmistakable because he referenced "a former rector, the Revd Harry Bull" and Harry Price's visits [1]. 

Cannell was also responsible for writing a sensational article about Borley titled **"Rectory Tale of Terror!"** which appeared in the *Daily Sketch* on February 6, 1932, likely using information supplied to him by Harry Price [1, 4]. 

**The Late-Night Visit and the "Ghost-Hunting Kit"**
Cannell paid a visit to Borley Rectory late one evening during the incumbency of the Rev. Lionel Foyster and his wife, Marianne [2, 3]. Arriving unannounced by taxi, he came equipped with a highly unusual array of paraphernalia intended to either assess the ghosts or expose hoaxers [2]. **His hidden kit included a wooden brick to wedge doors open, special ink to apply to door handles, screws to secure the windows, tin tacks to scatter on the floor to defeat intruders, and a small pistol secreted in his pocket** [2].

**Interviewing the Foysters**
Marianne Foyster answered the door to the journalist [2, 3]. Initially, the Foysters were reluctant to speak with him, as they had seen his articles and insisted they wanted no more publicity [3, 5]. Lionel Foyster pointed out that Cannell's employer, the *Daily Sketch*, had even published a photograph of the house [5]. 

Eventually invited into the Drawing Room, Cannell noted a stark contrast between the couple [3, 5, 6]. He observed that the aged Rector was tall, solemn, scholarly, and stood "strangely silent" during the visit [5, 6]. By contrast, he found Marianne to be "lively and shrewd" [5, 6]. Rejecting rumors that she was highly strung, Cannell concluded she was an **"alert, self-confident and well-balanced young woman"** [7].

While smoking a cigarette by the fire, Marianne brought up the significant age difference between herself and her husband, noting: **"There are people who say that I am hysterical and that I have been doing these things"** [6]. Cannell directly asked her: **"Is it suggested that you have been giving yourself black eyes and throwing bricks about?"** [8]. Marianne resignedly shrugged and replied that such accusations were ridiculous, given that the manifestations had been occurring in the Rectory long before she and her husband ever moved in [8].

During their lively discussion, Cannell noted that the exchange of words was "sharp enough" but good-humored [8]. However, Marianne's replies were so "vigorous" and "quick-witted" that the silent Rector was forced to mildly rebuke his wife on two occasions [8].

**Rejection and Final Conclusions**
Cannell pleaded to be allowed to stay the night to investigate, pointing out his experience and knowledge of such matters, but Lionel Foyster shook his head and refused permission [7, 8]. The Rector stated in a low voice: **"Things are quiet in this house now, and I believe they will remain so"** [7]. Cannell lingered as long as politely possible, hoping to witness some ghostly activity, before finally taking a taxi to Long Melford [8]. 

Reflecting on the Borley phenomena, Cannell theorized that the events might be caused by structural defects, a brilliant hoaxer, or vibrations resonating from past tragedy [7]. Ultimately, he admitted he could not decide on the true origin of the disturbances, but summarized the case with this definitive quote:
> **"One fact does emerge with certainty – that people of the highest reputation and integrity have testified that these things have happened in the Rectory. Whatever the cause of them may be, the happenings at the Rectory have provided what I consider to be the world’s best ghost story."** [7, 9].
