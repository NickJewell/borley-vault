# Guy Lyon Playfair

SPR member and co-investigator of the Enfield Poltergeist case (1977–78) with Maurice Grosse. Banks solicited his written views on the Borley Rectory case and Harry Price for Chapter 24 of *The Enigma of Borley Rectory*.

## Views on Borley

Playfair's written contribution to Banks's book is notable for its honest ambivalence:

> "The Borley saga is a classic instance of the great difficulty in researching any field of the so-called paranormal. Vigorously defended by Harry Price, equally vigorously attacked by Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall, what is the jury member to decide?"

He concludes with a qualified endorsement: **"Borley was, I think, haunted. I only wish I could be sure."**

His personal assessment of Price is mixed:

> "I am perhaps slightly biased against Price, having often discussed him with the late Dr. Dingwall, who knew him well. There can be no doubt that Price was (a) a brilliant writer, whose talent I envy, and (b) far more interested in self-promotion than in serious psychical research. As a member of the S.P.R., I feel that Price let the side down."

At the same time, Playfair draws an explicit parallel between his own experience on the Enfield case and Price's position at Borley:

> "When Maurice Grosse and I spent more than a year in 1977–78 on the Enfield poltergeist case, we felt we were making history by being the first psychical researchers to get in on the start of a case of this kind and stick with it until the finish, as we did. We did not expect Nobel Prizes, but we thought at least that our work would be received with rather more enthusiasm than it was... We both learned that the sure way to become very unpopular in psychic research circles is to present uncompromisingly positive evidence for the subject we are supposed to be researching."

## Significance

Playfair's endorsement — however qualified — carries weight because he brought both relevant first-hand investigative experience and a degree of scepticism about Price's character. He neither dismisses Borley nor fully vindicates Price, which Banks presents as a credible middle position.

Banks uses Playfair's contribution to illustrate that the dismissiveness of the psychic research "establishment" towards well-documented cases has changed little since Price's era.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Guy Lyon Playfair** is identified in the Borley sources as a paranormal author, a member of the Society for Psychical Research (SPR), and notably the co-author of a book on the famous Enfield Poltergeist case [1-3]. He was also listed as a notable member of the internet-based **Borley Ghost Society**, an organization founded by Marianne Foyster's adopted son, Vincent O'Neil [4]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following facts, events, and specific quotes regarding his involvement with the Borley Rectory case:

**His Views on the Borley Haunting and Harry Price**
Playfair contributed a formal précis of his thoughts on the case to Ivan Banks's book, *The Enigma of Borley Rectory* [1, 2]. He viewed the Rectory as a perfect example of the inherent challenges of psychical research, stating: **"The Borley saga is a classic instance of the great difficulty in researching any field of the so-called paranormal"** [2]. 

Regarding the haunting itself, Playfair expressed a cautious belief in the phenomena, concluding: **"Borley was, I think, haunted. I only wish I could be sure"** [5]. He noted that he felt **"fairly certain that odd things went on in Borley Rectory,"** but firmly believed that **"neither Price nor his detractors gave a wholly truthful account of the case; and that the true facts will never be known"** [6]. 

Regarding Harry Price, Playfair frankly admitted to a personal prejudice, writing that he was **"perhaps slightly biased against Price"** due to having frequently discussed him with the late Dr. Eric Dingwall, who was one of Price's harshest critics and co-author of the highly skeptical 1956 *Borley Report* [6]. While Playfair praised Price as a **"brilliant writer, whose talent I envy,"** he ultimately criticized him for being **"far more interested in self-promotion than in serious psychical research"** [6]. Speaking as a fellow SPR member, Playfair felt that **"Price let the side down"** [6]. 

**Interactions with Vincent O'Neil (September 1997)**
Playfair entered the Borley narrative personally between **September 17 and 26, 1997**, during Vincent O'Neil's extended research trip to England [7]. When veteran Borley investigator Peter Underwood had to unexpectedly cancel his appearance on a ghost tour that O'Neil was attending, Playfair and Jack the Ripper historian Stewart Evans generously stepped in to accompany O'Neil and improve the trip [8]. 

Playfair later accepted an invitation to dine with O'Neil on Fleet Street, where the two men extensively discussed the paranormal, with Borley dominating the conversation [3]. Furthermore, Playfair kindly facilitated a valuable connection for O'Neil by introducing him to Montague Keen, a fellow paranormal investigator who happened to live just a few miles from Borley [9].
