# Arthur S. Medcraft

Electrical engineer of 102 Ashgrove Road, Goodmayes, Essex. A self-described non-psychic visitor who made multiple journeys to the Borley ruins in 1943, reporting two unusual experiences on one specific visit.

## Experiences (Early July 1943)

Medcraft wrote to [[harry-price|Price]] on **August 27, 1943**, describing visits "many times" to the Rectory site, with all but one occasion being entirely uneventful [1]. The one exceptional visit was in early July 1943, on a day described as having "brilliant sunshine" [2]:

**The invisible walker:** Approaching the [[borley-rectory|Rectory]] on foot from Sudbury at approximately 12:15 p.m., Medcraft became aware of footsteps following him when he was within two hundred yards of the Rectory [2]. He turned and saw nothing. He then gripped his case firmly to eliminate the possibility of it knocking against his leg, and walked on. The footsteps resumed, though appearing to move slower than his own pace [2]. He turned his head suddenly while continuing to walk — the footsteps ceased, and the road was empty, leaving him "dogged by an invisible walker" [2, 3]. Price later used Medcraft's account as "striking confirmation" of similar phantom footsteps reported earlier by [[gerald-bull|Gerald Bull]] [1].

**Door slam:** About an hour later, while eating sandwiches on the lawn near the summer-house with the Rectory in view, he heard a tremendous bang of a door, with the click of the lock audible at that distance [2, 4]. He investigated immediately but found everything still and silent. There was no wind, and whatever had produced the sound would have had to overcome what he estimated was the inability of the remaining doors to close properly at all, given the fire damage [2]. Price noted that Medcraft's description suggested a door swinging easily on its hinges and clicking shut — particularly uncanny given that many of the doors in the burnt-out ruin were off their hinges and could neither open nor close [5].

**Aroma:** Near the French windows, Medcraft noticed "a strong aroma of something — quite pleasant — which I could not account for" [2]. There were no flowers or plants in the vicinity capable of producing it. Price compared this to other pleasant odours frequently reported at Borley, such as the smell of lavender or incense [6].

Medcraft is specific about what he did *not* experience: no depression, no fear, "only restfulness and stillness."

## Uneventful Return Visits

Medcraft returned to Borley later that same month. On the morning of **July 28, 1943** (the traditional date the phantom nun was said to appear) he spent several hours in the Rectory garden but experienced no phenomena whatsoever [3, 7]. Price highlighted the contrast between Medcraft's quiet daytime visit and the highly eventful night vigil conducted on that exact same date by a group of Polish officers, who reported significant paranormal activity including thrown stones and a moving shadow [3, 8]. He paid another uneventful daytime visit on **August 22, 1943** [9].

> **Contradiction:** In *The End of Borley Rectory*, Price dates Medcraft's letter as **August 27, 1943** [1]. However, in *The Ghosts of Borley*, the authors state that Medcraft wrote his report to Price on **27 April 1943** regarding a visit he paid "early one July" [2]. The April date is a logical impossibility if the visit took place in July 1943, highlighting a textual error in the later source [1, 2].

## Significance

Medcraft's calm, matter-of-fact tone and electrical engineering background make him a reliable observer. His subsequent visits on July 28 and August 22, 1943 — both entirely negative — give his one positive visit additional weight by comparison.
