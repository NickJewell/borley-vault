# Arthur S. Medcraft

Electrical engineer of 102 Ashgrove Road, Goodmayes, Essex. A self-described non-psychic visitor who made multiple journeys to the Borley ruins in 1943, reporting two unusual experiences on one specific visit.

## Experiences (July 1943)

Medcraft wrote to Price on August 27, 1943, describing visits "many times" to the Rectory site, with all but one occasion being entirely uneventful. The one exceptional visit was in early July 1943:

**Phantom footsteps on the road:** Approaching the Rectory on foot from Sudbury at approximately 12:15 p.m. on a warm, sunny day, Medcraft became aware of footsteps following him when he was within two hundred yards of the Rectory. He turned and saw nothing. He then gripped his case firmly to eliminate the possibility of it knocking against his leg, and walked on. The footsteps resumed. He turned his head suddenly while continuing to walk — the footsteps ceased, and the road was empty. He reached the Rectory drive without further incident.

**Door slam:** About an hour later, while eating sandwiches on the lawn near the summer-house with the Rectory in view, he heard a tremendous bang of a door, with the click of the lock audible at that distance. He investigated immediately but found everything still and silent. He noted there was no wind, and that whatever had produced the sound would have had to overcome what he estimated was the inability of the remaining doors to close properly at all, given the fire damage.

**Aroma:** Near the French windows, Medcraft noticed "a strong aroma of something — quite pleasant — which I could not account for."

## Significance

Medcraft is specific about what he did *not* experience: no depression, no fear, "only restfulness and stillness." His calm, matter-of-fact tone and electrical engineering background make him a reliable observer. His subsequent visits on July 28 and August 22, 1943 — both entirely negative — give his one positive visit additional weight by comparison.

The coincidence that his July 28 visit was uneventful while [[lt-nawrocki|Lt. Nawrocki]]'s group was at the Rectory the same night with many experiences is noted by Price without explanation.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Arthur S. Medcraft** was an electrical engineer who resided at 102 Ashgrove Road, Goodmayes, Essex [1]. Having cultivated a psychic interest in Borley Rectory, he visited the site many times over the course of more than a year [1]. Despite his frequent visits, he only experienced unusual happenings on one specific occasion, which he subsequently reported in a letter to the psychical researcher Harry Price, whom he had never personally met [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources includes the following details about his experiences:

**The Eventful Visit of Early July 1943**
Medcraft’s sole paranormal encounter occurred during a visit in early July 1943, on a day described as having "brilliant sunshine" [2]. His experience consisted of three distinct and classic Borley phenomena:
*   **The Invisible Walker:** While walking within 200 yards of the Rectory, Medcraft distinctly heard footsteps following him [2]. When he stopped and turned around, the sounds ceased and he found the road to be completely empty [2]. As he resumed his walk toward the Rectory, the footsteps began following him again, though this time they appeared to be moving slower than his own pace [2]. He turned his head suddenly, but the footsteps stopped once more, leaving him "dogged by an invisible walker" on an empty road [2, 3]. Harry Price later used Medcraft's account as "striking confirmation" of similar phantom footsteps reported earlier by Gerald Bull [1].
*   **The Slamming Door:** Medcraft proceeded to the large summer-house, sitting on the lawn to eat sandwiches while facing the ruined Rectory [2, 4]. Everything was perfectly quiet until about half an hour later when he heard a "tremendous bang" sounding exactly as if a door had slammed [2, 4]. He investigated immediately, but found everything still and no wind [2]. Price noted that Medcraft's description suggested a door swinging easily on its hinges and clicking shut, which was particularly uncanny given that many of the doors in the burnt-out ruin were off their hinges and could neither open nor close [5].
*   **The Phantom Perfume:** While passing the French windows of the building, Medcraft noticed a peculiar, strong, but quite pleasant smell [2]. He could not account for this aroma, as there were no flowers or plants in the vicinity capable of producing it [2]. Price compared this to other pleasant odours frequently reported at Borley, such as the smell of lavender or incense [6].

**Uneventful Return Visits**
Medcraft returned to Borley later that same month. On the morning of **July 28, 1943** (the traditional date the phantom nun was said to appear), he spent several hours in the Rectory garden but experienced no phenomena whatsoever [3, 7]. Price highlighted the contrast between Medcraft's quiet daytime visit and the highly eventful night vigil conducted on that exact same date by a group of Polish officers, who reported significant paranormal activity including thrown stones and a moving shadow [3, 8]. Medcraft paid another uneventful daytime visit to the Rectory site on **August 22, 1943** [9]. 

**Source Contradiction: The Date of Correspondence**
The Borley texts contain a specific chronological contradiction regarding the letter Medcraft sent to Harry Price documenting his experiences. 
*   In his book *The End of Borley Rectory*, Harry Price quotes Medcraft's letter directly and dates it **August 27, 1943** [1]. 
*   However, in the later book *The Ghosts of Borley*, the authors state that Medcraft wrote his report to Price on **27 April 1943** regarding a visit he paid "early one July" [2]. (This April date is a logical impossibility if the visit took place in July 1943, highlighting a textual error or discrepancy in the later source) [1, 2].
