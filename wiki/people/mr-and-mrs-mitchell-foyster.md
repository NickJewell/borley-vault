# Mr and Mrs Mitchell (Foyster Period)

Mr and Mrs Mitchell were the unnamed occupants of the rectory cottage adjoining [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] at the start of the [[foyster-phenomena|Foyster incumbency]] (from October 1930). They were later replaced by [[francois-darles|François d'Arles]] as cottage residents.

## Significance

The Mitchells' presence is relevant to several alleged phenomena during the early Foyster period:

1. **The "odd smell of cooking"** — [[lionel-foyster|Mr Foyster]] described noticing an inexplicable smell of cooking at 11–12 p.m. in his *Diary of Occurrences*. The SPR investigators note that he "does not appear to have connected this somewhat homely 'phenomenon' with the Mitchells, 'the people from the cottage', who may quite conceivably have prepared a meal at a late hour on occasions." The kitchen passage where the smell was most apparent was closest to the cottage.

2. **The acoustic effect** — The SPR report demonstrates throughout that the narrow enclosed courtyard between the rectory and cottage had an "extraordinary acoustic effect" by which sounds in the cottage and courtyard — including voices, footsteps, and the late-night operation of the hand-pump — were heard inside the rectory as if they were occurring within the building. Canon Lawton, who occupied the rectory as locum in 1933, confirmed this: when the cottage was without a tenant during his month's stay, none of the characteristic thumps, footsteps, and voices were heard.

The Mitchells were, in the SPR's analysis, an entirely normal household whose domestic activities accidentally contributed to Borley's poltergeist reputation during the most active period of the alleged haunting.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mr. and Mrs. Mitchell** were the tenants of the Rectory Cottage during the early part of the Foyster incumbency, residing there before the arrival of the controversial lodger Frank Pearless [1], [2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following biographical details, specific events, and contradictions regarding their time at Borley:

**Biographical Details and Role**
*   **Family Background:** Mr. Mitchell is explicitly described in the texts as a **Yorkshireman** [3]. He and his wife lived in the cottage with their **seventeen-year-old son**, whose name is contradictorily recorded in the sources as both **Dick** and **Jack** [4], [3], [5].
*   **Domestic Duties:** The family frequently assisted the Foysters with domestic chores, such as helping them unpack when they first moved in [3]. Their son was heavily relied upon to pump water from the well and perform various odd jobs around the Rectory [4], [5], [6]. 

**Immunity to Phenomena**
The Rev. Lionel Foyster explicitly noted in his writings that **paranormal manifestations seldom occurred when the Mitchells were inside the Rectory** [5]. Foyster theorized this was because the "Goblins" had no spite against the family and wished to keep their activities secret, or alternatively, because the presence of "non-psychic people" actively hindered the manifestations [5]. 

**Key Events and Observations**
Despite their apparent immunity to poltergeist activity, the Mitchells were involved in several notable incidents and misinterpretations:
*   **The Landing Figure:** Mrs. Mitchell inadvertently contributed to the Borley ghost lore when she informed Marianne Foyster that she had seen the Rector standing on the upstairs landing in his dressing gown, which was visible from the courtyard at night [7]. When Marianne told her she was mistaken, as Lionel had not left his room, Mrs. Mitchell grew **"very indignant and said she was quite sure she saw me there in my dressing gown"** [7]. While Marianne reportedly claimed this was an apparition of the late Harry Bull, commentators suggest it was most likely just Lionel [1].
*   **Acoustic Misunderstandings:** Mrs. Mitchell also experienced the Rectory's deceptive acoustics. While sitting in the sewing room with a supposedly unwell Marianne, Mrs. Mitchell remarked, **"Ee... how plainly this house does sound; how plainly you can hear Mr. Foyster walking about upstairs,"** despite the fact that Lionel was actually sitting quietly downstairs [8], [9].
*   **Stone-Throwing at Dick/Jack:** On two occasions, stones were thrown at the Mitchells' teenage son while he was working at the Rectory [5]. The boy initially assumed Marianne had thrown the first stone at him, and blamed three-year-old Adelaide for a stone thrown the previous Monday [5]. However, Lionel Foyster believed the entity was actually targeting himself; since the boy was about the same height as the Rector, Foyster assumed the poltergeist had mistaken the teenager for him [4], [5].
*   **The Kitchen Chaos:** The son arrived at the kitchen door one morning to find the room completely ransacked, with the table upside down and the store cupboard contents scattered everywhere [10]. Marianne Foyster noted that the boy probably thought she and Lionel had had a **"rare old scuffle"** the night before, but he dutifully helped her put the room straight [10].

**The "Cooking Smells" Theory**
The Foysters reported smelling an "odd smell of cooking" coming through their bedroom window between 11:00 p.m. and midnight [11]. Skeptics and critics suggested a mundane explanation for this: that the Mitchells were simply cooking late at night in the adjacent cottage [11]. Lionel Foyster dismissed this theory entirely, arguing that the Blue Room window was on the opposite side of the house from the cottage, making it impossible for the smells to travel that far [12].

**Departure**
The Mitchells eventually gave up their tenancy of the Rectory Cottage [13]. Their departure was keenly felt by the Foysters, who immediately missed the young man's help with the heavy physical work and water pumping [13]. By March 1931, the cottage was taken over by Frank Pearless (alias François d'Arles) [2].
