# Mr and Mrs Mitchell (Foyster Period)

Mr and Mrs Mitchell were the tenants of the [[rectory-cottage|Rectory Cottage]] adjoining [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] at the start of the [[foyster-phenomena|Foyster incumbency]] (from October 1930). They were later replaced by [[francois-darles|François d'Arles]] as cottage residents.

Mr Mitchell is described as a Yorkshireman. He and his wife lived in the cottage with their seventeen-year-old son, whose name is contradictorily recorded in the sources as both **Dick** and **Jack** [4, 3, 5]. The family frequently assisted the [[lionel-foyster|Foysters]] with domestic chores, including helping them unpack when they first moved in. Their son was heavily relied upon to pump water from the well and perform odd jobs around the Rectory.

## Immunity to Phenomena

The Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] explicitly noted in his writings that paranormal manifestations seldom occurred when the Mitchells were inside the Rectory. Foyster theorised this was because the "Goblins" had no spite against the family and wished to keep their activities secret, or alternatively, because the presence of "non-psychic people" actively hindered the manifestations.

## Key Incidents and Observations

**The Landing Figure:** Mrs Mitchell inadvertently contributed to the Borley ghost lore when she informed [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] that she had seen the Rector standing on the upstairs landing in his dressing gown, visible from the courtyard at night. When Marianne told her she was mistaken, as Lionel had not left his room, Mrs Mitchell grew "very indignant and said she was quite sure she saw me there in my dressing gown." While Marianne reportedly claimed this was an apparition of the late [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]], commentators suggest it was most likely just Lionel.

**Acoustic Misunderstandings:** Mrs Mitchell experienced the Rectory's deceptive acoustics while sitting in the sewing room with a supposedly unwell Marianne. She remarked, "Ee... how plainly this house does sound; how plainly you can hear Mr. Foyster walking about upstairs," despite the fact that Lionel was actually sitting quietly downstairs.

**Stone-Throwing at Dick/Jack:** On two occasions, stones were thrown at the Mitchells' teenage son while he was working at the Rectory. The boy initially assumed Marianne had thrown the first stone at him, and blamed three-year-old Adelaide for a stone thrown the previous Monday. Foyster believed the entity was actually targeting himself; since the boy was about the same height as the Rector, Foyster assumed the poltergeist had mistaken the teenager for him.

**The Kitchen Chaos:** The son arrived at the kitchen door one morning to find the room completely ransacked, with the table upside down and the store cupboard contents scattered everywhere. Marianne noted that the boy probably thought she and Lionel had had a "rare old scuffle" the night before, but he dutifully helped her put the room straight.

## The Cooking-Smells Controversy

The Foysters reported smelling an "odd smell of cooking" coming through their bedroom window between 11:00 p.m. and midnight. The SPR investigators note that Foyster "does not appear to have connected this somewhat homely 'phenomenon' with the Mitchells, 'the people from the cottage', who may quite conceivably have prepared a meal at a late hour on occasions." The kitchen passage where the smell was most apparent was closest to the cottage.

> **Contradiction:** Foyster dismissed the Mitchells-as-explanation by arguing that the Blue Room window was on the opposite side of the house from the cottage, making it impossible for cooking smells to travel that far. The SPR found this rebuttal unconvincing given the acoustic and environmental properties of the enclosed courtyard.

## The Acoustic Effect

The SPR report demonstrates that the narrow enclosed courtyard between the rectory and cottage had an "extraordinary acoustic effect" by which sounds in the cottage and courtyard — including voices, footsteps, and the late-night operation of the hand-pump — were heard inside the rectory as if they were occurring within the building. Canon [[henry-lawton|Lawton]], who occupied the rectory as locum in 1933, confirmed this: when the cottage was without a tenant during his month's stay, none of the characteristic thumps, footsteps, and voices were heard.

The Mitchells were, in the SPR's analysis, an entirely normal household whose domestic activities accidentally contributed to Borley's poltergeist reputation during the most active period of the alleged haunting.

## Departure

The Mitchells eventually gave up their tenancy of the Rectory Cottage, a loss keenly felt by the Foysters, who immediately missed the young man's help with heavy work and water pumping. By March 1931 the cottage was taken over by Frank Pearless (alias [[francois-darles|François d'Arles]]).
