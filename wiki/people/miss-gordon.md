# Miss E.R. Gordon

Resident of Borley Lodge during the time of the [[foyster-incumbency]]. She was contacted by [[trevor-hall]] who took on the task of investigating the [[foyster-phenomena]] for the SPR.

## Testimony

In a letter to Hall dated 26 July 1954, Miss Gordon described [[adelaide-foyster]]'s behaviour during the time she was at [[borley-rectory]]. She described Adelaide as a "very nervous" child who talked constantly of "having to burn down the rectory," and described apparent incidents whereby Adelaide attempted to build small fires out of pillows which were always discovered before any damage was done.

The [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] made use of Miss Gordon's comments as an explanation for the small fires which were discovered in the Rectory during the [[foyster-incumbency]] (see Chronology, 4 May 1931).

## Assessment

This testimony was used by [[trevor-hall]] as evidence that at least some of the fire-starting phenomena at Borley during the Foyster period could be attributed to a disturbed child, rather than poltergeist activity. However, it should be noted that Adelaide was a very young child at the time — born 1928, she would have been 2–3 years old in 1931 — and the testimony comes from a neighbour rather than from anyone living in the Rectory.

The BRC does not take a view on whether Miss Gordon's account is accurate, but notes that it forms part of the SPR's pattern of explaining away phenomena through attributions to individual household members, primarily [[marianne-foyster]] but also Adelaide.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Miss E. R. Gordon** (also identified in the texts as **Evelyn Gordon**) was a resident of the Borley district during the Foyster incumbency whose pragmatic observations provided researchers with crucial, non-paranormal explanations for some of the Rectory's poltergeist phenomena. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, specific events, and key testimony regarding her involvement:

**Biographical Details and Residence**
*   **Occupation:** She was an **experienced nursery nurse** who ran a **children’s playgroup** [1]. 
*   **Residence:** During the Foysters' tenancy in the early 1930s, she lived at **Borley Lodge**, which is described as a fifteenth-century manor house located in the nearby settlement of Borley Green [1, 2]. 
*   **Later Life:** By the 1950s, when investigators tracked her down, she was living in **Bournemouth** [1]. 

**Role in the SPR Investigation**
Miss Gordon was contacted by the skeptical investigator Trevor Hall during his preparation of the highly critical 1956 *Borley Report* for the Society for Psychical Research (SPR). She is officially acknowledged in the preface of the report for the help and information she provided the authors [3]. Through a specific letter she wrote to Hall on **July 26, 1954**, she provided a damning psychological portrait of the Foyster household [2].

**Testimony Regarding Adelaide Foyster and the "Poltergeist" Fires**
Miss Gordon’s most historically significant contribution to the Borley saga involves her observations of **Adelaide Foyster**, the Foysters' young adopted daughter. Because Marianne Foyster was frequently absent in London and could not keep domestic staff, Miss Gordon became highly concerned about the lack of care the five-year-old was receiving, noting that the young child had even started to cook simple dishes (like rice and potatoes) for herself [1]. 

Furthermore, Miss Gordon provided a completely mundane explanation for the mysterious, spontaneous outbreaks of fire at the Rectory (specifically those around May 4, 1931) that Harry Price had attributed to poltergeists [2]:
*   She described Adelaide as a **"very nervous"** and "excitable" child [1, 2].
*   She revealed that Adelaide talked constantly and incessantly of **"having to burn down the rectory"** [1, 2].
*   Most damningly, she recounted actual incidents where **Adelaide had attempted to build small fires out of pillows** in the bedrooms, though these were always discovered and extinguished before any real damage was done [1, 2]. As an experienced nursery nurse, Miss Gordon concluded that this was not the normal psychology or behavior of a five-year-old [1]. 

**The NSPCC Incident**
Driven by her observations of the girl's behavioral issues and the poor domestic conditions at the Rectory, Miss Gordon took formal action. She approached the **NSPCC (National Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children)** and reported that she believed Adelaide might be suffering from neglect [4]. 

When the NSPCC investigated the claim, they informed Miss Gordon that she was not the first person to complain to the society about the child's welfare [4]. Ultimately, however, the NSPCC concluded that while conditions at Borley Rectory were far from satisfactory, there was "no technical cruelty," and therefore they could take no action [4].
