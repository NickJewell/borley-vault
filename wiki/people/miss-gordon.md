# Miss E.R. Gordon

Also identified in the texts as **Evelyn Gordon**. An **experienced nursery nurse** who ran a **children's playgroup** [1], she lived during the Foysters' tenancy at **Borley Lodge** — a fifteenth-century manor house in the nearby settlement of Borley Green [1, 2]. By the 1950s, when investigators tracked her down, she was living in **Bournemouth** [1].

She was contacted by [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] during his preparation of the critical 1956 *Borley Report* for the SPR, and is officially acknowledged in the preface of that report for the help she provided the authors [3].

## Testimony Regarding Adelaide Foyster and the "Poltergeist" Fires

Miss Gordon's most historically significant contribution to the Borley saga involves her observations of **[[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide Foyster]]**, the Foysters' young adopted daughter. Because [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] was frequently absent in London and could not keep domestic staff, Miss Gordon became highly concerned about the lack of care the five-year-old was receiving — noting that the child had even started to cook simple dishes for herself [1].

In a letter to Hall dated **26 July 1954**, she provided a completely mundane explanation for the mysterious, spontaneous outbreaks of fire at the Rectory (specifically those around 4 May 1931) that [[harry-price|Harry Price]] had attributed to poltergeists [2]:

- She described [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide]] as a **"very nervous"** and "excitable" child [1, 2]
- She revealed that Adelaide talked constantly of **"having to burn down the rectory"** [1, 2]
- She recounted actual incidents where **Adelaide had attempted to build small fires out of pillows** in the bedrooms, though these were always discovered before any real damage was done [1, 2]

As an experienced nursery nurse, Miss Gordon concluded this was not the normal psychology or behaviour of a five-year-old [1]. The [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] made use of her comments as part of its pattern of explaining away phenomena through attributions to individual household members.

## The NSPCC Incident

Driven by her observations of Adelaide's behavioural issues and the poor domestic conditions at the Rectory, Miss Gordon took formal action and reported to the **NSPCC** that she believed Adelaide might be suffering from neglect [4]. When the NSPCC investigated, they informed Miss Gordon she was **not the first person** to complain to the society about the child's welfare [4]. Ultimately, however, the NSPCC concluded that while conditions at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] were far from satisfactory, there was "no technical cruelty," and therefore they could take no action [4].

## Assessment

The BRC does not take a view on whether Miss Gordon's account is accurate, but notes that it forms part of the SPR's pattern of explaining away phenomena through attributions to individual household members — primarily [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] but also Adelaide. It should be noted that Adelaide was a very young child at the time (born 1928; she would have been 2–3 years old in 1931), and the testimony comes from a neighbour rather than from anyone living in the Rectory itself.
