# Henry Francis Fisher (21 December 1900 – 1969)

Born Michaelchurch, Herefordshire; died Worcester. Commercial traveller for a Yorkshire ironmongery firm. Known as "Johnnie" by [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]]. The innocent victim of what [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] later characterised as an audacious financial scheme — a bigamous marriage engineered by Marianne while [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] was still alive and rector at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

> **Contradiction:** Sources present a slight contradiction regarding his exact birth date, recording it as either December 21 or December 22, 1900 [1, 2].

## Biographical Details

His father had been a farmer, and Fisher grew up in a well-to-do family in the West of England [2, 3]. He worked as a commercial traveller for a Yorkshire ironmongery firm (though Marianne later claimed he worked for "Harris's Cake") [4]. Fisher was considered an honest and sincere man, but highly vulnerable: he suffered from **periodic episodes of mental instability due to head injuries sustained in a motorcycle crash during his youth** [1, 5, 6]. Later in life he was hospitalised with a duodenal condition [4, 7].

## Meeting Marianne and the Deception (1934)

Fisher first met Marianne Foyster in the autumn of 1934 on a railway station platform at Marks Tey or Colchester as she was changing trains [5, 8]. At the time, Fisher was lodging at 38 London Road, Ipswich [9].

Marianne immediately deceived him. She introduced herself as an unmarried woman and **claimed that [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]] was her elderly, invalid father**, with [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide Foyster]] her younger sister [5, 6]. They corresponded, met at a London hotel, and began an affair. To secure the marriage, Marianne falsely told Fisher she was pregnant [11, 12]. Reflecting on their motives, Marianne later provided the specific quote: **"Fisher got caught. He thought I had money and I got caught, I thought he had. I was desperate…"** [13]

## The Bigamous Marriage (1935)

Fisher converted to Catholicism at Marianne's urging. On **February 23, 1935**, they were married at St Pancras Roman Catholic Church, Ipswich [3, 14, 15]. Lionel Foyster was still alive — still married to Marianne — and was, at that point, still rector at Borley.

The marriage certificate was riddled with Marianne's fabrications: while Fisher correctly gave his age as 34, Marianne gave her name as "Mariane Voyster," declared herself a 30-year-old spinster (she was actually 36 and still legally married to Foyster), and listed her father as "Leon Alphonse Voyster, of independent means" [3, 16].

## Life at Borley and the Fake Children

Incredibly, after the marriage, Fisher briefly lived at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] with Marianne and Lionel Foyster [17, 18]. Lionel actively colluded in the deception, pretending to be Fisher's father-in-law. To maintain the illusion of her pregnancy, Marianne secretly adopted a baby boy (Timothy Eves) in August 1935 while Fisher was visiting his family, sending him a telegram reading, **"It's a boy!"** [18, 20]. The boy was renamed John Henry Francis Fisher. Two years later, Marianne adopted a baby girl (Mary Lex, renamed Astrid Marianne Zaida), and presented her as Fisher's daughter, though Fisher had serious doubts and suspected Marianne of infidelity [22, 23].

The purpose of the scheme, according to Hall and [[robert-wood|Robert Wood]]: to secure a wage-earner for the Foyster household after Lionel's inevitable retirement due to his crippling arthritis, given that his pension alone would be wholly inadequate. Marianne wrote to [[ian-greenwood|Ian]] in 1956: "Lion had 125 pounds pension and about 16 pounds from rents. His medicine cost over two pounds a month and often as high as eight. I had none to turn to."

## Residences and Later Life

Financed partly by the Fisher family, the ménage à trois lived at several Suffolk addresses:

- **4 Ancaster Road, Ipswich** (July 1935 – Autumn 1936) [17, 18]
- **102 Woodbridge Road East, Ipswich** (Autumn 1936 – June 1938). Fisher's mother visited but was never allowed to see the "father-in-law," as Foyster's door was kept locked [24, 25].
- **Hill Farm House, Chillesford** (June 1938 – May 1940) [26]
- **[[the-whin-snape|The Whin, Snape]]** (May 1940 – June 1941). Fisher was largely absent during this period, and Marianne reverted to calling herself "Mrs. Foyster" [4].
- **[[dairy-cottage-rendlesham|Dairy Cottages, Rendlesham]]** (June 1941 onwards). During WWII, Fisher was conscripted to work in a munitions factory in Saxmundham and could seldom return home [7, 27, 28].

Fisher's sister, Edith Shaw, grew highly suspicious of Marianne, eventually contacted the [[harry-bull|Bull family]], discovered the truth about the bigamous marriage, and successfully removed her brother from Marianne's influence [7, 29]. When [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] died in April 1945, Marianne fled to America with a young American G.I. Fisher arrived at Dairy Cottage only to find that Marianne had vanished, taking the furniture, his car, and the children [30].

The revelation of the truth and the abandonment completely shattered Fisher. He suffered a total mental and emotional breakdown in 1945 [2, 30, 32]. To spare him further agony, his family chose not to pursue legal action against Marianne. Fisher lived the rest of his life in seclusion, cared for by his sister in the West Country, and died in an old people's home in Worcester in 1969 [1, 2, 32].

The Companion's epitaph is blunt: "Ultimately, his connection with Borley was as part of one of the most unpleasant aspects of the whole case."
