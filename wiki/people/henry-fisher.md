# Henry Francis Fisher (21 December 1900 – 1969)

Born Michaelchurch, Herefordshire; died Worcester. Commercial traveller for an ironmongery firm. Known as "Johnnie" by [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]]. The innocent victim of what [[trevor-hall]] later characterised as an audacious financial scheme — a bigamous marriage engineered by Marianne while [[lionel-foyster]] was still alive and rector at [[borley-rectory]].

## The Bigamous Marriage

Marianne told [[robert-swanson]] she met Fisher "by chance towards the end of 1934 at a railway station in Essex." They became lovers. On **23 February 1935**, they were married at St Pancras Roman Catholic Church, Ipswich. Marianne gave a false surname on the marriage certificate. Lionel Foyster was still alive — still married to Marianne — and was, at that point, still rector at Borley.

## The Deception

Fisher was convinced that Lionel Foyster was Marianne's **father** and [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide Foyster]] her younger sister. He then agreed to take on a baby, presented as Marianne's own child, to complete the fiction. (The child was actually adopted; see the Companion's note on this in connection with [[marianne-foyster]].)

The purpose, according to Hall and [[robert-wood|Robert Wood]]: to secure a wage-earner for the Foyster household after Lionel's inevitable retirement due to his crippling arthritis, given that his pension alone would be wholly inadequate. Marianne wrote to [[ian-greenwood|Ian]] in 1956: "Lion had 125 pounds pension and about 16 pounds from rents. His medicine cost over two pounds a month and often as high as eight. I had none to turn to."

## Later Life

The Fishers and the Foysters lived together at various Suffolk addresses from October 1935 until Lionel's death in April 1945. Fisher appears to have been unaware of the situation throughout — partly because his commercial-traveller work kept him away from home for extended periods, and partly because he suffered periodic mental instability from a motorcycle accident in his youth.

When the truth finally became clear, Fisher suffered "a complete breakdown." He was cared for by his sister in the West Country. He died in an old people's home in Worcester at the end of the 1960s. "Ultimately, his connection with Borley was as part of one of the most unpleasant aspects of the whole case" (the Companion).

Marianne wrote, somewhat bitterly: "Fisher got caught. He thought I had money and I got caught, I thought he had. I was desperate."
