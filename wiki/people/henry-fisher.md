# Henry Francis Fisher (21 December 1900 – 1969)

Born Michaelchurch, Herefordshire; died Worcester. Commercial traveller for an ironmongery firm. Known as "Johnnie" by [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]]. The innocent victim of what [[trevor-hall]] later characterised as an audacious financial scheme — a bigamous marriage engineered by Marianne while [[lionel-foyster]] was still alive and rector at [[borley-rectory]].

## The Bigamous Marriage

Marianne told [[robert-swanson]] she met Fisher "by chance towards the end of 1934 at a railway station in Essex." They became lovers. On **23 February 1935**, they were married at St Pancras Roman Catholic Church, Ipswich. Marianne gave a false surname on the marriage certificate. Lionel Foyster was still alive — still married to Marianne — and was, at that point, still rector at Borley.

## The Deception

Fisher was convinced that Lionel Foyster was Marianne's **father** and [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide Foyster]] her younger sister. He then agreed to take on a baby, presented as Marianne's own child, to complete the fiction. (The child was actually adopted; see the Companion's note on this in connection with [[marianne-foyster]].)

The purpose, according to Hall and [[robert-wood|Robert Wood]]: to secure a wage-earner for the Foyster household after Lionel's inevitable retirement due to his crippling arthritis, given that his pension alone would be wholly inadequate. Marianne wrote to [[ian-greenwood|Ian]] in 1956: "Lion had 125 pounds pension and about 16 pounds from rents. His medicine cost over two pounds a month and often as high as eight. I had none to turn to."

## Later Life

The Fishers and the Foysters lived together at various Suffolk addresses from October 1935 until Lionel's death in April 1945. Fisher appears to have been unaware of the situation throughout — partly because his commercial-traveller work kept him away from home for extended periods, and partly because he suffered periodic mental instability from a motorcycle accident in his youth.

When the truth finally became clear, Fisher suffered "a complete breakdown." He was cared for by his sister in the West Country. He died in an old people's home in Worcester at the end of the 1960s. "Ultimately, his connection with Borley was as part of one of the most unpleasant aspects of the whole case" (the Companion).

Marianne wrote, somewhat bitterly: "Fisher got caught. He thought I had money and I got caught, I thought he had. I was desperate."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Henry Francis Fisher** (often referred to as "Johnnie" or "Johnny" Fisher) was a tragic figure in the Borley Rectory narrative. An innocent party, he became the victim of a calculated, bigamous marriage plot orchestrated by Marianne Foyster (the wife of the Rev. Lionel Foyster). 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following specific facts, dates, events, quotes, and contradictions regarding his life and his entanglement with the Foysters:

**Biographical Details and Health**
*   **Birth Date Contradictions:** Sources present a slight contradiction regarding his exact birth date. He was born at Michaelchurch Court near Ross-on-Wye, Herefordshire, on either **December 21, 1900** [1] or **December 22, 1900** [2]. 
*   **Background:** His father had been a farmer, and Fisher grew up in a well-to-do family in the West of England [2, 3]. He worked as a commercial traveller for a Yorkshire ironmongery firm (though Marianne later claimed he worked for "Harris’s Cake") [4]. 
*   **Mental and Physical Health:** Fisher was considered an honest and sincere man, but he was highly vulnerable. He suffered from **periodic episodes of mental instability due to head injuries sustained in a motorcycle crash during his youth** [1, 5, 6]. Later in life, he was hospitalised with a duodenal condition [4, 7]. 

**Meeting Marianne Foyster and the Deception (1934)**
Fisher first met Marianne Foyster in the autumn of 1934 on a railway station platform at Marks Tey or Colchester as she was changing trains [5, 8]. At the time, Fisher was lodging at 38 London Road, Ipswich [9]. 

Marianne immediately deceived him. She introduced herself as an unmarried woman named Marianne Foyster (or "Voyster") and **claimed that the Rev. Lionel Foyster was her elderly, invalid father** [5, 6]. They corresponded, met at a London hotel, and began an affair [5, 10]. To secure the marriage, Marianne falsely told Fisher she was pregnant [11, 12]. 

Reflecting on their motives for marriage, Marianne later provided the specific quote: **"Fisher got caught. He thought I had money and I got caught, I thought he had. I was desperate ..."** [13]. 

**The Bigamous Marriage (1935)**
Fisher converted to Catholicism at Marianne's urging, and the couple were married on **February 23, 1935**, at St Pancras Roman Catholic Church in Ipswich [3, 14, 15]. 

The marriage certificate was riddled with Marianne's fabrications: while Fisher correctly gave his age as 34, Marianne gave her name as "Mariane Voyster," declared herself a 30-year-old spinster (she was actually 36 and still legally married to Foyster), and listed her father as "Leon Alphonse Voyster, of independent means" [3, 16]. 

**Life at Borley and the Fake Children**
Incredibly, after the marriage, Fisher briefly lived at Borley Rectory with Marianne and Lionel Foyster [17, 18]. The Rev. Foyster actively colluded in the deception, pretending to be Fisher's father-in-law, while the Foysters' adopted daughter, Adelaide, was passed off as Marianne's younger sister [6, 19].

To maintain the illusion of her pregnancy, Marianne secretly adopted a baby boy (Timothy Eves) in August 1935 while Fisher was visiting his family in Michaelchurch. She sent Fisher a telegram reading, **"It’s a boy!"** [18, 20]. The boy was renamed John Henry Francis Fisher (though Marianne called him Johnnie) [21]. Two years later, Marianne adopted a baby girl, Mary Lex (renamed Astrid Marianne Zaida), and presented her as Fisher's daughter, though Fisher had serious doubts and suspected Marianne of infidelity [22, 23]. 

**Residences and World War II**
Financed in part by the Fisher family, the bizarre ménage à trois lived at several addresses:
*   **4 Ancaster Road, Ipswich** (July 1935 – Autumn 1936) [17, 18].
*   **102 Woodbridge Road East, Ipswich** (Autumn 1936 – June 1938). Fisher's mother visited for a holiday but was never allowed to see the "father-in-law," as Foyster's door was kept locked [24, 25]. 
*   **Hill Farm House, Chillesford** (June 1938 – May 1940) [26].
*   **The Whin, Snape** (May 1940 – June 1941). Fisher was largely absent during this period, likely due to a mental breakdown or hospitalization, and Marianne reverted to calling herself "Mrs. Foyster" [4].
*   **Dairy Cottages, Rendlesham** (June 1941 onwards). During WWII, Fisher was conscripted to work in a munitions factory in Saxmundham and could seldom return home due to the blackout [7, 27, 28]. 

**Discovery, Breakdown, and Death**
Fisher's sister, Edith Shaw, grew highly suspicious of Marianne and eventually contacted the Bull family, discovering the truth about the bigamous marriage [7, 29]. She intervened and successfully removed her brother from Marianne's manipulative influence [7]. 

When Lionel Foyster died in April 1945, Marianne prepared to flee to America with a young American G.I. Fisher arrived at Dairy Cottage only to find that Marianne had vanished, taking the furniture, his car, and the children [30]. According to Marianne, she had one final meeting with Fisher before leaving England, during which they merely "talked about the war" [31]. 

The revelation of the truth and the abandonment completely shattered Fisher. He suffered a total mental and emotional breakdown in 1945 [2, 30, 32]. To spare him further agony, his family chose not to pursue legal action against Marianne [30]. Fisher lived the rest of his life in seclusion, cared for by his sister in the West Country, and ultimately died in an old people's home in Worcester in 1969 [1, 2, 32].
