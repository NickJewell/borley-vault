# Percy Pigott

Of Eagle House, Kirk Ella, Hull. Wrote to [[harry-price|Harry Price]] on February 12, 1941, offering what amounts to an early "stone tape" theory of haunting — the idea that the *ether* acts as a recording medium, storing and replaying events that occurred in a location with sufficient emotional intensity.

## The Ether Theory

In a long, carefully argued letter reproduced in full in Chapter 21 of *The End of Borley Rectory*, Pigott proposed:

> *"Is it not possible that the substance which pervades all space, interpenetrating and enveloping our earth and our bodies, which scientists simply name ether, but do not pretend to explain, is capable of receiving and retaining pictures of our actions and the sounds which emanating from such actions, and even of reproducing them when conditions are favorable?"*

He draws the analogy to cinema film: cinema reproduces form, motion, and sound. Why should nature not do the same? A past event sufficiently charged with emotion — particularly the "great tragedies of life" — would be most deeply "impressed" and therefore most clearly "reproduced."

On this basis he argues that the coach-and-horses, the footsteps, the nun's perambulations, and the "light in the window" are not conscious entities but etheric playbacks — "a mirage of a past event." No ghost is actually "there"; the ether is replaying a recording.

He reserves the Poltergeist phenomena (stone-throwing, furniture-moving, the wall-messages, the belt-lifting) for a different explanation: a "vaguely conscious, instinctual, elemental force" emanating from the distress of a departed being, not intelligent enough to compose a coherent sentence but capable of physical action.

The headless coachman, he suggests, may be either a mirage-artefact or a projection by the coachman himself, who — believing himself headless after death — "projects" that image.

## Significance

Pigott's letter, written in 1941, anticipates the "stone tape" concept popularised decades later. His framework is more sophisticated than many of the other theories Price received, and Price gives it prominent space in the causation chapter. The distinction Pigott draws between "recorded" phenomena (apparitions, sounds) and "active" phenomena (Poltergeist) corresponds to a distinction Price himself made in his general theorising about hauntings.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Percy Pigott** was a correspondent of investigator Harry Price who offered theoretical explanations for the paranormal activity at Borley Rectory. He resided at Eagle House, Kirk Ella, Hull [1].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following specific dates, quotes, and theories regarding his involvement:

**Connection to the Case and Harry Price's Opinion**
*   Pigott had no firsthand experiences at the Rectory but was characterized by Price as one of the "students and those who have studied the Borley hauntings from a dispassionate angle" [1].
*   On **February 12, 1941**, Pigott sent Price a long letter outlining his ideas, which Price described as a **"very modern ‘view of ghosts’"** [1].
*   Price held Pigott's theoretical input in high regard, publicly praising his speculations in *The End of Borley Rectory* as being **"novel, interesting, ingenious, and—in some cases—even exciting"** [1]. 

**Theories on the Hauntings and the Headless Coachman**
In his correspondence, Pigott presented specific theories regarding the origins and mechanisms of the Rectory's apparitions and poltergeists [2, 3]:
*   **The Origin Tragedy:** Pigott argued that a specific event was the catalyst for the haunting, stating: **"There is little doubt that at some time there was a cruel tragedy at Borley in which a group of people were involved. Both the apparitions and the Poltergeist manifestations would have their origin in this tragedy"** [2].
*   **The Headless Coachman:** Addressing the famous phantom of the headless coachman, Pigott suggested the coachman was involved in this ancient tragedy and "may have lost his head," theorizing that consequently **"he might very likely think of himself as headless after passing over"** [2].
*   **The Mechanism of Apparitions:** He offered a projective theory for ghostly sightings, noting: **"It is well known that all apparitions of the living are caused by the subject thinking of himself as with a distant friend or in a distant place, and thus he projects his form to that place and it is occasionally seen and even heard"** [2].

**Direct Quote and Demeanor**
Recognizing that his ideas were ultimately unprovable, Pigott closed his long letter to Price with a modest admission, turning Price's own words back upon him:
> **"This is a long letter. I hope I have not wearied you. I will close by using your own words and applying them to myself. ‘I do not know.’ These are just my speculations."** [3].

The available texts contain no further biographical details or contradictions regarding Percy Pigott beyond this theoretical correspondence.
