# Percy Pigott

Of Eagle House, Kirk Ella, Hull. Wrote to [[harry-price|Harry Price]] on **12 February 1941**, offering what amounts to an early "stone tape" theory of haunting — the idea that the *ether* acts as a recording medium, storing and replaying events that occurred in a location with sufficient emotional intensity. Price described Pigott's letter as offering a **"very modern 'view of ghosts'"** [1] and praised his speculations publicly in *The End of Borley Rectory* as **"novel, interesting, ingenious, and—in some cases—even exciting"** [1].

Pigott had no firsthand experiences at the Rectory but was characterised by Price as one of the "students and those who have studied the Borley hauntings from a dispassionate angle" [1].

## The Ether Theory

In a long, carefully argued letter reproduced in full in Chapter 21 of *The End of Borley Rectory*, Pigott proposed:

> *"Is it not possible that the substance which pervades all space, interpenetrating and enveloping our earth and our bodies, which scientists simply name ether, but do not pretend to explain, is capable of receiving and retaining pictures of our actions and the sounds which emanating from such actions, and even of reproducing them when conditions are favorable?"*

He drew the analogy to cinema film: cinema reproduces form, motion, and sound. Why should nature not do the same? A past event sufficiently charged with emotion — particularly the "great tragedies of life" — would be most deeply "impressed" and therefore most clearly "reproduced."

Pigott argued that a specific event was the catalyst for the haunting: **"There is little doubt that at some time there was a cruel tragedy at Borley in which a group of people were involved. Both the apparitions and the Poltergeist manifestations would have their origin in this tragedy"** [2].

On this basis he argued that the coach-and-horses, the footsteps, [[the-nun|the nun]]'s perambulations, and the "light in the window" are not conscious entities but etheric playbacks — "a mirage of a past event." No ghost is actually "there"; the ether is replaying a recording.

He reserved the Poltergeist phenomena (stone-throwing, furniture-moving, the wall-messages, the belt-lifting) for a different explanation: a "vaguely conscious, instinctual, elemental force" emanating from the distress of a departed being, not intelligent enough to compose a coherent sentence but capable of physical action.

## The Headless Coachman

Addressing the famous phantom of the [[phantom-coach|headless coachman]], Pigott offered a projective theory for ghostly sightings: **"It is well known that all apparitions of the living are caused by the subject thinking of himself as with a distant friend or in a distant place, and thus he projects his form to that place and it is occasionally seen and even heard"** [2]. The headless coachman, he suggested, was involved in the ancient tragedy and "may have lost his head," theorising that consequently **"he might very likely think of himself as headless after passing over"** [2] — meaning the apparition is either a mirage-artefact or a projection by the coachman himself.

## Significance

Pigott's letter, written in 1941, anticipates the "stone tape" concept popularised decades later. His framework is more sophisticated than many of the other theories Price received, and Price gives it prominent space in the causation chapter. The distinction Pigott draws between "recorded" phenomena (apparitions, sounds) and "active" phenomena (Poltergeist) corresponds to a distinction Price himself made in his general theorising about hauntings.

Recognising that his ideas were ultimately unprovable, Pigott closed with a modest admission, turning Price's own words back upon him: **"This is a long letter. I hope I have not wearied you. I will close by using your own words and applying them to myself. 'I do not know.' These are just my speculations."** [3].
