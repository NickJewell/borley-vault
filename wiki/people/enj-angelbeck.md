# E. N. J. Angelbeck

Investigator who participated in the [[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]], the group of mostly undergraduate students led by [[ajb-robertson|Dr Andrew J. B. Robertson]] that conducted a series of investigations at the ruins of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] between 1939 and 1944 [1–3].

## The "Eighteen Knocks" (Visit No. 3)

Angelbeck's most notable contribution occurred during the Cambridge Commission's "Visit No. 3," conducted alongside Robertson.

> **Contradiction regarding the date:** The Borley chronology records this visit as occurring in **June 1941** [2, 5], whereas the detailed Commission report dates it to the night of **20 December 1941** [4].

During this observation period, Angelbeck and Robertson took temperature readings and actively attempted to initiate communication with the Rectory's supposed spirits [2, 4]. They moved into the hall and addressed the entities, specifically **"calling on Harry Bull by name"** [4] and asking the entity to communicate by making "one knock for 'Yes,' two for 'No,' and three for 'I don't know'" [4]. The two men then moved to the drawing-room and knocked on the wall in a regular rhythm using an electric torch [4].

About five minutes later, at **12:35 a.m.**, both investigators heard a series of eighteen knocks that appeared to be **"directed by some intelligence"** [4]. The sounds were described as:

- **"Very distinct knocking, of a very regular kind"** seemingly originating from just outside the drawing room [4]
- The pattern: **"a double knock repeated three times; then a short pause of a few seconds, when the double knock was again repeated three times; then after another pause this occurred again exactly as before"** [4]
- The noise sounded as though **"it might have been a person operating a small hand-pump for water"**, though they felt it was a very peculiar time of night for such activity [4]

After conducting a search and failing to find anyone who could have produced the sounds, Angelbeck and Robertson concluded that there was **"a possibility of phenomena of a psychic nature having occurred"** [4]. Their later attempts to provoke further knocking were completely unsuccessful [4].

The SPR investigators subsequently cited this report as one of several demonstrating that the [[mr-and-mrs-arbon|Arbons']] late-night use of the hand-pump in the courtyard — clearly audible inside the rectory and sounding like it was in the corridor — accounted for many of the Commission's "unexplained" knocking phenomena.

## A Second Uneventful Visit (Visit No. 7)

Angelbeck returned to Borley for the Commission's "Visit No. 7," which took place over the night of **22–23 December 1942** [6]. He was again accompanied by Robertson, but this overnight vigil was entirely uneventful — **"no phenomena reported"** [6].
