# E. N. J. Angelbeck

E. N. J. Angelbeck was a member of the [[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]] investigation of the Borley ruins. He is recorded as being present on the night of 20 December 1941 with [[ajb-robertson|A. J. B. Robertson]], when they heard a peculiar regular knocking which, they noted, "sounded as though it might have been a person operating a small hand-pump for water, but it seemed rather a peculiar time for this (12:35 a.m.)." Their report stated they "could not find any person who might have produced the effect," but did not specify the time at which they investigated.

The SPR investigators cited this report as one of several demonstrating that the Arbons' late-night use of the hand-pump in the courtyard — clearly audible inside the rectory and sounding like it was in the corridor — accounted for many of the Commission's "unexplained" knocking phenomena.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**E. N. J. Angelbeck** was an investigator who participated in the "Cambridge Commission," a group of mostly undergraduate students led by Dr. Andrew J. B. Robertson that conducted a series of investigations at the ruins of Borley Rectory between 1939 and 1944 [1-3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, quotes, and historical contradictions regarding his involvement:

**The "Eighteen Knocks" (Visit No. 3)**
Angelbeck's most notable contribution to the Borley investigation occurred during the Cambridge Commission's "Visit No. 3," which he conducted alongside Andrew Robertson [2, 4]. 

The sources contain a **specific contradiction regarding the date** of this visit: the Borley chronology records the event as occurring in **June 1941** [2, 5], whereas the detailed Commission report dates the visit to the night of **December 20, 1941** [4]. 

During this observation period, Angelbeck and Robertson took temperature readings and actively attempted to initiate communication with the Rectory's supposed spirits [2, 4]. According to the official report, they moved into the hall and addressed the entities, specifically **"calling on Harry Bull by name"** [4]. They asked the entity to communicate by making **"one knock for 'Yes,' two for 'No,' and three for 'I don’t know'"** [4]. The two men then moved to the drawing-room and knocked on the wall in a regular rhythm using an electric torch [4]. 

About five minutes later, at **12:35 a.m.**, both investigators heard a series of eighteen knocks that appeared to be **"directed by some intelligence"** [4]. The report specifically describes the sounds:
*   They heard **"very distinct knocking, of a very regular kind"** that seemed to originate from just outside the drawing room [4]. 
*   The pattern consisted of **"a double knock repeated three times; then a short pause of a few seconds, when the double knock was again repeated three times; then after another pause this occurred again exactly as before"** [4]. 
*   The men noted that the noise sounded as though **"it might have been a person operating a small hand-pump for water,"** though they felt it was a very peculiar time of night for such an activity [4]. 

After conducting a search and failing to find anyone who could have produced the sounds, Angelbeck and Robertson concluded that **"there was a possibility of phenomena of a psychic nature having occurred"** [4]. However, their later attempts to provoke further knocking were completely unsuccessful [4]. 

**A Second Uneventful Visit (Visit No. 7)**
The only other recorded detail regarding Angelbeck is his return to Borley Rectory for the Commission's "Visit No. 7," which took place over the night of **December 22–23, 1942** [6]. He was once again accompanied by Andrew Robertson, but this overnight vigil was entirely uneventful, with the records noting that there were **"no phenomena reported"** [6]. 

The provided texts do not record any further biographical details or subsequent involvement regarding E. N. J. Angelbeck.
