# Montague Elelman (d. before 1974)

British journalist who first visited [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] ruins in March 1946 and removed a 17-inch piece of charred oak beam as a souvenir. For the next nine years, multiple independent witnesses — the majority with no prior knowledge of the wood's origin — reported paranormal phenomena in its presence.

## The Relic

A 17-inch piece of charred oak beam, picked up from the ruins in March 1946 and kept by Elelman until c.1955 when it was loaned and never returned.

## Chronology of Associated Incidents (1946–1955)

See [[bps-05]] for full detail. Summary:

- **London, March 1946 (night 1):** Elelman's sister saw a black-garbed nun in his room while he was downstairs; she had no knowledge of where he'd been.
- **Night 2:** Second unexplained scream woke the sister from deep sleep.
- **Night 3:** Brother Mike in the room felt intense unexplained cold; heard a voice say "Sit up."
- **Night 4:** Irregular chiming at 2:30 a.m. (no chiming clock in the house).
- **Westcliff-on-Sea:** Landlord's doorbell rang at 8 p.m. on three consecutive evenings (no caller); on Sunday a neighbour observed no one at the front door while ringing continued; neighbour also glimpsed a "darkly clad figure" on the landing.
- **Surrey:** Pre-dawn shuffling footsteps in the spare room where the wood was stored.
- **London flat:** Wife Veronica saw a figure in the hall alcove (where wood was hidden in a trunk). Father-in-law asked who was "walking past the partly open door" in the hall.

## Elelman's Own Assessment

"My story is true, but I think it includes coincidences — and perhaps it's possible for people themselves to create ghostly sights and happenings out of the stories — false or otherwise — that are in their heads, and for those things to be seen by other people too."

He acknowledged the most difficult cases were: the sister seeing the nun before knowing where he'd been, and the Garnett incidents when Elelman was absent and the wood still in his unopened luggage.

## Later Visit

In 1959 he returned to Borley; the ruins were gone and a bungalow was being built. He reached for a remaining gatepost to take another piece; his wife struck him on the head with a road atlas: "We are not starting that all over again."

## Broadcast

The account was delivered as a radio broadcast script, sent to Underwood in April 1974 with the note that it had "the added advantage of being completely true."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Montague Elelman** (whose surname is also recorded in the sources as **Ellman** and **Eldelman**) was a London journalist whose connection to the Borley saga centers on an infamous "cursed" souvenir he took from the site. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, events, dates, and quotes regarding his experience:

### Biographical Details
*   **Occupation and Residence:** Elelman was a journalist who lived in **Beckenham**, London, at the time of his initial visit to Borley [1]. He later lived in lodgings at **Westcliff-on-Sea**, Essex [2]. 
*   **Record of Death:** By the time parts of his story were published, he had passed away, being referred to in the texts as "the late Montague Elelman" [3]. 

### The 1946 Visit and the "Relic of Borley"
Shortly after beginning his career as a journalist in **1946**, Elelman traveled to Borley with the vague intention of picking up a story for a London newspaper [1]. 
*   While wandering the ruined site, he picked up a **piece of a charred oak beam** to take home as a souvenir [1].
*   Elelman maintained that for the **nine years** he possessed this piece of charred wood, a series of bizarre and inexplicable events occurred around him and those close to him [4, 5]. 

### The Phenomena
The sources detail specific paranormal incidents tied to Elelman's possession of the wood at two different locations:

**At his Sister's Home in London (1946)**
*   **The Black-Garbed Nun:** On the evening he brought the wood home, he placed it on the mantelpiece in the room he was occupying at his married sister's house. Shortly after, his sister passed the open door of the room and cried out, claiming to have seen a **"black-garbed nun" standing motionless in the gloom** [1].
*   **The Nightmare:** That same night, Elelman had a vivid dream in which a voice shouted the word "Borley!" and a nun with **skin like "grey leather"** and dead eyes glided up to him and screamed, waking him suddenly [1].
*   **His Brother's Experience:** The following day, Elelman's brother came to share the room. Despite mild weather, the brother was troubled by intense, freezing cold that left his teeth chattering, and he claimed to hear a voice command him to **"Sit up!"** [6]. The next night, at exactly 2:30 a.m., both brothers were awakened by a loud, irregular chiming sound inside the house, despite there being no chiming clocks on the premises [6]. 

**At Westcliff-on-Sea Lodgings**
*   **The Phantom Doorbells:** A few weeks later, Elelman moved to Westcliff-on-Sea. While he was away for a weekend in Surrey, his landlord (Mr. Garnett) and his wife were disturbed by prolonged ringing of their doorbell at exactly 8:00 p.m. on both Friday and Saturday, with no one at the door [2]. On Sunday, they closed the glass inner door to catch the pranksters, but at 8:00 p.m., the bell shrilled again while Mr. Garnett watched the completely empty front door [2]. 
*   **The Dark Figure:** The following Monday afternoon, while Mrs. Garnett was chatting with a neighbor at the door, the neighbor suddenly inquired about a **darkly clad figure** she had just glimpsed on the stair landing behind Mrs. Garnett, even though she was entirely alone in the house [5].

### Loss of the Relic
The nine years of phenomena came to an end after Elelman loaned the piece of wood to someone else. He noted, **"I handed it over — and never saw it, or the borrower again"** [7]. 

### Later Broadcasts, Quotes, and Opinions
Elelman's experiences were eventually turned into a broadcast and written script titled **"My Chip Off the Borley Block"** [3]. In **April 1974**, he sent this script to the psychical researcher Peter Underwood, asserting in an accompanying letter that the remarkable story had "the added advantage of being completely true" [3, 8]. 

Despite insisting on the truth of his experiences, Elelman offered a highly measured and psychological perspective on the hauntings. Acknowledging the skeptical 1956 SPR report that concluded the Borley evidence was negligible or rigged, Elelman provided a specific quote detailing his own balanced contradiction:
> **"Well, my story is true, but I think it includes coincidences — and perhaps it’s possible for people themselves to create ghostly sights and happenings out of the stories — false or otherwise — that are in their heads, and for those things to be seen by other people too."** [7].
