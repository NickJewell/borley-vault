# Montague Elelman (d. before 1974)

**Montague Elelman** (also recorded in sources as **Ellman** and **Eldelman**) was a British journalist who lived in **Beckenham**, London, at the time of his initial visit to Borley [1]. He later lived in lodgings at **Westcliff-on-Sea**, Essex [2]. By the time parts of his story were published, he had died — referred to in the texts as "the late Montague Elelman" [3].

He first visited the [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] ruins in March 1946, shortly after beginning his journalism career, travelling to Borley with the vague intention of picking up a story for a London newspaper [1]. He removed a 17-inch piece of charred oak beam as a souvenir. For the next nine years, multiple independent witnesses — the majority with no prior knowledge of the wood's origin — reportedly experienced paranormal phenomena in its presence.

## The Relic

A 17-inch piece of charred oak beam, picked up from the ruins in March 1946 and kept by Elelman until c.1955 when it was loaned and never returned. He later noted: **"I handed it over — and never saw it, or the borrower again"** [7].

## Chronology of Associated Incidents (1946–1955)

**At his sister's home in London (1946):**

- On the evening he brought the wood home and placed it on the mantelpiece, his married sister passed the open door and cried out, claiming to have seen a **"black-garbed nun" standing motionless in the gloom** [1] — before knowing where he had been
- That same night, Elelman had a vivid dream in which a voice shouted "Borley!" and a nun with skin like "grey leather" and dead eyes glided up to him and screamed [1]
- The following day, his brother sharing the room was troubled by intense, freezing cold despite mild weather, heard a voice command **"Sit up!"** [6], and that night both brothers were awakened at exactly 2:30 a.m. by loud, irregular chiming with no chiming clocks on the premises [6]

**At Westcliff-on-Sea lodgings:**

- While Elelman was away for a weekend in Surrey, his landlord (Mr Garnett) and wife were disturbed by prolonged ringing of the doorbell at exactly 8:00 p.m. on both Friday and Saturday evenings, with no one at the door [2]; on Sunday, Mr Garnett watched the completely empty front door while the bell shrilled again [2]
- The following Monday afternoon, a neighbour chatting with Mrs Garnett at the door suddenly inquired about a **darkly clad figure** she had glimpsed on the stair landing behind Mrs Garnett, even though she was entirely alone in the house [5]

**At his London flat:** His wife Veronica saw a figure in the hall alcove (where the wood was hidden in a trunk). His father-in-law asked who was "walking past the partly open door" in the hall.

## Elelman's Own Assessment

"My story is true, but I think it includes coincidences — and perhaps it's possible for people themselves to create ghostly sights and happenings out of the stories — false or otherwise — that are in their heads, and for those things to be seen by other people too." [7]

He acknowledged the most difficult cases were: the sister seeing the nun before knowing where he had been, and the Garnett incidents when Elelman was absent and the wood still in his unopened luggage.

## Later Visit

In 1959 he returned to Borley; the ruins were gone and a bungalow was being built. He reached for a remaining gatepost to take another piece; his wife struck him on the head with a road atlas: "We are not starting that all over again."

## Broadcast

The account was delivered as a radio broadcast script titled **"My Chip Off the Borley Block"** [3], sent to [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] in April 1974 with the note that it had "the added advantage of being completely true" [3, 8].
