# Mr. Hardy (Senior), Painter

Foreman painter; born in Borley; had lived there his entire life and worked at the Rectory on many occasions. Encountered [[harry-price|Price]]'s observer [[sidney-glanville|Glanville]] in November 1937 when the Chelmsford Diocesan Dilapidations Board had authorised redecoration of the exterior. Sole named witness of the "smoking phenomenon." Listed in Appendix D as "Hardy, Senior" and Appendix E as "Mr. Hardy, Senior." He had a nineteen-year-old son at the time of the 1937 investigations.

## The November 1937 Encounter and Broken Seals

Hardy's primary intersection with the official investigation occurred over the weekend of **November 20–21, 1937**. He and his crew had been contracted by the Chelmsford Diocesan Dilapidations Board to repaint the exterior and windows of the Rectory. When [[sidney-glanville|Glanville]] arrived that Saturday morning, he was reportedly highly annoyed to discover that Hardy's painters had broken the control seals previously placed across the doors and windows of the house. Despite this investigative setback, Glanville engaged Hardy in conversation, during which the painter relayed several strange stories.

## The Smoking Phenomenon

At some period before Glanville's November 1937 visit, Hardy was working inside the Rectory on a "bright summer afternoon." A fellow workman called him into the library and pointed out the window: a column of smoke — described in his account as a distinct "coil" or "column of smoke rising vertically from the centre of the lawn" — was visible. The phenomenon lasted for one or two minutes. Immediate investigation found no clue and no explanation was ever forthcoming.

Hardy did not speculate about the cause; he simply described what he and his colleague had seen. The "smoking phenomenon" is unique in the Borley record — no similar account is reported by any other witness.

Sceptics later dismissively argued that the men had merely seen a cloud of gnats, though defenders of the account point out that a country-born man like Hardy would easily know the difference between insects and smoke.

> **Note:** The name of Hardy's fellow workman who also witnessed the smoke column is not given in the source.

## Hardy's Assessment of the Haunting

Glanville records that Hardy "remarked that Borley Rectory had had the reputation of being haunted as long as he could remember, and that some years ago the villagers were afraid to pass it at night if they were alone." As a born-and-bred Borley resident with decades of contact with the property, Hardy provides local-knowledge context that supplements the testimony of outside investigators.

## The "Cursed" Skull Narrative

Hardy also provided a rather sensational backstory for a small human skull that had been discovered in a brown paper parcel in a library cupboard by [[mabel-smith|Mabel Smith]] in 1928. According to Hardy, previous attempts to bury the skull had resulted in such an increase in paranormal phenomena that it was thought best to return it to the house.

Commentators point out that Hardy's story appears to be an exaggeration — reportedly an attempt to add dramatic weight to the Borley events by linking them to other famous English "screaming skull" legends (such as the one at Bettiscombe Manor in Dorset). The more likely explanation, advanced by modern investigators, is that the skull was an unrecorded churchyard bone given to a previous rector for re-interment, and its presence in the cupboard was simply a practical joke played on the Smiths.

## Hardy Junior

A fortnight before Glanville's visit, Hardy's son — aged about nineteen — was returning from work at night when he heard singing or chanting coming from [[borley-church|the church]] as he passed. The building was completely dark, locked, and empty. Like many locals wary of the site's reputation, he did not stop to investigate. This incident is listed in Appendix D as "Church Music — Mr. Hardy, Junr."
