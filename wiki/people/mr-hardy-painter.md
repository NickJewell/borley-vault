# Mr. Hardy (Senior), Painter

Foreman painter; born in Borley; had lived there all his life and worked at the Rectory on many occasions. Encountered [[harry-price|Price's]] observer [[sidney-glanville]] in November 1937 when the Chelmsford Diocesan Dilapidations Board had authorised redecoration of the exterior. Sole named witness of the "smoking phenomenon." Listed in Appendix D as "Hardy, Senior" and Appendix E as "Mr. Hardy, Senior."

## The Smoking Phenomenon

At some period before Glanville's November 1937 visit, Hardy was working inside the Rectory on a "bright summer afternoon." A fellow workman called him into the library and pointed out the window: a column of smoke was rising vertically from the centre of the lawn. The phenomenon lasted for one or two minutes. Immediate investigation found no clue and no explanation was ever forthcoming.

Hardy did not speculate about the cause; he simply described what he and his colleague had seen. The "smoking phenomenon" is unique in the Borley record — no similar account is reported by any other witness — and Hardy's is the only firsthand account of it.

> **Note:** The name of Hardy's fellow workman who also witnessed the smoke column is not given in the source.

## Hardy's Assessment of the Haunting

Glanville records that Hardy "remarked that Borley Rectory had had the reputation of being haunted as long as he could remember, and that some years ago the villagers were afraid to pass it at night if they were alone." As a born-and-bred Borley resident with decades of contact with the property, Hardy provides local-knowledge context that supplements the testimony of outside investigators.

## Hardy Junior

A fortnight before Glanville's visit, Hardy's son — aged about nineteen — was returning from work at night when he heard singing or chanting coming from the church as he passed. The church was closed and empty. He did not stop to investigate. This incident is listed in Appendix D as "Church Music — Mr. Hardy, Junr."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mr. Hardy** was a local **foreman painter** whose involvement in the Borley Rectory saga occurred primarily during the Harry Price tenancy in 1937. As a lifelong local resident, his firsthand observations and the local lore he passed on to investigators provide an interesting cross-section of village beliefs regarding the haunted house. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, specific events, and narratives regarding his involvement:

**Biographical Details and Background**
*   **Residence and Experience:** Mr. Hardy was **born in Borley and lived there his entire life** [1, 2]. As a local tradesman, he had carried out work at the Rectory on numerous occasions over the years [2, 3]. 
*   **Family:** The texts note that he had a **nineteen-year-old son** at the time of the 1937 investigations [1, 3]. 

**The November 1937 Encounter and Broken Seals**
Mr. Hardy’s primary intersection with the official investigation occurred over the weekend of **November 20–21, 1937**. 
*   He and his crew had been contracted by the Chelmsford Diocesan Dilapidations Board to repaint the exterior and windows of the Rectory [2, 4]. 
*   When the chief observer Sidney Glanville arrived that Saturday morning, he was **highly annoyed to discover that Hardy's painters had broken the control seals** previously placed across the doors and windows of the house [5]. 
*   Despite this investigative setback, Glanville engaged Hardy in conversation, during which the painter relayed several strange stories [5].

**The "Column of Smoke" Phenomenon**
Hardy recounted a bizarre visual phenomenon he had witnessed "some years before" his 1937 interview with Glanville [2]. 
*   While working inside the Rectory on a bright summer afternoon, a fellow workman called him into the library [2]. 
*   Looking out the window, both men were puzzled to see a **distinct "coil" or "column of smoke rising vertically from the centre of the lawn"** [2, 3]. 
*   The smoke lasted for one or two minutes, and immediate investigation revealed no sensible reason or clue for its origin [2, 3]. Skeptics later dismissively argued that the men had merely seen a cloud of gnats, though defenders of the account point out that a country-born man like Hardy would easily know the difference between insects and smoke [3].

**The "Cursed" Skull Narrative and Contradictions**
Hardy also provided a rather sensational backstory for a small human skull that had been discovered in a brown paper parcel in a library cupboard by Mabel Smith in 1928. 
*   Hardy told Glanville that the skull had been lying around the Rectory for some time and was kept there on a permanent basis [4]. 
*   According to Hardy, **previous attempts to bury the skull had resulted in such an increase in paranormal phenomena that it was thought best to return it to the house** [4]. 
*   However, commentators point out that Hardy’s story appears to be an exaggeration. Modern investigators suggest his tale was merely **an attempt to add dramatic weight to the Borley events by linking them to other famous English "screaming skull" legends** (such as the one at Bettiscombe Manor in Dorset) [4]. The more likely explanation for the skull was that it was an unrecorded churchyard bone given to a previous rector for re-interment, and its presence in the cupboard was simply a practical joke played on the Smiths [4, 6]. 

**His Son's Experience**
Finally, Hardy shared a recent paranormal experience involving his nineteen-year-old son. About a fortnight before the November 1937 meeting, the young man was walking home from work late at night and passed Borley Church [1, 7]. Despite the building being completely dark, locked, and empty, the teenager **distinctly heard singing or chanting coming from within the fabric of the church** [1, 7]. Like many locals wary of the site's reputation, he did not stop to investigate [7].
