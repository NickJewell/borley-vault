# Sir George Whitehouse

Husband of [[lady-whitehouse]]; lived at [[arthur-hall-sudbury|Arthur Hall]] (formerly Holgate House), a late Georgian property on the Long Melford road just outside Sudbury, approximately one mile from [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] [1–3]. A civil engineer who was reportedly **knighted for building a bridge in India** [4]. He served as a **churchwarden at [[borley-church|Borley Church]]** [3, 6], and in 1929 **presented new bell ropes for Borley Church** when its three bells were dedicated by the Bishop of Colchester [7].

He died before [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] visited [[lady-whitehouse|Lady Whitehouse]] on March 29, 1939, when Price was told "with regret, that Sir George had passed away."

> **Contradiction:** The sources contain a stark contradiction regarding the date of his death. One historical entry explicitly states that "Sir George had died in March 1931" [1]. However, this is chronologically impossible, as multiple primary accounts — including Foyster's diary and Lady Whitehouse's own written testimony — place Sir George as an active participant in events on May 4, 1931 [9–12]. Other sections of the text vaguely note he died "in the 1930s" [23] or "the previous year" to Price's March 1939 interview [24], which would place his death around 1938.

## Personal Experiences at Borley

The Whitehouses were long-time friends of the [[bull-family|Bull family]] and were fully aware of the local legends regarding the haunting [7, 8]. Along with his wife, Sir George was among the few people to whom the Foysters confided their supernatural troubles, and they became primary witnesses to a spectacular outbreak of [[poltergeist-activity|phenomena]].

**May 4, 1931** — Sir George and Lady Whitehouse visited the Rectory for a church meeting (the Rural Dean also attended). When the Rural Dean left, Mrs. Foyster showed Lady Whitehouse the state of the kitchen cupboard — its contents "strewn all over the place." Sir George and [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] joined the women in the kitchen, and the bells began ringing.

After dinner they returned to support the Foysters. Lady Whitehouse writes:

> "My husband and Mr. Foyster went out to inspect the backyard... As they came in, a large flint came down the stairs by the back door."

After having tea in the kitchen, Sir George and Foyster made a tour of the house, returning minutes later to report a strange smell of burning [11, 12]. When the group investigated upstairs, they discovered the skirting-board of a locked, unused bedroom was ablaze, with a glowing hole in the middle of the wood [12, 13]. After they extinguished the fire with jugs of water, a large flint the size of a hen's egg fell among them and struck Sir George; he immediately picked it up and **kept it as a "memento and a witness"** [15, 16]. Two more flints fell before they descended. While they watched to ensure the fire was out, another flint "fell between us: the window was shut and there were no holes in the ceiling."

Believing the house entirely unsafe, Sir George and Lady Whitehouse insisted that the Foysters and their children leave the Rectory immediately and took them to stay at Arthur Hall for several days [17–19].

**A second visit, summer 1931** — The Foysters were in acute distress, and the Whitehouses drove to the Rectory at the call of Lady Whitehouse's nephew ([[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard Whitehouse]]). She describes hearing falling objects as she entered the front door, and the two small children "crouching at the dining-room door, saying: 'We don't like so many things falling.'"

## The SPR Visit (October 1931)

On **October 9, 1931**, Sir George accompanied W. H. [[william-salter|Salter]], a leading member of the Society for Psychical Research, on a visit to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] [20, 21]. After inspecting the house, they gave their professional opinion that the trouble **"must be due to human agency"**, and Salter explicitly warned Foyster that he would regret involving ghost-hunter Harry Price in the affair [20, 21].

## Marianne Foyster's Fantasies

In later years, [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] wove Sir George into her grandiose and fictitious tales about her background. She falsely claimed to friends that the Whitehouses were her cousins, and that she frequently visited their ancestral halls where she affectionately referred to Sir George as **"Georgy Porgy dear"** [22].

## Significance

Sir George Whitehouse is listed in Appendix D as a witness to bell-ringing and fire phenomena — independent confirmation of the Foyster diary accounts. He and his wife were the principal neighbours who provided the Foysters with refuge during the worst [[poltergeist-activity|poltergeist]] peaks (on at least two occasions the entire family moved temporarily to Arthur Hall).

[[lady-whitehouse]], writing to Price in 1939, describes Sir George and herself as having "never questioned the truth of what Mr. Bull and Mr. Foyster told us." She credits their sympathy as having been "a great help to the Foysters."
