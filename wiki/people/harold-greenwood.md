# Harold Gifford Greenwood

[[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster's]] first husband; married her in 1914 and disappeared a few weeks after the birth of their son, never to be seen again. His non-return — and the absence of divorce proceedings — made all of Marianne's subsequent marriages bigamous.

## Biographical Details

Born on **June 2, 1893** (some texts render the surname "Giffard"), Greenwood was an Englishman and the son of a Church of England clergyman who had died in 1909 [1–3]. He travelled to Northern Ireland to find work and became a clerk for the British Portland Cement Company — the same company where Marianne's father was employed [4].

When the 21-year-old Greenwood met the 15-year-old Marianne Shaw in 1914, they quickly fell in love [2]. Greenwood was a **"talented violinist"** who formed a music group with Marianne's parents that culminated in a large local concert [2]. Marianne recalled him fondly: **"I had never had a beau before... I thought he was beautiful, and was delighted to be the object of his affections."** [2]

## Marriage and Disappearance

The couple underwent two separate marriage ceremonies in 1914:

- **The Scottish Ceremony (June 8, 1914):** During a holiday in Scotland, the couple were married by declaration via Sheriff's warrant at Stranraer, Wigtownshire, in the presence of a lawyer's clerk and a witness [1, 4–6].
- **The Irish Ceremony (November 12, 1914):** Five months later, they were married again at St Anne's Cathedral, Belfast. For this ceremony, Greenwood gave his address as the Kensington Hotel, Belfast [6].

At both ceremonies, Marianne falsely gave her age as 17, though she was only 15 [4–6]. However, researchers note the marriage was entirely legal because the age of consent at the time was 12 for females and 14 for males [7]. The witnesses at the Belfast ceremony were Marianne's mother [[annie-shaw|Annie Shaw]] and her brother [[ian-shaw|Geoffrey Shaw]], both of whom were aware of the deception.

With financial assistance from Greenwood's parents, the young couple set up a home near Larne [3]. On **April 19, 1915**, Marianne gave birth to their only child, a son named [[ian-greenwood|Ian Geoffrey William]] [3, 5, 6]. A mere six weeks after the birth, at the end of May 1915, Greenwood abandoned his family, reportedly **"under pressure from his mother"** [1, 3, 5]. He disappeared and was never seen by Marianne again [8, 9].

## Legal Consequences

Following his disappearance, Marianne's father hired a solicitor to draw up a document of legal separation [10, 11]. However, a legal separation has never terminated a marriage under English law, and no evidence of a divorce has been found. No application was made to a court for a presumption-of-death order.

When [[lionel-foyster|the Rev. Lionel Foyster]] proposed to Marianne in 1922, she consulted a lawyer, telling him she had heard a rumour that Greenwood had died. She had written to Greenwood's sister in Blackpool for confirmation but received no reply [12]. The lawyer gave her the fateful advice that, since she had not had contact with him for over six years, she should **"take a chance on the man"** [12]. She married Foyster on 28 August 1922 without obtaining a court presumption-of-death order, rendering that union — and her subsequent marriages to [[henry-fisher|Henry Fisher]] and [[robert-oneil|Robert O'Neil]] — bigamous [10, 11].

## Significance

> **Contradiction:** Some earlier Borley sources state that all four marriages were illegal. Babbs and Mathias's legal research shows the 1914 Greenwood marriage was valid; the subsequent three were not.

The legal clarity about the Greenwood marriage (established through *Age of Marriage Act 1929* research) means that Marianne's notorious reputation as having been married four times illegally requires a small correction: the *first* marriage was in fact legal, but the second, third, and fourth were all bigamous.

## Contradictions Regarding His Later Life

Because Greenwood completely vanished from Marianne's life, the sources record several contradictory rumours regarding his ultimate fate:

- Different stories claimed he had gone to New Zealand, to Australia, or that he had remained in England [1, 3].
- Rumours circulated that he had remarried, and one of his aunts eventually wrote to Marianne years later to claim that he had died [5].
- Historical researchers eventually determined his true fate: Harold Gifford Greenwood died on **August 13, 1961** [1], and a nephew of his was successfully located in Lancashire in June 1972 [6].
