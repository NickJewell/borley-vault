# Harold Gifford Greenwood

[[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster's]] first husband; married her in 1914 and disappeared a few weeks after the birth of their son, never to be seen again. His non-return — and the absence of divorce proceedings — made all of Marianne's subsequent marriages bigamous.

## Marriage and Disappearance

Marianne married Harold Gifford Greenwood on 12 November 1914, at St Anne's Cathedral, Belfast. She was 15 years old at the time but gave her age as 17. The witnesses were her mother Annie Shaw and her brother Geoffrey Shaw, both of whom were aware of the deception. Their son [[ian-greenwood|Ian Geoffrey William Shaw]] was born on 19 April 1915. A few weeks after the birth, Harold left, under pressure from his mother according to some accounts, and was never seen again.

## Legal Consequences

Marianne's father subsequently had a legal separation drawn up by a solicitor. However, under English law, a legal separation has never terminated a marriage. No evidence of a divorce has been found. No application was made to a court for a presumption-of-death order. As Babbs and Mathias establish after consulting legal opinion, the absence of any such order means that Marianne's marriage to Harold Greenwood remained legally in force.

This in turn makes her marriage to [[lionel-foyster|the Rev Lionel Algernon Foyster]] on 28 August 1922 bigamous, as are her subsequent marriages (to Henry Fisher and to Robert O'Neil).

## Significance

The legal clarity about the Greenwood marriage (established through the *Age of Marriage Act 1929* research) means that Marianne's notorious reputation as having been married four times illegally requires a small correction: the *first* marriage was in fact legal (the age of consent for females was 12 before 1929), but the second, third, and fourth were all bigamous.

> **Contradiction:** Some earlier Borley sources state that all four marriages were illegal. Babbs and Mathias's legal research shows the 1914 Greenwood marriage was valid; the subsequent three were not.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Harold Gifford Greenwood** (also spelled Giffard in some texts) is a significant figure in the background of the Borley Rectory narrative. He was the first husband of Marianne Foyster (the wife of the Rev. Lionel Foyster), and his disappearance early in their marriage inadvertently made her subsequent marriages bigamous. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following specific facts, dates, quotes, and contradictions regarding his life and involvement:

**Biographical Details and Romance**
Born on **June 2, 1893**, Greenwood was an Englishman and the son of a Church of England clergyman who had died in 1909 [1-3]. He travelled to Northern Ireland to find work and became a clerk for the British Portland Cement Company—the same company where Marianne's father was employed [4]. 

When the 21-year-old Greenwood met the 15-year-old Marianne Shaw in 1914, they quickly fell in love [2]. Greenwood was a **"talented violinist"** who even formed a music group with Marianne's parents that culminated in a large local concert [2]. Marianne recalled him fondly, providing the specific quote: **"I had never had a beau before... I thought he was beautiful, and was delighted to be the object of his affections"** [2].

**The Double Marriage (1914)**
The couple went through two separate marriage ceremonies in 1914:
*   **The Scottish Ceremony (June 8, 1914):** During a holiday in Scotland, the couple were married by declaration via Sheriff's warrant at Stranraer, Wigtownshire, in the presence of a lawyer's clerk, James Henderson McMillen, and Maggie Isabella Glen [1, 4-6].
*   **The Irish Ceremony (November 12, 1914):** Five months later, they were married again at St Anne’s Cathedral, Belfast [1, 4-6]. For this ceremony, Greenwood gave his address as the Kensington Hotel, Belfast [6]. 

At both ceremonies, Marianne falsely gave her age as 17, though she was only 15 [4-6]. However, researchers note the marriage was entirely legal because the age of consent at the time was 12 for females and 14 for males [7]. 

**Fatherhood and Abandonment (1915)**
With financial assistance from Greenwood's parents, the young couple set up a home near Larne [3]. On **April 19, 1915**, Marianne gave birth to their only child, a son named Ian Geoffrey William [3, 5, 6]. 

However, the marriage was incredibly short-lived. A mere six weeks after the birth of his son, at the end of May 1915, Greenwood abandoned his family, reportedly **"under pressure from his mother"** [1, 3, 5]. He disappeared and was never seen by Marianne again [8, 9].

**The Bigamy Issue and Legal Advice**
Following his disappearance, Marianne's father hired a solicitor to draw up a document of legal separation [10, 11]. However, a legal separation does not terminate a marriage under English law, and researchers have never found any record of a divorce or annulment for the couple [8-11]. 

When the Rev. Lionel Foyster proposed to Marianne in 1922, she was concerned about the validity of marrying again [12]. She consulted a lawyer, telling him she had heard a rumor that Greenwood had died. She had written to Greenwood's sister in Blackpool for confirmation but received no reply [12]. The lawyer gave her the fateful advice that, since she had not had contact with him for over six years, she should **"take a chance on the man"** [12]. She married Foyster without obtaining a court presumption of death order, rendering that union (and her subsequent marriages) bigamous [10, 11].

**Contradictions Regarding His Later Life and Fate**
Greenwood is said to have gone overseas around 1920 [6]. Because he completely vanished from Marianne's life, the sources record several contradictory rumors and tales regarding his ultimate fate:
*   Different stories claimed that he had gone to New Zealand, that he had gone to Australia, or that he had remained in England [1, 3]. 
*   Rumors circulated that he had remarried, and one of his aunts eventually wrote to Marianne years later to claim that he had died [5].
*   Despite these conflicting rumors, historical researchers eventually pinned down his true fate: Harold Gifford Greenwood died on **August 13, 1961** [1], and a nephew of his was successfully located in Lancashire in June 1972 [6].
