# Mr Garnett

Landlord of lodgings in a "cheerful little house" in Westcliff-on-Sea, Essex, where journalist [[montague-elelman|Montague Elelman]] was staying after moving from his sister's London home. Described as part of a "charming middle-aged couple," Garnett and his wife were inadvertently drawn into the Borley story in 1946 when Elelman brought a piece of charred oak beam taken from the ruins of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] into the house concealed inside his luggage.

## The Phantom Doorbells (1946)

Elelman arrived on a Friday and then departed for a weekend in Surrey, leaving his belongings behind. During his absence, Mr and Mrs Garnett experienced a series of bizarre auditory phenomena:

- On both Friday and Saturday evenings, the couple was disturbed by prolonged ringing of their front doorbell at exactly 8:00 p.m. — but found no one there when they answered it.
- Suspecting practical jokers, Garnett took proactive measures on Sunday evening by closing only the glass inner door.
- At exactly 8:00 p.m. that Sunday, the bell shrilled again. Garnett hastened along the hall and witnessed firsthand that "although the ringing continued, no one was at the front door."

## The "Darkly Clad Figure"

The following Monday afternoon, while Mrs Garnett was chatting with a neighbour at the front door, the neighbour suddenly inquired about a **"darkly clad figure"** she had just glimpsed on the stair landing behind Mrs Garnett. Mrs Garnett was alone in the house at the time.

## Reactions

When Elelman returned on Monday evening, he told the Garnetts about the piece of haunted Borley wood he had brought into the house. The couple responded with measured concern:

- Mr Garnett thoughtfully noted, "In your suitcase," to which Mrs Garnett murmured, "Been there all weekend."
- Mr Garnett then pointedly asked his new lodger: "You did say that you were thinking of staying a month or two?"

Despite the unsettling weekend, nothing else strange occurred during Elelman's stay, and the Garnetts eventually "enjoyed relating the yarn to their friends." Elelman subsequently maintained that for the nine years he kept the piece of charred Borley wood, "odd and inexplicable things continued to happen around it."

No further details about the Garnett family appear in *Ghosts of Borley* or the *Borley Postscript*.
