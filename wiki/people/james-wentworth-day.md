# James Wentworth Day

Author and journalist residing in Ingatestone, Essex [1]. He witnessed what appeared to be a phantom cat at Borley Rectory about four months after the fire that destroyed it — placing the incident in late June or early July 1939 [5]. He is also the source of a second-hand account of a London journalist having the same experience. Harry Price acknowledged his contributions to *The End of Borley Rectory*, and Wentworth Day later accompanied [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] on group visits to the site [3].

## The Visit (~June–July 1939)

Wentworth Day visited the ruined rectory with a young soldier friend [4]. The night was clear, lit by what Wentworth Day described as a "full harvest moon" — an autumn event that contradicts the June/July timeline suggested by the four-months-post-fire calculation. (multiple witnesses) [1, 5]

The two men explored the roofless dining room and as much of the ground floor as they could [1]. When Wentworth Day suggested they go upstairs, his companion flatly refused, sensing **"something 'huge and black,' something that 'squatted, exuding evil'" at the top of the main stairs** [1]. The companion believed they were being watched from above. Wentworth Day raised the gun he was carrying, but his friend begged him not to shoot, fearing that firing might start "something unpleasant" and attract unwanted attention from the villagers [1].

## The Phantom Cat Sighting

Retreating from the house, the two men stood under a tree outside in the bright moonlight, both feeling a malevolent presence was watching them [6]. Suddenly, a **"gigantic black cat"** shot directly between Wentworth Day's legs. He reported a distinct physical sensation — noting he felt **"harsh bristles and snaky, undulating muscles"** as the creature brushed past him [6]. The cat rushed swiftly into the ruined building and did not emerge again [5, 6].

## The Journalist's Account

Approximately a year later, Wentworth Day spoke with a London journalist colleague who related an identical experience [6]. The journalist claimed that while standing outside the ruins at night, a huge black cat had shot between his legs "like a bullet" and hurtled into the house "like a shot from a gun," never to reappear [6]. When the journalist made local enquiries, he was assured that no black cats were kept in the vicinity, but was told that **many people who spent the night in the Rectory garden had reported seeing the exact same fast-moving cat** dart into the house without ever coming out [6, 7].

## Significance

The Wentworth Day account is one of the earlier records of what became a persistent series of phantom cat sightings at Borley. Later accounts came from [[russell-herbert-bem|Russell Herbert]] (1941, three arching cats that vanished instantly), [[james-turner|James Turner]] and his wife (1947–1950), [[hilary-wright|Hilary Wright]] (1974), and [[marie-poore|Marie Poore]] (1974).

> **Contradiction:** Wentworth Day described the night as being under a "full harvest moon" — an autumn phenomenon — despite the June/July timeline derived from the four-months-post-fire estimate [1, 5].
