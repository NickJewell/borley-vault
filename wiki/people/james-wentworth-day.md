# James Wentworth Day

Author and Essex resident who witnessed what appeared to be a phantom cat at Borley Rectory about four months after the fire that destroyed it — placing the incident in late June or early July 1939. He is also the source of a second-hand account of a London journalist having the same experience.

## The Cat Incident (~June–July 1939)

Wentworth Day visited the ruined rectory with a companion. The night was clear with good visibility. After exploring the ground floor of the ruins, Wentworth Day suggested they go upstairs; his companion refused, saying he felt there was an evil presence up there. The pair went instead into the moonlit garden.

After a short time, a **huge black cat** jumped between Wentworth Day's legs and ran into the rectory. They did not see the animal come out again. Wentworth Day related this experience to the authors of *The Ghosts of Borley* ([[peter-underwood]] and [[paul-tabori]]).

## The Journalist's Account

Wentworth Day also told the *Ghosts of Borley* authors that a London journalist colleague had exactly the same experience on another occasion. When the journalist made local enquiries about the huge black cat, he was told that no such animal was kept in the area, but that there had been many reports of such a creature being seen — always rushing into the ruined building but never coming out.

## Significance

The Wentworth Day account is one of the earlier records of what became a persistent series of reports of phantom cat sightings at Borley. Later accounts came from [[russell-herbert-bem|Russell Herbert]] (1941, three arching cats that vanished instantly), [[james-turner|James Turner]] and his wife, [[hilary-wright|Hilary Wright]] (1974), and [[marie-poore|Marie Poore]] (1974).
