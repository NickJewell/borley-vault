# William Herringham (Rector of Borley)

Rector of Borley from **1807 to 1819** (died 1819 while still holding the living), though one source dates his appointment slightly earlier to 1805 [7]. Appointed by the Waldegrave family, he was succeeded by his son [[john-herringham|John Herringham]], who held the living for a further forty-three years. Together, the two Herringhams held the Borley living continuously for approximately 55 years (1807–1862), until the living passed to the [[henry-bull|Rev Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] who built [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] on the site of the earlier Herringham residence.

## Family Life and Tragedies

William made his home at Borley with his wife **Anne**, with whom he had a large family consisting of **four sons and three daughters** [8]. The life of the rector and his family was heavily dominated by loss — specifically the **premature deaths of all three of their daughters** [8]. Two of these sisters were interred in a vault in the Borley churchyard, within sight of the Rectory, and all three are memorialized on a marble plaque inside [[borley-church|Borley Church]].

## The Herringham Rectory

The rectory that [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] replaced was almost certainly the home of the Herringham family. [[len-sewell|Leonard Sewell]] discovered this through documentary research: a tithe map of the area dated 1841 showed a building of approximately the same size as the rectory cottage in the same position, and another house occupying part of the site of the large Victorian rectory that the Rev Henry Bull would build in 1863.

> **Contradiction:** Several sources explicitly state that **William Herringham built** this residence in the early 1800s [1, 4, 7]. Conversely, other researchers note that it is "unclear which Herringham built the rectory" (William or his son John), though it was definitively standing prior to the 1841 tithe map [2, 6].

The Herringham rectory was described in 1848 as a "modern building," suggesting it was constructed in the early part of the 19th century and did not exist for very long. The much-excavated cellars under [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] were notably not in alignment with the building itself — indicating it was built on the site of an earlier house, which may have been the Herringham rectory and possibly structures before that. The Herringham rectory was eventually demolished in the early 1860s by the Rev. Henry D. E. Bull to make way for his massive red-brick mansion [1, 4].

## Paranormal Connections

Generally, there is no solid evidence to suggest that the Rectory was haunted during the Herringham period [9]. However, one Borley chronology lists a vague, lesser-known detail stating that between 1807 and 1819, William **"had experiences" that were never documented** [10].

## Significance

The Herringham incumbencies form the link between the medieval history of Borley parish and the famous Bull era. Sewell's discovery of the tithe and estate maps confirms that the pre-Victorian house on the site was of Herringham origin.
