# William Herringham (Rector of Borley)

Rector of Borley from 1807 to 1819; the father of [[john-herringham|John Herringham]] who succeeded him. Together, the two Herringhams held the Borley living continuously for 55 years (1807–1862), until the living passed to the [[henry-bull|Rev Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] who built [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] on the site of the earlier Herringham residence.

## The Herringham Rectory

The rectory that Borley Rectory replaced was almost certainly the home of the Herringham family. [[len-sewell|Leonard Sewell]] discovered this through documentary research: a tithe map of the area dated 1841 showed a building of approximately the same size as the rectory cottage in the same position, and another house occupying part of the site of the large Victorian rectory that the Rev Henry Bull would have built in 1863.

The Herringham rectory was described in 1848 as being a "modern building," suggesting it was constructed in the early part of the 19th century and did not exist for very long. The much-excavated cellars under Borley Rectory were notably not in alignment with the building itself, which indicates that it was built on the site of an earlier house — the Herringham rectory, and possibly structures before that.

## Significance

The Herringham incumbencies form the link between the medieval history of Borley parish and the famous Bull era. Sewell's discovery of the tithe and estate maps confirms that the pre-Victorian house on the site was of Herringham origin.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**William Herringham** was an early nineteenth-century Rector of Borley whose tenure established the first of the parish's two notable ecclesiastical "dynasties" (the second being the famous Bull family) [1]. While his historical footprint in the Borley saga is smaller than that of later rectors, the sources record several specific biographical details, tragedies, and chronological contradictions regarding his life and incumbency. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following:

**Incumbency Dates and Contradictions**
*   **Dates of Service:** The vast majority of the Borley sources record William Herringham's incumbency as beginning in **1807** [1-6]. However, there is a minor contradiction in the texts, with one source stating that he assumed the rectory at Borley slightly earlier, in **1805** [7]. 
*   **Succession:** His tenure ended with his death in **1819**, at which point the Borley living was passed down to his son, the Rev. John Philip (Joh. P.) Herringham, who would go on to hold the post for the next forty-three years [1-6, 8]. 

**Family Life and Tragedies**
*   William made his home at Borley with his wife, **Anne** [7]. 
*   Together, William and Anne had a large family consisting of **four sons and three daughters** [8]. 
*   **Premature Deaths:** The life of the rector and his family was heavily dominated by loss, specifically the **premature deaths of all three of their daughters** [8]. Two of these sisters were interred in a vault in the Borley churchyard, located within sight of the Rectory, and all three are memorialized on a marble plaque inside Borley Church [8].

**The Building of the "Herringham Rectory"**
William Herringham is primarily remembered for his connection to the "Herringham Rectory"—the Georgian building that immediately preceded the infamous Victorian "haunted" Rectory built by the Bulls. 
*   During a period of national improvement and rebuilding for rural clergy, this second rectory was constructed on the site [7].
*   **The Construction Contradiction:** There is some disagreement in the sources over who actually built this house. Several sources explicitly state that **William Herringham built it** in the early 1800s to serve as his family home [1, 4, 7]. Conversely, other researchers note that it is **"unclear which Herringham built the rectory"** (William or his son John), though it was definitively standing prior to an 1841 tithe map [2, 6]. 
*   Regardless of which man laid the bricks, the Herringham Rectory was eventually demolished in the early 1860s by the Rev. Henry D. E. Bull to make way for his massive red-brick mansion [1, 4]. 

**Paranormal Connections**
Generally, there is no solid evidence to suggest that the Rectory was haunted during the Herringham period [9]. However, one Borley chronology lists a vague, lesser-known detail stating that between 1807 and 1819, William **"had experiences" that were never documented** [10].
