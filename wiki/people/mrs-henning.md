# Mrs Henning (Annette Henning)

Full name **Annette Henning**; wife of [[rev-ac-henning|the Rev Alfred Clifford Henning]], rector of Borley and Liston from 1936 until his death in January 1955. Per O'Connor (*hbrs-ch14*), when Henning first showed Price around the Rectory in May 1937, he noted that his wife had "been longing to escape their tiny flat to make a new start in a country parish" — but had immediately exclaimed *"Not Borley! We don't want to start off in a place with a reputation like that."* Remembered by [[claudine-mathias|Claudine Mathias]] as a popular and practical woman who undertook much of the pastoral visiting that her husband's obsession with the alleged haunting prevented him from doing.

## Role in the Henning Incumbency

Many parishioners felt that Rev Henning tended to neglect his official duties because of his intense preoccupation with the Borley haunting. His wife compensated for this by undertaking a significant amount of visiting and other parochial work in both Borley and Liston. She was popular with the parishioners in a way that he was not universally.

## Personal Experiences at Borley

Mrs Henning was present during several of the significant incidents her husband described:

- She was at Borley Rectory in July 1937 with her husband and [[mark-kerr-pearse|Mark Kerr-Pearse]] when they heard footsteps and a swishing sound approaching down the stone-flagged passage from the kitchen quarters — followed by silence when they opened the door.
- She found an old tattered woman's coat hanging on the door of one of the bedrooms; she asked [[herbert-mayes|Herbert Mayes]] to burn it.
- She was present when the stone altar investigation was conducted in 1943 and on subsequent occasions.
- She drove Harry Price to the church on 4 June 1947 when he wished to see the restored altar stone before his Sudbury lecture.

## After Henning's Death

After her husband's unexpected death in January 1955 from a spell of exceptionally cold weather, Mrs Henning moved away from the area. She later returned to the nearby large village of Clare. [[claudine-mathias|CM]], who knew her, regarded her as a truthful and down-to-earth person. She was convinced of the genuineness of at least some of the Borley phenomena and continued to lecture occasionally on the alleged haunting.

Her most memorable public statement was in response to the 1956 publication of the SPR's critical *Haunting of Borley Rectory* report, when she declared it "a dastardly attack on Mr Price when he is no longer here to defend himself."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mrs. Annette Eva Henning** (a B.A. of London University) was the wife of the Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning, the rector of Borley-cum-Liston from 1936 to 1955 [1, 2]. Remembered as "hospitable, much-travelled, and well-educated," as well as a person of deep integrity, she was a central and highly active participant in the later years of the Borley Rectory investigation [2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, specific events, quotes, and her staunch defense of investigator Harry Price:

**Arrival and Role in the Parish**
*   **Initial Reluctance:** Annette Henning married the Rev. Henning in 1934, and they had two sons, Richard and Stephen [3, 4]. When her husband was offered Borley as his first parish in 1936, Annette was initially appalled by its notoriety, exclaiming, **"Not Borley! We don't want to start off in a place with a reputation like that"** [4]. 
*   **Residence and Duties:** Because of the Rectory's massive size and dilapidated state, the Hennings chose to live first with the Payne family at Borley Place, and subsequently moved into the more comfortable Liston Rectory [5]. While her husband was sometimes unpopular with parishioners for allegedly neglecting his duties in favor of ghost-hunting, Annette was highly popular and undertook much of the parish visiting and parochial work [6].

**Direct Paranormal Experiences**
Despite her initial reluctance, Mrs. Henning became convinced of the hauntings through firsthand experiences at the Rectory and the nearby church:
*   **The Swishing Footsteps (July 16, 1937):** While sitting in the Rectory's Base Room with her husband and investigator Mark Kerr-Pearse, Mrs. Henning heard the sound of a door opening followed by footsteps and a "swishing noise" like long trailing garments coming down the passage. When they flung the door open to investigate, the sounds instantly ceased, and the passage was empty [7-9]. 
*   **The "Strange Coat" Contradiction:** The sources record a minor contradiction regarding the famous "strange coat" found at the Rectory. While Harry Price discovered a blue serge coat, Mrs. Henning reported finding an "old tattered jacket" hanging behind a different bedroom door, which she explicitly instructed their gardener, Herbert Mayes, to burn [10, 11].
*   **The "Cold Spot" (August 1937):** While showing a friend, Miss Alice Reid, around the Rectory, Miss Reid suffered a terrifying reaction to a specific spot outside the Blue Room. Mrs. Henning touched her friend's hands and confirmed they were indeed "icy cold" despite the warm summer evening [12, 13].
*   **Church Phenomena:** After the Rectory burned down, she and her husband continued to experience phenomena, including hearing unexplained organ music, phantom bell-ringing, and an incident in autumn 1949 where the church door refused to lock, **"as if someone was holding the lock back"** [14, 15].

**Lectures and Public Speaking**
Mrs. Henning became a frequent and popular lecturer on the Borley hauntings:
*   **The Cambridge Lecture:** On **April 18, 1945**, she addressed the Cambridge Women’s Luncheon Club. She matter-of-factly detailed the phenomena, describing the ghost nun's sad appearance and the swishing of her garments, noting, **"I have heard these myself, and it made my flesh creep"** [16]. She also amused her audience by recounting how a tin trunk suddenly materialized in the kitchen during a meal [17]. In a striking coincidence, the former Borley rector, the Rev. Lionel Foyster, died on the exact same day she gave this talk [18, 19].
*   **Local Confidences:** When she gave talks to local Women's Institutes, women from the village would approach her afterward under a pledge of secrecy to share stories of similar paranormal happenings in their own homes [20]. 

**Staunch Defense of Harry Price**
Following Harry Price's death in 1948, the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) published the highly critical *Borley Report* (1956), which accused Price of fraud. Annette Henning emerged as one of Price's fiercest defenders:
*   **Public Condemnation of Critics:** On Christmas Eve 1954, after seeing a preview of the SPR's allegations, she told a national newspaper: **"My husband and I think that this investigation is a dastardly attack on Mr Price when he is no longer here to defend himself"** [21]. 
*   **Private Correspondence:** In a February 20, 1956 letter to Peter Underwood, she called the SPR document **"a hateful book,"** arguing, **"If anyone is to conform to SPR standards, then one must believe next to nothing anyone says. There is so much—too much—to question"** [22].
*   **Refuting the Bone-Planting Allegations:** The SPR strongly implied Price had fraudulently planted the human skull and jawbone found in the cellars in August 1943. In correspondence with Mrs. C. C. Baines, Mrs. Henning firmly rejected this: **"I don't think it is likely that Mr. Price planted the skull and other bones. He had a weak heart and could not dig... I don't think villagers would have lent themselves to such fraud."** [23]. (Mrs. Henning herself had provided sandwiches and drinks for Price and his team during the dig [24]). 
*   **Defending Price's "Treachery":** The SPR also accused Price of "calculated treachery" for not inviting his chief observer, Sidney Glanville, to the 1943 excavations. Mrs. Henning provided the mundane logistical reason for this: wartime conditions meant the local hotel was full of officers, and she had **"only two spare rooms"** at Liston Rectory, which were already occupied by Price and his secretary [23, 25]. 

**Later Life**
Mrs. Henning was present when the excavated Borley bones were given a Christian burial in Liston churchyard on **May 29, 1945** [26-28]. Her husband died suddenly on January 14, 1955. Following his death, she briefly left the area but eventually returned to live in the large village of Clare, Suffolk [2, 29]. She remained entirely convinced of the genuineness of the Borley hauntings and continued to lecture occasionally on the subject until her death on **May 29, 1964** [2, 29, 30].
