# Mrs Henning (Annette Henning)

Full name **Annette Eva Henning**, B.A. (London); wife of [[rev-ac-henning|the Rev Alfred Clifford Henning]], rector of Borley and Liston from 1936 until his death in January 1955. Remembered as "hospitable, much-travelled, and well-educated," as well as a person of deep integrity. She married Henning in 1934; they had two sons, Richard and Stephen.

When Henning was first offered Borley as his first parish in 1936, Annette was immediately appalled by its notoriety, exclaiming: **"Not Borley! We don't want to start off in a place with a reputation like that."** Because of the Rectory's massive size and dilapidated state, the Hennings chose to live first with the Payne family at Borley Place, and subsequently moved into the more comfortable Liston Rectory.

## Role in the Parish

Many parishioners felt that Rev Henning tended to neglect his official duties because of his intense preoccupation with the Borley haunting. His wife compensated by undertaking a significant amount of visiting and other parochial work in both Borley and Liston. She was popular with the parishioners in a way that he was not universally.

## Personal Paranormal Experiences

Despite her initial reluctance, Mrs Henning became convinced of the hauntings through firsthand experiences:

- **The Swishing Footsteps (July 16, 1937):** While sitting in the Rectory's Base Room with her husband and investigator [[mark-kerr-pearse|Mark Kerr-Pearse]], she heard the sound of a door opening followed by footsteps and a "swishing noise" like long trailing garments coming down the passage. When they flung the door open to investigate, the sounds instantly ceased and the passage was empty.
- **The "Strange Coat" (disputed):** Mrs Henning reported finding an "old tattered jacket" hanging behind a bedroom door, which she explicitly instructed their gardener, [[herbert-mayes|Herbert Mayes]], to burn.

> **Contradiction:** The sources record a minor contradiction regarding the coat found at the Rectory. While [[harry-price|Harry Price]] discovered a blue serge coat, Mrs Henning reported finding an "old tattered jacket" hanging behind a different bedroom door.

- **The "Cold Spot" (August 1937):** While showing a friend, Miss Alice Reid, around the Rectory, Miss Reid suffered a terrifying reaction to a specific spot outside the [[blue-room|Blue Room]]. Mrs Henning touched her friend's hands and confirmed they were "icy cold" despite the warm summer evening.
- **Church Phenomena:** After the Rectory burned down, she and her husband continued to experience phenomena, including unexplained organ music, phantom bell-ringing, and an incident in autumn 1949 where the church door refused to lock, "as if someone was holding the lock back."

She was present when the stone altar investigation was conducted in 1943 and on subsequent occasions, and drove [[harry-price|Harry Price]] to the church on 4 June 1947 when he wished to see the restored altar stone before his Sudbury lecture.

## Lectures and Public Speaking

Mrs Henning became a frequent and popular lecturer on the Borley hauntings:

- **The Cambridge Lecture (April 18, 1945):** She addressed the Cambridge Women's Luncheon Club, matter-of-factly describing the phenomena and noting, "I have heard these myself, and it made my flesh creep." She also recounted how a tin trunk suddenly materialised in the kitchen during a meal. In a striking coincidence, the former Borley rector the Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] died on the exact same day she gave this talk.
- **Local Confidences:** When she gave talks to local Women's Institutes, women would approach her afterward under a pledge of secrecy to share stories of similar paranormal happenings in their own homes.

## Staunch Defence of Harry Price

Following [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s death in 1948, the SPR published the highly critical *Borley Report* (1956), which accused Price of fraud. Mrs Henning emerged as one of Price's fiercest defenders:

- **Public Condemnation:** On Christmas Eve 1954, after seeing a preview of the SPR's allegations, she told a national newspaper: "My husband and I think that this investigation is a dastardly attack on Mr Price when he is no longer here to defend himself."
- **Private Correspondence:** In a February 20, 1956 letter to [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]], she called the SPR document "a hateful book," arguing: "If anyone is to conform to SPR standards, then one must believe next to nothing anyone says. There is so much — too much — to question."
- **Refuting the Bone-Planting Allegations:** The SPR implied Price had fraudulently planted the human skull and jawbone found in the cellars in August 1943. Mrs Henning firmly rejected this in correspondence with [[mrs-cc-baines|Mrs C. C. Baines]]: "I don't think it is likely that Mr. Price planted the skull and other bones. He had a weak heart and could not dig... I don't think villagers would have lent themselves to such fraud." (She herself had provided sandwiches and drinks for Price and his team during the dig.)
- **Defending Price's "Treachery":** The SPR accused Price of "calculated treachery" for not inviting his chief observer [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] to the 1943 excavations. Mrs Henning provided the mundane logistical reason: wartime conditions meant the local hotel was full of officers, and she had "only two spare rooms" at Liston Rectory, already occupied by Price and his secretary.

## Later Life

Mrs Henning was present when the excavated Borley bones were given a Christian burial in Liston churchyard on **May 29, 1945**. Her husband died suddenly on January 14, 1955. Following his death, she briefly left the area but eventually returned to live in the large village of [[borley-church|Clare]], Suffolk. She remained entirely convinced of the genuineness of the Borley hauntings and continued to lecture occasionally on the subject until her death on **May 29, 1964**.
