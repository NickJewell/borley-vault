# Lady Florence Whitehouse

Wife of Sir George Whitehouse of [[arthur-hall-sudbury|Arthur Hall]], Sudbury — approximately one mile from [[borley-rectory]]. Long-time friends of the Bull family and subsequently of the Foysters, the Whitehouses were familiar with the Rectory's legends; Sir George served as a churchwarden at [[borley-church|Borley Church]]. Lady Whitehouse is the aunt of [[dom-richard-whitehouse]]. She provided a signed protocol of her personal experiences at the Rectory during the Foyster period. Sir George died before Price and Glanville called in March 1939; Price interviewed Lady Whitehouse alone.

## Personality

Lady Whitehouse was widely regarded as a sensible, shrewd, and down-to-earth woman who would not be easily fooled by trickery. [[ethel-bull|Miss Ethel Bull]] described her as "a charming and kind person." Although she accepted that the Rectory was haunted, she initially believed that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster's]] fainting spells were genuine trances and that the young woman was naturally "mediumistic."

Her opening note to Price: "I do feel rather pleased that we never questioned the truth of what Mr. Bull and Mr. Foyster told us, and I think our sympathy was a great help to the Foysters."

## Phenomena Witnessed

**First visit (May 4, 1931):** On hearing of recent phenomena, Lady Whitehouse suggested fumigating the house with lavender: **"I will bring some lavender stalks in case they are needed... I think they will be better to burn than creosote."** When she and her husband returned that evening, the bells began to ring — prompting Marianne to say, "We are in for a bad time to-night." During inspection, a strange smell led the party upstairs where they discovered the skirting board of a locked, unused room on fire. After the flames were extinguished, Lady Whitehouse attempted to exorcise the house by scattering her dried lavender on glowing embers — an act that was immediately followed by a shower of stones and pebbles. Believing Marianne too exhausted to remain, Lady Whitehouse insisted the Foysters pack up the children and take sanctuary at Arthur Hall. She took the Foyster household with them that night.

**Second visit (summer 1931, June 8, 1931):** Her nephew [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin]] summoned her urgently — she arrived to find two small children crouching at the dining-room door saying "We don't like so many things falling." Found Mrs. Foyster in bed; as Lady Whitehouse left the room to fetch clothes, Marianne called **"Your things are going!"** — gloves and parasol had moved to the dressing-table. She also witnessed a small glass bottle apparently "start from the middle of the room" and fall at her feet. On the next trip, she heard a yell and found Marianne thrown from her bed for the third time that day. She later assured investigator [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] of the absolute truth of the floating medicine bottle incident and offered to swear an affidavit to that effect. She took the family to Arthur Hall again.

## Contradictions and the SPR Investigation

**The Delay in Testimony:** Price did not ask Lady Whitehouse for a formal written statement until **March 29, 1939** — eight years after the events took place. She sent her statement on **April 2, 1939**, noting: "They seemed very thrilling and exciting at the time, but in the light of later happenings, fade almost into insignificance."

**The Fire Contradiction:** The [[the-borley-report|SPR *Borley Report*]] highlighted a major contradiction between Lady Whitehouse's 1939 memory of the May 4 fire and [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster's]] contemporary diary. Lady Whitehouse's account implied that Marianne was with her in the kitchen (and thus under observation) when the men first smelled smoke. Foyster's diary, however, recorded that he and Sir George had gone to investigate the house, smelled the smoke, and *then* returned to the kitchen to inform the women.

**The "Corroboration" Error:** Price claimed Lady Whitehouse's testimony corroborated the sensational claims of her nephew Edwin Whitehouse. The SPR pointed out this was carelessly inaccurate or intentionally misleading, as **Edwin was not present during either of the specific visits (May 4 and the summer of 1931) that Lady Whitehouse recorded in her statement**.

## The Rift with Marianne and Edwin Whitehouse

Lady Whitehouse had introduced her nephew, Edwin, to the Rectory as a place to recuperate from a nervous breakdown. Although Edwin became fascinated by the haunting and an ardent defender of Marianne, Lady Whitehouse grew deeply concerned. She suspected Marianne had designs on her nephew, calling her **"man mad."** Feeling the bizarre atmosphere at Borley "did Edwin no good at all," she and Sir George eventually banned him from visiting the Rectory or staying at Arthur Hall.

Despite her eventual belief that Marianne was "mad," Lady Whitehouse never wavered in her conviction that the phenomena she personally witnessed were the result of genuine supernatural forces. She later supplied material for a 1947 BBC radio broadcast about the haunting, where her testimony was read by an actress.

## Significance

An independent, socially prominent witness with no interest in psychical research. Her account corroborates the Foyster diary entries and Dom Richard's account on specific incidents. She was present at the skirting-board fire (also recorded by Dom Richard) and witnessed objects thrown with the window shut and ceiling intact — a controlled circumstance difficult to explain by normal means.
