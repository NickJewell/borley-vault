Wife of Sir George Whitehouse of Arthur Hall, Sudbury — approximately one mile from [[borley-rectory]]. Long-time friends of the Bull family and subsequently of the Foysters. Lady Whitehouse is the aunt of [[dom-richard-whitehouse]]. She provided a signed protocol of her personal experiences at the Rectory during the Foyster period. Sir George died before Price and Glanville called in March 1939; Price interviewed Lady Whitehouse alone.

Her opening note to Price: "I do feel rather pleased that we never questioned the truth of what Mr. Bull and Mr. Foyster told us, and I think our sympathy was a great help to the Foysters."

## Phenomena Witnessed

**First visit (May 4, 1931)**: With Sir George and nephew [[dom-richard-whitehouse]]. Saw cupboard contents strewn everywhere; bells began ringing. During inspection found a skirting board on fire in an unused room — helped pour water on it. As they stood watching to confirm it was extinguished, "a flint about the size of a hen's egg fell between us: the window was shut and there were no holes in the ceiling." Two more flints fell descending the stairs. Then, during lavender fumigation, "a shower of little stones all round us." Departed at 11:30 p.m., bringing the Foyster household with them to Arthur Hall.

**Second visit (summer 1931)**: Her nephew summoned her urgently — she arrived to find two small children crouching at the dining-room door saying "We don't like so many things falling." Found Mrs. Foyster in bed; as Lady Whitehouse left the room, Mrs. Foyster called "Your things are going!" — gloves and parasol had moved to the dressing-table. Returned with tea; a small glass bottle "seemed to start from the middle of the room" and fell at her feet. On the next trip, she heard a yell and found Marianne thrown from her bed for the third time that day. She took the family to Arthur Hall again.

## Significance

An independent, socially prominent witness with no interest in psychical research. Her account corroborates the Foyster diary entries and Dom Richard's account on specific incidents. She was present at the skirting-board fire (also recorded by Dom Richard) and witnessed objects thrown with the window shut and ceiling intact — a controlled circumstance difficult to explain by normal means.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Lady Florence Whitehouse** (wife of Sir George Whitehouse) was a highly respected neighbor and frequent visitor to Borley Rectory during the tumultuous Foyster incumbency. Residing at Arthur Hall, Sudbury, the Whitehouses were long-time friends of the Bull family and were familiar with the Rectory’s legends, as Sir George served as a churchwarden at Borley Church [1, 2].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, specific events, quotes, and contradictions regarding her involvement in the case:

**Personality and Skepticism**
Lady Whitehouse was widely regarded as a sensible, shrewd, and down-to-earth woman who would not be easily fooled by trickery [3-5]. Miss Ethel Bull described her as **"a charming and kind person"** [3, 6]. Although she accepted that the Rectory was haunted, she initially believed that Marianne Foyster’s fainting spells were genuine trances and that the young woman was naturally **"mediumistic"** [7-9]. 

**The May 1931 Fire and Lavender Exorcism**
On **May 4, 1931**, the Whitehouses visited the Rectory to meet the Rural Dean [8, 10]. Hearing of the recent phenomena, Lady Whitehouse suggested fumigating the house, offering this specific quote: 
> **"I will bring some lavender stalks in case they are needed... I think they will be better to burn than creosote"** [11]. 

When she and her husband returned that evening, the bells began to ring, prompting Marianne to say, "We are in for a bad time to-night" [10]. During this visit, a strange smell led the party upstairs, where they discovered the skirting board of a locked, unused room on fire [10, 12]. After the flames were extinguished, Lady Whitehouse attempted to exorcize the house by scattering her dried lavender on glowing embers, an act that was immediately followed by a shower of stones and pebbles [13, 14]. Believing Marianne was too exhausted to remain in the house, Lady Whitehouse insisted the Foysters pack up the children and leave immediately to take sanctuary at Arthur Hall [15-17].

**The June 1931 Flying Objects**
On **June 8, 1931**, Lady Whitehouse witnessed further startling phenomena. Visiting Marianne, who was ill in bed, Lady Whitehouse placed her gloves and parasol on the bed. As she left the room to fetch clothes, Marianne cried out, **"Your things are going!"** [5, 18]. Lady Whitehouse turned back to find her items had inexplicably moved to the dressing table [18]. She also reported seeing a small glass bottle jump from the middle of the room and fall at her feet, and subsequently found Marianne thrown out of bed onto the floor for the third time that day [18, 19]. She later assured investigator Sidney Glanville of the absolute truth of the floating medicine bottle incident and offered to swear an affidavit to that effect [20].

**Contradictions and the SPR Investigation**
Lady Whitehouse’s testimony became a battleground for critics of Harry Price. 
*   **The Delay in Testimony:** Price did not ask Lady Whitehouse for a formal written statement until **March 29, 1939**—eight years after the events took place [21, 22]. Sending her statement on **April 2, 1939**, she wrote: **"They seemed very thrilling and exciting at the time, but in the light of later happenings, fade almost into insignificance. But I do feel rather pleased that we never questioned the truth of what Mr. Bull and Mr. Foyster told us, and I think our sympathy was a great help to the Foysters"** [23].
*   **The Fire Contradiction:** The SPR authors of the *Borley Report* highlighted a major contradiction between Lady Whitehouse's 1939 memory of the May 4 fire and Lionel Foyster’s contemporary diary [24, 25]. Lady Whitehouse's account implied that Marianne was with her in the kitchen (and thus under observation) when the men first smelled smoke [24, 26]. Foyster’s diary, however, recorded that he and Sir George had gone to investigate the house, smelled the smoke, and *then* returned to the kitchen to inform the women [24]. 
*   **The "Corroboration" Error:** Price claimed in his book that Lady Whitehouse's testimony corroborated the sensational claims of her nephew, Edwin Whitehouse. The SPR pointed out this was carelessly inaccurate or intentionally misleading, as **Edwin was not present during either of the specific visits (May 4 and the summer of 1931) that Lady Whitehouse recorded in her statement** [27].

**The Rift with Marianne and Edwin Whitehouse**
Lady Whitehouse had introduced her nephew, Edwin, to the Rectory as a place to recuperate from a nervous breakdown [28, 29]. Although Edwin became fascinated by the haunting and an ardent defender of Marianne, Lady Whitehouse grew deeply concerned. She suspected Marianne had designs on her nephew, calling her **"man mad"** [30, 31]. Feeling that the bizarre atmosphere at Borley "did Edwin no good at all," she and Sir George eventually banned him from visiting the Rectory or staying at Arthur Hall [30, 32]. 

Despite her eventual belief that Marianne was "mad," Lady Whitehouse never wavered in her conviction that the phenomena she personally witnessed at the Rectory were the result of genuine supernatural forces [9]. She later supplied material for a 1947 BBC radio broadcast about the haunting, where her testimony was read by an actress [3, 33].
