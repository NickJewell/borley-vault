# Sidney H. Glanville

[[harry-price|Harry Price's]] chief associate in the Borley investigation and one of three principal official observers. A meticulous, methodical collaborator who contributed some of the most technically significant evidence in the case.

## Role

Glanville worked alongside Price throughout the investigation, gathering testimony from witnesses including the Bull family, interviewing the Smiths, and compiling the detailed architectural plans of the Rectory that Price reproduces in *The Most Haunted House in England*. Price described him as one of the observers to whom his "thanks are specially due" — noting that they were strangers before the investigation began, which makes his commitment the more notable.

## The Architectural Plans

Glanville produced careful floor plans of both the ground floor and first floor of the Rectory — the definitive reference documents for the building's layout. These plans were used by all subsequent investigators and are referenced throughout the case literature.

## The Cellar Excavation (1 November 1937)

Glanville and Mark Kerr-Pearse excavated the subsided portion of the cellar floor. They found:
- Foundations of a much older building beneath the 1863 structure — two-inch bricks, not the modern type
- A cellar wall running diagonally under the library composed entirely of these older bricks
- The older foundations faced roughly south, suggesting a pre-Rectory structure of different orientation

Price established a direct correlation: the "cold spot" on the first-floor landing outside the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] sits directly above this ancient foundation. See [[borley-rectory]].

## The Well

At 61.9 feet below ground level, Glanville's flare and sinker were sucked into a cavity in the well's inner wall — suggesting a passage or tunnel leading off the shaft. This connects to the subterranean passage investigations covered in [[gotb-ch05]].

## The Locked Book

Glanville compiled the *Locked Book* — the systematic record of the observer programme during Price's tenancy (1937–38). It is described as a key primary document in the case. The wall-writings retouching controversy is partly resolved by the *Locked Book*: the idea of strengthening the writings for photography originated in Glanville's own letter to Price, not at Price's initiative. See [[wall-writings]].

## Observer Program Contributions (1937–38)

As the first official observer (beginning June 19, 1937), Glanville and son Roger witnessed:
- **Paranormal pencil markings**: Photographed the "Marianne get help" message; an hour later found new markings adjacent to it; re-photographed — the new marks appear in the second photograph only. The house was locked and sealed; the Glanvilles were always together.
- **The cats' cemetery**: Found that the grave of cat "Gem" at the end of the Nun's Walk had been extensively disturbed — headboard thrown into a bush, a large area dug and refilled. He excavated the same area and found large bones "that certainly never formed part of the anatomy of a cat." Who disturbed it and why was never established.
- **The tobacco tin incident**: Glanville secretly pencilled a tiny cross on the floor at the "cold spot" on the landing outside the Blue Room — known only to him, his son, and Dr. Bellamy. On a subsequent visit they found that the flat tobacco tin (left as a control object on the drawing-room mantelpiece by Kerr-Pearse) had been moved upstairs and placed "with mathematical precision" directly over that cross.
- **The swinging blind**: The Base Room blind was observed by four investigators (Glanville, his son, Captain Harrison, and Dr. Bellamy) palpitating and waving regularly — though cigarette smoke showed no draught in the gap between blind and window frame.

## The Séances (October–November 1937)

Glanville built a special lightweight table for table-tipping sessions at the Rectory. His son Roger, [[mark-kerr-pearse]], and Alan Cuthbert conducted sessions producing entities claiming to be [[harry-bull]] and a monk named "Fadenoch." The Harry Bull entity specified the nun's burial location: south-west of the house, within 2 yards of a large fir-tree, under a stone marked "B." See [[mhh-ch25]].

## Helen Glanville (daughter)

See [[helen-glanville]]. Her solo Planchette sessions (October 28 and 31, 1937) — conducted at home in Streatham with no prior information — produced the name "Mary Lairre," a French origin, a death date (May 17, 1667), strangulation by a Waldegrave, and a request for Mass. On March 27, 1938, a second session produced the "Sunex Amures" fire prediction, fulfilled eleven months later. See [[marie-lairre]] and [[fire-1939]].

## The Locked Book Affair (brc-ii-gh)

The Companion provides the most detailed account of the Locked Book's fate. After Price's death (1948), Glanville borrowed the Locked Book from the University of London for a projected *Borley Symposium*. In 1951 [[trevor-hall]] was introduced to him by [[eric-dingwall]] and they became close — Hall made multiple trips to Glanville's cottage at Fittleworth to discuss Borley.

By 1953 Glanville was seriously ill and "not thinking clearly." Hall visited him repeatedly and "insisted" that statements favourable to Glanville be inserted in the forthcoming [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]]. On 27 July 1953, Glanville inscribed and gave the Locked Book to Hall — writing an inscription that [[alan-wesencraft]] described as "pathetic... written by a man in very bad health under Hall's insistent browbeating." Glanville died five months later.

Hall subsequently sold the original to an American dealer for £1,000 and made four typescript copies. The University of London protested in a formal 1959 memo but did not pursue legal recovery. Wesencraft's verdict: Hall *stole* it.

Full history at [[locked-book]].

## SPR Report and His Testimony

The Borley Report (1956) acknowledged, in one paragraph, that Glanville's observations "constitutes one of the most puzzling features of the whole case." This was, according to Glanville himself, the result of him fighting hard for even that minimal concession. He was devastated by the Report's general dismissal of his years of careful work and told Peter Underwood it was "fatal to have anything to do with Borley."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Sidney Herbert Glanville** (born in Stratford, East London, on January 30, 1884; died in Fittleworth, West Sussex, on December 10, 1953) is arguably the most important lay investigator in the history of the Borley Rectory hauntings [1, 2]. A retired machine tool engineer’s agent, consulting engineer, and former architect's articled clerk, Glanville served as Harry Price’s “Chief Observer” during the 1937–1938 tenancy investigation [1, 3, 4]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, dates, quotes, and controversies regarding his involvement:

**Entering the Investigation**
Glanville was the very first person to respond to Harry Price’s advertisement in *The Times* on May 25, 1937, asking for "intrepid" and "unbiased" observers [3, 5, 6]. In his application, he noted that he had been "an interested observer of the supernormal" for several years and had experience with spiritualist meetings and automatic writing [3, 6]. He began his first observational vigil at Borley Rectory with his son, Roger H. Glanville, on June 19–20, 1937, and ultimately visited the site over a dozen times [6-8].

**Methodology, Floor Plans, and Equipment**
Glanville approached the haunting with intense professionalism. His contributions to the investigation included:
*   **Floor Plans:** Utilizing his early training as an architect's clerk, he took detailed measurements of the sprawling Rectory during his first visits and drew the highly accurate floor plans and site maps that were subsequently reproduced in all of Harry Price’s books [3, 9-12].
*   **Control Objects:** He invented a tracking device consisting of a small aluminum cup filled with corundum (aluminum oxide) powder, with a hole drilled in the bottom so that any movement would leave a visible trail [13]. 
*   **Séance Table:** He constructed a lightweight table (measuring 12 inches square and 2 feet 2 inches high) explicitly for the purpose of conducting table-tipping experiments at the Rectory [14, 15]. 
*   **Interviews:** Glanville independently tracked down and interviewed several key witnesses. On October 6, 1937, he conducted a lengthy interview with the Rev. Guy and Mabel Smith at Sevington Rectory, and on June 25, 1938, he interviewed the Misses Bull, recording their various (and sometimes malicious) claims [16-18]. He also maintained a vital correspondence with the retired Rev. Lionel Foyster, reading Foyster's manuscript *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House* and declaring it **"the most astonishing document I have ever read"** [19, 20].

**The Wall Writings Contradiction**
Glanville meticulously traced and photographed the mysterious penciled messages on the Rectory walls [21]. Years later, skeptical investigators accused Harry Price of cynically instructing Glanville to "re-touch" the writings to alter their meaning. In reality, correspondence proves that Glanville himself first suggested this action. On October 28, 1937, Glanville wrote to Price: **"Do you think it would be advisable to carefully bring the old writing, just visible now, up to a state where it would be \[photographically\] possible?"** Price simply agreed to the suggestion [22]. 

**Excavations**
Glanville actively dug on the Rectory site on multiple occasions:
*   **The Cats' Cemetery:** On August 14, 1937, Glanville noticed that the cats' cemetery had been mysteriously disturbed. He and his son re-dug the area, unearthing large bones that Glanville noted "certainly never formed part of the anatomy of a cat" (likely horse or ox bones) [23-25].
*   **The Cellar Footings:** On November 1, 1937, Glanville and Mark Kerr-Pearse excavated a subsidence in the cellar floor directly beneath the famous first-floor "cold spot," discovering the foundations of an older building composed of two-inch Tudor bricks [26, 27]. 
*   **The 1943 Excavation Slight:** When Harry Price excavated the cellars and famously discovered human remains on August 17, 1943, he entirely failed to inform or invite Glanville. Glanville later admitted that this omission was a "considerable surprise and disappointment to him" [28]. He and his son nonetheless traveled to Borley to dig in the cellars on August 20 and August 30 [29, 30].

**Planchette Séances and "Marie Lairre"**
Glanville brought a planchette board to Borley, though it yielded little on-site [31, 32]. However, when his daughter, Helen, used it at the family home in Streatham, it produced the famous communications from the phantom nun identifying herself as **"Marie Lairre"**, as well as the threatening prophecy from the entity **"Sunex Amures"** [31, 33-35].
*   **Personal Stance on Séances:** Despite facilitating these séances, Glanville **"placed no faith at all in this material"**. He firmly believed the messages originated solely in the subconscious minds of the operators, did not believe Marie Lairre ever existed, and deplored the fantastic theories built around her [36].

**Personal Paranormal Experiences**
Despite his skepticism regarding the séances and the alleged "apports" (which he also rejected), Glanville was utterly convinced that genuine, inexplicable phenomena occurred while he was at Borley. He fought hard to ensure the highly critical SPR *Borley Report* admitted this [36, 37]. His most notable experiences included:
*   **The "Swinging Blind" (Nov 20, 1937):** Along with three others, he observed the edges of a lowered window blind waving regularly with **"a sort of palpitating action as though being blown."** The men blew cigarette smoke at the window to test for drafts, but the smoke did not disperse, ruling out a natural breeze [38-40].
*   **The "Bathroom Shindy" (Oct 30, 1937):** While standing in the carriage way, Glanville and Kerr-Pearse heard **"a succession of heavy metallic sounds"** coming from the bathroom that lasted 20 to 30 seconds [41, 42]. 
*   **Auditory Phenomena:** He reported hearing muffled footsteps, heavy thuds, and "light tripping footsteps." Regarding the strict controls he implemented during these events, he wrote: **"It was therefore quite impossible for any person to enter the house without our being aware of it... We never had any evidence to suggest such \[trickery\]"** [43].

**Relationship with Harry Price and Later Controversies**
Though Price and Glanville worked closely, their relationship remained formal (always addressing each other as "Dear Mr. Price" and "Dear Mr. Glanville") [44]. When Price was posthumously accused of faking phenomena by a *Daily Mail* reporter, Glanville vehemently defended him, stating: **"You are perfectly right in your estimate of HP. He was the last man I would suspect of cheating. The story that has been given of this incident seems to me to be quite out of character with the man I knew fairly intimately for about thirteen years"** [45].

**The "Locked Book" Controversy**
Following his 1937–1938 investigations, Glanville compiled a massive, 360-page typescript dossier containing all his correspondence, plans, photographs, wall tracings, and séance records. Price had it bound in three-quarter calf leather and fitted with a Bramah lock to protect the sensitive personal details within; it became famously known as the ***Locked Book of Private Information*** [46-49]. 
*   In 1948, Glanville borrowed the *Locked Book* from the University of London library [50]. 
*   In 1953, the skeptical SPR investigator Trevor H. Hall visited the ailing Glanville in Fittleworth. Hall aggressively pursued the book, and on July 27, 1953, Glanville inscribed it and allegedly gave it to him [51, 52]. 
*   Hall subsequently sold the original volume to an American collector for £1,000 [53, 54]. The University of London's librarians firmly maintained that the book belonged to the Harry Price Library, that Glanville had only borrowed it, and bluntly accused Trevor Hall of stealing it by bullying the sick, elderly investigator [55-58].

Towards the end of his life, both Glanville and his wife grew incredibly tired of the haunting, with Glanville remarking that it was **"fatal to have anything to do with Borley"** and occasionally wishing he had never heard of the place [59].
