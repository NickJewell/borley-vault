# Sidney H. Glanville

**Sidney Herbert Glanville** (born Stratford, East London, January 30, 1884; died Fittleworth, West Sussex, December 10, 1953) was a retired machine-tool engineer's agent, consulting engineer, and former architect's articled clerk [1, 2]. He is arguably the most important lay investigator in the history of the Borley Rectory hauntings, serving as [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s "Chief Observer" and one of three principal official observers during the 1937–38 tenancy [1, 3, 4].

## Entering the Investigation

Glanville was the very first person to respond to [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s advertisement in *The Times* on May 25, 1937, asking for "intrepid" and "unbiased" observers [3, 5, 6]. In his application he noted that he had been "an interested observer of the supernormal" for several years. He began his first observational vigil at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] with his son Roger H. Glanville on June 19–20, 1937, and ultimately visited the site over a dozen times [6–8]. Price described him as one of the observers to whom his "thanks are specially due" — noting that they were strangers before the investigation began, which makes his commitment the more notable.

## Methodology, Floor Plans, and Equipment

Glanville approached the haunting with intense professionalism. Utilising his early training as an architect's clerk, he took detailed measurements of the sprawling Rectory during his first visits and drew the highly accurate **floor plans and site maps** that were subsequently reproduced in all of Price's books [3, 9–12] — the definitive reference documents for the building's layout.

He invented a tracking device consisting of a small aluminium cup filled with corundum (aluminium oxide) powder, with a hole drilled in the bottom so that any movement would leave a visible trail [13]. He also constructed a lightweight table (measuring 12 inches square and 2 feet 2 inches high) explicitly for the purpose of conducting table-tipping experiments at the Rectory [14, 15].

Glanville independently tracked down and interviewed several key witnesses. On October 6, 1937, he conducted a lengthy interview with the Rev. Guy and [[mabel-smith|Mabel Smith]] at Sevington Rectory, and on June 25, 1938, he interviewed the Misses Bull, recording their various claims [16–18]. He also maintained a vital correspondence with the retired Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]], reading Foyster's manuscript *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House* and declaring it **"the most astonishing document I have ever read"** [19, 20].

## The Cellar Excavation (1 November 1937)

Glanville and [[mark-kerr-pearse|Mark Kerr-Pearse]] excavated the subsided portion of the cellar floor directly beneath the famous first-floor "cold spot." They found foundations of a much older building beneath the 1863 structure — two-inch bricks, not the modern type — and a cellar wall running diagonally under the library composed entirely of these older bricks. The older foundations faced roughly south, suggesting a pre-Rectory structure of different orientation [26, 27]. Price established a direct correlation: the "cold spot" on the first-floor landing outside the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] sits directly above this ancient foundation.

When Price excavated the cellars in August 1943 and discovered human remains, he entirely failed to inform or invite Glanville — an omission that proved "a considerable surprise and disappointment to him" [28]. Glanville and his son nonetheless travelled to Borley to dig in the cellars on August 20 and August 30 [29, 30].

## The Wall Writings Contradiction

Glanville meticulously traced and photographed the mysterious pencilled messages on the Rectory walls [21]. Years later, sceptical investigators accused Price of cynically instructing Glanville to "re-touch" the writings to alter their meaning. In reality, correspondence proves that **Glanville himself first suggested this action**. On October 28, 1937, he wrote to Price: **"Do you think it would be advisable to carefully bring the old writing, just visible now, up to a state where it would be [photographically] possible?"** Price simply agreed to the suggestion [22]. See [[wall-writings]].

## The Cats' Cemetery

On August 14, 1937, Glanville noticed that the [[cats-cemetery|cats' cemetery]] had been mysteriously disturbed — the headboard of cat "Gem"'s grave thrown into a bush, a large area dug and refilled. He and his son re-excavated the area, unearthing large bones that Glanville noted "certainly never formed part of the anatomy of a cat" (likely horse or ox bones) [23–25]. Who disturbed the grave and why was never established.

## Observer Program Phenomena (1937–38)

Among Glanville's most notable personal paranormal experiences:

- **The tobacco tin incident:** He secretly pencilled a tiny cross on the floor at the "cold spot" on the landing outside the Blue Room — known only to him, his son, and [[dr-hf-bellamy|Dr. Bellamy]]. On a subsequent visit they found that the flat tobacco tin (left as a control object on the drawing-room mantelpiece by Kerr-Pearse) had been moved upstairs and placed "with mathematical precision" directly over that cross.
- **The "Swinging Blind" (November 20, 1937):** Along with three others, he observed the edges of a lowered window blind waving regularly with **"a sort of palpitating action as though being blown."** The men blew cigarette smoke at the window to test for draughts, but the smoke did not disperse [38–40].
- **The "Bathroom Shindy" (October 30, 1937):** While standing in the carriage way, Glanville and Kerr-Pearse heard **"a succession of heavy metallic sounds"** coming from the bathroom that lasted 20 to 30 seconds [41, 42].
- **Paranormal pencil markings:** He photographed the "Marianne get help" message; an hour later found new markings adjacent to it; re-photographed — the new marks appear in the second photograph only. The house was locked and sealed; the Glanvilles were always together.

Regarding the strict controls he implemented: **"It was therefore quite impossible for any person to enter the house without our being aware of it... We never had any evidence to suggest such [trickery]"** [43].

## The Séances (October–November 1937)

Glanville brought a planchette board to [[borley-rectory|Borley]], though it yielded little on-site [31, 32]. When his daughter [[helen-glanville|Helen]] used it at the family home in Streatham, however, it produced the famous communications from the phantom nun identifying herself as **"[[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]"**, as well as the threatening prophecy from the entity **"[[sunex-amures|Sunex Amures]]"** predicting the Rectory's destruction by fire [31, 33–35].

Glanville built a special lightweight table for table-tipping sessions at the Rectory. His son Roger, [[mark-kerr-pearse]], and [[alan-cuthbert|Alan Cuthbert]] conducted sessions producing entities claiming to be [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] and a monk named "Fadenoch." The Harry Bull entity specified the nun's burial location: south-west of the house, within 2 yards of a large fir-tree, under a stone marked "B." See [[mhh-ch25]].

**Personal Stance on Séances:** Despite facilitating these séances, Glanville **"placed no faith at all in this material."** He firmly believed the messages originated solely in the subconscious minds of the operators, did not believe [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] ever existed, and deplored the fantastic theories built around her [36].

## The Locked Book Affair

Following his 1937–38 investigations, Glanville compiled a massive, 360-page typescript dossier containing all his correspondence, plans, photographs, wall tracings, and séance records. Price had it bound in three-quarter calf leather and fitted with a Bramah lock; it became famously known as the ***[[locked-book|Locked Book of Private Information]]*** [46–49].

In 1948, Glanville borrowed the *Locked Book* from the University of London library [50]. In 1951 [[trevor-hall|Trevor H. Hall]] was introduced to him by [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] and they became close. By 1953 Glanville was seriously ill and "not thinking clearly." Hall visited him repeatedly at Fittleworth and "insisted" that statements favourable to Glanville be inserted in the forthcoming [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]]. On July 27, 1953, Glanville inscribed the *Locked Book* and apparently gave it to Hall [51, 52] — an inscription that [[ah-wesencraft|Alan Wesencraft]] described as "pathetic... written by a man in very bad health under Hall's insistent browbeating." Glanville died five months later.

Hall subsequently sold the original to an American collector for £1,000 [53, 54]. The University of London's librarians maintained that the book belonged to the Harry Price Library, that Glanville had only borrowed it, and bluntly accused Hall of stealing it by bullying the sick, elderly investigator [55–58]. Wesencraft's verdict: Hall *stole* it.

Full history at [[locked-book]].

## SPR Report and His Testimony

The [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] (1956) acknowledged, in one paragraph, that Glanville's observations "constitutes one of the most puzzling features of the whole case." This was, according to Glanville himself, the result of him fighting hard for even that minimal concession. He was devastated by the Report's general dismissal of his years of careful work.

When Price was posthumously accused of faking phenomena by a *Daily Mail* reporter, Glanville vehemently defended him: **"You are perfectly right in your estimate of HP. He was the last man I would suspect of cheating. The story that has been given of this incident seems to me to be quite out of character with the man I knew fairly intimately for about thirteen years"** [45].

Towards the end of his life, both Glanville and his wife grew incredibly tired of the haunting, with Glanville remarking that it was **"fatal to have anything to do with Borley"** and occasionally wishing he had never heard of the place [59].
