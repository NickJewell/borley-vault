# Lucie Kaye (later Mrs Meeker, d. 7 May 1955)

[[harry-price|Harry Price's]] secretary. Born **Lucy Kay** (b. ~1902); changed spelling to Lucie Kaye in 1929, mirroring what Price had done with medium Stella Cranshaw's name — an act of devotion to her employer. Price refers to her as "Miss Lucie Kaye" in *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940). Accompanied Price on his first visit to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] on 12 June 1929 and on many subsequent visits. Also present on the evening of the Sutton visit (25 July 1929).

## Background

Father: a German who settled in Britain and worked in insurance; met his English wife while she was teaching English in Germany. Lucie attended private schools and a finishing school in Folkestone before training at **RADA for four terms** — alongside John Gielgud, Charles Laughton and Edith Evans — though she left in spring 1925 without completing the course and began working as a model. She attended the opening of Price's Laboratory on 26 January 1926, admired the workspace and was immediately hired as his secretary. (hbrs-ch07)

She was "very fond of men." In 1927, she became pregnant from an affair and travelled to Zurich to have the child — a son named Ian Kallenbach (later anglicised to Ian). He was adopted by an Oxford family; Price wrote a supportive adoption reference. Her mother's family cut her off financially for the illegitimate birth. DNA tests later confirmed the child was not Price's. Ian bore no resemblance to Price. She admitted she was "not a loving person." (hbrs-ch07)

## Role in the Case

She took statements from witnesses alongside Price. In the first séance (June 1929 in the [[blue-room]]) she was one of the small circle present when the rapping communications were established.

Her most significant contribution is as the key counter-witness to the Sutton accusation.

## The Sutton Accusation

Charles Sutton (*Daily Mail*) claimed in a posthumous 1948 article that he had caught Price with his pockets full of "bricks and pebbles" during a visit to Borley. Kaye — identified by the text as the "colleague" present that evening — wrote to the *Daily Mail* in March 1949 with a firm denial:

> "I HAVE NO MEMORY WHATEVER OF A STONE, BRICK OR PEBBLE EPISODE THAT EVENING, and had there been anything dramatic or sensational like an 'exposure,' surely it would have made some impression."

She also noted that after returning to the Bull Inn at Long Melford, Sutton "did not approach a telephone when we reached the Bull Inn" — contradicting Sutton's claim that he had rushed to phone his editor with the story.

Her 1939 letter to Price (ten years after the alleged incident, mentioning Sutton's horse accident) makes no reference to any such dramatic confrontation — strongly suggesting it never happened.

## Assessment of Price

> "It is my considered conviction that Harry Price never, at any time, faked phenomena. I worked with him in close collaboration for some five years and, indeed, remained friends with him to the day of his death, and I am convinced he was a man of unimpeachable integrity."

Died 7 May 1955.

## Companion Corrections and Additions (brc-ii-kl)

**The Companion corrects the death date**: Lucy Kay died **3 May 1975** (not 1955 as previously recorded). She married and became Mrs Meeker; her son David Meeker destroyed several of her photograph albums (containing many Harry Price photographs) in 2002. She is buried in Bethel Green in an unmarked grave without a headstone.

**Born Germany, 1902**: The Companion confirms she was born in Germany in 1902. The alternative spelling "Kaye" was Price's invention; she officially signed herself "Kay" but accepted Price's version.

**The illegitimate child claim**: Richard Morris's biography of Price found "strong circumstantial evidence" including Price sending Kay £1,500 in payments and their close relationship, suggesting Price may have fathered the child Kay gave up for adoption in the early 1930s. Morris could not obtain definitive proof; the question remains open.

**Original handwritten notes at University of London**: Her notes from Price's first Borley visit on 12 June 1929 survive at the University of London. They record Harry Bull's "headless man" incident as: "Juvenal, retriever, terrified & growled saw man's legs, rest hid by fruit trees, thought poacher, followed with Juvenal, gate shut, but saw legs disappear thro' gate" — suggesting it was Price, not Kaye, who subsequently added the "headless" element to the account.

## First Night at Borley: Kaye's Own Account (bps-04)

Kaye sent Underwood her own account in September 1951, titled "The Ghost that kept Harry Price Awake." Key additions to what Price published:

**The candlestick:** She and Price had both been in the Blue Room remarking on the candlesticks' ugliness just before one hurled itself at them as they crossed the cold-spot landing. Her reflection: "had we not made derogatory remarks about it, it might not have hurled itself at us."

**The cold spot naming:** "It was not until some time later that the portion of the landing between the top of the staircase and the door of the Haunted Room became known as 'the cold spot.'" Established post-hoc from accumulated experience, not suggested in advance.

**The knock séance:** An hour and a half of knocking communications from the wooden back of the dressing-table's swing mirror in the Blue Room. The entity claimed to be "the late rector" (almost certainly [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]]) and communicated about his will being misinterpreted. "Names were spelled out — dates given." Much of this was never published.

**Price's migraine:** At 2 a.m., Price appeared at her door with dark-ringed eyes (his migraines turned the skin "almost black") and "a sepulchral voice said: 'Let's get out of here!'" He looked "awful" and "generally fed up." She negotiated him to wait until 3:30 a.m. for a train; he agreed on her promise to wake him. When she arrived at 3:20 a.m., he was sound asleep.
