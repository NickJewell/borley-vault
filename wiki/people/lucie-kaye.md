# Lucie Kaye (later Mrs Meeker)

[[harry-price|Harry Price's]] secretary and library assistant at his [[national-laboratory|National Laboratory of Psychical Research]]. Born **Lucy Kay** in Germany in 1902; changed spelling to "Lucie Kaye" around 1929, mirroring what Price had done with medium Stella Cranshaw's name — an act of devotion to her employer (she officially signed herself "Kay" but accepted Price's version). Accompanied Price on his first visit to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] on 12 June 1929 and on many subsequent visits. Also present on the evening of the Sutton visit (25 July 1929).

> **Contradiction:** The sources contain a direct conflict regarding her death date. One text records she died on May 7, 1955; others state she died on May 3, 1975. She is buried in an unmarked grave in Bethel Green.

## Background

Her German father worked in insurance and met his English wife while she was teaching in Germany. After attending private schools and a finishing school in Folkestone, Lucie trained at **RADA for four terms** — alongside John Gielgud, Charles Laughton, and Edith Evans — though she left in spring 1925 without completing the course and began working as a model.

In January 1926, the 24-year-old blonde attended the launch of Price's Laboratory, was impressed by the workspace, and was immediately hired as his secretary — despite not knowing shorthand — simply because she could type. She was described as attractive, sexually forthright, and "very fond of men."

In 1927, she became pregnant from an affair and travelled to Zurich to give birth to a son, Ian Kallenbach (born May 24, 1928, later anglicised to Ian). He was given up for adoption; Price wrote a supportive character reference, noting that "apart from her present predicament, I consider her a very moral person." Her mother's family cut her off financially for the illegitimate birth. Her son later noted she was "not a loving person," a trait she admitted in a letter to Price: "I do not seem to be able to really love anybody."

> **Contested:** Richard Morris's biography of Price found "strong circumstantial evidence" — including Price sending Kay £1,500 in payments and their close relationship — suggesting Price may have fathered the child. Morris could not obtain definitive proof; DNA tests by Ian Kallenbach's family did not confirm the claim, and Ian bore no resemblance to Price. The question remains open.

After leaving Price's employ, she married twice, became Mrs. Meeker, and had two other sons. In 2002, her estranged son David deliberately destroyed her photograph albums, which contained many pictures taken by Price.

## Role in the Case

She took statements from witnesses alongside Price. In the first séance (June 1929 in the [[blue-room|Blue Room]]) she was one of the small circle present when the rapping communications were established. Because she did not know shorthand, her handwritten notes form Price's primary original records of interviews with the Smiths, the Bull sisters, and the Coopers. Her original handwritten notes from Price's first Borley visit (June 12–14, 1929) survive at the University of London; they record Harry Bull's "headless man" incident in a form suggesting it was Price, not Kaye, who subsequently added the "headless" element to the account.

Her most significant contribution is as the key counter-witness to the Sutton accusation.

## First Night at Borley: Kaye's Own Account (bps-04)

Kaye sent Underwood her own account in September 1951, titled "The Ghost that kept Harry Price Awake." Key additions to what Price published:

**The candlestick:** She and Price had both been in the Blue Room remarking on the candlesticks' ugliness just before one hurled itself at them as they crossed the cold-spot landing. Her reflection: "had we not made derogatory remarks about it, it might not have hurled itself at us."

**The cold spot naming:** "It was not until some time later that the portion of the landing between the top of the staircase and the door of the Haunted Room became known as 'the cold spot.'" Established post-hoc from accumulated experience, not suggested in advance.

**The knock séance:** An hour and a half of knocking communications from the wooden back of the dressing-table's swing mirror in the Blue Room. The entity claimed to be "the late rector" (almost certainly [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]]) and communicated about his will being misinterpreted. "Names were spelled out — dates given." Much of this was never published.

**Price's migraine:** At 2 a.m., Price appeared at her door with dark-ringed eyes (his migraines turned the skin "almost black") and "a sepulchral voice said: 'Let's get out of here!'" She negotiated him to wait until 3:30 a.m. for a train; when she arrived at 3:20 a.m., he was sound asleep.

She later described it as "the only time I ever knew the atmosphere of a haunted house get the better of him."

## The St. Ignatius Medal

During a subsequent visit on July 5, 1929, alongside Price and [[lord-charles-hope|Lord Charles Hope]], Lucie picked up a medallion that inexplicably fell in the hall. Price later gave this "St. Ignatius" medal to her as a gift. She returned it to him in 1948 when he was writing a book, and it was returned to her again following his death.

## The Sutton Accusation

[[charles-sutton|Charles Sutton]] (*Daily Mail*) claimed in a posthumous 1948 article that he had caught Price with his pockets full of "bricks and pebbles" during a visit to Borley. Kaye — identified by the text as the "colleague" present that evening — wrote to the *Daily Mail* in March 1949 with a firm denial:

> "I HAVE NO MEMORY WHATEVER OF A STONE, BRICK OR PEBBLE EPISODE THAT EVENING, and had there been anything dramatic or sensational like an 'exposure,' surely it would have made some impression."

She also noted that after returning to the Bull Inn at Long Melford, Sutton "did not approach a telephone when we reached the Bull Inn" — contradicting Sutton's claim that he had rushed to phone his editor with the story.

> **Contradiction:** [[lord-charles-hope|Lord Charles Hope]] wrote notes in 1949 claiming that, just a fortnight after the incident in 1929, Lucie privately confessed to him that Sutton *had* caught Price throwing a stone, and that the shock of the accusation had given Price a heart attack. At a 1949 dinner with Hope and [[kathleen-goldney|SPR investigator Mollie Goldney]], she stated she "just don't remember the occasion at all." Researcher Robert J. Hastings later argued that Hope's claim was itself drawn from memory twenty years after the fact, not from contemporary notes.

Her 1939 letter to Price (ten years after the alleged incident, mentioning Sutton's horse accident) makes no reference to any such dramatic confrontation — strongly suggesting it never happened. She pointed out that she had lunched amicably with Sutton in Fleet Street as late as 1940, which she argued would not have happened if Sutton had genuinely exposed Price as a fraud in her presence.

## Assessment of Price

Lucie shared a warm, banter-filled relationship with Price, affectionately addressing him in letters from her travels as **"My dear Lord and Master."** Although investigators heavily pressured her to admit Price had faked phenomena, she fiercely defended him:

> "I know he told little fibs now and again, but he did not fake phenomena. I feel so very sure [of this]."

Her definitive assessment: "It is my considered conviction that Harry Price never, at any time, faked phenomena. I worked with him in close collaboration for some five years and, indeed, remained friends with him to the day of his death, and I am convinced he was a man of unimpeachable integrity."
