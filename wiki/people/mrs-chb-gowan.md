# Mrs C. H. B. Gowan

Widow of Captain Gowan, an acquaintance of [[harry-price|Harry Price]], and daughter of Mrs Savile. Mrs Gowan served as an important conduit between Price and independent witnesses, twice providing testimony of her own. She is explicitly listed in the acknowledgements of Price's *The End of Borley Rectory* (1946) as one of the contributors to whom he was "greatly indebted."

## The Carpenter Letter

Mrs Gowan's mother, Mrs Savile, received a letter from the Rev. [[rev-harry-carpenter|Harry Carpenter]] (122 Woodstock Road, Oxford), dated **October 30, 1941**, which contained a first-hand account of the Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]]'s personal experience of the phantom coach and headless coachman — heard directly from Bull during wartime special constable duty. Carpenter confirmed his familiarity with the Bull family and the early ghost sightings:

> "E. has handed on to us the part of your letter about the ghosts at Borley. As you say, the village is close to Foxearth, and we have read with much interest the book about the apparitions, especially as we knew the Bull family well and some of the people who are mentioned by Mr Price as having seen the ghosts."

Mrs Gowan forwarded the relevant portion to Price. This chain of transmission — Carpenter to Mrs Savile to Mrs Gowan to Price — is entirely independent of Price's primary contacts and provides an unsolicited corroboration of the coach phenomena via a witness who encountered Price's investigation only after publication.

## Wall-Writing Interpretation

Writing on October 23, 1940, Mrs Gowan contributed to the correspondence about the [[wall-writings|Marianne wall-messages]], interpreting the message in Plate IX (reading "Marianne-Please-help-get...") as **incomplete** — suggesting the writer was "forcibly pulled away just as she was beginning the last word," leaving the message ending in a scrawl rather than a period. She reads the message as: "Marianne, please help! Get…"

This interpretation — that the entity was interrupted mid-message rather than reaching a natural conclusion — adds to the picture of frantic urgency that [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] would later develop in his analysis of the French word *trompée*.

No further biographical details or contradictions regarding Mrs Gowan appear in the sources beyond these two contributions.
