# Father du Parc

Roman Catholic priest at **[[salmonhurst-new-brunswick|Salmonhurst Rectory]]**, New Brunswick, Canada, during the period when [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]] was Missioner of Drummond there (c. 1921–1927). [[ian-greenwood|Ian Greenwood Shaw]], Marianne's son by her first marriage, alleged that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] had an affair with Father du Parc during this period.

## The Salmonhurst Scandals

Father du Parc was a Roman Catholic priest in the area of [[salmonhurst-new-brunswick|Salmonhurst]], New Brunswick, where Lionel and Marianne Foyster lived between 1922 and 1927 [1, 2]. According to the testimony of Marianne's son Ian Greenwood Shaw, Marianne became involved with other men during her time in Salmonhurst, and **her list of alleged "conquests" specifically included Father du Parc**, as well as a young Anglican clergyman named Hall [2]. The sources suggest that Marianne's environment in Canada — where 85 percent of the population consisted of French-Canadian Catholics — may have stimulated her tendency to flirt with the Roman Catholic faith, an element she would later weave into her fantastic stories and even into her later bigamous marriage in England [2].

Ian also claimed these scandals came to the attention of the [[bishop-richardson|Bishop of Fredericton]] and contributed to pressure on Foyster to leave the Salmonhurst posting.

## Testimony Reliability

> **Contested:** Robert Wood is explicit that Ian's statements "must be treated cautiously, because he hated his mother and didn't hesitate to reveal the most damaging facts about her" [3]. Researchers investigating these Canadian scandals found **no hint or corroborating evidence of them in contemporary newspapers or in the diocesan records** of Fredericton [3]. The claim rests entirely on the uncorroborated testimony of a hostile witness.

Nevertheless, the texts conclude that Ian's account of his mother's escapades is highly plausible and **"in character"** when compared to her later, fully documented adulterous activities in England [2, 3].

## Context

The allegation illustrates the pattern Wood identifies across Marianne's life: a preference for men in positions of authority or religious significance (clergy, doctors, soldiers), often men with some degree of vulnerability or isolation. Whether true or not, the story of Father du Parc fits this pattern.
