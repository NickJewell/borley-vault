# Father du Parc

Roman Catholic priest at **Salmonhurst Rectory**, New Brunswick, Canada, during the period when [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]] was Missioner of Drummond there (c. 1921–1927). [[ian-greenwood|Ian Greenwood Shaw]], Marianne's son by her first marriage, alleged that Marianne had an affair with Father du Parc during this period.

## Account

Robert Wood records Ian's claim that Marianne's list of alleged conquests at Salmonhurst included "a young Anglican clergyman named Hall and a Roman Catholic priest, Father du Parc." Ian also claimed these scandals came to the attention of the Bishop of Fredericton ([[bishop-richardson|Bishop Richardson]]) and contributed to pressure on Foyster to leave the Salmonhurst posting.

> **Contested:** Wood is explicit that Ian's statements "must be treated cautiously, because he hated his mother and didn't hesitate to reveal the most damaging facts about her." He adds: "I have not found any hint of the scandals he reported in either the diocesan records of Fredericton or in contemporary newspapers." Father du Parc's affair with Marianne is therefore unverified — reported only by a hostile witness with no documentary corroboration.

## Context

The allegation illustrates the pattern Wood identifies across Marianne's life: a preference for men in positions of authority or religious significance (clergy, doctors, soldiers), often men with some degree of vulnerability or isolation. Whether true or not, the story of Father du Parc fits this pattern.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Father du Parc** has a very brief mention in the Borley Rectory sources, appearing exclusively in the biographical background regarding Marianne Foyster's life in Canada before her arrival at Borley.

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following details regarding his connection to the narrative:

**The Salmonhurst Scandals**
Father du Parc was a **Roman Catholic priest** in the area of Salmonhurst, New Brunswick, Canada, where Lionel and Marianne Foyster lived between 1922 and 1927 [1, 2]. According to the testimony of Marianne's son from her first marriage, Ian Greenwood Shaw, Marianne became involved with other men during her time in Salmonhurst, and **her list of alleged "conquests" specifically included Father du Parc**, as well as a young Anglican clergyman named Hall [2]. The sources suggest that Marianne's environment in Canada, where 85 percent of the population consisted of French-Canadian Catholics, may have stimulated her tendency to flirt with the Roman Catholic faith [2]. This Catholic influence was an element she would later weave into her fantastic stories and even into her later bigamous marriage in England [2].

**Testimony Reliability and Lack of Corroboration**
The sources explicitly note a caveat regarding the reliability of this information. The claim of Marianne's affair with Father du Parc rests entirely on the uncorroborated testimony of Ian Shaw, who deeply hated his mother and did not hesitate to share the most damaging facts about her [3]. Researchers investigating these Canadian scandals found **no hint or corroborating evidence of them in contemporary newspapers or in the diocesan records** of Fredericton [3]. However, the texts conclude that Ian's account of his mother's escapades with figures like Father du Parc is highly plausible and **"in character"** when compared to her later, fully documented adulterous activities in England [2, 3].
