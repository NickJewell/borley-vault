# Cynthia Thompson (née Ledsham)

American journalist and researcher on the staff of *Life* magazine; present at Borley Rectory on 5 April 1944 during the demolition of the ruins, with photographer David Scherman. Her signed testimony to the SPR demolished [[harry-price]]'s "levitated brick" claim — one of his most prominent later Borley assertions.

## The April 1944 Visit

Price brought Ledsham and Scherman to Borley to photograph the ruins for a proposed *Life* article on English ghosts. The demolition of the rectory was actively in progress.

## The "Flying Brick"

Price described in EBR (pp. 282–85) how, while standing some 100 feet from the ruins, a brick "shot up about four feet into the air" before Scherman's camera. Price published the resulting photograph and wrote: "If this is not proof of paranormal activity, I do not know the meaning of the word." He offered £1,000 to any charity that could prove it was faked.

## Cynthia Thompson's Signed Statement (1950)

Thompson (by then married and using her married name) provided a fully signed statement to the SPR after writing to the Society's Research Officer Dr D. J. West. Her statement reads:

> "As I told you at our first meeting about a year ago, I had firsthand experience of the most bare-faced hocus pocus on the part of the late Harry Price. In April 1944 Mr David Scherman and I were escorted down to Borley by Harry Price. Mr Price's version of what occurred appears on page 284 of *The End of Borley Rectory*. He refers to a mysterious 'flying brick', photographed by Mr Scherman. As Mr Price pointed out, there were no strings, no wire attached, but what he failed to mention is that there was a brawny workman still at work behind the wall. **All three of us saw him** as we passed the house towards the spot where the photograph was taken. There is no doubt at all that the flying bricks, several of which came out at regular intervals, were propelled by this workman as part of his demolition work."

In the follow-up interview with [[kathleen-goldney]] (October 1950), Thompson added:

> "Mrs Thompson remembers that as bricks were thrown up by workmen concealed behind a wall, she jokingly said 'Look, poltergeists'. H.P. apparently joined in the joke, walked up and picked up the brick and with a large smile 'Yes, indeed; look; no strings, no wires attached'. Mrs Thompson took this as so obviously a joke that she was subsequently astounded to see the form the story took on page 285 of *The End of Borley Rectory*."

## The Life Magazine Caption

The *Life* article described the photograph with the caption: "Borley Rectory, most haunted house in England, provided the photographer who took this picture in 1944 with a puzzling bit of ghost-craft. At instant the shutter was snapped, a brick rose from the floor, apparently nudged by unseen hands." Thompson clarified to Goldney that this caption was written "as good copy" and was not intended seriously — "no doubt the writer was having a dig at the British who believe in ghosts."

## Significance

Thompson's testimony represents one of the clearest instances of Price presenting a knowingly normal event as paranormal in EBR. The SPR concluded: "we cannot take a tolerant view of *The End of Borley Rectory* and its author."

Price's challenge of £1,000 was based on knowing the brick was not "faked" in the conventional sense — it was genuinely thrown by a workman — while concealing that fact from his readers.
