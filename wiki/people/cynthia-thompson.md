# Cynthia Thompson (née Ledsham)

American journalist and researcher on the staff of *Life* magazine; present at Borley Rectory on 5 April 1944 during the demolition of the ruins, with photographer David Scherman. Her signed testimony to the SPR demolished [[harry-price]]'s "levitated brick" claim — one of his most prominent later Borley assertions.

## The April 1944 Visit

Price brought Ledsham and Scherman to Borley to photograph the ruins for a proposed *Life* article on English ghosts. The demolition of the rectory was actively in progress.

## The "Flying Brick"

Price described in EBR (pp. 282–85) how, while standing some 100 feet from the ruins, a brick "shot up about four feet into the air" before Scherman's camera. Price published the resulting photograph and wrote: "If this is not proof of paranormal activity, I do not know the meaning of the word." He offered £1,000 to any charity that could prove it was faked.

## Cynthia Thompson's Signed Statement (1950)

Thompson (by then married and using her married name) provided a fully signed statement to the SPR after writing to the Society's Research Officer Dr D. J. West. Her statement reads:

> "As I told you at our first meeting about a year ago, I had firsthand experience of the most bare-faced hocus pocus on the part of the late Harry Price. In April 1944 Mr David Scherman and I were escorted down to Borley by Harry Price. Mr Price's version of what occurred appears on page 284 of *The End of Borley Rectory*. He refers to a mysterious 'flying brick', photographed by Mr Scherman. As Mr Price pointed out, there were no strings, no wire attached, but what he failed to mention is that there was a brawny workman still at work behind the wall. **All three of us saw him** as we passed the house towards the spot where the photograph was taken. There is no doubt at all that the flying bricks, several of which came out at regular intervals, were propelled by this workman as part of his demolition work."

In the follow-up interview with [[kathleen-goldney]] (October 1950), Thompson added:

> "Mrs Thompson remembers that as bricks were thrown up by workmen concealed behind a wall, she jokingly said 'Look, poltergeists'. H.P. apparently joined in the joke, walked up and picked up the brick and with a large smile 'Yes, indeed; look; no strings, no wires attached'. Mrs Thompson took this as so obviously a joke that she was subsequently astounded to see the form the story took on page 285 of *The End of Borley Rectory*."

## The Life Magazine Caption

The *Life* article described the photograph with the caption: "Borley Rectory, most haunted house in England, provided the photographer who took this picture in 1944 with a puzzling bit of ghost-craft. At instant the shutter was snapped, a brick rose from the floor, apparently nudged by unseen hands." Thompson clarified to Goldney that this caption was written "as good copy" and was not intended seriously — "no doubt the writer was having a dig at the British who believe in ghosts."

## Significance

Thompson's testimony represents one of the clearest instances of Price presenting a knowingly normal event as paranormal in EBR. The SPR concluded: "we cannot take a tolerant view of *The End of Borley Rectory* and its author."

Price's challenge of £1,000 was based on knowing the brick was not "faked" in the conventional sense — it was genuinely thrown by a workman — while concealing that fact from his readers.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Cynthia Thompson (née Ledsham)** was a researcher and archivist for the London Bureau of the American pictorial magazines *Time* and *Life* [1-3]. In the Borley Rectory sources, she is primarily known as a central witness to the controversial "flying brick" incident, which later became a cornerstone of the accusations of fraud against the psychical researcher Harry Price [1, 4]. 

**The Visit to Borley (April 5, 1944)**
On April 5, 1944, Miss Ledsham accompanied Harry Price and *Life* photographer David E. Scherman to the ruins of Borley Rectory to gather material for a proposed article entitled "Haunted England" [1, 3, 5]. Upon arrival, they found the building in the process of being demolished [1, 6]. During the visit, a brick was seen in mid-air and photographed by Scherman [1]. Price subsequently published the photograph in his book *The End of Borley Rectory*, describing the event as "the last phenomenon" and suggesting it might be the first photograph of a poltergeist projectile in flight [7-9]. Price claimed that he, Ledsham, and Scherman were standing at least 100 feet away and that no workmen were present on that side of the building [1, 10].

**The Accusation of "Bare-Faced Hocus Pocus"**
About a year prior to October 1950, Cynthia Thompson (now married) wrote an undated letter to Dr. D. J. West, Research Officer for the Society for Psychical Research (SPR), firmly contradicting Price's published account [2]. She stated:
> **"I had firsthand experience of the most bare-faced hocus pocus on the part of the late Harry Price... what he failed to mention is that there was a brawny workman still at work behind the wall... There is no doubt at all that the flying bricks, several of which came out at regular intervals, were propelled by this workman as part of his demolition work"** [11, 12].

On October 17, 1950, Thompson elaborated on her claims during a lunch interview with SPR investigator Kathleen M. Goldney [2, 13]. She explained that as the bricks were thrown up by the concealed workmen, she jokingly said, **"Look, poltergeists"** [6, 14]. She claimed Price joined in the joke, picked up the brick with a large smile, and said, **"Yes, indeed; look; no strings, no wires attached"** [6, 14]. Thompson noted she took the exchange as an obvious joke and was completely astounded when Price later presented the incident as a genuine paranormal event in his book [14, 15]. 

**The *Life* Magazine Caption**
An interesting lesser-known postscript to her testimony involves the article that *Life* magazine eventually published [16]. Written by Noel Busch, the article included the photograph with a sensational caption stating the brick had "apparently nudged [sic] by unseen hands" [16]. When questioned about this contradiction by Goldney, Thompson telephoned her on February 21, 1951, explaining that the caption was merely written as "good copy" by a journalist with his tongue in his cheek, intending to have a dig at the British belief in ghosts [16].

**Contradictions and Hastings' Criticisms**
When investigator Robert J. Hastings later re-examined the SPR's *Borley Report*, he highlighted several significant contradictions between Thompson's account and Price's, alongside severe criticisms of how Goldney conducted the 1950 interview [10, 17].
*   **One Brick vs. Several:** While Price spoke of only one brick rising from the ground, Thompson claimed *several* bricks came out at regular intervals from behind a wall [10, 18]. 
*   **Altered Details:** Hastings noted that Thompson altered her story during the lunch interview, claiming the bricks were thrown "up" [19]. Hastings argued this made no sense; if the bricks were thrown up from behind a wall, the workman would have had to be in the cellar and therefore completely invisible to the approaching party [19].
*   **A Flawed Interview:** Hastings heavily criticized Goldney's interview notes, which were "hastily written down during lunch" [17]. Goldney failed to test Thompson's memory by asking her to indicate the workman's precise location on a scale plan of the Rectory [20]. Without this crucial information, it was impossible to prove whether a workman in that exact position could genuinely have escaped Price's notice [21]. Furthermore, Goldney failed to ask if Thompson believed paranormal phenomena were possible, missing the chance to rule out any *a priori* bias that might have unconsciously distorted her memory of the event [21]. 

*(Note: Subsequent independent correspondence between investigator Michael Coleman and the photographer David Scherman in 1996 confirmed that Scherman had indeed deliberately positioned his camera to hide the workman tossing the rubble, acknowledging that Price was completely in on the joke)* [22].
