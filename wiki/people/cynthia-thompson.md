# Cynthia Thompson (née Ledsham)

Researcher and archivist for the London Bureau of the American pictorial magazines *Time* and *Life* [1–3]; present at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] on 5 April 1944 during the demolition of the ruins, with photographer [[david-scherman|David Scherman]]. Her signed testimony to the SPR demolished [[harry-price]]'s "levitated brick" claim — one of his most prominent later Borley assertions.

## The April 1944 Visit

Price brought Ledsham and Scherman to Borley to photograph the ruins for a proposed *Life* article on English ghosts, entitled "Haunted England" [1, 3, 5]. The demolition of the rectory was actively in progress [1, 6]. During the visit, a brick was seen in mid-air and photographed by Scherman [1]. Price subsequently published the photograph in his book *The End of Borley Rectory*, describing the event as "the last phenomenon" and suggesting it might be the first photograph of a poltergeist projectile in flight [7–9]. Price claimed that he, Ledsham, and Scherman were standing at least 100 feet away and that no workmen were present on that side of the building [1, 10].

## The "Flying Brick"

Price described in *EBR* (pp. 282–85) how, while standing some 100 feet from the ruins, a brick "shot up about four feet into the air" before Scherman's camera. Price published the resulting photograph and wrote: "If this is not proof of paranormal activity, I do not know the meaning of the word." He offered £1,000 to any charity that could prove it was faked.

## Cynthia Thompson's Signed Statement (1950)

Thompson (by then married and using her married name) provided a fully signed statement to the SPR after writing to the Society's Research Officer Dr D. J. West. Her statement reads:

> "As I told you at our first meeting about a year ago, I had firsthand experience of the most bare-faced hocus pocus on the part of the late Harry Price. In April 1944 Mr David Scherman and I were escorted down to Borley by Harry Price. Mr Price's version of what occurred appears on page 284 of *The End of Borley Rectory*. He refers to a mysterious 'flying brick', photographed by Mr Scherman. As Mr Price pointed out, there were no strings, no wire attached, but what he failed to mention is that there was a brawny workman still at work behind the wall. **All three of us saw him** as we passed the house towards the spot where the photograph was taken. There is no doubt at all that the flying bricks, several of which came out at regular intervals, were propelled by this workman as part of his demolition work." [11, 12]

In the follow-up interview with [[kathleen-goldney]] (17 October 1950), Thompson added:

> "Mrs Thompson remembers that as bricks were thrown up by workmen concealed behind a wall, she jokingly said 'Look, poltergeists'. H.P. apparently joined in the joke, walked up and picked up the brick and with a large smile 'Yes, indeed; look; no strings, no wires attached'. Mrs Thompson took this as so obviously a joke that she was subsequently astounded to see the form the story took on page 285 of *The End of Borley Rectory*." [6, 14]

## The *Life* Magazine Caption

The *Life* article described the photograph with the caption: "Borley Rectory, most haunted house in England, provided the photographer who took this picture in 1944 with a puzzling bit of ghost-craft. At instant the shutter was snapped, a brick rose from the floor, apparently nudged by unseen hands." Thompson clarified to Goldney (by telephone, 21 February 1951) that this caption was written "as good copy" and was not intended seriously — "no doubt the writer was having a dig at the British who believe in ghosts" [16].

## Contradictions and Criticisms of Her Testimony

When investigator [[robert-hastings|Robert J. Hastings]] re-examined the SPR's *Borley Report*, he highlighted several significant contradictions between Thompson's account and Price's, alongside severe criticisms of how [[kathleen-goldney|Goldney]] conducted the 1950 interview [10, 17]:

- **One brick vs. several:** While Price spoke of only one brick rising from the ground, Thompson claimed *several* bricks came out at regular intervals [10, 18].
- **Altered details:** Hastings noted that Thompson altered her story during the lunch interview, claiming the bricks were thrown "up" — which, if thrown up from behind a wall, would have required the workman to be in the cellar and therefore completely invisible [19].
- **A flawed interview:** Hastings criticised Goldney's notes as "hastily written down during lunch," with no attempt to test Thompson's memory against a scale plan of the Rectory to determine the workman's precise location [17, 20, 21].

Subsequent independent correspondence between Michael Coleman and [[david-scherman|David Scherman]] in 1996 confirmed that Scherman had deliberately positioned his camera to hide the workman tossing the rubble, acknowledging that Price was completely in on the joke [22].

## Significance

Thompson's testimony represents one of the clearest instances of Price presenting a knowingly normal event as paranormal in *EBR*. The SPR concluded: "we cannot take a tolerant view of *The End of Borley Rectory* and its author."

Price's challenge of £1,000 was based on knowing the brick was not "faked" in the conventional sense — it was genuinely thrown by a workman — while concealing that fact from his readers.
