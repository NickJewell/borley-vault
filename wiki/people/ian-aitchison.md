# Lieutenant Ian Aitchison

Canadian psychometrist, July 1944. Known to [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] and arranged to psychometrize the Borley relics while visiting Glanville at Fittleworth. Never met [[harry-price|Price]]. Had been informed of the general history of the Borley hauntings but was given no details about any particular object.

## Technique

Aitchison begins impressions in the third person, then may switch into speaking as the person associated with the object (or vice versa). Objects were handed to him in sequence.

## Impressions (July 1944)

**French Revolution medal**: "Old man bent, something wrong with his right leg... looking at a large building with castellated wall... the last thing he ever saw."

**Nail-file apport** (appeared in Rectory during an observer's visit in 1937): "Taken from a person who was drowned... I was pushed in... I was murdered."

**Miraculous Medal** (copper plaque): "A young girl... I am wearing this medal... something tight round my waist — very tight."

**Roman Catholic Confirmation medal** (found at Rectory 1929): "Troubled, frightened feeling."

**Gold pendant** (found under cellar floor 1943): Aitchison dropped it at once with "I want to throw it away!" After being persuaded to hold it: "A girl, arrogantly defiant of someone... lying on boards and someone is on top of me when I feel this medal, and I pull it off and throw it away. Very trying time!" The scene he describes: a small room, about 9 feet × 7 feet, boards forming a bed.

**Jaw-bone** (human remains, cellar excavation 1943): "Florence. Happiness!... it wasn't strangulation, but she was right when she said strangulation: it was rape. Waldegrave did it: he didn't want her to start a new nunnery on her own — he wanted her for his mistress. She had taken a vow that she would never have any intercourse with any man: it was forced upon her, and she fought. She does not know any more after the fight. As far as she knows she was buried in the cellar or small room she was in. She took off the medal because she had broken her vow: it was the medallion she got when she first went to the convent. Feeling of shame."

**Skull fragment + jaw-bone together**: "She wants the rest of her remains found and buried properly, not necessarily in churchyard — but out in the open away from the Rectory. She thinks she is French and seems to remember Queen Anne" (Anne was born 1665, aged two in 1667 — consistent with the date [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] gave for her death).

**Gold wedding-ring** (found in Blue Room, May 9, 1938): Aitchison dropped it immediately, shouted "Murder!" and refused to touch it again.

## Assessment

Price notes: "The reference to Florence is interesting" — if it refers to the Italian city, it supports the theory that an Italian workman ("Mercur") disturbed the jaw-bone when building the Rectory in 1863. The description of the girl tearing off the pendant during a violent assault is consistent with both the wall-message appeals and the Planchette séance identification. Price does not claim Aitchison's impressions as proof, but considers them "very interesting and thought-provoking."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Lieutenant Ian Aitchison** (occasionally spelled **Atchison** in the literature) appears in the Borley Rectory sources as a well-known Canadian psychometrist. He was utilized by investigators to perform "tactile clairvoyance" (psychometry) on various artifacts recovered from the Rectory ruins. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following biographical details, events, specific quotes, and theories regarding his involvement:

**Biographical Details and Background**
Aitchison was a personal friend of Sidney H. Glanville, the chief official observer during Harry Price's 1937–1938 tenancy at the Rectory [1, 2]. A lesser-known biographical fact is that **Aitchison and Harry Price never actually met** [2]. Prior to his involvement in the case, Aitchison was aware of the general history of the Borley hauntings—likely having read about them in Canada—but he had not been given any specific details concerning the individual objects he was asked to analyze [3].

**The Fittleworth Experiments (July 1944)**
Aitchison's direct contribution to the Borley case occurred in **July 1944**. While visiting Sidney Glanville at his home in Fittleworth, West Sussex, Glanville telephoned Harry Price and suggested testing the Canadian's abilities on the various Borley relics [2, 4]. Price agreed and shipped them a parcel containing several alleged "apports" (objects that had mysteriously appeared in the Rectory) as well as the skull and jawbone fragments that had been excavated from the Rectory cellars in 1943 [3].

**Psychometric Readings and Specific Quotes**
When Aitchison handled the objects, he produced a series of dramatic readings that seemingly corroborated the most violent legends associated with the site. The sources record his specific reactions to the following items:
*   **The Nail-File:** Handed a nail-file that had mysteriously appeared in a Rectory cupboard, Aitchison stated: **"Taken from a person who was drowned. A pond with very thick weeds round the edge and very isolated, but I am at the bottom of that pond. I didn’t feel anything. I was pushed in. In other words, I was murdered. The nail-file has been taken from my body. I don’t like that one"** [4, 5].
*   **The "Miraculous Medal":** He sensed a young girl wearing it, stating, **"The only thing I can say is that although I am very young, there is something tight round my waist—very tight"** [4, 6].
*   **The Roman Catholic Confirmation Medal:** Upon handling this child's medal, Aitchison simply reported, **"I have only a troubled, frightened feeling from it"** [4, 6].
*   **The French Revolution Medal:** He "saw" an old, bent man who had something wrong with his right leg [4].
*   **The Thin Gold Pendant:** This object deeply upset Aitchison. He felt it had belonged to a girl who wanted to get rid of it. He "saw" a small room with board beds, sensing that the girl had the pendant torn from her neck while lying on the boards with someone on top of her. He summarized the event with the quote: **"Very trying time"** [4].
*   **The Skull and Jawbone:** Holding the jawbone, he sensed the word "Florence" and "happiness," but also a deep "feeling of shame." He declared that the victim's death **"wasn't strangulation but rape, that Waldegrave did it"** [4]. Assessing both bone fragments together, he claimed the victim was French, seemed to remember "Queen Anne," and requested that her remaining bones be found and properly buried **"out in the open away from the Rectory"** [4, 7]. 
*   **The Gold Wedding Ring:** When handed the lady's gold wedding ring that Price and Geoffrey Motion had found in the Blue Room in 1938, Aitchison **"dropped it at once, shouted 'Murder!' and refused to touch it again"** [4, 7]. 

**Interpretations and Legacy**
Harry Price published the results of Aitchison's psychometric readings in his 1946 book, *The End of Borley Rectory* [8]. Price used the Lieutenant's testimony to bolster Canon W.J. Phythian-Adams’s theory that the Borley nun (alleged to be "Marie Lairre") had been brutally murdered [9]. 

Price argued that Aitchison's readings logically fit the historical timeline. For example, Aitchison's claim that the victim remembered "Queen Anne" was historically possible, as Anne was born in 1665, two years before the nun was allegedly murdered in 1667 [9]. Furthermore, Price noted that Aitchison's statement that the victim wanted to be buried "away from the Rectory" (rather than in the adjacent churchyard) made perfect sense, as a staunch 17th-century French Catholic nun would have had serious qualms about being interred in an Anglican burial ground [9]. Ultimately, Price left it to his readers to assess the value of Aitchison's impressions [8].
