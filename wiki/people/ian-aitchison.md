# Lieutenant Ian Aitchison

(Occasionally spelled **Atchison** in the literature.) Canadian psychometrist and personal friend of [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]], the chief official observer during [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s 1937–38 tenancy [1, 2]. Prior to his involvement in the case, Aitchison was aware of the general history of the Borley hauntings but had been given no details about any particular object he was asked to analyse [3]. He and Harry Price never actually met [2].

## Technique

Aitchison begins impressions in the third person, then may switch into speaking as the person associated with the object (or vice versa). Objects were handed to him in sequence.

## The Fittleworth Experiments (July 1944)

Aitchison's direct contribution to the Borley case occurred in **July 1944**. While visiting [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] at his home in Fittleworth, West Sussex, Glanville telephoned Price and suggested testing the Canadian's abilities on the various Borley relics [2, 4]. Price agreed and shipped a parcel containing several alleged "apports" — objects that had mysteriously appeared in the Rectory — as well as the skull and jawbone fragments excavated from the Rectory cellars in 1943 [3].

## Psychometric Impressions (July 1944)

**French Revolution medal**: "Old man bent, something wrong with his right leg... looking at a large building with castellated wall... the last thing he ever saw."

**Nail-file apport** (appeared in Rectory during an observer's visit in 1937): **"Taken from a person who was drowned. A pond with very thick weeds round the edge and very isolated, but I am at the bottom of that pond. I didn't feel anything. I was pushed in. In other words, I was murdered. The nail-file has been taken from my body. I don't like that one."** [4, 5]

**Miraculous Medal** (copper plaque): **"The only thing I can say is that although I am very young, there is something tight round my waist—very tight."** [4, 6]

**Roman Catholic Confirmation medal** (found at Rectory 1929): **"I have only a troubled, frightened feeling from it."** [4, 6]

**Gold pendant** (found under cellar floor 1943): Aitchison dropped it at once. After being persuaded to hold it: a small room, about 9 feet × 7 feet, boards forming a bed — a girl who wanted to get rid of the pendant, pulled from her neck while lying on the boards with someone on top of her. He summarised the scene: **"Very trying time."** [4]

**Jaw-bone** (human remains, cellar excavation 1943): "Florence. Happiness!... it wasn't strangulation, but she was right when she said strangulation: it was rape. Waldegrave did it: he didn't want her to start a new nunnery on her own — he wanted her for his mistress. She had taken a vow that she would never have any intercourse with any man: it was forced upon her, and she fought. She does not know any more after the fight. As far as she knows she was buried in the cellar or small room she was in. She took off the medal because she had broken her vow: it was the medallion she got when she first went to the convent. Feeling of shame."

**Skull fragment + jaw-bone together**: "She wants the rest of her remains found and buried properly, not necessarily in churchyard — but out in the open away from the Rectory. She thinks she is French and seems to remember Queen Anne" (Anne was born 1665, aged two in 1667 — consistent with the date [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] gave for her death). [4, 7]

**Gold wedding-ring** (found in [[blue-room|Blue Room]], May 9, 1938): Aitchison dropped it immediately, shouted "Murder!" and refused to touch it again. [4, 7]

## Assessment

Price published Aitchison's readings in *The End of Borley Rectory* (1946) to bolster [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]]'s theory that the Borley nun had been brutally murdered [8, 9]. Price argued the readings logically fit the historical timeline: Aitchison's claim that the victim remembered "Queen Anne" was historically possible, as Anne was born in 1665, two years before the nun was allegedly murdered in 1667 [9]. Furthermore, that the victim wanted to be buried "away from the Rectory" rather than in the Anglican churchyard made sense for a staunch seventeenth-century French Catholic nun [9].

Price notes: "The reference to Florence is interesting" — if it refers to the Italian city, it supports the theory that an Italian workman ("Mercur") disturbed the jaw-bone when building the Rectory in 1863. Price does not claim Aitchison's impressions as proof, but considers them "very interesting and thought-provoking."
