# Sir Edward Waldegrave (c. 1517–1561)

Tudor Catholic baronet; Lord of the Manor of Borley under Henry VIII; died in the Tower of London aged 44. Proposed by [[ivan-banks|Ivan Banks]] as the probable identity of the tall, old-fashioned-dressed male figure seen at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] — particularly the man in a tall hat seen on [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]]'s bedroom floor and in an upstairs corridor.

## Early Life and Inheritance

Born around 1517, he was the second son of John Waldegrave (d. 1543) and Lora Rochester, and a direct descendant of Sir Richard Waldegrave of Bures, who served as Speaker of the House of Commons in 1402 [1, 2].

> **Contradiction:** Historical sources present a contradiction regarding how he acquired the Borley estate. Some texts state he inherited lands at Borley from his father upon his death [1, 2]. However, multiple other records state that King Henry VIII granted the Manor of Borley directly to Edward Waldegrave in 1546, after the Dean and Chapter of Christchurch, Canterbury, surrendered it due to upkeep costs [1, 3–6].

## Political Career

- Member of Parliament for Wiltshire (1553), Somerset (1554), and Essex (1557–1558) [2, 7, 8]
- **Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster**
- Master of the Great Wardrobe to King Philip and Queen Mary
- Senior member of the household of Princess (later Queen) Mary Tudor

## First Imprisonment (under Edward VI, 1551)

During the Reformation, Waldegrave was attached to the household of Princess Mary. In August 1551, Edward VI ordered him to enforce prohibition on Catholic Mass in Mary's household. Waldegrave refused and was sent to the Tower; after falling ill, he served the rest of his sentence under house arrest at Mary's residence at Copped Hall [8–10]. Queen Mary rewarded his loyalty when she ascended the throne in 1553 — he was **knighted at her coronation** [3, 7, 11, 12] and granted the manor of Chewton Mendip in Somerset, which became the primary seat of the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]] [9, 11]. When Waldegrave later objected to Mary's marriage to Philip of Spain, he was reportedly appeased by the Spanish establishment with a pension of 500 crowns [9, 13].

## Second Imprisonment and Death (under Elizabeth I, 1561)

When Elizabeth I acceded in 1558, Waldegrave refused to accept the Oath of Supremacy or the Act of Uniformity. He continued Catholic practices at Borley Hall, apparently using a **Rectory on the Borley site** for sensitive meetings.

**The betrayal:** Waldegrave secretly continued to hold Mass at Borley Hall with his wife, his physician (Dr. Fryer), and others. He was betrayed by the priest [[john-cox-devon|John Cox (alias John Devon)]], who had stayed at Borley for a month [15–18]. Historical letters suggest Cox was in turn betrayed to the authorities by a cunning, deceitful manservant named **Goldney**, against whom Waldegrave had been warned in a 1561 letter from Thomas Parker. This same letter notably mentions an individual named **"Forster the fishmonger"** — a detail that later proved significant in séance research [19–21].

**The arrest (April 19–20, 1561):** The Earl of Oxford searched Borley Hall, finding "Popish books and superstitious ornaments," as well as **"letters... diverse presumptions of scheming minds and doings towards the state and government"** [17, 22, 23]. Waldegrave was indicted and convicted before the Earl of Oxford at the Commission of Oyer in Brentwood, Essex, on June 3, 1561 [22, 24]. At the same time [[sir-thomas-wharton|Sir Thomas Wharton]] at Newhall, Essex, was arrested for the same religious offences — though Waldegrave, uniquely, was suspected of more than heresy.

**His will:** Made in August 1561, indicating he knew his situation was terminal [28].

**Death:** September 1, 1561, in the Tower, aged 44. The cause of death is contested.

> **Contradiction:** While some local lore speculated he might have been executed for heresy or succumbed to torture on the rack [26–28], historical consensus points to a natural death from ill health [10, 14, 26], supported by the fact that the Marquis of Winchester requested the wardship of Waldegrave's son on August 8, 1561 — suggesting his death from illness was imminent and anticipated [26].

**Burial:** Records state he was buried in the Tower of London's chapel [29]. However, local Borley legend and other historical accounts maintain that his body was exhumed and returned to Borley to be interred inside [[borley-church|Borley Church]] [3, 12, 30]. To devout Catholics, his burial under Anglican Protestant rites would have been viewed as a profound, final insult [30, 31].

## The Waldegrave Monument

Sir Edward is immortalised by a massive 14-foot-high clunch and marble altar-tomb in the east end of the nave at [[borley-church|Borley Church]], erected by his wife Frances (who survived him by 38 years, dying in 1599) [7, 14, 32, 33]. The elaborate tomb features a stone canopy supported by six Corinthian columns and bears the painted effigies of Sir Edward in full plate armour and Frances in a ruff, with a squirrel (an emblem of thrift) at her feet [32, 33]. The sides depict their six children [32, 33]. A prominent Latin inscription carved in black marble translates to: **"Edward died in the year of our Lord one thousand five hundred and sixty-one... Behold, O man, what honours, what descent, what wealth will profit thee, when breath of life shall leave thy frame!"** [25, 34].

## The Séance Connection — "Tfismong"

At the table-tipping session of October 23, 1937 (sitters: [[alan-cuthbert|Cuthbert]], [[roger-glanville|Roger Glanville]], [[mark-kerr-pearse|Kerr-Pearse]]), the table spelled out "**Tfismong**" in response to "What are you?" Banks in 1994 identified this as **"T\*fis\*mong"** = "**THE fishmonger**" — referring to **Forster the fishmonger**, named in Thomas Parker's letter in the *Calendars of State Papers Domestic* alongside the traitorous servant Goldney. Banks reads this as Waldegrave's identification of himself through a key figure from his betrayal narrative [21, 35, 36].

Banks also notes that "Forster" is strikingly close to "Foyster" — a further irony in Borley's interconnected history.

## The Ghost Case

Banks argues that Waldegrave's ghost would be expected at the Rectory site because:
1. Archaeological evidence shows a brick building on the site during Tudor times
2. Documents confirm Waldegrave maintained a Rectory at Borley (same site as Henry Bull's later pile)
3. He was betrayed at Borley, arrested at Borley, his papers seized at Borley Hall
4. Buried against his faith, in the church whose living was in his gift
5. Ethel Bull described the tall man in "very old-fashioned costume" with a tall hat — consistent with Elizabeth I-era dress

When the professional psychic Fred Marion handled a piece of touchwood found in the Rectory, he sensed a tall, disillusioned public man involved in a feud, to whom "people in the Church were enemies." Canon [[canon-phythian-adams|Phythian-Adams]] matched this psychic profile to Sir Edward Waldegrave [37–40].

The Latin Catholic appeals in the séances (Mass, chant, light, prayer) are also consistent with Waldegrave's Catholic faith.

The [[waldegrave-monument|Waldegrave monument]] in Borley Church has itself been the epicentre of church phenomena: in March 1949, the Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|A. C. Henning]] and his confirmation class heard **three loud and distinct blows** emanate from the tomb exactly as Henning spoke the words "Christ is here" [42, 43]; in 1974, investigators reported an intense cold spot and a regular "thump-thump" like a clock originating from the monument [44]; and in the late 1950s, a mysterious piece of inscribed parchment allegedly appeared out of nowhere on top of the tomb [45].

> **Contradiction:** Banks acknowledges this entire case is speculative; no direct identification of "Sir Edward Waldegrave" ever appeared in the séance records. The Fred Marion psychometric reading could equally describe [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]].
