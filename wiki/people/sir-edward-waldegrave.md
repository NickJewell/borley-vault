# Sir Edward Waldegrave (c. 1517–1561)

Tudor Catholic baronet; Lord of the Manor of Borley under Henry VIII; died in the Tower of London aged 44. Proposed by [[Ivan Banks]] as the probable identity of the tall, old-fashioned-dressed male figure seen at Borley Rectory — particularly the man in a tall hat seen on Ethel Bull's bedroom floor and in an upstairs corridor.

## Background

Born c.1517, the second son of John Waldegrave and Lora Rochester; a descendant of Sir Richard Waldegrave of Bures (Speaker of the House of Commons). Originally from Bures/Smallbridge Hall; came to Borley as a result of the Dissolution when the Manor of Borley was granted to him by Henry VIII.

## Political Career

- Master of the Great Wardrobe to King Philip and Queen Mary
- Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster
- Senior member of the household of Princess (later Queen) Mary Tudor
- Essex representative at Parliament, January 20, 1557

## First Imprisonment (under Edward VI, 1551)

Edward VI ordered Waldegrave to enforce prohibition on Catholic Mass in Princess Mary's household. Waldegrave refused, was imprisoned in the Tower; released when the sickly boy-king died and Mary acceded. Queen Mary rewarded Waldegrave with extensive properties including Chewton Mendip, Somerset.

## Second Imprisonment and Death (under Elizabeth I, 1561)

When Elizabeth I acceded in 1558, Waldegrave refused to accept the Oath of Supremacy or the Act of Uniformity. He continued Catholic practices at Borley Hall, apparently using a **Rectory on the Borley site** (not Borley Place) for sensitive meetings.

**The betrayal (April 1561):** A priest, **John Cox alias John Devon** (parish priest of Pentlow), was arrested and confessed to saying Mass at Waldegrave's house at Borley and at Sir Thomas Wharton's home. Cox/Devon was apparently betrayed by a manservant named **"Goldney"** — described in a November 1560 Thomas Parker letter (*Calendars of State Papers Domestic*) as "a lying, deceitful and cunning individual." Banks identifies Goldney as a turncoat paid by the Queen's agents.

**The arrest (April 19–20, 1561):** The **Earl of Oxford** searched Borley Hall, finding "letters... diverse presumptions of scheming minds and doings towards the state and government." Waldegrave was taken before the Earl of Oxford at the Commission of Oyer, Brentwood, Essex, June 3, 1561; interrogated throughout June and July; sent to the Tower for the second time.

**His will:** Made in August 1561, indicating he knew his situation was terminal.

**Death:** September 1, 1561, in the Tower, aged 44. The cause — illness from prison conditions, torture, or execution — is unverified. The PRO has no Tower records before the 1600s. The Earl of Oxford's letter includes the phrase: "until last, and then as little as might be" — Banks reads as a possible indication of extracted confession.

**Burial:** Buried in the Tower Chapel under Protestant rites. Later exhumed and returned to Borley Church, where the Waldegrave tomb stands (effigies of Sir Edward and Lady Frances). Banks regards burial in a Protestant church as a posthumous insult compounding the injustice.

## The Séance Connection — "Tfismong"

At the table-tipping session of October 23, 1937 (sitters: Cuthbert, Roger Glanville, Kerr-Pearse), the table spelled out "**Tfismong**" in response to "What are you?" Banks in 1994 identified this as **"T\*fis\*mong"** = "**THE fishmonger**" — referring to **Forster the fishmonger**, named in a Thomas Parker letter in the *Calendars of State Papers Domestic* (same letter warning Waldegrave about Goldney). Banks reads this as Waldegrave's identification of himself through a key figure from his betrayal narrative.

Banks also notes that "Forster" is strikingly close to "Foyster" — a further irony in Borley's interconnected history.

## The Ghost Case

Banks argues that Waldegrave's ghost would be expected at the Rectory site because:
1. Archaeological evidence shows a brick building on the site during Tudor times
2. Documents confirm Waldegrave maintained a Rectory at Borley (same site as Henry Bull's later pile)
3. He was betrayed at Borley, arrested at Borley, his papers seized at Borley Hall
4. Buried against his faith, in the church whose living was in his gift
5. Ethel Bull described the tall man in "very old-fashioned costume" with a tall hat — consistent with Elizabeth I-era dress

The Latin Catholic appeals in the séances (Mass, chant, light, prayer) are also consistent with Waldegrave's Catholic faith.

> **Contradiction:** Banks acknowledges this is speculative; no direct identification of "Sir Edward Waldegrave" ever appeared in the séance records. The Fred Marion psychometric reading (a piece of touchwood) described "a tall disillusioned man who became an enemy of people in the church" — which Price tentatively attributed to Waldegrave, though Banks notes this could equally describe [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]].

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Sir Edward Waldegrave** (c. 1517–1561) was a highly influential Tudor politician, a staunch Roman Catholic, and a central historical figure in the Borley Rectory saga. As the Lord of the Manor of Borley and an ancestor of the family that held the estate for three centuries, his life, persecution, and death provide the backdrop for several of the paranormal theories surrounding the haunting.

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, contradictions, and paranormal associations:

### **Early Life, Inheritance, and Contradictions**
*   **Background:** Born around 1517, he was the second son of John Waldegrave (d. 1543) and Lora Rochester, and a direct descendant of Sir Richard Waldegrave, who served as Speaker of the House of Commons in 1402 [1, 2]. 
*   **The Borley Inheritance Contradiction:** Historical sources present a contradiction regarding how he acquired the Borley estate. Some texts state he inherited lands at Borley from his father upon his death [1, 2]. However, multiple other records state that King Henry VIII granted the Manor of Borley directly to Edward Waldegrave in 1546, after the Dean and Chapter of Christchurch, Canterbury, surrendered it due to upkeep costs [1, 3-6]. 

### **Political Ascent Under Queen Mary**
Sir Edward was a powerful political figure who served as a **Member of Parliament for Wiltshire (1553), Somerset (1554), and Essex (1557–1558)**, as well as holding the prominent office of **Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster** [2, 7, 8]. 
*   **First Imprisonment:** During the Reformation, he was attached to the household of Princess Mary (Mary Tudor). In August 1551, under the Protestant reign of Edward VI, Waldegrave was sent to the Tower of London for refusing to enforce orders forbidding the celebration of the Catholic Mass in Mary's household [2, 8-10]. After falling ill, he was allowed to serve the rest of his sentence under house arrest at Mary's residence at Copped Hall [9, 10]. 
*   **Rise to Power:** When Mary ascended the throne in 1553, Waldegrave’s loyalty was heavily rewarded. He was **knighted at her coronation** [3, 7, 11, 12]. The Queen also granted him the manor of Chewton Mendip in Somerset, which became the primary seat of the Waldegrave family [9, 11]. 
*   **The Spanish Pension:** When Waldegrave objected to Queen Mary’s marriage to Philip of Spain, he was appeased and "reassured" by the Spanish establishment with a pension of 500 crowns [9, 13].

### **Downfall Under Queen Elizabeth I**
With the accession of the Protestant Queen Elizabeth I, the Catholic Waldegraves faced severe persecution. Sir Edward adamantly refused to take the Oath of Supremacy (which made Elizabeth head of the Church) and rejected the Act of Uniformity [14, 15]. 
*   **The Betrayal:** Waldegrave secretly continued to hold Mass at Borley Hall with his wife, his physician (Dr. Fryer), and others. He was betrayed by a Papist priest named John Cox (alias John Devon), who had stayed at Borley for a month [15-18]. 
*   *Lesser-Known Detail:* Historical letters suggest Cox was likely betrayed to the authorities by a cunning, deceitful manservant named Goldney, against whom Waldegrave had been warned in a 1561 letter from Thomas Parker. This same letter notably mentions an individual named **"Forster the fishmonger"** [19-21]. 
*   **Arrest and Trial:** On or around April 20, 1561, Waldegrave’s home was raided. Authorities discovered "Popish books and superstitious ornaments," as well as letters hinting at treasonous "scheming minds and doings towards the state and government" [17, 22, 23]. He was indicted and convicted before the Earl of Oxford at the Commission of Oyer in Brentwood, Essex, on June 3, 1561 [22, 24]. 

### **Death and Burial Anomalies**
Sir Edward was consigned to the Tower of London for a second time, where he **died on September 1, 1561, at the age of 44** [10, 14, 25]. 
*   **Contradiction on the Cause of Death:** While some local lore speculated he might have been executed for heresy or succumbed to torture on the rack [26-28], historical consensus points to a natural death from ill health [10, 14, 26]. This is supported by the fact that the Marquis of Winchester requested the wardship of Waldegrave's son on August 8, 1561, suggesting his death from illness was imminent and anticipated [26], and that Waldegrave drew up his final will that same month [28].
*   **Contradiction on Burial Site:** Records state that Sir Edward was buried in the Tower of London's chapel [29]. However, local Borley legend and other historical accounts maintain that his body was exhumed and returned to Borley to be interred inside Borley Church [3, 12, 30]. To devout Catholics, his burial under Anglican Protestant rites would have been viewed as a profound, final insult [30, 31]. 

### **The Waldegrave Monument**
Sir Edward is immortalized by a massive 14-foot-high clunch and marble altar-tomb in the east end of the nave at Borley Church, erected by his wife Frances (who survived him by 38 years, dying in 1599) [7, 14, 32, 33].
*   The elaborate tomb features a stone canopy supported by six Corinthian columns and bears the painted effigies of Sir Edward in full plate armor and Frances in a ruff, with a squirrel (an emblem of thrift) at her feet [32, 33]. The sides depict their six children [32, 33].
*   **Specific Quote:** A prominent Latin inscription carved in black marble translates to: **"Edward died in the year of our Lord one thousand five hundred and sixty-one... Behold, O man, what honours, what descent, what wealth will profit thee, when breath of life shall leave thy frame! Thou seest nothing remain when the structure of man is dissolved. Earth its part reclaims, and so doth heaven."** [25, 34].

### **Connections to the Borley Hauntings**
Although Sir Edward died a century before the legendary 1667 murder of the phantom French nun "Marie Lairre" (a crime planchette spirits blamed on a "Waldegrave"), he is independently linked to several specific paranormal phenomena:
*   **The "Tfismong" Séance Data:** During a table-tipping séance at the Rectory, sitters received cryptic Latin messages and an apparently nonsensical response spelled **"Tfismong"**. Borley researcher Ivan Banks later realized this decoded to **"T[HE] fis[H]mong[ER]"**. Banks connected this directly to the 1561 state paper from Thomas Parker to Sir Edward Waldegrave, which specifically mentioned "Forster the fishmonger" alongside the traitorous servant Goldney. This astonishing historical correlation led Banks to hypothesize that Sir Edward Waldegrave himself was the entity communicating at the séance [21, 35, 36].
*   **Psychometry of the Touchwood Apport:** When the professional psychic 'Fred Marion' handled a piece of touchwood found in the Rectory, he sensed a tall, disillusioned public man involved in a feud, to whom "people in the Church were enemies." Canon Phythian-Adams explicitly matched this psychic profile to Sir Edward Waldegrave, who died a persecuted prisoner for his Catholic faith [37-40]. 
*   **Visual Apparitions:** Some researchers theorize that Sir Edward’s ghost was the tall man dressed in old-fashioned Elizabethan clothing and a tall hat who appeared sitting on Ethel Bull's bed during her youth [40, 41].
*   **Tomb Phenomena:** The Waldegrave monument itself has been the epicenter of church phenomena. In March 1949, the Rev. A. C. Henning and his confirmation class heard **three loud and distinct blows** emanate from the tomb exactly as Henning spoke the words "Christ is here" [42, 43]. In 1974, investigators reported an intense cold spot and a regular "thump-thump" like a clock originating from the monument [44], and in the late 1950s, a mysterious piece of inscribed parchment allegedly appeared out of nowhere on top of the tomb [45].
