# Catherine Turner

Wife of the poet and writer [[james-turner|James Turner]], with whom she lived at [[rectory-cottage|Borley Priory cottage]] and the Rectory site from May 1947 until July 1950. Also recorded in the sources as **Cathy Turner** and, in one text, by her full name **Lucie Alice 'Cathy' Turner** [1]. Catherine was a calm, balanced witness who, like her husband, accepted the unexplained happenings at Borley as "part of the furniture" rather than a source of fear.

## Arrival and Departure

Catherine and James purchased the Rectory Cottage and seven acres of the upper Rectory grounds from Captain Russell for £1,500, moving in around April 29 or May 1947 [2-4]. They lived there for three years before moving out on July 1, 1950 [5, 6]. While they publicly claimed they were driven away by the constant, irritating stream of uninvited sightseers [6-9], the primary reason for their departure was financial: two successive failures of a mushroom-growing business in the property's sheds left them short of money [10]. *(Catherine and James looked upon their own two pet cats, Holly and Fred, as a protection against evil spirits [21, 22].)*

## Phenomena Experienced

**Orchard voices.** During May and June 1947, James Turner cleared the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] with a sickle each evening. As he broke through the undergrowth into the old orchard, the sound of laughter and chatter came clearly to both of them from the direction of the Rectory site — voices that were "happy and laughing" and fled whenever Turner dashed toward them. As soon as he stopped work, the voices ceased. Mrs Turner often came to listen as her husband worked. Once the path was clear, they returned many times but heard nothing more. *(James sometimes attributed these voices to distant aeroplanes, though Catherine was less certain [7, 37, 38].)*

**Furniture sounds.** On May 27, 1947, the day after excavations in Borley church, both Turners at breakfast were startled by loud sounds of heavy furniture being moved in the upper rooms [23-26]. James mounted the stairs to investigate — the sounds stopped and nothing had been disturbed. A similar "tremendous bang, like a pistol shot," occurred some weeks later while both were in the kitchen [31, 32].

**The "Deckchair" Incident (July 1948).** While sitting alone in a deckchair under the lime trees near the foundations of the vanished Rectory, Catherine heard heavy, distinct footsteps — sounding exactly as if walking on echoing resonant wooden floorboards, even though no house existed there [11-14]. The invisible walker came right up to her, circled around her, and walked away [12, 13]. She reported feeling no fear during the incident, only curiosity [12, 14]. The experience was repeated two weeks later at the same spot.

**Phantom cat — bedroom.** On the night of 28 July 1949 (the traditional "Nun's day"), Catherine was awakened by something scratching at the bedclothes from the floor beside the bed. Assuming one of their cats had got into the room, she lit a candle and searched — but found no cat in the room, on the landing, or at the window, which could not have been scaled. When they returned to bed they noticed a distinct dip or hollow in the surface of the bedclothes, "just as if some animal had recently been lying there" [18].

**Phantom cat — kitchen doorway.** On 28 August, exactly a month later, both Turners were looking out of the open kitchen doorway when they saw a mysterious "furry form" — thin, grey-white, with a scraggy tail, unlike any local cat — chasing their own cat Fred [15, 16, 19, 20]. It followed Fred under the car. Fred emerged and shot up the walnut tree. The mysterious creature was never seen to leave; a careful search under the open-ground car found nothing.

**Further sounds.** In September 1948, Catherine and James heard a massive crash of breaking crockery from the kitchen; they rushed in to find nothing disturbed. The noise was so loud that two workmen mixing cement outside came to the door to ask what had happened [30-32]. On August 4, 1949, while sitting in a parked car with investigators, she heard light footsteps and the sound of a freewheeling bicycle that ceased immediately when the car window was lowered [33, 34].

**Final phantom-cat incident.** On the night of 5 March 1950, Catherine was in the kitchen while her husband was in the sitting room with Fred asleep beside him. Holly had just been let outside. A piercing feline squeal came from within the cottage, apparently from the direction of the landing. Both cats were accounted for elsewhere; no explanation was found.

## The 1948 Séance and the Church Porch

On June 8, 1948, Catherine participated in a planchette séance in the Cottage alongside James, [[john-dening|Rev. John Dening]], [[gay-taylor-mass-obs|Mrs. Gay Taylor]], and Tommy Frankland. The group allegedly contacted entities claiming to be "Harry Price" and "Harry Bull" [35, 36]. The sitters subsequently moved their vigil to the locked [[borley-church|Borley Church]] porch. It was Catherine who eventually broke the uneasy silence, stating in a low voice, **"I don't think we ought to be here,"** prompting the party to abandon the vigil [11].

## Contradictions: Public Scepticism vs. Confidential Confessions

> **Contradiction:** While the Turners sometimes sought to discount their experiences as having normal origins, [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] revealed that the Turners confided in him — and in [[harry-price|Harry Price]] — under a strict promise of confidentiality that they *did* believe they had experienced genuine phenomena [39]. Catherine and James both privately admitted that they had contacted an entity claiming to be Rev. Harry Bull, and were both convinced they had glimpsed a nun-like figure near the Nun's Walk and a tall clerical figure in the churchyard. They allowed Underwood to publish these admissions after their deaths [39].

## Character

Underwood and Tabori emphasised the Turners' refusal to dramatise their experiences. The Turners left Borley in July 1950 primarily because of the "constant stream of uninvited and inquisitive visitors" — not because they were frightened.

## Later Life

After leaving Borley, Catherine and James moved to the Mill House in Belchamp Walter, then to Grundisburgh, and eventually settled in Cornwall [5-8]. James died in 1975. Catherine outlived him by many years, residing in a flat at St Giles House above Wadebridge until her death in 1992 [7, 38, 40, 41].

Before her death, she provided Underwood with James's unpublished manuscripts and periodical diaries [5, 42], and presented him with a chalice that had been made for an amateur dramatic play re-enacting the story of the Borley nun on the Nun's Walk in December 1947 [43].
