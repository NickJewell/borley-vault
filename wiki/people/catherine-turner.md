# Catherine Turner

Wife of the poet and writer [[james-turner|James Turner]], with whom she lived at [[rectory-cottage|Borley Priory cottage]] and the Rectory site from May 1947 until July 1950. Catherine was a calm, balanced witness who, like her husband, accepted the unexplained happenings at Borley as "part of the furniture" rather than a source of fear.

## Phenomena Experienced

**Orchard voices.** During May and June 1947 James Turner cleared the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] with a sickle each evening. As he broke through the undergrowth into the old orchard, the sound of laughter and chatter came clearly to both of them from the direction of the Rectory site — voices that were "happy and laughing" and fled whenever Turner dashed toward them. As soon as he stopped work, the voices ceased. Mrs Turner often came to listen as her husband worked. Once the path was clear, they returned many times but heard nothing more.

**Phantom cat — bedroom.** On the night of 28 July 1949 (the traditional "Nun's day"), Catherine was awakened by something scratching at the bedclothes from the floor beside the bed. Assuming one of their cats had got into the room, she lit a candle and searched — but found no cat in the room, on the landing, or at the window, which could not have been scaled. When they returned to bed they noticed a distinct dip or hollow in the surface of the bedclothes, "just as if some animal had recently been lying there." They could not account for it.

**Phantom cat — kitchen doorway.** On 28 August, exactly a month later, both Turners were looking out of the open kitchen doorway when they saw a mysterious "furry form" chasing their own cat Fred. Thin, grey-white, with a scraggy tail, unlike any local cat, it followed Fred under the car. Fred emerged and shot up the walnut tree. The mysterious creature was never seen to leave; a careful search under the open-ground car found nothing.

**Final phantom-cat incident.** On the night of 5 March 1950, Catherine was in the kitchen while her husband was in the sitting room with Fred asleep beside him. Holly (their other cat) had just been let outside. A piercing feline squeal came from within the cottage, apparently from the direction of the landing. Both cats were accounted for elsewhere; no explanation was found.

**Furniture sounds.** The day after the excavations in Borley church in May 1947, both Turners at breakfast were startled by loud sounds of heavy furniture being moved in the upper rooms. James Turner mounted the stairs to investigate — the sounds stopped and nothing had been disturbed. A similar "tremendous bang, like a pistol shot," occurred some weeks later while both were in the kitchen.

**Footsteps.** In July 1948, sitting in a deck chair under the lime trees near the Rectory foundations, Catherine became aware of heavy footsteps that approached, intensified, and gradually died away — "like someone walking along a passage in the house that no longer existed." The experience was repeated two weeks later at the same spot.

## Character

Underwood and Tabori emphasised the Turners' refusal to dramatise their experiences. When [[ronald-blythe|Ronald Blythe]] saw the [[phantom-cat|phantom cat]] in the bathroom, James Turner reported it the following morning "in a calm, nonchalant way, just as if the incident were quite unimportant." The Turners left Borley in July 1950 primarily because of the "constant stream of uninvited and inquisitive visitors" — not because they were frightened.

After leaving [[rectory-cottage|Borley]], the Turners lived at Belchamp Walter, then Grundisburgh, and eventually settled in Cornwall.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Catherine Turner** (also widely referred to in the sources as **Cathy Turner**, and recorded in one text by her full name, **Lucie Alice ‘Cathy’ Turner** [1]) lived at the Borley Rectory Cottage with her husband, the poet and author James Turner. They resided there during the late 1940s, a period when the main Rectory had already been destroyed but paranormal reports were still frequent. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, and lesser-known biographical details regarding her life and experiences at Borley:

**Arrival and Departure**
Catherine and James Turner purchased the Rectory Cottage and seven acres of the upper Rectory grounds (including the site of the ruined house itself) from Captain Russell for £1,500, moving in around April 29 or May 1947 [2-4]. They lived there for three years before moving out on July 1, 1950 [5, 6]. While they publicly claimed they were driven away by the constant, irritating stream of uninvited sightseers who trespassed on the grounds day and night [6-9], the primary reason for their departure was financial; two successive failures of a mushroom-growing business in the property's sheds left them short of money [10].

**The "Deckchair" Incident (July 1948)**
Catherine’s most vividly remembered experience occurred in July 1948. While sitting alone in a deckchair under the lime trees near the foundations of the vanished Rectory, she heard heavy, distinct footsteps of a man approaching her [11-14]. The footsteps sounded exactly as if they were walking on echoing, resonant wooden floorboards, even though no house existed there [13, 14]. The invisible walker came right up to her, circled around her, and walked away [12, 13]. She reported feeling no fear during the incident, only curiosity [12, 14].

**The Phantom Cat and the Hollow Bed (Summer 1949)**
Catherine was at the center of a bizarre series of events involving a spectral cat during the summer of 1949:
*   On the night of July 28 (traditionally regarded as the anniversary of the "Nun's" appearances), Catherine was awakened by the sound and sensation of something scratching at the bedclothes near the floor [15-17]. She lit a candle, and she and James searched the room but found nothing [17]. However, getting back into bed, they discovered a dip or hollow in the bedclothes at the foot of the bed, exactly as if an animal had just been lying there [18]. 
*   Exactly a month later, on August 28, Catherine and James were looking out the kitchen window when they saw a strange, grey-white, scraggy cat chase their own cat, Fred, up a walnut tree. When they went out to look under the car where the strange cat had run, it had completely vanished [15, 16, 19, 20]. 
*   *(Lesser-known detail: Catherine and James looked upon their own two pet cats, Holly and Fred, as a protection against evil spirits [21, 22].)*

**Auditory Phenomena**
Catherine witnessed a wide array of unexplained noises inside the Cottage and in the grounds:
*   **May 27, 1947:** While at breakfast, she and James heard a loud, prolonged noise from the upper rooms sounding like heavy furniture being shifted. James rushed upstairs, but the noise ceased and nothing was found disturbed [23-26].
*   **May/June 1947:** While James was using a sickle to clear the overgrown Nun's Walk, Catherine would join him and they both plainly heard the sounds of happy voices, laughter, and chatter coming from the Rectory site. The voices would instantly fade away the moment James stopped working [27-29]. 
*   **September 1948:** Catherine and James heard a massive crash of breaking crockery coming from the kitchen. They rushed in to find nothing disturbed. The noise was so loud that two workmen mixing cement outside came to the door to ask what had happened [30-32].
*   **Late 1948/1949:** She and James heard a loud report like a pistol shot that seemed to originate inside the kitchen where they were standing [31, 32], and on August 4, 1949, while sitting in a parked car with investigators, she heard light footsteps and the sound of a freewheeling bicycle that ceased immediately when the car window was lowered [33, 34]. 

**The 1948 Séance and the Church Porch**
On June 8, 1948, Catherine participated in a planchette séance in the Cottage alongside James, Rev. John Dening, Mrs. Gay Taylor, and Tommy Frankland. The group allegedly contacted entities claiming to be "Harry Price" and "Harry Bull" [35, 36]. The sitters subsequently decided to move their vigil to the locked Borley Church, sitting in the dark porch. It was Catherine who eventually broke the spell of the uneasy silence by stating in a low voice, **"I don’t think we ought to be here,"** prompting the party to abandon the vigil [11].

**Contradictions: Public Skepticism vs. Confidential Confessions**
The sources note a contradiction in how the Turners presented their experiences. While they sometimes circulated accounts of phantom animals and voices, they also sometimes sought to discount them as having normal origins (such as James blaming the "voices" on distant aeroplanes) [7, 37, 38]. However, investigator Peter Underwood revealed that the Turners confided in him (and Harry Price) under a strict promise of confidentiality that they *did* believe they had experienced genuine phenomena. Catherine and James both privately admitted that they had contacted an entity claiming to be Rev. Harry Bull, and were both convinced they had glimpsed a nun-like figure near the Nun's Walk and a tall clerical figure in the churchyard. They allowed Underwood to publish these admissions after their deaths [39]. 

**Later Life and Legacy**
After leaving Borley, Catherine and James moved to the Mill House in Belchamp Walter, then to Grundisburgh, and finally settled in Cornwall [5-8]. James died in 1975, and Catherine outlived him by many years, residing in a flat at St Giles House above Wadebridge until her death in 1992 [7, 38, 40, 41]. 

Before her death, she provided Peter Underwood with James's unpublished manuscripts and periodical diaries [5, 42]. She also presented Underwood with a unique Borley relic: a chalice that had been specially made for, and used in, an amateur dramatic play which re-enacted the story of the Borley nun on the Nun's Walk in December 1947 [43].
