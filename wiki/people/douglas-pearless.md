# Douglas Stuart Pearless

(Born 31 July 1928) Son of [[francois-darles|Frank Pearless]] (Francois d'Arles) and Kate Emily Fernie. When Frank Pearless went to [[borley-rectory]] under the name Francois d'Arles in March 1931, Douglas was known as Francois d'Arles Jr.

## Role at Borley

Most accounts describe Douglas as having been brought to the Rectory as a playmate for [[adelaide-foyster]]. [[edward-babbs]] suggests that it was Frank Pearless who advertised in *The Times* for a home for his young son, and the [[lionel-foyster|Foysters]] responded — though it could have been the other way around.

A famous photograph of [[harry-price]] together with [[kathleen-goldney]] and the Foysters was taken outside the Rectory in October 1931. The young boy holding [[marianne-foyster]]'s hand beside little Adelaide is almost certainly Douglas.

One of the few documented instances of phenomena directly involving Douglas: he and Adelaide were walking along the kitchen passage when a stone came tumbling down the back stairs. On another occasion both toddlers are reported to have "cowered together at the entrance to the Dining Room in some discomfort, with Adelaide mumbling that they did not like things flying about."

## After Borley

Little is known about Douglas following Frank Pearless's departure from Borley after the collapse of the [[jonquille-et-cie|Jonquille et Cie]] flower business. He appears to have married in June 1947. His subsequent life has never been traced or documented by researchers.

## Significance

Douglas Pearless's presence at Borley is primarily evidence of the domestic complexity of the [[foyster-incumbency]] and his father's deep integration into the Rectory household. Like Adelaide, he was a child witness to some of the phenomena, lending an additional dimension to the case: the events were experienced not just by adults with possible motives, but by young children with none.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Douglas Stuart Pearless** (also known within the Borley Rectory case as **François d'Arles Jr.**) was a young boy who lived at Borley Rectory during the turbulent Foyster incumbency in the early 1930s. Though he was only a toddler during his time at the Rectory, his presence features in some of the theories regarding the famous paranormal phenomena. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, dates, contradictions, and lesser-known biographical details about his life:

**Early Life and Maternal Abandonment**
Douglas was born on **July 31, 1928** [1]. He was the illegitimate son of Frank Charles Pearless (who later adopted the pseudonym François d'Arles) and Kate Emily Fernie, the daughter of a Worcestershire gamekeeper [1, 2]. According to the sources, Pearless and Fernie had been having an affair since 1927 while living in Wood Green, London, but the relationship did not last [1, 2]. Emily Fernie abandoned the unmarried child to Pearless's care, leaving him to raise Douglas as a single parent while holding down casual jobs [2]. 

**Arrival at Borley and the Advertisement Contradiction**
Douglas arrived at Borley Rectory in **March 1931**, when he was roughly two and a half years old [1, 3]. Because his father introduced himself to the Foysters under the false identity of a French-Canadian widower named François d'Arles, Douglas was consequently known to everyone at the Rectory as **"François d'Arles Jr."** [1, 4].

There is a distinct contradiction in the sources regarding exactly how Douglas came to live at the Rectory:
*   **The Pearless Advertisement:** Investigator Edward Babbs suggests that Frank Pearless himself placed an advertisement in *The Times* seeking a home for his young son, and that the Rev. Lionel and Marianne Foyster responded to it [1, 5]. 
*   **The Foyster Advertisement:** Conversely, other records state that it was Marianne Foyster who answered (or placed) an advertisement for a companion for her own adopted toddler, Adelaide Foyster, and that Pearless responded [5]. 

Regardless of who initiated the contact, Douglas boarded with the Foysters to act as a playmate for Adelaide (who was about the same age), while his father either lodged in the Rectory cottage or stayed in the main house [1, 5-7]. 

**Paranormal Suspicions: The "Bright as a Button" Theory**
During the Foyster incumbency, one of the most famous phenomena was the appearance of mysterious scribbles and messages on the Rectory walls. While some investigators were eager to ascribe the very first scribblings to a spirit, others suspected the Foysters' adopted daughter, Adelaide, who was "an inveterate scribbler" [8]. 

However, investigators noted that Adelaide was considered very "backward" for her age and was physically incapable of executing the complex "'m's and u's, loops and letters" found on the walls [8]. Critics of the haunting point out that the real culprit may have been little Douglas Pearless, who was described as being **"bright as a button and staying at the Rectory full-time"** [8]. 

**The Famous October 1931 Photograph**
Douglas is visually immortalized in the Borley Rectory archives. When the famous psychical researcher Harry Price visited the Rectory with Kathleen Goldney in October 1931 (during the height of the Foyster phenomena), a well-known photograph was taken on the lawn in front of the house [9]. The picture shows Price, Goldney, and the Foysters, alongside two small children. The sources note that the young boy holding Marianne Foyster’s hand next to little Adelaide is **"almost certainly Douglas"** [9].

**Departure and Later Life**
Between 1932 and 1934, when Marianne Foyster and Frank Pearless engaged in a disastrous business partnership running the "Jonquille et Cie" flower shop in Wimbledon, London, Douglas and Adelaide lived with them in the flat above the shop [10]. When the business and the relationship eventually collapsed in acrimony, Frank Pearless departed and took his son with him [9, 11]. 

Douglas Pearless's involvement in the Borley drama ended there. He appears to have **married in June 1947**, but unfortunately for historical records, he had long since ceased to be part of the Borley story and was **"never been traced or interviewed by subsequent investigators of the case"** [9].
