# Douglas Stuart Pearless

(Born 31 July 1928) Son of [[francois-darles|Frank Pearless]] (François d'Arles) and Kate Emily Fernie. When Frank Pearless went to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] under the name François d'Arles in March 1931, Douglas was known as François d'Arles Jr [1, 4].

## Early Life and Maternal Abandonment

Douglas was the illegitimate son of Frank Charles Pearless and Kate Emily Fernie, the daughter of a Worcestershire gamekeeper [1, 2]. Pearless and Fernie had been having an affair since 1927 while living in Wood Green, London, but the relationship did not last [1, 2]. Emily Fernie abandoned the unmarried child to Pearless's care, leaving him to raise Douglas as a single parent while holding down casual jobs [2].

## Arrival at Borley and the Advertisement Contradiction

Douglas arrived at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in **March 1931**, when he was roughly two and a half years old [1, 3].

> **Contradiction:** Investigator [[edward-babbs|Edward Babbs]] suggests that Frank Pearless himself placed an advertisement in *The Times* seeking a home for his young son, and that the [[lionel-foyster|Foysters]] responded [1, 5]. Other records state that it was [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] who answered (or placed) an advertisement for a companion for her own adopted toddler, [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide Foyster]], and that Pearless responded [5]. Most accounts describe Douglas as having been brought to the Rectory as a playmate for Adelaide.

Regardless of who initiated the contact, Douglas boarded with the Foysters while his father either lodged in the Rectory cottage or stayed in the main house [1, 5–7].

## Paranormal Suspicions: The "Bright as a Button" Theory

During the Foyster incumbency, one of the most famous phenomena was the appearance of mysterious scribbles and messages on the [[wall-writings|Rectory walls]]. While investigators were eager to ascribe the very first scribblings to a spirit, others suspected [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide Foyster]], who was "an inveterate scribbler" [8]. However, Adelaide was considered very "backward" for her age and was physically incapable of executing the complex "m's and u's, loops and letters" found on the walls [8]. Critics point out that the real culprit may have been Douglas Pearless, who was described as being "bright as a button and staying at the Rectory full-time" [8].

## At Borley

A famous photograph of [[harry-price|Harry Price]] together with [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen Goldney]] and the Foysters was taken outside the Rectory in October 1931. The young boy holding [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]]'s hand beside little Adelaide is almost certainly Douglas [9].

One of the few documented instances of phenomena directly involving Douglas: he and Adelaide were walking along the kitchen passage when a stone came tumbling down the back stairs. On another occasion both toddlers reportedly "cowered together at the entrance to the Dining Room in some discomfort, with Adelaide mumbling that they did not like things flying about."

## After Borley

Between 1932 and 1934, when [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] and Frank Pearless ran the disastrous "[[jonquille-et-cie|Jonquille et Cie]]" flower shop in Wimbledon, Douglas and Adelaide lived with them in the flat above the shop [10]. When the business and the relationship eventually collapsed in acrimony, Frank Pearless departed and took his son with him [9, 11].

Douglas appears to have married in June 1947, but his subsequent life has never been traced or documented by researchers. He was never tracked down and interviewed by subsequent investigators [9].

## Significance

Douglas Pearless's presence at Borley is primarily evidence of the domestic complexity of the Foyster incumbency and his father's deep integration into the Rectory household. Like [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide]], he was a child witness to some of the phenomena, lending an additional dimension to the case: the events were experienced not just by adults with possible motives, but by young children with none.
