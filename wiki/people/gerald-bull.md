# Gerald Bull

**Edward Gerald Bull** (baptised December 18, 1870; died 1940) was born at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] to the Rev. [[henry-bull|Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] and Caroline Sarah Foyster — one of fourteen children [1, 3]. He resided at the Rectory with his unmarried sisters until 1920, then moved with them to [[chilton-lodge|Chilton Lodge]] in Great Cornard, near Sudbury [5, 6]. He was interviewed by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] on March 29, 1939, during one of Price's last visits to the site before the demolition of the ruins.

## Scepticism About the Indoor Haunting

Despite growing up in "the most haunted house in England" and being surrounded by family members who claimed to see apparitions, **Gerald was entirely sceptical of the indoor phenomena** [5, 7]. He was particularly friendly with Canon Lawton, a frequent visitor to the Rectory [5]. Gerald confided to Lawton that **he had never seen anything of an abnormal nature during all the years he spent at Borley** [5, 7, 8].

He completely dismissed his sisters' famous sightings of the phantom nun. Gerald firmly held the view that the spectral nun was nothing more than a **"product of feminine imagination"** (also recorded as a "figment of feminine imagination") [5, 7].

## Phantom Footsteps on the Lane

In a notable contradiction to his dismissal of the indoor hauntings, Gerald did claim to experience a persistent auditory phenomenon *outside* the house. On March 29, 1939, [[harry-price|Price]] and [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. A. C. Henning]] formally interviewed Gerald [9, 10]. During this interview, Gerald recounted that when walking up the lane approaching the Rectory, **he had heard footsteps following him "at least fifty times"** [9, 10]. He stated that he always turned around to look, but never saw anyone and could not rationally account for the sounds [9].

The accumulation of fifty experiences over a number of years — gathered by a person born in the house and thoroughly familiar with its surroundings — makes this one of the more robust corroborations of the lane-footsteps phenomenon. It rules out startle-effect, unfamiliarity with the environment, and suggests the sounds were distinctive enough to be noticed consistently throughout a lifetime's acquaintance with the place.

This testimony confirms accounts from multiple other witnesses of auditory phenomena on and near the road outside the Rectory. The "hollow road" — noted by Price as producing unusually resonant sounds when walked on — runs immediately past the front of the Rectory and was suggested by Price to overlay an underground tunnel. See also [[mysterious-footsteps]] for the broader record of footstep phenomena at Borley.

## Bringing the Foysters to Borley

Gerald Bull played a direct, though perhaps unwitting, role in sparking the most violent and controversial period of the Borley hauntings [11, 12]. In 1930, his cousin, [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]], and his wife [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] had returned from Canada and were struggling to find affordable, suitable housing [11, 12]. While the Foysters were visiting the Bulls at [[chilton-lodge|Chilton Lodge]] and discussing their difficulties, Gerald offered an alternative suggestion. He persuaded them to move into the empty [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] with the quote that it **"was always such fun"** [11, 12]. The Foysters took his advice, setting the stage for the infamous 1930–1935 haunting outbreak.
