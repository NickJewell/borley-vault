# Gerald Bull

One of the children of the Rev. [[henry-bull|Henry D. E. Bull]], born at Borley Rectory during the family's long occupancy. Interviewed by [[harry-price]] on March 29, 1939, during one of Price's last visits to the site before the demolition of the ruins.

## Phantom Footsteps on the Lane

Gerald Bull provided striking testimony about one of the most persistently reported phenomena at Borley: the phantom footsteps heard on the lane approaching the Rectory.

He told Price that when walking up the lane and approaching the Rectory, he had heard footsteps following him **at least fifty times**. On each occasion he turned round but never saw anyone, and could find no normal explanation.

The accumulation of fifty experiences over a number of years — gathered by a person born in the house and thoroughly familiar with its surroundings — makes this one of the more robust corroborations of the lane-footsteps phenomenon. It rules out startle-effect, unfamiliarity with the environment, and suggests the sounds were distinctive enough to be noticed consistently throughout a lifetime's acquaintance with the place.

## Context

This testimony confirms accounts from multiple other witnesses of auditory phenomena on and near the road outside the Rectory. The "hollow road" — noted by Price as producing unusually resonant sounds when walked on — runs immediately past the front of the Rectory and was suggested by Price to overlay an underground tunnel.

See also [[mysterious-footsteps]] for the broader record of footstep phenomena at Borley.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Edward Gerald Bull** (baptized December 18, 1870; died 1940) was a prominent member of the Bull family dynasty and a central, though highly skeptical, figure in the Borley Rectory narrative [1, 2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, quotes, and contradictions regarding his life and involvement:

**Biographical Details**
Born at Borley Rectory to the Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull and Caroline Sarah Foyster, he was one of fourteen children [1, 3]. The 1871 census records him living at the Rectory at five months of age [4]. He continued to reside at the Rectory with his unmarried sisters until 1920 [5]. Following this, he moved with his sisters to Chilton Lodge in Great Cornard, near Sudbury, where he lived for many years [6]. 

**Skepticism and Quotes on the Haunting**
Despite growing up in "the most haunted house in England" and being surrounded by family members who claimed to see apparitions, **Gerald was entirely skeptical of the indoor phenomena** [5, 7]. He was particularly friendly with Canon Lawton, a frequent visitor to the Rectory [5]. Gerald confided to Lawton that **he had never seen anything of an abnormal nature during all the years he spent at Borley** [5, 7, 8]. 

Furthermore, he completely dismissed his sisters' famous sightings of the phantom nun. Gerald firmly held the view that the spectral nun was nothing more than a **"product of feminine imagination"** (also recorded as a "figment of feminine imagination") [5, 7]. 

**A Contradictory Paranormal Experience**
In a notable contradiction to his dismissal of the indoor hauntings, Gerald did claim to experience a persistent auditory phenomenon *outside* the house [9]. On March 29, 1939, psychical researcher Harry Price (along with the Rev. A. C. Henning) formally interviewed Gerald [9, 10]. During this interview, Gerald recounted that when walking up the lane approaching the Rectory, **he had heard footsteps following him "at least fifty times"** [9, 10]. He stated that he always turned around to look, but never saw anyone and could not rationally account for the sounds [9]. 

**Bringing the Foysters to Borley**
Gerald Bull played a direct, though perhaps unwitting, role in sparking the most violent and controversial period of the Borley hauntings [11, 12]. In 1930, his cousin, the Rev. Lionel Foyster, and his wife Marianne had returned from Canada and were struggling to find affordable, suitable housing in the district [11, 12]. While the Foysters were visiting the Bulls at Chilton Lodge and discussing their housing difficulties, Gerald offered an alternative suggestion [12]. He suggested they move into the empty Borley Rectory, persuading them with the quote that the Rectory **"was always such fun"** [11, 12]. The Foysters took his advice, setting the stage for the infamous 1930–1935 haunting outbreak [11, 12].
