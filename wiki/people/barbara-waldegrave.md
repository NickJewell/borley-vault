# Barbara Waldegrave (Sister Mary Apollonia)

Born **Barbara Waldegrave** at Borley, 1603; died April 18, 1638, Brussels, aged approximately 36. Daughter of **Sir Nicholas Waldegrave of Borley** and his wife Catherine, née Browne, of Wellhall, Essex. One of two Waldegrave daughters born at Borley who became nuns (the other being Jeronima, who joined the Ghent community and died of consumption).

## Conventual Career

Barbara entered the **English Benedictine community in Brussels** (an expatriate order of English nuns in exile on the Continent):
- Entered the order: 1622
- Received the habit: September 12, 1623
- Professed as a nun: November 24, 1624
- Died: April 18, 1638, aged about 36

The Brussels chronicle describes her death as **"happily deceased"** — a phrase [[Ivan Banks|Banks]] regards as possibly ambiguous, potentially meaning "fortunately deceased" rather than straightforwardly "died in contentment."

## Banks's Case for Barbara as the Ghost Nun

[[Ivan Banks]] concluded in *The Enigma of Borley Rectory* (1994) that Barbara Waldegrave is the most likely candidate for the identity of the [[The Nun|Borley ghost nun]], using the Holmesian argument: of all the Waldegrave nuns investigated, she is:

1. **Born at Borley** — of the Borley branch of the Waldegrave family
2. **A professed nun** — not a novice or secular Waldegrave woman
3. **Died young** — aged about 36; the Borley bone fragments were estimated at a person "possibly in her thirties"
4. **Cause of death unexplained** — the "happily deceased" entry does not specify illness or natural causes
5. **Connected to Brussels Benedictines** — who eventually re-settled at East Bergholt, Suffolk (18 miles from Borley) after fleeing the French Revolution in 1794

Banks contrasts this with:
- **Joanna Waldegrave** (Sister Theodosia) — died 1719, not 1667; ruled out
- **Arabella Waldegrave** (granddaughter of James II) — became Paris Benedictine nun; Countess Mary Waldegrave confirmed she did not disappear, keeping correspondence with her mother and brother
- **Marie Lairre** as a named historical person — no documentary confirmation found in Normandy

> **Contradiction:** The [[séances|planchette sessions]] consistently identified the nun as "Marie Lairre" (French, from Le Havre, murdered 1667). Banks's Barbara Waldegrave theory would place the nun about 30 years earlier and in Belgium rather than France. Banks argues the séance contacts were fragmentary, multiple entities were overlapping, and place-names were misinterpreted.

## Sibling

Her sister **Jeronima Waldegrave** also became a nun (at Ghent), and died from consumption. Banks notes that of the two Borley-born Waldegrave nuns, the one whose cause of death was not straightforward consumption is Barbara.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Barbara Waldegrave** (who took the religious name **Sister Mary Apollonia** or **Apolonia**) was the daughter of Sir Nicholas Waldegrave of Borley and his wife Catherine (née Browne) of Wellhall, Essex [1, 2]. Born at Borley in 1603, she was the granddaughter of the prominent Catholic figure Sir Edward Waldegrave, though he died long before she was born [2, 3]. Along with her sister Jeronima (who became a nun at Ghent), Barbara was one of the few literal "Borley Nuns," having been born directly into the Borley arm of the Waldegrave family [2]. 

A thorough reference profile based on the Borley sources reveals her as the primary historical candidate proposed by researcher Ivan Banks for the identity of the famous "phantom nun." 

**Religious Life and Death**
Barbara entered the Brussels community of English Benedictine nuns in exile in 1622 [1, 3]. She officially received the habit on September 12, 1623, and was professed as a nun, taking the name Apollonia, on November 24, 1624 [1, 2]. 

She died on **April 18, 1638** [1, 2]. A minor contradiction exists in the sources regarding her exact age at the time of her death: one passage states she died at the age of 38 [1], while another states she was only 36 years old [2]. 

**The Ivan Banks Theory and the 1943 Bone Fragments**
Barbara Waldegrave is central to the theories of Borley author Ivan Banks. When Harry Price excavated the cellars of the ruined Borley Rectory in 1943, he discovered a human jawbone that a dental surgeon subsequently identified as belonging to a woman in her thirties [1, 2]. Because Barbara Waldegrave died in her thirties and was a Catholic nun actually born at Borley, Banks hypothesized that she was the true identity of the phantom nun, eliminating other candidates whose origins were tied to France [1, 4]. 

**The "Happily Deceased" Quote and the "Ghost of Conscience"**
A major physical contradiction to Banks's theory is that Barbara Waldegrave actually died in Belgium, and her remains presumably still lie there—making her burial under the Borley Rectory cellar highly unlikely [1]. 

To reconcile why her ghost would haunt Borley if she was not murdered and buried there, Banks scrutinized the Brussels monastic chronicles. The chronicles recorded her death simply with the phrase **"happily deceased"** [5, 6]. Banks argued this could be a "double-edged remark," noting that while it might be an innocent religious sentiment, it could also conceal the possibility that she was considered *"fortunately deceased"* by her peers [5]. 

Because her sister Jeronima's death was clearly recorded as being from consumption, while Barbara's early death carried no such clear medical cause, Banks theorized that Barbara may not have been a murder victim at all [2, 6]. Instead, he proposed she was a **"ghost of conscience"** who died unfulfilled in her religious life, carrying some deep, internal spiritual conflict [6, 7]. According to this theory, she returned in spirit to her native East Anglia, doomed to wander the grounds of Borley Rectory "waiting after some four hundred years for release from her doubts and misgivings of some long-forgotten moment in history" [6, 8].
