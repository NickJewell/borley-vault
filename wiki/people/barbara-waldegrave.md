# Barbara Waldegrave (Sister Mary Apollonia)

Born **Barbara Waldegrave** at Borley, 1603; died April 18, 1638, Brussels. Daughter of **Sir Nicholas Waldegrave of Borley** and his wife Catherine, née Browne, of Wellhall, Essex — and granddaughter of the prominent Catholic figure [[sir-edward-waldegrave]], though he died long before she was born. One of two Waldegrave daughters born at Borley who became nuns (the other being [[jeronima-waldegrave|Jeronima]], who joined the Ghent community and died of consumption).

## Conventual Career

Barbara entered the **English Benedictine community in Brussels** (an expatriate order of English nuns in exile on the Continent):
- Entered the order: 1622
- Received the habit: September 12, 1623
- Professed as a nun (taking the name Apollonia): November 24, 1624
- Died: April 18, 1638

> **Contradiction:** The sources disagree on her exact age at death. One passage states she died at 38 [1]; another gives her age as approximately 36 [2]. Given her recorded birth year of 1603, she was most likely 34 or 35.

The Brussels chronicle describes her death as **"happily deceased"** — a phrase [[ivan-banks|Banks]] regards as possibly ambiguous, potentially meaning "fortunately deceased" rather than straightforwardly "died in contentment."

## Banks's Case for Barbara as the Ghost Nun

[[ivan-banks]] concluded in *The Enigma of Borley Rectory* (1994) that Barbara Waldegrave is the most likely candidate for the identity of the [[the-nun|Borley ghost nun]], using the Holmesian argument: of all the Waldegrave nuns investigated, she is:

1. **Born at Borley** — of the Borley branch of the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]]
2. **A professed nun** — not a novice or secular Waldegrave woman
3. **Died young** — in her thirties; the Borley bone fragments were estimated at a person "possibly in her thirties"
4. **Cause of death unexplained** — the "happily deceased" entry does not specify illness or natural causes
5. **Connected to Brussels Benedictines** — who eventually re-settled at East Bergholt, Suffolk (18 miles from Borley) after fleeing the French Revolution in 1794

Banks contrasts this with:
- **[[joanna-waldegrave|Joanna Waldegrave]]** (Sister Theodosia) — died 1719, not 1667; ruled out
- **[[arabella-waldegrave|Arabella Waldegrave]]** (granddaughter of James II) — became a Paris Benedictine nun; Countess Mary Waldegrave confirmed she did not disappear, keeping correspondence with her mother and brother
- **[[marie-lairre]]** as a named historical person — no documentary confirmation found in Normandy

> **Contradiction:** The [[séances|planchette sessions]] consistently identified the nun as "Marie Lairre" (French, from Le Havre, murdered 1667). Banks's Barbara Waldegrave theory would place the nun about 30 years earlier and in Belgium rather than France. Banks argues the séance contacts were fragmentary, multiple entities were overlapping, and place-names were misinterpreted.

## The "Ghost of Conscience" Theory

A major physical problem with Banks's theory is that Barbara Waldegrave died in Belgium, and her remains presumably still lie there — making her burial under the Borley Rectory cellar highly unlikely [1]. To reconcile why her ghost would haunt Borley if she was not murdered and buried there, Banks scrutinised the Brussels monastic chronicles closely.

Because Jeronima's death was clearly recorded as being from consumption while Barbara's carried no such clear medical cause, Banks proposed she was not a murder victim at all [2]. Instead, he theorised she was a **"ghost of conscience"** who died unfulfilled in her religious life, carrying some deep internal spiritual conflict [6]. According to this interpretation, she returned in spirit to her native East Anglia, reportedly doomed to wander the grounds of Borley Rectory "waiting after some four hundred years for release from her doubts and misgivings of some long-forgotten moment in history" [6, 8].

## Sibling

Her sister **Jeronima Waldegrave** also became a nun (at Ghent), and died from consumption. Banks notes that of the two Borley-born Waldegrave nuns, the one whose cause of death was not straightforward consumption is Barbara.
