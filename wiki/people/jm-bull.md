# J. M. Bull

Author of *[[a-short-history-borley-churches|A Short History of Borley and Liston Churches]]* (1937), a booklet describing the architecture and history of [[borley-church|Borley Church]] and Liston Church. This work is the primary architectural source cited by Underwood and Tabori in their Chapter 6 description of the church building.

J. M. Bull's relationship to the [[henry-bull|Bull family]] of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] is not explicitly stated in *Ghosts of Borley*. Given the family's long incumbency and their surname, a family connection is plausible but not confirmed.

## The 1937 History of Borley and Liston Churches

Bull's 1937 publication provides a detailed architectural and historical breakdown of Borley Church, which sits opposite the site of the Rectory [1]. His principal findings:

- **Structure and Composition:** The church is a small building composed chiefly of flint rubble, consisting of a chancel, a nave, a south porch, and a west tower [1].
- **Twelfth-Century Origins:** Bull theorised it was highly probable that a church was originally built on the site during the twelfth century, and that its chancel and nave were the same length as the modern ones [1].
- **The c.1500 Rebuilding:** The original chancel and north wall eventually deteriorated, leading to a significant rebuilding phase around 1500, during which the three-stage tower and the brick south porch were added [1].
- **Windows:** The church's small original windows were replaced during the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries. Bull specifically identified the middle window in the north wall of the nave as the earliest surviving window, dating to the fourteenth century, preserved when the rest of the wall was rebuilt [1].
- **The Tower:** The three-stage west tower features gargoyles projecting from each of its four sides [1]. The doorway to the tower's staircase leads to winding steps "worn by many feet," ascending to a top that provides a panoramic view of the surrounding countryside [1].

This architectural detail — the twelfth-century origin, the c.1500 rebuilding of chancel and north wall, the addition of the tower and south porch, and the replacement of original windows — is the framework used by Underwood and Tabori in their Chapter 6 description of the church.
