# Miss Vivienne Wilson

The 13-year-old sister-in-law of [[mrs-ag-wilson|Mrs A. G. Wilson]], with whom she cycled to Borley at the end of **August 1949** [1, 2]. They had heard the Rectory was haunted, but had no idea the building had already been destroyed by fire, and no prior knowledge that the haunting phenomena allegedly extended to the nearby church [1, 2].

## The Phantom Organ Music

Upon discovering only the ruins of the Rectory, the two women decided to look at [[borley-church|Borley Church]] out of mere "general interest" [2]. Finding the main door locked, they walked along a small side path. While walking along this path, both women independently heard the distinct sound of an organ playing from within the building [1, 2].

Before [[mrs-ag-wilson|Mrs Wilson]] could voice her thoughts about the music, Vivienne called attention to it first, asking: **"Can you hear the organ?"** [2].

Not realising they were experiencing anything unusual, they gave the music "not a second thought" at the time [2]. Finding the side door also locked, they left the grounds.

The significance of the encounter only became apparent when they stopped at a nearby cottage and purchased a copy of [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Henning]]'s recently published 1949 booklet, *Haunted Borley* [2]. It was only upon reading the booklet that they discovered the church's reputation for phantom organ music, prompting Mrs Wilson to write a formal report of their experience from her home in Cricklewood in February 1950 [1, 2].

## Significance

Her age, spontaneous reaction, and complete ignorance of the church's paranormal reputation make this a clean, independent observation. Peter Underwood met Vivienne in person and confirmed the details of her account. The fact that she spoke first — before her sister-in-law could prompt her — rules out the possibility that she was responding to a cue.
