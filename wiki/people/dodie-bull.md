# Caroline Sarah "Dodie" Bull (1864–1929)

Eldest daughter of [[henry-bull|Reverend Henry Dawson Bull]]; arguably the single most important figure in the creation of the Borley haunting legend.

## Background

Born 17 April 1864 at Pentlow Rectory; moved to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] shortly after. Second child of Henry and Caroline Bull. Her diary of 1885 (age 21) is a remarkable document: frank, sexually awake, frustrated by the limited horizon of a country rector's daughter's life.

> "I long to do something wicked even for the sake of change. I have an intensely wicked heart I fear... I have a strong imagination and a rather good short stories."

## Role in Creating the Legend

O'Connor's analysis (hbrs) makes Dodie the primary architect of the haunting legend:
- She and her brother Harry **"encouraged the notion of the Rectory being haunted by the tragic nun and it soon became part of family lore."**
- She experimented with spiritualism: diary entry records she "made a chair walk all over the room, and answer questions."
- The summer 1885 visit of Percy Shaw Jeffrey (Harry's Oxford friend, interested in folklore and witchcraft) was probably engineered partly by Dodie to impress him — the stones, boots on the wardrobe, and dictionary that disappeared were consistent with a teenage prank between two sisters competing for his affections.
- She was the dominant imaginative force in a household of 13 children and many servants, with access to the latest Gothic fiction, sensation novels, and spiritualist literature.

## Life Outside Borley

- Sent to nurse typhoid patients at Vichy with the Red Cross during WWI; served four years; decorated for service; fell ill herself.
- Her brother Basil was killed in Belgium on 1 August 1917.
- Her husband died in 1909; she did not return to Borley but established an independent life in Wandsworth, London, with her sister Mabel. They named their terrace house "Borley."
- She chose to be buried at Borley, east of the church, next to [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] — a poignant choice. "A sad sight: all of the Bull family graves have been vandalised." (hbrs-aft)
- Died 29 November 1937 in a nursing home in Lowestoft, aged 73, of heart disease and dementia.

## Significance

She lived at the Rectory during the formative period when the legend was being assembled. Her "strong imagination" and "rather good short stories," her frustration at being denied the freedom available to her brothers, and her enthusiasm for spiritualism created the cultural conditions in which the Borley ghost became "part of family lore" — the tradition that every subsequent generation of witnesses inherited.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Caroline Sarah Elizabeth Bull**, affectionately known in the family as **"Dodie"** to avoid confusion with her mother, was the eldest daughter and second child of the Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull and Caroline Sarah Foyster [1, 2]. The sources present a minor contradiction regarding her exact birth date: one text states she was born on February 18, 1864, and baptized on April 17 [1], while another asserts she was born on April 17, 1864 [2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, contradictions, and paranormal connections regarding her life:

**The 1884-1885 Diary and Sibling Rivalry**
Within the large Bull family, Dodie was educated at home and, in turn, helped provide basic schooling for her younger sisters [3]. A significant primary source regarding her early life is a personal diary she kept between December 1884 and October 1885, which was discovered in a Sudbury second-hand shop in 1970 by investigator Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth [1, 4]. 

The diary paints a vivid and unexpectedly risqué portrait of a passionate, frustrated young woman navigating Victorian social life [5]. She frequently attended local balls, noting in one entry that the Rectory nursery was used for "spooning" and that she had engaged in **"a hot flirtation with Charlie"** [6]. Despite her active social life, she lamented her perceived lack of marital prospects, noting at barely twenty-one years old: **"How old one begins to feel. What a crop of old maids are growing up"** [6]. Exhibiting deep insecurity, she also wrote, **"Why wasn’t I pretty and fascinating? Damn my ugly face, it won’t attract anyone, worse luck"** [5]. 

The diary also reveals a fierce sibling rivalry with her sister Freda over P. Shaw Jeffrey, an Oxford friend of her brother Harry who visited the Rectory. Dodie bluntly declared: **"I mean to go in for him. Freda had better not thwart me for I mean to have him"** [7].

**Marriage, Widowhood, and War Heroism**
Dodie holds a unique distinction among the Rev. Henry Bull's daughters, as **she was the only one to ever marry** [8]. She became the wife of the Rev. William Hayden and moved to Dent in Yorkshire [8, 9]. Following her husband's death in 1909, she chose not to return to Borley, but instead established an independent life in Wandsworth, South London, alongside her sister Mabel [10]. In a nostalgic tribute to their childhood home that showed no fear of its ghosts, the two sisters named their terraced house "Borley" [10].

A remarkable and lesser-known biographical detail is her service during the First World War [11]. Seeking adventure, the widowed **Dodie joined the Red Cross as a nurse and was deployed to a military hospital in Vichy, France, to care for typhoid patients** [11]. She was highly esteemed by the doctors she worked under, remaining there for four years until she fell ill herself, eventually returning home decorated for her service [11]. 

**Paranormal Connections and Psychokinesis**
Dodie is famously central to the collective sighting of the phantom nun on July 28, 1900 [12, 13]. When her sisters Ethel, Freda, and Mabel spotted a dark figure on the lawn, one of them ran into the house to fetch Dodie [12, 14]. Initially dismissing their fears as "nonsense," Dodie went outside, saw the figure, and **bravely walked toward it to offer help**, assuming it was a real Sister of Mercy who had become lost [14, 15]. The figure reportedly turned toward her with an expression of intense grief before completely vanishing [14]. 

Evidence of Dodie's own lifelong fascination with the supernatural survives in her diary. It records that following the sudden death of a neighbor in 1885, Dodie conducted a "chair-moving" or "chair-walking" experiment (similar to a Ouija board) with two of her sisters and their Swiss governess, Marie Rolt [16, 17]. Researchers analyzing the diary entries noted that **Dodie herself appeared to possess psychokinetic abilities**, seemingly able to will objects to move unaided [18]. 

**Death and Burial**
Dodie died on November 29, 1937, at the age of 73 [19]. There is a geographical contradiction in the texts regarding her death: one source claims she died in Felixstowe [8], while another asserts she died in a nursing home in Lowestoft from heart disease and dementia [19]. A funeral service, attended by Marianne Foyster, was held at Borley Church on December 3, 1937 [20]. Dodie was buried in the Borley churchyard directly next to her brother, Harry Bull, positioned looking out over the Stour Valley behind their parents' graves [21].
