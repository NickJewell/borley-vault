# Caroline Sarah "Dodie" Bull (1864–1937)

Eldest daughter of [[henry-bull|Reverend Henry Dawson Bull]]; arguably the single most important figure in the creation of the Borley haunting legend. She was known in the family as "Dodie" to avoid confusion with her mother, who shared the given name Caroline.

> **Contradiction:** One source records her birth date as 18 February 1864, baptised 17 April [1], while another asserts she was born on 17 April 1864 [2].

## Background

Born at Pentlow Rectory; moved to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] shortly after. Second child of Henry and Caroline Bull. Dodie was educated at home and, in turn, helped provide basic schooling for her younger sisters [3].

Her diary of 1885 (age 21) is a remarkable document: frank, sexually awake, frustrated by the limited horizon of a country rector's daughter's life. She attended local balls, noting in one entry that the Rectory nursery was used for "spooning" and that she had engaged in "a hot flirtation with Charlie" [6]. Despite her active social life, she lamented her perceived lack of marital prospects: "How old one begins to feel. What a crop of old maids are growing up" [6]. She also wrote, "Why wasn't I pretty and fascinating? Damn my ugly face, it won't attract anyone, worse luck" [5].

> "I long to do something wicked even for the sake of change. I have an intensely wicked heart I fear... I have a strong imagination and a rather good short stories."

The diary also reveals a fierce sibling rivalry with her sister [[freda-bull|Freda]] over [[p-shaw-jeffrey|P. Shaw Jeffrey]], an Oxford friend of her brother Harry who visited the Rectory. Dodie declared: "I mean to go in for him. Freda had better not thwart me for I mean to have him" [7].

## Role in Creating the Legend

O'Connor's analysis (hbrs) makes Dodie the primary architect of the haunting legend:
- She and her brother Harry "encouraged the notion of the Rectory being haunted by the tragic nun and it soon became part of family lore."
- Her diary records she "made a chair walk all over the room, and answer questions" — evidence of her own lifelong fascination with the supernatural. Researchers analysing the diary entries noted that Dodie apparently possessed psychokinetic abilities, seemingly able to will objects to move unaided [18].
- The summer 1885 visit of [[p-shaw-jeffrey|Percy Shaw Jeffrey]] (Harry's Oxford friend, interested in folklore and witchcraft) was probably engineered partly by Dodie to impress him — the stones, boots on the wardrobe, and dictionary that disappeared were consistent with a teenage prank between two sisters competing for his affections.
- She was the dominant imaginative force in a household of 13 children and many servants, with access to the latest Gothic fiction, sensation novels, and spiritualist literature.

## The 1900 Nun Sighting

Dodie is centrally associated with the collective sighting of the phantom nun on [[july-28th|July 28, 1900]] [12, 13]. When her sisters [[ethel-bull|Ethel]], [[freda-bull|Freda]], and [[mabel-bull|Mabel]] spotted a dark figure on the lawn, one of them ran into the house to fetch Dodie [12, 14]. Initially dismissing their fears as "nonsense," Dodie went outside, saw the figure, and **bravely walked toward it to offer help**, assuming it was a real Sister of Mercy who had become lost [14, 15]. The figure reportedly turned toward her with an expression of intense grief before completely vanishing [14].

## Marriage, Widowhood, and War Heroism

Dodie holds a unique distinction among [[henry-bull|Rev. Henry Bull]]'s daughters as **the only one to ever marry** [8]. She became the wife of the Rev. William Hayden and moved to Dent in Yorkshire [8, 9]. Following her husband's death in 1909, she chose not to return to Borley, but instead established an independent life in Wandsworth, South London, alongside her sister [[mabel-bull|Mabel]] [10]. In a nostalgic tribute to their childhood home, the two sisters named their terraced house "Borley" [10].

A remarkable biographical detail is her service during the First World War [11]. The widowed Dodie joined the Red Cross as a nurse and was deployed to a military hospital in Vichy, France, to care for typhoid patients [11]. She was highly esteemed by the doctors she worked under, remaining there for four years until she fell ill herself, eventually returning home decorated for her service [11]. Her brother Basil was killed in Belgium on 1 August 1917.

## Death and Burial

> **Contradiction:** One source claims Dodie died in Felixstowe [8], while another asserts she died on **29 November 1937** in a nursing home in Lowestoft from heart disease and dementia [19], at the age of 73.

A funeral service, attended by [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]], was held at [[borley-church|Borley Church]] on 3 December 1937 [20]. Dodie was buried in the Borley churchyard directly next to her brother [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]], positioned looking out over the Stour Valley behind their parents' graves [21] — a poignant choice for a woman who had spent decades away from the village. "A sad sight: all of the Bull family graves have been vandalised." (hbrs-aft)
