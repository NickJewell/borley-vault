# Mary Lycett

Researcher from Surbiton, Surrey, who wrote a letter dated August 17, 1952, addressed to Harry Price's files, setting out an elaborate and imaginative theory connecting [[arabella-waldegrave|Arabella Waldegrave]] and the séance entity [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] into a single narrative. Banks quotes the theory at length and analyses it carefully, finding it a creative synthesis of earlier material rather than independent evidence.

## The Lycett Theory

Mary Lycett proposed that the "ghost nun" Marie Lairre was in fact **Arabella Waldegrave**, the granddaughter of King James II, who had taken the religious name "Soeur Marie Lairre" upon entering a Parisian convent. The full scenario she describes:

1. The infant Arabella is taken to France with her parents (the Waldegraves following King James II into exile) in 1688
2. She is placed in the convent at Pontoise to be educated
3. Against her inclination, she is destined to become a nun; she begins her novitiate in Paris, taking the name "Soeur Marie Lairre"
4. She falls in love with her cousin Henry Waldegrave, son of Philip Lord Waldegrave of Borley
5. Henry Waldegrave effects her escape from a convent near Le Havre
6. They meet Arabella's mother Henrietta (now a spy for the Hanoverian party), who entrusts Arabella with secret documents
7. Henry marries Arabella; they sail for England, arriving at Langenhoe in Essex
8. Henry places Marie in the old Rectory at Borley; tells his father; is sent away
9. Marie waits at the gate each evening watching for Henry's return
10. Lord Waldegrave prepares to send her back; she tries to escape with a servant "Janen" and a coachman "Enoch"
11. Henry and Lord Waldegrave intercept them; Enoch is killed; Janen murdered to silence her; Marie is imprisoned in a priest's cell and eventually strangled by Henry

Banks's note: "I must offer a note of caution. If one studies the Pythian Adams scenario and then Georgina Dawson's apparent revelations concerning Arabella Waldegrave, and takes into account some of the ideas resulting from the planchette experiments, it becomes apparent that Mary Lycett's theory is something of a mix of the two."

## Evaluation

Banks's key observation is that Lycett's theory was written **after** Harry Price's *The End of Borley Rectory* (1946), which contained both Canon Phythian-Adams's scenario and the details about Arabella Waldegrave. "It is rather a pity that Mary Lycett's theory came to light after and not before Price's publication, for had the latter been the case, it might have carried more clout as a possible train of events."

However, Banks finds the theory partially vindicated by subsequent research: the [[countess-mary-waldegrave|Countess Mary Waldegrave]] confirmed that Arabella did become a nun in Paris and maintained correspondence — she did not simply disappear. This collapses part of Lycett's narrative (the murder) but does not entirely invalidate the romantic-involvement subplot.

## Significance

Lycett's theory is significant as a synthetic reading of the evidence that anticipates several connections Banks independently confirms through archival research — particularly the Pontoise/Paris Benedictine connection and the Waldegrave family exile. Her confusion of the two Arabellas ([[arabella-fitzjames|Arabella FitzJames/Sister Ignatia]] vs. the granddaughter [[arabella-waldegrave|Arabella Waldegrave]]) leads to errors, but the underlying intuition — that the ghost nun is a Waldegrave — Banks regards as sound.

**Mary Lycett** was a resident of Surbiton, Surrey, who contributed one of the most elaborate and sensational hypotheses regarding the identity of the famous Borley phantom nun [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following specific details, dates, events, and contradictions regarding her theoretical contributions to the case:

**The August 1952 "Marie Lairre/Arabella" Theory**
On **August 17, 1952**, Lycett wrote a letter setting out a highly detailed, dramatic theory that attempted to synthesize the disparate legends of the Borley haunting [1]. Her theory, which was later published in Ivan Banks's 1996 book *The Enigma of Borley Rectory*, combined the earlier hypotheses of Canon Phythian-Adams (who theorized about a murdered French nun named Marie Lairre) and local historian Georgina Dawson (who theorized about a missing Waldegrave daughter) [2]. 

Lycett boldly claimed that **Arabella Waldegrave and Marie Lairre were the exact same person** [2]. 

According to Lycett's narrative:
*   **Early Life:** Arabella, the grand-daughter of King James II, was taken by her loyalist parents to France in 1668, where she was placed in the convent school of Pontoise to be educated [1]. Against her own inclination, she took vows and assumed the name **"Soeur Marie Lairre"** [2].
*   **The Romance and Escape:** She fell in love with her handsome cousin, Henry Waldegrave, who helped her escape the convent before her final vows were taken. They secretly married and fled back to England [2, 3].
*   **Imprisonment at Borley:** Upon their return, Marie/Arabella was hidden at Borley and placed under the strict, secluded care of family servants named **"Old Amos," his wife, and a serving girl named Janen** [3].
*   **The Stuart Spy Plot:** Expanding on Dawson's spy theories, Lycett claimed that Arabella's mother (Henrietta) was a Stuart spy. Arabella had been secretly entrusted with documents to deliver to the Hanoverian party in London, a fact she kept hidden from her husband, Henry [3].

**The Tragic Climax**
Lycett's theory provided a highly specific, bloody conclusion to the story:
*   The serving girl, Janen, persuaded her sweetheart, **Enoch**, to bring a coach to help Marie escape to London [4]. 
*   As Marie and Janen were boarding the coach in the dark, they were ambushed by Lord Waldegrave, Henry, and Amos. Enoch was stabbed to death by Henry in a jealous fury, and Janen was murdered to silence her and thrown into an old plague pit [4].
*   The Waldegraves discovered the secret spy documents and realized Marie's "treachery." She was **imprisoned in a secret priest's cell in the old crypt, starved, taunted, and denied water** to induce a confession [4]. 
*   Finally, an infuriated Henry strangled her. Her body was dumped in a stream and later, fearing discovery, the Waldegraves dredged the stream and **scattered her bones under the deserted Rectory floor** [4]. The family believed they had performed an "act of justice" on a woman who had betrayed their religion and their King [5].

**Contradictions and Criticisms of Lycett's Account**
Investigators and authors have pointed out several factual errors and logical issues with Lycett's romanticized version of events:
*   **Geographical Error:** In her 1952 letter, Lycett claimed that Baron Waldegrave was of **"Chewton, Devon,"** but researchers noted this was a geographical mistake, as the Waldegrave seat of Chewton Mendip is actually located in Somerset [1].
*   **Romantic Fiction:** Critics note that her theory strays heavily into the "realms of romantic fiction" [3], reading more like **"a novel by Georgette Heyer or Barbara Cartland"** than a serious historical hypothesis [2].
*   **Derivative Origins:** Ivan Banks points out that Canon Phythian-Adams’s sequence of events and Georgina Dawson’s theories about Arabella Waldegrave had both been heavily detailed in Harry Price’s book *The End of Borley Rectory*, which was published in 1946 [5]. Banks notes that it is **"a pity that Mary Lycett's theory came to light after and not before Price's publication,"** because its emergence six years *after* Price's book makes it highly likely that she simply absorbed his published material and fused it together into a single, sensational story [5].
