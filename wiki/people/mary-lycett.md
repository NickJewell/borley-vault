# Mary Lycett

Researcher from Surbiton, Surrey, who on **17 August 1952** wrote a letter to Harry Price's files setting out an elaborate theory connecting [[arabella-waldegrave|Arabella Waldegrave]] and the séance entity [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] into a single narrative [1]. Banks quotes the theory at length in *The Enigma of Borley Rectory* and analyses it carefully, finding it a creative synthesis of earlier published material rather than independent evidence.

## The Lycett Theory

Mary Lycett boldly claimed that **Arabella Waldegrave and Marie Lairre were the exact same person** [2]. Her theory combined the earlier hypotheses of [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] (who theorised about a murdered French nun named Marie Lairre) and local historian [[georgina-dawson|Georgina Dawson]] (who theorised about a missing Waldegrave daughter) [2]:

1. The infant Arabella, granddaughter of King James II, is taken to France in 1668 where she is placed in the convent school of Pontoise; against her inclination she takes vows and assumes the name **"Soeur Marie Lairre"** [1, 2]
2. She falls in love with her handsome cousin Henry Waldegrave, son of [[philip-waldegrave-last|Philip Lord Waldegrave of Borley]], who helps her escape the convent; they secretly marry and sail for England, arriving at Langenhoe in Essex [2, 3]
3. Henry places Marie/Arabella at Borley in the strict care of family servants named "Old Amos," his wife, and a serving girl named Janen [3]
4. Arabella's mother (Henrietta) was a Stuart spy; Arabella had been secretly entrusted with documents for the Hanoverian party, a fact she concealed from Henry [3]
5. The serving girl Janen persuades her sweetheart Enoch to bring a coach to help Marie escape to London; as they board, they are ambushed by Lord Waldegrave, Henry, and Amos [4]
6. Enoch is stabbed to death; Janen is murdered and thrown into an old plague pit; the secret documents are discovered [4]
7. Marie is imprisoned in a secret priest's cell in the old crypt, starved, taunted, and denied water; Henry finally strangles her; her body is dumped in a stream and her bones later scattered under the rectory floor [4, 5]

## Evaluation

Banks's key observation is that Lycett's theory was written **after** Harry Price's *The End of Borley Rectory* (1946), which had already published both Canon Phythian-Adams's scenario and the details about Arabella Waldegrave. "It is rather a pity that Mary Lycett's theory came to light after and not before Price's publication, for had the latter been the case, it might have carried more clout as a possible train of events" [5].

Specific criticisms include:

- **Geographical error**: Lycett described Baron Waldegrave as being of "Chewton, Devon," but the Waldegrave seat of Chewton Mendip is actually in Somerset [1]
- **Romantic fiction**: Critics note the theory strays into "the realms of romantic fiction," reading more like "a novel by Georgette Heyer or Barbara Cartland" than a serious historical hypothesis [2, 3]
- **Derivative origins**: The theory almost certainly absorbed and fused material already published in Price's 1946 book [5]

However, Banks finds the theory partially vindicated by subsequent research: the [[arabella-waldegrave|Countess Mary Waldegrave]] confirmed that Arabella did become a nun in Paris and maintained correspondence — she did not simply disappear. This collapses part of Lycett's narrative (the murder) but does not entirely invalidate the romantic-involvement subplot. Her underlying intuition — that [[the-nun|the ghost nun]] is a Waldegrave — Banks regards as sound, though her confusion of two Arabellas ([[arabella-fitzjames|Arabella FitzJames/Sister Ignatia]] vs. the granddaughter [[arabella-waldegrave|Arabella Waldegrave]]) leads to errors.
