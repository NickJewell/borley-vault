# Rev. F. A. Heaton

Vicar of St Michael's Vicarage, Tenterden, Kent. In a letter of January 25, 1941, Heaton made what proved to be one of the more perceptive early observations about the [[wall-writings|Borley wall-messages]].

## Analysis of the Writing Style

Heaton observed that the wall-messages appeared to have been written by someone who could not look at what they were doing — or who was writing in complete darkness:

> *"The reason is the way the letters are all joined in one continuous line, as if the person could not take the pencil off lest they should put it down to continue in the wrong place."*

He proposed that the writer was "imprisoned in the dark" and that the cry for "light" was therefore literal — the plea of someone writing in absolute darkness, who desperately needed light.

This reading — the writing as the act of someone confined in darkness — gives an additional layer of pathos to the wall-messages and is consistent with [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]]'s later analysis of the messages as showing physical constraint, confusion, and urgency.

## Well-Tank Reading

Independently, Heaton also interpreted the "Marianne-At-Get-Help-entant-Bottom-Me" message as reading "Well-Bottom-We" (rather than "Me"), suggesting the bottom *well* — consistent with Phythian-Adams's "Well-Tank-Bottom-Me" reading, which Price regarded as the correct decipherment.

His reading of the "odd marks" message (inverted "light") also converged independently with [[he-adshead|H. E. Adshead]]'s interpretation.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Reverend F. A. Heaton** of St Michael’s Vicarage, Tenterden, Kent, appears in the Borley Rectory sources solely as a correspondent of Harry Price. He offered detailed, analytical interpretations of the famous "Marianne" wall-writings.

A reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific dates, quotes, and analytical contributions regarding his involvement:

**The January 1941 Letter**
Following the publication of Price's first book on Borley, Rev. Heaton wrote to him on **January 25, 1941**, to offer alternative readings and theories about the mysterious wall messages [1]. 

**Theories on the Writing Method and the "Spirit"**
Heaton suggested that the physical appearance of the script provided clues to how it was executed. 
*   He proposed the messages were written by someone **"either (a) when they were not looking at what they were doing; or (b) in the dark"** [1]. 
*   He based this on the observation that the letters were **"all joined in one continuous line, as if the person could not take the pencil off lest they should put it down to continue in the wrong place"** [1].
*   He also noted that the overlapping "writing over writing" likely occurred because **"the writer could not see and did get muddled"** [1].
*   Theorizing on the condition of the entity, he suggested that if spirits were indeed communicating, the author might have been someone who was imprisoned and died in the dark, which would explain the repeated **"crying for ‘light’"** in the messages [1]. 

**Specific Textual Interpretations**
Rev. Heaton challenged several of Price's transcriptions of the wall-writings, offering the following specific corrections:
*   **The "Bottom Well":** Regarding a message Price read as *‘Marianne-at-Get-Help-entant-Bottom-Me’*, Heaton suggested it should be read as **‘... Bottom-We’**, indicating the "bottom well." He noted that **"the line of the e, leading up like it does, suggests another letter to follow"** [1].
*   **"And Open":** He suggested that a message read as *‘and O’* was actually meant to be **‘and open’** [1].
*   **The Inverted "Light":** He noticed that certain **"odd marks"** in a message that had appeared near the Blue Room on June 16, 1931, looked **"very near looking like ‘light’ again"** when the image was turned upside down [1, 2].
*   **Ancient Script:** He observed that the bottom word in one of the messages reminded him of **"ancient script, with the delight they seemed to take in ‘flourishes’"** [1].

**Theological Correction**
Finally, Rev. Heaton pointed out a specific ecclesiastical inaccuracy in the phantom's requests. Addressing the repeated appeals for a "light Mass," he provided the following quote: 
> **"There is no such usage as ‘light Mass,’ though there is, of course, ‘low Mass’"** [1]. 

The provided Borley sources contain no further biographical details or records of Rev. F. A. Heaton's involvement beyond this single 1941 correspondence.
