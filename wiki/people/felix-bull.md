# Felix Bull

**Rev. Felix Bull** (1852–1927) was an Anglican clergyman, the son of the Rev. Edward Bull, and the younger brother of the Rev. [[henry-bull|Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] — the builder and first occupant of Borley Rectory [1, 2]. After the death of his father, Felix became Rector of [[pentlow|Pentlow]] — the Bull family's ancestral village four miles from Borley.

## Family Position

Felix is associated with the thirteen children baptised at Borley Parish Church under Henry Dawson Bull, though Banks suggests he was "neither born nor baptised at Borley" — perhaps born before the family moved to the newly built [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], or baptised elsewhere.

Felix resided at [[pentlow|Pentlow Rectory]] (often called [[bulls-tower-pentlow|Pentlow Tower]]), an eccentric red-brick building about four miles from Borley featuring a 95-foot high folly built by his father [2, 4, 5]. He had at least one daughter who married a man named Yellowlee; their son later became a solicitor in Presteigne on the Welsh border, but the family frequently returned to visit Pentlow [3].

> **Contradiction:** The sources contain significant confusion about Felix's relationship to [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]]. While Felix is correctly identified as Henry D. E. Bull's younger brother — making him Harry Bull's *uncle* — one passage mistakenly refers to Felix as "Harry Bull's brother" [5]. Another passage reverses the relationship entirely, referring to Harry as Felix's uncle [4].

> **Contradiction:** Felix's incumbency dates are also disputed. One source states he was Rector of Pentlow from **1877 to 1927** [1]; another claims he became Rector in **1887** [4]. Furthermore, one passage states that Felix outlived his brother Henry by only 3 years, but since Henry died in 1892 and Felix in 1927, Felix actually outlived his brother by 35 years [2, 4, 9].

## The Pentlow Poltergeist (August 1886)

Felix is featured in the Borley records primarily because of a poltergeist outbreak at his own home. In **August 1886**, Felix experienced intense supernatural disturbances and drove to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] to tell his family that he was "having trouble with a Poltergeist" [6].

The next day, his nephew [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] and Harry's Oxford friend, [[p-shaw-jeffrey|P. Shaw Jeffrey]], borrowed the family coach and drove to Pentlow to investigate [6]. Felix was out when they arrived, but his cook directed them to a new housemaid named Mary, stating: **"Well, Master Harry, if there's any foolishness going on, it's all along of our new housemaid, that's what I say!"** [6, 7].

Harry and Shaw Jeffrey found the maid making beds in the large best bedroom [6]. Harry challenged her directly: **"Well, Mary, cook says you can show us a few tricks. What about it?"** [6]. The maid remained completely silent, but immediately a tooth glass flew across the room from behind her back, circling gracefully to hit the door jamb just above Shaw Jeffrey's head [8]. This was followed by the room's fender and fire-irons flying across the floor with a loud clatter [5, 8].

Banks presents the incident as evidence that the Bull family had some pre-existing familiarity with poltergeist phenomena — which may have shaped their relaxed attitude toward ghost-watching at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in later years.

## Significance

Felix and his nephew [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] both died in 1927, bringing the Bull family's long connection with the Pentlow and Borley parishes to a definitive end [4]. Felix's main function in Banks's account is to establish that poltergeist-type phenomena appeared in the Bull family's ambit prior to 1929, and to connect another member of the Bull family line to the Pentlow rectory — which Banks notes was built by the same family as Borley Rectory and shares its distinctive red-brick architecture.
