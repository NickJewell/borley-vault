# J. H. Russell

Undergraduate at Trinity College, Oxford. Visited the Borley ruins alongside [[harry-marshall-oxford|Harry Marshall]] from January 3–6, 1944, submitting an independent written report to [[harry-price|Harry Price]].

## Phantom Voice Incident (January 5, 1944, 5:25 p.m.)

Russell was walking in the orchard, near the stream at the north-east end of the garden — in the vicinity of the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] — when he heard what he was certain was Marshall's voice calling him. He responded. Marshall had not spoken.

From Russell's own report:

> *"Russell was certain I called him. I did not. At the time (5:25 p.m.) he claimed to have recognized my voice. When he knew I had not called him he naturally began to doubt whether he really had heard a voice."*

Russell's independent account confirms the incident and specifies his position: by the stream near the Nun's Walk.

Price notes that the tradition connecting the Nun's Walk stream with the legend (one variant holds the nun committed suicide by throwing herself into the stream) makes it the most psychically "active" area of the garden, and that voices have been reported there and on the adjacent lane by multiple witnesses over decades.

## Other Observations

Russell also provides an independent confirmation of the cellar crash on Tuesday morning (January 4) — noting that neither observer was directly above the objects involved, and that a stone of sufficient size to produce such a noise would not have been inadvertently kicked.

Russell's reporting is notably measured: he explicitly says his certainty that he heard Marshall's voice weakened once he knew Marshall had not spoken — a candid acknowledgement of the psychological difficulty of evaluating auditory phenomena in a "haunted" location.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**J. H. Russell** was an undergraduate student at Trinity College, Oxford, who participated in a wartime investigation of the ruined Borley Rectory alongside his friend and fellow undergraduate, Harry Marshall [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific events, dates, and quotes regarding his involvement:

**The January 1944 Visit and Photographic Mission**
Russell and Marshall visited the Borley site between **January 3 and January 6, 1944** [1, 2]. Despite staying in the area for several days, they spent a total of only ten hours on the Rectory premises [1]. This short duration was due to a delay in obtaining the necessary permission to investigate, as the ruined property had just recently changed hands [1]. The primary purpose of their visit was to photograph the constructional details of the ruined fabric before it was completely demolished; this was a highly successful endeavor, resulting in Marshall securing fifty photographs [1]. 

**The "Doubtful" Bag Incident (January 4, 1944)**
The two undergraduates recorded a few incidents that they themselves categorized as "doubtful" [3]. On Tuesday, January 4, they placed a bag containing their tools and photographic accessories on a shelf inside the large summer-house [3]. Upon returning for the bag at **12:25 p.m.**, they found it missing from the shelf [3]. After searching the area, they discovered the bag lying on the grass behind the summer-house [2, 3]. Harry Price noted that because the bag was not actively under observation, it was entirely possible the "transportation" was the work of a mischievous living intruder rather than a paranormal entity [3].

**The Disembodied Voice (January 5, 1944)**
A more puzzling auditory phenomenon occurred the following afternoon at **5:25 p.m.** [4]. While the two men were walking down the orchard away from the ruined house, Marshall was walking ahead on the cart-track that led to the fields, while Russell was positioned about fifteen yards behind him near the north-east end of the garden stream [4, 5]. 

Russell suddenly heard Marshall call his name, and he replied [2, 4]. However, Marshall emphatically denied having spoken [4]. Russell summarized his confusion regarding the auditory experience with the specific quote: **"I felt certain at the time that he had called to me, but when he denied that he had spoken I naturally began to doubt whether I had heard him at the time"** [4]. 

Marshall corroborated Russell's reaction in his own official report, stating: **"On Wednesday evening Russell was certain I called him. I did not. At the time (5:25 p.m.) he claimed to have recognized my voice. When he knew I had not called him he naturally began to doubt whether he really had heard a voice"** [5]. 

Harry Price found Russell's experience highly significant because Russell was standing right next to the stream by the famous "Nun's Walk" at the time [5]. Price pointed out that "voices" and "sibilant whisperings" were a commonly reported phenomenon at Borley, having previously been experienced by the Rev. G. Eric Smith, the Rev. Lionel Foyster, and Marianne Foyster [5].
