# J. H. Russell

Undergraduate at Trinity College, Oxford. Visited the Borley ruins alongside [[harry-marshall-oxford|Harry Marshall]] between 3 and 6 January 1944, submitting an independent written report to [[harry-price|Harry Price]].

## The January 1944 Visit

Russell and Marshall spent a total of only ten hours on the Rectory premises during their stay, due to a delay in obtaining permission to investigate — the ruined property had just recently changed hands [1]. The primary purpose of their visit was to photograph the constructional details of the ruined fabric before it was completely demolished; Marshall secured fifty photographs [1].

## Doubtful Bag Incident (4 January 1944)

The two undergraduates recorded a few incidents they themselves categorised as "doubtful" [3]. On Tuesday 4 January, they placed a bag containing tools and photographic accessories on a shelf inside the large summer-house [3]. Upon returning for the bag at **12:25 p.m.**, they found it missing from the shelf and discovered it lying on the grass behind the summer-house [2, 3]. Price noted that because the bag was not actively under observation, the "transportation" could plausibly be the work of a living intruder [3].

## Phantom Voice Incident (5 January 1944, 5:25 p.m.)

A more puzzling auditory phenomenon occurred the following afternoon at **5:25 p.m.** [4]. While the two men were walking down the orchard, Marshall was ahead on the cart-track while Russell was positioned about fifteen yards behind him near the north-east end of the garden stream — in the vicinity of the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] [4, 5].

Russell suddenly heard what he was certain was Marshall's voice calling him. He responded. Marshall had not spoken.

From Russell's own report:

> *"I felt certain at the time that he had called to me, but when he denied that he had spoken I naturally began to doubt whether I had heard him at the time."* [4]

Marshall corroborated Russell's reaction in his own official report: *"Russell was certain I called him. I did not. At the time (5:25 p.m.) he claimed to have recognized my voice. When he knew I had not called him he naturally began to doubt whether he really had heard a voice."* [5]

Price found the experience significant because Russell was standing right next to the stream by the famous Nun's Walk — an area where voices and sibilant whisperings had previously been reported by [[rev-eric-smith|Rev. G. Eric Smith]], [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]], and [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] [5]. One variant of the Borley legends holds that the nun committed suicide by throwing herself into the stream, making it reportedly the most psychically "active" area of the garden.

Russell's reporting is notably measured: he explicitly acknowledges that his certainty he heard Marshall's voice weakened once he knew Marshall had not spoken — a candid acknowledgement of the psychological difficulty of evaluating auditory phenomena in a "haunted" location.

## Cellar Crash Corroboration

Russell also provides an independent confirmation of a cellar crash on Tuesday morning (4 January) — noting that neither observer was directly above the objects involved, and that a stone of sufficient size to produce such a noise would not have been inadvertently kicked.
