# Miss Rosemary M. Williams

Daughter of Captain Williams of Borley Lodge, a property near the Rectory. Familiar with the local legend — she noted in her written statement that she had "often visited Borley Rectory before, spending many hours watching and listening" but admitted to always being "terrified and in the company of several others in case anything should happen!" On the night of Sunday, 26 March 1939 — approximately one month after the Rectory [[fire-1939|fire]] of 27–28 February — Williams and [[cg-browne|Mr. C. G. Browne]] of Pound Hall, Long Melford, were among a group examining the ruins by moonlight. She is the primary witness to one of the most discussed post-fire apparition accounts.

## The Blue Room Apparition

Having obtained permission from the owner, [[captain-gregson|Capt. William Gregson]], the party met at the Rectory gates around 10:30 p.m. Standing on the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]], Williams and Browne both observed a figure at what had been the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] window. Quoting from Williams's own report:

> *"I had the impression of someone in a light buff-coloured coat, but as the figure approached the window, I could see it was a woman clothed in blue. She remained for several seconds and then turned towards the wall and, as it seemed, walked through it."*

She was struck by the natural and attractive appearance of the figure, noting: "The most extraordinary part to me, was that the apparition should seem so natural and unfrightening."

[[cg-browne|Mr. Browne]] confirmed her statement in full.

> **Contradiction:** [[harry-price|Price]]'s chronologies alternatively describe the figure as a "woman in blue" or a "woman in white," a minor discrepancy between his published accounts.

## Physical Impossibility

[[harry-price|Price]] makes the physical argument explicit: the floor of the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] had been destroyed in the fire. Anyone standing at that window would have needed to stand on either nothing or charred and rotten rafters. Captain Gregson had placed "Keep Out" signs on the ruins due to their dangerous condition. In Price's assessment, no human being could have climbed up to that window under the existing conditions, let alone done so unseen from the Nun's Walk below.

## The "One Guinea" Controversy

Following the incident, [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. A. C. Henning]] reported her sighting to Price, who wrote to Williams on 3 April 1939. In an undated, business-like reply, she offered to provide her story for the fee of one guinea. The SPR authors later presumed that Price actually paid this fee. However, multiple other sources explicitly contradict this assumption, stating that Price flatly refused to pay because he maintained that any testimony given for remuneration was virtually worthless. Miss Williams subsequently backed down, and her report was placed in Price's dossier without payment being made.

The SPR also criticised Price's handling of her account, pointing out that he selectively omitted her admission of being previously terrified of the site, and her initial rational assumption that the figure was merely her friend who had wandered into the building. The SPR ultimately dismissed her sighting as likely an illusion caused by "expectant imagination and a trick of the bright moonlight shining on the window aperture and the ruined room behind it."

## Significance

Williams's account was submitted in writing to Price and is reproduced verbatim. As the principal tenant of Borley Lodge, she had both local knowledge and a direct view of the ruins. Her simultaneous sighting with [[cg-browne|Browne]], in moonlight, under conditions that preclude a living interloper, makes this one of the strongest post-fire apparition accounts in the record.
