# Miss Rosemary M. Williams

Resident of Borley Lodge, a property near the Rectory. On the night of Sunday, March 26, 1939 — approximately one month after the Rectory fire of February 27–28 — Williams and [[cg-browne|Mr. C. G. Browne]] of Pound Hall, Long Melford, were among a group examining the ruins by moonlight. She is the primary witness to one of the most discussed post-fire apparition accounts.

## The Blue Room Apparition

Standing on the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]], Williams and Browne both observed a figure at what had been the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] window. Quoting from Williams's own report:

> *"I had the impression of someone in a light buff-coloured coat, but as the figure approached the window, I could see it was a woman clothed in blue. She remained for several seconds and then turned towards the wall and, as it seemed, walked through it."*

[[cg-browne|Mr. Browne]] confirmed her statement in full.

## Physical Impossibility

[[harry-price|Price]] makes the physical argument explicit: the floor of the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] had been destroyed in the fire. Anyone standing at that window would have needed to stand on either nothing or charred and rotten rafters. [[captain-gregson|Captain Gregson]] had placed "Keep Out" signs on the ruins due to their dangerous condition. In Price's assessment, no human being could have climbed up to that window under the existing conditions, let alone done so unseen from the Nun's Walk below.

The figure was clothed in blue — consistent with numerous earlier descriptions of the Borley Nun, who was sometimes described as wearing blue or dark garments.

## Significance

Williams's account was submitted in writing to Price and is reproduced verbatim. As the principal tenant of Borley Lodge — a property whose name reflects its long local association with the Rectory's grounds — she had both local knowledge and a direct view of the ruins. Her simultaneous sighting with [[cg-browne|Browne]], in moonlight with two witnesses, under conditions that preclude a living interloper, makes this one of the strongest post-fire apparition accounts in the record.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Miss Rosemary M. Williams** was the daughter of Captain Williams of Borley Lodge, near Sudbury [1, 2]. She was already familiar with the local legend, noting in her written statement that she had "often visited Borley Rectory before, spending many hours watching and listening" but admitted to always being **"terrified and in the company of several others in case anything should happen!"** [3].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following specific events, quotes, and controversies regarding her involvement in the Borley haunting:

**The Sighting in the Blue Room Window**
Miss Williams is best known for a spectacular visual encounter that occurred on the cold, moonlit night of **Sunday, March 26, 1939**—exactly one month after the Rectory was gutted by fire [2-4]. 
*   Having obtained permission from the owner, Capt. William Gregson, she and a party of friends (including Mr. Charles Graham Browne) met at the Rectory gates around 10:30 p.m. [3]. 
*   While standing on the Nun's Walk looking up at the blackened ruins, she saw the figure of a small woman appear in the burnt-out aperture of the famous Blue Room window [2, 3]. 
*   She initially assumed the figure was a member of her party wearing a **"light buff-coloured coat,"** but as the figure approached the window, she realized it was a **"woman clothed in blue"** [3] (though some of Price's chronologies alternatively describe the figure as being a "woman in white") [5]. 
*   The figure remained visible for several seconds before turning and seemingly walking straight through the wall or falling back into the debris [2, 3]. 
*   Miss Williams was struck by the natural and attractive appearance of the figure [1], providing the specific quote: **"The most extraordinary part to me, was that the apparition should seem so natural and unfrightening."** [3].

**The "One Guinea" Controversy and Contradictions**
Following the incident, the Rev. A. C. Henning reported her sighting to Harry Price, who wrote to Miss Williams on April 3, 1939 [4, 6]. In an undated, business-like reply, **she offered to provide her story for the fee of one guinea** [6]. 
*   **The Contradiction:** In their highly critical *Borley Report*, the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) authors presumed that Price actually paid this fee for her testimony [6]. However, multiple other sources explicitly contradict this SPR assumption, stating that **Price flatly refused to pay her** because he maintained that any testimony given for remuneration was virtually worthless [1, 4]. Miss Williams subsequently backed down, and her report was placed in Price's dossier without payment being made [4].

**SPR Criticisms of Price's Editing**
The SPR investigators severely criticized Harry Price's handling of Miss Williams's account in his published books (*The Most Haunted House in England* and *The End of Borley Rectory*). They pointed out that Price selectively omitted details from her original statement that tended to diminish the paranormality of the experience—specifically, her admission of being previously terrified of the site, and her initial, rational assumption that the figure was merely her friend who had wandered into the building [3, 6]. Ultimately, the SPR authors dismissed her sighting, concluding it was likely a simple illusion caused by **"expectant imagination and a trick of the bright moonlight shining on the window aperture and the ruined room behind it."** [6].
