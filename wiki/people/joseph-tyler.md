# Joseph Tyler

Bull family coachman at Borley Rectory, recorded in the **1881 Borley parish census returns**. [[Ivan Banks]] proposed him as the driver of the coach that reportedly carried the dying [[Kate Boreham|Kate Boreham]] from the Rectory to a house in Priory Walk, Sudbury, after her alleged death or near-death in the Rectory kitchen.

## Banks's Theory

Banks located Joseph Tyler on the 1881 census as the Bulls' coachman, living at the cottage behind the Rectory. He proposes that:

1. Kate Boreham became dangerously ill (from lead poisoning or a difficult birth) in the Rectory kitchen
2. Henry Dawson Bull ("Carlos") summoned Tyler from the stable cottage (possibly via the yard bell) to prepare the carriage
3. Tyler drove the dying Kate at speed to Sudbury, after dark, possibly with Henry Bull riding up on the driver's box
4. This explains the phantom coach phenomenon: two men on the driver's box, an old-fashioned vehicle moving at speed across the Rectory grounds

Banks notes that the description of the phantom coach as "an old-fashioned cab" fits a Victorian-era vehicle, not the 13th-century carriage required by the "novice of Bures" legend or the 17th-century carriage of the Marie Lairre theory.

The original planchette voice heard by [[Rev. Eric Smith|Guy Smith]] pleading "No Carlos, Don't" on the Blue Room landing is interpreted by Banks as an echo of the moment Katie protested — or of someone (perhaps Henry's wife Caroline) protesting Henry's actions.

## Note

No independent record of Joseph Tyler beyond the 1881 census has been found. Banks's theory is speculative, building on coincidence of name and occupation. His death and life after 1881 are unrecorded in the Borley chronicles.
