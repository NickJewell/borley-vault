# Squadron-Leader Horniman

RAF officer; official observer at Borley Rectory during [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s 1937–38 tenancy. He visited the Rectory on September 20, 1937, with an unnamed friend.

## Cold Spot Corroboration

Horniman reported that his friend, brought to the Rectory without any prior information about the "cold spot," experienced the same physical cold at the landing outside the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] that [[miss-reid|Miss Reid]] had reported in August 1937.

In Horniman's own words:

> *"What is interesting is that, without any previous information, this friend came over desperately cold on the landing where Kerr-Pearse's friend did.... His hands were much below normal temperature."*

The friend had not been told about Miss Reid's experience, or about the cold spot's location, or about [[rupert-haig|Rupert Haig]]'s experience the following day.

## Pattern of Independent Corroboration

September 1937 at Borley produced a remarkable cluster of cold-spot corroborations: [[miss-reid|Miss Reid]] in August; Horniman's unnamed friend on September 20; and [[rupert-haig|Rupert Haig]] (Kerr-Pearse's cousin) on September 21. Three independent visitors, over three separate visits within a few weeks, all reporting the same physical effect at the same location without prior knowledge.

This pattern of independent replication — from different people, at different times, who had not communicated with one another — is one of the stronger elements in the cold-spot evidence and was later experimentally confirmed by the [[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]] in 1943.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Squadron-Leader Horniman** was an "official observer" during Harry Price's year-long tenancy investigation of Borley Rectory in 1937. He became involved in the investigation through his friendship with Captain H. G. Harrison, who was the brother-in-law of Price's chief observer, Sidney H. Glanville [1, 2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals his involvement in excavations, specific auditory phenomena, and a notable textual contradiction regarding his companions:

**The August 1937 Visit and Excavation**
*   **Dates and Companions:** Horniman spent the night at the Rectory over the weekend of **August 28–29, 1937**, accompanied by Sidney Glanville, Roger Glanville, and Captain Harrison [1, 3].
*   **The Cats' Cemetery:** During this visit, Horniman was present when the Glanvilles re-excavated the mysteriously disturbed grave of a cat named 'Gem', turning up a considerable number of large bones that did not belong to a feline [4].
*   **Auditory Phenomena:** Between 11:00 p.m. and 4:00 a.m. on Sunday, the four men reported hearing **"several light thuds or bumps,"** but they could not locate the source of the noises [1]. 
*   **The Gramophone Incident:** Earlier that evening at 8:24 p.m., while the Glanvilles were out for a meal, Horniman and Harrison remained on duty. While the two men were **"singing to a gramophone record,"** they clearly heard **"light tripping footsteps"** coming down the main stairs [1, 3]. Despite the distinct sound, they could see nothing there [1].

**The September 1937 Visit and the "Cold Spot" Contradiction**
Horniman's second recorded visit took place on Monday, **September 20, 1937**, during which his companion experienced a dramatic physical reaction on the first-floor landing outside the highly active Blue Room [2, 5, 6]. 
*   **The Incident:** While standing on the landing, Horniman's companion suddenly became "desperately cold" [2, 6]. Fellow observer Mark Kerr-Pearse, who was independently on duty at the Rectory at the time, physically felt the companion's hands and confirmed they were much below normal temperature [2].
*   **Specific Quote:** Horniman officially reported this event (which was forwarded to Sidney Glanville by Capt. Harrison on September 24), writing: **"What is interesting is that, without any previous information, this friend came over desperately cold on the landing where Kerr-Pearse’s friend did. . . . His hands were much below normal temperature"** [2, 6].
*   **The Contradiction of Identity:** The Borley sources contain a stark contradiction regarding the identity of Horniman's companion during this event. One chronological entry explicitly states that Horniman was accompanied by **"his sister"**, and that she was the one who became desperately cold [5]. However, this is directly contradicted by Horniman's own quoted report, which refers to the companion as a **"friend"** and explicitly uses the male pronoun, stating **"His hands were much below normal temperature"** [2, 6]. Furthermore, a published list of Borley observers includes a specific entry for **"Horniman's friend, Squadron-Leader"**, heavily implying the companion was actually a fellow male RAF officer rather than his sister [7].
