# Squadron-Leader Horniman

RAF officer; official observer at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] during [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s 1937–38 tenancy. He became involved in the investigation through his friendship with Captain H. G. Harrison, who was the brother-in-law of Price's chief observer [[sidney-glanville|Sidney H. Glanville]] [1, 2].

## August 1937 Visit

Horniman spent the night at the Rectory over the weekend of **August 28–29, 1937**, accompanied by [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]], Roger Glanville, and Captain Harrison [1, 3].

During this visit, Horniman was present when the Glanvilles re-excavated the mysteriously disturbed grave of a cat named "Gem" at the [[cats-cemetery|cats' cemetery]], turning up a considerable number of large bones that did not belong to a feline [4].

Between 11:00 p.m. and 4:00 a.m., the four men reported hearing **"several light thuds or bumps"** but could not locate their source [1]. Earlier that evening, at 8:24 p.m., while the Glanvilles were out for a meal, Horniman and Harrison remained on duty. While **"singing to a gramophone record,"** they clearly heard **"light tripping footsteps"** coming down the main stairs — though nothing was visible [1, 3].

## September 1937 Visit and the "Cold Spot" Corroboration

Horniman's second recorded visit took place on Monday, **September 20, 1937**. He was accompanied by an unnamed companion who was brought to the Rectory without any prior information about the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] cold spot [2, 5, 6].

While standing on the first-floor landing outside the Blue Room, Horniman's companion suddenly became "desperately cold." Fellow observer [[mark-kerr-pearse|Mark Kerr-Pearse]], who was independently on duty at the Rectory at the time, physically felt the companion's hands and confirmed they were much below normal temperature [2].

Horniman officially reported the event — forwarded to [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] by Capt. Harrison on September 24:

> *"What is interesting is that, without any previous information, this friend came over desperately cold on the landing where Kerr-Pearse's friend did.... His hands were much below normal temperature."* [2, 6]

> **Contradiction:** The sources contain a stark contradiction regarding the identity of Horniman's companion during this event. One chronological entry explicitly states that Horniman was accompanied by **"his sister"**, and that she was the one who became desperately cold [5]. However, Horniman's own quoted report refers to the companion as a **"friend"** and explicitly uses the male pronoun — "His hands were much below normal temperature" [2, 6]. A published list of Borley observers includes a specific entry for **"Horniman's friend, Squadron-Leader"**, heavily implying the companion was actually a fellow male RAF officer [7].

## Pattern of Independent Corroboration

September 1937 at Borley produced a remarkable cluster of cold-spot corroborations: [[miss-reid|Miss Reid]] in August; Horniman's unnamed companion on September 20; and [[rupert-haig|Rupert Haig]] (Kerr-Pearse's cousin) on September 21. Three independent visitors, over three separate visits within a few weeks, all reporting the same physical effect at the same location without prior knowledge — later experimentally confirmed by the [[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]] in 1943.
