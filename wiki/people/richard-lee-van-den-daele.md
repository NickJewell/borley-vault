# Richard Lee-Van den Daele

Richard Lee's interest in Borley began in the early 1970s when an annual containing an article on the haunting and photographs of a dilapidated piggery — mistakenly claimed as the remains of [[borley-rectory]] — caught his imagination.

## Tracking the Gregsons

Trying to shed light on the truth behind the Rectory fire, Lee-Van den Daele wrote to government departments at home and abroad to track down the sons of the late [[captain-gregson]]. Despite receiving no initial response, his dogged letter-writing eventually paid off: both Gregson sons were tracked down at either end of the globe.

[[alan-gregson]]'s letters from Australia stated that the fire was accidental. [[anthony-gregson]] told Lee-Van den Daele quite bluntly that his father bought the rectory as a real estate venture and, when this didn't pan out, "torched the place for the insurance money." Both accounts were included in [[ivan-banks]]' *The Enigma of Borley Rectory* (1996).

Lee-Van den Daele also discovered that Captain Gregson, while living at Maldon, Essex, had been an area organiser for the British Union of Fascists, Oswald Mosley's Blackshirts.

## Site Investigations

Visits to the Rectory site made with his brother Sean convinced the pair that there was still much occurring that could not be explained away. His brother's group heard sibilant whisperings from inside the locked church one night in the 1980s while monitoring microphones and using headphones outside.

## Harry Price Research

Lee-Van den Daele came into contact with the late [[ivan-banks]], [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth]], [[paul-moss-kemp|Paul Moss-Kemp]] (East Anglian Group), [[peter-underwood]] and [[ah-wesencraft|Alan Wesencraft]], with whom he engaged in lengthy correspondence. Trips to the [[national-laboratory|Harry Price Library]] aided his degree course thesis on the works of Price. Qualified as a librarian, he continued to seek out newspaper references to the haunting.

## Gate Posts

Lee-Van den Daele discovered in 1984 that the last surviving original driveway [[gate-posts|gate posts]] from Borley Rectory were in the garden of the final bungalow built on the site. He corresponded with [[ivan-banks]] who travelled to Borley and bought the posts for £20, eventually giving them a home in his garden in Maidenhead.

## Borleyrectory.com

Lee-Van den Daele contributed extensively to the website formerly run by [[vincent-oneil]].

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Richard Lee-Van den Daele** (also referred to as Richard Lee) is a qualified librarian from Shipley, West Yorkshire, who became a dedicated researcher, archivist, and authority on the Borley Rectory case [1-4].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, research discoveries, specific quotes, and network of correspondence regarding his involvement in the Borley saga:

**Early Interest and Academic Work**
*   **Origins of Interest:** His fascination with Borley began in the early 1970s after he read an annual containing an article about the haunting alongside photographs of a dilapidated piggery, which the book falsely claimed was all that remained of Borley Rectory [1]. 
*   **Research:** Utilizing his local library in Yorkshire, he proved adept at tracking down out-of-print books by Harry Price, Trevor Hall, and various Society for Psychical Research (SPR) publications [1]. 
*   **Thesis:** He later wrote his degree course thesis on the works of Harry Price, an effort aided by research trips to the Harry Price Library at the University of London [2].

**Defense of Harry Price**
Unlike many late-twentieth-century commentators who relied on the highly critical 1956 SPR *Borley Report*, Lee-Van den Daele was a **staunch defender of Harry Price** [5]. He provided the following forceful quotes assessing Price's legacy and condemning his critics:
*   **"In my opinion, Harry Price's years of painstaking work on the haunting of Borley Rectory was undone in a frenzied and shameful free-for-all following his untimely death in 1948"** [5].
*   He stated that critics, **"too unsure of their facts to tackle Price whilst he was still in a position to sue them, crept out of the woodwork en masse to malign and attempt to discredit his decade of diligent work"** [5]. 
*   He concluded that despite the invective poured upon the story, **"perhaps Price’s most ardent supporter over the years has turned out to be Borley itself"** [5]. 

**Major Discoveries: Capt. William Gregson**
One of Lee-Van den Daele's most significant historical contributions to the Borley literature was unearthing previously unknown information about Capt. William Gregson, the final owner of the Rectory before it burned down [1, 4]. 
*   **Tracking the Sons:** In an attempt to discover the truth behind the 1939 fire, Lee-Van den Daele doggedly wrote to government departments locally and abroad until he successfully tracked down Gregson's two sons, Anthony and Alan, at opposite ends of the globe [1]. 
*   **The Insurance Arson Motive:** Through correspondence with Anthony Gregson, he uncovered the blunt admission that **Capt. Gregson had bought the Rectory as a real estate venture to capitalize on its haunted reputation, and when that failed, he torched the place for the insurance money** [6, 7]. 
*   **Fascist Connections:** He also discovered that before purchasing the Rectory, while living in Maldon, Essex, Capt. Gregson had been an **area organiser for Oswald Mosley’s Blackshirts** (the British Union of Fascists) [6]. 

**Fieldwork and the Borley Relics**
*   **The Gate Posts (1984):** In 1984, Lee-Van den Daele located and photographed the last surviving timber driveway gate posts of Borley Rectory, which were standing in the garden of a 1970s bungalow built on the former Rectory lawn [8]. He informed fellow researcher Ivan Banks, who subsequently traveled to Borley and purchased the posts for £20 [8]. 
*   **The Kate Boreham Mystery:** During a visit to the Borley churchyard, he noted a highly suspicious anomaly regarding the locally famous "Kate Boreham" legend: he was completely unable to find her grave anywhere in the churchyard, despite her death certificate claiming she was buried there [9].
*   **His Brother's Investigations:** His brother, Sean Lee, was also an intrepid Borley investigator [2, 10]. During visits to Borley in July and August 1984 with Philip Dickinson and other university friends, Sean and his group courageously slept in the church porch, where they recorded anomalous "sibilant whisperings" on studio-quality tape decks [10-12]. These visits firmly convinced both Richard and Sean that unexplained phenomena were still occurring at the site [2]. 

**Correspondence and Network**
Because of his extensive research, Lee-Van den Daele engaged in lengthy correspondence with almost every major surviving figure in the Borley investigations:
*   **The Skeptics:** He corresponded with SPR investigator Trevor Hall. In a letter dated November 1984, Hall dismissively told Lee-Van den Daele that in his view "the case is closed" and that it was difficult to see what more could usefully be written about it [13]. 
*   **Fellow Investigators:** He was in contact with Peter Underwood, Alan Wesencraft, and Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth [2]. In a letter to Lee-Van den Daele dated July 7, 1984, Croom-Hollingsworth pettily disparaged the rival Enfield Parapsychical Research Group, calling them "a joke" with "rubbish equipment" [14, 15]. 
*   **Publications and Societies:** As a qualified librarian, he continuously sought out obscure newspaper references to the haunting and contributed his findings extensively to Vincent O'Neil's *Borleyrectory.com* website [2]. He was also a member of O'Neil's Internet-based Borley Ghost Society [16] and provided vital research material for Ivan Banks's 1996 book, *The Enigma of Borley Rectory* [1, 3].
