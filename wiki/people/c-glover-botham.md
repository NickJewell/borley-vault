# C. Glover Botham and Harry Collard

Two London mediums who held the very first official séance at Borley Rectory during the Smith incumbency — the séance produced no result whatsoever.

## The June 1929 Séance

Botham and Collard travelled to Borley Rectory during the early, highly publicised period of the Smith incumbency to conduct a séance [1, 2]. They sat by lamplight and also in complete darkness. Price records only this much: "the 'spirits' were completely silent and no paranormal phenomena were reported" [1, 2].

> **Contradiction:** The sources disagree on the exact date. Harry Price's own account states the séance took place on **Sunday, June 16, 1929** [2], while the detailed chronology compiled by later researchers places it on **June 19, 1929** [1]. The later researchers note that June 19 was a particularly chaotic day at the Rectory, during which Price's secretary [[lucie-kaye|Lucy Kaye]] reported hearing inexplicable bell-ringing and crowds of rowdy sightseers descended upon the house during the night [1].

No further biographical details for either Botham or Collard are given in any source. Neither appears in the later observer programme or is mentioned again in the Borley record.

## Context and Significance

The blank result from these two professional mediums stands in contrast to the subsequent success of amateur table-tipping by [[sidney-glanville]] and his family. Price uses this contrast in Chapter 22 of *The Most Haunted House in England* to argue that "the professional mediums threw little light on the Borley drama" while Glanville's untrained group "were much more successful — or at least more entertaining."

The equal failure of the August 1931 [[mr-johnson-medium|Johnson medium]] séance — which produced nothing evidentially useful — forms part of Price's case that the Borley phenomena were distinct from conventional séance activity and could not be triggered by professional sensitives.
