# C. Glover Botham and Harry Collard

Two London mediums who held the very first official séance at Borley Rectory on Sunday, June 16, 1929 — four days after [[harry-price|Price's]] first visit, and the evening of the same day as Price's return to London. The séance produced no result whatsoever.

## The June 16, 1929 Séance

Botham and Collard sat at Borley by lamplight and also in darkness. Price records only this much: "the 'spirits' were silent." No further detail of the sitting is recorded in *The Most Haunted House in England*.

## Context and Significance

The blank result from these two professional mediums stands in contrast to the subsequent success of amateur table-tipping by [[sidney-glanville]] and his family. Price uses this contrast in Chapter 22 to argue that "the professional mediums threw little light on the Borley drama" while Glanville's untrained group "were much more successful — or at least more entertaining."

The séance on June 16 was presumably arranged at Price's invitation (he visited June 12–13) to provide a controlled test of the claimed manifestations using established mediums. Its failure — and the equal failure of the August 1931 Johnson medium séance, which produced nothing evidentially useful — form part of Price's case that the Borley phenomena were distinct from conventional séance activity and could not be triggered by professional sensitives.

> **Note:** No further biographical details for either Botham or Collard are given in the source. Neither appears in the later observer program or is mentioned again in the Borley record.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**C. Glover Botham** and **Harry Collard** are briefly recorded in the Borley sources as **two London mediums** who attempted to investigate the haunting [1, 2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific details and contradictions regarding their involvement:

**The June 1929 Séance and Lack of Results**
Botham and Collard travelled to Borley Rectory to conduct a séance during the early, highly publicized period of the Smith incumbency [1, 2]. Despite their professional status as mediums, their efforts were entirely unsuccessful [1, 2]. The sources note that they sat in both lamplight and in complete darkness, but **the "spirits" were completely silent and no paranormal phenomena were reported** [1, 2]. 

**Chronological Contradiction**
The texts present a specific discrepancy regarding the exact date of their visit:
*   Harry Price's own account explicitly states that Botham and Collard held their fruitless séance on **Sunday, June 16, 1929** [2]. 
*   However, the detailed chronology compiled by later researchers lists their séance as taking place on **June 19, 1929** [1]. The sources note that this was a particularly chaotic day at the Rectory, during which Harry Price's secretary, Lucy Kaye, reported hearing inexplicable bell-ringing, and crowds of rowdy sightseers descended upon the house during the night [1].
