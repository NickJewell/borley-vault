# R. M. Christie

R. M. Christie was one of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s official observers at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]]. He visited on the night of 8 December 1937 with [[dr-pe-ryberg|Dr P. E. Ryberg]] and [[l-g-cooper|L. G. Cooper]].

Their joint report — the contents of which Price suppressed — recorded that no unexplained sounds were heard, that mice were observed actively ravaging boxes under the table, and that gnawed paper was found. This was one of several observer reports that contradicted Price's published claim that rats and mice were never mentioned by any observer at Borley.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**R. M. Christie** was one of the official observers who participated in Harry Price's year-long investigation of Borley Rectory. The sources record only one specific instance of Christie's involvement, which became highly significant to skeptics due to Price's deliberate suppression of their findings.

A reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following details regarding this observer:

**The December 1937 Vigil**
*   On the night of **8 December 1937**, Christie conducted an observational vigil at the Rectory alongside co-investigators **Dr. P. E. Ryberg** and **L. G. Cooper** [1]. 

**Findings and Direct Quote**
*   During their stay, the team found a completely natural explanation for the mysterious "scrabbling" and rustling noises that other investigators had frequently reported. 
*   In their official report submitted to Price, Christie and the team explicitly recorded the following quote: **"No sounds of unexplained origin were heard. . . . Mice were heard ravaging in some boxes under the table. Gnawed paper was found in these boxes. . . . Mice were seen to scuttle around . . ."** [1].

**Suppression by Harry Price**
*   Christie's involvement was highlighted years later by the authors of the critical *Borley Report* (Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) to demonstrate Price's biased methodology. 
*   The authors noted that **Harry Price completely omitted Christie's report from his published works** [1]. By failing to quote this mundane discovery of mice and gnawed paper, Price deliberately allowed the natural noises to remain "unexplained" in his books to artificially bolster the narrative that the Rectory was haunted [1].
