# Walter Hubbell

Walter Hubbell (also spelled **Hubble** in one text) was a nineteenth-century Canadian actor, theatrical impresario, and author who published *The Haunted House: A True Ghost Story. Being an Account of the Mysterious Manifestations that have taken place in the presence of Esther Cox... The Great Amherst Mystery* (St John, New Brunswick, 1879). His book became one of the best-selling accounts of alleged poltergeist phenomena ever published, running to at least ten editions and selling over **55,000 copies** — making Hubbell "a great deal of money" [5, 6]. The book's commercial success — and the suggestion that its more sensational elements were embellished or invented — is directly paralleled by [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s treatment of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

## The Amherst Book

Hubbell arrived in Amherst, Nova Scotia, to observe [[esther-cox|Esther Cox]]'s poltergeist phenomena at first hand. His literary method involved keeping an "alleged diary" of the supernatural events, which he subsequently expanded into his full book [4]. He then arranged a commercial lecture tour with Esther as the attraction.

> **Contested:** Esther Cox's sister Olive (née Cox, Mrs Teed) disputed Hubbell's more dramatic claims, saying he had sensationalised and invented details. Esther Cox herself refused to discuss the case in later life, reportedly "afraid they would come back."

As [[walter-prince|Dr Walter F. Prince]] demonstrated in his 1919 critical analysis, the published version of Hubbell's account was greatly embellished compared with the original testimony [2].

## Relevance to Borley

The [[spr|SPR]] report notes several reasons why the [[great-amherst-mystery|Amherst case]] is relevant to Borley:

1. The case occurred five miles from Sackville, Nova Scotia, where [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] was rector immediately before the Foysters moved to Borley (1928–30), making it highly probable that the Foysters knew Hubbell's book [5].
2. [[lionel-foyster|Mr Foyster]] used the name "Teed" as a pseudonym for a character in his fictional manuscript *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House* — "Teed" being the unusual surname of [[daniel-and-olive-teed|Esther Cox's sister and brother-in-law]], in whose house the phenomena occurred.
3. The commercial template Hubbell created — a sensational, largely uncritical first-hand account that sold by the tens of thousands — was precisely what Price sought to replicate with *The Most Haunted House in England*. Knowing how much money Hubbell had made, critics allege that [[lionel-foyster|Foyster]] deliberately copied Hubbell's diary method with the specific intention of turning it into a profitable bestseller to solve his own dire financial difficulties [4, 6].

**Contradicting Harry Price:** [[harry-price|Harry Price]] repeatedly insisted that the Borley [[wall-writings|wall-writings]] were a phenomenon entirely "unique in the annals of psychical research" [1, 9, 10]. However, Hubbell's 1879 book explicitly detailed sensational messages being paranormally scrawled on walls addressed to Esther Cox [1, 7]. The authors of the [[the-borley-report|*Borley Report*]] pointed out that **Harry Price actually possessed a first edition of Walter Hubbell's book in his own personal library** — proving he could not have been unaware of the Amherst parallel [1].
