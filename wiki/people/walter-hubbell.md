# Walter Hubbell

Walter Hubbell was a Canadian actor, theatrical impresario, and author who published *The Haunted House: A True Ghost Story. Being an Account of the Mysterious Manifestations that have taken place in the presence of Esther Cox... The Great Amherst Mystery* (St John, New Brunswick, 1879). His book became one of the best-selling accounts of alleged poltergeist phenomena ever published, running to at least ten editions and selling over 55,000 copies.

## The Amherst Book

Hubbell arrived in Amherst, Nova Scotia, to observe [[esther-cox|Esther Cox]]'s poltergeist phenomena at first hand, and subsequently arranged a commercial lecture tour with Esther as the attraction. He then wrote the book which made both his fortune and the case famous. The book's commercial success — and the suggestion that its more sensational elements were embellished or invented — is directly paralleled by [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s treatment of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

## Relevance to Borley

The SPR report notes several reasons why the [[great-amherst-mystery|Amherst case]] is relevant to Borley:

1. The case occurred five miles from Sackville, Nova Scotia, where [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] was rector immediately before the Foysters moved to Borley (1928–30), making it highly probable that the Foysters knew Hubbell's book.
2. [[lionel-foyster|Mr Foyster]] used the name "Teed" as a pseudonym for a character in his fictional manuscript *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House* — "Teed" being the unusual surname of Esther Cox's sister and brother-in-law, in whose house the phenomena occurred.
3. The commercial template Hubbell created — a sensational, largely uncritical first-hand account that sold by the tens of thousands — was precisely what Price sought to replicate with *The Most Haunted House in England*.

As [[dr-walter-prince|Dr Walter F. Prince]] demonstrated in a 1919 critical analysis, the published version of Hubbell's account was greatly embellished compared with the original testimony.

> **Contested:** Esther Cox's sister Olive (née Cox, Mrs Teed) disputed Hubbell's more dramatic claims, saying he had sensationalised and invented details. Esther Cox herself refused to discuss the case in later life, reportedly "afraid they would come back."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Walter Hubbell** (also spelled **Hubble** in one text) was a nineteenth-century author whose writings on a famous Canadian poltergeist case heavily influenced the skeptical theories regarding the haunting of Borley Rectory. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following details about his work and its profound connection to the Borley case:

**The Amherst Mystery and His Bestselling Book**
*   **The Case:** Hubbell investigated and wrote about the "Great Amherst Mystery," a celebrated 1878–1879 poltergeist infestation surrounding a young woman named Esther Cox in Amherst, Nova Scotia [1, 2].
*   **The Publication:** In 1879, he published his account in Saint John, New Brunswick, under the title *The Haunted House: A True Ghost Story. Being an Account of the Mysterious Manifestations that have taken place in the presence of Esther Cox . . . The Great Amherst Mystery* [1, 3]. 
*   **Methodology:** His literary method involved keeping an "alleged diary" of the supernatural events, which he subsequently expanded into his full book for publication [4].
*   **Commercial Success:** The book was an absolute sensation, running into at least ten editions and selling a minimum of 55,000 copies [5]. This massive success ensured the story was widely known and made Hubbell a **"great deal of money"** [5, 6].

**The Inspiration for Lionel Foyster**
Hubbell's name is central to the theories of skeptical researchers (such as Eric Dingwall, Kathleen Goldney, and Trevor Hall) who sought to expose the Borley hauntings as a fraud. 
*   **The Geographic Link:** Critics noted the highly suspicious fact that the Rev. Lionel Foyster lived in Sackville, New Brunswick—just five miles from Amherst—from 1828 to 1930, immediately before returning to England to take up the living at Borley Rectory [5]. 
*   **The Blueprint for Fraud:** Because of the striking similarities between the two cases (such as mysterious fires, moving bedclothes, and wall-writings addressed to a specific woman), skeptics proposed that Hubbell's book served as an available **"textbook"** for the Borley phenomena [7, 8]. 
*   **Financial Motive:** Knowing how much money Hubbell had made, it is alleged that Foyster deliberately copied Hubbell's method of keeping a "diary" (*Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*) with the specific intention of turning it into a highly profitable bestseller to solve his own dire financial difficulties [4, 6]. 

**Contradicting Harry Price**
Hubbell's book also provides a key piece of evidence used by critics to contradict the claims of ghost hunter Harry Price.
*   **The False Claim:** In his Borley writings, Price repeatedly insisted that the Borley "wall-writings" (the mysterious messages scrawled to Foyster's wife, Marianne) were a phenomenon entirely **"unique in the annals of psychical research"** [1, 9, 10].
*   **The Evidence in Price's Own Library:** Hubbell's 1879 book explicitly detailed sensational messages being paranormally scrawled on the walls addressed to Esther Cox [1, 7]. The authors of the skeptical *Borley Report* pointed out that Price's claim of uniqueness was blatantly incorrect, highlighting the highly damaging fact that **Harry Price actually possessed a first edition of Walter Hubbell's book in his own library** [1].
