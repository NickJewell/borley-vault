# Andrew Clarke

**Andrew Clarke** — clinical psychologist, East Anglian local historian, and author of *Sidelights on Borley Rectory* (web-published, foxearth.org.uk) [1, 2]. Clarke died recently. Borley researchers describe his work as the "Bible of the Borley sceptics" and the modern successor to the highly critical 1956 SPR *Borley Report* [1].

## Background

Clarke lived in East Anglia for approximately 50 years and within 3 miles of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] for around 18 years. He was associated with the **Foxearth Historical Society**, which conducted a 15-year systematic search of all local newspapers from 1780 onward looking for evidence of the Borley haunting (result: nothing before 1929).

His local connections gave him access to witnesses and informants unavailable to outside researchers:
- He knew personally the plumber who maintained Borley Rectory's plumbing system in the 1930s
- He met and knew the last East Anglian itinerant knife-sharpeners (possibly forebears of those who walked the Borley road in 1900)
- A member of the Borley church choir told him the "icy hand" story
- He heard local pub stories about wartime pranksters at the Rectory

## Research Approach and Core Philosophy

Clarke worked from primary documents, particularly the Harry Price Library at Senate House. He had access to material made available by [[vincent-oneil|Vince O'Neil]], including the Swanson interview transcripts and Marianne's autobiography. His approach was to let primary sources speak, acknowledge complexity, and resist simple labels. He summarised his views in an email to investigator Paul Adams: "For me, the Borley Rectory affair is a saga of the times they lived in: the snobbery, the superstition, the sexual undercurrents, the vital necessity to 'keep up appearances'" [3].

Clarke was sceptical but not dismissive — he regarded the Borley affair as important social history regardless of its paranormal status. In the afterword of a later book, he further noted that the haunting serves as an "allegory for the age," "telling us about the pains and strivings of a generation," and adding that "no author could have ever had the temerity to construct such a potent myth" [4].

## Key Contributions

- Identified Bush, Boake and Allen as the source of "paranormal" lavender and cooking smells
- Definitive four-document analysis of the Foyster manuscripts
- Foxearth Historical Society research: no haunting before 1929
- Mechanical analysis of the bell system; identified Frank Pearless's cellar-trapdoor access
- Trace of legend borrowing from Acton, Glemsford, and Great Yeldham
- Identification of three superimposed rectory buildings on the same site
- Theory that Price switched a pig's jawbone for a human one at the 1943 dig

## Rational Explanations

Clarke dismisses the haunting as a "hysterical, fabricated joke" that grew out of the eccentric Bull family's predilection for telling ghost stories in a "boring rural backwater" [5, 6]. He attributes auditory phenomena to the natural creaks of the old house settling, and dismisses sightings of the phantom nun and other apparitions as trespassers, courting couples, or pranks played on gullible housemaids [5]. He characterises the saga as a "put up job by Londoners" [7], reduces [[harry-price|Harry Price]] to an unlikable, expert conjurer looking to make money, dismisses [[francois-darles|François d'Arles]] as a "Cockney con man," and controversially describes the Rev. Edwin Whitehouse as a deeply troubled man with a sexual obsession for the Foysters [6, 7].

Drawing on his background as a clinical psychologist, Clarke also proposes a medical explanation for the specific visions and confusion experienced by the Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] and the Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]]. He pointed out that both men were taking heavy cocktails of prescription drugs: Foyster took artificial digitalin for his heart and painkillers for rheumatism, while Bull was severely depressed and taking painkillers (likely for cancer) [2]. Clarke drew parallels to Evelyn Waugh's book *The Ordeal of Gilbert Pinfold*, noting that in his own clinical practice, "In a surprising number of cases, all symptoms ceased before I did anything else" once patients were taken off such drugs [8].

## The Pig Bones Controversy

Clarke fiercely challenges [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s 1943 excavation of human remains in the Rectory cellars, arguing that Price deliberately substituted human bones for ordinary animal bones found in the clay [9]. He bases this on the testimony of Mr. Jackson, a local farm worker who assisted the dig and was convinced he had pulled out a pig's jaw [9]. According to Clarke, the locals refused to allow pig bones to be interred in [[borley-church|Borley churchyard]], which is why the Rev. Henning had to bury them in the neighbouring parish of Liston [9].

> **Contradiction:** Borley researchers highlight a major flaw in Clarke's theory: the Rev. Henning was the rector of *both* Borley and Liston, meaning the residents of Liston would have objected just as strongly to a pig burial in their churchyard [10]. Furthermore, Price had deliberately brought a qualified pathologist (Dr. Eric Bailey) to the dig to identify any findings, making the substitution of a pig's jaw highly improbable [10].

## Tone and Reception

While some peers respect Clarke as an "honourable sceptic" [2], others are highly critical of his approach. Defenders of the haunting describe *The Bones of Borley* as "continually condescending, arrogant, irritating, full of 'nudge, nudge, wink, wink' innuendo" [7]. They argue that his selective use of out-of-context evidence is designed purely to debunk the story.

He believed that previous critics had overemphasised the roles of [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] at the expense of the rich wider cast of characters [3].

## Source Assessment

The most locally grounded author to write about Borley. Uniquely placed to identify industrial, ecological, and social explanations that outside researchers missed. His essays are consistently well-sourced, fair to all parties, and represent the most careful sceptical analysis of any single Borley source.
