# Andrew Clarke

East Anglian local historian and author of *Sidelights on Borley Rectory* (web-published, foxearth.org.uk). Clarke died recently.

## Background

Clarke lived in East Anglia for approximately 50 years and within 3 miles of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] for around 18 years. He was associated with the **Foxearth Historical Society**, which conducted a 15-year systematic search of all local newspapers from 1780 onward looking for evidence of the Borley haunting (result: nothing before 1929).

His local connections gave him access to witnesses and informants unavailable to outside researchers:
- He knew personally the plumber who maintained Borley Rectory's plumbing system in the 1930s
- He met and knew the last East Anglian itinerant knife-sharpeners (possibly forebears of those who walked the Borley road in 1900)
- A member of the Borley church choir told him the "icy hand" story (slbr-19)
- He heard local pub stories about wartime pranksters at the Rectory

## Research Approach

Clarke worked from primary documents, particularly the Harry Price Library (HPL) at Senate House. He had access to material made available by [[vincent-oneil|Vince O'Neil]], including the Swanson interview transcripts and Marianne's autobiography. His approach: let primary sources speak, acknowledge complexity, resist simple labels. Clarke was skeptical but not dismissive — he regarded the Borley affair as important social history regardless of its paranormal status.

## Key Contributions

- Identified Bush, Boake and Allen as the source of "paranormal" lavender and cooking smells ([[slbr-08]])
- Definitive four-document analysis of the Foyster manuscripts ([[slbr-07]])
- Foxearth Historical Society research: no haunting before 1929 ([[slbr-11]])
- Mechanical analysis of the bell system; identified Frank Pearless's cellar-trapdoor access ([[slbr-09]], [[slbr-13]])
- Trace of legend borrowing from Acton, Glemsford, and Great Yeldham ([[slbr-11]], [[slbr-17]])
- Identification of three superimposed rectory buildings on the same site ([[slbr-21]])
- Theory that Price switched a pig's jawbone for a human one at the 1943 dig ([[slbr-15]])

## Source Assessment

The most locally grounded author to write about Borley. Uniquely placed to identify industrial, ecological, and social explanations that outside researchers missed. His essays are consistently well-sourced, fair to all parties, and represent the most careful skeptical analysis of any single Borley source.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Andrew Clarke** is a **clinical psychologist, an Essex resident, and a historian** associated with the Foxearth and District Local History Society [1, 2]. He is known for authoring ***The Bones of Borley***, a series of essays originally written for Vincent O'Neil's discontinued *Borleyrectory.com* website (under the title *Borley Sidelights*) and later hosted on the Foxearth website [1]. 

Borley researchers describe his work as the **"Bible of the Borley sceptics"** and the modern successor to the highly critical 1956 SPR *Borley Report* [1]. A thorough reference profile of his contributions and theories includes the following details:

**Core Philosophy and Quotes**
Clarke approaches the Borley case from the perspective of human psychology and social history rather than the paranormal [1]. He summarized his views in an email to investigator Paul Adams, stating: **"For me, the Borley Rectory affair is a saga of the times they lived in: the snobbery, the superstition, the sexual undercurrents, the vital necessity to ‘keep up appearances’"** [3]. 

He believed that previous critics had overemphasized the roles of Harry Price and Marianne Foyster at the expense of the rich wider cast of characters [3]. In the afterword of a later book, he further noted that the haunting serves as an **allegory for the age**, "telling us about the pains and strivings of a generation," and adding that "no author could have ever had the temerity to construct such a potent myth" [4].

**Rational Explanations and Character Assassinations**
Clarke dismisses the haunting as a "hysterical, fabricated joke" that grew out of the eccentric Bull family's predilection for telling ghost stories in a "boring rural backwater" [5, 6]. He systematically explains away the phenomena:
*   **Physical/Visual Phenomena:** He attributes auditory phenomena to the natural creaks of the old house settling, and dismisses sightings of the phantom nun and other apparitions as trespassers, courting couples, or pranks played on gullible housemaids [5].
*   **The Investigators:** He characterizes the saga as a "put up job by Londoners" [7]. He reduces Harry Price to an unlikable, expert conjurer looking to make money, dismisses François d’Arles as a "Cockney con man," and controversially describes the Rev. Edwin Whitehouse as a deeply troubled, emotionally scarred man with a sexual obsession for the Foysters [6, 7].

**The Psychological / Drug Theory**
Drawing on his background as a clinical psychologist, Clarke proposes a medical explanation for the specific visions and confusion experienced by the Rev. Lionel Foyster and the Rev. Harry Bull. He pointed out that **both men were taking heavy cocktails of prescription drugs**: Foyster took artificial digitalin for his heart and painkillers for rheumatism, while Bull was severely depressed and taking painkillers (likely for cancer) [2]. 

Clarke drew parallels to the author Evelyn Waugh, whose book *The Ordeal of Gilbert Pinfold* documents the intense hallucinations caused by the prescription drug cocktails common at the time [8]. Clarke noted that in his own clinical practice, "In a surprising number of cases, all symptoms ceased before I did anything else" once patients were taken off such drugs, implying the Rectors' supernatural experiences were drug-induced hallucinations [8]. 

**The Pig Bones Contradiction**
Clarke fiercely challenges Harry Price's 1943 excavation of human remains in the Rectory cellars. Clarke argues that **Price deliberately substituted human bones for ordinary animal bones** that were found in the clay [9]. He bases this on the testimony of Mr. Jackson, a local farm worker who assisted the dig and was convinced he had pulled out a pig's jaw [9]. According to Clarke, the locals refused to allow pig bones to be interred in Borley churchyard, which is why the Rev. Henning had to bury them in the neighboring parish of Liston [9].

However, Borley researchers highlight a major **contradiction** in Clarke's theory: the Rev. Henning was the rector of *both* Borley and Liston, meaning the residents of Liston would have objected just as strongly to a pig burial in their churchyard [10]. Furthermore, Price had deliberately brought a qualified pathologist (Dr. Eric Bailey) to the dig to identify any findings, making the substitution of a pig's jaw highly improbable [10].

**Tone and Reception**
While some peers respect Clarke as an "honorable skeptic" [2], others are highly critical of his approach. Defenders of the haunting describe *The Bones of Borley* as **"continually condescending, arrogant, irritating, full of 'nudge, nudge, wink, wink' innuendo"** [7]. They argue that his selective use of out-of-context evidence is designed purely to debunk the story and draw a line around the village to tell outsiders, "strangers keep away, nothing to see here" [5, 7].
