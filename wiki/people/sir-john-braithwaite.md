# Sir John Braithwaite

A neighbour of [[lady-whitehouse|Sir George and Lady Whitehouse]], and like them known to the [[bull-family]] and the Foysters at Borley in the early 1930s.

## Visit and Assessment

Sir John and Lady Braithwaite visited [[borley-rectory]] in May 1931 at the height of the disturbances. They were disinclined to accept a paranormal origin for the [[foyster-phenomena]] and came to the conclusion that [[marianne-foyster]] was hysterical and the root cause of the disturbing events.

During August 1931, Sir John Braithwaite compiled a detailed report in which he put forward his view that the [[wall-writings]] were carried out by Marianne Foyster herself (see Chronology, 13–17 August 1931). This was sent to [[william-salter]] at the [[society-for-psychical-research]], but the SPR took no action on the Borley case at this time.

His sister Miss Mary Braithwaite was also associated with the case and later passed to [[harry-price]] a report by her brother on the [[joe-miles-seance]] — the August 1931 sitting involving a medium named Johnson, during which a drunken publican called "Joe Miles" apparently claimed responsibility for the haunting. Braithwaite's report was included in _EBR_. Miss Braithwaite was of the opinion that the wall writings were not produced by poltergeists but by Marianne Foyster.

## The Séance Connection

At one time the Braithwaites were in possession of one of the paper messages addressed to Marianne — one of the early apported paper slips bearing her name — which later became part of [[peter-underwood]]'s Borley collection.

## Significance

Braithwaite's early report to the SPR identifying Marianne as the source of the wall writings is significant as an early, independent sceptical assessment that predated the SPR's formal investigation by over twenty years. His view anticipated the conclusion ultimately reached by [[trevor-hall]] in the 1956 [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]].

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Sir John Braithwaite** (frequently referred to in the texts as **Mr. W. John Braithwaite** or simply **Mr. Braithwaite**) was a London-based civil servant who played a pivotal, highly skeptical role during the poltergeist outbreak of the Foyster incumbency at Borley Rectory. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, investigations, specific quotes, and textual contradictions regarding his involvement:

**Biographical Details and Background**
*   **Family:** He was the brother of Miss Mary E. Braithwaite, J.P., and Miles Braithwaite (a former mayor and town clerk of Sudbury), who resided together at Brook House in Long Melford [1]. He was also identified by Lady Whitehouse as a **"cousin of the [Braithwaite]s of Norton"** [2]. Following his death, his widow lived at ‘Greenwood,’ Bidborough Ridge, Tunbridge Wells [3].
*   **Connections to Borley:** Braithwaite had known Borley Rectory and the Bull family since his childhood [4]. He maintained a lifelong interest in psychical research and was acquainted with neighbors Sir George and Lady Whitehouse [1, 5]. 

**The August 1931 Séance**
During a summer holiday visiting his siblings in Long Melford in August 1931, Braithwaite organized a séance at the Rectory to help the Foysters [1, 6]. 
*   **Contradictions Regarding Companions:** The sources contain direct contradictions regarding the men Braithwaite brought to the house. The Rev. Lionel Foyster’s own manuscript records that Braithwaite introduced two companions: **"Mr. Thompson, the medium, and Mr. Teed, the interrogator"** [7]. However, other historical sources and Braithwaite's own records state that he brought his friend **Ernest Meads** and a 67-year-old masseur and medium named **Mr. Johnson** [6, 8, 9].
*   **Contradictions Regarding Dates:** The texts also offer conflicting dates for this séance, citing it as occurring on either **August 11** [8] or **August 12, 1931** [6]. A separate entry also erroneously places Braithwaite's visit during the height of the disturbances in **May 1931** [5]. 
*   **Séance Details:** Held in the Rectory library, the medium’s "guides" included a Red Indian and a **"Dr. Haslam of Harley Street,"** who diagnosed Foyster with rheumatism and instructed him to **"avoid potatoes, and starchy foods, such as tapioca and sago"** [8]. An entity named "Joe Miles" also purportedly communicated [10]. 
*   **Physical Phenomena:** Braithwaite compiled a 5,000-word report on the sitting, noting dramatic phenomena, including the sudden appearance of 24 old-fashioned colored crockery egg-cups, twelve of which **"were thrown across the room, denting the door, and falling without breaking"** [4, 8].

**Conclusions on Marianne Foyster**
Despite the dramatic phenomena, Braithwaite was entirely disinclined to accept a paranormal origin [5]. He returned from the séance unnerved but convinced that **Marianne Foyster was hysterical and the root cause of the activity** [5, 11].
*   **Psychological Theory:** He theorized that her behavior was psychological, stemming from her exhaustion in running a huge house without servants, her marriage to a much older and sickly husband, and her infertility [12]. 
*   **Confronting the Rector:** Braithwaite subsequently paid a personal visit to Lionel Foyster. According to Foyster, Braithwaite arrived with a **"special message"** and, with great hesitation and diffidence, told the Rector the harsh truth: **"It was my wife that was behind it all"** [13].
*   **The Wall Writings:** In a report dated **August 13, 1931** (forwarded to W. H. Salter of the Society for Psychical Research by his sister Mary), Sir John explicitly concluded that the famous Borley wall-writings were **"obviously done by Mrs Foyster"** when she was not in a normal condition, basing his verdict on comparisons with her regular handwriting [14]. 

**Surviving Artifacts**
Following his death, his widow discovered a specific Borley artifact among his effects: a **"paper-message"** written on the back of an old envelope. It featured the word "Marianne" in handwriting identical to the wall-messages, under which Mrs. Foyster had written "What do you want?" and the word "Rest" had mysteriously appeared [15]. This envelope, along with his extensive 5,000-word report on the séance, was eventually given to Harry Price [8, 15].
