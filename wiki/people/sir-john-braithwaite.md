# Sir John Braithwaite

Also frequently referred to in the texts as **Mr. W. John Braithwaite** or simply **Mr. Braithwaite**. A London-based civil servant who played a pivotal, highly sceptical role during the poltergeist outbreak of the Foyster incumbency at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

## Background

He was the brother of Miss Mary E. Braithwaite, J.P., and Miles Braithwaite (a former mayor and town clerk of Sudbury), who resided together at Brook House in Long Melford [1]. He was also identified by [[lady-whitehouse|Lady Whitehouse]] as a "cousin of the [Braithwaites] of Norton" [2]. Braithwaite had known Borley Rectory and the [[bull-family|Bull family]] since his childhood [4], maintained a lifelong interest in psychical research, and was acquainted with neighbours Sir George and [[lady-whitehouse|Lady Whitehouse]] [1, 5].

## Visit and Assessment

Sir John and Lady Braithwaite visited [[borley-rectory]] in May 1931 at the height of the disturbances. They were disinclined to accept a paranormal origin for the [[foyster-phenomena]] and came to the conclusion that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] was hysterical and the root cause of the disturbing events [5, 11].

He theorised that her behaviour was psychological, stemming from her exhaustion in running a huge house without servants, her marriage to a much older and sickly husband, and her infertility [12]. Braithwaite subsequently paid a personal visit to [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] and with great hesitation and diffidence told the Rector the harsh truth: **"It was my wife that was behind it all"** [13].

## The August 1931 Séance

During a summer holiday visiting his siblings in Long Melford in August 1931, Braithwaite organised a séance at the Rectory to help the Foysters [1, 6].

> **Contradiction:** The sources contain direct contradictions regarding Braithwaite's companions. Foyster's own manuscript records that Braithwaite introduced two companions: **"Mr. Thompson, the medium, and Mr. Teed, the interrogator"** [7]. However, other historical sources and Braithwaite's own records state that he brought his friend [[ernest-meads|Ernest Meads]] and a 67-year-old masseur and medium named **Mr. Johnson** [6, 8, 9].

> **Contradiction:** The texts also offer conflicting dates for this séance: either **August 11** [8] or **August 12, 1931** [6].

Held in the Rectory library, the medium's "guides" included a Red Indian and a **"Dr. Haslam of Harley Street,"** who diagnosed Foyster with rheumatism and instructed him to **"avoid potatoes, and starchy foods, such as tapioca and sago"** [8]. An entity named "Joe Miles" also purportedly communicated. Braithwaite compiled a 5,000-word report on the sitting, noting dramatic physical phenomena: the sudden appearance of 24 old-fashioned coloured crockery egg-cups, twelve of which **"were thrown across the room, denting the door, and falling without breaking"** [4, 8].

## The Wall Writings Report

In a report dated **August 13, 1931** (forwarded to [[william-salter|W. H. Salter]] of the Society for Psychical Research by his sister Mary), Sir John explicitly concluded that the famous Borley [[wall-writings|wall-writings]] were **"obviously done by Mrs Foyster"** when she was not in a normal condition, basing his verdict on comparisons with her regular handwriting [14]. This report — sent to the SPR, who took no action on the Borley case at this time — is a significant early independent sceptical assessment predating the SPR's formal investigation by over twenty years, and anticipated the conclusion ultimately reached by [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] in the 1956 [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]].

## The Séance Connection

At one time the Braithwaites were in possession of one of the paper messages addressed to Marianne — an early apported paper slip bearing her name — which later became part of [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]]'s Borley collection. Following his death, his widow discovered among his effects a **"paper-message"** written on the back of an old envelope: the word "Marianne" in handwriting identical to the wall-messages, under which Mrs. Foyster had written "What do you want?" and the word "Rest" had mysteriously appeared [15]. This envelope, along with his extensive 5,000-word séance report, was eventually given to Harry Price [8, 15].
