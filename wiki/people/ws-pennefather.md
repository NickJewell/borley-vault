# W. S. Pennefather

Wrote to [[harry-price]] during the preparation of *The Most Haunted House in England* to report that his father — a clergyman — had rented Borley Rectory for a six-week holiday before 1896. No phenomena were experienced during the stay.

## Account

Price contacted Pennefather after receiving his letter, asking whether he could fix the exact year of the visit. Pennefather could not, but noted that his father was appointed Vicar of Kensington in 1896, and the Borley holiday was before that appointment. If the year was 1895, the visit would fall during the incumbency of the Rev. [[harry-bull]], who succeeded his father as rector in 1892.

The letter states plainly: "to the best of my knowledge, no incidents occurred during our stay, and it is unlikely that the then incumbent said anything to my father, as he was desirous of letting the house."

Price accepts the negative report as legitimate — noting that after 45 years "it would be difficult to remember trivial, but unusual happenings, had they occurred" — and uses it as one of three "blank" reports out of all observers during his tenancy and investigations.

## Significance

Pennefather's account is valuable as evidence of the Rectory's appearance on the market during the Bull incumbency — confirming that Harry Bull periodically let the house out, presumably during periods when he and his sisters were not in residence. It also demonstrates that at least one outside party spent six weeks there in the 1880s–1890s without experiencing obvious phenomena.

> **Note:** Only his surname and one initial are recorded. His father's name and the exact year of the visit are unconfirmed.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**W. S. Pennefather** of Walton-on-Thames is a minor but significant figure in the Borley Rectory literature. His importance stems entirely from a letter he wrote providing first-hand testimony of a completely peaceful, un-haunted stay at the Rectory during a period when it was supposedly plagued by supernatural phenomena. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following details regarding his family's visit and the subsequent controversy his testimony created among psychical researchers:

**The 1895 Summer Tenancy**
*   **Family Background:** He was the son of the **Rev. Somerset E. Pennefather**, a clergyman who was appointed Vicar of Kensington in 1896 [1].
*   **The Visit:** Prior to this 1896 appointment, the Rev. Somerset Pennefather rented Borley Rectory for his family to use for a **six-week summer holiday** [2, 3]. Harry Price calculated that this vacation most likely took place in the summer of **1895**, placing it firmly during the incumbency of the Rev. Harry Bull [1, 4].

**Pennefather's Testimony to Harry Price**
During the preparation of his Borley writings, Harry Price received a letter from W. S. Pennefather detailing his recollections of that six-week stay [3]. In direct contradiction to the legend that Borley was constantly active with poltergeists during the Bull era, Pennefather reported absolutely no paranormal activity. 
*   **The Quote:** He wrote to Price: **"My father, a clergyman, rented this house for his six weeks' holiday one summer, previous to 1896. To the best of my knowledge, no incidents occurred during our stay, and it is unlikely that the then incumbent said anything to my father, as he was desirous of letting the house"** [3]. 

**Price's Dismissal and the Skeptical Backlash**
Pennefather's statement became a prime example of the intense methodological disputes between Harry Price and his skeptical critics.
*   **Price's Reaction:** Harry Price essentially brushed aside Pennefather's testimony [2]. In his book, Price noted that while he did not question Pennefather's assertion that there were "no incidents," he rationalized the lack of phenomena by arguing that **"after forty-five years it would be difficult to remember trivial, but unusual happenings, had they occurred"** [1]. 
*   **The SPR's Criticism:** The authors of the highly skeptical *Borley Report* (Eric Dingwall, Kathleen Goldney, and Trevor Hall) seized upon Price's dismissal of Pennefather's letter as clear evidence of his bias. They argued that Price deliberately discounted normal causes and silenced critics whose experiences weakened the "flimsy structure" of the Borley legend [2, 5]. The authors pointedly remarked that if Pennefather had managed to remember anything that actually supported the ghost story, **"we may be sure that it would have found a place"** in Price's book [2].
