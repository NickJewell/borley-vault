# W. S. Pennefather

Of Walton-on-Thames. Son of the **Rev. Somerset E. Pennefather**, a clergyman who was appointed Vicar of Kensington in 1896 [1]. Wrote to [[harry-price|Harry Price]] during the preparation of *The Most Haunted House in England* to report that his father had rented [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] for a six-week holiday before 1896. No phenomena were experienced during the stay.

> **Note:** Only his surname and one initial are recorded. His father's name and the exact year of the visit are unconfirmed.

## The 1895 Summer Tenancy

Prior to the Rev. Somerset Pennefather's 1896 appointment to Kensington, he rented Borley Rectory for his family to use for a **six-week summer holiday** [2, 3]. [[harry-price|Harry Price]] calculated that this vacation most likely took place in the summer of **1895**, placing it firmly during the incumbency of the Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]], who succeeded his father as rector in 1892 [1, 4].

In direct contradiction to the legend that Borley was constantly active with poltergeists during the Bull era, Pennefather reported absolutely no paranormal activity. He wrote to Price: **"My father, a clergyman, rented this house for his six weeks' holiday one summer, previous to 1896. To the best of my knowledge, no incidents occurred during our stay, and it is unlikely that the then incumbent said anything to my father, as he was desirous of letting the house"** [3].

## Price's Dismissal and the Sceptical Backlash

Price essentially brushed aside Pennefather's testimony. He rationalised the lack of phenomena by arguing that **"after forty-five years it would be difficult to remember trivial, but unusual happenings, had they occurred"** [1], while accepting the negative report as legitimate.

The authors of the [[the-borley-report|*Borley Report*]] ([[eric-dingwall|Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Hall]]) seized upon Price's dismissal as clear evidence of his bias. They argued that Price deliberately discounted normal causes and silenced critics whose experiences weakened the "flimsy structure" of the Borley legend [2, 5], pointedly remarking that if Pennefather had managed to remember anything that actually supported the ghost story, "we may be sure that it would have found a place" in Price's book [2].

## Significance

Pennefather's account is valuable as evidence that [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] periodically let the house out during his incumbency, and that at least one outside party spent six weeks there in the 1880s–1890s without experiencing obvious phenomena. It also demonstrates Price's selective use of testimony — positive reports were amplified; negative ones minimised.
