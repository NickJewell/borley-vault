# Payne Family

A well-known local family, landowners and farmers, living at Borley Hall and [[borley-place|Borley Place]] (formerly Borley Manor House). The family had long-standing connections with [[borley-church]].

## Borley Place and the Rectors

When [[rev-ac-henning|Alfred Henning]] took the living in 1936, he and his family lived for a time with Mr and Mrs Basil Payne at Borley Place while arrangements were made to find the Hennings a suitable permanent home. Later Henning had a dispute with the Paynes concerning a right of way and thereafter the Paynes took little interest in church affairs during the rest of Henning's incumbency.

## Witnesses to Phenomena

Both Mr and Mrs Payne witnessed unexplained lights in the Rectory windows in 1937. Earlier, during the [[smith-incumbency|Smiths' occupancy]], Mrs E.E. Payne recalled [[mabel-smith]] pointing out the "mysterious lights" in the Rectory windows to her and other neighbours (see [[window-lights]]).

On 2 June 1937, [[harry-price]] and [[ellic-howe]] were entertained to tea at Borley Place by Mr and Mrs Basil Payne. Both Paynes later watched the fire that burned down [[borley-rectory]] on 27/28 February 1939. [[peter-underwood]] met the Paynes on his first visit to Borley in 1947 and remained friends with them for the rest of their lives.

## Mrs Payne's View of Marianne

Mrs Payne was for a time churchwarden and held the keys to [[borley-church]] in the 1970s, sometimes agreeing to let investigators spend a night inside the locked church. During one of Underwood's many visits to Borley Place, Mrs Payne told him much about [[marianne-foyster]], whom she described as "a very funny woman" — and she did not mean "funny ha ha." She said she had always felt sure that Marianne was responsible for the [[wine-into-ink-trick|wine into ink episode]] that occurred when [[harry-price]] lunched at the Rectory in 1931 — also the impression of the Honourable Everard Fielding and others.

She and her husband used to refer to [[borley-rectory]] as "that horrible old place." In one of her last letters to Underwood (dated 22 October 1979) she reported that Borley Place had been sold to a local farmer, adding: "we are not leaving Borley." She never did.

## Property Legacy

The Payne family owned not only Borley Hall but also Borley Place and the adjacent farm; they were in effect the principal landowners in Borley village through the twentieth century. When [[captain-gregson]] bought the Rectory in 1938, the Rectory Cottage was in the possession of the Paynes.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

The **Payne Family** were prominent local landowners and farmers whose properties and lives were deeply intertwined with the Borley Rectory saga [1, 2]. By the end of the 1890s, the family had acquired the Borley estate, which included Borley Hall (the former seat of the historic Waldegrave family), Borley Place, the adjacent farm, and the Rectory Cottage [3-5]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following specific biographical details, events, quotes, and interactions regarding the family's involvement in the haunting:

**Key Family Members**
*   **Robert and Annie Payne:** The patriarch and matriarch who lived at Borley Hall and had five sons [6, 7].
*   **Basil Payne and his wife Edna (also recorded as Mrs. E. E. Payne):** Robert’s eldest son, who lived across the road from the Rectory at the historic Borley Place [7-9].
*   **Patrick Payne:** One of the five brothers and the last of the family to own Borley Hall. He occupied the property until May 1983, after which he passed away and the Hall moved out of local hands [5, 7, 10].

**Relationship with the Borley Clergy**
Because they owned Borley Place, the Paynes acted as landlords to the local clergy during periods of transition. 
*   The Rev. Harry Bull and his wife Ivy resided at Borley Place for nine years following their marriage in 1911, before moving into the Rectory [3]. 
*   When the Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning arrived to take the living in 1936, the Paynes hosted him and his family at Borley Place for nine months [1-3]. However, this relationship soured when the Rev. Henning and the Paynes fell into a **dispute concerning a right of way** [1, 11]. Following this disagreement, the Paynes took very little interest in church affairs for the remainder of Henning’s incumbency [1, 11].

**Witnessing the Rectory Phenomena**
Both Mr. and Mrs. Basil Payne claimed to have witnessed the famous "mysterious lights" shining from the Rectory windows.
*   **The Smith Incumbency:** Mrs. Payne recalled that Mrs. Mabel Smith had pointed out the unexplained lights in the Rectory windows to her and other neighbors during their 1928–1929 residency [12, 13].
*   **The Price Tenancy:** On the night of **November 9, 1937**, the Paynes again saw a "bright light" emanating from a south-facing window (identified as Room 11) while the house was locked and unoccupied [14, 15]. They were adamant that this phenomenon could not be explained by moonlight or reflections from passing trains or cars [16].
*   **The Fire:** On the night of **February 27/28, 1939**, Basil and Edna Payne personally stood and watched the fire that gutted the Rectory [12].

**Opinions on Marianne Foyster**
Mrs. Payne was highly suspicious of the Rev. Lionel Foyster's young wife, Marianne, and did not believe her to be the victim of supernatural forces. 
*   During an interview with investigator Peter Underwood, she described Marianne as **"a very funny woman,"** explicitly noting that **"she did not mean 'funny ha ha'"** [17]. 
*   Mrs. Payne felt absolutely certain that Marianne was fraudulently responsible for the famous "wine into ink" poltergeist episode that occurred when Harry Price lunched at the Rectory in 1931 [8, 17].
*   **The Dressing Gown Incident:** In a well-known anecdote, Marianne approached Mrs. Payne to ask if the late Harry Bull had possessed a plum-colored dressing gown, claiming to have seen a ghostly figure wearing one [18, 19]. Confirming the detail, Mrs. Payne replied: **"Yes, indeed, he almost lived in it – but how on earth did you know that?"** [19].

**Interactions with Investigators**
The Paynes were generally hospitable and helpful to researchers visiting the site. 
*   On **June 2, 1937**, when Harry Price and his colleague Ellic Howe were setting up the year-long investigation, Mr. and Mrs. Basil Payne entertained them to tea at Borley Place and had a long chat about the Rectory [12, 20]. 
*   Despite the allegations of fraud leveled against Price after his death, Mrs. Payne told investigator Grant Vallender in 1968 that she considered Price to have been an **"honest man"** [8]. 
*   She and her husband harbored no affection for the Rectory itself, frequently referring to it as **"that horrible old place"** [17].

**Later Years and Role in the Church**
By the 1970s, Mrs. Payne was acting as the churchwarden and had charge of the keys to Borley Church [17]. In this capacity, she occasionally permitted investigators to spend the night locked inside the building [17, 21]. In her final years, she wrote a letter to Peter Underwood dated **October 22, 1979**, confirming that Borley Place had been sold to a local farmer. However, she defiantly added the quote, **"we are not leaving Borley,"** and the texts confirm she never did [17].
