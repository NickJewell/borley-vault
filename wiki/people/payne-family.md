# Payne Family

A well-known local family, landowners and farmers, living at Borley Hall and [[borley-place|Borley Place]] (formerly Borley Manor House). The family had long-standing connections with [[borley-church]].

## Borley Place and the Rectors

When [[rev-ac-henning|Alfred Henning]] took the living in 1936, he and his family lived for a time with Mr and Mrs Basil Payne at Borley Place while arrangements were made to find the Hennings a suitable permanent home. Later Henning had a dispute with the Paynes concerning a right of way and thereafter the Paynes took little interest in church affairs during the rest of Henning's incumbency.

## Witnesses to Phenomena

Both Mr and Mrs Payne witnessed unexplained lights in the Rectory windows in 1937. Earlier, during the [[smith-incumbency|Smiths' occupancy]], Mrs E.E. Payne recalled [[mabel-smith]] pointing out the "mysterious lights" in the Rectory windows to her and other neighbours (see [[window-lights]]).

On 2 June 1937, [[harry-price]] and [[ellic-howe]] were entertained to tea at Borley Place by Mr and Mrs Basil Payne. Both Paynes later watched the fire that burned down [[borley-rectory]] on 27/28 February 1939. [[peter-underwood]] met the Paynes on his first visit to Borley in 1947 and remained friends with them for the rest of their lives.

## Mrs Payne's View of Marianne

Mrs Payne was for a time churchwarden and held the keys to [[borley-church]] in the 1970s, sometimes agreeing to let investigators spend a night inside the locked church. During one of Underwood's many visits to Borley Place, Mrs Payne told him much about [[marianne-foyster]], whom she described as "a very funny woman" — and she did not mean "funny ha ha." She said she had always felt sure that Marianne was responsible for the [[wine-into-ink-trick|wine into ink episode]] that occurred when [[harry-price]] lunched at the Rectory in 1931 — also the impression of the Honourable Everard Fielding and others.

She and her husband used to refer to [[borley-rectory]] as "that horrible old place." In one of her last letters to Underwood (dated 22 October 1979) she reported that Borley Place had been sold to a local farmer, adding: "we are not leaving Borley." She never did.

## Property Legacy

The Payne family owned not only Borley Hall but also Borley Place and the adjacent farm; they were in effect the principal landowners in Borley village through the twentieth century. When [[captain-gregson]] bought the Rectory in 1938, the Rectory Cottage was in the possession of the Paynes.
