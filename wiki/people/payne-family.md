# Payne Family

A well-known local family, landowners and farmers, living at Borley Hall and [[borley-place|Borley Place]] (formerly Borley Manor House). The family had long-standing connections with [[borley-church]].

## Family Members

By the end of the 1890s, the Payne family had acquired the Borley estate, which included Borley Hall (the former seat of the historic [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]]), Borley Place, the adjacent farm, and the Rectory Cottage [3–5].

**Robert and Annie Payne** were the patriarch and matriarch who lived at Borley Hall and had five sons [6, 7]. Their eldest son, **Basil Payne** (and his wife **Edna**, also recorded as Mrs E. E. Payne), lived across the road from the Rectory at the historic Borley Place [7–9]. **Patrick Payne** was one of the five brothers and the last of the family to own Borley Hall; he occupied the property until May 1983, after which he passed away and the Hall moved out of local hands [5, 7, 10].

## Borley Place and the Rectors

Because they owned Borley Place, the Paynes acted as landlords to the local clergy during periods of transition. The Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] and his wife Ivy resided at Borley Place for nine years following their marriage in 1911, before moving into the Rectory [3].

When [[rev-ac-henning|Alfred Henning]] took the living in 1936, he and his family stayed with Mr and Mrs Basil Payne at Borley Place for nine months while arrangements were made to find the Hennings a suitable permanent home [1–3]. This relationship later soured: Henning and the Paynes fell into a **dispute concerning a right of way** [1, 11], and thereafter the Paynes took very little interest in church affairs during the rest of Henning's incumbency [1, 11].

## Witnesses to Phenomena

Both Mr and Mrs Basil Payne witnessed unexplained lights in the Rectory windows on multiple occasions.

During the **Smith incumbency**, Mrs Payne recalled that [[mabel-smith|Mrs Mabel Smith]] had pointed out the mysterious lights in the Rectory windows to her and other neighbours (see [[window-lights]]) [12, 13].

On the night of **9 November 1937**, during the [[the-observer-program|Price tenancy]], the Paynes again saw a bright light emanating from a south-facing window (identified as Room 11) while the house was locked and unoccupied [14, 15]. They were adamant that this phenomenon could not be explained by moonlight or reflections from passing trains or cars [16].

On **2 June 1937**, [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and [[ellic-howe|Ellic Howe]] were entertained to tea at Borley Place by Mr and Mrs Basil Payne; both Paynes had a long conversation with Price about the Rectory [12, 20]. On the night of **27/28 February 1939**, Basil and Edna Payne stood and watched the fire that gutted [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] [12]. [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] met the Paynes on his first visit to Borley in 1947 and remained friends with them for the rest of their lives.

## Mrs Payne's View of Marianne

Mrs Payne was highly suspicious of the Rev. Lionel Foyster's young wife, [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]], and did not believe her to be the victim of supernatural forces. During an interview with Underwood, she described Marianne as **"a very funny woman,"** explicitly noting that she did not mean "funny ha ha" [17]. She was absolutely certain that Marianne had fraudulently staged the famous [[wine-into-ink-trick|wine into ink]] poltergeist episode that occurred when [[harry-price|Harry Price]] lunched at the Rectory in 1931 [8, 17] — also the impression of the Honourable Everard Fielding and others.

One well-known anecdote records Marianne approaching Mrs Payne to ask if the late Harry Bull had possessed a plum-coloured dressing gown, claiming to have seen a ghostly figure wearing one [18, 19]. Confirming the detail, Mrs Payne replied: **"Yes, indeed, he almost lived in it – but how on earth did you know that?"** [19].

Despite the fraud allegations levelled against [[harry-price|Price]] after his death, Mrs Payne told investigator Grant Vallender in 1968 that she considered Price to have been an **"honest man"** [8]. She and her husband used to refer to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] as **"that horrible old place"** [17].

## Property Legacy and Later Years

The Payne family owned not only Borley Hall but also Borley Place and the adjacent farm; they were in effect the principal landowners in Borley village throughout the twentieth century. When [[captain-gregson|Captain Gregson]] bought the Rectory in 1938, the Rectory Cottage was in the possession of the Paynes.

By the 1970s, Mrs Payne was acting as churchwarden and held the keys to [[borley-church|Borley Church]], occasionally permitting investigators to spend the night locked inside the building [17, 21]. In one of her last letters to Underwood (dated **22 October 1979**) she reported that Borley Place had been sold to a local farmer, adding: **"we are not leaving Borley"** [17]. She never did.
