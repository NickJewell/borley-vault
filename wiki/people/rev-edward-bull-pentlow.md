# Rev. Edward Bull (Pentlow)

Anglican clergyman (c. 1803/1812–1877); father of [[henry-bull|Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] and the Bull family patriarch who shaped the dynasty that would build [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

## Early Life and Lineage

Edward was the son of the Rev. John Bull (who served as Rector of Pentlow from 1802 until 1834) and his wife, Margaret [1–4]. He broke his family's previous tradition at Christ's College when he was admitted as a sizar at St John's College, Cambridge, on **4 November 1831** [4].

> **Contradiction:** The sources conflict regarding his birth year. Several record his life as spanning **"c. 1812–1877"** [5, 6], while another source states that in 1830 Edward began working as a curate to his father **"at the age of twenty-seven,"** which would place his birth around **1803** [2].

## First Marriage, Family, and Tragedy

He married **Elizabeth Hoddson** in 1830 [2]. Together they had three children: Mary (born 1831), **Henry Dawson Ellis Bull** (born 1833, the future rector of Borley), and Winifred (born 1834) [2]. The family was struck by intense grief when nine-year-old Winifred died in 1843, followed by Elizabeth's death from breast cancer the following year [2].

## The Nottingham Tragedy (1848)

Just before Easter 1848, the widowed Edward temporarily moved with his surviving children to Snetterton in Nottingham, sharing a rectory with a 26-year-old curate, **William Brown** [2]. Brown became intensely infatuated with Edward's 17-year-old daughter Mary. When Brown asked Edward for Mary's hand in marriage on 9 November, Edward discussed it with his daughter, who rejected the proposal [2]. Edward relayed her decision, after which Brown went to the ruins of Nottingham Castle and killed himself by shooting himself in the head [2]. Following this "shocking occurrence," Edward and his family returned to Pentlow [2].

## Second Marriage and Felix Bull

In 1851, following his period of widowhood, Edward married his second wife **Cornelia**, who gave birth to his youngest son, [[felix-bull|Felix Edward Pepys Bull]] — Latin for *happy* or *lucky* [2, 7]. Felix would later serve as rector of Pentlow himself and experienced a famous poltergeist in his own household.

## Clerical Career

Edward succeeded his father as Rector of Pentlow in **1834**, holding the position until his death in **1877** [4, 6]. In **1861**, he formally procured the living of Borley and Pentlow [5]. As a result, the next two Rectors of Borley were his son (Henry) and his grandson (Harry), while Felix succeeded him as Rector of Pentlow [4, 6, 7].

## The Pentlow Tower

In **1859** (though some sources date it to 1858 [3, 8, 11]), Edward commissioned a memorial tower in the rectory garden at Pentlow in honour of his father John. This was a massive octagonal brick tower in the Tudor style, designed by **[[john-johnson-architect|John Johnson]]**, an architect from Bury St Edmunds. A plaque on the tower reads: **"Erected to the memory of his honoured parents the Rev. John Bull, M.A. and Margaret his wife. On a spot they loved so well by Edw. Bull, M.A. 1859"** [1, 6, 9].

> **Contradiction:** The sources conflict regarding the tower's height: descriptions range from **"some sixty feet high"** [8], to exactly **90 feet** [3, 12, 13], to **95 feet** [9].

Henry Dawson, admiring its style and scale, chose the same architect ([[john-johnson-architect|John Johnson]]) to design [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

Eccentric legends sprang up around the tower: some villagers believed Edward built it to spy on farm labourers, while others claimed he built it hoping to see the sea but was disappointed. Furthermore, some locals allegedly **"sniggered that Reverend Bull's phallic tower celebrated his father's fertility more than it did his memory"** [3].

## Character

Like his son Henry, Edward Bull had a reputation as an authoritarian and eccentric rural "squarson." An infamous local legend states that during a local shoot, while waiting for birds to fly overhead, an innocent pig happened to amble by — and Edward shot it [14]. In a humorous variant, **"an element of divine retribution"** features: the pig allegedly bit the rector first [14].
