# Rev. Edward Bull (Pentlow)

Anglican clergyman; father of [[henry-bull|Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] and the Bull family patriarch who shaped the dynasty that would build Borley Rectory.

## Biography

Edward Bull was born into the clerical Bull family. His own father, **Rev. John Bull**, had been rector of **St Gregory and St George** at Pentlow, two and a half miles from Borley, since 1802. In 1830, at the age of twenty-seven, Edward began working as curate to his father, the same year he married **Elizabeth Hoddson**. They had three children: Mary (born 1831), **Henry Dawson** (born 1833, the future rector of Borley), and Winifred (born 1834).

Edward succeeded his father as rector of Pentlow. The family was stricken with grief: Winifred died aged nine in 1843, and his wife Elizabeth died of breast cancer the following year.

## Second Marriage and Felix Bull

In 1851, following his period of widowhood, Edward married again. His second wife was **Cornelia**, and they had a much-wanted son, **Felix** — Latin for *happy* or *lucky*. [[felix-bull|Felix Bull]] would later serve as rector of Pentlow himself and experienced a famous poltergeist in his own household.

## The Pentlow Tower

In 1859, Edward commissioned a memorial tower in the rectory garden at Pentlow in honour of his father John, who had died in 1834. This was a **90-foot-high octagonal brick tower** in the Tudor style, designed by **John Johnson**, an architect from Bury St Edmunds. The tower was sometimes snickered at locally as celebrating Edward's father's fertility rather than his memory. Henry Dawson, admiring its style and scale, chose the same architect ([[john-johnson-architect|John Johnson]]) to design [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

## The Suicide of William Brown

One of the most dramatic events in Edward's tenure was the suicide of **William Brown**, a curate at St James's Church, Nottingham. In 1848, when Edward temporarily moved to Snetterton with his children Mary and Henry, the twenty-six-year-old Brown fell hopelessly in love with the seventeen-year-old Mary Bull. After Edward relayed Mary's rejection, Brown was seen rushing through Nottingham in a "very hurried and excited manner" before going to the ruins of Nottingham Castle and shooting himself in the head. The Bull family were plunged into "deep affliction" and returned to Pentlow.
