# Rev. Edward Bull (Pentlow)

Anglican clergyman; father of [[henry-bull|Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] and the Bull family patriarch who shaped the dynasty that would build Borley Rectory.

## Biography

Edward Bull was born into the clerical Bull family. His own father, **Rev. John Bull**, had been rector of **St Gregory and St George** at Pentlow, two and a half miles from Borley, since 1802. In 1830, at the age of twenty-seven, Edward began working as curate to his father, the same year he married **Elizabeth Hoddson**. They had three children: Mary (born 1831), **Henry Dawson** (born 1833, the future rector of Borley), and Winifred (born 1834).

Edward succeeded his father as rector of Pentlow. The family was stricken with grief: Winifred died aged nine in 1843, and his wife Elizabeth died of breast cancer the following year.

## Second Marriage and Felix Bull

In 1851, following his period of widowhood, Edward married again. His second wife was **Cornelia**, and they had a much-wanted son, **Felix** — Latin for *happy* or *lucky*. [[felix-bull|Felix Bull]] would later serve as rector of Pentlow himself and experienced a famous poltergeist in his own household.

## The Pentlow Tower

In 1859, Edward commissioned a memorial tower in the rectory garden at Pentlow in honour of his father John, who had died in 1834. This was a **90-foot-high octagonal brick tower** in the Tudor style, designed by **John Johnson**, an architect from Bury St Edmunds. The tower was sometimes snickered at locally as celebrating Edward's father's fertility rather than his memory. Henry Dawson, admiring its style and scale, chose the same architect ([[john-johnson-architect|John Johnson]]) to design [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

## The Suicide of William Brown

One of the most dramatic events in Edward's tenure was the suicide of **William Brown**, a curate at St James's Church, Nottingham. In 1848, when Edward temporarily moved to Snetterton with his children Mary and Henry, the twenty-six-year-old Brown fell hopelessly in love with the seventeen-year-old Mary Bull. After Edward relayed Mary's rejection, Brown was seen rushing through Nottingham in a "very hurried and excited manner" before going to the ruins of Nottingham Castle and shooting himself in the head. The Bull family were plunged into "deep affliction" and returned to Pentlow.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Reverend Edward Bull** (c. 1803/1812–1877) was the Rector of Pentlow and the patriarch of the famous Bull clerical dynasty of the region, primarily remembered in the Borley corpus as the father of Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull (the builder of the "haunted" Borley Rectory). 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, events, dates, quotes, and chronological contradictions regarding his life and legacy:

**Lineage and Early Life**
*   **Parents:** Edward was the son of the Rev. John Bull (who served as Rector of Pentlow from 1802 until 1834) and his wife, Margaret [1-4].
*   **Education:** He broke his family's previous tradition at Christ's College when he was admitted as a sizar at St John's College, Cambridge, on November 4, 1831 [4].
*   **Contradiction regarding Birth Year:** The texts contain conflicting details regarding his age. Several sources record his life as spanning **"c. 1812–1877"** [5, 6]. However, another source specifically states that in **1830**, Edward began working as a curate to his father **"at the age of twenty-seven,"** which would place his birth around **1803** [2].

**Marriages, Family, and Tragedy**
*   **First Marriage and Children:** He married Elizabeth Hoddson in 1830 [2]. Together they had three children: Mary (born 1831), Henry Dawson Ellis Bull (born 1833), and Winifred (born 1834) [2]. 
*   **Tragedies (1843-1844):** The family was struck by intense grief when nine-year-old Winifred died in 1843, followed by Elizabeth’s death from breast cancer the following year [2].
*   **The Nottingham Tragedy (1848):** Just before Easter 1848, the widowed Edward temporarily moved with his surviving children to Snetterton in Nottingham, sharing a rectory with a 26-year-old curate, William Brown [2]. Brown became intensely infatuated with Edward's 17-year-old daughter, Mary. When Brown asked Edward for Mary's hand in marriage on November 9, Edward discussed it with his daughter, who rejected the proposal [2]. Edward relayed her decision to the curate, who subsequently went to the ruins of Nottingham Castle and killed himself by shooting himself in the head [2]. Following this **"shocking occurrence,"** Edward and his family returned to Pentlow [2].
*   **Second Marriage:** In 1851, Edward married his second wife, Cornelia, who gave birth to his youngest son, Felix Edward Pepys Bull [2, 7].

**Clerical Career**
*   He succeeded his father as Rector of Pentlow in **1834**, holding the position until his death in **1877** [4, 6]. 
*   In **1861**, he formally procured the living of Borley and Pentlow [5]. As a result, the next two Rectors of Borley were his son (Henry) and his grandson (Harry), while his other son (Felix) succeeded him as Rector of Pentlow [4, 6, 7]. 

**Bull’s Tower (Bull’s Folly)**
Edward Bull’s most famous physical legacy is a massive, eccentric brick structure he erected in the Pentlow Rectory garden, known today as **"Pentlow Tower"** or **"Bull's Folly"** [3, 6, 8]. 
*   **Purpose:** It was built specifically as a memorial to his parents, bearing a plaque reading: **"Erected to the memory of his honoured parents the Rev. John Bull, M.A. and Margaret his wife. On a spot they loved so well by Edw. Bull, M.A. 1859"** [1, 6, 9]. During the laying of the first brick, Edward gave an address with a **"feeling of allusion to his brother's labours to this parish and his own endeavours to follow in the same steps"** [10].
*   **Local Legends:** Due to the tower's unusual design and height, eccentric legends sprang up around it. Some villagers believed Edward built it so he could stand at the top and spy on farm labourers to ensure they didn't shirk, while others claimed he built it hoping to see the sea but was disappointed when he could not [8]. Furthermore, some locals allegedly **"sniggered that Reverend Bull’s phallic tower celebrated his father’s fertility more than it did his memory"** [3].
*   **Contradictions regarding the Tower:** The sources conflict regarding both the date of its construction and its exact height. Some sources date its building to **1858** [3, 8, 11], while others point to **1859**, providing local newspaper records from February and March 1859 detailing its construction [6, 10, 12]. Similarly, its height is varyingly described as being **"some sixty feet high"** [8], exactly **90 feet** [3, 12, 13], and **95 feet** [9]. 

**The Pig Incident**
Like his son Henry, Edward Bull had a reputation as an authoritarian and eccentric rural "squarson." An infamous local legend states that during a local shoot, while Edward was waiting for birds to fly overhead, an innocent pig happened to amble by [14]. Because there was no other action, Edward shot the pig [14]. In a humorous variant of the same story with **"an element of divine retribution in it,"** the pig allegedly bit the rector first [14].
