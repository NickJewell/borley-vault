# Francis Cowley Whitehouse

Brother of [[lady-whitehouse|Sir George Whitehouse]]; father of **Leslie Edwin Whitehouse**, who became [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard Whitehouse]] — the Benedictine monk and key Foyster-period witness at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

## Background

Francis Cowley Whitehouse served as **canon to the British Embassy in Istanbul** prior to the outbreak of the First World War [1]. It was during this posting that his son Leslie Edwin Whitehouse was born in Istanbul in 1899 — a fact that connects the Whitehouse family to the Ottoman world and explains Edwin's multilingual, cosmopolitan outlook. Francis married the daughter of J. W. Whittall, who headed a successful trading empire exporting mohair, hazelnuts, barley, maize, seeds, and opium from Turkey.

## Intervention in the Borley Case

Francis played no direct role in witnessing or investigating the phenomena at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]]. His only recorded connection to the events occurred when his son Edwin became obsessively involved with the hauntings and the Foyster household during 1931. Edwin suffered from fragile mental health (having previously experienced a severe nervous breakdown) and his aunt, Lady Florence Whitehouse, felt that his constant visits to Borley did him "no good at all" [2]. According to investigator [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]]'s later interviews with Lady Whitehouse, **Francis Cowley Whitehouse ultimately intervened and forbade his son from visiting the Rectory** [2]. Following his father's command in late 1931, Edwin left the district [2].

It was Francis's wife's connection with the Whittall family trading empire that gave Edwin his financial independence and his international background, and probably contributed to his psychological fragility — the shell shock, the broken romance, and the nervous breakdown that preceded his arrival at Borley.

## Significance

Francis Cowley Whitehouse is the biographical link between the prominent local family of [[lady-whitehouse|Sir George and Lady Whitehouse]] of [[arthur-hall-sudbury|Arthur Hall]] (key Foyster-period witnesses) and the monk [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard Whitehouse]] who made approximately thirty visits to the Rectory during the Foyster period and became one of its most important witnesses.
