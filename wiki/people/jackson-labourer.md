# Jackson

Local labourer engaged by the Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|A. C. Henning]] to assist with the August 1943 excavation of Borley Rectory's cellars. Jackson came equipped with picks, shovels, and other gear.

## Role in the Excavation

Jackson performed the physical labour of the three-day excavation — breaking up the hard, tenacious "blue vinney" (blue billy) clay marl beneath the cellar floor, which had been sealed under the original brick paving. He worked alongside Henning with picks and shovels while others sifted the removed earth.

### The Discovery

At 1:45 p.m. on August 17, 1943, Jackson handed up what he described as a jaw-bone from a pig. [[dr-eric-bailey|Dr. Eric Bailey]] immediately identified it as human. Five minutes later, Jackson shovelled up a fragment of skull from the same area — "no mistaking to what 'animal' this fragment belonged."

### The Round Well

Jackson also emptied the round bricked well in the cellar, standing in water up to his shoulders by the end, bailing it out with a large brass preserving-pan recovered from the same well. At five feet six inches depth he found the silver-plate cream-jug (Sheffield plate) that Price associated with the Planchette reference to a "cup." He eventually stood on the bottom of the well at approximately six feet depth and confirmed it was solid brick.

### Second and Third Days

Jackson worked steadily through August 17 and 18, with a third visit on August 30. Price notes he and [[rev-ac-henning]] "had been working like niggers" — Price's language of the era. Jackson was left to clear up the mess after the initial visits.

## Significance

Jackson's identification of the first bone as "pig" is evidentially important: it means the first person to see the bones did not recognise them as human, making Price's team's methodological point that they were not searching for (or expecting) human remains and that the discovery was not engineered. [[mrs-alex-english|Mrs English]]'s real-time notes record both Jackson's initial identification and Dr Bailey's immediate correction.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mr. Jackson** was a local Borley farm worker and labourer who played a central, physical role in the famous 1943 excavations of the ruined Borley Rectory cellars. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, events, quotes, and a major controversy surrounding his involvement:

**Biographical Details and Hiring**
Described by the Rev. Alfred Henning's wife as "old Jackson" and noted by Harry Price's secretary as "slow but steady," Jackson lived in one of the cottages opposite Liston Church [1-4]. Because of severe wartime labor shortages, Rev. Henning was only able to secure Jackson's services to assist with the digging "after much effort" [3]. He is officially recorded as participating in all three days of Harry Price's excavations on August 17, 18, and 30, 1943, and was photographed assisting the Rector [2, 5, 6]. 

**The Round Well and a Suffolk Superstition**
On the morning of August 17, 1943, armed with picks and shovels, Jackson spent two and a half hours clearing debris from the cellar's "round well" [4, 7, 8]. Standing directly on top of the rubbish, he eventually hit the water-level at eighteen inches down, uncovering myriads of young frogs [8, 9]. 
*   **Lesser-Known Detail/Quote:** Upon finding the frogs, Jackson shared a piece of local folklore, recalling **"an old Suffolk superstition that if one puts a live frog on one’s navel it turns into a sovereign"** (though Price noted no one tried the experiment) [9]. 
*   As he dug deeper, he handed up a large antique brass preserving-pan and broken glass [9]. Eventually, Jackson reached the solid brick bottom at a depth of about six feet, at which point his head had almost disappeared and the team had to haul him out [10].

**The Discovery of the Bones and the "Pig" Contradiction**
Jackson is the man who physically unearthed the famous Borley bones, an event that sparked one of the most significant contradictions and controversies in the case's history. 

After clearing the well, Jackson took a pickaxe to break up the brick floor of the cellar passage [4, 11]. At 1:45 p.m., he pulled a jawbone out of the blue clay [4, 12, 13]. 
*   **The Contradiction:** When Jackson handed the bone up, he explicitly remarked that it came from a pig [12]. However, Dr. Eric Bailey, a pathologist present at the dig, immediately identified it as a human mandible [12]. A few minutes later, Jackson unearthed a fragment of a skull, which was also declared human [12, 14].
*   **The Aftermath:** Jackson was "ribbed" quite a bit by his colleagues for supposedly mistaking a human jawbone for a pig's [15]. However, Jackson **maintained all his life that what he picked out of the ground was from a pig** [15]. Local historians note that for an experienced farm worker like Jackson, mistaking pig bones for human bones would have been a highly elementary error [15]. 
*   **Skeptical Theories:** Because Jackson was so certain of his find, and because the Borley locals generally shared his belief that the bones were animal in origin, skeptics later formulated the theory that Harry Price used his skills as an amateur conjuror to execute a sleight-of-hand trick, confidently taking the animal bone from Jackson and substituting it with a human jawbone before passing it to the pathologist [16, 17].
