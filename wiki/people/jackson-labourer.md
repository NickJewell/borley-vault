# Jackson

Local Borley farm worker and labourer who played a central, physical role in the famous 1943 excavations of the ruined [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] cellars. He lived in one of the cottages opposite [[liston-rectory|Liston Church]] [1–4]. Engaged by the Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|A. C. Henning]] to assist with the August 1943 excavation. Described by Rev. Henning's wife as "old Jackson" and noted by [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s secretary as "slow but steady." Due to severe wartime labour shortages, Henning secured Jackson's services only **"after much effort"** [3].

## Role in the Excavation

Jackson performed the physical labour of the three-day excavation — breaking up the hard, tenacious "blue vinney" (blue billy) clay marl beneath the cellar floor, which had been sealed under the original brick paving. He worked alongside Henning with picks and shovels while others sifted the removed earth. He is officially recorded as participating in all three excavation days: **August 17, 18, and 30, 1943**, and was photographed assisting the Rector [2, 5, 6].

### The Round Well

On the morning of August 17, 1943, armed with picks and shovels, Jackson spent two and a half hours clearing debris from the cellar's "round well" [4, 7, 8]. Standing directly on top of the rubbish, he eventually hit the water-level at eighteen inches down, uncovering myriads of young frogs [8, 9]. Upon finding the frogs, Jackson shared a piece of local folklore, recalling **"an old Suffolk superstition that if one puts a live frog on one's navel it turns into a sovereign"** (Price noted no one tried the experiment) [9]. As he dug deeper, he handed up a large antique brass preserving-pan and broken glass [9]. Eventually, Jackson reached the solid brick bottom at a depth of about six feet and at five feet six inches depth found the silver-plate cream-jug (Sheffield plate) that Price associated with the Planchette reference to a "cup." His head had almost disappeared by the time he stood on the solid bottom.

### The Discovery of the Bones

After clearing the well, Jackson took a pickaxe to break up the brick floor of the cellar passage [4, 11]. At **1:45 p.m.** on August 17, he pulled a jawbone out of the blue clay [4, 12, 13].

When Jackson handed the bone up, he explicitly remarked that it came from **a pig** [12]. However, [[dr-eric-bailey|Dr. Eric Bailey]], a pathologist present at the dig, immediately identified it as a human mandible [12]. A few minutes later, Jackson unearthed a fragment of a skull, which was also declared human [12, 14]. Jackson was subsequently "ribbed" quite a bit by his colleagues for supposedly mistaking a human jawbone for a pig's — but he **maintained all his life that what he picked out of the ground was from a pig** [15]. Local historians note that for an experienced farm worker, mistaking pig bones for human bones would have been a highly elementary error [15].

### Second and Third Days

Jackson worked steadily through August 17 and 18, with a third visit on August 30. Price notes he and Henning "had been working like niggers" — Price's language of the era. Jackson was left to clear up the mess after the initial visits.

## Significance

Jackson's identification of the first bone as "pig" is evidentially important: it means the first person to see the bones did not recognise them as human, supporting the claim that the discovery was not engineered. [[alex-english|Mrs English]]'s real-time notes record both Jackson's initial identification and Dr Bailey's immediate correction.

> **Contradiction:** Because Jackson was so certain of his find, and because the Borley locals generally shared his belief that the bones were animal in origin, sceptics later formulated the theory that [[harry-price|Harry Price]] used his skills as an amateur conjuror to execute a sleight-of-hand trick — confidently taking the animal bone from Jackson and substituting it with a human jawbone before passing it to the pathologist [16, 17].
