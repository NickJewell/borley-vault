# Gilbert Hayes

Film comedian; appeared in approximately 350 films; had travelled 75,000 miles in two years; had adventured widely including seal-hunting in a ninety-ton schooner. His own description: "the strangest things in my life" were his September 1939 visit to Borley.

## The Tea Garden Adventure

In September 1939, Hayes and his wife answered an advertisement in *Dalton's Weekly* for a property near Sudbury suitable for conversion to tea-gardens. The address was given as "Borley Priory" — Hayes had never heard of the haunting and did not know who [[harry-price|Harry Price]] was.

Walking the grounds, Hayes heard footsteps following him continuously — through the lean-to greenhouse, round the house, and up to the well. He assumed it was his wife. He spoke to her over his shoulder throughout, explaining the property's potential. The footsteps stopped at the well. When he turned to look, no one was there. He then discovered his wife had never been with him at all — she had gone to fetch the key and only joined him at the well.

Upstairs in the cottage (the rooms over the stables where [[edward-cooper|Edward Cooper]] had lived), Hayes found some grocery receipts in a paper hold-all, which he folded and stuffed in. He walked fifteen paces. When he turned back, the bills had been relocated two feet to a shelf. His wife, who had been elsewhere, confirmed the bills had been moved. Meanwhile, his wife had heard someone moving upstairs while she was there — which proved to be no one, as Hayes was outside.

When told the name "Borley Priory," a postman at Sudbury station identified it at once as "the most haunted house in all England" and described the psychical research investigations.

Hayes did not rent the property. Writing in his letter of August 8, 1943: "I don't know Harry Price; he doesn't know me; but our experiences are facts."

> **Note:** Price identifies Hayes's cottage experience as analogous to the haunting of the [[rectory-cottage|Rectory Cottage]] which [[edward-cooper|Cooper]] and [[james-turner|Turner]] also experienced in the same rooms.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Gilbert Hayes** was a well-known film comedian whose chance encounter with the Borley site provided Harry Price with an important piece of independent testimony shortly after the 1939 Rectory fire. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, events, and specific quotes regarding his involvement:

**Biographical Details**
Beyond his career as a film comedian who had appeared in some 350 films, Hayes was described by Harry Price as a highly adventurous **"man of the world"** [1]. His travels included covering 75,000 miles in two years and partaking in seal-hunting expeditions aboard a ninety-ton schooner [1]. During the Second World War, he resided at Rose Cottage, Bastanford Powick, Worcester [1].

**The September 1939 Visit**
With the outbreak of the war in **September 1939**, Hayes and his wife sought a quiet rural retreat where they could rest and run a tea-garden [2, 3]. Hayes answered an advertisement in *Dalton's Weekly* that gave no specific location but offered a property named "Borley Priory" (the new name Captain W.H. Gregson had given the estate) [4, 5]. 

Harry Price heavily emphasized Hayes's value as an unbiased witness because of several key facts: **Hayes had no interest in psychical research, had never heard of Borley Rectory or Harry Price, and visited the site before Price's first book on the hauntings was even published** [6, 7].

**Paranormal Experiences in the Rectory Cottage**
While exploring the Rectory Cottage and the grounds, Hayes experienced several inexplicable events [7, 8]:
*   **Footsteps and Voices:** He reported hearing the distinct noise of a door closing and the sound of footsteps following him [7]. He noted that the footsteps followed him through a greenhouse, around the house, and finally left him near the site's well [9]. Additionally, while in a room and on the landing, he heard what he mistakenly thought was his wife speaking to him [9].
*   **The Grocery Bills:** In one room, Hayes found some grocery bills and joked about them [9]. He folded the papers, placed them inside a hold-all, and walked fifteen paces away [9]. Within that brief time, the papers were mysteriously extracted and placed back on the shelf, having moved a distance of two feet [7, 9]. 

Unsurprisingly, Hayes chose not to rent the property for his tea-garden [8]. 

**Correspondence with Harry Price and Quotes**
Hayes only learned of the site's haunted history years later when he read an article Harry Price published in *Everybody's Weekly* in August 1943 [7, 10]. On **August 8, 1943**, Hayes wrote a letter to Price, detailing his experiences and stating emphatically that **"the events at Borley were the strangest in my life"** [1]. 

In his letter, he challenged skeptics with four rhetorical questions about who could have followed him and moved the bills, concluding with the definitive quote:
> **"I have related this adventure many times to various people ever since it happened. I don’t know Harry Price; he doesn’t know me; but our experiences, as related here, are facts."** [9]

Price utilized Hayes's letter in his 1946 book, *The End of Borley Rectory*, noting that the mysterious footsteps Hayes heard in the cottage were a fascinating "flashback" to the "padding dog" phenomena experienced by the Cooper family in those exact same rooms between 1916 and 1919 [8].
