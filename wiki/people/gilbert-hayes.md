# Gilbert Hayes

Film comedian; appeared in approximately 350 films; covered 75,000 miles in two years; adventured widely including seal-hunting in a ninety-ton schooner. His own description: "the strangest things in my life" were his September 1939 visit to Borley [1]. During the Second World War, he resided at Rose Cottage, Bastanford Powick, Worcester [1].

[[harry-price|Harry Price]] heavily emphasised Hayes's value as an unbiased witness because **Hayes had no interest in psychical research, had never heard of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] or Harry Price, and visited the site before Price's first book on the hauntings was even published** [6, 7].

## The September 1939 Visit

With the outbreak of war in **September 1939**, Hayes and his wife sought a quiet rural retreat where they could rest and run a tea-garden [2, 3]. Hayes answered an advertisement in *Dalton's Weekly* for a property named "Borley Priory" — the new name [[captain-gregson|Captain W.H. Gregson]] had given the estate — having never heard of the haunting and not knowing who Harry Price was [4, 5].

Walking the grounds with his wife, Hayes heard footsteps following him continuously — through the lean-to greenhouse, round the house, and up to the well. He assumed it was his wife and spoke to her over his shoulder throughout, explaining the property's potential. The footsteps stopped at the well. When he turned to look, no one was there. He then discovered his wife had never been with him at all — she had gone to fetch the key and only joined him at the well [9].

Additionally, upstairs in the cottage (the rooms over the stables where [[edward-cooper|Edward Cooper]] had lived), Hayes found some grocery receipts in a paper hold-all, which he folded and stuffed in. He walked fifteen paces. When he turned back, the bills had been relocated two feet to a shelf. His wife, who had been elsewhere, confirmed the bills had been moved. Meanwhile, his wife had heard someone moving upstairs while she was there — which proved to be no one, as Hayes was outside [7, 9].

When told the name "Borley Priory," a postman at Sudbury station identified it at once as "the most haunted house in all England" and described the psychical research investigations.

Unsurprisingly, Hayes chose not to rent the property for his tea-garden [8].

> **Note:** Price identifies Hayes's cottage experience as analogous to the haunting of the [[rectory-cottage|Rectory Cottage]] which [[edward-cooper|Cooper]] and [[james-turner|Turner]] also experienced in the same rooms — specifically the "padding dog" phenomena experienced by the Cooper family between 1916 and 1919 [8].

## Correspondence with Harry Price

Hayes only learned of the site's haunted history years later when he read an article Harry Price published in *Everybody's Weekly* in August 1943 [7, 10]. On **August 8, 1943**, Hayes wrote a letter to Price, stating emphatically that **"the events at Borley were the strangest in his life"** [1]. In his letter, he challenged sceptics and concluded:

> **"I have related this adventure many times to various people ever since it happened. I don't know Harry Price; he doesn't know me; but our experiences, as related here, are facts."** [9]
