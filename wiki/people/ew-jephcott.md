# E. W. Jephcott

A resident of Alcester, Warwickshire, who wrote to the [[rev-ac-henning|Rev Alfred Clifford Henning]] in late October 1947 after reading a report in *The East Anglian Daily Times* about the sound of organ music heard in the empty and locked [[borley-church|Borley Church]].

## The Letter

Jephcott's letter, reproduced in Henning's booklet *Haunted Borley* and discussed by Babbs and Mathias, states that on the afternoon of Tuesday 16 September 1947, he and a friend visited Borley to see the site of the former rectory. After looking at the ruins, they walked across to the church. As they approached along the pathway, his friend said, "the organ's playing." Jephcott himself could hear no music, but his friend's impression was sufficiently strong that the friend wondered whether a funeral service was in progress and whether they should not enter. On going in, the church was empty and the organ closed.

Jephcott wrote:

> "You will see from this how remarkably similar this was to Mrs Walrond's experience. I should be much interested to know whether you yourself heard the music and whether any definite tune was apparent. If the playing should recur, it might be possible to take note of the melody and get it identified."

He reported returning to Borley on 19 September but having no paranormal experience.

## Context

The Jephcott letter provides independent corroboration of the organ phenomenon at Borley Church reported in the same period by Henning and [[norah-walrond|Mrs Walrond (Norah Burke)]] in October 1947, and later by [[thomas-musk|Thomas Musk]] in 1987. In each case, the church was confirmed to be empty and the instrument locked. The experience was one-sided on this occasion — only Jephcott's unnamed companion heard the music while Jephcott himself did not.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**E. W. Jephcott** was a resident of Alcester, Warwickshire, who became a secondary but corroborating witness to one of the later phenomena associated with Borley Church: the phantom organ music. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, dates, and quotes regarding his involvement:

**The Phantom Organ Music (September 16, 1947)**
On the afternoon of **Tuesday, September 16, 1947**, at approximately 3:30 p.m., Jephcott and a friend visited the site of the ruined Borley Rectory [1]. After inspecting the ruins, the two men walked across the road to Borley Church [1]. As they were walking up the church pathway, Jephcott's companion suddenly announced, "the organ’s playing" [1]. 

A specific point of interest in this account is that **Jephcott himself could hear absolutely no music** [1]. Despite his companion's claim, when the two men entered the church, they found it completely empty and the organ closed [1, 2]. 

Jephcott's friend maintained that his impression of the organ music was so strong and distinct that he had actually hesitated to enter the building, assuming a funeral service was in progress [1]. Jephcott later confirmed that his companion had not read Harry Price’s book and had no prior knowledge of the local legends claiming that ghostly organ music and singing were frequently heard issuing from the empty church [1]. 

**A Second Visit (September 19, 1947)**
Three days later, on **September 19, 1947**, Jephcott returned to Borley [3, 4]. However, this follow-up visit was uneventful; as he later recorded, he **"had no paranormal experience"** on this second occasion [3, 4].

**The Letter to Reverend Henning (October 1947)**
Jephcott’s experience remained a private anecdote until he read an article in *The East Anglian Daily Times* published on **October 24, 1947** [5]. The article detailed a nearly identical experience had by the Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning and novelist Mrs. Norah Walrond, who also heard phantom organ music while walking up the same church path in October of that year [5, 6]. 

Prompted by this, Jephcott wrote a letter to the Rev. Henning a few days later, providing a detailed account of his September 16th visit [5]. He noted that Henning and Walrond's encounter provided **"astonishing confirmation"** of what had happened to his friend [5]. Displaying an analytical interest in the phenomenon, Jephcott concluded his letter with a practical suggestion for the Rector: 
> **"I should be much interested to know whether you yourself heard the music and whether any definite tune was apparent. If the playing should recur, it might be possible to take note of the melody and get it identified"** [1, 4].
