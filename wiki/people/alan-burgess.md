# Alan Burgess

BBC. Visited the Borley Rectory site in June 1947 with BBC colleague [[peter-eton|Peter Eton]], two days before the BBC broadcast "The Haunted Rectory" (29 June 1947). Burgess stood on the opposite side of the ruined cellars from Eton while sharp raps were heard, apparently from the cellars beneath them. The raps responded to Eton's requests to stop and start. A recording was included in the broadcast.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Alan Burgess** was a BBC writer and editor who co-authored the 1947 radio documentary covering the Borley hauntings [1, 2]. Working alongside BBC producer Peter Eton, Burgess edited and helped script a 45-minute program titled **"The Haunted Rectory"**, which aired on the BBC Home Service on June 29, 1947, at 9:30 p.m. as "No. 10 in the True Escape Stories" series [1, 2]. 

**His Approach to the Investigation**
Burgess edited the program by drawing upon material from Harry Price’s two books, *The Most Haunted House in England* and *The End of Borley Rectory*, supplementing this foundation with freshly recorded interviews from principal witnesses [3]. In the broadcast, Burgess outlined their objective stance, stating: **"We were two normal, sceptical young men with no axes to grind. If we interviewed and recorded all the principle witnesses would that solve anything? Supposing we presented the evidence as impartially as we could—the people who believed the story—the people who said it was nonsense. Would that solve the mystery? For no doubt about it, it was a strange story"** [4].

**The May 1947 Paranormal Experience**
Despite his self-described skepticism, Burgess experienced an unexplained phenomenon at Borley while gathering material for the show. He and Peter Eton visited the Rectory site on the evening of May 15, 1947 (two days before investigators Peter Underwood and Tom Brown arrived) [5, 6]. 

While Peter Eton stood on a concrete slab on one side of the ruined cellars and Burgess stood facing him on the opposite side, the two men heard a series of short, sharp raps [5, 6]. Even though this occurred in full daylight, when Eton spoke to the raps and asked them to stop and then recommence, the mysterious sounds obeyed his commands [6]. A reproduction of these responsive rapping sounds was subsequently included in their radio broadcast [6].
