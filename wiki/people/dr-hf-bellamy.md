# Dr. H. F. Bellamy

Psychical researcher and one of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s collaborators during the 1937–38 Borley tenancy. Bellamy participated in several important séances and is notable for introducing his own Planchette board to one of the sessions.

## Séance Participation

Bellamy took part in two séances specifically documented in the [[locked-book|Locked Book]]:

**November 20–21, 1937 (Borley Rectory):** Bellamy sat alongside [[capt-hg-harrison|Captain H. G. Harrison]], [[sidney-glanville|S. H. Glanville]], and [[roger-glanville|R. H. Glanville]] at a night séance at the Rectory itself. Notably, at this session **two Planchette boards were used successively**, as Bellamy brought his own — Price remarks "it made no difference, apparently." Some of the answers at this séance were written in "mirror-writing" — backward — which Price notes as "not at all an easy way to write." The session confirmed that the dining-room window was bricked up on account of the nun, and that "Marie Lairre" was responsible for the lighted schoolroom window.

**December 14, 1937 (Lancaster Gate, London W.2):** This séance was hosted at Bellamy's own home, attended by Bellamy and his wife, [[sidney-glanville|S. H. Glanville]], and [[roger-glanville|Roger Glanville]]. The "Joseph Glanvill" entity communicated and gave the specific instruction to **"look under the brick floor in the cellar"** at Borley — directly predicting the 1943 excavation — specifying "Well" as the location and writing the letters "P-E-N," later interpreted as referring to the *pendants* found there. This was one of the most specific and later-verified predictions in the séance record.

**January 12–13, 1938 (Borley Rectory):** Bellamy also attended a night séance at the Rectory with the Rev. and Mrs [[rev-eric-smith|G. Eric Smith]] and the Glanvilles. A "Roman centurion" entity communicated, and the "Harry Bull" entity was questioned about the origin of the skull found by Mrs Smith in the library cupboard.

## Significance

Bellamy's hosting of the December 14, 1937 séance places him at a session that produced the most specific directional instruction in the entire Planchette record — the instruction to search under the cellar floor, near the well, at Borley. That instruction was not acted upon until 1943, and by that point the scripts that produced it had been locked away unread for five years.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Dr. Henry F. Bellamy** was an individual engaged in medical research who acted as one of Harry Price's "official observers" during the year-long tenancy of Borley Rectory from 1937 to 1938 [1]. He frequently investigated the site alongside his son, Francis C. Bellamy, and was considered by Price's chief associate, Sidney Glanville, to be a highly reliable witness [1, 2].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following events, experiments, and controversies regarding his involvement:

**The Electrical Contact-Breaker Experiment (October 1937)**
Dr. Bellamy is most famous in the Borley annals for a "Harry Price type" electrical experiment he rigged up during a vigil on the night of **October 16–17, 1937**, accompanied by his son and Mr. Hugh E. Fee-Smith [3, 4]. Dr. Bellamy placed a simple electric bell contact-breaker on the dining-room mantelpiece, holding the metal contacts apart with a pile of books; if the books were displaced, the circuit would close and ring the bell [1]. At 12:50 a.m., while the party was quietly watching the moonlight in the upstairs Blue Room, the bell suddenly started ringing and continued for about a minute before abruptly stopping [5]. Upon rushing to the dining room, they discovered that **every book had been displaced from its original position**, and the bottom book was pushed completely off the contact-breaker [5]. 

**Contradictions and the "Phantom Cat"**
This striking incident became a major point of contention between believers and critics. In the highly skeptical *Borley Report*, investigators Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall pointed out a crucial detail found in the original observer logs: **a cat had been found to have entered the house the evening before** the disturbance [6]. The critics argued the cat could easily have knocked over the books, noting that the door to the dining room had been left unlocked [6]. Furthermore, Harry Price suspiciously **omitted all mention of the cat** when he published the account in his book *The Most Haunted House in England* [6]. However, Dr. Bellamy and his son **"rejected totally this explanation,"** maintaining that the window was sealed, the house regularly patrolled, and that they had tested the delicate apparatus at 6:00 a.m. and found it in perfect working order [4, 5, 7].

**Other Rectory Vigils and Paranormal Experiences**
Dr. Bellamy was present for several other notable incidents at Borley:
*   **The Swinging Blind Incident (November 20–21, 1937):** While sitting with Sidney Glanville and Captain H. G. Harrison, the men observed the edges of a lowered blind waving with an irregular, palpitating action for about five minutes. They unanimously concluded there was no draft to account for it, implying a paranormal origin [2, 8]. 
*   **Tactile Phenomena and Wall Writings (May 26–27, 1938):** During his final visit with his son and Fee-Smith, Dr. Bellamy helped locate a fresh pencil line in Room No. 7. During this same visit, his companion Fee-Smith reported the distinct sensation of unseen hands lightly ruffling his hair and touching his face in the dark near the garden door [9, 10].
*   He also participated in uneventful visits, such as one on December 3–4, 1937, while house painters were on-site [11], and another on January 12–13, 1938, alongside the former rector, the Rev. Guy E. Smith, during which they conducted a fruitless planchette experiment [12, 13].

**Hosting the Lancaster Gate Séance**
Dr. Bellamy’s involvement extended beyond the Rectory grounds. On **December 14, 1937**, he hosted a planchette séance at his own home in Lancaster Gate, London [14, 15]. Alongside his wife, Sidney Glanville, and Roger Glanville, the sitters allegedly made contact with the spirit of "Joseph Glanvill" (a 17th-century philosopher) [14, 15]. When asked to provide proof of the afterlife, the entity instructed the investigators to **"look under the brick floor in the cellar" at Borley**, promising they would find "writing" and a "missal", and explicitly spelling out the letters **"P-E-N"** [14, 16]. This séance directly influenced Harry Price's decision to excavate the Borley cellars, where bone fragments and two French medallions ("pendants") were ultimately discovered [15].
