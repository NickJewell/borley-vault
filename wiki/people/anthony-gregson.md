# Anthony Hart Gregson

Older son of [[Captain Gregson|Captain W.H. Gregson]] (last owner of Borley Rectory). Located by [[Richard Lee van den Daele]] of Shipley, West Yorkshire, in Canada (Cultus Lake, British Columbia). Provided the most damning account of his father's role in the 1939 fire.

## Statement on the Fire

In a letter to van den Daele (published in Banks's *Enigma* 1994), Anthony Hart Gregson stated:

> "My father Capt. (Retd.) W. H. Gregson bought Borley Rectory as a real estate venture around 1937 at which time it was classified by a prominent spiritualist, Harry Price, as the most haunted house in England. When this didn't pan out, he torched it for the insurance."

This directly contradicts his brother [[alan-gregson|Allan Gregson]]'s account that the fire was accidental (books falling on an oil lamp).

## Family Background

According to Anthony, Captain Gregson came from Rusland Valley in the north of England (father was a local preacher); was living at Maldon, Essex, when he purchased the Rectory in December 1938; became involved with Sir Oswald Mosley's British Union of Fascists as an area organiser in the Maldon district; later became a garrison engineer with the Royal Engineers at Southend-on-Sea.

After the Borley episode (1943), the Captain and Allan departed for Tasmania. Anthony himself stowed away to Canada, eventually coming to Discovery Mine, Northwest Territories, where (by his own account) he substituted lead for gold in a gold shipment in 1957 and was arrested. He received a two-year prison sentence.

Anthony describes his father as an "upright citizen" in the context of joining the BUF — the irony of this phrase is noted by Banks.

## Note on Reliability

Banks notes that the two sons give fundamentally contradictory accounts: Anthony says deliberate arson; Allan says accidental. Banks's conclusion is that both have probably told the truth as they experienced it — Allan was sent away before the fire was established, while Anthony knew his father's real intentions.

The [[brfa|Final Analysis]] book (Babbs & Mathias) cited an "Alan Gregson" letter from Australia stating the fire was accidental and the purchase price £350; Banks's chapter on the fire cites £500. These details may reflect the different sons' letters or different timeframes for the same correspondence.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Anthony Hart Gregson** (who is contradictorily identified as **Andrew Gregson**, aged 14, in one source [1]) was the younger son of Captain William Hart Gregson, the final owner of Borley Rectory [1, 2]. He moved to Borley on December 16, 1938, alongside his father and older brother, Alan, initially taking up residence in the stable cottage rather than the main house [2, 3]. He is listed as one of the official witnesses to the "unidentified footprints in the snow" found at the Rectory [4]. 

**The Arson Accusation Against His Father**
Anthony's most notable contribution to the Borley literature is his blunt accusation that his father deliberately burned down the Rectory. After fading from the scene for some years, Anthony was tracked down by researcher Richard Lee-Van den Daele while living at **Cultus Lake, British Columbia** [5]. In a letter to the researcher, Anthony portrayed his father as a "man of straw" and explicitly accused him of arson and insurance fraud [5]. He summarized the events with the following quote: 
**"My father Capt. (Retd.) W. H. Gregson bought Borley Rectory as a real estate venture around 1937 at which time it was classified by a prominent spiritualist, Harry Price, as the most haunted house in England. When this didn’t pan out, he torched it for the insurance"** [6]. 

This dramatic claim is heavily contradicted by his brother, Alan, who maintained the February 1939 fire was an accident caused by an overturned oil lamp. Alan dismissed Anthony's accusations entirely, claiming his younger brother suffered from **"overexposure to James Cagney"** [7, 8].

**Military Service**
During the Second World War, the brothers went their separate ways into the armed forces. Anthony served in Europe, while harbouring apparent resentment toward his brother's assignment, complaining that Alan had secured an "easy billet" in East Africa [6].

**The Canadian Bullion Theft Contradiction**
As noted in our previous discussion regarding Alan Gregson, the sources present a major biographical contradiction regarding which of the two brothers committed a spectacular gold heist. One detailed account explicitly states that it was **Anthony** who stowed away on a ship to Canada, eventually arriving at the **Discovery Mine, a gold camp in the Northwest Territories** [9]. According to this version, Anthony stole a portion of a gold shipment by **substituting lead for the gold bars** [9]. He was reportedly apprehended in Tasmania in 1957—the same year his father died—and was sent back to Yellowknife, Canada, to serve a two-year prison sentence [9, 10]. However, another passage in the texts attributes this exact crime, apprehension in Tasmania, and Canadian prison sentence to his brother, Alan [11].
