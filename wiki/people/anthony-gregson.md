# Anthony Hart Gregson

Younger son of [[captain-gregson|Captain W.H. Gregson]] (last owner of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]]). Located by [[richard-lee-van-den-daele|Richard Lee-Van den Daele]] of Shipley, West Yorkshire, in Canada (Cultus Lake, British Columbia) [5]. Provided the most damning account of his father's role in the [[fire-1939|1939 fire]].

> **Contradiction:** One source identifies Anthony as "Andrew Gregson," aged 14, at the time of the move to Borley [1]. He is also listed as one of the official witnesses to "unidentified footprints in the snow" found at the Rectory [4].

## Arrival at Borley

Anthony moved to Borley on **December 16, 1938**, alongside his father and older brother, [[alan-gregson|Alan]], initially taking up residence in the stable cottage rather than the main house [2, 3].

## Statement on the Fire

In a letter to Lee-Van den Daele (published in Banks's *Enigma* 1994), Anthony Hart Gregson stated:

> "My father Capt. (Retd.) W. H. Gregson bought Borley Rectory as a real estate venture around 1937 at which time it was classified by a prominent spiritualist, Harry Price, as the most haunted house in England. When this didn't pan out, he torched it for the insurance."

This directly contradicts his brother [[alan-gregson|Allan Gregson]]'s account that the fire was accidental (books falling on an oil lamp). Alan dismissed Anthony's accusations entirely, claiming his younger brother suffered from "overexposure to James Cagney" [7, 8].

Banks notes that the two sons give fundamentally contradictory accounts: Anthony says deliberate arson; Alan says accidental. Banks's conclusion is that both have probably told the truth as they experienced it — Alan was sent away before the fire was established, while Anthony knew his father's real intentions.

## Family Background

According to Anthony, Captain Gregson came from Rusland Valley in the north of England; was living at Maldon, Essex, when he purchased the Rectory in December 1938; became involved with Sir Oswald Mosley's British Union of Fascists as an area organiser in the Maldon district; later became a garrison engineer with the Royal Engineers at Southend-on-Sea.

Anthony describes his father as an "upright citizen" in the context of joining the BUF — the irony of this phrase is noted by Banks.

## Military Service

During the Second World War, the brothers went their separate ways into the armed forces. Anthony served in Europe, harbouring apparent resentment toward his brother's assignment, complaining that Alan had secured an "easy billet" in East Africa [6].

## The Canadian Bullion Theft

> **Contradiction:** The sources present a major ambiguity regarding which of the Gregson brothers committed a spectacular gold heist. One detailed account explicitly states that it was **Anthony** who stowed away on a ship to Canada, eventually arriving at the **Discovery Mine, a gold camp in the Northwest Territories** [9]. According to this version, Anthony stole a portion of a gold shipment by substituting lead for the gold bars, was apprehended in Tasmania in 1957 — the same year his father died — and sent back to Yellowknife, Canada, to serve a two-year prison sentence [9, 10]. However, another passage attributes this exact crime, apprehension in Tasmania, and Canadian prison sentence to his brother, [[alan-gregson|Alan]] [11].
