# Anthony Hart Gregson

Older son of [[Captain Gregson|Captain W.H. Gregson]] (last owner of Borley Rectory). Located by [[Richard Lee van den Daele]] of Shipley, West Yorkshire, in Canada (Cultus Lake, British Columbia). Provided the most damning account of his father's role in the 1939 fire.

## Statement on the Fire

In a letter to van den Daele (published in Banks's *Enigma* 1994), Anthony Hart Gregson stated:

> "My father Capt. (Retd.) W. H. Gregson bought Borley Rectory as a real estate venture around 1937 at which time it was classified by a prominent spiritualist, Harry Price, as the most haunted house in England. When this didn't pan out, he torched it for the insurance."

This directly contradicts his brother [[alan-gregson|Allan Gregson]]'s account that the fire was accidental (books falling on an oil lamp).

## Family Background

According to Anthony, Captain Gregson came from Rusland Valley in the north of England (father was a local preacher); was living at Maldon, Essex, when he purchased the Rectory in December 1938; became involved with Sir Oswald Mosley's British Union of Fascists as an area organiser in the Maldon district; later became a garrison engineer with the Royal Engineers at Southend-on-Sea.

After the Borley episode (1943), the Captain and Allan departed for Tasmania. Anthony himself stowed away to Canada, eventually coming to Discovery Mine, Northwest Territories, where (by his own account) he substituted lead for gold in a gold shipment in 1957 and was arrested. He received a two-year prison sentence.

Anthony describes his father as an "upright citizen" in the context of joining the BUF — the irony of this phrase is noted by Banks.

## Note on Reliability

Banks notes that the two sons give fundamentally contradictory accounts: Anthony says deliberate arson; Allan says accidental. Banks's conclusion is that both have probably told the truth as they experienced it — Allan was sent away before the fire was established, while Anthony knew his father's real intentions.

The [[brfa|Final Analysis]] book (Babbs & Mathias) cited an "Alan Gregson" letter from Australia stating the fire was accidental and the purchase price £350; Banks's chapter on the fire cites £500. These details may reflect the different sons' letters or different timeframes for the same correspondence.
