# Kitty Bull

Widow of [[felix-bull|Rev. Felix Bull]], rector of [[pentlow|Pentlow]]; she hosted [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]] at Pentlow Rectory while repairs were carried out on [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in the autumn of 1930.

## Role

When the Foysters decided to move into Borley Rectory in the summer of 1930, work on the house was needed before it was habitable. While [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] visited her grandmother in Ireland, Kitty Bull suggested that Lionel stay with her at Pentlow Rectory. He did so, using the opportunity to prepare for his induction as the new rector.

During his stay at Pentlow, Lionel scandalised local gentry by hitching lifts in farm carts to Sudbury — entirely normal behaviour in rural Canada but decidedly un-English for a clergyman.

## Background

As [[felix-bull|Felix Bull]]'s widow, Kitty was part of the extended Bull family network centred on [[chilton-lodge|Chilton Lodge]] in Great Cornard. Felix had been rector of Pentlow — the same parish where the whole Bull clerical dynasty had its roots (see [[rev-edward-bull-pentlow|Rev. Edward Bull]]).

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Ethel May Bull**, known affectionately within her family as **"Kitty"**, was a central figure in the Borley Rectory saga and one of the primary original witnesses to its alleged hauntings. Born at Borley in 1868, she was the daughter of the Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull and Caroline Sarah Foyster [1], [2]. She lived an exceptionally long life, dying at the age of 93 on April 29, 1961, at Chilton Lodge in Great Cornard [1], [3]. 

As the acknowledged centre of the surviving Bull siblings, Ethel was a dominant, talkative personality (Harry Price’s secretary, Lucy Kaye, specifically noted her as "the talkative one" in her 1929 notes) who often acted as the family spokesperson regarding the ghosts [1], [4], [5].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following specific events, quotes, contradictions, and biographical details regarding her life and involvement in the Borley case:

**The 1900 Phantom Nun Sightings and Price's Embellishments**
Ethel was a primary witness to the most famous collective sighting of the Borley "phantom nun." On **July 28, 1900**, Ethel, returning from a garden party with her sisters Freda, Mabel, and Elsie, claimed to see a dark, nun-like figure gliding along the path known as the "Nun's Walk" [6], [7], [8], [9]. Ethel later recalled to investigator Peter Underwood that she was "scared stiff!" by the encounter [9]. A few months later, in November 1900, Ethel and the Rectory cook claimed to see the nun again, this time leaning over a gate [10], [11], [12]. 

However, Ethel’s testimony exposes a major contradiction in Harry Price’s later reporting. Price wrote in his book that the July 28 sighting occurred in "sunlight" and that the nun had an "expression of intense grief on her face" [13], [14]. Ethel strongly contradicted this to investigators, stating she was annoyed that Price had exaggerated her account for sensationalism without letting her approve the text [15], [16], [17]. She clarified that the sighting actually happened at twilight (around 9:00 p.m.), the light was poor, the figure was bowed over, and they could not see a face at all [18], [19], [20]. 

**Interior Poltergeist and Apparitional Experiences**
Beyond the nun, Ethel claimed to have experienced numerous terrifying phenomena inside the Rectory:
*   **The Tall, Dark Man:** In 1893 (or the early 1900s), Ethel awoke in the twilight to find a tall, dark man in old-fashioned clothes and a tall hat standing by her bed [21], [22], [23], [24]. She also reported feeling someone or something sitting on the edge of her bed [10], [21], [23]. 
*   **The Rapping Door:** She reported that her bedroom door was subjected to three distinct raps every night between 9:00 and 10:00 p.m. [25], [26].
*   **The Servant Bells:** Ethel claimed to be alone in the house on one occasion when all twenty of the Rectory's servant bells inexplicably began pealing simultaneously, followed by the sound of rushing water [27], [28]. 

**The Feud with Ivy Brackenbury and Murder Allegations**
Ethel harboured a bitter, lifelong hatred for her brother Harry Bull's widow, Ivy Brackenbury. When Harry married Ivy in 1911 and later moved her into the Rectory in 1920, Ethel and her unmarried sisters were exiled from their childhood home to Chilton Lodge [29], [30]. Ethel led a campaign of vilification against Ivy, describing her to investigator Sidney Glanville as a "gold digger" who made Harry's life miserable [31], [32]. 

Most shockingly, Ethel and her sisters spread the village rumour that Harry had been poisoned by Ivy [31], [32]. Ethel alleged to Glanville that after Harry's death, they had found a half-empty bottle of "sugar of lead" in the cellar [32], [33]. During the famous "Blue Room" seance on June 12-13, 1929, Ethel and her sister used a tapping mirror to ask leading questions of an entity claiming to be Harry Bull, openly guiding the "spirit" to confess that he had been murdered by his wife over money troubles [34], [35], [36]. 

**Opinions on Other Rectory Residents**
Ethel was highly critical of the rectors and wives who succeeded her family:
*   **The Smiths:** Ethel liked the Rev. Guy Eric Smith but dismissed him as "quite charming but an ignoramus in psychic matters" [37], [38]. She deeply disliked his wife, Mabel Smith, observing that she was "frightened of shadows" and mocking her ambition to write a bestselling thriller called *Murder at the Parsonage* [37], [38]. When Mabel Smith later wrote to the press claiming she had never believed the house was haunted, Ethel retorted in a 1954 letter: "All nonsense Mrs. Smith making out they were not frightened, she used to shriek with fright" [39].
*   **Marianne Foyster:** Ethel held an incredibly low opinion of the Rev. Lionel Foyster's young wife, describing her to Sidney Glanville as "mad as a hatter", "very self-centred", "eccentric", and a woman who "did some very disreputable things" [40], [41], [42].

**The Crypt Contradiction**
The sources record a specific contradiction regarding Ethel's knowledge of the Borley Church crypt. Harry Price originally published that Ethel had told him about heavy coffins moving inexplicably in the church vault [43], [44]. However, when interviewed by SPR skeptics Eric Dingwall and Trevor Hall in 1953, Ethel claimed she had absolutely no knowledge of the crypt or the coffins [45]. Complicating this further, investigator G. Croom-Hollingsworth later stated he possessed evidence that Ethel *did* know about the crypt and had personally been down into the vault [44].

**Steadfast Belief**
Despite her annoyance with Price's exaggerations, the pressure from skeptical investigators (such as W.H. Salter, who tried to pressure her to cut ties with Price—a request she deemed a "cheek to ask" [46], [47]), and mockery from villagers, Ethel May Bull never retracted her core claims [48], [49]. When questioned late in life about the authenticity of her 1900 sighting, she provided her most famous quote on the matter: **"What would be the use of an old lady like me, waiting to meet her Maker, telling a lot of fairy stories?"** [50].
