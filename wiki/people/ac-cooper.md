# A.C. Cooper

Governing director of A.C. Cooper Ltd., fine-art photographers to the Royal Family; studios in London's West End, famous for photographing Royal Academy pictures, *objets d'art*, and old china [1]. Known to [[harry-price|Price]] slightly through a publisher introduction, Cooper had no prior interest in or knowledge of psychical research. Having occupied his studios for twenty-five years, he was a meticulous professional who had never experienced an accident involving valuable property until his encounter with the Borley Rectory relics [2].

## Introduction to the Borley Relics (September 1943)

On **September 13, 1943**, Price brought several objects excavated from the Borley Rectory cellars to Cooper's studio for photography — most notably a fragment of a human skull, a gold pendant, a Miraculous Medal, and other associated items [2, 3]. Cooper remarked that he had "photographed some queer things in his time, but the 'exhibits' I showed him were, he said, the strangest that had been brought to him" [3].

During this initial consultation, the brittle skull fragment slipped from the hands of both Price and Cooper, falling to the wooden studio floor and breaking into four pieces [2]. A highly concerned Cooper noted that this was the very first time in his twenty-five-year career that anything of value had suffered an accident on his premises [2].

## The Studio Phenomena

When Price returned to collect the items the following morning (**September 14**), Cooper greeted him with the emphatic remark: **"Thank goodness you've come — the sooner your property is off my premises the better!"** [4]. Cooper then described a bizarre string of unexplained events that had occurred in the studio while the Borley relics were in his possession:

- **Falling paintings**: A 1,000-guinea oil painting inexplicably fell off its easel while Cooper was in the room, though not near the easel [4]. Later that same afternoon, a second painting also fell under similar circumstances [4]. Fortunately, neither was damaged. Cooper considered the odds of two paintings falling naturally in the same studio on the same day to be "astronomical" [4].
- **Spoiled film**: Cooper, despite his vast professional experience, uncharacteristically ruined two batches of film because he forgot to place the necessary stop in the camera lens [4]. This was something he had never done before.
- **The broken clock**: An old dark-room clock previously used for timing exposures, and completely broken and silent for ten years despite all of Cooper's attempts at "tinkering" with it, suddenly began ticking [5]. It ran for exactly twenty minutes, then stopped permanently and would not go again [5].

Price carefully declines to assert a causal link between the relics and the events — he states only the facts. The relics had been in his own possession for weeks without incident. He calls the events "occurrences" rather than "phenomena," acknowledging ambiguity. Following these events, Price explained the mischievous nature of poltergeist phenomena, leaving Cooper "very interested, and very puzzled" [5].

## The Studio Destroyed

Five months later, on **February 23, 1944**, Price brought rare pamphlets on poltergeists to Cooper to be photographed for another book [6]. Price was anxious about leaving the valuable books in London overnight due to the threat of air raids, but Cooper promised to complete the photography that afternoon. Price, acting on an "intuition," collected the prints the same day rather than leaving them overnight [7].

That night, a severe air raid completely destroyed Cooper's West End studios, reducing the street to an unrecognisable area of rubble [7]. The building was empty at the time [7]. Cooper — perhaps acting on an intuition of his own — had taken the precaution of carrying the finished photographic prints home with him that evening, ensuring their survival [7]. Following the destruction of his premises, Cooper utilised duplicate apparatus he kept at his home and soon re-established his photography business at a new location [7].
