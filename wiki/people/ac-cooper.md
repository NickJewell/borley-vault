# A.C. Cooper

Governing director of Messrs A.C. Cooper Ltd., photographers to the Royal Family; studios in the West End of London; famous for photographing Royal Academy pictures, *objets d'art*, old china. Known to [[harry-price|Price]] slightly through a publisher introduction. Had no prior interest in or knowledge of psychical research.

## The Studio Phenomena

On September 13, 1943, Price brought the Borley relics (human skull fragment, gold pendant, Miraculous Medal, other objects) to Cooper's studio for photography. That afternoon — the only afternoon in Cooper's 25-year career when such things occurred:

- **Two oil paintings fell from their easels** for no reason, while Cooper was in the studio but not near them. Neither was damaged. "The odds against two paintings normally falling off their respective easels, for no particular reason, on one afternoon, and in the same studio, were astronomical."
- **Two batches of photographic films ruined** by failing to set the lens stop — something Cooper had never done before.
- **A dark-room clock that had not worked for ten years began ticking** spontaneously while Cooper was occupied in the studio. It ran for twenty minutes, then stopped permanently and would not go again.

Cooper's greeting to Price the following morning: "Thank goodness you've come — the sooner your property is off my premises the better!" He then described the preceding afternoon.

Price carefully declines to assert a causal link between the relics and the events — he states only the facts. The relics had been in his own possession for weeks without incident. He calls the events "occurrences" rather than "phenomena" — acknowledging ambiguity.

## The Studio Destroyed

Five months later (February 23, 1944), Price brought Cooper rare pamphlets on Poltergeists to photograph for another book. He had an "intuition" that they should not remain in London that night and collected them the same day. That night, Cooper's studios were destroyed in a Blitz air raid. Cooper had taken the prints home with him that evening — he too had an intuition. Neither was in the building; no one was harmed; Cooper soon re-established his business at new premises.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mr. A.C. Cooper** was the governing director of A.C. Cooper, Ltd., a prominent firm of fine-art photographers to the Royal Family based in London’s West End [1]. His business specialized in the photographic reproduction of Royal Academy pictures, old china, and *objets d'art* [1]. Having occupied his studios for twenty-five years, Cooper was a meticulous professional who had never experienced an accident involving valuable property until his encounter with the Borley Rectory relics [2].

**Introduction to the Borley Relics (September 1943)**
On **September 13, 1943**, psychical researcher Harry Price visited Cooper's studio to commission photographs of several objects excavated from the Borley Rectory cellars, most notably a fragment of a human skull [2, 3]. Cooper had no prior knowledge of psychical research, but upon viewing the items, he remarked that **"he had photographed some queer things in his time, but the 'exhibits' I showed him were, he said, the strangest that had been brought to him"** [3]. 

During this initial consultation, a notable mishap occurred. The brittle skull fragment slipped from the hands of both Price and Cooper, falling to the wooden studio floor and breaking into four pieces [2]. A highly concerned Cooper noted that this was the very first time in his twenty-five-year career that anything of value had suffered an accident on his premises [2]. 

**The Studio Incidents**
When Price returned to collect the items the following morning (**September 14**), Cooper greeted him with the emphatic remark: **"Thank goodness you’ve come—the sooner your property is off my premises the better!"** [4]. Cooper then related a bizarre string of unexplained events that had occurred in the studio while the Borley relics were in his possession:
*   **Falling Paintings:** A 1,000-guinea oil painting inexplicably fell off its easel while Cooper was in the room (though not near the easel) [4]. Later that same afternoon, a second painting also fell under similar circumstances [4]. Fortunately, neither painting was damaged, but Cooper considered the odds of two paintings falling naturally in the same studio on the same day to be "astronomical" [4].
*   **Spoiled Film:** Cooper, despite his vast professional experience, uncharacteristically ruined two batches of film because he forgot to place the necessary "stop" in the camera lens [4].
*   **The Broken Clock:** The most remarkable anomaly involved an old dark-room clock previously used for timing exposures [5]. The clock had been completely broken and silent for ten years, defying all of Cooper's attempts at "tinkering" with it [5]. Suddenly, Cooper heard its "familiar loud tick"; the clock functioned perfectly for exactly twenty minutes and then stopped, never to work again [5]. 

Following these events, Price explained the mischievous nature of poltergeist phenomena, leaving Cooper "very interested, and very puzzled" [5]. 

**The Destruction of the Studio (February 1944)**
Cooper's involvement with Price had a dramatic coda five months later. On **February 23, 1944**, Price brought some rare pamphlets about poltergeists to Cooper to be photographed [6]. Price was anxious about leaving the valuable books in London overnight due to the threat of air raids, but Cooper promised to complete the photography that afternoon so the prints would be ready the next morning [6]. 

That night, **a severe air raid completely destroyed Cooper's West End studios, reducing the street to an unrecognizable area of rubble** [7]. Fortunately, the building was empty at the time [7]. Furthermore, Cooper—perhaps acting on an "intuition" of his own—had taken the precaution of carrying the finished photographic prints home with him that evening, ensuring their survival [7]. Following the destruction of his premises, Cooper utilized duplicate apparatus he kept at his home and soon resumed his photography business at a new location [7].
