# Rev. Henry Charles Brereton Foyster

Cousin of [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Algernon Foyster]]; son of [[rev-henry-brereton-foyster|Rev. Henry Brereton Foyster]]. He inherited the family living of **St Clement's, Hastings**, from his father in 1906.

## Significance

His inheritance of St Clement's from his father in 1906 — rather than its passing to his cousin Lionel — is one of the clues Robert Wood reads as evidence of an early scandal in Lionel's career. By 1906, Lionel was a fully ordained priest with three years of experience, yet the family patronage went within the direct line rather than to him.

Wood lists him among the five Foysters in the 1911 edition of *Crockford's Clerical Directory* (all related), illustrating the dynasty's extent and the network of relatives who would have known if Lionel was in genuine distress during his later years of supposed "imprisonment" by Marianne.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Reverend Henry Charles Brereton Foyster** appears very briefly in the Borley Rectory sources as a member of the wealthy "Victorian ecclesiastical dynasty" of the Foyster family based in Hastings [1]. 

A reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific details regarding his life and his contextual significance to the wider Borley saga:

**Family Lineage**
*   He was the son of the **Rev. Henry Brereton Foyster** [2].
*   Through his uncle, George Alfred Foyster, he was the first cousin of the **Rev. Lionel Algernon Foyster**, the famous incumbent of Borley Rectory from 1930 to 1935 [2, 3].

**Clerical Career and Succession**
*   In **1906**, he officially succeeded his father as the **Rector of St Clement’s in Hastings** [2]. 
*   Because his father acted as both the rector and the patron of the St Clement's living, he possessed the legal right to appoint the next clergyman and directly **"handed over to his son"** [2].

**Contextual Significance in the Borley Literature**
*   Rev. Henry Charles Brereton Foyster's direct inheritance of the St Clement's parish is utilized by author Robert Wood (*The Widow of Borley*) to illustrate the standard, lucrative practice of passing church livings from father to son within the Foyster dynasty [1, 2]. 
*   His smooth succession serves as a stark historical contrast to the highly suspicious circumstances surrounding his cousin, Lionel Algernon Foyster [2, 4]. While Henry Charles Brereton was readily given St Clement's by his father, Lionel was inexplicably bypassed for the equally desirable family living of All Saints' by his own father [4]. Wood uses this comparison to argue that there must have been some underlying family scandal or difficulty that forced Lionel to be excluded from the family's direct patronage [4].
