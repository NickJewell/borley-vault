# Miss A. Goodrich-Freer

**Ada Goodrich-Freer** (1857–1930), also referred to as Miss A. Goodrich-Freer or Miss Freer, was a clairvoyant and medium who joined the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) in 1888 [1, 2]. She was not directly involved in the events at Borley Rectory, but her prior work significantly influenced how [[harry-price|Harry Price]] subsequently approached his own investigation there.

## The Ballechin House Investigation

Miss Freer is best known for her investigation of Ballechin House, a poorly constructed rural mansion in the Scottish Highlands, commissioned by [[lord-bute|Lord Bute]] (1897). She stayed approximately three months at the house, kept a detailed diary, and **invited dozens of witnesses to spend time at the property and record their experiences** [3]. Price consciously modelled his 1937–38 investigation of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] upon this exact template [3].

Alongside John, Marquess of Bute, she published her findings in the 1899 book ***The Alleged Haunting of B— House*** [4]. The book became a bestseller and cemented the myth of Ballechin in the popular imagination [2]. The work drew the famous *Times* controversy of 1897, in which sceptics who had not been present wrote dismissively to the newspaper — a direct parallel to the SPR controversy over Borley decades later.

## The Phantom Nun at Ballechin

Goodrich-Freer and members of her party saw the Ballechin phantom nun multiple times. On one occasion she observed a nun-like figure gliding up a snow-covered glen:

> *"The figure stopped, looked at Miss Freer, and then slowly mounted a slope apparently too steep for a human to climb. Then she disappeared."*

She had further sight of the nun weeping:

> *"Her weeping seemed to me passionate and unrestrained… she speaks upon a rather high note with a quality of youth in her voice."*

Via Ouija board, the Ballechin nun gave her name as "Ishbel" — a Scottish Gaelic form of Isabella.

[[harry-price|Price]] points out that both Goodrich-Freer and the Borley witnesses described the nun-phantom in nearly identical terms: "sad, ill, pale, and appeared to have been weeping" (Borley); "pale, and her weeping seemed to be passionate and unrestrained" (Ballechin). In both cases the nun haunted the *grounds* only, and never entered the house. Sir Oliver Lodge and F. W. H. Myers both visited Ballechin during the investigation and were impressed.

Miss Freer also kept a detailed diary recording typical poltergeist phenomena, including dragging objects, voices, footsteps, explosive bangs, and knockings [5]. On the morning after her arrival she reported being **"startled by a loud clanging sound, which seemed to resound through the house,"** describing it as resembling a long metal bar being struck by a wooden mallet [6]. Like Borley, Ballechin House was noted for its abnormal coldness: **"The room was so cold that we had to cover our faces. . . . The house felt like a vault"** [7].

## Scepticism and Discrediting

Miss Freer's investigation attracted severe criticism both during her lifetime and after:

- *The Times* exposed her investigation as inept and unscientific [2]. The psychiatrist Sir James Crichton-Browne derided the SPR for giving the case credence: **"The costly and very generously conducted experiment at Ballechin was a fiasco – an illustration of a myth and the magnification and misrepresentation of a few, simple natural phenomena by sensitive minds in quest of the supernatural"** [2].
- In 1968, highly sceptical investigator [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] contributed a large monograph on the Ballechin House case to John L. Campbell's book *Strange Things* — a **"lengthy discrediting exercise of the work of Ada Goodrich-Freer"** [1].
