# Miss A. Goodrich-Freer

Co-investigator at Ballechin House, Perthshire (1897), alongside Colonel Lemesurier Taylor. Commissioned by [[lord-bute|Lord Bute]]; stayed approximately three months at the house; kept a detailed diary and co-authored the investigation report with Bute.

## The Phantom Nun at Ballechin

Goodrich-Freer and members of her party saw the Ballechin phantom nun multiple times. On one occasion she observed a nun-like figure gliding up a snow-covered glen:

> *"The figure stopped, looked at Miss Freer, and then slowly mounted a slope apparently too steep for a human to climb. Then she disappeared."*

She had further sight of the nun weeping:

> *"Her weeping seemed to me passionate and unrestrained… she speaks upon a rather high note with a quality of youth in her voice."*

Via Ouija board, the Ballechin nun gave her name as "Ishbel" — a Scottish Gaelic form of Isabella.

## Parallels with Borley

[[harry-price|Price]] points out that both Goodrich-Freer and the Borley witnesses described the nun-phantom in nearly identical terms: "sad, ill, pale, and appeared to have been weeping" (Borley); "pale, and her weeping seemed to be passionate and unrestrained" (Ballechin). In both cases the nun haunted the *grounds* only, and never entered the house. Sir Oliver Lodge and F. W. H. Myers both visited Ballechin during the investigation and were impressed.

## Publication

Goodrich-Freer and Bute published their report as *The Alleged Haunting of B---- House* (1899). The work drew the famous *Times* controversy of 1897, in which sceptics who had not been present during the investigation wrote dismissively to the newspaper. The controversy is a direct parallel to the SPR controversy over Borley decades later.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Ada Goodrich-Freer** (1857–1930), also referred to as Miss A. Goodrich-Freer or Miss Freer, was a clairvoyant and medium who joined the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) in 1888 [1, 2]. While she was not directly involved in the events at Borley Rectory, her prior work significantly influenced how Harry Price subsequently approached his own famous investigation.

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following facts, events, quotes, and controversies regarding her life and work:

**The Ballechin House Investigation and Influence on Borley**
Miss Freer is best known for her investigation of Ballechin House, a poorly constructed rural mansion in the Scottish Highlands that was situated in an area prone to earthquakes and subsidence [2]. To investigate the alleged haunting, **she invited dozens of witnesses to spend time at the property and record their experiences** [3]. Harry Price consciously modeled his 1937–1938 investigation of Borley Rectory upon this exact template [3]. 

Alongside John, Marquess of Bute, she published her findings in the 1899 book ***The Alleged Haunting of B— House*** [4]. The book became a bestseller and cemented the myth of Ballechin in the popular imagination [2].

**Paranormal Experiences and Quotes at Ballechin**
During her investigation, Miss Freer kept a detailed diary recording what appeared to be typical poltergeist phenomena, including dragging objects, voices, footsteps, explosive bangs, and knockings [5]. She recorded several specific experiences with distinct quotes:
*   **Auditory Phenomena:** On the morning after her arrival at Ballechin, she reported being **"startled by a loud clanging sound, which seemed to resound through the house,"** describing it as resembling a long metal bar being struck by a wooden mallet [6]. She also recorded a persistent aural impression of a priest reading aloud, noting that while words could not be distinguished, the tone and cadence reminded observers of a priest **"saying his office"** [6].
*   **Extreme Cold:** Like Borley, Ballechin House was noted for its abnormal coldness. Miss Freer explicitly stated: **"The room was so cold that we had to cover our faces. . . . The house felt like a vault"** [7]. 
*   **The Phantom Nun:** Miss Freer and her friends reported seeing the phantasm of a nun gliding up a snowy glen, who stopped to look at Miss Freer before climbing a steep slope and vanishing [8]. Using a Ouija board, the investigators ascertained the nun's name was "Ishbel" [8]. On one occasion, Miss Freer observed the nun in tears, recording: **"Her weeping seemed to me passionate and unrestrained,"** and adding that **"she speaks upon a rather high note with a quality of youth in her voice"** [8]. 

**Skepticism and Discrediting of Her Work**
Miss Freer's investigation at Ballechin House attracted severe criticism both during her lifetime and long after her death:
*   **Contemporary Critics:** *The Times* exposed her investigation and subsequent book as being entirely inept and unscientific [2]. The psychiatrist Sir James Crichton-Browne derided the SPR for giving the case credence, stating in his memoirs: **"The costly and very generously conducted experiment at Ballechin was a fiasco – an illustration of a myth and the magnification and misrepresentation of a few, simple natural phenomena by sensitive minds in quest of the supernatural"** [2].
*   **Later Critics:** In 1968, the highly skeptical investigator Trevor Hall (who was a primary author of the SPR's critical *Borley Report*) contributed a large monograph on the Ballechin House case to John L. Campbell’s book *Strange Things* [1]. In keeping with his literary trend of debunking classic hauntings, Hall's contribution was a **"lengthy discrediting exercise of the work of Ada Goodrich-Freer"** [1].
