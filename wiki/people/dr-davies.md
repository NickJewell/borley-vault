# Dr Davies

A doctor from the English Midlands (first name not recorded by Wood) who became [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]]'s most bizarre wartime victim. After the death of his wife [[mabel-davies|Mabel]] from cancer, he abandoned his practice, moved to the **Grosvenor Hotel, Victoria**, and took up spiritualism in hopes of contacting her. His six-month relationship with Marianne (autumn 1941–March 1942) was one of the more sinister episodes in her career.

## Background

Davies suffered a complete emotional collapse after Mabel's death and lived on capital — no longer working. He read [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s *The Most Haunted House in England* and concluded that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] was "clearly the most powerful medium who had ever lived." He asked Price for her address and wrote to her; a meeting in London followed.

When they first met Davies was forty-five and Marianne forty-two. He described her:

> "An exceedingly attractive woman, looking not a day older than the mid-thirties. She was of medium height and build, with an excellent figure and the most graceful movements. She was dark-haired with a round face and regular features."

He noted her eyes as "her most striking feature — dark, shining and luminous" and observed something "satanic" about her regard when she thought he wasn't watching.

## The Relationship

Davies paid Marianne's train fare from Wickham Market (nearest station to [[dairy-cottage-rendlesham|Dairy Cottage]]) to London. Together they visited the offices of the Society for Psychical Research at Tavistock Square, where an official recalled them as on "very friendly terms." Davies admitted she occasionally stayed overnight at the Grosvenor Hotel.

The most remarkable feature of the relationship was Marianne's use of fake spiritualist trances: she fell into apparent trances in the hotel lounge and "embraced" Davies, allowing him to believe it was the spirit of dear departed Mabel. As Wood observes, "this called for a good deal of imagination on everyone's part."

Marianne told Davies truthfully that her husband "Lion" (Foyster) was not expected to live long — she was already planning her escape.

## The Break

In March 1942 Marianne wrote to Davies ending the relationship, precisely when he told her his capital was running low. She never returned his many love-letters, leaving him to live for years in fear of blackmail.

Her final act was extraordinary and cruel:

> "Marianne's final touch is astounding. Davies had given her many of his dead wife's clothes, and she returned these to him twisted into strange doll-like shapes, with mysterious pieces of cardboard upon which what Davies described as 'cabbalistic symbols' were inscribed. Not unnaturally he gained the impression that Marianne was trying to place a curse on him."

Wood considers this one of the clearest illustrations of Marianne's "cruel and vindictive nature" — deliberate psychological intimidation of a man already destabilised by grief.

## Significance

Davies fits the pattern Wood identifies across Marianne's male relationships: all were men with mental or emotional vulnerabilities — [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] (shell-shock, religious crisis), [[henry-fisher|Henry Fisher]] (head injury, instability), [[robert-oneil|Robert O'Neil]] (alcoholism). Davies's grief for his wife made him an ideal target. The relationship also shows Marianne deliberately positioning herself for Foyster's death: she used Davies as a potential escape route until his money ran low.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Dr. J. R. A. Davies** was a former general medical practitioner from the Midlands and a member of the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) [1, 2]. In the Borley Rectory sources, he is primarily recorded as the only male victim to have spoken at length about a brief but intense and highly manipulative relationship he had with the Rev. Lionel Foyster's wife, Marianne, during the autumn and winter of 1941–1942 [2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, quotes, and contradictions regarding his involvement:

**Personal Tragedy and Introduction to Borley**
Following the death of his beloved wife, Mabel (whom he affectionately called "Tweatie"), from cancer, Dr. Davies suffered a complete emotional collapse [2, 3]. Grief-stricken, he abandoned his medical practice, moved into the Grosvenor Hotel in Victoria, London, and lived off his capital [2, 4]. Hoping to make contact with his dead wife, he began researching spiritualism [4]. This led him to read Harry Price’s book *The Most Haunted House in England*, which convinced him that Marianne Foyster must be the most powerful medium who had ever lived [4]. Davies met with Harry Price, acquired Marianne's address (she was then living at Dairy Cottage in Rendlesham, Suffolk), and wrote to her, leading to a meeting in London [4, 5]. 

**The Relationship and Marianne's "Trances"**
When they first met, Davies was 45 and Marianne was 42 [6]. He became utterly infatuated with her, describing her as an **"exceedingly attractive woman, looking not a day older than the mid-thirties"** [6]. He noted she had an excellent figure, graceful movements, regular features, dark luminous eyes, thick sensuous lips, and spoke fluent French [6]. However, he also recalled that when he caught her off-guard, **there was "something satanic" about her gaze** [6, 7].

During the wartime autumn of 1941, Davies regularly paid Marianne's train fare to London, treating her to restaurant dinners and theatre trips, and even providing her with his late wife's clothing because Marianne claimed she had nothing suitable to wear [7-9]. They also visited the SPR offices in Tavistock Square together, where an official recalled Davies being "very flashily dressed" [8]. 

Exploiting his grief, Marianne allegedly fell into a trance in the hotel lounge, attempted automatic writing, and embraced him while pretending to be the spirit of his dead wife, Tweatie [10]. Though embraces soon followed when Marianne was *not* in a trance, and she occasionally stayed overnight at his hotel, Davies insisted that nothing improper ever occurred, claiming his devotion to his late wife gave him the strength to resist temptation [9, 10]. 

**Financial Motives and the Breakup**
Marianne was remarkably frank with Davies about her circumstances, confiding that her husband, Lionel (whom she called "Lion"), was not expected to live long and had "no growl left in him" [11]. She showed a keen interest in Davies's financial situation, specifically wanting to know how much capital he had secured from selling his practice [7, 11]. 

The relationship lasted exactly six months [12]. In March 1942, when Davies confessed that his capital was running low, Marianne's enthusiasm immediately chilled [7, 12]. She wrote to him to end the relationship and demanded the return of her love letters [7, 12]. Davies agreed, on the condition that she return his letters and his late wife's clothing [7]. 

**The "Cabalistic" Curse**
When the package of clothing arrived at the Grosvenor Hotel, Davies was deeply disturbed [7, 12]. Marianne had **twisted the dresses into strange doll-like shapes, knotted silk stockings through colored circles of cardboard, and pinned scraps of paper with insulting messages and "cabbalistic symbols" to the garments** [7, 12]. Davies firmly believed she was trying to place a Satanic curse on him [7, 12]. Furthermore, she never returned his letters, leaving the unfortunate doctor to live for years in dread of being blackmailed [9, 12].

**Contradictions: Marianne's Denial**
During a 1958 interview with investigator Robert Swanson, Marianne fiercely contradicted Davies's account. When asked if she had sexual relations with him, she replied: **"No, I did not. The matter never came up in any way."** [3]. She claimed Davies had approached her because she was a "dead ringer" for Tweatie, and she dismissed him as being **"mentally retarded, due to the recent loss of a beloved person"** [3]. She also contradicted the accounts of her hotel "trances" by claiming she refused his suggestion to visit a medium because "that sort of thing always makes me feel ill" [3]. 

**Dr. Davies's Final Verdict on the Haunting**
Despite his initial belief that Marianne was a powerful medium, Dr. Davies ultimately rejected the paranormality of the Borley phenomena [1]. Just before the SPR published their highly critical *Borley Report* in 1956, Davies submitted a signed statement to the authors [1]. Based on his own 1941 inquiries and his correspondence with both Harry Price and Marianne, he stated his convinced professional opinion that **the famous Borley "wall-messages" were actually executed in "a thinly disguised version of Mrs Foyster's own handwriting."** [1].
