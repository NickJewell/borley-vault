# H. E. Adshead, J.P., B.A.

Of Beslyns, Great Bardfield, Braintree, Essex. A frequent correspondent with [[harry-price|Price]] about the Borley case, contributing both wall-writing interpretation and theoretical observation following the publication of Price's work.

## Wall-Writing Contribution

Adshead agreed with [[rev-fa-heaton|Rev. Heaton]] that the "odd marks" in the "Get-Light-Mass-and-Prayers-Here" wall-message (reproduced at p.197 of *The Most Haunted House in England*) — when the page is turned upside down — read as the inverted word "light" [1]. Analysing a seemingly indecipherable scribble that appeared near the Blue Room on **June 16, 1931**, Adshead independently arrived at the same conclusion as Heaton, making them one of several independent readers who converged on the same reading of the inverted marks.

## Clergy Theory

In a letter about the Borley case, Adshead made a pointed observation about the pattern of witnesses and investigators:

> **"You do not seem to notice that it is clergymen who cause the big events! Even after the fire it was two clergymen again."** [2]

This refers to the fact that after the fire, one of the early phenomena was reported by a clerical friend of the Bull family passing the unoccupied Rectory; and subsequently [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Henning]] noticed a picture fall from a nail during a parish meeting.

Price acknowledged Adshead's observation in his 1946 book, *The End of Borley Rectory*, assuring him that he *had* noticed the clergy connection, and agreeing that the Borley story was "almost completely centred on the clergy, their families, priests, monks, nuns, and the Church generally — especially the Roman Catholic Church" [2].

Adshead's observation anticipates Howard Spring's *Country Life* theory (December 18, 1942) about the relationship between ecclesiastical settings and poltergeist activity.
