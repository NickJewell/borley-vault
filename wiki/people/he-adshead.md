# H. E. Adshead, J.P., B.A.

Of Beslyns, Great Bardfield, Braintree, Essex. A frequent correspondent with [[harry-price|Price]] about the Borley case, contributing both wall-writing interpretation and theoretical observation.

## Wall-Writing Contribution

Adshead agreed with [[rev-fa-heaton|Rev. Heaton]] that the "odd marks" in the "Get-Light-Mass-and-Prayers-Here" wall-message (reproduced at p.197 of *The Most Haunted House in England*) — when the page is turned upside down — read as the inverted word "light." This is one of several independent readers who converged on the same reading of the inverted marks.

## Clergy Theory

In a letter about the Borley case, Adshead made a pointed observation about the pattern of witnesses and investigators:

> *"You do not seem to notice that it is clergymen who cause the big events! Even after the fire it was two clergymen again."*

This refers to the fact that after the fire, one of the early phenomena was reported by a clerical friend of the Bull family passing the unoccupied Rectory; and subsequently the Rev. Henning noticed a picture fall from a nail during a parish meeting.

Price acknowledges he did notice the clergy connection but had not highlighted it: "The Borley story is almost completely centred on the clergy, their families, priests, monks, nuns, and the Church generally — especially the Roman Catholic Church."

Adshead's observation anticipates [[mr-howard-spring|Howard Spring]]'s *Country Life* theory (December 18, 1942) about the relationship between ecclesiastical settings and Poltergeist activity.

**H. E. Adshead, J.P., B.A.**, appears in the Borley Rectory sources as an attentive reader and correspondent of Harry Price who offered specific theories and observations regarding the hauntings following the publication of Price's work. 

A brief but specific reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following biographical details, theories, and quotes:

**Biographical Details**
The sources record that H. E. Adshead resided at **Beslyns, Great Bardfield, Braintree, Essex** [1].

**Interpretation of the Wall-Writings**
Adshead contributed a specific theory regarding the famous "Marianne" wall appeals that appeared during the Foyster incumbency. Analyzing a seemingly indecipherable scribble that appeared near the Blue Room on **June 16, 1931**, Adshead independently arrived at the same conclusion as another correspondent, Mr. Heaton: he posited that a series of "odd marks" in the message actually formed the **inverted word "light"** [1]. 

**The Clerical Connection and Quote**
Adshead also astutely observed a recurring pattern in the Borley narrative: the prominent and central role that members of the clergy played in witnessing and reporting the phenomena. He wrote to Harry Price to point this out, supplying the following direct quote: 
> **"You do not seem to notice that it is clergymen who cause the big events! Even after the fire it was two clergymen again"** [2]. 

Price acknowledged Adshead's observation in his 1946 book, *The End of Borley Rectory*, assuring him that he *had* noticed the clergy connection, and agreeing that the Borley story was "almost completely centred on the clergy, their families, priests, monks, nuns, and the Church generally" [2].
