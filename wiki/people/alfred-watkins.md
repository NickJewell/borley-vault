# Alfred Watkins

Herefordshire businessman, amateur archaeologist and photographer who re-discovered — or first articulated — the theory of **ley lines** in 1921. His ideas form the basis of Babbs and Mathias's proposed explanation for the mechanical and electrical disturbances reported at Borley.

## The Discovery of Ley Lines

In 1921, during an outing in the hills of Herefordshire, Watkins surveyed a familiar landscape and noticed for the first time that ancient monuments — churches, bridges, crossroads, barrows, and stone circles — were frequently in alignment with each other. He spent years investigating this phenomenon and eventually published books arguing that these alignments were deliberate, prehistoric planning choices rather than accidents.

His criteria for a ley line: if at least five ancient monuments and/or named places (especially those with names ending in *-ley* or *-leigh*) are in alignment with each other within a total distance of 25 miles, it could be reasonably assumed to be a genuine ley line.

Watkins believed neolithic peoples were the originators of ley lines, tracing routes along which essential commodities — flints, salt, clay — were transported across the country. He further proposed that ley lines follow **lines of magnetic force**, and that early peoples may have been innately aware of these, much as migratory birds navigate by magnetism.

## Criticism

Not all agree with Watkins's theories. Dr Simon Broadbent, an advertising statistician with a lifelong interest in megalithic monuments, argued in a paper to the Royal Statistical Society that the number of alleged ley lines was fewer than would be expected from a random distribution of ancient sites.

## Application to Borley

Babbs and Mathias identified four possible ley lines running through Borley, all meeting Watkins's criteria:
1. Roughly W–E: Sturmer, Wixoe, Stoke-by-Clare, Borley, Hadleigh, Chattisham
2. NW–SE: Cavendish, Pentlow, Foxearth, Borley, Great Cornard, Honey Tye, Nayland, Boxted Cross, Ardleigh, Little Bentley
3. SW–NE: Great Saling, Sible Hedingham, Gestingthorpe, Borley, Rodbridge, Thorpe Morieux
4. SW–NE: Belchamp Walter, Borley, Rodbridge, Lavenham, Preston St Mary, Stowmarket

If these lines carry the magnetic properties Watkins described, this could explain why cars, tape recorders, cameras, and the church organ have all reportedly malfunctioned at Borley — while functioning normally once removed from the site.
