# Alfred Watkins

Herefordshire businessman, amateur archaeologist and photographer who re-discovered — or first articulated — the theory of **ley lines** in 1921. His ideas form the basis of Babbs and Mathias's proposed explanation for the mechanical and electrical disturbances reported at Borley.

## The Discovery of Ley Lines

In 1921, while resting during an outing in the local Herefordshire hills, Watkins surveyed a familiar panorama and noticed for the first time that ancient monuments — stone circles, barrows, crossroads, bridges, and churches — were frequently in direct alignment with one another [1]. This observation led him to spend significant time studying these alignments, ultimately convincing him that the straight lines were constructed deliberately and purposefully, rather than being the result of accident [1, 2]. He subsequently published books detailing his findings [2].

His criteria for a ley line: if at least five ancient monuments and/or named places (especially those with names ending in *-ley* or *-leigh*) are in alignment with each other within a total distance of 25 miles, it could reasonably be assumed to be a genuine ley line [2]. He noticed that an unusually high number of places situated on these lines had names ending in "ley" or "leigh," theorizing that these suffixes indicated man-made clearings or pastures established by small communities in the ancient forests that once covered the country [2].

Watkins believed neolithic peoples were the originators of ley lines, tracing routes along which essential commodities — flints, salt, and clay — were transported across the country [3]. He further proposed that ley lines follow **lines of magnetic force**, and that early peoples may have been innately aware of these, much as migratory birds navigate by magnetism. He also theorized that subsequent civilizations made unwitting use of these established lines; for example, the famously straight Roman roads were built following the exact direction of his proposed neolithic ley lines [3].

## Criticism

Not all agree with Watkins's theories. Dr. Simon Broadbent, an advertising statistician with a lifelong interest in megalithic monuments, argued in a paper to the Royal Statistical Society that the number of alleged ley lines was actually fewer than would be expected from a purely random distribution of ancient sites [4]. This statistical challenge has never been fully resolved by ley-line proponents.

## Application to Borley

While Alfred Watkins had no personal involvement with Borley Rectory, his theories were later applied by paranormal investigators to explain the haunting [1]. Writers such as Frank Smyth suggested that intersecting ley lines — sometimes understood as lines of magnetic force — might be responsible for the phenomena at Borley [4, 5]. Babbs and Mathias identified four possible ley lines running through Borley, all meeting Watkins's criteria [5]:

1. Roughly W–E: Sturmer, Wixoe, Stoke-by-Clare, Borley, Hadleigh, Chattisham
2. NW–SE: Cavendish, Pentlow, Foxearth, Borley, Great Cornard, Honey Tye, Nayland, Boxted Cross, Ardleigh, Little Bentley
3. SW–NE: Great Saling, Sible Hedingham, Gestingthorpe, Borley, Rodbridge, Thorpe Morieux
4. SW–NE: Belchamp Walter, Borley, Rodbridge, Lavenham, Preston St Mary, Stowmarket

If these lines carry the magnetic properties Watkins described, this could explain why cars, tape recorders, cameras, and the church organ have all reportedly malfunctioned at Borley — while functioning normally once removed from the site.
