# William Playne

Rector of Borley from 1660 to 1680 (died 1680, still holding the living). Appointed in 1660 by Philip Waldegrave *per curatores* (with the patron not of age). In 1662, Playne moved to Langenhoo — another incumbency in the Waldegrave gift — abandoning residence at Borley while technically remaining its rector.

## Significance in the Marie Lairre Theory

[[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] draws attention to Playne's departure date (1662) in his analysis of the murder of [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]], alleged by Planchette to have occurred on May 17, 1667.

If the murder occurred in 1667, Playne had already been absent from the Rectory for five years. The Borley Rectory — potentially still habitable — would therefore have been **unoccupied by its rector**, creating the conditions under which a young woman could be housed there secretly, and subsequently murdered and buried under the cellar floor, without the incumbent having any knowledge of it.

Phythian-Adams's argument is that both Playne's transfer (1662) and his successor [[thomas-muriell|Thomas Muriell]]'s similar arrangement (1681 onward) may have been orchestrated by the Waldegrave family — who held the patronage — to ensure the Rectory remained without a resident clergyman for the crucial decades.

> **Note:** There is no direct evidence that Playne's departure was anything other than an ordinary non-residential plurality arrangement, common in the seventeenth century. Phythian-Adams's reading is inferential.
