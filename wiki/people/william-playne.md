# William Playne

Rector of Borley from approximately **1660 to 1680** (died 1680, still holding the living). Appointed by Philip Waldegrave *per curatores* (with the patron not of age). In 1662, Playne moved to Langenhoe — another incumbency in the Waldegrave gift — abandoning residence at Borley while technically remaining its rector.

> **Contradiction:** A formal list of Borley rectors records his induction as taking place in **1661**, while a detailed historical text states he was appointed in **1660** [1, 2].

## Significance in the Marie Lairre Theory

[[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] draws attention to Playne's departure date (1662) in his analysis of the alleged murder of [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]], which Planchette communications dated to 17 May 1667.

If the murder occurred in 1667, Playne had already been absent from the Rectory for five years. The Borley parsonage — potentially still habitable — would therefore have been **unoccupied by its rector**, creating the conditions under which a young woman could be housed there secretly, and subsequently murdered and buried under the cellar floor, without the incumbent having any knowledge of it.

Phythian-Adams argued that Playne's sudden transfer by the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegraves]] might assume a **"sinister significance"** [4]. He theorised that a member of the Waldegrave family deliberately transplanted Playne to ensure Borley Rectory remained unoccupied by the clergy [4]. This allowed the empty building to be used "for more secular purposes" — namely, to secretly house the young French woman, and subsequently to bury her corpse beneath the cellar floor without a resident rector asking questions [3, 4]. Phythian-Adams's argument is that both Playne's transfer (1662) and his successor [[thomas-muriell|Thomas Muriell]]'s similar arrangement (1681 onward) may have been orchestrated by the Waldegrave family to ensure the Rectory remained without a resident clergyman for the crucial decades.

> **Note:** There is no direct evidence that Playne's departure was anything other than an ordinary non-residential plurality arrangement, common in the seventeenth century. Phythian-Adams's reading is inferential.
