# William Playne

Rector of Borley from 1660 to 1680 (died 1680, still holding the living). Appointed in 1660 by Philip Waldegrave *per curatores* (with the patron not of age). In 1662, Playne moved to Langenhoo — another incumbency in the Waldegrave gift — abandoning residence at Borley while technically remaining its rector.

## Significance in the Marie Lairre Theory

[[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] draws attention to Playne's departure date (1662) in his analysis of the murder of [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]], alleged by Planchette to have occurred on May 17, 1667.

If the murder occurred in 1667, Playne had already been absent from the Rectory for five years. The Borley Rectory — potentially still habitable — would therefore have been **unoccupied by its rector**, creating the conditions under which a young woman could be housed there secretly, and subsequently murdered and buried under the cellar floor, without the incumbent having any knowledge of it.

Phythian-Adams's argument is that both Playne's transfer (1662) and his successor [[thomas-muriell|Thomas Muriell]]'s similar arrangement (1681 onward) may have been orchestrated by the Waldegrave family — who held the patronage — to ensure the Rectory remained without a resident clergyman for the crucial decades.

> **Note:** There is no direct evidence that Playne's departure was anything other than an ordinary non-residential plurality arrangement, common in the seventeenth century. Phythian-Adams's reading is inferential.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**William Playne** was a seventeenth-century Rector of Borley whose tenure and unusual relocation provided a central piece of circumstantial evidence for one of the most famous theories surrounding the Borley Rectory haunting. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, dates, and sinister theoretical connections:

**Dates of Incumbency and Contradictions**
*   **Appointment:** William Playne was appointed Rector of Borley by Philip Waldegrave *per curatores* (meaning through guardians or caretakers, which suggested that the Waldegrave patron was not of age at the time) [1]. 
*   **Contradictory Dates:** The sources contain a minor contradiction regarding the start of his tenure. A formal list of Borley rectors records his induction as taking place in **1661**, while a detailed historical text states he was appointed in **1660** [1, 2]. 
*   **Death:** He died in **1680**, at which point he was succeeded by Thomas Muriell (or Murrell) [1, 2]. 

**The Relocation to Langenhoe**
The most significant event of Playne's incumbency occurred in **1662**, when he left Borley and went to Langenhoe (spelled "Langenhoo" in the texts) [1]. Langenhoe was another parish whose living was in the gift of the Waldegrave family [1]. Despite moving away, Playne remained officially the Rector of Borley until his death in 1680 [1]. 

**Connection to the "Marie Lairre" Murder Theory**
Playne's relocation is a critical element in the theory put forward by Canon W. J. Phythian-Adams to explain the murder of the phantom French nun, "Marie Lairre" [1, 3]. 
*   **The Empty Rectory:** Phythian-Adams noted that because Playne moved to Langenhoe in 1662, the parsonage at Borley was left untenanted [4]. Consequently, when Marie Lairre was allegedly murdered in **1667**, the rectory was still highly habitable, yet completely empty [3]. 
*   **The "Sinister Significance":** The Canon argued that Playne's sudden transfer by the Waldegraves might assume a **"sinister significance"** [4]. He theorized that a member of the Waldegrave family deliberately transplanted Playne to ensure Borley Rectory remained unoccupied by the clergy [4]. This allowed the empty building to be used "for more secular purposes"—namely, to secretly house the young French woman, and subsequently provided a safe place to bury her corpse beneath the cellar floor without a resident rector asking questions [3, 4].
