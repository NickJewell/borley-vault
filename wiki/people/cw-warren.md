# C. W. Warren

A local tradesman and postmaster who, alongside his wife [[emily-warren|Emily]], organised the [[marks-tey-spiritualist-circle|Marks Tey Circle]] — a spiritualist group whose members came from across East Anglia, though the Warrens were the only members who actually resided in the village of Marks Tey, near Colchester [1–4]. Warren served as the principal psychic at the Marks Tey séance of 26 April 1932 and played a central role in what the Circle claimed was the successful laying of the Borley ghost. He has since died.

## Initial Contact and the January 1932 Visit

In **January 1932**, having heard of the intense poltergeist activity at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], Warren wrote to the Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] offering the Circle's help to rid the house of its ghosts [5, 6]. [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] initially objected to his intervention, frustrated by previous investigators who had accused her of faking the phenomena [6, 7]. After Lionel declined the offer, Warren sent a polite follow-up letter clarifying his desire to help, and Lionel agreed to a visit strictly on the condition that Warren would not openly accuse Marianne of fraud [5, 8].

Warren subsequently arrived at the Rectory with his colleague [[hf-frost|H. H. Frost]], claiming they had received an urgent message from their spirit guide, W. T. Stead, warning that Marianne's "band of helpers on the other side" were failing and she was in danger [9]. Warren and his group returned on the night of **23 January 1932**, accompanied by Mrs. Warren (who brought a planchette) and the medium [[guy-lestrange|Guy L'Estrange]] [10–12]. This visit resulted in a tremendous display of smashing glass and flying bottles, after which the house reportedly became quiet [10, 11].

## The April 1932 Séance

While [[guy-lestrange|Guy L'Estrange]] acted as the medium during the January visit, **Warren himself served as the principal psychic** during a later séance held on **26 April 1932** [1]. During this sitting, he was apparently controlled by the spirit of the Borley nun, who identified herself as **"Evangeline Westcott"** (with a "John Westcott" — presumably her father — also mentioned) [13–15]. This is the only named appearance of "Evangeline Westcott" in the Borley record, and it remains entirely unverified.

While in a trance state, Warren reverently made the sign of the cross, gestured as if displaying a veil and rosary, and nervously reached out to his spirit control, "Zenith," for help [14]. Captain [[captain-deane|V. M. Deane]] records his performance in detail. Persuaded by the sitters to use a planchette, the possessed Warren wrote: "Virgin! Do forgive me! Oh, Mother of Jesus, help me" [14].

Speaking in the character of the nun, Warren delivered the following dramatic statement:

> *"How long since that living tomb? I did no wrong: my Father Confessor thought me guilty. Ah, the walls! I was thirsty and they gave me empty bottles! Oh, those bottles!... Am I now to go to the Judgment throne, the great white throne?... What will the Father Confessor say? He will not forgive me. Bring me spring flowers."* [14]

Mrs. Warren reportedly brought a bowl of primroses to the table, and Warren buried his face in them, smiling and enjoying their scent, before taking a small bunch and relinquishing the spirit's control [14].

## The Successful Laying

After this séance, [[captain-deane]] and the Circle asserted that "the ghost had been successfully laid" — claiming that from that point the phenomena at Borley ceased. The Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Foyster]] confirmed in his diary that on the morning of 24 January 1932, "the house was entirely different," and that with two exceptions "demonstrations definitely stopped until 1935."

> **Contradiction:** [[harry-price|Price]] noted that while phenomena did become quieter after January 1932, they did not cease entirely, and that the cessation may have been coincidental rather than caused by the Marks Tey séance.

## Contradictions and Later Life

Following the Marks Tey Circle's apparent success, a bitter dispute broke out among the investigators [16]. [[guy-lestrange|Guy L'Estrange]] wrote an account of the exorcism that attempted to completely omit the Warrens from the historical record so that he could claim sole credit for laying the ghost [16]. However, [[captain-deane|Captain Deane]] fiercely defended Warren, insisting that the Warrens and the rest of the Circle deserved the credit for stopping the manifestations [16].

A tragic postscript concludes Warren's involvement. Shortly after these events, **Mr. Warren fell severely ill and died, attributing his fatal illness directly to the evil spirits he had encountered at Borley Rectory** [16] (single witness — Deane's account).
