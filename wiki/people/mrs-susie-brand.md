# Mrs Susie J. Brand

Of St Feock, Cornwall. Writing in two letters (January 5 and 13, 1943), Mrs Brand provided the only first-hand testimony about the alleged "nunnery at Bures" — a key element in the [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] Planchette narrative.

## The Bures Nunnery

The [[communicating-entities|Planchette scripts]] produced by [[helen-glanville|Helen Glanville]] and others repeatedly referred to a "nunnery at Bures," about seven miles from Borley, as the place from which "Marie Lairre" came before being taken to England by a Waldegrave. Several correspondents challenged this as pure fiction — no nunnery at Bures appeared in any historical record.

Mrs Brand changed that. She and her husband had lived at Bures for eleven years (1910–21), and she wrote:

> *"The nunnery was then used as a private house."*

In her second letter she added that the nunnery had "no particular interest for her" at the time, "excepting from the view of being picturesque." She was trying to procure a photograph.

## Significance

This is the only existing testimony that a building in Bures was locally known as "the nunnery" within living memory. It does not establish that a functioning religious house ever existed there, nor that it has any specific connection to 1667. But it supports the possibility that the Planchette reference to "Bures nunnery" drew on a genuine local tradition rather than inventing one from nothing.

Mrs Brand's family connection to the Bulls (her father-in-law Edmund Brand was a member of the Suffolk and Essex Hunt with the Rev. Henry Bull) meant she had independent knowledge of the area and the family, predating Price's investigation.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mrs. Susie J. Brand** of St. Feock, Cornwall, appears in the Borley Rectory sources as a correspondent of Harry Price who provided him with historical context regarding a potential "nunnery" at the nearby village of Bures, thereby offering possible support for the "Marie Lairre" ghost nun legend [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following biographical details, dates, and quotes regarding her involvement:

**Biographical Details and Associations**
*   **Residence:** She and her husband lived in the village of Bures for eleven years, from **1910 to 1921** [2]. By 1943, she was residing in **St. Feock, Cornwall** [1]. 
*   **The Bull Family Connection:** Mrs. Brand and her family were personal friends of the Bulls [1]. She had known Borley Rectory for many years [1]. 
*   **Her Father-in-Law:** Her father-in-law, **Edmund Brand**, was a member of the **Suffolk and Essex Hunt**, as was the original builder of the Rectory, the Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull [1]. 

**The Bures "Nunnery" Correspondence (January 1943)**
Mrs. Brand’s primary contribution to the Borley investigation centers on information she sent to Harry Price regarding a supposed convent at Bures. Skeptics had declared the "nunnery at Bures" to be a complete myth, which posed a problem for the planchette scripts that claimed the ghost of Marie Lairre was a novice abducted from a convent at Bures [1]. 

Mrs. Brand provided Price with evidence—which he himself admitted was "not very impressive"—that there had indeed been an establishment at Bures that might have been designated a nunnery, convent school, or temporary resting place for nuns coming from the Continent [1]. 
*   In a "long and interesting letter" dated **January 5, 1943**, Mrs. Brand informed Price about her time living in Bures, explicitly stating: **"The nunnery was then used as a private house"** [1, 2]. 
*   In a follow-up letter dated **January 13, 1943**, she elaborated that during her eleven years in the village, the old nunnery building had held no particular interest for her, providing the direct quote: **"excepting from the view of being picturesque"** [2].

**Attempt to Procure Photographic Evidence**
Mrs. Brand actively tried to assist Price's investigation by attempting to procure a photograph of the Bures nunnery for him, though the texts note that at the time of publication she "has not yet succeeded" [2]. Nevertheless, Price used Mrs. Brand's letters to argue that some house in Bures had at one time been used for conventual purposes, which could account for the "nunnery" element of the Borley legend [2].
