# Mrs Susie J. Brand

Of St Feock, Cornwall. She and her husband had lived in the village of [[bures-en-bray|Bures]] for eleven years (1910–21); by 1943 she was residing in St Feock. Writing in two letters (January 5 and 13, 1943), Mrs Brand provided the only first-hand testimony about the alleged "nunnery at Bures" — a key element in the [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] planchette narrative.

## Family Connection to Borley

Mrs Brand and her family were personal friends of the Bulls, and she had known [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] for many years. Her father-in-law, **Edmund Brand**, was a member of the **Suffolk and Essex Hunt**, as was the original builder of the Rectory, the Rev. [[henry-bull|Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] — giving her independent knowledge of the area and the family predating Price's investigation.

## The Bures Nunnery

The [[communicating-entities|planchette scripts]] produced by [[helen-glanville|Helen Glanville]] and others repeatedly referred to a "nunnery at Bures," about seven miles from Borley, as the place from which "Marie Lairre" came before being taken to England by a Waldegrave. Several correspondents challenged this as pure fiction — no nunnery at Bures appeared in any historical record.

Mrs Brand changed that. In a "long and interesting letter" dated **January 5, 1943**, she informed [[harry-price|Price]] about her time living in Bures, explicitly stating: **"The nunnery was then used as a private house."**

In a follow-up letter dated **January 13, 1943**, she elaborated that during her eleven years in the village, the old nunnery building had held no particular interest for her **"excepting from the view of being picturesque."**

Mrs Brand actively attempted to assist Price's investigation by trying to procure a photograph of the Bures nunnery building, though she had not yet succeeded at the time of Price's publication.

## Significance

This is the only existing testimony that a building in Bures was locally known as "the nunnery" within living memory. Price himself admitted that her evidence was "not very impressive," but used it to argue that some house in Bures had at one time been used for conventual purposes — which could account for the "nunnery" element of the Borley legend. Mrs Brand's letters do not establish that a functioning religious house ever existed there, nor that it has any specific connection to the year 1667. They support the possibility that the planchette reference to "Bures nunnery" drew on a genuine local tradition rather than inventing one from nothing.
