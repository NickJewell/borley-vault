# Kathleen Mary ('Mollie') Goldney (23 July 1893 – 27 February 1992)

Also known as Mrs A. Peel Goldney. Long-standing [[spr|SPR]] Council Member and Vice-President from 1965; co-author of the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] (1956); MBE for war work (1945). Lived to 98.

## Background

Born on July 23, 1893, Mollie was raised in India where her father was a Civil Service judge. Her husband was involved with the Indian railway network before returning to England to run an artificial insemination clinic. She qualified as a midwife and suffered significant personal tragedies: her first child died at birth, and her elder daughter was killed in a car crash. She joined the SPR in December 1927 after writing to Sir Oliver Lodge about fire-walking experiments, joined the Council in 1945, and became Vice-President in 1965. She became Honorary Treasurer of [[harry-price|Harry Price's]] [[national-laboratory|National Laboratory of Psychical Research]] and Honorary Secretary of the [[ghost-club|Ghost Club]]. Attended séances with [[rudi-schneider|Rudi Schneider]], Helen Duncan, and [[eileen-garrett|Eileen Garrett]]; participated in the R101 séance. In 1945 she was awarded an MBE for her war work. She died on February 27, 1992, at the age of 98.

## Personality

[[nandor-fodor|Nandor Fodor]] scathingly described Goldney as having a **"steam-roller personality, capable of crushing any witness and any independent opinion around her,"** adding that she was "eminently capable of convincing almost any witness that he did not see what he saw" and that he was haunted by the "pitiful state in which she left her victim" after hours of cross-examination. Her epitaph in the psychical research community was that she was "always sincere but never infallible."

## Relationship with Harry Price

Goldney worked closely with Price for over a decade before the SPR investigation. She visited Borley with him in October 1931 (the famous visit when Price accused [[marianne-foyster]] of fakery) and apparently took a strong dislike to Marianne. Per Marianne's later testimony (Swanson interviews, 1958), she and Price were conducting an affair at the Rectory — a charge Marianne was known to embellish, but which [[eileen-garrett]] mentioned in correspondence with [[trevor-hall]]. Marianne alleged she had caught Goldney and Price behind the butler's pantry door "in the throes of having a mild romance," with Price's arms around Goldney and her dress reportedly hiked up, revealing she was wearing "pink pants... the kind that we used to call 'bird cages.'" Marianne claimed her amused sniggering was the true reason Goldney took such a "violent dislike" to her (single witness; disputed).

## Direct Observations at Borley (1931)

KMG (as she is referred to throughout the SPR Report, being one of its three authors) was present at the October 13, 1931 visit with Price and two other women. During Marianne's apparent "fainting fit," Goldney noted that the young rector's wife looked "extremely vital" and gave "the impression of a girl whose interests lay in hockey-playing and so on." Utilizing her medical training, she felt Marianne's pulse (which "seemed to be normal") and declared to her husband: **"There's nothing wrong with her."** She saw "no signs of genuine physical distress."

Other direct observations from that visit:

- She witnessed Marianne fling herself on her knees and pray aloud to St Anthony for vindication after the investigators were about to leave
- Wine was allegedly transformed into ink; Goldney immediately recognized it as a trivial conjurer's trick and reportedly "roared with laughter," signalling to Price her belief that Marianne was faking
- She observed the bell-ringing incident (possibly [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide]] operating the wire) that remained unexplained
- She was present when a bottle was thrown down the stairs immediately after Marianne went upstairs alone

## Role in the Borley Report

Recruited by [[william-salter]] to examine Price's Borley involvement. She was the primary author of the sections on the Smith and Foyster incumbencies, and the most assiduous fieldworker among the three authors — conducting or participating in the majority of the witness interviews. She was influenced by Salter's institutional motives and personal antipathy towards Price, but her own direct observations at Borley in 1931 gave her specific credibility that her co-authors lacked.

On learning Mrs Smith's full account in 1949 and its allegations of Price faking phenomena, Goldney wrote in her private notes: **"I admit I am shocked at Mrs Smith's statement, for, though I credited [Price] with intellectual dishonesty, I had not imagined he would ever himself stoop to fraudulent actions."**

Her most egregious specific act during the investigation: visiting [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] in his abbot's presence on the day the Report was published, and allegedly threatening to "bring up unpleasant facts about his health" if he defended [[marianne-foyster]]. Whitehouse had suffered a breakdown in 1924/1925 — which she mistakenly assumed had occurred during his 1931 Borley visits, an error she later had to acknowledge, distorting the Report's treatment of his testimony.

She also obtained [[cynthia-ledsham|Cynthia Thompson's]] (née Ledsham) signature on a statement discrediting Price's "flying brick" photograph during a hastily written lunch meeting — a method that later critics argued allowed glaring inconsistencies to slip into the testimony.

## Private Doubts

Despite dedicating years to exposing Borley as a mixture of fraud and legend, Goldney told [[peter-underwood]] in front of witnesses at an SPR meeting that she had **"no doubt whatsoever that genuine paranormal activity did take place at Borley"** — directly contradicting the Report she had co-authored. Within months of the *Borley Report* being published, Goldney had a severe falling out with her co-author [[trevor-hall]] and was no longer on speaking terms with him. She grew to regret her association with the Report towards the end of her long life.
