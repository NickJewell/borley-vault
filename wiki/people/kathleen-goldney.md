# Kathleen Mary ('Mollie') Goldney (23 July 1893 – 27 February 1992)

Also known as Mrs A. Peel Goldney. Long-standing SPR Council Member and Vice-President from 1965; co-author of the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] (1956); MBE for war work (1945). Lived to 98.

## Background

Raised in India where her father was a civil service judge. Qualified as a midwife; joined the SPR in December 1927 after writing to Sir Oliver Lodge about fire-walking experiments. Became Honorary Treasurer of [[harry-price|Harry Price's]] [[national-laboratory|National Laboratory of Psychical Research]] and Honorary Secretary of the [[ghost-club|Ghost Club]]. Attended séances with Rudi Schneider, Helen Duncan, and Eileen Garrett; participated in the R101 séance. Described by Nandor Fodor as having a "steam-roller personality" capable of "crushing any witness or independent opinion."

## Relationship with Harry Price

Goldney worked closely with Price for over a decade before the SPR investigation. She visited Borley with him in October 1931 (the famous visit when Price accused [[marianne-foyster]] of fakery) and apparently took a strong dislike to Marianne. Per Marianne's later testimony (Swanson interviews, 1958), she and Price were conducting an affair at the Rectory — a charge Marianne was known to embellish, but which [[eileen-garrett]] mentioned in correspondence with [[trevor-hall]].

## Direct Observations at Borley (1931)

KMG (as she is referred to throughout the SPR Report, being one of its three authors) was present at the October 1931 visit with Price and two other women. Her direct observations from that visit, recorded in KMG's notes, are significant:

- During Marianne's apparent "fainting fit" she took the opportunity to feel her pulse, which "seemed to be normal"; KMG had hospital training and saw "no signs of genuine physical distress"
- She witnessed Marianne fling herself on her knees and pray aloud to St Anthony for vindication after the investigators were about to leave
- She observed the bell-ringing incident (possibly Adelaide operating the wire) that remained unexplained
- She was present when the bottle was thrown down the stairs immediately after Marianne went upstairs alone

KMG's private notes (1931): "I admit I am shocked at Mrs Smith's statement, for, though I credited [Price] with intellectual dishonesty, I had not imagined he would ever himself stoop to fraudulent actions" — written on learning Mrs Smith's full account in 1949.

## Role in the Borley Report

Recruited by [[william-salter]] to examine Price's Borley involvement. She was the primary author of the sections on the Smith and Foyster incumbencies. She was the most assiduous fieldworker among the three authors — conducting or participating in the majority of the witness interviews. She was influenced by Salter's institutional motives and personal antipathy towards Price, but her own direct observations at Borley in 1931 gave her specific credibility that her co-authors lacked.

Her most egregious specific act: interviewing [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] in his abbot's presence on the day the Report was published, and threatening to "bring up unpleasant facts about his health" if he defended [[marianne-foyster]]. Whitehouse had suffered a breakdown in 1925 — which she misidentified as recent, distorting the Report's treatment of his testimony.

## Private Doubts

Goldney told [[peter-underwood]] in front of witnesses at an SPR meeting that she had "no doubt whatsoever that genuine paranormal activity did take place at Borley" — directly contradicting the Report she had co-authored. She grew to regret her association with it, and towards the end of her life was not on speaking terms with [[trevor-hall]].

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Kathleen Mary Goldney (née Peel)**, widely known as "Mollie" or Mrs. A. Peel Goldney, was a prominent psychical researcher and one of the three co-authors of the highly critical 1956 *Borley Report* (*The Haunting of Borley Rectory*), published by the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) [1, 2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, investigation tactics, specific quotes, and contradictions regarding her long association with the case:

**Lesser-Known Biographical Details**
*   **Early Life and Career:** Born on July 23, 1893, Mollie was raised in India, where her father was a Civil Service judge [1]. Her husband was involved with the Indian railway network before returning to England to run an artificial insemination clinic [1]. Mollie was a qualified midwife [1, 3]. 
*   **Tragedies and Honors:** She suffered significant personal tragedies; her first child died at birth, and her elder daughter was killed in a car crash [4]. In 1945, she was awarded an MBE for her war work [1]. She died on February 27, 1992, at the age of 98 [1, 5].
*   **Psychical Research:** Her interest in the paranormal was sparked by a correspondence with physicist Sir Oliver Lodge regarding fire-walking [1, 3]. She joined the SPR in 1927, joined the Council in 1945, and became Vice-President in 1965 [1]. She also worked closely with Harry Price, serving as the Honorary Treasurer of his National Laboratory of Psychical Research (NLPR) [6]. 

**Personality and "Steam-Roller" Reputation**
Goldney was a formidable, highly capable, and polarizing figure. The psychoanalyst and psychical researcher Dr. Nandor Fodor scathingly described her as having a **"steam-roller personality, capable of crushing any witness and any independent opinion around her"** [7, 8]. Fodor added that she was **"eminently capable of convincing almost any witness that he did not see what he saw"** and that he was haunted by the "pitiful state in which she left her victim" after hours of cross-examination [8]. Her epitaph in the psychical research community was that she was **"always sincere but never infallible"** [4].

**The 1931 Visit and the Marianne Foyster Feud**
Goldney's direct involvement with Borley Rectory began on October 13, 1931, when she accompanied Harry Price to investigate the phenomena occurring under the Rev. Lionel Foyster and his wife, Marianne [9, 10]. 
*   **Medical Assessment:** Utilizing her medical training, Goldney was highly skeptical of Marianne’s claims of ill health. She noted that the young rector's wife looked "extremely vital" and gave **"the impression of a girl whose interests lay in hockey-playing and so on"** [11]. When Marianne collapsed in an apparent faint, Goldney administered first aid, took her pulse (which was normal), and dismissively declared to her husband, **"There's nothing wrong with her"** [12, 13]. 
*   **The "Wine into Ink" Trick:** During this visit, wine was mysteriously transformed into ink. Goldney immediately recognized it as a trivial conjurer's trick and "roared with laughter," signaling to Price her belief that Marianne was faking the phenomena [14]. 
*   **Marianne's Salacious Allegations:** Marianne later retaliated against Goldney's hostility with scandalous claims. In a 1958 interview, Marianne alleged that she had caught Goldney and Price behind the butler's pantry door **"in the throes of having a mild romance."** According to Marianne, Price had his arms around Goldney, whose dress was "hiked up," revealing that she was wearing **"pink pants... the kind that we used to call 'bird cages'"** [15]. Marianne claimed her amused sniggering at this sight was the true reason Goldney took such a "violent dislike" to her [15]. 

**The Borley Report and Investigation Tactics**
Following Price’s death, Goldney co-authored the 1956 *Borley Report* with Dr. Eric J. Dingwall and Trevor H. Hall, which accused Price of fraud and misrepresentation [2, 16]. During her research, Goldney’s aggressive interviewing tactics were frequently noted:
*   **Shock at Price's Alleged Fraud:** When she interviewed the former rector's wife, Mabel Smith, in 1949 and heard allegations of Price faking phenomena, Goldney wrote: **"I credited [Price] with intellectual dishonesty, I had not imagined he would ever himself stoop to fraudulent actions"** [17].
*   **Threatening a Monk:** In an attempt to silence Price's supporters, Goldney visited Dom Richard Whitehouse (formerly Edwin Whitehouse) at St Augustine’s Abbey on the day of the report's publication. She allegedly threatened that **"if he defended Marianne they would bring up unpleasant facts about his health"** (referring to a past nervous breakdown) [18]. However, Goldney bungled the timeline, mistakenly assuming his breakdown occurred during his 1931 Borley visits, when in fact it had occurred in 1924/1925—an error she later had to acknowledge [19].
*   **Lunchtime Confessions:** She obtained Cynthia Thompson's (née Ledsham) signature on a statement discrediting Price's "flying brick" photograph during a hastily written lunch meeting, a method that later critics argued allowed glaring inconsistencies to slip into the testimony [20, 21].

**Contradictions and Later Regrets**
Despite dedicating years to exposing Borley Rectory as a mixture of fraud and legend, Goldney harbored profound personal contradictions regarding the case:
*   **Belief in the Haunting:** In front of witnesses at an SPR meeting, Goldney stunningly admitted to Peter Underwood that **she had "no doubt whatsoever that genuine paranormal activity did take place at Borley,"** even though she doubted the honesty of Harry Price [22]. 
*   **Regret:** Within months of the *Borley Report* being published, Goldney had a severe falling out with her co-author Trevor Hall and was no longer on speaking terms with him. Towards the end of her long life, she openly regretted her association with the *Borley Report* [22].
