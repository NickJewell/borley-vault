# Douglas Craggs

Vice president of the **Magic Circle** and the first person to publicly denounce [[harry-price|Harry Price]] after his death in March 1948.

## Rejected by Harry Price (1937)

When [[harry-price|Harry Price]] placed an advertisement in *The Times* on 25 May 1937, seeking "intelligent, competent and cultured strangers" to act as official observers during a year-long investigation of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], Craggs and several other members of the Magic Circle applied to be considered [1]. Price was determined to recruit "enthusiastic amateurs" rather than professional psychical researchers, and was firm that spiritualists should be strictly excluded [1]. Consequently, the appeals of Craggs and his fellow Magic Circle members were rejected [1].

> O'Connor characterises Craggs's denunciation as driven at least partly by professional pique: he had been shut out of Price's investigation.

## The Denunciation

Within twenty-four hours of Price's death, Craggs made an extraordinarily contentious claim to the press: Price had "bamboozled the public into believing things in which he placed no credence whatsoever."

The [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] later noted the Magic Circle exclusion as suspicious: Price was supposedly conducting a scientific investigation, so why exclude expert illusionists and conjurors who would be best placed to detect trickery?

## Contribution to the SPR *Borley Report*

Although excluded from Price's on-site investigation, Craggs later assisted the critics who sought to debunk Price's findings. After Price's death, [[eric-dingwall|Dr. Eric J. Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen M. Goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Trevor H. Hall]] conducted their highly critical re-examination of the Borley case for the [[spr|Society for Psychical Research]], published in 1956 as *The Haunting of Borley Rectory* [2, 3]. In the preface to their report, the authors explicitly state that they were "much indebted for information and advice" to Douglas Craggs, listing him among a select group of individuals who helped them compile their case against Price [3].

## Significance

Craggs's immediate post-mortem attack opened the floodgates for the subsequent campaign against Price's reputation led by [[william-salter|Salter]], [[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]]. His claim that Price had cynically deceived the public, combined with [[charles-sutton|Charles Sutton]]'s later published accusation (the *Inky Way Annual*, December 1948), formed the opening salvos of what became the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]].
