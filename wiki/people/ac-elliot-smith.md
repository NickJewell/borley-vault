# A. C. Elliot Smith

Master at Harrow School; visited Borley Rectory on July 28–29, 1937 alongside the Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|A. C. Henning]], coinciding with a period when [[mark-kerr-pearse]] was in residence. He had no dramatic personal experiences but wrote one of the most analytically useful letters in the observer file.

## The Visit

Arrived about 4 p.m. on July 28, accompanied Kerr-Pearse on a house inspection, sat in the summer-house until 10:40 p.m. hoping to see the "nun" on the traditional anniversary of her appearance — and was disappointed (as Price also was on his own July 28 visits). He left for London the next morning after a "not very exciting time."

## Analytical Observations

Despite the negative phenomenal result, Elliot Smith wrote a letter to Price listing five incisive analytical observations about the pencil marks:

1. Though some marks might be fakes or normal pencil marks, "I find it difficult to believe the whole lot are. Apart from the testimony of a variety of people and the difficulty of always escaping observation, because a faker would surely endeavour to be more sensational and not content himself with mere jabs."
2. Most marks seem to be at either 4 feet or 2 feet 6 inches from the ground, or just over a mantelpiece — a consistent height pattern worth investigating.
3. "Have you ever shown the specimens to a handwriting expert? I find it difficult to believe that the usual 'Marianne,' the single 'Marianne' and 'Edwin' are all by the same hand."
4. "Accepting as an hypothesis a discarnate spirit or psychic factor, where does it get the material lead? Can it sharpen a pencil?"
5. "I had no feeling of atmosphere."

Point 4 — the "material lead" question — was taken up directly by Price in Chapter 24 and attributed as originating partly with Elliot Smith and partly with Dr. C.E.M. Joad, who raised it independently. Price uses it to frame his defence of the paranormal pencil-mark hypothesis.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**A. C. Elliot Smith** was a schoolmaster at Harrow School who participated as an official observer during Harry Price's year-long investigation of Borley Rectory [1]. 

**Visit to Borley Rectory (July 28, 1937)**
Elliot Smith visited the Rectory on July 28, 1937, a date specifically chosen because it was traditionally regarded as the anniversary of the phantom nun's appearance [1]. He arrived at approximately 4:00 p.m. and immediately took a comprehensive tour of the house with Mark Kerr-Pearse, who was the observer in residence at the time [2]. 

That evening, Elliot Smith joined Kerr-Pearse and the Rev. A. C. Henning in the Rectory's large summer-house to keep watch over the "Nun's Walk." However, the vigil proved uneventful; the group waited until 10:40 p.m. but were disappointed when the apparition failed to appear [1]. Elliot Smith left for London the following morning after what Harry Price described as a "not very exciting time" [3].

**Observations and Letter to Harry Price**
Despite the lack of spectacular phenomena, Elliot Smith wrote a highly analytical letter to Harry Price on July 29, 1937, detailing his impressions. His logical, five-point tabulated assessment focused heavily on the mysterious "wall-writings" and raised several practical questions about the nature of the hauntings [2]:

1. **Authenticity of the Markings:** While allowing that some marks might be fakes or normal pencil marks, he found it difficult to believe the entirety were fraudulent. He reasoned that escaping observation would be difficult, and additionally noted: "because a faker would surely endeavour to be more sensational and not content himself with mere jabs" [2].
2. **Location of the Writings:** He observed a distinct pattern in the placement of the marks, noting that the majority seemed to be positioned "either 4 feet or 2 feet 6 inches from the ground or just over a mantelpiece" [3].
3. **Graphological Analysis:** Elliot Smith questioned whether the writings had ever been shown to a handwriting expert. He expressed strong doubts that the different messages—specifically the "usual 'Marianne'," the "single 'Marianne'," and the word "'Edwin'"—were executed by the same hand [3].
4. **The Logistics of a Ghostly Pencil:** Demonstrating a highly practical mindset, he challenged the hypothesis that a discarnate spirit or psychic factor was responsible by asking: "where does it get the material lead? Can it sharpen a pencil?" [3].
5. **Lack of Paranormal Atmosphere:** In sharp contrast to other observers who reported feelings of terror, icy chills, or an overwhelming sense of evil in the house, Elliot Smith bluntly concluded his list with: "I had no feeling of atmosphere" [3].

Harry Price valued Elliot Smith's objective and questioning approach, noting that his queries regarding the pencil marks were addressed elsewhere in his investigation, though Price himself admitted that July 28 had historically always been an uneventful date in his own experience [3].
