# A. C. Elliot Smith

Schoolmaster at Harrow School; visited [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] on **July 28–29, 1937** alongside the Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|A. C. Henning]], coinciding with a period when [[mark-kerr-pearse]] was in residence [1]. He had no dramatic personal experiences but wrote one of the most analytically useful letters in the observer file.

## The Visit

Elliot Smith arrived at approximately 4 p.m. on July 28 — a date specifically chosen because it was traditionally regarded as the anniversary of the phantom nun's appearance [1]. He immediately took a comprehensive tour of the house with Kerr-Pearse, who was the observer in residence at the time [2]. That evening, he joined Kerr-Pearse and the Rev. Henning in the Rectory's large summer-house to keep watch over the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] until 10:40 p.m. — and was disappointed when the apparition failed to appear, as Price also was on his own July 28 visits [1]. He left for London the next morning after what Price described as a "not very exciting time" [3].

## Analytical Observations

Despite the negative phenomenal result, Elliot Smith wrote a highly analytical letter to Price on July 29, 1937, detailing his impressions. His logical, five-point assessment focused on the mysterious [[wall-writings|wall-writings]] and raised several practical questions about the nature of the hauntings [2]:

1. **Authenticity of the markings**: While allowing that some marks might be fakes or normal pencil marks, he found it difficult to believe the entirety were fraudulent, reasoning that "escaping observation would be difficult, and additionally... a faker would surely endeavour to be more sensational and not content himself with mere jabs" [2].
2. **Location of the writings**: He observed a distinct pattern in the placement of the marks, noting that the majority seemed to be positioned "either 4 feet or 2 feet 6 inches from the ground or just over a mantelpiece" — a consistent height pattern he considered worth investigating [3].
3. **Graphological analysis**: Elliot Smith questioned whether the writings had ever been shown to a handwriting expert. He expressed strong doubts that the different messages — specifically the "usual 'Marianne'," the "single 'Marianne'," and the word "'Edwin'" — were executed by the same hand [3].
4. **The logistics of a ghostly pencil**: Demonstrating a highly practical mindset, he challenged the hypothesis that a discarnate spirit or psychic factor was responsible by asking: "where does it get the material lead? Can it sharpen a pencil?" [3].
5. **Lack of paranormal atmosphere**: In sharp contrast to other observers who reported feelings of terror, icy chills, or an overwhelming sense of evil in the house, he bluntly concluded: "I had no feeling of atmosphere" [3].

Point 4 — the "material lead" question — was taken up directly by Price in Chapter 24 and attributed as originating partly with Elliot Smith and partly with [[cem-joad|Dr. C.E.M. Joad]], who raised it independently. Price uses it to frame his defence of the paranormal pencil-mark hypothesis.

Harry Price valued Elliot Smith's objective and questioning approach, noting that his queries were addressed elsewhere in his investigation, though Price himself admitted that July 28 had historically always been an uneventful date in his own experience [3].
