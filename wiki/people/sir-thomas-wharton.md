# Sir Thomas Wharton

An Essex Catholic gentleman arrested alongside [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Sir Edward Waldegrave]] in the spring of 1561, following the confession of the priest [[john-cox-devon|John Cox / John Devon]].

## Background

Sir Thomas Wharton resided at **Newhall, Essex**. Like Waldegrave, he was a committed Catholic who harboured forbidden religious practices during the reign of Elizabeth I.

## Arrest

Cox/Devon confessed to the Justice of the Peace Hugh Darrell that he had celebrated the forbidden Catholic Mass first at **Wharton's house at Newhall**, before proceeding to Waldegrave's at Borley Hall.

The Earl of Oxford's letter to Sir William Cecil, dated around April 19–20, 1561, notes Waldegrave and Wharton together in the context of the arrests. Banks's reading of the *Calendars of State Papers Domestic* indicates that:

- Wharton was charged primarily with **religious offenses** — taking part in Mass and possessing implements of heresy (crucifixes, images of the Virgin Mary, and other articles illegal under Protestant law)
- Waldegrave was in considerably more serious trouble, being found in possession of apparently **treasonable papers** in addition to the heretical religious items

This distinction between the two men suggests that Wharton, while guilty of the same liturgical offenses, was not suspected of involvement in any active plot against Queen Elizabeth — unlike Waldegrave, whose papers hinted at broader Catholic-reformation activities.

## Significance

Wharton is a secondary figure in the Borley story, but his inclusion in the Calendars of State Papers alongside Waldegrave confirms the breadth of Catholic activity in the Essex/Suffolk region at this period. The network of recusant gentry — Wharton, Waldegrave, and others — provides the social context for Waldegrave's use of Borley Hall as a place for dangerous Catholic worship.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Sir Thomas Wharton** was a prominent Catholic living at **Newhall, Essex**, during the Protestant reign of Queen Elizabeth I [1]. He appears in the Borley Rectory sources strictly in connection to the downfall and arrest of Borley's Lord of the Manor, Sir Edward Waldegrave, during a period of intense anti-Catholic persecution [1, 2].

A reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific events, dates, and historical distinctions regarding his involvement:

**The Betrayal and Arrest (Spring 1561)**
*   During the **spring of 1561**, Wharton and Waldegrave were betrayed to the authorities by a fellow Catholic priest named **John Cox (also known as John Devon)** [1, 2].
*   Under interrogation by Hugh Darrell, a Justice of the Peace for Kent, Cox admitted to **celebrating the forbidden Catholic Mass first at Sir Thomas Wharton's home at Newhall**, before subsequently travelling to Waldegrave's house at Borley [1].
*   Following this confession, **both Sir Thomas Wharton and Sir Edward Waldegrave were arrested and brought before the Earl of Oxford at Brentwood**, where they were charged with **heresy and very possibly with treason** [2]. 

**Religious Offenses vs. Treason**
The sources highlight a specific distinction made by the authorities regarding the severity of the crimes committed by Wharton compared to Waldegrave:
*   According to a letter written by the Earl of Oxford, **Sir Thomas Wharton was guilty primarily of religious offenses** [3]. These crimes included participating in the forbidden Mass and being in possession of **"implements of heresy"**, which were specifically identified as **crucifixes, images of Mary, and other such articles** that were strictly illegal under Protestant law [3].
*   In contrast, the Earl of Oxford indicated that Waldegrave was in much more serious trouble because a search of his house had uncovered **"apparently treasonable papers"** [3].
*   However, despite this distinction in the Earl's letter, entries in the *Calendars of State Papers Domestic* relating to the arrest and arraignment of both Wharton and Waldegrave collectively **"hint darkly at various plots against the Queen"**, suggesting the authorities suspected both men might have been involved in a wider Catholic reformation movement [4].
