# Sir Thomas Wharton

A prominent Catholic gentleman residing at **Newhall, Essex**, during the Protestant reign of Queen Elizabeth I [1]. He appears in the Borley Rectory sources strictly in connection to the downfall and arrest of Borley's Lord of the Manor, [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Sir Edward Waldegrave]], during a period of intense anti-Catholic persecution [1, 2].

## Background

Like Waldegrave, Wharton was a committed Catholic who harboured forbidden religious practices. During the **spring of 1561**, both men were betrayed to the authorities by the priest [[john-cox-devon|John Cox (also known as John Devon)]].

## Arrest

Under interrogation by Hugh Darrell, a Justice of the Peace for Kent, Cox admitted to **celebrating the forbidden Catholic Mass first at Sir Thomas Wharton's home at Newhall**, before subsequently travelling to Waldegrave's house at Borley Hall [1]. Following this confession, **both Wharton and Waldegrave were arrested and brought before the Earl of Oxford at Brentwood**, where they were charged with **heresy and very possibly with treason** [2].

A letter written by the Earl of Oxford highlights a specific distinction in the severity of the crimes: **Sir Thomas Wharton was guilty primarily of religious offenses** — participating in the forbidden Mass and being in possession of **"implements of heresy"** (crucifixes, images of Mary, and other articles illegal under Protestant law) [3]. In contrast, Waldegrave was in much more serious trouble because a search of his house had uncovered **"apparently treasonable papers"** [3].

However, entries in the *Calendars of State Papers Domestic* relating to the arrest and arraignment of both men collectively **"hint darkly at various plots against the Queen"** [4] — suggesting the authorities suspected both might have been involved in a wider Catholic reformation movement.

## Significance

Wharton is a secondary figure in the Borley story, but his inclusion in the Calendars of State Papers alongside Waldegrave confirms the breadth of Catholic activity in the Essex/Suffolk region at this period. The network of recusant gentry — Wharton, Waldegrave, and others — provides the social context for Waldegrave's use of Borley Hall as a place for dangerous Catholic worship.
