# Dr Guy Warman

Bishop of Chelmsford 1923–1929; the Church of England official who appointed [[rev-eric-smith|Eric Smith]] to Borley, withheld critical information from him about the post, and dismissed the Smiths' subsequent complaints about the Rectory.

## Character and Theological Stance

Warman is described as a "formidable character and firm administrator" [2, 3]. An evangelical Anglican with progressive views, he notably **refused to wear a bishop's mitre** [2]. In 1905, he co-founded the **Group Brotherhood**, an organisation proposing reform within the Church of England [2]. He was highly pragmatic about the appeal of materialism and paganism following the Great War, had little patience for anxieties surrounding Darwinian theories, and **deeply disliked reactionary Anglo-Catholicism** [2] — a position that put him at immediate odds with the Anglo-Catholic [[rev-eric-smith|Eric Smith]].

## The Appointment of Eric Smith and Withheld Information (Summer 1928)

Warman was given sole responsibility by the Bull family for filling the vacant living at Borley following the death of [[harry-bull|Rev. Harry Foyster Bull]] in 1927 [1]. By the summer of 1928, Warman had failed to secure a new rector and was obliged to offer the living to the Rev. Guy Eric Smith [4]. Their relationship was "cool from the start" and quickly turned "icy," largely because Smith's Anglo-Catholicism clashed directly with Warman's progressive ambitions for his diocese [4]. The Bull family were also unhappy with Warman's choice of Smith for the parish [5].

Warman did not tell Smith that the position had stood vacant since Harry Bull's death in 1927, nor that it had already been refused by twelve other clergy — making Smith the **thirteenth candidate** [4].

> **Contradiction:** Warman's failure to inform Smith of the position's toxic history is cited by O'Connor as a significant act of institutional bad faith that set the Borley drama in motion.

## Dismissal of the "Haunting" and the Smiths' Complaints

When [[rev-eric-smith|Eric]] and [[mabel-smith|Mabel Smith]] eventually appealed to Dr Warman for a transfer to another parish — citing the intolerable, dilapidated living conditions and the rumours of ghosts — Warman flatly refused [3]. He viewed the Smiths as being "needy and neurotic in equal measure" and felt they were stirring up an "unnecessary 'mare's nest'" [3]. He noted that the previous rector, [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]], had lived happily at the Rectory for over thirty years without making any fuss about ghosts [3].

His unyielding ultimatum to Eric Smith was "Borley or nothing" [6].

## Secret Plans and Departure to Manchester (1928–1929)

Warman's disdain for the Smiths was partly driven by his own career trajectory; he was scheduled to take over as the **Bishop of Manchester in early 1929**, leaving him with little patience for local parish difficulties [6].

Furthermore, behind closed doors in November 1928, Warman and the Ecclesiastical Commission had secretly agreed that the tiny Borley parish was surplus to requirements and would be amalgamated with the neighbouring Liston parish the next time a vacancy arose [6]. Under this secret plan, the new joint rector would live in the much more comfortable Liston Rectory on a substantially higher salary of £400 a year, and Borley Rectory would be sold [6]. Although this would have been an ideal solution for the struggling Smiths, Warman never suggested it to them as a possibility [6].

## Departure

When Warman moved to Manchester in January 1929, the incoming Bishop **Henry Wilson** proved equally dismissive of the Smiths' concerns, rejecting them with "sulphurous sarcasm."
