# Dr Guy Warman

Bishop of Chelmsford 1923–1929; the Church of England official who appointed [[rev-eric-smith|Eric Smith]] to Borley and dismissed the Smiths' complaints about the Rectory.

## Character and Views

Warman was an evangelical Anglican with progressive views. He refused to wear a bishop's mitre and had co-founded the **Group Brotherhood** in 1905 to promote reform within the Church. He had little patience with those still troubled by the challenge to Christianity posed by Darwinism, was pragmatic about the appeal of paganism, and was no admirer of what he considered reactionary Anglo-Catholics — a position that put him at immediate odds with the Anglo-Catholic Eric Smith.

## Role at Borley

Warman offered the living at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] to Smith in the summer of 1928 after failing to secure any other candidate. He did not tell Smith that the position had stood vacant since Harry Bull's death in 1927, nor that it had already been refused by twelve other clergy — making Smith the **thirteenth candidate**.

When Smith and his wife appealed for a transfer to another parish on account of the Rectory's dreadful condition and alleged haunting, Warman was unsympathetic. He felt the Smiths were raising an unnecessary "mare's nest" and dismissed their complaints. He was also distracted by his own imminent move to the Diocese of Manchester in January 1929.

Behind closed doors, Warman had already approved a plan to unite the tiny parishes of Borley and Liston and sell the Rectory — but this option was never mentioned to the Smiths.

## Departure

When Warman moved to Manchester, the incoming Bishop **Henry Wilson** proved equally dismissive of the Smiths' concerns, rejecting them with "sulphurous sarcasm."

> **Contradiction:** Warman's failure to inform Smith of the position's toxic history is cited by O'Connor as a significant act of institutional bad faith that set the Borley drama in motion.

**Dr Guy Warman** was the Bishop of Chelmsford who was given sole responsibility by the Bull family for filling the vacant living at Borley following the death of the Rev. Harry Foyster Bull in 1927 [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following specific biographical details, events, and controversies regarding his involvement in the Borley Rectory story:

**Lesser-Known Biographical Details and Theological Stance**
Warman is described as a **"formidable character and firm administrator"** who held progressive, evangelical Anglican views [2, 3]. Demonstrating his radical approach, he notably **refused to wear a bishop's mitre** [2]. In 1905, he co-founded the **Group Brotherhood**, an organization proposing reform within the Church of England [2]. He was highly pragmatic about the appeal of materialism and paganism following the Great War, had little patience for the anxieties surrounding Darwinian theories, and **deeply disliked reactionary Anglo-Catholicism** [2]. 

**The Appointment of Eric Smith and Withheld Information (Summer 1928)**
By the summer of 1928, Warman had failed to secure a new rector for Borley and was obliged to offer the living to the Rev. Guy Eric Smith [4]. Their relationship was "cool from the start" and quickly turned "icy," largely because Smith's Anglo-Catholicism clashed directly with Warman's progressive ambitions for his diocese [4]. The Bull family (who formally retained the gift of the living) were also unhappy with Warman's choice of Smith for the parish [5].

Crucially, Warman engaged in significant omissions of fact when appointing Smith: he **deliberately failed to advise Smith that the Rectory had been vacant since Harry Bull's death, nor did he inform him that twelve other clergymen had already refused the position due to the building's sinister reputation** [4]. Eric Smith was the thirteenth candidate to be offered the job [4].

**Dismissal of the "Haunting" and the Smiths' Complaints**
When Eric and Mabel Smith eventually appealed to Dr. Warman for a transfer to another parish—citing the intolerable, dilapidated living conditions and the rumors of ghosts—Warman flatly refused [3]. He viewed the Smiths as being **"needy and neurotic in equal measure"** and felt they were stirring up an **"unnecessary 'mare's nest'"** [3]. He unsympathetically noted that the previous rector, Harry Bull, had lived happily at the Rectory for over thirty years without making any fuss about ghosts [3].

**Secret Plans and Departure to Manchester (1928–1929)**
Warman's disdain and lack of sympathy for the Smiths was partly driven by his own career trajectory; he was scheduled to take over as the **Bishop of Manchester in early 1929**, leaving him with little patience for local parish difficulties [6]. 

Furthermore, behind closed doors in November 1928, Warman and the Ecclesiastical Commission had secretly agreed that the tiny Borley parish was surplus to requirements and would be amalgamated with the neighboring Liston parish the next time a vacancy arose [6]. Under this secret plan, the new joint rector would live in the much more comfortable Liston Rectory on a substantially higher salary of £400 a year, and the Borley Rectory would be sold [6]. Although this would have been an ideal solution for the struggling Smiths, Warman never suggested it to them as a possibility [6]. Instead, his unyielding ultimatum to Eric Smith was **"Borley or nothing"** [6]. 

Dr. Warman subsequently left to take up his new post in Manchester in January 1929, abandoning the Smiths to deal with the Rectory's escalating supernatural reputation and deteriorating conditions [6].
