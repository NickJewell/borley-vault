# Ernest Ambrose (1878–1972+)

Organist at [[borley-church|Borley Church]] for 16 years before WWI during the [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] incumbency. Long Melford local and memoirist; author of *Melford Memories*. Interviewed by Underwood in 1969 at age 91, accompanied by Underwood's wife and Dr Peter Hilton-Rowe, at his Sudbury home. He lived to be over 94.

## Testimony (bps-08)

Ambrose cycled from Melford to Borley every Friday evening for choir practice and every Sunday for services; in bad weather he crossed fields. He was always given a glass of beer on arrival at the Rectory and invited to lunch. He came to know [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] well.

**The Bull sisters on the ghost:** They pointed out the path and lawn where they had seen the figure, saying "Oh, we are quite used to it. It doesn't bother us at all." He described them as "very sensible and practical women who were not given to exaggeration or emotionalism; nor were they especially interested or inclined to search for evidence of the supernatural."

**Harry Bull's character:** "Rather flamboyant figure" who talked about boxing, cats or the Borley ghosts. He was especially fond of boxing: once, when attacked by two street thugs in the East End of London who thought a parson was easy prey, "he knocked them both out cold."

**The housemaid:** A young housemaid Ambrose was attracted to, who had heard nothing about ghosts, saw "a person dressed like a nun or nurse standing by the lower garden gate" in semi-darkness. When she approached the figure, it suddenly vanished. "She was so terrified that she fainted on the spot and left the rector's employment shortly afterwards."

**Church knocking:** Harry Bull and a friend (just after a service Ambrose had just left) heard "distinct and loud knocking sounds from outside, near the porch of the church. As they puzzled as to what it could be, the knocking sound moved and slowly travelled the whole way round the church." Nothing was found. They never heard it again.

**The choir boy and the nun:** Harry Bull told Ambrose that a choir boy — wanting a word with the rector one evening — saw "a nun or sister of mercy seemingly about to knock on the door" and went away to avoid interrupting. Next day, the rector said no one had called. When the boy mentioned seeing a nun: "Oh yes, it could well have been the ghost nun; she was very active last night . . ."

Despite 16 years of evening choir practices and dark walks home past the Rectory, Ambrose himself apparently never had a paranormal experience of his own.

## Assessment

Ambrose's account (recorded when he was 91, in 1969) confirms that the haunting was treated as a matter-of-fact family reality by the Bulls — not a sensational spectacle — and extends the record of phenomena to include new witnesses (the housemaid, the unnamed choir boy). His own null experience over 16 years is notable. His *Melford Memories* is cited by [[andrew-clarke]] as a valuable primary source for East Anglian social history.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Ernest Ambrose** (1878–1972) was a local resident who served as the organist at Borley Church for sixteen years prior to the First World War, taking up the position at the age of 18 [1-4]. Because of his close relationship with the Bull family during his tenure, he provided researchers with some of the most grounded, first-hand glimpses into the daily life and early paranormal legends surrounding Borley Rectory. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, quotes, and lesser-known biographical details regarding his life and connection to the case:

**Lesser-Known Biographical Details**
*   **Dedication to Duty:** As a young man, Ambrose cycled every Friday evening and Sunday from Long Melford to Borley for choir practice and services [4]. During severe winters, he would sometimes abandon his bicycle to walk across fields in feet-deep snow to ensure he arrived half an hour early [4]. 
*   **Later Career:** Following the First World War, he opened a small shop in Hall Street, Long Melford, where he sold photographic equipment and offered photography services [5]. 
*   **Interviews and Publications:** In 1969, at the age of 91, the "sprightly" Ambrose was interviewed at his home in Sudbury by Peter Underwood, Steuart Kiernan, and Dr. Peter Hilton-Rowe [1, 3]. He lived to be over 94 [3]. An article he wrote outlining his Borley associations, *"That Ghost Again"*, was published in the *East Anglian Magazine* in 1974, and his book, *Melford Memories*, was published posthumously in 1972 [1, 6]. A 1911 group photograph of the Borley choir featuring Ambrose alongside the Rev. Harry Bull still hangs in the tower of Borley Church [1].

**Relationship with the Bull Family**
Ambrose came to know the Rev. Harry Bull well, describing him as a "rather flamboyant figure" [7]. He was routinely invited to take lunch at the Rectory and was given a glass of beer upon his morning arrivals [8]. 
*   **Practical Women:** Ambrose noted that the Bull sisters discussed their ghost sightings in a completely "casual" and "matter-of-fact" tone, pointing out the garden paths and windows where the nun appeared [8-10]. He described the women as "very down to earth," noting that on one occasion when his bicycle suffered a puncture, one of the Bull sisters cheerfully mended the tire for him and seemed to enjoy doing it [8, 10]. 
*   **The East End Thugs:** Ambrose related a story showcasing Harry Bull's physical prowess, noting that the Rector had once been attacked by two thugs while visiting London's East End, but had "knocked them both cold" [9].
*   **A Comical Reunion:** Decades later, Ambrose was playing the organ as a guest at a church in Cornard and spotted the elderly Alfred Bull in the congregation [7]. When Ambrose approached him and reminded him that he had been the Borley organist some seventy years prior, Alfred paused and then quipped, **"We had a strange organist here this morning, and we didn’t get on very well with him!"** [7].

**Paranormal Accounts and The Terrified Housemaid**
Ambrose provided several distinct accounts of paranormal events from the Bull incumbency:
*   **The Nun at the Door:** One evening, Ambrose walked to the front door of the Rectory to speak with Harry Bull, but turned away when he saw a figure dressed like a nun or "sister of mercy" standing there, seemingly about to knock [11, 12]. The next day, when Ambrose apologized for almost interrupting his visitor, the Rector replied that no one had called, casually adding, **"Oh yes, it could well have been the ghost nun, she was very active last night ... !"** [11, 12].
*   **The Housemaid:** Ambrose admitted he had been attracted to a young housemaid working at the Rectory [13]. The girl, who knew nothing of the haunting rumors, told her "young admirer" that she had come home in the semi-darkness and seen a nun or nurse standing by the lower garden gate; when the figure vanished upon her approach, the girl fainted in terror and left her job shortly thereafter [10, 13]. 
*   **The Knocking Church:** Harry Bull shared a personal experience with Ambrose, explaining that one summer evening just after Ambrose had finished playing the organ and left, Bull and a friend distinctly heard loud knocking sounds from outside the church porch that slowly travelled all the way around the building, with no visible cause [10, 13, 14].

**The Doctored Photograph Controversy**
Ambrose unintentionally played a central role in a highly sensationalized media event during the subsequent Smith incumbency. 
*   The sources contain a slight geographical contradiction, stating that either an "Irish American" or a "Canadian journalist" walked into Ambrose's photography shop in Long Melford and asked him to take pictures of the Rectory, providing an address in New York to send them to [1, 2, 5]. 
*   Ambrose cycled to Borley, took six photographs, and charged what he thought was an exorbitant fee of £4, which the agency promptly paid [15].
*   Six weeks later, Ambrose received an American newspaper featuring his photograph on the front page, but it had been doctored to include a phantom nun and a spectral coach drawn by horses with distended nostrils, driven by headless coachmen [15]. The image was captioned "Mystery of the Walled-up Spook of Borley Rectory" [15]. 
*   Ambrose found the image amusing and copied it onto glass plates [15]. However, a few hours later, the Rev. Guy Eric Smith arrived at the shop in a "towering rage" holding the same newspaper and demanding an explanation [16]. When Ambrose handed the furious Rector one of the glass plate copies, Smith threw it to the floor and smashed it to pieces with his foot [16]. The Rector left, completely unaware that Ambrose had secretly kept a second copy [16].
