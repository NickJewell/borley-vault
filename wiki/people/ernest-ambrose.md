# Ernest Ambrose (1878–1972+)

Organist at [[borley-church|Borley Church]] for sixteen years before WWI during the [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] incumbency, taking up the position at the age of 18 [1–4]. Long Melford local and memoirist; author of *Melford Memories* (published posthumously 1972). Interviewed by [[peter-underwood|Underwood]], Steuart Kiernan, and Dr [[peter-hilton-rowe|Peter Hilton-Rowe]] in 1969 at age 91 at his Sudbury home [1, 3]. He lived to be over 94.

## Relationship with the Bull Family

Ambrose cycled from Melford to Borley every Friday evening for choir practice and every Sunday for services; in severe winters he would sometimes abandon his bicycle to walk across fields in feet-deep snow to ensure he arrived half an hour early [4]. He was always given a glass of beer on arrival at the Rectory and invited to lunch. He came to know [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] well, describing him as a "rather flamboyant figure" who talked about boxing, cats, or the Borley ghosts [7].

**The Bull sisters on the ghost:** They discussed their sightings in a completely casual, matter-of-fact tone, pointing out garden paths and windows where the nun appeared. He described them as "very sensible and practical women who were not given to exaggeration or emotionalism; nor were they especially interested or inclined to search for evidence of the supernatural" [8–10]. On one occasion when Ambrose's bicycle suffered a puncture, one of the Bull sisters cheerfully mended the tyre and seemed to enjoy doing it [8, 10].

**Harry Bull's character:** "Rather flamboyant figure" who talked about boxing, cats or the Borley ghosts. He was especially fond of boxing: once, when attacked by two street thugs in the East End of London who thought a parson was easy prey, "he knocked them both out cold" [9].

**A comical reunion:** Decades later, Ambrose was playing the organ as a guest at a church in Cornard and spotted the elderly Alfred Bull in the congregation. When Ambrose reminded him that he had been the Borley organist some seventy years prior, Alfred paused and quipped: **"We had a strange organist here this morning, and we didn't get on very well with him!"** [7]

## Paranormal Accounts

**The housemaid:** A young housemaid Ambrose was attracted to, who had heard nothing about ghosts, saw "a person dressed like a nun or nurse standing by the lower garden gate" in semi-darkness. When she approached the figure, it suddenly vanished. "She was so terrified that she fainted on the spot and left the rector's employment shortly afterwards" [10, 13].

**The choir boy and the nun:** Harry Bull told Ambrose that a choir boy — wanting a word with the rector one evening — saw "a nun or sister of mercy seemingly about to knock on the door" and went away to avoid interrupting. Next day, the rector said no one had called. When the boy mentioned seeing a nun: "Oh yes, it could well have been the ghost nun; she was very active last night . . ." [11, 12].

**Church knocking:** Harry Bull and a friend (just after a service Ambrose had just left) heard "distinct and loud knocking sounds from outside, near the porch of the church. As they puzzled as to what it could be, the knocking sound moved and slowly travelled the whole way round the church." Nothing was found. They never heard it again [10, 13, 14].

Despite sixteen years of evening choir practices and dark walks home past the Rectory, Ambrose himself apparently never had a paranormal experience of his own.

## The Doctored Photograph Controversy

Ambrose played an unintended central role in a sensationalised media event during the subsequent Smith incumbency. The sources contain a slight geographical contradiction, stating that either an "Irish American" or a "Canadian journalist" walked into Ambrose's photography shop in Long Melford (where he sold photographic equipment and offered photography services after WWI [5]) and asked him to take pictures of the Rectory, providing a New York address to send them to [1, 2, 5]. Ambrose cycled to Borley, took six photographs, and charged what he thought was an exorbitant fee of £4, which the agency promptly paid [15].

Six weeks later, Ambrose received an American newspaper featuring his photograph on the front page — doctored to include a phantom nun and a spectral coach drawn by horses with distended nostrils, driven by headless coachmen [15]. The image was captioned "Mystery of the Walled-up Spook of Borley Rectory" [15].

Ambrose found the image amusing and copied it onto glass plates [15]. A few hours later, the [[rev-eric-smith|Rev Guy Eric Smith]] arrived at the shop in a "towering rage" holding the same newspaper and demanding an explanation [16]. When Ambrose handed the furious Rector one of the glass plate copies, Smith threw it to the floor and smashed it to pieces with his foot [16]. The Rector left, completely unaware that Ambrose had secretly kept a second copy [16].

## Publications and Legacy

An article he wrote outlining his Borley associations, *"That Ghost Again"*, was published in the *East Anglian Magazine* in 1974. His book *Melford Memories* appeared posthumously in 1972 [1, 6]. A 1911 group photograph of the Borley choir featuring Ambrose alongside the Rev. Harry Bull still hangs in the tower of [[borley-church|Borley Church]] [1]. His *Melford Memories* is cited by [[andrew-clarke]] as a valuable primary source for East Anglian social history.

## Assessment

Ambrose's account (recorded when he was 91, in 1969) confirms that the haunting was treated as a matter-of-fact family reality by the Bulls — not a sensational spectacle — and extends the record of phenomena to include new witnesses (the housemaid, the unnamed choir boy). His own null experience over sixteen years is notable.
