# Mrs G. Taylor of Chelsea

One of the participants in the planchette séance held at the [[rectory-cottage|Borley Priory cottage]] on the evening of 8 June 1948. The company present comprised Mr and Mrs [[james-turner|James Turner]], the Reverend [[john-dening|John Dening]], [[ts-frankland|T. S. Frankland]] of Trinity College Cambridge, and Mrs Taylor.

Messages received that night included communications from an entity claiming to be "Harry Bull," who stated among other things that he would haunt Borley for "4004 years" and that it was "Lionel Foyster" whose footsteps had been heard on the churchyard path. The group subsequently proceeded to [[borley-church|Borley Church]] porch at the entity's invitation. No further details about Mrs Taylor appear in *Ghosts of Borley*.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mrs. G. Taylor of Chelsea** (also referred to in the sources as **Mrs. Gay Taylor** and **Mrs. Guy Taylor of London**) was a reporter, researcher, and deeply invested follower of the Borley haunting whose ultimate theories took a uniquely dark turn. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, events, quotes, and specific theories regarding her involvement:

**Biographical Details and Background**
*   **Occupation:** Mrs. Taylor worked as a reporter for the **Mass-Observation Archive**, an organization that had begun recording everyday life in Britain in 1937 [1].
*   **Early Involvement:** She became interested in the Rectory phenomena after reading Harry Price's books and began corresponding with him in 1942 to discuss the Waldegrave family and the Catholic background of the story [1]. 
*   **The "Marie Lairre" Message:** About a year before Harry Price's death (around 1947), she wrote to him claiming that during a séance she had been given a message specifically to pass on to him: it stated that the reputed ghost nun, **Marie Lairre, was born near Le Havre on September 27, 1647**, and had later entered a nunnery [2]. 

**Visits to Borley and Local Skepticism**
Mrs. Taylor visited the Borley area herself and became acquainted with key figures in the saga, including Miss Ethel Bull and James and Catherine Turner, who were then living at the Rectory Cottage [1].
*   While in the village, she claimed to have experienced **"a variety of small experiences"** of a paranormal nature [1].
*   However, she was disappointed to find that the local villagers met her with widespread skepticism; one local bluntly advised her that **"there waunt no ghosts till Harry Price brought ’um"** [1]. 

**The June 1948 Séance**
On the evening of **June 8, 1948** (shortly after Harry Price’s death), Mrs. Taylor participated in a highly notable planchette séance held at the Rectory Cottage [3]. 
*   Her fellow sitters included Mr. and Mrs. Turner, the Rev. John C. Dening, and an eccentric Cambridge undergraduate named T. S. Frankland (Tommy Frankland) [2, 3]. 
*   During this sitting, the group supposedly established contact with an entity calling itself **"Harry Price,"** as well as an entity claiming to be the late **"Harry Bull"** [2, 3]. 
*   The "Harry Bull" entity made a bizarre prediction that he would haunt Borley for exactly **"4004 years"** (a number reflecting Archbishop Ussher's 18th-century calculation for the date of Creation), and further alleged that there had been foul play involved in the death of the Rev. Lionel Foyster [3].

**Theories of Satanism and Black Magic**
Mrs. Taylor's most distinct contribution to the Borley literature is her eventual conviction that the phenomena were rooted in profound evil and black magic, a theory that she developed after holding several of her own Borley-devoted séances in 1947 [4]. She detailed these beliefs in a series of "very curious letters" sent to the skeptical investigator Trevor Hall in 1957, which Hall kept in his unpublished papers [5]. 
*   **The 1480 Curse:** On January 26, 1957, she wrote to Hall: **"I am unalterably convinced that something happened in that countryside in the past that caused it to be disturbed, at times, to this day . . . it was begun by [a priest and two others] who round about 1480 raised something they couldn't control"** [4].
*   **Satanic Murder:** On February 22, 1957, she elaborated on this theory, stating that a local group practiced black magic at Long Melford and Borley (preferring Borley because it was out of the way) [6]. She claimed that when she asked her séance if a crime had occurred at Borley before the 17th century, the response was **"Death - Violent,"** and that it was due to **Satanism** [6].
*   **The Devil and the Medium:** She further claimed to Hall that a medium had once gone into a trance at Borley and **"woke up in a terrible state, declaring that she had seen the devil and that the whole place was reeking in Black Magic,"** after which no medium for miles around would agree to go into a trance at the site [7].
*   **Accusations against Harry Price:** In her sprawling theory of demonic possession, she saw a pattern indicating that the perpetrators of the Borley crimes were possessed, and she alleged that **even Harry Price had fallen under this evil spell**, causing him to manufacture or misrepresent the paranormal evidence [8]. 

**Later Investigations and Opinions**
*   During her research, Mrs. Taylor also spoke with Mrs. Annette Henning (the wife of the Rev. A. C. Henning). Mrs. Henning confided to her that **"the whole of the Stour Valley was far more disturbed than anyone knew,"** revealing that after she gave talks to the local Women's Institute, village women would secretly approach her to share similar stories of paranormal happenings in their own homes [9].
*   Because of her growing involvement, the SPR investigators Eric Dingwall and Mollie Goldney arranged to meet Mrs. Taylor at the Society for Psychical Research headquarters in Tavistock Square to discuss her findings [1]. 
*   Ultimately, Trevor Hall dismissed Mrs. Taylor's extreme theories as unfounded. In his unpublished manuscripts, he concluded: **"I do not at any rate accept Mrs. Taylor's theory that there is evidence to show that certain people behaved criminally because they came to live in the supposedly evil orbit of Borley"** [10].
