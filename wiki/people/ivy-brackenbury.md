# Ivy Johnson Brackenbury (Mrs Harry Bull)

(Born Lincoln, 27 November 1881; died Hastings, Sussex, 2 August 1955) Wife of [[harry-bull]] while he was Rector of Borley.

## Background

Born Ivy Johnson Prince. Her first marriage to Harold Brackenbury, an electrical engineer from a distinguished Lincolnshire family, produced a daughter Constance, born 7 October 1901. According to research by [[baines-mrs-ryan|Mrs Cecil Ryan Baines]], the Brackenburys separated rather than divorced; Harold remarried and died of stomach cancer in Harrow in 1948.

Ivy was working as a nurse at the Walnut Hospital in Sudbury when she met Harry Bull, who was a patient there. They married at the Parish Church of St Alban's, Holborn, on 12 September 1911 — she was 29, he was 49.

## Life at Borley

Ivy and her daughter Constance lived at [[borley-place]] with Harry Bull after the marriage, before moving into [[borley-rectory]] in 1920 when the [[bull-family|Bull sisters]] moved to [[chilton-lodge]]. Neither Ivy nor Constance are recorded as having witnessed classic Bull phenomena during their time there.

Shortly after the marriage, Harry altered his will to give Ivy the benefit of a regular income from the Bull estate for life. This caused bitterness among the sisters, who considered her a "gold digger." The sisters also alleged to [[sidney-glanville]] in 1938 that Harry Bull had been poisoned, and that a half-full bottle of "sugar of lead" had been found in the Rectory cellars after his death. These allegations, included in the [[locked-book]] for Price's private information, are not substantiated and were never published by [[harry-price]].

> **Contested claim:** The poison allegations came entirely from the Bull sisters' testimony to Glanville. [[lord-charles-hope]] heard similar stories when he visited Chilton Lodge in 1929. No independent evidence supports them.

## Post-Rectory

After Harry Bull's death in 1927, Ivy and Constance left the Rectory and continued to live in the neighbourhood. Constance became a dressmaker, married a Colonel Boothby, and eventually died in South Africa in 1970. Ivy moved to Hastings where she died in 1955.

## Significance

The acrimonious relationship between Ivy Brackenbury and the Bull sisters influenced how the family's testimony was shaped — [[ethel-bull]] and others' clear hostility toward Ivy coloured some of their accounts to investigators, and [[mrs-ryan-baines|Mrs Baines]] warned [[peter-underwood]] to discount the poison story as family feuding. At the [[blue-room-seance]] in June 1929, when Ethel asked the entity claiming to be Harry Bull whether he had been killed, it replied "Yes," which may reflect the sister's own private suspicions being channelled through the séance format.
