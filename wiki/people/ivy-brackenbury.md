# Ivy Johnson Brackenbury (Mrs Harry Bull)

(Born Lincoln, 27 November 1881; died Hastings, Sussex, 2 August 1955) Wife of [[harry-bull]] while he was Rector of Borley.

## Background

Born Ivy Johnson Prince. Her first marriage to Harold Brackenbury, an electrical engineer from a distinguished Lincolnshire family, produced a daughter Constance, born 7 October 1901. According to research by [[baines-mrs-ryan|Mrs Cecil Ryan Baines]], the Brackenburys separated rather than divorced; Harold remarried and died of stomach cancer in Harrow in 1948.

Ivy was working as a nurse at the Walnut Hospital in Sudbury when she met Harry Bull, who was a patient there. They married at the Parish Church of St Alban's, Holborn, on 12 September 1911 — she was 29, he was 49.

## Life at Borley

Ivy and her daughter Constance lived at [[borley-place]] with Harry Bull after the marriage, before moving into [[borley-rectory]] in 1920 when the [[bull-family|Bull sisters]] moved to [[chilton-lodge]]. Neither Ivy nor Constance are recorded as having witnessed classic Bull phenomena during their time there.

Shortly after the marriage, Harry altered his will to give Ivy the benefit of a regular income from the Bull estate for life. This caused bitterness among the sisters, who considered her a "gold digger." The sisters also alleged to [[sidney-glanville]] in 1938 that Harry Bull had been poisoned, and that a half-full bottle of "sugar of lead" had been found in the Rectory cellars after his death. These allegations, included in the [[locked-book]] for Price's private information, are not substantiated and were never published by [[harry-price]].

> **Contested claim:** The poison allegations came entirely from the Bull sisters' testimony to Glanville. [[lord-charles-hope]] heard similar stories when he visited Chilton Lodge in 1929. No independent evidence supports them.

## Post-Rectory

After Harry Bull's death in 1927, Ivy and Constance left the Rectory and continued to live in the neighbourhood. Constance became a dressmaker, married a Colonel Boothby, and eventually died in South Africa in 1970. Ivy moved to Hastings where she died in 1955.

## Significance

The acrimonious relationship between Ivy Brackenbury and the Bull sisters influenced how the family's testimony was shaped — [[ethel-bull]] and others' clear hostility toward Ivy coloured some of their accounts to investigators, and [[mrs-ryan-baines|Mrs Baines]] warned [[peter-underwood]] to discount the poison story as family feuding. At the [[blue-room-seance]] in June 1929, when Ethel asked the entity claiming to be Harry Bull whether he had been killed, it replied "Yes," which may reflect the sister's own private suspicions being channelled through the séance format.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Ivy Johnson Brackenbury (Mrs. Harry Bull)** is a key, if somewhat marginalized, figure in the Borley Rectory saga. As the controversial wife of the Rev. Harry Foyster Bull, she became the target of intense family hatred and malicious gossip, which ultimately bled into the haunted lore of the Rectory itself. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, events, specific quotes, and contradictions regarding her life:

**Early Life and First Marriage**
Ivy was born Ivy Prince in Lincoln on November 27, 1881 [1-4]. In October 1899, she married an electrical engineer named Harold Brackenbury, and the couple had a daughter, Constance, born on October 7, 1901 [2, 4]. A significant contradiction existed regarding her marital status when she later met Harry Bull. Ivy described herself as a widow, and stories circulated that she had been abandoned by Brackenbury "at the church door" [2, 4, 5]. However, later research by Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth and Mrs. Cecil Ryan Baines suggested that the Brackenburys had merely separated, and Harold Brackenbury actually survived until 1948, dying of stomach cancer in Harrow [6].

**Courtship and the Family Feud**
Ivy was working as a nurse at Walnut Tree Hospital in Sudbury when she met the 49-year-old Rev. Harry Bull, who was a patient there [2, 4, 7]. Parochial gossip noted Harry's frequent hospital visits, with one resident remarking, "I do believe that he comes here to see her and not you!" [8]. 

The Bull family—particularly Harry's unmarried sisters—bitterly opposed the match, viewing the 29-year-old Ivy as a "gold digger" intent on extracting money from the wealthy rector [5, 9, 10]. The sisters spread rumors that her marriage to Harry was bigamous [11]. Consequently, Harry and Ivy were married not at Borley, but at the Parish Church of St Alban’s, Holborn, London, on September 12, 1911 [2, 4, 10]. Ivy even stayed in a nearby women's hostel to establish the necessary parochial residency for the ceremony [10]. 

**Life at Borley Rectory and Personal Quirks**
Initially, the newlyweds lived at Borley Place across from the church, while Harry's mother and sisters remained in the Rectory [2, 4, 12]. After Harry's mother died in 1914, Ivy initiated a strategy to take possession of the Rectory [13]. In 1920, Harry and Ivy moved into Borley Rectory, forcing his sisters to vacate their childhood home and move to Chilton Lodge [2, 4, 13]. This displacement turned the sisters' dislike into a bitter and abiding hatred [13]. 

Ivy and her daughter were seen as smart and up-to-date, presenting a sharp contrast to the old-fashioned, "frumpish" Bull sisters [14]. Former maid Margaret Finch recalled Ivy's particular quirks: she always wore clothes that rustled, and she had a habit of coming down the stairs backwards to ensure they had been dusted properly [15]. Finch noted that Ivy acted as if "once a nurse, always a nurse," treating her role in the house as if she had achieved the dizzy heights of a hospital "matron" [16]. 

The marriage was reportedly very unhappy [5, 9]. Harry and Ivy slept in separate bedrooms [15]. Harry's relationship with his stepdaughter Constance was also incredibly strained, and it was said he even struck the girl [12, 17]. In an act of petty domestic warfare, Ivy and Constance were known to hide the cream from the house cow in the cellar so that Harry would not get his share [18]. According to the Bull sisters, Ivy found Harry "hysterical and affected," leading to violent quarrels, and his affection for her soon turned into hatred [19]. 

**Attitude Towards the Haunting**
Unlike her husband, who was deeply fascinated by spiritualism and ghosts, Ivy had no interest in the paranormal [20, 21]. Both Ivy and her daughter Constance were adamant that they witnessed absolutely nothing of a supernatural origin while living at Borley [22]. 

**The Will and Murder Accusations**
When Harry Bull died of chronic bronchitis and heart disease in the Rectory's Blue Room on June 9, 1927, he left a gross estate of £6,643 [23-25]. His will placed his assets into a trust that provided Ivy with a life income, provided she did not remarry, effectively cutting his siblings out of any immediate inheritance [5, 9, 24]. The enraged Bull sisters referred to Ivy as "that vile woman" who "gets off free and lives to enjoy herself" [26, 27]. 

In their malice, the sisters began spreading rumors that Ivy had murdered Harry before he could alter his will in their favor [5, 9, 28]. They claimed they had found a half-full bottle of "sugar of lead" (lead acetate) in the Rectory cellars, suggesting Ivy used it to poison him [5, 9, 29]. 

This fabricated murder story became a bizarre cornerstone of the Borley investigations. During a seance held by Harry Price on June 12–13, 1929, with the Bull sisters present, an entity claiming to be Harry tapped out on a mirror that he was unhappy, that there were money troubles, and that he had been killed by his wife [30-32]. Years later, SPR investigator Trevor Hall thoroughly investigated the claims and proved that Harry's death certificate was entirely in order and that the murder accusations were baseless [33]. 

**Later Life and The 1947 Flower Incident**
Following her husband's death, Ivy and her daughter moved away from Borley, eventually taking a flat in Primrose Hill, London [26]. She retained ownership of parts of the Rectory land, and in 1944, some Borley residents were still paying rent to her via her agents, Stanley Moger [34]. 

Ivy occasionally returned to Borley to tend to Harry's grave and the church flowers [35]. In a notable incident on Easter Saturday in 1947, Ivy spent a long time beautifully decorating Borley Church with flowers as a nostalgic gesture to her family's history [36]. The next morning, despite the church having been locked overnight, the flowers were found inexplicably scattered all over the interior [36]. 

Ivy Bull never remarried [37]. She died in a nursing home in Hastings, Sussex, on August 2, 1955 [1, 3, 37]. 

**Literary Oddity**
In a bizarre footnote to her life, the highly skeptical investigator Trevor Hall later wrote a fictional pastiche titled *The Last Case of Sherlock Holmes – Ivy Johnson Bull of Borley* (1986) [38]. In this 31-page exercise in literary self-indulgence, Hall imagined meeting the illegitimate son of Sherlock Holmes, who supposedly helps him establish the correct historical facts regarding Ivy's 1911 marriage to Harry Bull [38].
