# Ivy Johnson Brackenbury (Mrs Harry Bull)

(Born Lincoln, 27 November 1881; died Hastings, Sussex, 2 August 1955) Wife of [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] while he was Rector of Borley.

## Early Life and First Marriage

Ivy was born Ivy Prince in Lincoln on November 27, 1881 [1–4]. In October 1899, she married an electrical engineer named Harold Brackenbury, and the couple had a daughter, Constance, born on October 7, 1901 [2, 4].

> **Contradiction:** A significant contradiction existed regarding her marital status when she later met Harry Bull. Ivy described herself as a widow, and stories circulated that she had been abandoned by Brackenbury "at the church door" [2, 4, 5]. However, later research by [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth|Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth]] and [[mrs-ryan-baines|Mrs. Cecil Ryan Baines]] suggested that the Brackenburys had merely separated, and Harold Brackenbury actually survived until 1948, dying of stomach cancer in Harrow [6]. Under English law, the Brackenbury marriage had never been dissolved.

## Courtship and the Family Feud

Ivy was working as a nurse at Walnut Tree Hospital in Sudbury when she met the 49-year-old Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]], who was a patient there [2, 4, 7]. Parochial gossip noted Harry's frequent hospital visits, with one resident remarking, "I do believe that he comes here to see her and not you!" [8]

The Bull family — particularly Harry's unmarried sisters — bitterly opposed the match, viewing the 29-year-old Ivy as a "gold digger" intent on extracting money from the wealthy rector [5, 9, 10]. The sisters spread rumours that her marriage to Harry was bigamous [11]. Consequently, Harry and Ivy were married not at Borley, but at the Parish Church of **St Alban's, Holborn**, London, on **September 12, 1911** [2, 4, 10]. Ivy even stayed in a nearby women's hostel to establish the necessary parochial residency for the ceremony [10].

## Life at Borley

Initially, the newlyweds lived at [[borley-place|Borley Place]] across from the church, while Harry's mother and sisters remained in the Rectory. After Harry's mother died in 1914, Ivy initiated a strategy to take possession of the Rectory [13]. In **1920**, Harry and Ivy moved into [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], forcing his sisters to vacate their childhood home and move to [[chilton-lodge|Chilton Lodge]] — a displacement that turned the sisters' dislike into a bitter and abiding hatred [13].

Ivy and her daughter were seen as smart and up-to-date, presenting a sharp contrast to the old-fashioned, "frumpish" Bull sisters [14]. Former maid [[margaret-finch|Margaret Finch]] recalled Ivy's particular quirks: she always wore clothes that rustled, and she had a habit of coming down the stairs backwards to ensure they had been dusted properly [15]. Finch noted that Ivy acted as if "once a nurse, always a nurse" [16].

The marriage was reportedly very unhappy [5, 9]. Harry and Ivy slept in separate bedrooms [15]. Harry's relationship with his stepdaughter Constance was also incredibly strained [12, 17]. In an act of petty domestic warfare, Ivy and Constance were known to hide the cream from the house cow in the cellar so that Harry would not get his share [18]. According to the Bull sisters, Ivy found Harry "hysterical and affected," leading to violent quarrels [19].

Unlike her husband, who was deeply fascinated by spiritualism and ghosts, Ivy had no interest in the paranormal [20, 21]. Both Ivy and her daughter Constance were adamant that they witnessed **absolutely nothing of a supernatural origin** while living at Borley [22].

## The Will and Poison Allegations

When [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] died of chronic bronchitis and heart disease in the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] on June 9, 1927, he left a gross estate of £6,643 [23–25]. His will placed his assets into a trust that provided Ivy with a life income, provided she did not remarry, effectively cutting his siblings out of any immediate inheritance [5, 9, 24]. The enraged Bull sisters referred to Ivy as "that vile woman" who "gets off free and lives to enjoy herself" [26, 27].

In their malice, the sisters began spreading rumours that Ivy had murdered Harry before he could alter his will in their favour. They claimed they had found a half-full bottle of "sugar of lead" (lead acetate) in the Rectory cellars, suggesting Ivy used it to poison him [5, 9, 29].

This fabricated murder story became a bizarre cornerstone of the Borley investigations. During a séance held by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] on June 12–13, 1929, with the Bull sisters present, an entity claiming to be Harry tapped out on a mirror that he was unhappy, that there were money troubles, and that he had been killed by his wife [30–32].

> **Contested claim:** The poison allegations came entirely from the Bull sisters' testimony to [[sidney-glanville|Glanville]]. [[lord-charles-hope|Lord Charles Hope]] heard similar stories when he visited [[chilton-lodge|Chilton Lodge]] in 1929. No independent evidence supports them. SPR investigator [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] thoroughly investigated the claims and proved that Harry's death certificate was entirely in order and that the murder accusations were baseless [33]. [[mrs-ryan-baines|Mrs Baines]] warned [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] to discount the poison story as family feuding.

These allegations, included in the [[locked-book|Locked Book]] for Price's private information, are not substantiated and were never published by Harry Price.

## Post-Rectory and the 1947 Flower Incident

Following her husband's death, Ivy and her daughter moved away from Borley, eventually taking a flat in Primrose Hill, London [26]. She retained ownership of parts of the Rectory land, and in 1944, some Borley residents were still paying rent to her via her agents, [[stanley-moger|Stanley Moger]] [34].

Ivy occasionally returned to Borley to tend to Harry's grave and the church flowers. On Easter Saturday in 1947, Ivy spent a long time beautifully decorating [[borley-church|Borley Church]] with flowers as a nostalgic gesture [36]. The next morning, despite the church having been locked overnight, the flowers were found inexplicably scattered all over the interior [36] (single witness).

Ivy Bull never remarried [37]. She died in a nursing home in Hastings, Sussex, on **August 2, 1955** [1, 3, 37].

## Literary Oddity

In a bizarre footnote, the highly sceptical investigator [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] later wrote a fictional pastiche titled *The Last Case of Sherlock Holmes – Ivy Johnson Bull of Borley* (1986) — a 31-page exercise imagining the illegitimate son of Sherlock Holmes helping Hall establish the correct historical facts regarding Ivy's 1911 marriage [38].
