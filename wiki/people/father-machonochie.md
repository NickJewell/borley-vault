# Father Alexander Heriot Mackonochie

Anglo-Catholic priest and incumbent at [[st-albans-holborn|St Alban's Church, Holborn]] from 1863 (when the then-new building was completed) until his virtually forced resignation in 1882. His legacy explains why the [[harry-bull|Rev Harry Foyster Bull]] chose to be married at St Alban's Holborn in 1911.

## Incumbency at St Alban's, Holborn (1863–1882)

Mackonochie was a fervent follower of the Oxford Movement and championed an "Anglo-Catholic" approach to worship, favoring highly ceremonial and flamboyant services emphasising communion [1]. His specific practices included:

- Frequent use of incense
- Many lighted candles at the altar
- Priests conducting much of the service facing east
- Frequent genuflection
- A confessional within the church

## Conflict with the Church Association

These practices caused severe outrage among influential members of the Church of England who felt his methods were too close to Roman Catholic procedure [1]. The **Church Association** was formed to oppose such practices and targeted Mackonochie specifically, regarding him as **"the worst of all of the offending clergymen"** [1]. The Association took legal action against him on multiple occasions. The authorities apparently made no allowance for the fact that Father Mackonochie's spectacular style of service was immensely popular and was **"packing them in"** [1]. Nevertheless, relentless pressure led to his **"virtually forced resignation in 1882"** [1].

## Legacy and Connection to Borley

Despite his forced departure, the church authorities surprisingly allowed the Anglo-Catholic style of service to continue at St Alban's, and the church became famous for this specific tradition [1].

This history becomes directly relevant to the Borley case in 1911. On 12 September 1911, the Rev. Harry Foyster Bull (Rector of Borley) chose to marry [[ivy-brackenbury|Ivy Brackenbury]] at St Alban's, Holborn [2]. [[robert-wood|Robert Wood]] has suggested that Bull chose St Alban's because it was an "unfashionable" church, chosen deliberately to hide a bitter family feud over the marriage [2]. However, the *Borley Rectory – The Final Analysis* authors point out that St Alban's was far from unfashionable; because of Father Mackonochie's legacy, it was a highly notable centre of Anglo-Catholicism [1, 2]. They suggest that the Rev. Harry Bull — a wealthy, ordained priest — likely harboured Anglo-Catholic beliefs himself and deliberately chose to be married in a church associated with Mackonochie's flamboyant tradition, rather than in his simple country church at Borley [3].

The original St Alban's church building that Father Mackonochie knew, and where Harry Bull was married, was almost totally destroyed during the air raids of 1941 and had to be rebuilt after the Second World War [3].

## Significance

Mackonochie is an important contextual figure for understanding the Bull family's religious orientation. The likelihood that Harry Bull held Anglo-Catholic beliefs himself is supported by other evidence, including his known sympathy with spiritualism and the fact that his cousin [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Algernon Foyster]] was explicitly Anglo-Catholic.
