# Father Alexander Heriot Mackonochie

Anglo-Catholic priest and incumbent at St Alban's Church, Holborn from 1863 (when the then-new building was completed) until his virtually forced resignation in 1882. He is the key figure in understanding why the [[harry-bull|Rev Harry Foyster Bull]] chose to be married at St Alban's Holborn in 1911.

## The Anglo-Catholic Controversy

Mackonochie was the most controversial of a group of Church of England clergymen who in the mid-19th century advocated a highly ceremonial, "Anglo-Catholic" style of worship, drawing heavily on Roman Catholic practice. This included:
- Frequent use of incense
- Many lighted candles at the altar
- Priests conducting much of the service facing east
- Frequent genuflection
- A confessional within the church

The **Church Association**, formed to oppose such practices, took legal action against Mackonochie on many occasions, claiming he was acting unlawfully in the conduct of his services. Babbs and Mathias note that "apparently no allowance was made for the fact that Father Mackonochie (and/or his style of service) was 'packing them in'."

Despite the legal battles and his eventual forced resignation in 1882, the church authorities allowed the Anglo-Catholic style of service to continue at St Alban's after his departure, and the church became noted for it.

## Connection to Borley

When Robert Wood (*The Widow of Borley*) described St Alban's Holborn as "unfashionable," Babbs and Mathias reject this characterisation. Harry Bull — an ordained priest with Anglo-Catholic sympathies — may have chosen St Alban's specifically because of its Anglo-Catholic tradition and association with Mackonochie, rather than simply to avoid the awkwardness of marrying in his own Borley church amid disapproving family members.

The St Alban's church that Mackonochie and Harry Bull knew was destroyed almost totally during the air raids of 1941 and had to be rebuilt after the war.

## Significance

Mackonochie is an important contextual figure for understanding the Bull family's religious orientation. The likelihood that Harry Bull held Anglo-Catholic beliefs himself is supported by other evidence, including his known sympathy with spiritualism and the fact that his cousin [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Algernon Foyster]] was explicitly Anglo-Catholic.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Father Alexander Heriot Machonochie** (as his surname is spelled in the texts) was a prominent and highly controversial nineteenth-century Anglican priest whose history is tied to the Borley Rectory narrative through the marriage of the Rev. Harry Foyster Bull. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, and biographical details regarding his career and connection to the Borley narrative:

**Incumbency at St Alban’s, Holborn (1863–1882)**
Father Machonochie was the incumbent priest at St Alban’s Church in Holborn, London, from 1863—the year the new building was completed—until 1882 [1]. 

He was a fervent follower of the Oxford Movement and championed an "Anglo-Catholic" approach to worship, favoring highly ceremonial and flamboyant services that emphasized communion [1]. His specific practices included using incense and numerous lighted candles at the altar, conducting much of the service facing east, frequently genuflecting, and erecting a confessional within the church [1].

**Conflict with the Church Association**
These practices caused severe outrage among influential members of the Church of England, who felt his methods were too close to the procedures of the Roman Catholic Church [1]. An organization called the Church Association was formed to oppose such practices and targeted Machonochie specifically, regarding him as **"the worst of all of the offending clergymen"** [1]. 

The Association took legal action against him on multiple occasions, accusing him of acting unlawfully in the conduct of his services [1]. The authorities apparently made no allowance for the fact that Father Machonochie's spectacular style of service was immensely popular with the public and was **"packing them in"** [1]. Nevertheless, the relentless pressure ultimately led to his **"virtually forced resignation in 1882"** [1]. 

**Legacy and Connection to Borley**
Despite his forced departure, the church authorities surprisingly allowed the Anglo-Catholic style of service to continue at St Alban’s, and the church became famous for this specific tradition [1]. 

This history becomes highly relevant to the Borley case thirty years later. On September 12, 1911, the Rev. Harry Foyster Bull (Rector of Borley) chose to marry Ivy Brackenbury at St Alban’s, Holborn [2]. Borley critic Robert Wood has suggested that Bull chose St Alban’s because it was an "unfashionable" church, chosen deliberately to hide a bitter family feud over the marriage [2]. However, the texts point out that St Alban's was far from unfashionable; because of Father Machonochie's legacy, it was a highly notable center of Anglo-Catholicism [1, 2]. The texts suggest that the Rev. Harry Bull—a wealthy, ordained priest—likely harbored Anglo-Catholic beliefs himself and deliberately chose to be married in a church associated with Machonochie's flamboyant tradition, rather than in his simple country church at Borley [3]. 

**Lesser-Known Detail**
A final historical detail noted in the sources is that the original St Alban’s church building that Father Machonochie knew, and where Harry Bull was married, was almost totally destroyed during the air raids of 1941 and had to be rebuilt after the Second World War [3].
