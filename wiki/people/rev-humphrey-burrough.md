# Rev. Humphrey Burrough MA

Rector of [[borley-church|Borley]] from 1722 to 1757; prior to that, rector of St Gregory's church in Sudbury and headmaster of the local grammar school. He is one of the more historically significant of Borley's early incumbents, both because of his longevity in the post and because of his connection to the painter Thomas Gainsborough.

## Life and Career

Before coming to Borley, Burrough held two appointments concurrently: he was the incumbent at St Gregory's, a relatively large church in Sudbury (the parish adjoins Borley), and the headmaster of the local grammar school. One of his pupils — and, more significantly, his nephew — was the painter **Thomas Gainsborough** (1727–1788), one of the most important British artists of the 18th century.

Burrough's appointment as rector of Borley in 1722 was likely in the nature of a semi-retirement from his Sudbury duties. He held the Borley living for 35 years, dying in post in 1757.

## Discovery of His Tomb and Vault (1947)

On 26 May 1947 (Whit Monday), the [[rev-ac-henning|Rev Alfred Clifford Henning]], accompanied by author [[james-turner|James Turner]] and John Durrant (a Sudbury resident interested in the case), undertook investigations beneath the wooden flooring of the sanctuary in Borley Church. When some of the floorboards were removed, they immediately discovered a **black marble tombstone** identifying it as the resting place of Humphrey Burrough, MA.

The sandy soil beneath was dug and probed; many old and presumably human bones were found and later re-interred in the churchyard. James Turner then broke into the vault itself. There was no escape of foul air and the brickwork was in excellent condition. With the aid of a powerful torch, the group observed **three skeletons** lying in about three feet of water, stacked on top of one another. These were assumed to be the remains of Burrough and his daughters.

On the following day the aperture was sealed and the new stone flooring was put in place.

## Significance

The discovery of Burrough's vault was an important finding, confirming that interments had indeed been made beneath the church floor. However, this small vault was clearly not the legendary crypt of the Waldegrave family, and the doubt about the existence of a larger crypt remained for another 40 years — until the [[borley-church-crypt|1988 discovery]].

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Reverend Humphrey Burrough, MA** (also spelled **Borough** or **Burroughs** in the texts) was an important 18th-century clergyman in the Sudbury and Borley districts. While he had no direct connection to the paranormal phenomena at Borley Rectory, his name features prominently in the texts due to the dramatic, accidental discovery of his waterlogged burial vault during 20th-century church renovations.

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, contradictions, and events regarding his life and posthumous exhumation:

**Biographical Details and Career**
*   **Early Career:** In 1714, Burrough held two positions concurrently: he was listed as the Rector of St Gregory’s Church in Sudbury (a relatively large church in a parish adjoining Borley) and simultaneously served as the headmaster of the local grammar school [1, 2]. 
*   **Famous Nephew:** During his time as headmaster, one of his pupils was his own nephew, the famous painter **Thomas Gainsborough**, who was born in Sudbury [1, 2].
*   **Rector of Borley:** According to official lists of Borley incumbents, Burrough was inducted as the Rector of Borley in **1722** and served for 35 years until **1757** [1-3]. Because Borley was such a small and isolated rural hamlet, it is generally assumed that this appointment was **"in the nature of a semi-retirement"** for the important clergyman, though he managed to hold onto the position for over three decades [4]. 

**Textual Contradiction Regarding His Incumbency**
While the official lists record Burrough as the true Rector from 1722 to 1757, Dr. W.J. Phythian-Adams (Canon of Carlisle) highlighted a historical contradiction during his research into the parish. According to his findings, a man named F. Perry was appointed Rector of Borley in 1719 and held the title until his death in 1758. However, Perry never actually resided at Borley, and the Canon notes that Perry's parish work **"appears to have been done by Humphrey Burroughs from 1721 to 1757"** [5]. 

**The 1947 Vault Discovery**
Rev. Burrough's ultimate fate became a point of macabre fascination in the Borley literature following an accidental discovery made by the Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning and the author James Turner.
*   **The Excavation:** On **Whit-Monday, May 26, 1947**, Henning, his wife, Turner, and a local archaeologist named John Durrant were taking up the wooden floorboards in the sanctuary of Borley Church to prepare for the reinstatement of a pre-Reformation stone altar [1, 2]. 
*   **The Black Marble Tomb:** Directly beneath the floorboards, they discovered a perfectly preserved black marble tombstone [1, 2]. The stone was identified as the resting place of the Rev. Humphrey Burrough [1, 2].
*   **Breaking into the Vault:** Later that afternoon, while probing the loose sandy soil with a crowbar, James Turner suddenly broke through the brickwork into a hidden cavity [4, 6, 7]. The investigators noted that there was no escape of foul gas or air, and the brickwork of the vault was in pristine condition, looking **"as if it had been built the day before"** [4, 7]. 
*   **The Macabre Scene:** When the men lowered a torch on the end of a pole into the dark aperture, they were met with a gruesome sight. The wood of the coffins had entirely rotted and been eaten away [6, 7]. Without the coffins to hold them, **three skeletons were clearly visible, lying directly on top of one another** in the standing water [4, 6, 7]. The investigators assumed the remains were those of Rev. Burrough and his daughters [4, 6, 7]. 
*   *(Note on Contradiction: The sources conflict slightly on the depth of the water inside the vault; one account states the skeletons were lying in **"some 5ft of water"** [6], while other accounts describe it as **"about three feet of water"** [4, 7]).*
*   **Resealing:** Having disturbed the 200-year-old grave, the group sealed the hole the very next day, and new stone flooring was laid over the vault [4, 6, 7].
