# Rev. Humphrey Burrough MA

(Also spelled **Borough** or **Burroughs** in the texts.) Rector of [[borley-church|Borley]] from 1722 to 1757; prior to that, rector of St Gregory's church in Sudbury and headmaster of the local grammar school. One of the more historically significant of Borley's early incumbents, both because of his longevity in the post and because of his connection to the painter Thomas Gainsborough.

## Life and Career

In **1714**, Burrough held two positions concurrently: he was listed as the Rector of St Gregory's Church in Sudbury (a relatively large church in a parish adjoining Borley) and simultaneously served as the headmaster of the local grammar school [1, 2]. One of his pupils — and, more significantly, his nephew — was the painter **Thomas Gainsborough** (1727–1788), one of the most important British artists of the eighteenth century [1, 2].

Burrough's appointment as Rector of Borley in **1722** was likely in the nature of a semi-retirement from his Sudbury duties [4]. He held the Borley living for 35 years, dying in post in **1757** [1–3].

> **Contradiction:** Dr W. J. Phythian-Adams (Canon of Carlisle) highlighted a historical contradiction during his research: a man named F. Perry was apparently appointed Rector of Borley in 1719 and held the title until his death in 1758, but Perry never actually resided at Borley, and the Canon notes that Perry's parish work **"appears to have been done by Humphrey Burroughs from 1721 to 1757"** [5].

## Discovery of His Tomb and Vault (1947)

On **Whit-Monday, 26 May 1947**, the [[rev-ac-henning|Rev Alfred Clifford Henning]], his wife, author [[james-turner|James Turner]], and a local archaeologist named John Durrant were taking up the wooden floorboards in the sanctuary of [[borley-church|Borley Church]] to prepare for the reinstatement of a pre-Reformation stone altar [1, 2].

Directly beneath the floorboards, they discovered a perfectly preserved **black marble tombstone** identifying it as the resting place of Rev. Humphrey Burrough, MA [1, 2]. The sandy soil beneath was dug and probed; many old and presumably human bones were found and later re-interred in the churchyard.

Later that afternoon, while probing the loose sandy soil with a crowbar, James Turner suddenly broke through the brickwork into a hidden cavity [4, 6, 7]. The investigators noted that there was no escape of foul gas or air, and the brickwork of the vault was in pristine condition, looking **"as if it had been built the day before"** [4, 7]. When the men lowered a torch on the end of a pole into the dark aperture, they observed a gruesome sight: the wood of the coffins had entirely rotted and been eaten away [6, 7], and **three skeletons were clearly visible, lying directly on top of one another** in the standing water [4, 6, 7]. These were assumed to be the remains of Rev. Burrough and his daughters [4, 6, 7].

> **Contradiction:** The sources conflict on the depth of the water inside the vault; one account states the skeletons were lying in **"some 5ft of water"** [6], while other accounts describe it as **"about three feet of water"** [4, 7].

On the following day the aperture was sealed and the new stone flooring was put in place [4, 6, 7].

## Significance

The discovery of Burrough's vault confirmed that interments had indeed been made beneath the church floor. However, this small vault was clearly not the legendary crypt of the Waldegrave family, and the doubt about the existence of a larger crypt remained for another 40 years — until the [[borley-church-crypt|1988 discovery]].
