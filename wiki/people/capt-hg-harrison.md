# Captain H. G. Harrison

One of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s official observers at Borley Rectory during the 1937–38 tenancy. Harrison took part in the table-tipping and Planchette séance held at the Rectory on the night of November 20–21, 1937.

## Séance of November 20–21, 1937

Harrison sat alongside [[dr-hf-bellamy|Dr. H. F. Bellamy]], [[sidney-glanville|S. H. Glanville]], and [[roger-glanville|R. H. Glanville]] at this night séance in the Rectory. Two Planchette boards were used (Bellamy brought his own), and several unusual features were noted: some of the scripts were written in mirror-writing (backwards), and at one point the entity appeared to communicate that the "schoolroom window" would light up at midnight — though no observer saw it do so.

Key communications at this séance included confirmation that the dining-room window had been bricked up because of the Nun, an apparent offer from "Marie Lairre" to reveal whose skull had been found in the library cupboard (which the entity then refused to name), and a statement that the lights in the windows were caused by "Marie Lairre."

Harrison's role in the Borley record is otherwise not independently documented; he appears primarily as one of the group present at this specific séance. See [[communicating-entities]] for full séance context.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Captain H. G. Harrison** served as one of Harry Price's official observers during the year-long tenancy investigation of Borley Rectory [1]. Crucially, he was the **brother-in-law of Sidney H. Glanville**, who acted as Price's chief investigator [2, 3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals that Harrison participated in several highly eventful observational vigils during 1937, experiencing a wide array of phenomena:

**The Visit of August 14–15, 1937**
Harrison arrived at the Rectory with S. H. Glanville at 9:00 a.m. on Saturday, August 14, to inspect the house and grounds [3]. During this visit, they took photographs of four of the more elaborate wall writings [3]. During their routine evening patrols (between 9:40 p.m. and 1:00 a.m.), they **discovered fresh pencil marks** that had mysteriously appeared on a section of the wall they had left only shortly before [2, 3]. They also discovered that the Rectory's cats' cemetery had been inexplicably disturbed and dug over [4]. The only noises they noted during this visit were "a sharp crack and some scrabbling noises" [2]. Harrison and Glanville co-signed the official report detailing these events [3].

**The "Tripping Footsteps" (August 28–29, 1937)**
Harrison returned for an overnight stay alongside Sidney Glanville, Roger Glanville, and Squadron-Leader Horniman [5, 6]. During the night, between 11:00 p.m. and 4:00 a.m., the party reported hearing several light thuds or bumping noises that they could not locate [6, 7]. The most dramatic auditory phenomenon occurred while the two Glanvilles were out for a meal, leaving Harrison and Horniman on duty [7]. At exactly 8:24 p.m., while the two men were singing along to a gramophone record, **they "clearly heard the sound of light tripping footsteps coming down the main stairs,"** though nothing was visible [5, 7].

**The Visit of November 20–21, 1937**
Harrison visited the Rectory again with the Glanvilles and Dr. H. F. Bellamy [8, 9]. This visit yielded two notable incidents:
*   **The Swinging Blind Incident:** At approximately 8:25 p.m., while the men were stationed in the library (which served as the Base Room), they all noticed the window blind moving at the sides with a palpitating action, as if blown by a draught [9, 10]. Because all the doors to the room were shut, they tested for a draught by puffing cigarette smoke into the narrow gap between the blind and the window [9]. The smoke did not disperse, leading the observers to unanimously conclude that there was no normal draught to account for the inexplicable movement [9, 10].
*   **The Planchette Séance:** During this same visit, Harrison acted as an operator alongside the Glanvilles and Dr. Bellamy in a planchette séance [11]. The script recorded communications from an entity identifying itself as the former rector "Harry Bull," who claimed it was his father (Henry) who had ordered the dining-room window to be bricked up [11]. The operators also ostensibly contacted "Marie Lairre" (the phantom nun), who confirmed she was from France and predicted that the Rectory windows would inexplicably light up that night [11].
