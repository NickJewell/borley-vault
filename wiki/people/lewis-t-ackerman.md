# Lewis T. Ackerman (also spelled Ackermann)

A professional graphologist whose contribution to the Borley Rectory saga centred entirely on the forensic examination of the infamous [[wall-writings]]. His findings are cited in both the [[the-borley-report|SPR Report]] and in essay 20 of [[andrew-clarke|Andrew Clarke's]] *Sidelights* as expert confirmation that [[marianne-foyster]] was the author of the wall-writings.

## The Graphological Analysis

Ackermann conducted a detailed graphological analysis of the various messages, appeals, and scribbles that mysteriously appeared on the walls of the Rectory during the Foyster incumbency. After examining the scripts, he stated **"with conviction"** that, in his professional view, **"all the Borley scripts with the exception of 'Edwin' were executed by the same personality."**

The single exception — the word "Edwin" — is consistent with the possibility that [[dom-richard-whitehouse]] (Edwin Whitehouse) added it himself, as [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] later suggested Edwin was "in some way responsible" for the writings.

## Publication Context

Ackermann's analysis was originally prepared at the request of [[harry-price|Harry Price]] for inclusion in his projected but ultimately unfinished third book on the hauntings (*Borley III*). Following Price's death, investigator [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] intended to publish the analysis as part of the *Borley Symposium* — a collaborative volume abandoned in 1954. Consequently, Ackermann's report remained unprinted, surviving within Underwood's unpublished manuscript titled *The Borley Haunting 1947–1952*.

## Reactions from Other Researchers

- **[[canon-phythian-adams|Canon W. John Phythian-Adams]]:** The Canon of Carlisle wrote approximately 3,000 words of commentary specifically discussing Ackermann's graphological analysis, also slated for inclusion in the *Borley Symposium*. Phythian-Adams offered the alternative that an "entity borrowed the hand and arm of Mrs Foyster."
- **The SPR Investigators:** The authors of the 1956 *Borley Report* (Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) firmly agreed with Ackermann's assessment. They noted that a close examination of the letters 'M', 's', and 'r' across the various messages seemed "conclusive on the point" that a single person had written them.
- **Clarke's Essay:** Clarke uses the analysis in conjunction with Peter Underwood's submission of samples to a separate graphologist (who reached the same conclusion) and comparison photographs of Marianne's later signature against the wall-writing samples.

## Significance

Ackermann represents the professional graphological consensus that the wall-writings are in Marianne's hand. His analysis is technically stronger evidence than the visual comparisons offered by Clarke's essay, as it was conducted as a professional commission rather than a retrospective comparison.

> **Contested:** [[vincent-oneil|Vincent O'Neil]] (Marianne's son) examined the wall-writings against his mother's letters and stated: "The more I compare the wall writings to my mother's letters, the more convinced I become she was not responsible." Vince's opinion is a personal assessment, not a professional graphological analysis.
