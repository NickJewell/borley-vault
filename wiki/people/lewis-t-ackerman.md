# Lewis T. Ackerman

A professional graphologist who analyzed the [[wall-writings]] at [[borley-rectory]] and concluded that all of them, with a single exception, were produced by the same hand. His findings are cited in both the SPR Report and in essay 20 of [[andrew-clarke]]'s *Sidelights* as expert confirmation that [[marianne-foyster]] was the author of the wall-writings.

## Analysis

Ackerman's graphological analysis is quoted in the SPR Report (*The Haunting of Borley Rectory*, Dingwall, Goldney and Hall):

> *"In his analysis of the wall-writings prepared for Price's intended third book on Borley [never published], Mr. Lewis T. Ackerman states with conviction that in his view, that of a professional graphologist, all the Borley scripts with the exception of 'Edwin' were executed by the same personality."*
> — Dingwall, Goldney, Hall, *The Haunting of Borley Rectory*, p. 112, quoted in *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, essay 20

The single exception — the word "Edwin" — is consistent with the possibility that [[dom-richard-whitehouse]] (Edwin Whitehouse) added it himself, as Marianne later suggested Edwin was "in some way responsible" for the writings.

## Context

Ackerman's analysis was prepared for Harry Price's unpublished third Borley book, and was not available to Price's readers. Clarke uses it in conjunction with:
- Peter Underwood's submission of samples to a separate graphologist, who reached the same conclusion
- Comparison photographs of Marianne's later signature against the wall-writing samples
- Marianne's own shifting and contradictory denials of authorship

## Significance

Ackerman represents the professional graphological consensus that the wall-writings are in Marianne's hand. His analysis is technically stronger evidence than the visual comparisons offered by Clarke's essay, as it was conducted as a professional commission rather than a retrospective comparison.

> **Contested:** [[vincent-oneil]] (Marianne's son) examined the wall-writings against his mother's letters and stated: "The more I compare the wall writings to my mother's letters, the more convinced I become she was not responsible." Vince's opinion is a personal assessment, not a professional graphological analysis. [[dr-w-j-phythian-adams]] offered the alternative that an "entity borrowed the hand and arm of Mrs Foyster."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Lewis T. Ackermann** (also spelled **Ackerman** in the sources) was a **professional graphologist** whose contribution to the Borley Rectory saga centered entirely on the forensic examination of the infamous "wall writings" [1-4]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific details, conclusions, and publication history regarding his involvement:

**The Graphological Analysis and Key Conclusion**
Ackermann conducted a detailed graphological analysis of the various messages, appeals, and scribbles that mysteriously appeared on the walls of the Rectory during the Foyster incumbency [1, 2]. After examining the scripts, Ackermann provided a definitive expert opinion on their authorship. He stated **"with conviction"** that, in his professional view, **"all the Borley scripts with the exception of 'Edwin' were executed by the same personality"** [3, 4]. 

**Publication Context and the "Borley Symposium"**
Ackermann's analysis was originally prepared at the request of Harry Price for inclusion in his projected, but ultimately unfinished, third book on the hauntings (*Borley III*) [3]. 

Following Price's death, the investigator Peter Underwood intended to publish Ackermann's detailed analysis as part of the *Borley Symposium*—a collaborative volume that was ultimately abandoned in 1954 [2, 4, 5]. Consequently, Ackermann's report remained unprinted, surviving within Underwood's unpublished manuscript titled *The Borley Haunting 1947–1952* [1].

**Reactions from Other Researchers**
Ackermann's findings were well-received by other prominent Borley researchers:
*   **Canon W. John Phythian-Adams:** The Canon of Carlisle wrote approximately 3,000 words of commentary specifically discussing Ackermann's graphological analysis, which was also slated for inclusion in the *Borley Symposium* [1, 2, 6].
*   **The SPR Investigators:** The authors of the highly critical 1956 *Borley Report* (Eric J. Dingwall, Kathleen M. Goldney, and Trevor H. Hall) firmly agreed with Ackermann's assessment. They noted that his conclusion supported their theory of normal (human) agency, observing that a close examination of the letters **'M', 's', and 'r'** across the various messages seemed **"conclusive on the point"** that a single person had written them [7].
