# Mr and Mrs Fenton

Owners of a chemist's shop on **Worple Road, Wimbledon**, adjacent to [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]]'s flower shop [[jonquille-et-cie|Jonquille et Cie]] (No. 20a). They befriended Marianne during her Wimbledon years (February 1933 – November 1934) and received the most elaborate of all her fabricated biographies. Mrs Fenton is also referred to in the sources as **"Billy" Fenton**; the couple were Jewish.

## Marianne's Wimbledon Fantasy

Marianne told the Fentons she was not Mary Anne Emily Rebecca Shaw from Guy Wood Cottages, Romiley — she was **Marianne Monk**, daughter of **Santiago Monk** (Chilean diplomat) and the **Countess Sarah von Kiergraff**, late of Schleswig-Holstein.

> **Contradiction:** The sources record two different accounts of her claimed parentage. In one version she told the Fentons she was the daughter of the Chilean diplomat [[santiago-monk|Santiago Monk]]; in another she claimed to be the daughter of a **Count and Countess von Kiergraff of Schleswig-Holstein**. The contradictory lineages are recorded in different sources and likely represent the story evolving across multiple tellings.

The story wove real people from her life into a romantic soap-opera:

- [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Foyster]] became the family friend who had raised her in Canada (because her diplomat father was constantly away on business)
- [[francois-darles|François d'Arles]] became Foyster's adopted son — and therefore Marianne's brother; the Fentons believed him to be her husband
- [[sir-george-whitehouse|Sir George]] and [[lady-whitehouse|Lady Whitehouse]] became her cousins whom she visited at their "grand country seat," calling Sir George "Georgy Porgy dear"
- She claimed to be a graduate of a Cambridge women's college
- She claimed Santiago Monk (listed in diplomatic registers as Commercial Attaché at the Chilean Embassy until 1939) was her "spiritual godfather"

Mrs Fenton never doubted the tale. She later believed that Marianne had been employed by the British Secret Service during the Second World War.

## Lionel Foyster's Complicity

When the Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] visited Wimbledon, and later when Mrs Fenton visited the family in Suffolk, Foyster actively colluded in the deception by pretending to be Marianne's elderly father rather than her husband. He even expressed satisfaction to Mrs Fenton that Marianne had broken up with the "brutal d'Arles" and found a "nice chap" in [[henry-fisher|Henry Fisher]] — the man Marianne would later bigamously marry.

> "Foyster himself was moved to remark to Mrs. Fenton of Wimbledon that he was so glad that she had got rid of [d'Arles] and found a 'nice chap' like Fisher" — evidence of Foyster's collusion in the entire charade, since he was supposedly Marianne's father at the time.

A photograph (Plate 6 in Robert Wood's *The Widow of Borley*) exists showing Marianne alongside Mrs Fenton.

## After the Wimbledon Period

Mrs Fenton retained contact with Marianne after the shop closed in 1934. Marianne visited her in Wimbledon on subsequent trips to London, including from Ipswich and the Suffolk countryside. The Fentons later visited the Foysters at Snape (near Aldeburgh, Suffolk) following Foyster's retirement. After the [[death-of-lionel-foyster|death of Foyster]] in 1945 and Marianne's departure to America in August 1946, Marianne wrote to Mrs Fenton from [[hokah-minnesota|Hokah, Minnesota]] — falsely claiming she (not O'Neil) had been injured in a car crash on Highway 44.

## Marianne's Retrospective Justifications

Years later, investigator [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] tracked down and interviewed Mrs Fenton. When Marianne was subsequently asked about the lies she had told (by Robert Swanson in 1958 and in letters to [[ian-greenwood|Ian Greenwood]]), she offered the following justifications:

- She admitted her lies were **"flights of fancy; kind of a soap opera,"** adding: "It seems so horribly silly, but it seemed a lot of fun at the time to tell a continuous story." She called her behaviour "a wild form of fairy tales which did nobody any harm," though she conceded that "it looks God awful in print."
- Rather than apologising, she blamed Mrs Fenton for credulity. In a 1956 letter she described Mrs Fenton as "wild for excitement of knowing what where when and how before other folk." In her 1958 interview she stated: "I told her all kinds of silly things and if she'd had sense enough to say shut up and don't tell me fairy tales, it would have been much better."
- She also recalled that her relationship with [[francois-darles|Pearless]] was so controlling that "Every time I spoke to Mrs. Fenton or conversed with her by telephone, he'd start an argument."

## Significance

The Fenton relationship is Wood's richest case-study of Marianne's fantasy-building. Her "soap opera" (her own term) drew on every real connection she had — the Whitehouses, the Bulls, Cambridge, the Foyster family, the diplomatic world — and wove them into a coherent and plausible alternative identity. The Fentons represent the widest and most sustained audience for this persona.
