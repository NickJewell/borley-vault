# Mr and Mrs Fenton

Owners of a chemist's shop on **Worple Road, Wimbledon**, adjacent to Marianne's flower shop **Jonquille et Cie** (No. 20a). They befriended [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] during her Wimbledon years (1933–34) and received the most elaborate of all her fabricated biographies.

## Marianne's Wimbledon Fantasy

Marianne told the Fentons she was not Mary Anne Emily Rebecca Shaw from Guy Wood Cottages, Romiley — she was **Marianne Monk**, daughter of **Santiago Monk** (Chilean diplomat) and the **Countess Sarah von Kiergraff**, late of Schleswig-Holstein. The story wove real people from her life into a romantic soap-opera:

- [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Foyster]] became the family friend who had raised her in Canada
- [[francois-darles|François d'Arles]] became Foyster's adopted son (and therefore her brother)
- [[sir-george-whitehouse|Sir George]] and [[lady-whitehouse|Lady Whitehouse]] became her cousins whom she visited at their "grand country seat" Arthur Hall, calling Sir George "Georgy Porgy dear"
- She claimed to be a graduate of a Cambridge women's college
- She claimed [[santiago-monk|Santiago Monk]] (listed in diplomatic registers as Commercial Attaché at the Chilean Embassy until 1939) was her "spiritual godfather"

Mrs Fenton never doubted the tale. She later believed that Marianne had been employed by the British Secret Service during the Second World War.

## After the Wimbledon Period

Mrs Fenton retained contact with Marianne after the shop closed in 1934. Marianne visited her in Wimbledon on subsequent trips to London, including from Ipswich and the Suffolk countryside. After the [[death-of-lionel-foyster|death of Foyster]] in 1945 and Marianne's departure to America, Marianne wrote to Mrs Fenton from Hokah, Minnesota — falsely claiming she (not O'Neil) had been injured in a car crash on Highway 44.

> "Foyster himself was moved to remark to Mrs. Fenton of Wimbledon that he was so glad that she had got rid of [d'Arles] and found a 'nice chap' like Fisher" — evidence of Foyster's collusion in the entire charade, since he was supposedly Marianne's father at the time.

## Significance

The Fenton relationship is Wood's richest case-study of Marianne's fantasy-building. Her "soap opera" (her own term) drew on every real connection she had — the Whitehouses, the Bulls, Cambridge, the Foyster family, the diplomatic world — and wove them into a coherent and plausible alternative identity. The Fentons represent the widest and most sustained audience for this persona.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mr. and Mrs. Fenton** (Mrs. Fenton is also specifically referred to as **“Billy” Fenton**) were a Jewish couple who became friends with Marianne Foyster during her time living in London in the 1930s. Their involvement in the Borley Rectory saga centers entirely on their role as unwitting and highly credulous audiences to a series of spectacular lies concocted by Marianne regarding her background and domestic arrangements. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific biographical details, events, quotes, and contradictions regarding their involvement:

**Biographical Details and Friendship Timeline**
*   **Occupation and Residence:** Mr. and Mrs. Fenton owned and ran a chemist’s shop in Wimbledon, South London, located in the same parade of shops on Worple Road as *Jonquille et Cie*, the florist shop that Marianne Foyster ran with her lover Frank Pearless (alias François d’Arles) [1, 2]. 
*   **Duration of Contact:** The Fentons befriended Marianne while the flower shop was in business between February 1933 and November 1934 [1, 3]. The friendship endured after the business failed; the Fentons later visited the Foysters at Snape (near Aldeburgh, Suffolk) following the Rev. Lionel Foyster's retirement, and they continued to correspond for a time after Marianne moved to America in August 1946 [1]. 
*   **Photographic Record:** A photograph (Plate 6 in Robert Wood's *The Widow of Borley*) exists showing Marianne alongside Mrs. Fenton [4]. 

**The "Soap Opera" Deceptions**
Marianne Foyster used the Fentons as a primary outlet for her fantasies, feeding Mrs. Fenton a series of wild, fictional stories about her life, which Mrs. Fenton apparently never doubted [2, 3, 5]. The sources highlight the following specific deceptions:
*   **Contradictory Lineage:** Marianne told the Fentons highly romanticized (and contradictory) versions of her parentage. One source notes she claimed to be the daughter of the **Chilean diplomat Santiago Monk** [6], while another records she told Mrs. Fenton she was the daughter of **Count and Countess von Kiergraff of Schleswig-Holstein** [2]. 
*   **Education and Career:** She falsely claimed to be a graduate of a Cambridge ladies college [2, 6]. Because Mrs. Fenton believed these tall tales so implicitly, she later even came to believe that Marianne had been employed by the **British Secret Service** during the Second World War [5]. 
*   **The False Husband and Father:** Because Marianne was living as man and wife with Frank Pearless during the week, the Fentons believed Pearless was her husband, and Marianne further elaborated that he was the Rev. Lionel Foyster's son [3, 6]. 
*   **Lionel Foyster's Complicity:** When the Rev. Lionel Foyster visited Wimbledon, and later when Mrs. Fenton visited Suffolk, **Foyster actively colluded in the deception by happily pretending to be Marianne’s elderly father** rather than her husband [5, 7]. Marianne explained this by telling the Fentons that Foyster had raised her because her diplomat father was constantly away on business [6]. Foyster even went so far as to express his satisfaction to Mrs. Fenton that Marianne had broken up with the "brutal d’Arles" and found a "nice chap" in Henry Fisher (the man Marianne would later bigamously marry) [5]. 

**Marianne’s Retrospective Quotes and Opinions**
Years later, investigator Trevor Hall tracked down and interviewed Mrs. Fenton [2]. When Marianne was subsequently interviewed about the lies she told the Fentons (specifically by Robert Swanson in 1958 and in letters to Ian Greenwood), she offered the following justifications and quotes regarding Mrs. Fenton's credulity:
*   **"A Soap Opera":** Marianne admitted her lies were **"flights of fancy; kind of a soap opera,"** noting: **"It seems so horribly silly, but it seemed a lot of fun at the time to tell a continuous story"** [2, 4]. She called her behavior **"a wild form of fairy tales which did nobody any harm,"** though she conceded that **"it looks God awful in print"** [6]. 
*   **Blaming Mrs. Fenton:** Rather than apologizing, Marianne blamed Mrs. Fenton for being too gullible. In a 1956 letter, she described Mrs. Fenton as being **"wild for excitement of knowing what where when and how before other folk"** [6]. In her 1958 interview, she explicitly stated: **"I told her all kinds of silly things and if she’d had sense enough to say shut up and don’t tell me fairy tales, it would have been much better"** [4]. 
*   **Pearless's Jealousy:** Marianne also recalled that her relationship with Pearless was so dictatorial that **"Every time I spoke to Mrs. Fenton or conversed with her by telephone, he'd start an argument"** [7].
