# Mrs Jean Clark

Of Fobbing, Essex, described as an expert in transporting dogs to any part of the world. Clark's experience near Borley is notable primarily because she had no prior knowledge of the haunting when it occurred, and only realised the significance some months later.

## Encounter (Late November 1950)

On a dark and drizzly evening, Clark was driving alone through the Suffolk-Essex border on her way back from Bury St Edmunds, accompanied only by her cocker spaniel Bugg in the back seat. She lost her way and pulled up to consult her map by dashboard light.

Suddenly her dog began to howl. She looked up from the map and noticed a man sitting in the front passenger seat who had not been there before — dressed in a long, old-fashioned fawn coat. She assumed he must have been involved in an accident and had climbed in while she was engrossed in the map. When he pointed forward, she started the car; but after about forty yards he motioned her to stop. She then became aware that the atmosphere in the car had become "very cold." As the car came to a standstill, her passenger "just 'seemed to float' through the door."

All the time the figure was in the car, Bugg did not cease howling. For the rest of Clark's ownership of that car, "Bugg would never get into it voluntarily but had to be lifted into the vehicle."

Shortly after the figure disappeared, Clark discovered she was in the village of Borley. At the time this meant nothing to her — during the years of Harry Price's investigation she had lived in Germany. Only some months later, reading that Borley was reputed to be haunted by ghosts other than the [[the-nun|Nun]], did she realise she might have given a lift to one.

Clark told Underwood and Tabori that she "still has an open mind on the subject of ghosts, but she would never laugh off ghost stories at Borley" after her own experience.
