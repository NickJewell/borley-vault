# Mrs Jean Clark

Of Fobbing, Essex, described as an expert in transporting dogs to any part of the world. Clark's experience near Borley is notable primarily because she had no prior knowledge of the haunting when it occurred, and only realised the significance some months later.

## Encounter (Late November 1950)

On a dark and drizzly evening, Clark was driving alone through the Suffolk-Essex border on her way back from Bury St Edmunds, accompanied only by her cocker spaniel Bugg in the back seat. She lost her way and pulled up to consult her map by dashboard light.

Suddenly her dog began to howl. She looked up from the map and noticed a man sitting in the front passenger seat who had not been there before — dressed in a long, old-fashioned fawn coat. She assumed he must have been involved in an accident and had climbed in while she was engrossed in the map. When he pointed forward, she started the car; but after about forty yards he motioned her to stop. She then became aware that the atmosphere in the car had become "very cold." As the car came to a standstill, her passenger "just 'seemed to float' through the door."

All the time the figure was in the car, Bugg did not cease howling. For the rest of Clark's ownership of that car, "Bugg would never get into it voluntarily but had to be lifted into the vehicle."

Shortly after the figure disappeared, Clark discovered she was in the village of Borley. At the time this meant nothing to her — during the years of Harry Price's investigation she had lived in Germany. Only some months later, reading that Borley was reputed to be haunted by ghosts other than the [[the-nun|Nun]], did she realise she might have given a lift to one.

Clark told Underwood and Tabori that she "still has an open mind on the subject of ghosts, but she would never laugh off ghost stories at Borley" after her own experience.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mrs. Jean Clark** (frequently spelled **Mrs. Jean Clarke** in some entries) was a resident of Fobbing, Essex, who contributed one of the most bizarre and spectacular post-fire anecdotes to the Borley saga. Her involvement centers entirely on an alleged "phantom hitchhiker" experience. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following biographical details, specific events, dates, quotes, and contradictions regarding her experience:

**Biographical Details and Background**
*   **Occupation:** She was an expert in transporting dogs to any part of the world [1]. 
*   **Wartime Experience:** The *Sunday Express* newspaper reported that she was a **former Second World War British spy who saw action in Nazi Germany** [2]. The texts confirm that during the years Harry Price was actively investigating the Borley hauntings, she was indeed living in Germany [3].
*   **Pet:** She traveled with her cocker spaniel, named **Bugg** [1]. 

**The Phantom Hitchhiker Incident**
The specific event occurred on a dark, drizzly winter evening as she was driving alone (with her dog) from Bury St Edmunds en route to Lavenham [1, 2]. 
*   Having lost her way, she pulled over in a lonely hamlet to read a road sign and check her map by the light of the dashboard [1, 2]. 
*   Suddenly, her dog Bugg began to howl. He stood up tense in the back of the car, his fur bristling, staring at the front passenger seat [1]. 
*   Looking over, Mrs. Clark saw a man sitting beside her. He was dressed in a long, old-fashioned fawn coat with a cape collar [1, 2]. 

**Interaction and Quotes**
Showing remarkable "nerves of steel," Mrs. Clark did not panic [2]. She thought it strange she hadn't heard him enter the car, but assumed she had been too engrossed in her map [1]. 
*   She rationalized the sudden appearance, providing the following quote: **"I thought he just wanted a lift and put him down as a village yokel"** [2]. She also assumed he might have been stunned by a bad accident [1]. 
*   She promptly asked the figure the way to Sudbury, and the apparition silently gestured forward [1, 2].
*   She drove about forty yards further down the road before the man motioned for her to stop. At this point, she realized the atmosphere inside the car had become intensely cold [1, 2]. 
*   Describing his departure, she explicitly stated: **"I pulled up and as I turned to put my handbrake on, he just went straight through the door, and disappeared"** [2]. (Another account notes the passenger "seemed to float" through the closed door [1]). 

**Aftermath and Realization**
*   Immediately after the figure vanished, she drove a little further and came across a corporeal man walking down the road. She stopped and asked him, **"What is this place?"** to which he replied, **"Borley"** [2].
*   Because she had lived in Germany during the height of the Rectory's fame, the name Borley meant nothing to her at the time [3]. It was only months later, after reading about other ghosts at Borley (besides the famous Nun), that she realized she might have given a lift to a phantom [3].
*   Although Mrs. Clark maintained an open mind on the subject of ghosts, she noted that she would **never laugh off ghost stories at Borley after her own experience** [3]. 
*   The incident left a profound psychological mark on her dog; for the rest of the time she owned that particular car, Bugg would never get into it voluntarily and had to be physically lifted inside [3]. 

**Chronological and Descriptive Contradictions**
The sources contain notable contradictions regarding the exact date of this event and the specific appearance of the ghost, highlighting how stories may have been altered by the press:
*   **The Date Discrepancy:** The exact date of her drive through Borley is confused in the records. One chronological entry firmly places the event in **late November 1949** [4]. However, another detailed account of the event dates it to **late November 1950** [1]. 
*   **Media Sensationalism and the "Headless" Man:** The story was not made public until it was published in the *Sunday Express* in **January 1956**, around the same time Mrs. Clark wrote a personal account to investigator Peter Underwood [2]. While her account to Underwood simply describes a man in an old-fashioned coat [1], the *Sunday Express* sensationalized the story by claiming the figure was a **"headless man"** [2]. Authors of the texts suggest that her dramatic background as a "British spy" and the "headless" detail were likely journalistic embellishments [2].
