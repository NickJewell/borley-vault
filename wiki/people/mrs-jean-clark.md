# Mrs Jean Clark

Also spelled **Mrs Jean Clarke** in some sources. Of Fobbing, Essex; expert in transporting dogs to any part of the world. The *Sunday Express* reported that she was a former Second World War British spy who had seen action in Nazi Germany — a detail that may have been journalistic embellishment. During the years Harry Price was actively investigating the Borley hauntings, she was indeed living in Germany.

Clark's experience near Borley is notable primarily because she had no prior knowledge of the haunting when it occurred, and only realised the significance some months later.

## The Phantom Hitchhiker Incident

> **Contradiction:** The exact date of her drive through Borley is recorded inconsistently in the sources. One chronological entry places the event in **late November 1949**; another detailed account dates it to **late November 1950**. The event was not made public until published in the *Sunday Express* in January 1956, around the same time Clark wrote a personal account to investigator [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]].

On a dark, drizzly winter evening, Clark was driving alone — accompanied only by her cocker spaniel **Bugg** in the back seat — en route from Bury St Edmunds toward Lavenham. She lost her way and pulled over to read a road sign and check her map by dashboard light.

Suddenly her dog began to howl, standing up tense in the back of the car with fur bristling, staring at the front passenger seat. Looking over, Clark saw a man sitting beside her — dressed in a long, old-fashioned fawn coat with a cape collar. She assumed he must have been involved in an accident and had climbed in while she was engrossed in the map: "I thought he just wanted a lift and put him down as a village yokel."

She asked the figure the way to Sudbury, and the apparition silently gestured forward. She drove about forty yards further before the man motioned her to stop. At this point, she realised the atmosphere inside the car had become intensely cold. Describing his departure: "I pulled up and as I turned to put my handbrake on, he just went straight through the door, and disappeared." Another account notes the passenger "seemed to float" through the closed door.

## Aftermath and Realization

Immediately after the figure vanished, Clark drove a little further and came across a man walking down the road. She asked him, "What is this place?" to which he replied, "Borley." Because she had lived in Germany during the height of the Rectory's fame, the name meant nothing to her at the time. It was only months later, after reading about other ghosts at Borley besides the famous [[the-nun|Nun]], that she realised she might have given a lift to a phantom.

The incident left a profound mark on Bugg: for the rest of Clark's ownership of that car, "Bugg would never get into it voluntarily but had to be lifted into the vehicle."

Clark told [[peter-underwood|Underwood]] and Tabori that she "still has an open mind on the subject of ghosts, but she would never laugh off ghost stories at Borley" after her own experience.

## The "Headless" Discrepancy

> **Contradiction:** The *Sunday Express* sensationalised the story by describing the phantom as a **"headless man"**, while Clark's own account to Underwood simply describes a man in an old-fashioned coat. The "headless" detail is likely a journalistic embellishment, along with her characterisation as a "British spy."
