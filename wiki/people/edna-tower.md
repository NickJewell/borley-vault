# Edna Tower

Sister of [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide Tower Cartwright]] (formerly Barbara Tower); one of the seven Tower children left orphaned in 1928 when their parents [[alice-tower|Alice]] and [[seward-tower|Seward Tower]] died in succession.

## Contact with Adelaide

[[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] had instructed Adelaide to treat Edna as a *cousin*, not a sister, suppressing the family connection. Edna did correspond with Adelaide regularly, but after [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]]'s death in 1945, when Marianne moved to Ipswich and eventually left for America, the letters stopped and the last one was returned marked "Address Unknown." For twenty-nine years the Tower siblings heard nothing.

## Role in Adelaide's Rediscovery

When [[paulene-mitchell|Paulene Mitchell]] wrote to a New Brunswick newspaper in search of information about Adelaide, Edna was the one who responded. She explained to Paulene that:

1. Adelaide had unhappy memories of her life at Borley Rectory and did not want to talk about it.
2. There were very bad feelings between the Tower family and Marianne.
3. Marianne had never told Adelaide that she had a family of blood relatives in North America.

Edna's response to Paulene's letter set in motion the process that eventually led to [[wallace-tower|Wallace Tower]] finding Adelaide in 1974.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Edna Tower** was the older biological sister of Adelaide Foyster (who was originally named Barbara Tower before her adoption by the Rev. Lionel and Marianne Foyster) [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details and events regarding her life and her connection to the case:

**Family Tragedy and Separation**
Edna was one of seven children born to Seward and Alice Tower, a farming family from the isolated rural village of Westcock, New Brunswick, Canada [1]. In January of 1929, the family suffered a double tragedy when Edna's mother died of a brain tumour; just a month later, her father fractured his skull and died after falling off a load of marsh hay on his farm [1]. The children were left as orphans [1]. While the infant Barbara (Adelaide) was taken in by the Foysters, Edna and her other siblings (Wallace, Fred, Royal, and Merton) were **"parcelled out among their various aunts and uncles"** [1]. 

**Marianne's Deception and Censored Letters**
A state of **"very bad feelings"** existed between the Tower family and Marianne Foyster [2]. Marianne actively deceived her adopted daughter, telling Adelaide that she was an only child and deliberately concealing the fact that she had a family of blood relatives in North America [2]. 

Despite the separation, Edna wrote letters to Adelaide regularly [3]. However, to maintain the deception, **Marianne forced Edna to refer to herself in these letters only as a "cousin, not a sister"** [3]. 

**Loss of Contact (1945)**
Following the death of Lionel Foyster in 1945, all correspondence between the sisters abruptly stopped [3]. The final letter that Edna sent to Adelaide was returned to her in Canada marked **"Address Unknown"** [3]. As a result of Marianne cutting ties, Edna and her siblings heard absolutely nothing about their youngest sister for the next twenty-nine years [3]. 

**Contact with Researchers (1976)**
Edna Tower eventually played a role in helping psychical researchers uncover the truth about Marianne Foyster's past. In 1976, a Toronto book reviewer and researcher named Paulene Mitchell wrote a letter to a local New Brunswick newspaper asking for information regarding Adelaide Foyster's whereabouts [2]. Within a couple of weeks, Mitchell was successfully put in touch with Edna Tower [2]. Edna provided the researcher with crucial family background and informed Mitchell that **Adelaide had very unhappy memories of her life at Borley Rectory and did not want to talk about it** [2].
