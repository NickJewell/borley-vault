# Dr Alec Ritchie

A medical practitioner who ran a practice in the nearby village of Cavendish [1]. His connection to the Borley Rectory haunting stems from his professional visits to the house during the Foyster incumbency between October 1930 and October 1935, as [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] was one of his patients [1]. Both Dr Ritchie and his son Ted have since died.

## Source of Testimony

Because both Dr Ritchie and his son Ted have passed away, the historical record of the doctor's experiences was preserved through oral testimony [1]. Ted used to accompany his father on patient house calls; years later, Ted recounted their paranormal encounters at Borley to a friend, Nancy Goodale, who subsequently relayed the information to Borley investigators [[edward-babbs|Babbs]] and [[claudine-mathias|Mathias]] [1].

## The Airborne Medicine Bottle

On a visit to [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] who was ill and in bed, Dr Ritchie witnessed a **medicine bottle rise from the bedside table, hover, and return** — without breaking and without any human assistance [2]. He was "quite definite" that Marianne remained in bed and did not stretch her arm out or touch the bottle in any way [2]. The authors note this is consistent with [[poltergeist-activity|poltergeist phenomena]] reported by other witnesses during the Foyster period: it occurred when Marianne could not have directly manipulated the object. Investigators have used the doctor's absolute certainty regarding this event to argue that, despite Marianne's later admissions to faking certain manifestations, genuine paranormal activity may have occurred around her [2].

## The Staircase Force

On another visit to a bedridden Marianne, after leaving her room and descending the main staircase, the doctor experienced the sensation of being **forced and pushed down the stairs** by an invisible presence — with the strong impression that some entity wanted him to leave the house. No one else was on the staircase (single witness).

## Car Engine Failure

Dr Ritchie also reported a strange mechanical anomaly outside the Rectory. While driving along Hall Lane, specifically on the stretch of road **between the church and the rectory**, his engine cut out for no apparent reason [3]. Once the doctor managed to get the vehicle moving again, the car performed perfectly and had no further problems [3].

A lesser-known detail: **years later, his son Ted experienced the exact same mechanical failure** at the same location [3]. While driving his own car through Borley, Ted's engine also inexplicably died, an anomaly that similarly did not repeat once he was away from the village [3]. This pattern of vehicle malfunction at Borley is discussed by Babbs and Mathias in connection with the [[ley-lines-at-borley|ley lines]] hypothesis.
