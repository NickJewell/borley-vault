# Dr Alec Ritchie

A medical practitioner with a practice at Cavendish, another nearby village. Both Dr Ritchie and his son Ted have died. His son Ted described his father's experiences to Nancy Goodale, who relayed them on behalf of Babbs and Mathias. One of Dr Ritchie's patients during the Foyster period was Marianne Foyster.

## The Airborne Medicine Bottle

On a visit to [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] who was ill and in bed, Dr Ritchie witnessed a **medicine bottle rise from the bedside table, hover, and return** — without breaking and without any human assistance. He stated definitively that Marianne remained in bed and did not touch the bottle.

The authors note this is consistent with [[poltergeist-activity|poltergeist phenomena]] reported by other witnesses during the Foyster period: it occurred when Marianne could not have directly manipulated the object.

## The Staircase Force

On another visit to a bedridden Marianne, after leaving her room and descending the main staircase, the doctor experienced the sensation of being **forced and pushed down the stairs** by an invisible presence — with the strong impression that some entity wanted him to leave the house. No one else was on the staircase.

## Car Engine Failure

While driving along Hall Lane and reaching the section **between the church and the rectory**, Dr Ritchie's engine cut out without explanation. When he got the car moving again, it performed normally elsewhere. His son Ted had a similar experience at the same location years later. This pattern of vehicle malfunction at Borley is discussed by Babbs and Mathias in connection with the [[ley-lines-at-borley|ley lines]] hypothesis.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Dr Alec Ritchie** was a medical practitioner who ran a practice in the nearby village of Cavendish [1]. His connection to the Borley Rectory haunting stems from his professional visits to the house during the Foyster incumbency between October 1930 and October 1935, as Marianne Foyster was one of his patients [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details and paranormal events regarding his experiences:

**Source of Testimony**
Because both Dr Ritchie and his son Ted have passed away, the historical record of the doctor's experiences was preserved through oral testimony [1]. In his late teens, Ted used to accompany his father on patient house calls; years later, Ted recounted his and his father's paranormal encounters at Borley to a friend, Nancy Goodale, who subsequently relayed the information to Borley investigators [1]. 

**The Levitating Medicine Bottle**
Dr Ritchie claimed to have witnessed two examples of paranormal phenomena inside the Rectory [2]. The most famous of these occurred while he was visiting Marianne Foyster in her bedroom [2]. While Marianne was lying in bed, Dr Ritchie watched as **a medicine bottle placed on a bedside table rose up into the air and then came down again, without breaking and without any human assistance** [2]. Dr Ritchie was "quite definite" that Marianne had remained in bed and did not stretch her arm out or touch the bottle in any way [2]. Investigators have used the doctor's absolute certainty regarding this event to argue that, despite Marianne's later admissions to faking certain manifestations, genuine paranormal activity did occur around her [2].

*(Note: While our previous conversation mentioned Dr Ritchie experiencing a sensation of being pushed down a staircase, the provided sources do not record this; instead, they detail the medicine bottle and the following automotive incident as his primary encounters).*

**The Car Engine Failure**
Dr Ritchie also reported a strange mechanical anomaly outside the Rectory. While driving along Hall Lane, specifically on the stretch of road between Borley Church and the Rectory, **the engine of his car cut out for no apparent reason** [3]. Once the doctor managed to get the vehicle moving again, the car performed perfectly and he had no further engine problems elsewhere [3]. 

A lesser-known detail about this event is that it was seemingly hereditary: **years later, his son Ted experienced the exact same mechanical failure** [3]. While driving his own car through Borley, Ted's engine also inexplicably died, an anomaly that similarly did not repeat itself once he was away from the village [3].
