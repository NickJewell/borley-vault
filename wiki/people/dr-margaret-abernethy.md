# Dr Margaret Abernethy

Senior partner in a medical group based at [[long-melford|Long Melford]]; the family doctor to the [[rev-lanfranc-mathias|Mathias family]] during their incumbency at Borley and Liston. [[claudine-mathias|CM]] describes her as "a blunt, no-nonsense Scotswoman" — a highly credible witness [1, 2]. Because of her pragmatic nature and professional standing, she is considered one of the most credible and responsible witnesses to the Borley phenomena [3].

## The Nun Sighting — 26 July 1949

Driving up Hall Lane towards Borley to visit a patient, Dr Abernethy saw the figure of a nun standing by the hedge at the point outside the former rectory garden [2, 4]. Initially assuming the figure was a real person, she continued driving for a moment before deciding she ought to stop and offer the woman a lift [1, 2]. She reversed her car to the exact spot but found that the figure had completely vanished [1, 2]. Even after getting out of her car to conduct a quick search of the deserted area, she found no trace of anybody [1, 3].

She provided highly specific visual details about the apparition, insisting that the figure was **"solid in appearance and not in the least wraith-like"** [2]. She noted that she plainly saw the figure's face and described it as appearing sad; she estimated the nun to be **around 40 years old** [1–3, 5].

The sighting was published in [[peter-underwood|Underwood]] and [[paul-tabori|Tabori]]'s *The Ghosts of Borley* (1973).

She appeared in a television programme (*About Religion*, transmitted **31 October 1955**) describing this experience (multiple sources record this as a BBC transmission, though the source also cites ITV) [1, 2, 5].

## The Vanishing Rosary

Not previously published before *The Final Analysis*: Dr Abernethy later drove up Hall Lane and, at exactly the same spot as the 1949 sighting, saw what appeared to be a **rosary lying shining on the road** [6]. She stopped, got out to pick it up — and it had vanished [6]. [[claudine-mathias|CM]] notes the Roman Catholic theme of the rosary is consistent with the broader pattern of the haunting.

## The Age of the Nun

Her estimate that the nun appeared about 40 years old is significant. It conflicts with the [[marie-lairre]] Planchette theory (which describes a young novice of about 19, murdered in 1667) but might indicate a different entity — perhaps a nun associated with the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave]] household, who were staunch Catholics. [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth|Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth]] similarly estimated the nun appeared elderly during his 1970 sighting.

Following the incident, Dr Abernethy and the local rector, the [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning]], made numerous local inquiries but could find absolutely no record of any real nun visiting Borley that day [1, 2]. Investigators frequently compare her account to the 1927 sightings made by the carpenter [[fred-cartwright|Fred Cartwright]], who similarly encountered a solid-looking nun near the Rectory gate who inexplicably vanished when he stopped to offer assistance [7].
