# Dr Margaret Abernethy

Senior partner in a medical group based at Long Melford; the family doctor to the [[rev-lanfranc-mathias|Mathias family]] during their incumbency at Borley and Liston. [[claudine-mathias|CM]] describes her as "a blunt, no-nonsense Scotswoman" — a highly credible witness.

## The Nun Sighting — 26 July 1949

Driving up Hall Lane towards Borley to visit a patient, Dr Abernethy saw the figure of a nun standing by the hedge at the point outside the rectory garden. She drove past for a few yards, then thought she should offer the nun a lift and reversed. No one was to be seen. She described the figure as **solid in appearance, not wraith-like**, and said she had a clear view of the face: the nun appeared to be **about 40 years old**. Both she and her predecessor [[rev-ac-henning]] made local enquiries to find whether any nun had been in the area that day, without success.

The sighting was published in [[peter-underwood|Underwood]] and [[paul-tabori|Tabori]]'s *The Ghosts of Borley* (1973).

She appeared in an ITV programme (*About Religion*) transmitted on **31 October 1955** describing this experience.

## The Vanishing Rosary

Not previously published before *The Final Analysis*: Dr Abernethy later drove up Hall Lane and, at exactly the same spot as the 1949 sighting, saw what appeared to be a **rosary lying shining on the road**. She stopped, got out to pick it up — and it had vanished. CM notes the Roman Catholic theme of the rosary is consistent with the broader pattern of the haunting.

## The Age of the Nun

Her estimate that the nun appeared about 40 years old is significant. It conflicts with the [[marie-lairre]] Planchette theory (which describes a young novice of about 19, murdered in 1667) but might indicate a different entity — perhaps a nun associated with the Waldegrave household, who were staunch Catholics. [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth]] similarly estimated the nun appeared elderly during his 1970 sighting.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Dr Margaret Abernethy** was a local physician who served as the senior partner in a medical group based in nearby Long Melford, acting as the family general practitioner for many in the district [1, 2]. The sources consistently describe her as a **"blunt, no-nonsense Scotswoman"** who was caring and efficient in her profession [1, 2]. Because of her pragmatic nature and professional standing, she is considered one of the most credible and responsible witnesses to the Borley phenomena [3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following paranormal events and details regarding her involvement:

**The Sighting of the Phantom Nun (July 26, 1949)**
Dr. Abernethy’s most famous encounter occurred on **July 26, 1949**, while she was driving up Hall Lane to visit a patient in Borley village [2, 4]. As she passed the site of the former Rectory, she observed the figure of a **stooping nun standing close to the hedge** [1, 4]. Initially assuming the figure was a real person, she continued driving for a moment before deciding she ought to stop and offer the woman a lift [1, 2]. She reversed her car to the exact spot but found that the figure had completely vanished [1, 2]. Even after getting out of her car to conduct a quick search of the deserted area, she found no trace of anybody [1, 3].

She provided highly specific visual details about the apparition, insisting that the figure was **"solid in appearance and not in the least wraith-like"** [2]. She noted that she plainly saw the figure's face, describing her as looking sad, and estimated the nun to be **around 40 years old** [1-3, 5]. Following the incident, Dr. Abernethy and the local rector, the Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning, made numerous local inquiries but could find absolutely no record of any real nun visiting Borley that day [1, 2].

**The Vanishing Rosary**
A lesser-known detail—which was not widely reported—is that Dr. Abernethy experienced a second uncanny event at the exact same location some years later, following her television appearance [6]. While driving up the hill towards Borley, she noticed a shining object lying on the road precisely where she had previously seen the nun [6]. As she drew closer, she recognized the object as a **rosary** [6]. She stopped her car and got out with the intention of retrieving it, but **as she reached for the rosary, it vanished entirely** [6]. 

**Public Accounts and Television**
Dr. Abernethy never forgot her experience and publicly recounted her story of the phantom nun on a **BBC television programme about the Borley haunting, which was broadcast on October 31, 1955** [1, 2, 5]. Investigators frequently compare her account to the 1927 sightings made by the carpenter Fred Cartwright, who similarly encountered a solid-looking nun near the Rectory gate who inexplicably vanished when he stopped to offer assistance [7].
