# R. Hawkin

R. Hawkin visited [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in February 1938 with [[m-knox|M. P. Knox]]. Together they entered the house via the cellars, confirming that the building was easily accessible without a key throughout [[harry-price|Price]]'s tenancy — a fact potentially relevant to "fresh" markings and moved objects discovered during visits.

Nothing further about Hawkin is recorded in the SPR report.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**R. Hawkin** (also referred to in the texts as **Hawkins** and likely named **Robert**) was an undergraduate student at Oxford University who served as an official observer during Harry Price's tenancy at Borley Rectory. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, specific dates, events, quotes, and textual contradictions regarding his involvement:

**Biographical Details and Affiliations**
*   **Education:** He was an undergraduate at **University College, Oxford** [1]. 
*   **First Name:** Though usually referred to as "R. Hawkin," he signed his official report to Harry Price as **"Robt. Hawk."**, strongly indicating his first name was Robert [2]. 

**The February 1938 Vigil**
Hawkin participated in a single recorded observation period at the Rectory.
*   He visited the site overnight on **February 16–17, 1938**, alongside three other University College undergraduates: S.G. Welles (a Rhodes Scholar), Samuel J. Millberg, and M.P. Knox [1, 3]. 
*   During this visit, the group reported several phenomena, including a luminous patch on the Blue Room ceiling, unexplained footsteps, and the sudden appearance of objects [1, 3]. 

**Specific Incidents and Observations**
Hawkin was directly involved in investigating the appearance of objects and auditory phenomena during the night:
*   **The Wooden Lath and Dried Grass:** At one point, Welles and Milberg found a 42-inch wooden lath on the scullery floor near a crumpled cigarette card. When they brought Hawkin to see it, he confirmed that he remembered the card from a previous round but had not seen the lath [4]. On the next round at **10:20 p.m.**, Hawkin and Welles found a 9-inch piece of dried grass lying perfectly parallel to the lath. Because Hawkin and Knox had just come from the long dry grasses of the summer-house path, Welles theorized that Hawkin or Knox might have unconsciously tracked it in on the sole of their shoe, though he noted it was strange that the grass fell so perfectly parallel without anyone stepping on the chalked lath [5].
*   **The Base Room Footsteps:** Shortly after midnight, Hawkin was left alone in the Base Room while the others were in distant parts of the house. At **12:20 a.m.**, Milberg heard a noise that sounded like "somebody is throwing things against the floor carelessly" and assumed it was Hawkin; however, upon checking, Hawkin declared he had made no noise [6]. 

**Direct Quotes**
Because S.G. Welles took the time to write a highly detailed log of the night's events, Hawkin sent a shorter confirmation report from University College. He provided the following specific quote regarding what he heard while alone:
> **"I confirm the report of Mr. Welles with whom I was most of the night, making particular mention of (a) We clearly heard footsteps at 12:17, when I was alone in the Base Room, and the others in distant parts of the house. The footsteps appeared to come from the kitchen, along the passage to the front hall, and towards the drawing-room. I imagined at the time that it was one of the other investigators and, therefore, did not leave the Base Room; and (b) the peculiar thumping noise at 12.30 a.m. (approx.), which could not be traced to any apparent cause."** [2].

**Textual Contradictions**
The sources exhibit a glaring inconsistency regarding his surname. Within S.G. Welles's single narrative account, his name alternates unpredictably between **"Hawkin"** and **"Hawkins"** [1, 4, 5].
