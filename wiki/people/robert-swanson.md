# A. Robert Swanson

American private investigator hired by [[eileen-garrett|Eileen Garrett]] to carry out interviews with [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] in the late 1950s, on behalf of [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] who had been investigating Marianne's life. Tape recordings of Swanson's interviews survive in the archives of the Parapsychology Foundation, New York (though some material was destroyed in a basement flood).

## Background

Following the publication of [[the-borley-report|*HBR*]] in January 1956, Hall continued his investigation into the life of Marianne Foyster, who had by then emigrated to the United States. Through [[ian-greenwood|Ian Greenwood]], Hall obtained Marianne's address, and in 1957 contacted [[eileen-garrett|Eileen Garrett]], founder of the Parapsychology Foundation, who agreed to assist. Garrett employed Swanson — described as "tough" and chosen in part, according to Hall's own calculation, because of his "masculine good looks" that might exploit Marianne's purported weakness for men — and armed him with the results of Hall's long enquiry.

Marianne already knew Hall was trying to reach her: [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard Whitehouse]] had written to warn her earlier in 1956.

## The 1958 Jamestown Investigation

In February 1958, during a severe winter blizzard, Swanson arrived at Marianne O'Neil's house in Jamestown, North Dakota. Before approaching her, he spent time interviewing her friends and neighbours. He initially introduced himself under the pretext of discussing "a good book for a book" before admitting he was a private detective. After an initial meeting with her husband [[robert-oneil|Robert O'Neil]], Marianne agreed to talk. On **28 February 1958**, she met Swanson at the Gladstone Hotel in Jamestown; the first taped interview was conducted with questions largely prepared by Hall — aggressively covering her bigamous marriages, sexual urges, and alleged affairs with various figures at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], including Frank Pearless (François d'Arles) and Dr. Davies. Marianne later complained that Swanson (whom she mistakenly referred to as a "lawyer") seemed far more interested in her sex life than in what actually happened at Borley, and felt threatened by him.

## The New York Interview

Swanson subsequently persuaded the "flattered" Marianne to travel to New York by offering to pay her airfare and expenses. He conducted a second round of recorded interviews with her between **9 and 13 May 1958** at the Governor Clinton Hotel. Marianne later told researchers that she had gone into these interviews with every intention of lying to Swanson, treating it as a "game."

## Swanson's Private Impressions

Without Marianne's knowledge, Swanson compiled confidential reports for Hall and Garrett. His assessments included:

- **Physical appearance**: "Marianne O'Neil is not attractive from a physical standpoint. She does not have a slim figure but is inclined to look heavy and out of shape. Her hair is reddish and in need of work by a beautician... Her clothes are inexpensive although neat" [20].
- **Personality**: He reported she had "no mean opinion of herself" and identified with the actress Rita Hayworth, noting that like Hayworth she had had many husbands [21].
- **Sexual advances**: Swanson boasted to a colleague that "if he had stayed around Marianne much longer, she most certainly would have made a pitch for him" [21].
- **The murder theory**: Most significantly, Swanson formed the "distinct impression" that Marianne had actually murdered the Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]], and noted that the memory of this seemed to greatly disturb her [3].

## Significance

The Swanson interviews are among the most important primary sources for Marianne's own account of the Borley period. Her statements — confirming some phenomena while denying others, admitting to trickery while insisting some events were genuine — underpin the "admixture of truth and fraud" framework later articulated by [[iris-owen|Iris Owen]] and Pauline Mitchell in the [[nhf|New Horizons Foundation report]]. Hall's comment that "it was made quite clear from the report of [Swanson] that Marianne would not have agreed to this interrogation if she had not been aware of the information I had already obtained about her" suggests the interviews were conducted under a degree of implicit pressure. Transcripts were used extensively by Hall for his unpublished manuscript *Marianne Foyster of Borley Rectory* and later by [[robert-wood|Robert Wood]] in *The Widow of Borley* (1992).
