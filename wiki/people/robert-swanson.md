# A. Robert Swanson

American private investigator hired by [[eileen-garrett]] to carry out interviews with [[marianne-foyster]] in the late 1950s, on behalf of [[trevor-hall]] who had been investigating Marianne's life. Tape recordings of Swanson's interviews survive in the archives of the Parapsychology Foundation, New York.

## Background

Following the publication of [[the-borley-report|*HBR*]] in January 1956, Hall continued his investigation into the life of Marianne Foyster who had by then emigrated to the United States. Through [[ian-greenwood]], Hall obtained Marianne's address, and in 1957 contacted [[eileen-garrett]] who agreed to assist. Garrett employed Swanson — who was "armed with the results of Hall's long enquiry" — to find and interview Marianne.

Marianne already knew Hall was trying to reach her: [[dom-richard-whitehouse]] had written to warn her earlier in 1956.

## The Interviews

In February 1958, in the middle of a winter blizzard, Swanson arrived at Marianne O'Neil's house in Jamestown, North Dakota. After an initial meeting with her husband Robert O'Neil, Marianne agreed to talk. On 28 February they met at the Gladstone Hotel in Jamestown; the first taped interview was conducted with questions largely prepared by Hall. Later the same year, Marianne was flown to New York as a guest of Garrett and the Parapsychology Foundation. On 12 May 1958, at the Governor Clinton Hotel in New York, Swanson conducted a second interview.

Both interviews were transcribed and copies were sent to Hall, who used the material extensively in his unpublished *Marianne Foyster of Borley Rectory*. [[robert-wood]] also quotes sections in *The Widow of Borley* (1992), including Swanson's own perceptions of Marianne (compiled for Hall and Garrett without Marianne's knowledge): "his distinct impression that Marianne murdered Lionel Foyster."

## Significance

The Swanson interviews are among the most important primary sources for Marianne's own account of the Borley period. Her statements — confirming some phenomena while denying others, admitting to trickery while insisting some events were genuine — underpin the "admixture of truth and fraud" framework later articulated by [[iris-owen]] and Pauline Mitchell in the [[nhf|New Horizons Foundation report]]. Hall's comment that "it was made quite clear from the report of [Swanson] that Marianne would not have agreed to this interrogation if she had not been aware of the information I had already obtained about her" suggests the interviews were conducted under a degree of implicit pressure.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**A. Robert Swanson** was a "tough" American private investigator hired in the late 1950s to track down and interrogate Marianne Foyster (then living as Marianne O'Neil), the controversial central figure of the Borley Rectory hauntings [1, 2]. 

**Hiring and Background**
Swanson was officially employed by the prominent psychic Eileen Garrett, founder of the Parapsychology Foundation in New York [3-5]. However, his involvement was entirely instigated by the skeptical psychical researcher Trevor H. Hall, who was determined to debunk the Borley hauntings and investigate Marianne's private life [4, 5]. Hall specifically recommended Swanson for the job because of his "masculine good looks," calculating that this would exploit Marianne's purported weakness for men [5]. 

**The 1958 Jamestown Investigation**
In February 1958, Swanson arrived in Jamestown, North Dakota, during a severe winter blizzard [6, 7]. Before approaching Marianne, he spent time interviewing her friends and neighbors [8]. When he initially arrived at her home, he introduced himself with the pretext of discussing "a good book for a book" before admitting he was a private detective [6]. 

Although Marianne initially refused to speak with him, Swanson's charm ultimately persuaded her to grant a tape-recorded interview on February 28, 1958, at the Gladstone Hotel [6, 8, 9]. Swanson asked her a series of probing, highly personal questions that had been prepared in advance by Trevor Hall [3, 10]. Marianne later complained to other researchers that Swanson (whom she mistakenly referred to as a "lawyer") seemed far more interested in her sex life than in what actually happened at Borley, noting that she felt threatened by him and therefore only answered the direct questions put to her [11]. During the interview, Swanson aggressively questioned her about her bigamous marriages, her sexual urges, and whether she had affairs with various figures at Borley, including Frank Pearless (Francois d'Arles) and Dr. Davies [12-15].

**The New York Interview**
Swanson's pursuit of the case consumed him, and he successfully persuaded the "flattered" Marianne to travel to New York by offering to pay her airfare and expenses [12, 16]. Swanson conducted a second round of recorded interviews with her between May 9 and May 13, 1958, at the Governor Clinton Hotel [17, 18]. Marianne later told researchers that she had gone into these interviews with every intention of lying to Swanson, treating it as a "game" [19].

**Swanson's Private Impressions**
Without Marianne's knowledge, Swanson compiled confidential reports of his perceptions for Hall and Garrett [3]. His assessments were remarkably blunt and included the following observations:
*   **Physical Appearance:** Swanson was decidedly unimpressed with her looks, writing, "Marianne O’Neil is not attractive from a physical standpoint. She does not have a slim figure but is inclined to look heavy and out of shape. Her hair is reddish and in need of work by a beautician... Her clothes are inexpensive although neat" [20]. 
*   **Personality:** He reported that she had "no mean opinion of herself" and identified with the actress Rita Hayworth, noting that like Hayworth, she had many husbands [21]. 
*   **Sexual Advances:** Swanson boasted to a colleague that "if he had stayed around Marianne much longer, she most certainly would have made a pitch for him" [21].
*   **The Murder Theory:** Most significantly, Swanson formed the "distinct impression" that Marianne had actually murdered her former husband, the Rev. Lionel Foyster, and noted that the memory of this seemed to greatly disturb her [3, 22]. 

**Legacy**
Swanson's tape-recorded interviews survive today in the archives of the Parapsychology Foundation in New York, although some of the material was destroyed during a basement flood [1, 11]. Transcripts of his aggressive interrogations were utilized extensively by Trevor Hall for his unpublished manuscript *Marianne Foyster of Borley Rectory*, and later by author Robert Wood in his 1992 book *The Widow of Borley* [3].
