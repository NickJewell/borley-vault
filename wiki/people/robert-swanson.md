# A. Robert Swanson

American private investigator hired by [[eileen-garrett]] to carry out interviews with [[marianne-foyster]] in the late 1950s, on behalf of [[trevor-hall]] who had been investigating Marianne's life. Tape recordings of Swanson's interviews survive in the archives of the Parapsychology Foundation, New York.

## Background

Following the publication of [[the-borley-report|*HBR*]] in January 1956, Hall continued his investigation into the life of Marianne Foyster who had by then emigrated to the United States. Through [[ian-greenwood]], Hall obtained Marianne's address, and in 1957 contacted [[eileen-garrett]] who agreed to assist. Garrett employed Swanson — who was "armed with the results of Hall's long enquiry" — to find and interview Marianne.

Marianne already knew Hall was trying to reach her: [[dom-richard-whitehouse]] had written to warn her earlier in 1956.

## The Interviews

In February 1958, in the middle of a winter blizzard, Swanson arrived at Marianne O'Neil's house in Jamestown, North Dakota. After an initial meeting with her husband Robert O'Neil, Marianne agreed to talk. On 28 February they met at the Gladstone Hotel in Jamestown; the first taped interview was conducted with questions largely prepared by Hall. Later the same year, Marianne was flown to New York as a guest of Garrett and the Parapsychology Foundation. On 12 May 1958, at the Governor Clinton Hotel in New York, Swanson conducted a second interview.

Both interviews were transcribed and copies were sent to Hall, who used the material extensively in his unpublished *Marianne Foyster of Borley Rectory*. [[robert-wood]] also quotes sections in *The Widow of Borley* (1992), including Swanson's own perceptions of Marianne (compiled for Hall and Garrett without Marianne's knowledge): "his distinct impression that Marianne murdered Lionel Foyster."

## Significance

The Swanson interviews are among the most important primary sources for Marianne's own account of the Borley period. Her statements — confirming some phenomena while denying others, admitting to trickery while insisting some events were genuine — underpin the "admixture of truth and fraud" framework later articulated by [[iris-owen]] and Pauline Mitchell in the [[nhf|New Horizons Foundation report]]. Hall's comment that "it was made quite clear from the report of [Swanson] that Marianne would not have agreed to this interrogation if she had not been aware of the information I had already obtained about her" suggests the interviews were conducted under a degree of implicit pressure.
