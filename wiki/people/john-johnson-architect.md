# John Johnson (architect)

Victorian ecclesiastical architect from Bury St Edmunds who designed [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in 1862 and was responsible for the building's distinctive — and controversial — Gothic style.

## Career

Johnson specialised in ecclesiastical buildings and churches. While best known in paranormal circles for his connection to Borley, his most famous architectural legacy is his collaboration with **Alfred Meeson** on **Alexandra Palace** in London [1]. His architectural fingerprint was High Victorian Gothic: grandiose, constructed of materials that often clashed with their rural surroundings, and designed to broadcast the wealth and status of the patron.

A notable earlier project was **St Matthew's vicarage in Oakley Square, Camden** (1861) — described as "a High Victorian Gothic extravaganza of yellow London brick" [2]. Many elements of this building, particularly a square stair tower topped with a steep spire-like pyramid roof and an ironwork pinnacle, were integrated directly into his Borley design.

## The Pentlow Tower (1859)

Johnson's relationship with the Bull ecclesiastical dynasty began with [[henry-bull|Henry Bull]]'s father, [[rev-edward-bull-pentlow|Rev. Edward Bull]], who in 1859 commissioned a **90-foot-high octagonal brick memorial tower in the Tudor style** to be built in the garden of [[pentlow|Pentlow]] Rectory, in memory of his own father [1]. The *Suffolk Free Press* of 24 February 1859 reported: *"The erection of the tower will be under superintendence of Mr. John Johnson, architect, of Bury St. Edmunds"* [3]. Admiring the impressive scale of this tower, the young Rev. Henry Bull decided that Johnson was the perfect architect for a new rectory at Borley.

## Borley Rectory (1861–1863)

At the beginning of June 1861, Johnson put out a tender in the local press to demolish the old [[herringham-rectory|Herringham Rectory]] at Borley and build a new one. The builder [[thomas-farrow|Thomas Farrow]] of Bury St Edmunds submitted an estimate of £3,000, which was accepted [2].

The resulting building was constructed in the Gothic style using materials at odds with its rural surroundings — a style fashionable in the smarter suburbs of nearby Ipswich and Halstead. Johnson integrated many elements from his recent Camden vicarage work into the Borley design, most notably the "square stair tower situated to the side of the building, topped with a steep, spire-like pyramid roof and an ironwork pinnacle" [2]. The bombastic design, completed in the summer of 1863, broadcast the wealth of the Bulls but was widely considered ugly. The lawyer William Charles Crocker later described it as "as ugly as the bad taste of 1863 could make it."

Johnson also sourced the dining room's distinctive **multicoloured marble fireplace** — decorated with carved monks — from the Great Exhibition of 1851 [5]. This fireplace became one of the most noticed features of the house; locals later believed it was installed to perpetuate the legend of a monastery on the site [5, 6].

## Construction Issues

Johnson directed the builder to lay the floorboards tightly and make the doors with securely tacked panels to eradicate draughts. This had an unintended consequence: the woodwork would creak and groan dramatically whenever there was a change in temperature — a frequent occurrence on the exposed ridge above the Stour Valley. This structural noise would later be interpreted by numerous residents as paranormal activity.

## Architectural Controversy

> **Contradiction:** While all detailed historical accounts universally attribute the designs of both the Pentlow Tower and Borley Rectory to John Johnson, one source text proposes an alternative architect: *"It is possible that the Pentlow Rectory, Pentlow Folly, and Borley Rectory were built by Webb of Sudbury and designed by Frederick Chancellor, a London-based architect"* [7].
