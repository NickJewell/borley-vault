# John Johnson (architect)

Victorian ecclesiastical architect from Bury St Edmunds who designed [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in 1862 and was responsible for the building's distinctive — and controversial — Gothic style.

## Career

Johnson specialised in ecclesiastical buildings and churches. He is best remembered today for his collaboration with **Alfred Meeson** on **Alexandra Palace**. His architectural fingerprint was High Victorian Gothic: grandiose, constructed of materials that often clashed with their rural surroundings, and designed to broadcast the wealth and status of the patron.

A notable earlier project was **St Matthew's vicarage in Oakley Square, Camden** (1861) — a High Victorian Gothic extravaganza in yellow London brick. Many elements of this building, particularly a square stair tower topped with a steep spire-like pyramid roof and an ironwork pinnacle, were integrated into his Borley design.

## Design of the Pentlow Tower

Before Borley, Johnson had designed the **Pentlow memorial tower** (1859) in the rectory garden at [[pentlow|Pentlow]], commissioned by [[rev-edward-bull-pentlow|Rev. Edward Bull]] as a memorial to his father. This 90-foot-high octagonal brick tower in the Tudor style was admired by Edward's son Henry, who therefore selected Johnson to design the new Borley Rectory.

## Borley Rectory

At the beginning of June 1861, Johnson put out a tender in the local press to demolish the old [[herringham-rectory|Herringham Rectory]] at Borley and build a new one. The builder [[thomas-farrow|Thomas Farrow]] of Bury St Edmunds submitted an estimate of £3,000, which was accepted.

The resulting building was constructed in the Gothic style using materials at odds with its rural surroundings — a style fashionable in the smarter suburbs of nearby Ipswich and Halstead. The bombastic design, completed in the summer of 1863, broadcast the wealth of the Bulls but was widely considered ugly. The lawyer William Charles Crocker later described it as "as ugly as the bad taste of 1863 could make it."

Johnson also sourced the dining room's distinctive **multicoloured marble fireplace** — decorated with carved monks — from the Great Exhibition of 1851. This fireplace became one of the most noticed features of the house and the monks on it were often remarked upon by visitors, including [[harry-price|Harry Price]].

## Construction Issues

Johnson directed the builder to lay the floorboards tightly and make the doors with securely tacked panels to eradicate draughts. This had an unintended consequence: the woodwork would creak and groan dramatically whenever there was a change in temperature — a frequent occurrence on the exposed ridge above the Stour Valley. This structural noise would later be interpreted by numerous residents as paranormal activity.
