# John Johnson (architect)

Victorian ecclesiastical architect from Bury St Edmunds who designed [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in 1862 and was responsible for the building's distinctive — and controversial — Gothic style.

## Career

Johnson specialised in ecclesiastical buildings and churches. He is best remembered today for his collaboration with **Alfred Meeson** on **Alexandra Palace**. His architectural fingerprint was High Victorian Gothic: grandiose, constructed of materials that often clashed with their rural surroundings, and designed to broadcast the wealth and status of the patron.

A notable earlier project was **St Matthew's vicarage in Oakley Square, Camden** (1861) — a High Victorian Gothic extravaganza in yellow London brick. Many elements of this building, particularly a square stair tower topped with a steep spire-like pyramid roof and an ironwork pinnacle, were integrated into his Borley design.

## Design of the Pentlow Tower

Before Borley, Johnson had designed the **Pentlow memorial tower** (1859) in the rectory garden at [[pentlow|Pentlow]], commissioned by [[rev-edward-bull-pentlow|Rev. Edward Bull]] as a memorial to his father. This 90-foot-high octagonal brick tower in the Tudor style was admired by Edward's son Henry, who therefore selected Johnson to design the new Borley Rectory.

## Borley Rectory

At the beginning of June 1861, Johnson put out a tender in the local press to demolish the old [[herringham-rectory|Herringham Rectory]] at Borley and build a new one. The builder [[thomas-farrow|Thomas Farrow]] of Bury St Edmunds submitted an estimate of £3,000, which was accepted.

The resulting building was constructed in the Gothic style using materials at odds with its rural surroundings — a style fashionable in the smarter suburbs of nearby Ipswich and Halstead. The bombastic design, completed in the summer of 1863, broadcast the wealth of the Bulls but was widely considered ugly. The lawyer William Charles Crocker later described it as "as ugly as the bad taste of 1863 could make it."

Johnson also sourced the dining room's distinctive **multicoloured marble fireplace** — decorated with carved monks — from the Great Exhibition of 1851. This fireplace became one of the most noticed features of the house and the monks on it were often remarked upon by visitors, including [[harry-price|Harry Price]].

## Construction Issues

Johnson directed the builder to lay the floorboards tightly and make the doors with securely tacked panels to eradicate draughts. This had an unintended consequence: the woodwork would creak and groan dramatically whenever there was a change in temperature — a frequent occurrence on the exposed ridge above the Stour Valley. This structural noise would later be interpreted by numerous residents as paranormal activity.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**John Johnson** was an architect from Bury St Edmunds who specialized in ecclesiastical buildings and churches [1]. In the Borley sources, he is primarily identified as the architect who designed the famously haunted Borley Rectory for the Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, architectural contributions, specific events, and a notable historical contradiction regarding his work:

**Lesser-Known Biographical Details and Other Works**
*   **Alexandra Palace:** While best known in paranormal circles for his connection to Borley Rectory, Johnson’s most famous architectural legacy is his collaboration with Alfred Meeson on the design of Alexandra Palace in London [1].
*   **St Matthew’s Vicarage (1861):** In 1861, just prior to designing Borley Rectory, Johnson was completing work on St Matthew’s vicarage in Oakley Square, Camden [2]. The sources describe this building as "a High Victorian Gothic extravaganza of yellow London brick" [2]. 

**The Pentlow Tower (1859)**
Johnson's relationship with the Bull ecclesiastical dynasty began with Henry Bull's father, the Rev. Edward Bull [1]. In 1859, Edward Bull commissioned a 90-foot-high octagonal brick memorial tower in the Tudor style to be built in the garden of Pentlow Rectory to honor his own father [1]. 
*   The February 24, 1859 edition of the *Suffolk Free Press* reported on the project, stating: **"The erection of the tower will be under superintendence of Mr. John Johnson, architect, of Bury St. Edmunds. Mr. Webb of Sudbury who's satisfactory completion of important works we are called upon to notice being selected as builder"** [3]. 

**Designing Borley Rectory (1861–1863)**
Admiring the impressive style and scale of his father's memorial tower at Pentlow, the young Rev. Henry Bull decided that John Johnson was the perfect architect to design a brand-new rectory for him at Borley [1]. 
*   **Design and Tendering:** At the beginning of June 1861, Johnson placed a tender in the local press for builders to demolish the old Borley rectory and construct the new one [2]. A builder and surveyor named Thomas Farrow consulted Johnson’s plans and won the contract with an accepted estimate of £3,000 [2]. 
*   **Architectural Borrowing:** Johnson integrated many elements from his recent work on the Camden vicarage into Borley Rectory's design [2]. Most notably, he borrowed the "square stair tower situated to the side of the building, topped with a steep, spire-like pyramid roof and an ironwork pinnacle" [2].
*   **A "Slightly Alien" Aesthetic:** Like many of Johnson’s projects, Borley Rectory was built in the Gothic style using materials that were at odds with its rural surroundings, leaving the house with a "slightly alien design" [4]. 

**The 1851 Great Exhibition Fireplace**
Johnson is also credited with acquiring one of Borley Rectory's most famous interior features. He purchased the heavy marble fireplace that adorned the Rectory's dining room from the Great Exhibition in London in 1851 [5]. This specific fireplace was decorated with the carved figures of a monk on either side, which locals later believed was installed to perpetuate the legend of a monastery on the site [5, 6]. 

**The Architectural Contradiction**
A direct historical contradiction exists in the Borley sources regarding the true architect behind the Rectory and the Pentlow Folly. While the detailed historical accounts universally attribute the designs to John Johnson [1-3], one source text explicitly contradicts this by proposing an alternative architect, stating: "It is possible that the Pentlow Rectory, Pentlow Folly, and Borley Rectory were built by Webb of Sudbury and designed by Frederick Chancellor, a London-based architect" [7].
