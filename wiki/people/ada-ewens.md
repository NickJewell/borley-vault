# Ada Ewens

Frank Pearless's first wife, whom he divorced in 1933–34, clearing the way for his marriage to his second wife Jessie Irene Dorothy Mitchell in August 1934.

> **Contradiction:** [[andrew-clarke|Andrew Clarke]]'s *Sidelights on Borley Rectory* essay 14 records the divorce as occurring in 1934; the *Borley Rectory Companion* (brc) independently dates it to November 1933. Ivan Banks's *The Enigma of Borley Rectory* records her name as **Ada Ewens**, while the *Borley Rectory Companion* identifies her as **Ada Evans**.

## Background and Marriage

Ada married Frank Charles Pearless — a native of Bermondsey, South London — on **Boxing Day, December 26, 1918**, at a ceremony in West Hackney, London [1, 2]. Pearless was 24 years old at the time of the wedding [1]. Following their marriage, the couple lived together for a time in France [1, 2]. Researchers note that this period of living abroad with Ada is likely when Pearless adopted the French-sounding pseudonym "François d'Arles" [2].

Clarke records the detail of the divorce in his biographical account of [[francois-darles|François d'Arles]] (Frank Pearless) in essay 14:

> *Pearless managed to divorce his wife, Ada Ewens, in 1934, and disappeared off with the 16 year-old shop assistant. He then married Jessie Irene Dorothy Mitchell in August 1934.*
> — *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, essay 14

Borley historians specifically highlight the timing of the divorce, noting that it coincided directly with Pearless's intimate and illicit association with [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]], the young wife of the Borley rector. Because Pearless and Marianne were jointly running a Wimbledon flower shop ([[jonquille-et-cie|Jonquille et Cie]]) and spending their weeks together away from the Rectory between 1932 and 1934, researchers explicitly "wonder whether this [divorce] arose out of his association with Marianne" [2].

## Significance

Ada Ewens establishes that Frank Pearless was still legally married throughout the entire period of his involvement with [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] (1931–1934). He was therefore not a widower as he had implied to the Foysters, and had no legal freedom to marry during this period. This is consistent with the general pattern of deception that characterised Pearless's conduct throughout the Borley affair.

Clarke's biography of Pearless traces his subsequent marriages: after Ada Ewens came Jessie Irene Dorothy Mitchell (divorced 1944, died 1978), then Jessie (Pring) Steed (married 10 November 1944). He emigrated to Australia in the 1950s and died in London in 1966 in a pauper's grave.

Ada Ewens herself plays no direct role in the Borley events; she is simply evidence of Pearless's deception about his marital status.
