# Ada Ewens

Frank Pearless's first wife, whom he divorced in 1934, clearing the way for his marriage to his second wife Jessie Irene Dorothy Mitchell in August of that year.

## Background

[[andrew-clarke]] records the detail of the divorce in his biographical account of [[francois-darles]] (Frank Pearless) in essay 14:

> *Pearless managed to divorce his wife, Ada Ewens, in 1934, and disappeared off with the 16 year-old shop assistant. He then married Jessie Irene Dorothy Mitchell in August 1934.*
> — *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, essay 14

## Significance

Ada Ewens establishes that Frank Pearless was still legally married throughout the entire period of his involvement with [[marianne-foyster]] at [[borley-rectory]] (1931–1934). He was therefore not a widower as he had implied to the Foysters, and had no legal freedom to marry during this period. This is consistent with the general pattern of deception that characterized Pearless's conduct throughout the Borley affair.

Clarke's biography of Pearless traces his subsequent marriages: after Ada Ewens came Jessie Irene Dorothy Mitchell (divorced 1944, died 1978), then Jessie (Pring) Steed (married 10 November 1944). He emigrated to Australia in the 1950s and died in London in 1966 in a pauper's grave.

Ada Ewens herself plays no direct role in the Borley events; she is simply evidence of Pearless's deception about his marital status.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Ada Ewens** (also recorded in some historical sources as **Ada Evans**) is a peripheral but historically relevant figure in the Borley Rectory saga. She is known entirely for her marriage to one of the haunting's most controversial and mysterious characters: Frank Charles Pearless, who notoriously resided at the Rectory under the pseudonym **François d'Arles** [1, 2].

Based on the Borley sources, a thorough reference profile of her life yields the following details:

**Marriage and Time in France**
Ada married Pearless—a native of Bermondsey, South London—on **Boxing Day, December 26, 1918**, at a ceremony in West Hackney, London [1, 2]. Pearless was 24 years old at the time of the wedding [1]. Following their marriage, the couple lived together for a time in France [1, 2]. This detail is particularly significant to the Borley story, as researchers note that this period of living abroad with Ada is likely when Pearless decided to adopt the French-sounding pseudonym "François d'Arles" [2]. 

**Divorce and the Borley Connection**
Ada herself played no direct role in the hauntings or the investigations at Borley Rectory. However, the dissolution of her marriage is closely tied to the scandal surrounding the house. **Her marriage to Pearless ended in divorce in November 1933** [1, 2]. 

Borley historians specifically highlight the timing of this divorce, noting that it coincided directly with Pearless's intimate and illicit association with Marianne Foyster, the young wife of the Borley rector, Reverend Lionel Foyster [2]. Because Pearless and Marianne were jointly running a Wimbledon flower shop (Jonquille et Cie) and spending their weeks together away from the Rectory between 1932 and 1934, researchers explicitly "wonder whether this [divorce] arose out of his association with Marianne" [2]. 

**Contradictions in the Sources**
A minor discrepancy exists in the Borley literature regarding the exact spelling of her maiden name. While Ivan Banks's *The Enigma of Borley Rectory* records her name as **Ada Ewens** [2], *The Borley Rectory Companion* identifies her as **Ada Evans** [1].
