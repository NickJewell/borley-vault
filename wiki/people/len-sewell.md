# Leonard Sewell

Photographer by profession; one of the most dedicated and long-serving lay investigators of the Borley mysteries in the post-Price era. Lived for a time in a caravan on the former Rectory grounds. He bequeathed his extensive Borley archive to [[peter-underwood]].

## Excavations (1954–1957)

Sewell was Philip Paul's "chief helper" in the excavations carried out at the former Rectory site during 1954–56. He regularly supervised digging work at the site and took many photographs. He was one of the first people to enter the tunnel under the road when it was accidentally found by road workers in September 1957 — a Tudor-brick passage, 32 inches wide and 28 inches high, running between Borley Place and the former Rectory grounds.

He also discovered the Borley Tithe Map of 1841, which clearly shows the [[rectory-cottage|Rectory Cottage]] in its present position and the Herringham Rectory occupying a smaller footprint to the west.

## Planchette Sessions (1955–1957)

Sewell conducted approximately 32 planchette séances between 1953 and 1957, held either in his caravan on the old Rectory garden, in the Rectory Cottage with the [[bacon-family|Bacon family]], or at his home in Long Melford. Many sessions were conducted alone, late at night. The communicators included various alleged spirits: "Marie Lairre," "Harry Price," "H.D.E. Bull," and most prominently "Dominic Drossy" — a Benedictine monk who claimed responsibility for Marie Lairre's murder, allegedly on the orders of Leslie Waldegrave.

The transcripts describe a gold cream jug found in the cellar well as having been put there by "some of the Bulls' servants," and state the human remains came from the churchyard — contrary to Price's interpretation. See [[seance-data]] and [[communicating-entities]].

## Character

Described by Peter Underwood as "one of the most ardent, enthusiastic and active lay investigators of the Borley mysteries." He "corresponded with many people connected with the haunting and collected a wealth of books and photographs." He helped enormously with photographs for Underwood and Tabori's *The Ghosts of Borley* (1973).

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Leonard (Len) Sewell** was a professional photographer and one of the most ardent, enthusiastic lay investigators during the "Later Borley" period [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, investigation records, discoveries, and specific quotes regarding his extensive involvement in the case:

**Biographical Details and Personality**
Sewell lived in Long Melford but spent several years living directly on the old Rectory site in a caravan he named "San Michele" [1-3]. He was known for having "nerves of steel" and being "not easily spooked," frequently conducting paranormal investigations and planchette sessions entirely alone in his caravan late at night [2]. He amassed a massive personal archive of books, photographs, and correspondence regarding the haunting, which he bequeathed to the prominent investigator Peter Underwood upon his death [1, 4]. 

**The 1950s Cellar Excavations**
Between 1954 and the autumn of 1957, Sewell was a leading figure in the physical excavation of the demolished Rectory's cellars [5, 6]. Initially acting as the chief helper to the journalist Philip Paul, Sewell and his colleague L. G. Rayner took over the methodical clearing of the cellars when Paul abandoned the project in 1956 [3, 4, 7]. 
*   **Artifacts Discovered:** Through painstaking weekend digs, Sewell and his team unearthed old brick wine bins, drains, charred beams, white marble fireplace fragments, a horseshoe dated circa 1600, a Pembroke College egg-cup, part of an Iron Age quern (a hand-mill for grinding corn), and an old Rectory bell [7-9]. 
*   **A Change of Opinion:** The excavations reveal a specific contradiction in Sewell's assessment of Harry Price's theories. In a letter dated May 20, 1957, Sewell argued that Price was wrong in his assertion that the odd shape of the cellars was dictated by the foundations of an older building, noting that the "angle wall" he was excavating had no footings at all [9]. However, as Sewell dug deeper and uncovered original walls and ancient drains beneath the floor, he formally reversed his opinion and admitted that Price's original conclusion had been correct [9]. 

**Documentary Map Discoveries**
Sewell made a major contribution to the historical record of Borley by discovering a 1772 map and an 1841 Tithe Map [6, 8, 10]. For over twenty-five years, investigators had sought documentary proof of an earlier building on the site. Sewell’s maps finally provided this, detailing the location of the "Herringham Rectory" and proving it occupied a much smaller area than the enormous house built by Henry Bull in 1863 [6, 10].

**The 1957 Tunnel Exploration**
On September 12, 1957, rural contractors laying water mains in Hall Lane accidentally broke through brickwork into a subterranean tunnel with their mechanical digger [11, 12]. Sewell was immediately contacted and rushed to the site, becoming one of the first people to enter and explore it [4, 11, 12]. 
*   **Measurements:** Sewell made precise measurements, recording that the Tudor-brick tunnel was 32 inches wide, 28 inches high, and had a domed roof that sat 12 inches below the road surface [13]. 
*   **Conclusions:** After continuing the excavation the following Saturday, he concluded that its trajectory would have originally taken it directly into the cellars of Borley Place [14, 15].

**Séances and Planchette Experiments (1953–1958)**
Sewell was a dedicated planchette operator, conducting numerous sessions in his caravan, his Long Melford home, and the Rectory Cottage [2]. Thirty-two transcripts of his sittings survive [2]. Often sitting alongside the Bacon family and Mrs. Williams, Sewell contacted numerous "entities" [16, 17].
*   **The Communicators:** The entities included H.D.E. Bull, A.C. Henning, Henry Waldegrave, "J.O. Steed," "Percy Long," "Ronald James Hewitt," "Stanley Gardiner," and a butcher named "Harry Doy" [16-18]. 
*   **Dominic Drossy:** The most prolific communicator was "Dominic Drossy," who claimed to be a Benedictine monk who had lived at Borley Place. According to the board, Drossy had ordered the drowning of the French novice Marie Lairre, and was himself murdered for money in 1633 and thrown down a well by a man named Leslie Waldegrave [2, 19]. 
*   **Harry Price:** Sewell also supposedly contacted the spirit of Harry Price, who instructed the sitters to "Hurry, dig more" and insisted that he had never faked anything in his published books [20].
*   **Marie Lairre and the "Dead" Board:** In one dramatic instance, the entity of "Marie Lairre" begged for help to find her scattered remains in the cellar. Her final communication ended with the poignant quote, **"Must I walk this earth for ever?"**. Immediately below this line, Sewell added a specific note to the transcript: **"Here the board went dead"** [20].

In addition to his own planchette work, Sewell served as the official note-taker at a June 1, 1958 séance organized by the Rev. John Dening with the medium Mrs. May Lampard. The session produced highly confused and inaccurate information, and the following day Sewell accompanied the group to investigate the haunted Langenhoe church [4, 21].
