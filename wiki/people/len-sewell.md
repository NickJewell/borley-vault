# Leonard Sewell

Photographer by profession and one of the most ardent, enthusiastic lay investigators of the Borley mysteries in the post-Price era. Sewell lived in Long Melford but spent several years living directly on the old Rectory site in a caravan he named "San Michele." He was known for having "nerves of steel" and being "not easily spooked," frequently conducting paranormal investigations and planchette sessions entirely alone in his caravan late at night. He bequeathed his extensive Borley archive to [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]].

## Excavations (1954–1957)

Between 1954 and the autumn of 1957, Sewell was a leading figure in the physical excavation of the demolished Rectory's cellars. He was initially [[philip-paul|Philip Paul's]] "chief helper" in the excavations carried out at the former Rectory site during 1954–56. When Paul abandoned the investigation in August 1956, Sewell and his colleague [[leonard-rayner|L. G. Rayner]] took over the project methodically, working at weekends and concluding their "painstaking and patient labours" in the autumn of 1957.

**Artifacts discovered:** Through their work, Sewell and his team unearthed old brick wine bins, drains, charred beams, white marble fireplace fragments, a horseshoe dated circa 1600, a Pembroke College egg-cup, part of an Iron Age quern, and an old Rectory bell.

Sewell's excavations also reveal a specific contradiction in his assessment of [[harry-price|Harry Price's]] theories. In a letter dated May 20, 1957, he argued that Price was wrong in his assertion that the odd shape of the cellars was dictated by the foundations of an older building, noting that the "angle wall" he was excavating had no footings at all. However, as Sewell dug deeper and uncovered original walls and ancient drains beneath the floor, he formally reversed his opinion and admitted that Price's original conclusion had been correct.

## Documentary Map Discoveries

Sewell discovered both a 1772 map and the Borley Tithe Map of 1841 — a major contribution to the historical record. For over twenty-five years, investigators had sought documentary proof of an earlier building on the site. These maps finally provided it, clearly showing the [[rectory-cottage|Rectory Cottage]] in its present position and the Herringham Rectory occupying a much smaller footprint to the west.

## The 1957 Tunnel Exploration

He was one of the first people to enter the tunnel under the road when it was accidentally found by rural contractors in September 1957. On September 12, 1957, contractors laying water mains in Hall Lane broke through brickwork into a subterranean passage. Sewell was immediately contacted and rushed to the site. He made precise measurements, recording the Tudor-brick tunnel as 32 inches wide, 28 inches high, with a domed roof sitting 12 inches below the road surface. After continuing the excavation the following Saturday with Rayner, he concluded that its trajectory would have originally taken it directly into the cellars of [[borley-place|Borley Place]].

## Planchette Sessions (1953–1958)

Sewell conducted approximately 32 planchette séances between 1953 and 1957, held either in his caravan on the old Rectory garden, in the Rectory Cottage with the Bacon family, or at his home in Long Melford. The communicators included various alleged spirits: "Marie Lairre," "Harry Price," "H.D.E. Bull," and most prominently **"Dominic Drossy"** — a Benedictine monk who claimed responsibility for [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre's]] murder, allegedly on the orders of Leslie Waldegrave, and who claimed to have himself been murdered for money in 1633 and thrown down a well.

The entity of [[marie-lairre|"Marie Lairre"]] on one occasion begged for help to find her scattered remains in the cellar. Her final communication ended with the poignant quote, **"Must I walk this earth for ever?"** — below which Sewell noted in the transcript: **"Here the board went dead."**

The transcripts describe a gold cream jug found in the cellar well as having been put there by "some of the Bulls' servants," and state the human remains came from the churchyard — contrary to Price's interpretation. See [[communicating-entities]].

Sewell also supposedly contacted the spirit of [[harry-price|Harry Price]], who instructed the sitters to "Hurry, dig more" and insisted that he had never faked anything in his published books.

In addition to his own planchette work, Sewell served as the official note-taker at a June 1, 1958 séance organised by the Rev. [[john-dening|John Dening]] with the medium Mrs. May Lampard. The session produced confused and inaccurate information, and the following day Sewell accompanied the group to investigate the haunted [[langenhoe|Langenhoe]] church.

## Character

Described by [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] as "one of the most ardent, enthusiastic and active lay investigators of the Borley mysteries." He "corresponded with many people connected with the haunting and collected a wealth of books and photographs." He helped enormously with photographs for Underwood and Tabori's *The Ghosts of Borley* (1973).
