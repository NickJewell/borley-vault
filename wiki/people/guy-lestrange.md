# Guy P.J. L'Estrange JP

**Guy P.J. L'Estrange (1898–1973)** was a prominent 1930s physical medium, psychic artist, and healer [1]. Town councillor, county councillor, and Justice of the Peace from Bungay, Suffolk. Also a former headmaster of Colchester Grammar School. Member of the [[marks-tey-spiritualist-circle|Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle]].

He claimed to be a Justice of the Peace, a county councillor, and a town councillor, though researchers have pointed out a curious contradiction: **his name does not appear in either the 1925 or 1937 Suffolk and Norfolk directories**, suggesting his civic eminence might have occurred later or outside East Anglia [2, 5]. He resided at various times at 'Nazareth', 119 Beccles Road, Bungay, Suffolk, and Beresford Road, Great Yarmouth [2, 4]. He married a woman named Hilda, had at least two daughters, and eventually passed away at Firsview Drive, Northampton, in 1973 [2].

He contributed a weekly column titled "Psychic Corner" for the *Yarmouth Independent* and was appointed "Life President" of the Great Yarmouth Spiritualist Church in 1934, resigning two years later due to "business pressure" [1, 2]. His psychic abilities had been tested by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] at the National Laboratory of Psychical Research in March 1931, yielding negative results, which Price blamed on the truculent behaviour of fellow Spiritualist Dennis Bradley [3, 5]. He was known for materialising a **"topless ghost named the Female Dancer"**, an Islamic spirit guide called **"Abu ben Mohammed"**, the spirit of a springer spaniel called **Lady**, and a primary control spirit named **"Zenith"** [3, 6].

He was interviewed by Underwood, his wife, and Steuart Kiernander at L'Estrange's home in Bungay, Suffolk, on 23 August 1969.

## The January 1932 Visit

L'Estrange arrived at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in the afternoon of **January 23, 1932**, as a medium operating with the [[marks-tey-spiritualist-circle|Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle]] [3, 7]. Upon arriving, he noted the house felt "exactly the sort of place one associates with ghosts" and remarked on its "heavy and supernormally cold" atmosphere [8]. After the rest of the party left, L'Estrange spent the night with the Foysters — just the rector, his wife, and one maid in the house.

### Phenomena Experienced

**Tall clerical figure:** Before entering, he saw "a tall figure, like a man in ecclesiastical attire, near the porch" — it vanished when approached and did not reappear when he returned to the original position.

**Crockery and bottles:** A loud crash in the hall; floor littered with broken crockery. Then "an appalling series of crashing sounds": bottles "being hurled about in all directions, although no human person was to be seen." All three stood and watched bottles appear in mid-air and smash. "One large wine bottle missed my left ear by about an inch." The shed of empty bottles outside was locked with the window bolted.

**The thirty bells silenced:** As the three descended the stairs, all thirty bells in the kitchen passage began clanging wildly by themselves (Lionel said wires of all but three had been cut in a previous attempt to stop the ringing). L'Estrange said aloud: "If some invisible person is present and can hear these words, please stop the ringing for a moment." **Instantly, every bell became silent** [9].

**Footsteps through the wall:** In the drawing-room, cautious but distinct footsteps entered the room, paused directly behind him on the settee, and then "went through the wall at the far end of the room." [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] confirmed there had once been a doorway at that spot.

**Exorcism with relic:** As the phenomena escalated, L'Estrange, Lionel Foyster, and [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] proceeded from room to room making the sign of the cross with a relic of the Curé d'Ars, which practically ceased the immediate disturbances [7, 10].

**Luminous form:** In the "haunted bedroom" in the small hours, L'Estrange saw "a vague and slightly luminous form" beside his bed that gradually grew "larger and denser" into a shape resembling a human figure in long robes. He addressed it; no reply. He attempted to approach and felt "considerable willpower and persistence" required just to maintain his position — as though "an attempt was being made to push him away." The form abruptly faded; the icy chill disappeared; he sank back "exhausted."

**Marianne in trance:** Saw [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] "glide rather than walk" past the Blue Room door at 1 a.m. in a long night-dress. She replied to his question in a trance-like voice: **"To — make — some — tea"** [11]. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel]], alerted, came out and was "very annoyed."

### Aftermath

A week after the visit, Foyster wrote to thank L'Estrange: "there had been no more disturbances and quite a different feeling throughout the house." Three years later: "the house is now perfectly normal." But phenomena resumed.

## The Marks Tey Circle Séance

On 23 January 1932, L'Estrange also led a separate séance with [[george-warren|George Warren]] in the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] — [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] and [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] declined to attend. Guided by his control 'Zenith', L'Estrange claimed to channel the intense, evil spirit of the Borley Nun, who identified herself as **Evangeline Westcott** [6].

> **Note:** This is the *only* communication naming the nun "Evangeline Westcott." Subsequent planchette sessions (1937–38) by [[helen-glanville|Helen Glanville]] independently produced the name "[[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]." These are contradictory identifications.

## Writings, Contradictions, and Rivalries

On **December 24, 1942**, L'Estrange published a highly sensationalised account of his visit in the *Norwich Mercury*, an abridged version of which [[harry-price|Harry Price]] later included in *The End of Borley Rectory* [4, 12]. However, this account was deeply flawed and heavily contradicted both his own contemporaries and established facts:

- **Erasing the Circle:** L'Estrange wrote the rest of the [[marks-tey-spiritualist-circle|Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle]] completely out of his narrative, making it appear as though he was the sole invited investigator in the house, and entirely "forgot" to mention the Blue Room séance [13, 14]. Researchers attribute this "amnesia" to a spat with [[george-warren|Mr. Warren]]; L'Estrange wanted sole credit for cleansing the Rectory of its spirits [14].
- **The Levitating Pencil:** L'Estrange claimed [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Foyster]] told him he watched a pencil rise from a desk on its own to scrawl a message on the wall. This contradicted Foyster's meticulous insistence that he *never* saw paranormal phenomena actually happen, only the aftermath [15–17].
- **Geographical Errors:** He mistakenly claimed the Rectory was built of stone (which Price quietly edited to "red brick" before publishing) and claimed he saw bottles thrown in the front hallway when the event actually occurred in the back stairwell [13, 15].

When confronted with his numerous errors by Price, L'Estrange admitted in a December 1944 letter: **"I was simply staggered to learn that I had made so many mistakes in my article about Borley Rectory. It just shows what tricks one's memory can play."** [12, 15].

## The SPR Report and Libel Apology

The 1956 [[the-borley-report|SPR Report]] contained "quite untrue and libellous" paragraphs about L'Estrange. The authors tore his narrative apart but went too far in their personal attacks; L'Estrange considered some statements libellous, forcing the SPR to publish a formal retraction and apology in the *SPR Journal* in **December 1959** [18–20]. [[captain-deane|Captain V.M. Deane]] separately confirmed the bottle materialisation to Underwood: "he had cross-examined the witnesses for hours on end, and there was no shadow of doubt." [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard Whitehouse]] also confirmed the bottle materialisation independently. Despite the scepticism and his own admitted memory tricks, L'Estrange maintained until his death that he had genuinely experienced paranormal activity at Borley Rectory [19].
