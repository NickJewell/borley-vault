# Guy P.J. L'Estrange JP

Town councillor, county councillor, Justice of the Peace, and medium from Bungay, Suffolk. Also a former headmaster of Colchester Grammar School. Member of the Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle. Visited [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in January 1932 during the [[lionel-foyster|Foyster]] incumbency. Interviewed by Underwood, his wife, and Steuart Kiernander at L'Estrange's home in Bungay, Suffolk, on 23 August 1969.

## Background

His psychic abilities had been tested by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] at the National Laboratory of Psychical Research in March 1931. He was known for materialising a "topless ghost named the Female Dancer," an Islamic spirit guide called "Abu ben Mohammed," and the spirit of a springer spaniel called Lady.

## The January 1932 Visit (bps-08)

L'Estrange arrived at Borley Rectory in the afternoon of January 23, 1932, with fellow members of the Marks Tey Circle including Captain V.M. Deane. After the rest of the party left, L'Estrange spent the night with the Foysters — just the rector, his wife, and one maid in the house.

### Phenomena Experienced

**Tall clerical figure:** Before entering, he saw "a tall figure, like a man in ecclesiastical attire, near the porch" — it vanished when approached and did not reappear when he returned to the original position.

**Crockery and bottles:** A loud crash in the hall; floor littered with broken crockery. Then "an appalling series of crashing sounds": bottles "being hurled about in all directions, although no human person was to be seen." All three stood and watched bottles appear in mid-air and smash. "One large wine bottle missed my left ear by about an inch." The shed of empty bottles outside was locked with the window bolted.

**The thirty bells silenced:** As the three descended the stairs, all thirty bells in the kitchen passage began clanging wildly by themselves (Lionel said wires of all but three had been cut in a previous attempt to stop the ringing). L'Estrange said aloud: "If some invisible person is present and can hear these words, please stop the ringing for a moment." **Instantly, every bell became silent.**

**Footsteps through the wall:** In the drawing-room, cautious but distinct footsteps entered the room, paused directly behind him on the settee, and then "went through the wall at the far end of the room." Lionel confirmed there had once been a doorway at that spot.

**Luminous form:** In the "haunted bedroom" in the small hours, L'Estrange saw "a vague and slightly luminous form" beside his bed that gradually grew "larger and denser" into a shape resembling a human figure in long robes. He addressed it; no reply. He attempted to approach and felt "considerable willpower and persistence" required just to maintain his position — as though "an attempt was being made to push him away." The form abruptly faded; the icy chill disappeared; he sank back "exhausted."

**Marianne in trance:** Saw Marianne "glide rather than walk" past the Blue Room door at 1 a.m. in a long night-dress. She replied to his question in a trance-like voice: "To — make — some — tea." Lionel, alerted, came out and was "very annoyed."

### SPR Retraction

The 1956 [[the-borley-report|SPR Report]] contained "quite untrue and libellous" paragraphs about L'Estrange. Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall had to publish a retraction. Captain V.M. Deane separately confirmed the bottle materialization to Underwood: "he had cross-examined the witnesses for hours on end, and there was no shadow of doubt." [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard Whitehouse]] also confirmed the bottle materialization independently.

### Aftermath

A week after the visit, Foyster wrote to thank L'Estrange: "there had been no more disturbances and quite a different feeling throughout the house." Three years later: "the house is now perfectly normal." But phenomena resumed.

## The Marks Tey Circle Séance (hbrs-ch13)

On 23 January 1932, L'Estrange also led a separate séance with George Warren (the Marks Tey Circle postmaster-spiritualist) in the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] — [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] and [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] declined to attend. L'Estrange made contact with his spirit control "Zenith," and the entity identified itself as the Borley Nun, giving the name **Evangeline Westcott**.

> **Note:** This is the *only* communication naming the nun "Evangeline Westcott." Subsequent planchette sessions (1937–38) by [[helen-glanville|Helen Glanville]] independently produced the name "Marie Lairre." These are contradictory identifications.

**Biographical Details and Background**
**Guy P.J. L'Estrange (1898–1973)** was a prominent 1930s physical medium, psychic artist, and healer [1]. Born in 1898, he was married to a woman named Hilda and had at least two daughters, eventually passing away at Firsview Drive, Northampton, in 1973 [2]. At the time of his involvement with Borley Rectory, he was described as a cheerful 32-year-old freelance journalist from Norfolk [3]. He resided at various times at 'Nazareth', 119 Beccles Road, Bungay, Suffolk, and Beresford Road, Great Yarmouth [2, 4]. 

He claimed to be a Justice of the Peace, a county councillor, and a town councillor, though researchers have pointed out a curious contradiction: **his name does not appear in either the 1925 or 1937 Suffolk and Norfolk directories**, suggesting his civic eminence might have occurred later or outside East Anglia [2, 5]. As a spiritualist, he contributed a weekly column titled "Psychic Corner" for the *Yarmouth Independent* and was appointed "Life President" of the Great Yarmouth Spiritualist Church in 1934, resigning two years later due to "business pressure" [1, 2]. 

L'Estrange claimed an exotic cast of spirit guides, including a topless ghost called the **'Female Dancer'**, an Islamic guide named **'Abu ben Mohammed'**, a springer spaniel named **'Lady'**, and a primary control spirit named **'Zenith'** [3, 6]. In March 1931, Harry Price tested L'Estrange's abilities at the National Laboratory of Psychical Research, yielding negative results which Price blamed on the truculent behavior of fellow Spiritualist Dennis Bradley [3, 5].

**The January 1932 Investigation at Borley Rectory**
L'Estrange is most famous for his visit to Borley Rectory on **January 23–24, 1932**, as a medium operating with the Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle [3, 7]. Upon arriving, L'Estrange noted the house felt "exactly the sort of place one associates with ghosts" and remarked on its "heavy and supernormally cold" atmosphere [8].

During this visit, he reported experiencing a barrage of intense phenomena:
*   **The Bells:** L'Estrange witnessed 30 servant bells clanging wildly all at once in the kitchen passage. He called out, **"If some invisible person is present and can hear these words, please stop the ringing for a moment,"** and the bells instantly went silent [9].
*   **Exorcism with Relic:** As the phenomena escalated into smashing bottles and thrown objects, L'Estrange, Rev. Lionel Foyster, and Marianne Foyster proceeded from room to room making the sign of the cross with a relic of the Curé d’Ars, which practically ceased the immediate disturbances [7, 10].
*   **The Séance:** During a late-night séance in the freezing Blue Room, guided by his control 'Zenith', L'Estrange claimed to channel the intense, evil spirit of the famous Borley nun, who identified herself through him as **Evangeline Westcott** [6]. 
*   **Marianne Foyster's Trance:** At around 1:00 a.m., L'Estrange observed Marianne Foyster gliding through the house in a diaphanous night-dress in a trance-like state. When he asked her where she was going, she replied very slowly, **"To - make - some - tea"**—an incident that greatly annoyed her husband [11].

**Writings, Contradictions, and Rivalries**
On December 24, 1942, L'Estrange published a highly sensationalized account of his visit in the *Norwich Mercury*, an abridged version of which Harry Price later included in his book *The End of Borley Rectory* [4, 12]. However, this account was deeply flawed and heavily contradicted both his own contemporaries and established facts:
*   **Erasing the Circle:** L'Estrange wrote the rest of the Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle completely out of his narrative, making it appear as though he was the sole invited investigator in the house, and entirely "forgot" to mention the Blue Room séance [13, 14]. Researchers attribute this "amnesia" to a spat with Mr. Warren (the leader of the Circle); L'Estrange wanted sole credit for cleansing the Rectory of its spirits [14].
*   **The Levitating Pencil:** In his article, L'Estrange claimed Rev. Foyster told him he watched a pencil rise from a desk on its own to scrawl a message on the wall. This contradicted Foyster's meticulous insistence that he *never* saw paranormal phenomena actually happen, only the aftermath [15-17].
*   **Geographical Errors:** He mistakenly claimed the Rectory was built of stone (which Price quietly edited to "red brick" before publishing) and claimed he saw bottles thrown in the front hallway when the event actually occurred in the back stairwell [13, 15].

When confronted with his numerous errors by Harry Price, L'Estrange admitted in a December 1944 letter: **"I was simply staggered to learn that I had made so many mistakes in my article about Borley Rectory. It just shows what tricks one’s memory can play."** [12, 15].

**The SPR Report and Libel Apology**
L'Estrange’s sensationalized claims were heavily criticized by the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) in their highly skeptical 1956 *Borley Report*. The authors tore his narrative apart but went too far in their personal attacks against him; L'Estrange considered some of their statements libelous, forcing the SPR to publish a formal retraction and apology in the *SPR Journal* in December 1959 [18-20]. Despite the skepticism and his own admitted memory tricks, L'Estrange maintained until his death that he had genuinely experienced paranormal activity at Borley Rectory [19].
