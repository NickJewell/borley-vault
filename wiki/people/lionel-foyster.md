# Reverend Lionel Algernon Foyster, MA (1878–1945)

Rector of Borley 1930–1935. A cousin of the late [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] — his uncle Henry Bull and cousin Harry Bull had held the parish for over seventy years. His incumbency produced the most intense and varied paranormal reports in Borley's history.

## Background

Born on **7 January 1878** in Hastings, East Sussex, the fourth son of the Rev. George Alfred Foyster, who was the rector and patron of the affluent All Saints' parish [1-3]. He was educated at Bilton Grange, Haileybury, and Pembroke College, Cambridge, receiving a BA in 1900 and an MA in 1907 [3-5]. He was ordained a deacon in 1903 and a priest in 1904 [3, 6].

Rather than taking up the affluent family living at Hastings, Foyster served as a curate in Yorkshire and then in Cheshire under his brother-in-law [7]. Researcher [[robert-wood|Robert Wood]] theorised this was an exile due to a "sexual misdemeanor" or scandal, suggesting Foyster may have had abnormal urges — though others strongly dispute this for lack of evidence [7-9].

A "delightful, much-travelled and cultured person" — [[harry-price|Price's]] description. He had a tremendous love of music: wrote new masses, trained choirs, produced operettas and plays. "He always felt he could have become a great actor if he had not been in the Church."

## Canadian Career and Marriage

In 1910 he emigrated to Canada, serving as a missionary and Rector in remote areas of New Brunswick and Nova Scotia — rector of Hardwick NB; rector of Sackville NS; missionary work [5, 6, 10]. On **22 August 1922** he married the 23-year-old **[[marianne-foyster|Marianne Emily Rebecca Shaw]]** at Salmonhurst, New Brunswick [11-13]. The union was, in law, bigamous — Marianne had never dissolved her first marriage. Contradictorily, **Foyster himself had baptised Marianne in 1906 when she was just seven years old** and he was a 28-year-old curate [14, 15]. An officiating priest allegedly warned Foyster that marrying his spiritual daughter was equivalent to "spiritual incest," a detail that reportedly shocked him deeply [16, 17].

His only child was adopted daughter [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide]], brought with them when they returned to England [18, 19].

## Health and the Decision to Come to Borley

By 1930 Lionel had suffered:
- Arthritis (worsening significantly)
- A heart attack while swimming at Prince Edward Island in 1925
- Loss of most of his personal money in a Canadian venture in 1928

He was described as "living through a death sentence" — he knew his illness would worsen progressively. Canadian winters were no longer possible. [[borley-rectory|Borley]] was offered to him as "an ideal spot for a sick man." He accepted knowing it would be a temporary arrangement. He was afflicted with chronic rheumatoid arthritis, walked with a limp, required a wheelchair by the end of his life, and suffered heart trouble that caused memory lapses [33, 46-48]. He took artificial digitalin for his heart [49, 50].

## The Borley Incumbency (1930–1935)

The Foysters moved into [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] on **16 October 1930** [20-22]. Foyster claimed that from their very first day, paranormal activity commenced [23]. During their five-year stay, an estimated 2,000 phenomena were reported — bells ringing, objects teleporting, bottles smashing, and mysterious [[wall-writings]] [26, 27]. Foyster attributed these to "goblins" or "figis" [24, 25]. The frail Rector attempted to combat them using Holy Water, relics of the Curé d'Ars, and by burning creosote. After fumigating the house on May 2, 1931, he declared: **"Now, goblins, how do you like this?"** [28, 29].

## The Fictional Book Project (Owen/Mitchell Finding)

The most important revelation about Lionel's motivations comes from [[iris-owen|Iris Owen]] and Pauline Mitchell's 1979 report, based on [[marianne-foyster|Marianne's]] own testimony:

**Lionel was writing a fictional best-seller to solve the Foysters' financial problems.**

He was well aware that [[walter-hubbell|Walter Hubbell]] had made significant money from *The Great Amherst Mystery* (an account of a Canadian poltergeist case). He conceived a parallel project: a novelised account of Borley to be titled *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*. The financial pressure was acute — they were "penniless when they left the rectory" [33-35].

Evidence that this is correct:
- The manuscript (FM) uses pseudonyms — consistent with fiction, not memoir
- Marianne told Swanson it was "supposed to be more or less fiction — the whole thing was"
- Owen/Mitchell: Lionel "threw things in order to observe [guests'] reactions and to note what they would say. She says the minute these people left the house all such throwing of objects stopped. The phenomena ceased completely when Lionel became confined to a wheelchair."
- He "would relate, with great relish, stories of phenomena that were alleged to have happened, and which the family members present knew were not true, in order to observe his visitor's reactions"
- Sceptics note Foyster may have been inspired by [[walter-hubbell|Walter Hubbell's]] book on the Canadian "Amherst Mystery," going so far as to use the Amherst name "Teed" in his manuscript [42]

## The Three Manuscripts — SPR Analysis (spr-v)

The 1956 SPR Report established the provenance of Lionel's three accounts, demonstrating that [[harry-price|Price's]] claim to have reprinted "verbatim" extracts from "the Rev. L. A. Foyster's Diary" was deliberately misleading on multiple counts:

- The **Summary of Experiences** (January–February 1938) — what Price published as the "Diary" — was written specifically at Price's request for inclusion in MHH, seven years after the events
- Price opened his chapter by saying "from the very first day when the Rev. L. A. Foyster entered upon his duties at Borley, he kept a diary" — knowing this was false
- The **Diary of Occurrences** (the actual first account, 1931) was never quoted in either MHH or EBR
- Each successive account grew more dramatic: events vague in the Diary became precise and paranormal-sounding in the Summary
- The most striking example: "Marianne dear" was heard by one person (Marianne) in the Diary; by EBR (1946) it was heard by three people "on the very first day"

The SPR also noted Lionel's notable omissions in his later accounts: no mention of the wine-into-ink transformation (October 1931); no mention of Edwin Whitehouse's three most sensational incidents in the Summary. These suggest selective memory or deliberate suppression where Marianne's possible agency was too obvious.

See [[foyster-manuscripts]] for full analysis. In brief:

1. **Diary of Occurrences (DO)** — March–July 1931; written from memory; 31 pages; sent to Price October 1931 [36, 37]
2. **Fifteen Months in a Haunted House (FM)** — after May 1934; novelistic version with pseudonyms; intended for publication [38-41]
3. **Summary of Experiences (SE)** — January–February 1938; written at Price's request for MHH; presented by Price as "the Diary" [43-45]

All three were written retrospectively, from memory, with increasing fictionalisation.

## Clarke's Four-Document Analysis (slbr-07)

[[andrew-clarke|Andrew Clarke]] provides the definitive analysis of Lionel's writings, identifying four documents (not three as sometimes stated):

1. **"Writing A" (the inferred contemporary diary)** — Clarke argues this almost certainly never existed. Lionel's own words in "Fifteen Months" (*"no exact record"* of post-June events; things *"lost in oblivion"*) are incompatible with having a diary in hand. Marianne did not remember any such diary.

2. **"Diary of Occurrences"** — Three installments written March–June 1931, within ~4 months of the first events. The closest thing to a contemporary record. Written primarily to send to family members. Lionel swore it was accurate on oath. Price borrowed the only copy and never returned it.

3. **"Fifteen Months in a Haunted House"** — A fictionalized account written c.1933–34 for commercial publication. Pseudonyms throughout; Edwin Whitehouse became "Miss Edith Greycastle"; Frank Pearless became "Lawless." Marianne: *"It was fun, and he started it as something to do… it would have been published as a book of fiction."* Price did not obtain a copy until after *The End of Borley Rectory* (1946).

4. **"Summary of Experiences at Borley Rectory" (January–February 1938)** — Written at Price's request, seven years after the events, for inclusion in MHH as a "diary." This is what appeared in print.

**Critical finding:** What Price printed in *The Most Haunted House in England* as a day-by-day diary was the **1938 Summary**, written seven years later. It is the most arresting chapter of the book precisely because Lionel's arthritis had forced him into a terse style. It is not a diary.

## Relationship to Price

Welcomed [[harry-price|Price]] in October 1931 but the visit ended badly over the wine-into-ink episode. The breach lasted seven years; reconciled through [[sidney-glanville|Glanville's]] patient intercession. Lionel continued to correspond with Price throughout the incumbency, "feeding him with stories of the alleged happenings, evidently to gain Price's opinion, and to obtain background knowledge for his book."

On **14 October 1931**, [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and his investigators accused [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] of faking the phenomena. Foyster was furious, staunchly defending his wife's integrity, and barred Price from the Rectory for years [30-32].

Whether Price knew the manuscripts were fictional is unresolved. Marianne believed he did but found it useful to pretend otherwise. Lionel eventually gave Price permission to quote the SE for *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940).

## Physical Decline

By the end of the incumbency he was increasingly crippled by arthritis. Marianne tried running a flower shop with d'Arles to support them. She worked as an usherette, sold washing machines. He collapsed in the pulpit on his last visit to Borley church. In his final years he was completely confined to a wheelchair or bed — for ten years.

"The phenomena ceased completely when Lionel became confined to a wheelchair." (Owen/Mitchell)

## Reliability as a Witness

The traditional view: the authors of *Ghosts of Borley* and Price regarded him as honest. The score or so witnesses who gave evidence about the Foyster period were "all emphatic in their conviction of the integrity of the Rector."

The revised view: Lionel was writing fiction, deliberately staging phenomena, and was also (by the end) suffering from cardiac-related brain oxygen deprivation that made him "very, very forgetful" and may have blurred the line between fabrication and belief in his own accounts. Clinical psychologist [[andrew-clarke|Andrew Clarke]] suggests his drug cocktail (digitalin, painkillers for arthritis, possibly depression medication) may have contributed to perceptual distortions he interpreted in terms of the Borley legend [63-66].

## Wood's Thesis: Voyeuristic Accomplice

[[robert-wood|Robert Wood]] (*The Widow of Borley*) advances the most radical reassessment of Lionel's role:

**Not a dupe, but a willing agent.** Lionel knew d'Arles's real name (Pearless) from the outset — confirmed by his own writings where "Pearless" is used. [[ian-greenwood|Ian Shaw]] (Marianne's son) testified that Lionel scattered ashes from the hall stove over the floor and blamed "The Things." Wood concludes Lionel derived vicarious satisfaction from his wife's relationships and was effectively a voyeur.

**The "Spiritual Father" justification:** Lionel baptized [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] on 19 June 1906 when she was seven and he was a curate at Oughtrington. He later invoked this sacramental relationship as a theological justification — claiming the baptism made him her "Spiritual Father" in a way that precluded their marriage from being valid, and therefore permitted Marianne to have sexual relations with other men.

**In Fifteen Months**: Lionel gave d'Arles the pseudonym **"Mr Lawless"** — a telling choice for the man he was allegedly deceived by.

**The denial of family living:** Wood argues Lionel was denied the Bull family living at All Saints', Hastings (1904) due to some early sexual indiscretion or scandal — explaining his exile first to serve under his brother-in-law's watchful eye at Oughtrington, then to remote Canadian backwoods at Bay du Vin, New Brunswick. The precise nature of this alleged early difficulty remains unresolved.

**Babbs and Mathias's counter:** They favour giving Lionel "the benefit of the doubt" — that he was duped about Marianne's first marriage and that his declining mental health made him increasingly confused. They find the voyeur thesis "unconvincing."

## The "Father-in-Law" Deception and Death

After collapsing in the Borley pulpit in October 1935, Foyster retired [54, 55]. [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] bigamously married a commercial traveller named [[henry-fisher|Henry Fisher]] [56, 57]. **Foyster lived with the bigamous couple in Ipswich and Rendlesham, actively participating in the fraud by pretending to be Marianne's elderly, invalid father** [58-60].

Foyster died on **18 April 1945** at Dairy Cottages, Rendlesham, Suffolk, aged 67, and was buried at [[campsey-ashe|Campsea Ashe]] churchyard [67, 70]. His death certificate, signed by Dr. K.J.T. Keer, listed the causes as **"exhaustion, bedsores, and rheumatoid arthritis"** [69]. Medical experts later deemed these causes highly irregular, leading researchers (and Foyster's own relatives) to suspect that Marianne had deliberately hastened his death — perhaps by smothering him with a pillow — motivated by her desire to leave England with her new American G.I. lover [69, 71, 72]. This suspicion was never proven. [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] visited his grave in 1997 via [[vincent-oneil]]. His ghost — "with clerical skirts and a limp caused by rheumatism" — has reportedly been seen walking the churchyard.

His will (made 1922) left everything to Marianne. He died before the SPR's 1956 critical investigation and could not respond to its charges.
