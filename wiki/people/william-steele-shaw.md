# William Steele Shaw

Father of [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] (born Marianne Emily Rebecca Shaw). His unsuccessful career and near-poverty shaped Marianne's lifelong tendency to fabricate a grander background for herself.

## Career

Shaw's career was a series of failures. He had been a schoolmaster at **Grafton House**, a small public school near Manchester, but left for reasons unknown. He then attempted to become a publican, running the **Woodman Inn at Hyde, near Stockport**, but was as unsuccessful at that as he had been in teaching. By the time Marianne was born in January 1899 he was eking out a precarious existence as a **private tutor of shorthand and book-keeping**, and the family lived in near poverty at 5 Guy Wood Cottages, Romiley — a terrace of very small houses with a single outside lavatory shared by neighbours.

Robert Wood observes that Shaw was "probably a frustrated man, who had aspired to, but failed to achieve, better things." He speculates that his unfulfilled aspirations were transferred to his daughter, who spent her adult life claiming to have an aristocratic background.

## Moves

- *Birth of Marianne (1899)*: living in Romiley, near Stockport, Cheshire
- *c. 1901*: family moved to **Lymm, near Warrington** — where the fateful encounter with the curate [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] occurred
- *1907*: moved to **Northern Ireland**, where Shaw obtained a post as **time-keeper for the British Portland Cement Company**
- Marianne was later born to say she lived near **Larne**, County Antrim, and it was there that she met and secretly married [[harold-greenwood|Harold Greenwood]] in 1914

## Collusion with Marianne

When Foyster visited the Shaws in Northern Ireland (on leave from Canada), William and Annie Shaw are alleged to have concealed Marianne's first marriage and son [[ian-greenwood|Ian Greenwood Shaw]] from him. Ian was passed off as Marianne's younger brother. Wood suggests this family dishonesty directly modelled the deceptions Marianne practised throughout her adult life.

Marianne herself attributed her tendency to fantasise partly to her childhood poverty: "As a child I used to make up stories that I was a princess, all kinds of silly things."

> **Contradiction:** Marianne consistently gave false details about her father on official documents. On her 1914 marriage certificate to Greenwood, Shaw's occupation is stated as "schoolmaster" — which he once had been, but no longer was. On her 1935 bigamous marriage certificate to Fisher, her father is rendered as "Leon Alphonse Voyster, of independent means" — a conflation of [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]]'s name.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**William Steele Shaw** is a notable secondary figure in the Borley Rectory saga as the biological father of the highly controversial Marianne Foyster. A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals a life marked by financial instability, shifting careers, and an active role in concealing the scandalous early life of his daughter. 

**Family and Early Career**
*   **Marriage and Children:** William was married to Anne Elizabeth Shaw (formerly Woodyatt), with whom he had a son, Geoffrey (born in 1897), and a daughter, Mary Anne Emily Rebecca Shaw—later known as Marianne—who was born on January 26, 1899 [1, 2]. 
*   **A "Chequered" Career:** The sources describe William Shaw's professional life as a "chequered and unsuccessful career" [3]. He initially worked as a schoolmaster at a small public school near Manchester named Grafton House, but left this position for reasons that remain unknown [3]. He then attempted to become a publican at the Woodman Inn in Hyde, near Stockport, but was just as unsuccessful in this venture as he had been in teaching [3]. 
*   **Poverty in Romiley:** By the time Marianne was born in 1899, William was eking out a precarious and impoverished existence as a private tutor, giving lessons in shorthand, arithmetic, and book-keeping to make ends meet, while his wife gave piano lessons [1, 3, 4]. The family lived in near poverty in a small terrace cottage at 5 Guy Wood Cottages in Romiley, Cheshire, sharing a single outside lavatory with several other families [3, 4]. 

**The Connection to Lionel Foyster**
*   Despite their humble circumstances, the Shaws became acquainted with a young local curate named Lionel Foyster, who actually baptized Marianne when she was seven years old [5]. 
*   The Shaws and Foyster maintained a friendship over the years, exchanging Christmas and Easter cards even after Foyster emigrated to Canada in 1910 to work as a missionary [6, 7].

**Relocation to Ireland**
*   In 1907, William relocated his family to Northern Ireland, settling in the rural hamlet of Drumnadreagh, near Larne [8, 9]. 
*   Here, William finally secured a steady position working as a time-keeper for the British Portland Cement Company [9]. 

**Covering for Marianne's Scandals**
William Shaw played a significant role in attempting to maintain his family's respectability amidst his daughter's disastrous first marriage and subsequent affairs:
*   **The "Pathetic Dishonesty":** When fifteen-year-old Marianne married a clerk named Harold Giffard Greenwood in 1914, William's occupation on the marriage certificate was listed as "schoolmaster" [9]. Because he no longer held this profession, researchers point to this "pathetic dishonesty" by the Shaws—clinging to the outward appearances of gentility—as the psychological catalyst that reinforced Marianne's lifelong tendency to fantasize and lie about her own background [9].
*   **The Legal Separation:** When Greenwood abandoned Marianne only six weeks after she gave birth to their son, Ian, William Shaw intervened by having a solicitor draw up a document of legal separation, though a formal divorce never took place [10]. 
*   **Raising His Grandson:** To protect Marianne from gossip, William and his wife took over the upbringing of her infant son, Ian Geoffrey William [10, 11]. The Shaws maintained a strict pretense that the boy was actually their own son and Marianne’s younger brother [11, 12]. This deception was so successfully maintained by William that when Ian Shaw died decades later in 1986, even his own widow was still under the impression that William and Annie Shaw had been his true parents [13].
