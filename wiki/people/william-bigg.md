# William Bigg

Farmer, churchwarden, and the closest neighbour of the rectors at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] throughout the Smith and Foyster periods.

## Background

Bigg was a farmer who lived at **[[borley-place|Borley Place]]**, the historic manor house immediately across the road from [[borley-church|Borley Church]] and almost exactly opposite the Rectory, which he leased from the wealthy Payne family (who owned Borley Hall) [1–4]. He served as churchwarden at Borley Church during [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]]'s long incumbency and continued in that role after Bull's death.

## Relationship with the Bull Family

The Biggs appear to have been on close terms with the Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Foyster Bull]]. During Harry Bull's final illness in 1927, Mrs. Bigg visited him and "saw him a good deal" [1]. When [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] was trying to confirm whether [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] had worn a plum-coloured dressing gown — she had been seeing his apparition — she asked Mrs Bigg discreetly. Mrs Bigg confirmed that Reverend Bull did indeed wear a plum-coloured smoking jacket and was barely seen out of it in the months before he died. William Bigg attended Harry Bull's funeral on Tuesday, 14 June 1927, representing the parish as the churchwarden [5, 7].

## Smith Period

When the [[rev-eric-smith|Smiths]] arrived in 1928, Bigg was one of the first to call. Though not unfriendly, he resented relinquishing the authority he had accumulated during the vacancy since Bull's death, which made [[rev-eric-smith|Eric Smith's]] church responsibilities difficult from the start. The sources note that William Bigg and the Smiths "had not got on" [6].

Bigg was highly sceptical of the paranormal phenomena reported during the Smiths' time at the Rectory. During conversations with Canon [[henry-lawton|H. Lawton]] (who visited Borley during the 1930s), Bigg bluntly revealed his personal belief that **the "haunt" during the Smiths' time had been deliberately "engineered by Eric Smith"** — to depreciate the Rectory's market value so that a friend could buy it cheaply [6].

## Foyster Period

During the poltergeist peak of early 1931, Bigg served as a practical lifeline to the besieged Foysters. When [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] collapsed after the failed blessing of the house, [[lionel-foyster|Lionel]] rushed to Borley Place to fetch brandy from Mrs Bigg. When phenomena were at their worst and the Foysters debated whether to spend the night with their neighbours rather than face the poltergeist, they feared that "the Biggs would think they were mad or suffering with their nerves."

The Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] also recorded at least one occasion when — having a premonition that a disturbance was about to occur — he specifically crossed the road to ask Mr. Bigg, who was travelling into Sudbury, to drop off a message requesting that Edwin Whitehouse come to the Rectory to serve as an independent witness [2].

By the time [[henry-lawton|Henry Lawton]] served as locum at the Rectory in summer 1933, Bigg had retired as churchwarden but remained a key source of local oral history.
