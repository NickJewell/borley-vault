# Squadron-Leader Alan J. Cuthbert

RAF officer and one of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s official observers during the 1937–38 tenancy of Borley Rectory. Cuthbert was among the most active sitters at the table-tipping and Planchette séances conducted in the Rectory and at the [[glanvilles|Glanville family]] home.

> **Contradiction:** The Borley sources contain a direct contradiction regarding Cuthbert's profession. Some passages describe him as an officer friend of [[roger-glanville|Roger Glanville]] in the RAF, explicitly giving him the rank of Squadron-Leader [3, 5]. However, another passage detailing his arrival at the Rectory states he "worked for the Bank of England and was a 'responsible sort of person'" [1]. The two accounts have never been reconciled.

## Séance Participation

Cuthbert visited Borley Rectory for an observational period from October 23 to October 25, 1937, alongside [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]], [[roger-glanville|Roger Glanville]], and [[mark-kerr-pearse|Mark Kerr-Pearse]] [4]. During this visit, seven table-tilting séances and one planchette séance were conducted [4]. Because Sidney Glanville was unable to be present at the very start of the visit, he credited Cuthbert, Roger Glanville, and Kerr-Pearse with having "started the ball rolling" regarding the table-tipping experiments [6].

These sessions produced the first extended interrogation of the [[communicating-entities|communicating entities]] recorded in the [[locked-book|Locked Book]], including the earliest references to "Caldibec," "Katie," "Fadenoch," and the gradual development of the [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] narrative.

On the night of October 23, between 9:55 p.m. and 10:30 p.m., Cuthbert acted as a sitter with Roger Glanville and Kerr-Pearse during a session held in the ground-floor kitchen passage, just outside the sewing room [7, 8].

Cuthbert is specifically mentioned as one of the first to operate the Planchette at Borley, alongside [[roger-glanville|Roger Glanville]]:

> *"The Planchette board … was there first used by A. J. Cuthbert and my son, Roger H. Glanville, who immediately obtained writings."*

The first recorded Planchette scripts from Borley (from October 25, 1937 onward) were thus produced under Cuthbert's hands as one of the operators.

## The Footsteps Incident (October 24/25, 1937)

Cuthbert was at the centre of one of the investigation's most notable auditory events, albeit unconsciously. During the night, Cuthbert retired to the Rectory's [[base-room|Base Room]] (the former library) to sleep on a camp bed, with session notes recording his bedtime as 12:20 a.m. [9, 10]. While he slept, the other three investigators sat in the dark on the landing outside the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] [10, 11].

> **Contradiction:** The sources contain a slight discrepancy regarding exactly when the subsequent event occurred. One report logs it at 2:40 a.m. on Sunday, October 24 [11], while other accounts place it at 2:00 a.m. on October 25 [2, 10, 12].

The three awake observers distinctly "heard heavy muffled footsteps walk across the hall, just under us," accompanied by the sounds of general bodily movement [2, 11]. Certain that the noises must be Cuthbert, they went downstairs immediately to investigate, only to find the hall empty and Cuthbert still "soundly sleeping in the Base Room" [2, 10, 11].

## The Streatham Planchette Séance (January 8, 1938)

On January 8, 1938, at Streatham, Cuthbert sat alongside [[helen-glanville|Helen Glanville]] at a séance in which Miss Glanville's brother Roger wrote the questions on paper so neither sitter was aware of them — a control measure [5]. Cuthbert and Miss Helen Glanville rested their hands on the board while [[roger-glanville|Roger Glanville]] wrote down the questions, ensuring the sitters were entirely unaware of what was being asked [5]. At this session "Henry" communicated and another entity signed itself "Jeanne Waldegrave," promising to help the sitters find proof.

## The March 1938 Visit

Cuthbert paid another visit to Borley Rectory on March 12, 1938, accompanying Sidney Glanville, Mr. M. Savage, and Mr. Bridgewater [13]. During a routine patrol of the house at 10:00 p.m., the party discovered fresh, dark, sticky stains "with the consistency of glycerine" on the floor of the chapel, which had not been present during earlier inspections [13]. They scraped up some of the fluid but could not gather enough for a proper chemical analysis [13].

Later that night, at 1:45 a.m., the men heard a noise from the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] above them that sounded like a chair leg scraping along the floor [13]. This was immediately followed by a "sharp, hard sound sufficiently loud to make us jump" emanating from the top middle shelf of the [[base-room|Base Room]] bookcase [13]. The investigators ruled out wood crepitation, noting that the room's temperature had varied by only 1° Fahrenheit over the preceding ten hours [13].

## Significance

Cuthbert's contribution to the séance record is substantial: as one of the earliest Planchette operators at the Rectory itself, he was present when the [[communicating-entities|communicating entities]] first began to develop the narrative that would, six years later, lead to the August 1943 excavation.
