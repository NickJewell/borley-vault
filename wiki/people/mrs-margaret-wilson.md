# Mrs. Margaret E. Wilson

Born 1890. Artist and exhibitor; known in art circles for studies of insects and still life, with much of her professional work done outdoors. Visited [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] on or about August 22, 1938 with [[rev-ac-henning|Henning]]'s permission to paint the house. She was not a spiritualist and had no interest in psychical research — her purpose was purely artistic and documentary.

> **Contradiction:** There is a minor discrepancy in the sources regarding the exact date of her visit. Several accounts firmly place her excursion on **August 22, 1938**, however a chronological record compiled by Price contradictorily lists the date as **August 12, 1938**.

Her visit produced two significant results: the only surviving oil painting of Borley Rectory, and the sole account of the "extraordinary insect" phenomenon.

## The Oil Painting

Because [[harry-price|Price]]'s tenancy had expired, he directed her to the Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|A. C. Henning]] to obtain permission to paint on the grounds. She arrived at Sudbury just before midday and proceeded to the Rectory's garden, setting up her easel and camp-stool under a large Cedar of Lebanon near the summer-house, opposite the Nun's Walk, with the back elevation — "the only elevation really worth painting" — as her subject.

In the light of later events (the Rectory burning down in 1939), her visit proved extremely fortunate, as the resulting oil painting was **cleverly and faithfully executed and became the only coloured picture of the Rectory extant**, preserved within the [[borley-dossier|Harry Price Library]].

## Atmospheric Impressions

Despite having no interest in ghosts, Mrs Wilson found the environment deeply unsettling. In her subsequent report she described the atmosphere of the grounds:

> "As the hours passed and I was quite alone, the forbidding silence seemed to envelop everything with apprehension and I felt conscious of time and invisible watchers near me and around me. A neglected garden can present a cheerful appearance, but here one feels the very weeds do not wish to flourish — or cannot — and lack the joy which most growing plants have."

## The Extraordinary Insect

About 3 p.m., while she was at work, an unidentified creature approached her:

> "I suddenly noticed a sound, such as of approaching wings or rushing wind coming towards me. Looking up, I saw the queerest object with impelling eyes advancing towards me at about eye level. It seemed to be coming out of a mist. It was accompanied by a wasp on its left, and this must have been the wings I heard."

Mrs Wilson struck the creature with her mahlstick, apparently knocking it into the grass. She searched for it but could not find it. Her retrospective description:

> "Certainly, I had never seen anything like it before, and should describe it as being quite three inches in length, the body entirely black and composed of sections enabling it to bend and expand with ease. Its eyes were large and the colour of bloomy black grapes. The object was flattish."

Returning home, she immediately painted the creature from memory — essential since it had not been recovered. She then asked her friend Miss Joyce Mercer to re-draw it in black and white for publication. Price showed the sketch to "several naturalists and entomologists and they all declare that it is like 'nothing on earth'."

## Significance and Sceptical Assessment

Price notes that Mrs Wilson's credentials as a witness are specifically relevant: "she happens to be well acquainted with insects... well known for her studies of insects and still life." He also describes her, on the basis of their later acquaintance, as "particularly unemotional and... a very hard-headed businesswoman."

The insect account is listed in Appendix D under "A Strange Insect — Mrs. Margaret E. Wilson." It stands entirely alone in the Borley record — no comparable creature was reported by any other observer.

Later commentators strongly dismissed the incident, noting that despite Price's enthusiasm, the tale of the "strange insect" "lies more in the realm of [[borley-hysteria|Borley hysteria]] and appears to have been used by Price the journalist rather than considered by Price the objective psychical researcher."

> **Contested:** Whether the creature was a genuine paranormal manifestation, an unfamiliar but real insect, or a trick of perception on a hot August afternoon remains unresolved.
