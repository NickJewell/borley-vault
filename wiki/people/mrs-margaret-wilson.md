# Mrs. Margaret E. Wilson

Artist and exhibitor; known in art circles for studies of insects and still life, with much of her professional work done outdoors. Visited Borley Rectory on August 22, 1938 with [[rev-ac-henning|Henning's]] permission to paint the house. She was not a spiritualist and had no interest in psychical research — her purpose was purely artistic and documentary. Her visit produced two significant results: the only surviving oil painting of Borley Rectory, and the sole account of the "extraordinary insect" phenomenon.

## The Oil Painting

Price had told Mrs. Wilson that Borley Rectory was "not old; that it was ugly; that it reminded me of the red bricks of suburbia." Nevertheless she obtained permission from Henning, travelled to Borley, and set up her easel on the lawn under one of the large Cedars of Lebanon, opposite the Nun's Walk, with the back elevation — "the only elevation really worth painting" — as her subject. The resulting oil painting was subsequently preserved in the Borley records at the [[borley-dossier|Harry Price Library]] and is the only colour depiction of the house from life. It is described as "cleverly and faithfully executed."

## The Extraordinary Insect

About 3 p.m., while she was at work, an unidentified creature approached her:

> "I suddenly noticed a sound, such as of approaching wings or rushing wind coming towards me. Looking up, I saw the queerest object with impelling eyes advancing towards me at about eye level. It seemed to be coming out of a mist. It was accompanied by a wasp on its left, and this must have been the wings I heard."

Mrs. Wilson struck the creature with her mahlstick, apparently knocking it into the grass. She searched for it but could not find it. Her retrospective description:

> "Certainly, I had never seen anything like it before, and should describe it as being quite three inches in length, the body entirely black and composed of sections enabling it to bend and expand with ease. Its eyes were large and the colour of bloomy black grapes. The object was flattish."

Returning home, she immediately painted the creature from memory — essential since it had not been recovered. She then asked her friend Miss Joyce Mercer to re-draw it in black and white for publication. Price showed the sketch to "several naturalists and entomologists and they all declare that it is like 'nothing on earth'."

Price notes that Mrs. Wilson's credentials as a witness are specifically relevant: "she happens to be well acquainted with insects... well known for her studies of insects and still life." He also describes her, on the basis of their later acquaintance, as "particularly unemotional and... a very hard-headed businesswoman."

## Significance

The insect account is listed in Appendix D under "A Strange Insect — Mrs. Margaret E. Wilson." It stands entirely alone in the Borley record — no comparable creature was reported by any other observer — and the combination of Mrs. Wilson's professional insect expertise and her complete indifference to the haunting make her a particularly robust single witness.

> **Contested:** Whether the creature was a genuine paranormal manifestation, an unfamiliar but real insect, or a trick of perception on a hot August afternoon remains unresolved.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mrs. Margaret E. Wilson** (born in 1890) was a well-known painter and art exhibitor whose involvement in the Borley Rectory saga centers around a visit to paint the house, during which she experienced an oppressive atmosphere and encountered an unidentifiable creature [1-3].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, specific dates, events, quotes, and contradictions regarding her experience:

**Biographical Details and Background**
*   **A Non-Psychic Observer:** In early August 1938, Mrs. Wilson telephoned investigator Harry Price requesting permission to visit Borley to paint a picture of the Rectory [2]. She emphasized that she had absolutely no interest in Borley from a "psychic" point of view, was not a spiritualist, and knew nothing about ghost-hunting; her interest was purely artistic and aesthetic [2]. 
*   **Demeanor and Expertise:** Although she was painting a "haunted" house, Price personally found her to be **"particularly unemotional and... a very hard-headed businesswoman"** [4]. Furthermore, Price noted that she spent much of her time outdoors painting still life and was **"well acquainted with insects,"** which made her subsequent sighting all the more notable to him [3]. 

**The 1938 Visit and Date Contradiction**
Because Price's tenancy had expired, he directed her to the Rev. A. C. Henning to obtain permission to paint on the grounds [5]. She arrived at Sudbury just before midday and proceeded straight to the Rectory's garden, setting up her easel and camp-stool under a large Cedar of Lebanon near the summer-house [6]. 
*   **The Sole Color Record:** In the light of later events (the Rectory burning down in 1939), her visit proved extremely fortunate, as the resulting oil painting was cleverly and faithfully executed and became the **only coloured picture of the Rectory extant**, preserved within the Borley records [5]. 
*   **Chronological Contradiction:** There is a minor discrepancy in the sources regarding the exact date of her visit. Several accounts firmly place her excursion on **August 22, 1938** [6-8]. However, a chronological record compiled by Price contradictorily lists the date as **August 12, 1938** [9]. 

**Atmospheric Impressions and Quotes**
Despite having no interest in ghosts, Mrs. Wilson found the environment deeply unsettling. In her subsequent report, she provided the following quote regarding the atmosphere of the grounds:
> **"As the hours passed and I was quite alone, the forbidding silence seemed to envelop everything with apprehension and I felt conscious of time and invisible watchers near me and around me. A neglected garden can present a cheerful appearance, but here one feels the very weeds do not wish to flourish—or cannot—and lack the joy which most growing plants have"** [4]. 

**The "Strange Insect" Incident**
At around 3:00 p.m. on the day of her visit, Mrs. Wilson was disturbed by the approach of a large, unknown flying insect accompanied by an ordinary wasp [7]. Startled, she struck out, knocking the strange creature into the grass, but despite searching for some time, she was unable to find it [7]. 
*   **Description of the Creature:** In her report, she explicitly described the object as **"being quite three inches in length, the body entirely black and composed of sections enabling it to bend and expand with ease. Its eyes were large and the colour of bloomy black grapes. The object was flatish"** [7]. She was absolutely convinced that she had **"never seen anything like it before"** [7]. 
*   **The Insect Painting:** Upon returning home, she immediately painted the articulated, goggle-eyed creature while her memory of it was fresh [3]. A black-and-white version of this painting was created by her friend, Miss Joyce Mercer, and was reproduced in Harry Price's book *The Most Haunted House in England* [3, 10]. 
*   **Skeptical Analysis:** Price claimed that he showed the sketch to several naturalists and entomologists who declared the insect was "like 'nothing on earth'" [3]. However, later commentators strongly dismissed the incident, noting that despite Price's enthusiasm, the tale of the "strange insect" **"lies more in the realm of Borley hysteria and appears to have been used by Price the journalist rather than considered by Price the objective psychical researcher"** [10].
