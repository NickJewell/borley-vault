# Russell Herbert BEM (1922–c.2002)

Born 1922, spent his early years in Sudbury. Was interviewed by Babbs and Mathias and described an experience in the summer of 1941. He held a British Empire Medal (BEM).

## Three Vanishing Cats — Summer 1941

On a hot, sunny summer day Herbert and some mixed-sex friends set out for a long hike across the fields, passing Brundon Mill and up the hill towards Borley. When the group reached the hamlet, Herbert left his friends briefly to answer a call of nature in the rectory grounds, entering through one of the main gates (then painted a creamy colour) and walking down the gravel driveway lined with tall firs and cedars toward the ruined rectory [1].

As he approached the ruins, he was suddenly struck by an **"awful smell which reminded him of rotting vegetation"** [1], alongside a feeling of intense cold. Looking towards the ruins, he saw **three large cats arching their backs and hissing** at him [1]. He continued walking toward them. **They vanished — not running, not darting into undergrowth, but simply ceasing to exist.** He later stated explicitly that "one second the animals were in front of him and the next second they were not there" [1].

The sudden disappearance terrified Herbert so completely that he forgot his pressing need to relieve himself and immediately ran back to his friends in Hall Lane [2]. Upon seeing him, his friends remarked, **"You look awful. Don't you feel well?"** [2]. One of the girls laughingly asked if he had seen the Borley ghost, a remark that caused the rest of the group to laugh [2].

Reflecting on the encounter years later, Herbert explicitly stated he **"would not like to go through it again,"** emphasising that he had had "only one thought in mind and that it was nothing to do with the alleged haunting" when he walked into the grounds [2].

## Insights on Local Belief

Herbert provided valuable commentary on local attitudes toward the Borley phenomena during his youth in Sudbury. He recalled that there was a great deal of talk about the "Borley ghost" at the time, and summarised the general local consensus: while some accounts were considered **"a bit far-fetched... sensible people all believed that there was something awry at Borley"** [3].

## Significance

This account is among the most compelling for the [[phantom-cat]] phenomenon because:
1. Herbert was not there to observe the haunting — his only purpose was a mundane personal need
2. The sudden cold and smell are consistent with other reported apparitional phenomena at Borley
3. The instantaneous disappearance (not flight into cover) distinguishes it from any normal cat behaviour
