# Rev. Harry Carpenter

Clergyman of 122 Woodstock Road, Oxford. His letter of **30 October 1941**, forwarded to [[harry-price]] by [[mrs-chb-gowan|Mrs C. H. B. Gowan]], contains an important independent account of [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]]'s personal experiences of the Borley phenomena, heard directly from Bull during the First World War.

## Background

Carpenter was well acquainted with the Bull family and personally knew several of the individuals mentioned in Harry Price's books who had claimed to see ghosts [1]. During the First World War, he served as a special constable; his direct superior officer in this role was the Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Foyster Bull]], the Rector of Borley [2]. Carpenter occasionally accompanied Bull to spend part of his patrol with him [2].

## The October 1941 Letter

Having recently read Price's first book, *The Most Haunted House in England*, Carpenter wrote on **30 October 1941** to a Mrs Savile — whose daughter, Mrs C. H. B. Gowan, subsequently forwarded a copy to Harry Price — to share what Harry Bull had personally confided in him years earlier [1, 2].

## Accounts of Harry Bull's Apparitions

During one of their wartime patrols, Harry Bull detailed two specific paranormal encounters to Carpenter:

**The Headless Coachman:** Carpenter recounted Bull's description of encountering the famous [[phantom-coach|phantom coach]] while walking home from Sudbury at night:

> **"Walking home from Sudbury one night, he became aware of galloping horses; then he saw lights coming towards him and he stepped off the road to let the carriage pass. He saw distinctly one man or two on the box, driving; they had no heads, only hands and the lower part of the body. As he watched the whole thing vanished."** [2]

**The Man in the Conservatory:** Carpenter also recorded a daylight encounter Bull had inside the Rectory [2]. Bull was in the dining-room, which opened into a conservatory, when his dog became highly agitated [2]. Bull looked into the conservatory and saw a man dressed in the exact same coloured clothing as the headless coachman he had previously seen on the road [2]: **"He got up and went into the conservatory; the man went out and vanished."** [2, 3]

## Significance

Carpenter's account is notable for several reasons:

1. **Harry Bull's own words**: Most testimony about Bull's experiences comes through his sisters after his death. Carpenter records Bull speaking in the first person about what he saw.
2. **The headless coachman**: The detail of headless (or rather upper-body-absent) figures on the coach box corroborates the testimony of [[ethel-bull]] about the same incident, as independently recorded by Price in *The Most Haunted House in England*.
3. **Dog reaction**: The dog's agitation in the conservatory parallels [[edward-cooper]]'s padding-dog experience and [[captain-gregson]]'s two lost spaniels — a consistent thread across decades.
4. **Independent pathway**: The account reached Price via an entirely independent chain (Mrs Savile → Mrs Gowan → Price), free of contamination from Price's own published version.

Harry Price highly valued Carpenter's letter — arriving roughly twelve months after his first Borley book was published — because it provided independent, unsolicited confirmation of the exact same stories that Harry Bull's sister, Miss [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]], had previously relayed to Price [4].
