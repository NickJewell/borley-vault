# Rev. Harry Carpenter

Clergyman of 122 Woodstock Road, Oxford. His letter of October 30, 1941, forwarded to [[harry-price]] by [[mrs-chb-gowan|Mrs C. H. B. Gowan]], contains an important independent account of [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]]'s personal experiences of the Borley phenomena, heard directly from Bull during the First World War.

## The Wartime Account

Carpenter served as a special constable in the First World War, with Harry Bull as his superior officer. During patrol duties, Bull described experiences that he had had "years before":

> *"Walking home from Sudbury one night, he became aware of galloping horses; then he saw lights coming towards him and he stepped off the road to let the carriage pass. He saw distinctly one man or two on the box, driving; they had no heads, only hands and the lower part of the body. As he watched the whole thing vanished."*

A second incident, in daylight in the dining-room:

> *"A dog with him was agitated, and he saw in the conservatory a man dressed in the same coloured clothes as the coachman he had seen before. He got up and went into the conservatory; the man went out and vanished."*

## Significance

Carpenter's account is notable for several reasons:

1. **Harry Bull's own words**: Most testimony about Bull's experiences comes through his sisters after his death. Carpenter records Bull speaking in the first person about what he saw.
2. **The headless coachman**: The detail of headless (or rather upper-body-absent) figures on the coach box corroborates the testimony of [[ethel-bull]] about the same incident, as independently recorded by Price in *The Most Haunted House in England*.
3. **Dog reaction**: The dog's agitation in the conservatory parallels [[edward-cooper]]'s padding-dog experience and [[captain-gregson]]'s two lost spaniels — a consistent thread across decades.
4. **Independent pathway**: The account reached Price via an entirely independent chain: Mrs Gowan received it from her mother, who received it from Carpenter. It was thus free of contamination from Price's own published version.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Reverend Harry Carpenter** appears in the Borley Rectory sources as a former acquaintance of the Bull family who provided secondary testimony confirming the Rev. Harry Bull’s belief in, and encounters with, paranormal phenomena. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, specific dates, quotes, and accounts regarding his connection to the Borley story:

**Biographical Details and WWI Service**
*   **Residence and Acquaintances:** Rev. Carpenter resided at 122 Woodstock Road, Oxford [1]. He was well acquainted with the Bull family and personally knew several of the individuals mentioned in Harry Price's books who had claimed to see ghosts [1]. 
*   **Relationship with Harry Bull:** During the First World War, Carpenter served as a special constable [2]. His direct superior officer in this role was the Rev. Harry Foyster Bull (the Rector of Borley) [2]. Carpenter occasionally accompanied Bull to spend part of his patrol with him [2].

**The October 1941 Letter**
Carpenter's contribution to the Borley literature stems from a letter he wrote on **October 30, 1941**, to a Mrs. Savile (whose daughter, Mrs. C. H. B. Gowan, subsequently forwarded a copy to Harry Price) [1]. Having recently read Price's first book, *The Most Haunted House in England*, Carpenter wrote to share what Harry Bull had personally confided in him years earlier [1, 2]. 

**Accounts of Harry Bull's Apparitions**
During one of their wartime patrols, Harry Bull detailed two specific paranormal encounters to Carpenter:
*   **The Headless Coachman:** Carpenter recounted Bull's description of encountering the famous phantom coach while walking home from Sudbury at night [2]. Carpenter provided the following specific quote detailing Bull's experience: **"Walking home from Sudbury one night, he became aware of galloping horses; then he saw lights coming towards him and he stepped off the road to let the carriage pass. He saw distinctly one man or two on the box, driving; they had no heads, only hands and the lower part of the body. As he watched the whole thing vanished"** [2].
*   **The Man in the Conservatory:** Carpenter also recorded a daylight encounter Bull had inside the Rectory [2]. Bull was in the dining-room, which opened into a conservatory, when his dog became highly agitated [2]. Bull looked into the conservatory and saw a man dressed in the exact same colored clothing as the headless coachman he had previously seen on the road [2]. Carpenter noted: **"He got up and went into the conservatory; the man went out and vanished"** [2, 3]. 

**Significance to the Investigation**
Harry Price highly valued Rev. Carpenter's letter—which arrived roughly twelve months after his first Borley book was published—because it provided independent, unsolicited confirmation of the exact same stories that Harry Bull's sister, Miss Ethel Bull, had previously relayed to Price [4].
