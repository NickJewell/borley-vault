# Leslie J. Godden (L.D.S., R.C.S.)

The "West End dental surgeon of repute" to whom [[harry-price]] submitted the human remains (fragments of a skull and jawbone) unearthed in the cellar at [[borley-rectory]] on 17 August 1943, after they had been examined by Dr E.H. Bailey *in situ* (see [[bones-excavation|Bones and Excavations]]). A member of [[harry-price|Harry Price's]] [[ghost-club|Ghost Club]], holding Membership No. 317.

## Dental Report

Godden's expert report (subsequently published in Price's *The End of Borley Rectory*) provided several specific forensic conclusions:

- **The Skull:** The skull fragment had in all probability belonged to a youngish person, but the fragments were too small to determine the sex. He estimated the age of the fragments at "anything over a hundred years." The lime clay in which the remains had been found had caused the skull portion to become very decayed, brittle, porous, and soft.
- **The Jawbone and the "Abscess Theory":** The jawbone he adjudged to have belonged to a youngish woman, approximately aged around 30 — "with more probability of its being under than over."

Crucially, Godden discovered that **the woman had suffered from a deep-seated dental abscess**. In a covering letter to [[harry-price]] dated 23 May 1945, Godden noted:

> "The condition indicated ... could be responsible for anything from a persistent mild ache to any one or any combination of the following: a raging toothache; a persistent severe neuralgia; pain in and under or over the ear; pain above the eye or down on the side of the nose. Which of these really occurred, I cannot say; but the fact that the disease is so deep-seated and is in such close association with the mandibular nerve, does suggest that the owner of the bone had a good deal of pain from it."

[[harry-price|Price]] made much of this finding: many of those who claimed to see the [[the-nun|ghost nun]] had remarked on her appearing to be "in pain," with a "pale face," "looking sad," or "as if she had been crying." A deep-seated abscess, Godden's report suggested, could well have accounted for such an appearance.

## Views on the Borley Report

Godden talked at length with [[peter-underwood]] at a Ghost Club meeting about the SPR [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]].

> **Contradiction:** One source places this meeting in **1958**, another dates it to **February 1959**.

He regarded the *Borley Report* as published "primarily for the sake of publicity on the part of the authors, particularly Trevor Hall; for notoriety; for the royalties; and as 'a vicious, unprovoked and dastardly attack on a man no longer in a position to reply.'"

He also pointed out that "it is always possible to attack and apparently discredit somebody or something when the chief defender cannot reply or respond" — a point echoed by many Price supporters.

## Significance

Godden's dental report is the primary medical evidence supporting the claim that the Borley bones belonged to a young woman who suffered significant pain in life. His age estimate (around 30) was consistent with the [[marie-lairre]] hypothesis — the séance data had given 1667 as the date of death, making her a young woman at death. The BRC's resolution of the well-tank controversy (see [[well-tank-controversy]]) supports the conclusion that Godden was examining genuinely old bones, not planted material.
