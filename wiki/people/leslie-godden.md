# Leslie J. Godden

The "West End dental surgeon of repute" to whom [[harry-price]] submitted the human remains (fragments of a skull and jawbone) unearthed in the cellar at [[borley-rectory]] on 17 August 1943, after they had been examined by Dr E.H. Bailey *in situ* (see [[bones-excavation|Bones and Excavations]]).

## Dental Report

Godden's expert opinion was that the skull fragment had in all probability belonged to a youngish person, but the fragments were too small to determine the sex. The age of the fragments he put at "anything over a hundred years." The lime clay in which the remains had been found had caused the skull portion to become very decayed, brittle and porous.

The jawbone he adjudged to have belonged to a youngish woman, perhaps aged around 30 — "with more probability of its being under than over." His detailed report is included in *EBR*.

In a covering letter to [[harry-price]] dated 23 May 1945, Godden noted:

> "The condition indicated ... could be responsible for anything from a persistent mild ache to any one or any combination of the following: a raging toothache; a persistent severe neuralgia; pain in and under or over the ear; pain above the eye or down on the side of the nose. Which of these really occurred, I cannot say; but the fact that the disease is so deep-seated and is in such close association with the mandibular nerve, does suggest that the owner of the bone had a good deal of pain from it."

Price made much of this finding — many of those who claimed to see the [[the-nun|ghost nun]] had remarked on her appearing to be "in pain," with a "pale face," "looking sad" or "as if she had been crying." A deep-seated abscess, Godden's report suggested, could well have accounted for such an appearance.

## Views on the Borley Report

A member of [[harry-price]]'s [[ghost-club]] (Membership No. 317), Godden talked at length with [[peter-underwood]] at a Ghost Club meeting in February 1959. He regarded the SPR [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] as published "primarily for the sake of publicity on the part of the authors, particularly Trevor Hall; for notoriety; for the royalties; and as 'a vicious, unprovoked and dastardly attack on a man no longer in a position to reply.'"

He also pointed out that "it is always possible to attack and apparently discredit somebody or something when the chief defender cannot reply or respond" — a point echoed by many Price supporters.

## Significance

Godden's dental report is the primary medical evidence supporting the claim that the Borley bones belonged to a young woman who suffered significant pain in life. His age estimate (around 30) was consistent with the [[marie-lairre]] hypothesis — the séance data had given 1667 as the date of death, making her a young woman at death. The BRC's resolution of the well-tank controversy (see [[well-tank-controversy]]) supports the conclusion that Godden was examining genuinely old bones, not planted material.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Leslie J. Godden** (recorded with the qualifications L.D.S., R.C.S.) was a **"West End dental surgeon of repute"** and a member of Harry Price’s Ghost Club, holding Membership No. 317 [1-3]. In the Borley sources, he is primarily significant for his forensic dental examination of the human remains excavated from the Rectory cellars, and for his later vehement defense of Harry Price.

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific details, conclusions, quotes, and a chronological contradiction regarding his involvement:

**The 1943 Bone Examination**
After Harry Price and his team unearthed fragments of a skull and jawbone in the cellars of Borley Rectory on **August 17, 1943**, the remains were initially examined *in situ* by Dr. E. H. Bailey before being submitted to Dr. Godden for a specialized assessment [1]. 

Godden's expert report (which was subsequently published in Price's book *The End of Borley Rectory*) provided several specific forensic conclusions:
*   **The Skull:** Godden determined that the skull likely belonged to a "youngish person," but noted that the fragments were too small to definitively determine the sex of the owner [1]. 
*   **Age and Condition:** He estimated the age of the fragments to be **"anything over a hundred years,"** observing that the lime clay in which they were buried had caused the skull portion to become highly decayed, porous, soft, and brittle [1].
*   **The Jawbone and the "Abscess Theory":** He judged that the jawbone belonged to a youngish woman, approximately 30 years of age [1]. Crucially, Godden discovered that **the woman had suffered from a deep-seated dental abscess** which would have caused her considerable pain [2, 4]. 

Harry Price and other researchers heavily utilized Godden's "abscess" finding to support the Borley mythology. They pointed out that this painful affliction perfectly accounted for the fact that witnesses who claimed to have seen the phantom "nun" invariably described her as looking miserable, "pale-faced," "sad," with a "drawn face," or "as if she had been crying," and never looking happy or smiling [2, 4]. 

**Defense of Harry Price and a Date Contradiction**
In his later years, Godden was highly critical of the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) and their highly skeptical 1956 *Borley Report*, which had posthumously accused Harry Price of fraud. 

The sources record that Godden discussed his views at length with the investigator Peter Underwood during a Ghost Club meeting. However, **the texts contain a specific contradiction regarding the date of this meeting**, with one source placing it in **1958** [4] and another dating it to **February 1959** [2].

During this conversation, Godden offered a staunch defense of Price and severely criticized the SPR authors (particularly Trevor Hall). Godden argued that the *Borley Report* had been published primarily for the authors' own publicity, notoriety, and royalties [2]. He condemned the report with this specific, scathing quote, describing it as:
> **"a vicious, unprovoked and dastardly attack on a man no longer in a position to reply"** [2]. 

Godden further pointed out the inherent unfairness of the situation, noting to Underwood that **"it is always possible to attack and apparently discredit somebody or something when the chief defender cannot reply or respond"** [2].
