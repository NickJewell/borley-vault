# Fred Cartwright

A journeyman carpenter from Sudbury. One of the most significant "independent" witnesses in the Borley case — he reportedly had no prior knowledge of the haunting when he allegedly saw the Nun. Despite his prominence in the literature, he remains a "shadowy figure" whose very existence has been fiercely debated by psychical researchers [2, 3].

## The Sightings (Autumn 1927)

Cartwright's experiences occurred in the **autumn of 1927**, during the months when [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] stood completely empty following the death of [[harry-bull|Rev. Harry Bull]] and before the arrival of [[rev-eric-smith|the Smiths]] [2, 4]. At the time, he was lodging in Sudbury and engaged in repairing some farm buildings near the village of **Clare** — a peculiarly ironic detail, given that Clare was the actual historical location of a genuine Augustinian monastic order, whereas the "Borley Monastery" of the nun legends never actually existed [20, 21].

His daily commute required him to walk through Borley, passing the Rectory at **approximately 6:45 a.m.** just as it was getting light [6, 7]. He saw [[the-nun|a Sister of Mercy]] at the drive gate on **four separate occasions**:

1. **First (Tuesday):** She stood very still by the lower drive gate nearest Sudbury; he assumed she was resting after an errand of mercy and walked past [6].
2. **Second (Friday):** Same spot; looked perfectly normal but with eyes closed [7, 8].
3. **Third (Wednesday):** She looked **"pale, tired and ill"**, with eyes closed as if asleep; he turned back to offer assistance, but she had vanished [7, 9].
4. **Fourth (following Friday):** He resolved to say "Good morning"; before he reached the gate she disappeared completely; he pushed open the closed gate and walked the entire Rectory driveway — finding no one [5, 10].

That evening Cartwright recounted the experience to his landlord in Sudbury, which was when he first learned of the Rectory's haunted reputation and the legend of the ghostly nun [5, 11]. He passed the Rectory many times in the months that followed but never saw the figure again [1, 11].

## The Meeting with Harry Price (June 1930)

Having heard of the carpenter's experiences while collecting information in the district, [[harry-price|Harry Price]] tracked Cartwright down in **June 1930** and interviewed him **"over a pint of ale at the 'White Horse' pub in North Street, Sudbury"** [5, 13]. Price heavily utilised Cartwright's story in both his bestselling books on Borley as evidence of a "full-form daylight apparition" seen repeatedly by an unbiased witness [1, 5].

## Significance and Reliability

Cartwright is the strongest example in the literature of an independent witness who reportedly saw the Nun before knowing anything about the case. Critics tried to dismiss him as a pub-story spinner who had "had a few" when he told Price the story over a pint; the authors note he told his landlord and others the same evening, before any publicity existed.

- Accounts given to Price approximately three years after the events (single witness)
- No corroboration from other witnesses
- Price made some attempts to trace him later, without success

## Skepticism and the "Invented Witness" Theory

Cartwright's account became a major target for critics, notably [[eric-dingwall|Dr. Eric Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen Goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] in their highly skeptical 1956 *Borley Report* [14, 15]. The critics highlighted that there were no first-hand written accounts from Cartwright, no specific dates, and no corroboration [3, 16]. They dismissed the sightings as an **"imaginary or exaggerated incident"** and suggested Cartwright had embellished the story simply to encourage the eager Price to buy him more free pints [14, 17].

Some cynics went further, suggesting that Cartwright **never actually existed** and was an outright invention of Price's imagination [3, 13]. Decades later, veteran researcher [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] attempted for years to trace the journeyman carpenter or his descendants, without success [3].

> **Contradiction:** While almost all Borley texts firmly date Cartwright's four sightings to the autumn of 1927 [1, 2, 4, 18], a strange error exists in one of Price's own tabulated lists of phenomena. In *The End of Borley Rectory*, a bulleted timeline mistakenly records the event under the year **1889**: *"1889. Fred Cartwright four times saw the nun standing at the gate of Borley Rectory"* [19].

Despite the accusations that he was a fictional character, researchers later consulted the 1901 Census returns, which listed 119 men named Frederick Cartwright. One promising candidate was **Frederick C. Cartwright**, a carpenter born in East Denham, Norfolk — 23 years old in 1901 and living in Telscombe, Sussex, making him approximately 50 at the time of the 1927 incidents [3].
