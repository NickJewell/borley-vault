# Fred Cartwright

A journeyman carpenter from Sudbury. One of the most significant "independent" witnesses in the Borley case — he had no prior knowledge of the haunting when he saw the Nun.

## The Sightings (Autumn 1927)

Cartwright was working at farm buildings between Borley and Clare. Each morning he walked past [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] before dawn (c. 6:45 am) on his way to work. The Rectory was empty at this time — between [[harry-bull|Harry Bull's]] death and the [[rev-eric-smith|Smiths']] arrival.

He saw [[the-nun|a Sister of Mercy]] at the drive gate on **four separate occasions**:
1. First morning: standing very still by the gatepost; appeared perfectly normal
2. Following Friday: same spot; eyes closed; assumed she was resting after nursing the sick
3. Following Wednesday: pale, tired, ill-looking; eyes closed; turned back to offer help — she had vanished
4. Final occasion: glimpsed her and decided to speak; she vanished before he reached the gate; he searched the entire drive — found no one (the Rectory was empty)

He told his landlord and others that same evening. It was *only then* that he heard the stories of the Borley haunting for the first time.

## Significance

Cartwright is the strongest example in the book of an independent witness who saw the Nun before knowing anything about the case. Critics tried to dismiss him as a pub-story spinner who had "had a few" when he told Price the story "over a pint." The authors note: he told his landlord and others the same evening of the experiences, before any publicity existed.

## Reliability

- His accounts were given to Price approximately three years after the events
- No corroboration from other witnesses
- Price made some attempts to trace him later, without success
- Despite this, his pre-knowledge innocence is hard to explain away

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Fred Cartwright** was a journeyman carpenter who holds a prominent place in the Borley Rectory narrative as one of the most famous witnesses to the apparition of the "phantom nun" [1, 2]. Despite his fame in the literature, he remains a "shadowy figure" whose very existence has been fiercely debated by psychical researchers [2, 3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, contradictions, and biographical details regarding his involvement:

**The Autumn 1927 Sightings**
Cartwright's experiences occurred in the **autumn of 1927**, during the months when Borley Rectory stood completely empty following the death of the Rev. Harry Bull and before the arrival of the Rev. Guy Eric Smith [2, 4]. 

At the time, Cartwright was lodging in Sudbury and was engaged in repairing some farm buildings near the village of Clare [5, 6]. His daily commute required him to walk through Borley, passing the Rectory at **approximately 6:45 a.m.** just as it was getting light [6, 7]. Ignorant of the local ghost legends, he experienced four distinct sightings of what he assumed was a living woman [1, 7]:
*   **Sighting 1 (Tuesday):** Cartwright saw a woman dressed in the habit of a "Sister of Mercy" standing perfectly still by the lower drive gate nearest Sudbury. Assuming she was resting after an errand of mercy or waiting for a vehicle, he walked past without speaking [6].
*   **Sighting 2 (Friday):** He saw her again at the identical spot. She looked perfectly normal, but he noticed her eyes were closed [7, 8]. 
*   **Sighting 3 (Wednesday):** Passing her a third time, he looked at her intently and noted she looked **"pale, tired and ill"**, with her eyes closed as if asleep [7, 9]. Sensing something might be wrong, he turned back to offer assistance, but the woman had vanished [7, 9]. 
*   **Sighting 4 (Following Friday):** On his final sighting, Cartwright resolved to say "Good morning" to her [10]. However, before he reached the gate, she completely disappeared [7, 10]. Thoroughly bemused, he pushed open the closed gate and walked the entire length of the Rectory driveway to search for her, but found no one [5, 10]. 

That evening, Cartwright recounted the strange experience to his landlord in Sudbury, which was when he first learned of the Rectory's haunted reputation and the legend of the ghostly nun [5, 11]. He passed the Rectory many times in the months that followed but never saw the figure again [1, 11]. 

**The Meeting with Harry Price (June 1930)**
Having heard of the carpenter's experiences while collecting information in the district, psychical researcher Harry Price tracked Cartwright down in **June 1930** [5, 12]. Price interviewed him **"over a pint of ale at the ‘White Horse’ pub in North Street, Sudbury"** [5, 13]. Price heavily utilized Cartwright's story in his two bestselling books on Borley, as it provided compelling evidence of a "full-form daylight apparition" seen repeatedly by an unbiased witness [1, 5]. 

**Skepticism and the "Invented Witness" Theory**
Cartwright’s account became a major target for critics, notably Dr. Eric Dingwall, Kathleen Goldney, and Trevor Hall in their highly skeptical 1956 *Borley Report* [14, 15]. The critics highlighted that there were no first-hand written accounts from Cartwright, no specific dates, and no corroboration [3, 16]. They dismissed the sightings as an **"imaginary or exaggerated incident"** and suggested Cartwright had embellished the story simply to encourage the eager Harry Price to buy him more free pints of beer [14, 17]. 

Some cynics went even further, suggesting that Fred Cartwright **never actually existed** and was an outright invention of Harry Price's fertile imagination [3, 13]. Decades later, veteran researcher Peter Underwood attempted for years to track down the journeyman carpenter or his descendants, but was totally unsuccessful [3]. 

**Lesser-Known Biographical Details and Contradictions**
*   **Census Corroboration:** Despite the accusations that he was a fictional character, researchers later consulted the 1901 Census returns, which listed 119 men named Frederick Cartwright. One promising candidate was **Frederick C. Cartwright**, a carpenter born in East Denham, Norfolk. In 1901 he was 23 years old and living in Telscombe, Sussex; this man would have been 50 years old at the time of the 1927 Borley incidents, perfectly fitting the profile of the journeyman [3]. 
*   **A Chronological Contradiction:** While almost all Borley texts and chronologies firmly date Cartwright's four sightings to the autumn of 1927 [1, 2, 4, 18], a strange contradiction exists in one of Harry Price's own tabulated lists of phenomena. In *The End of Borley Rectory*, a bulleted timeline mistakenly records the event under the year **1889**: *"1889. Fred Cartwright four times saw the nun standing at the gate of Borley Rectory"* [19]. 
*   **The Clare Coincidence:** Historians have noted a peculiar coincidence regarding Cartwright's destination. He was traveling to repair farm buildings near the village of Clare, which was the actual historical location of a genuine Augustinian monastic order—an ironic detail given that the "Borley Monastery" of the nun legends never actually existed [20, 21].
