# Miss Jane Conolly

One of [[gerard-kelman|Gerard Kelman]]'s companions on a July 1966 overnight visit to Borley. Her surname is also recorded as **Connally** in some chronological entries [1, 2]. In the early hours of Sunday 24 July, at approximately 5 a.m., she and her companion [[miss-margaret-conolly|Miss Margaret Anne Conolly]] independently described a grey figure near the Rectory site that was also seen by Kelman.

## The Visit of July 23–24, 1966

Miss Conolly paid an overnight visit to the ruined site of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] over the weekend of **23–24 July 1966**, accompanied by ghost-hunter Gerald Kelman from Buckhurst Hill, Essex, and another woman named [[miss-margaret-conolly|Miss Margaret Anne Conolly]] [1, 2].

The group stood outside the Rectory drive gate for a considerable amount of time without incident. Eventually they all **"heard the crunch of footsteps on gravel but they could see no one"** [3].

## The Apparition — 5 a.m., 24 July

In the half-light just before daybreak, Jane Conolly looked across the deserted Rectory site and saw a **"grey-blue shape"** standing by the door of a shed — "vaguely human and covered from head to toe with what appeared to be a flowing garment," leading the observers to classify it as a nun-like figure [1, 2]. She wrote in her own account:

> "As I looked across the Rectory site, I thought I could make out a grey figure standing by the door of the shed. Suddenly the figure seemed to move, but it did not advance in a normal way. I could make out a small head on broad shoulders, but from the waist downwards, the figure was blurred... Then it moved about a yard or so to the left and eventually disappeared near the large tree at the edge of the former site of Borley Rectory. I was not afraid in any way, only delighted to have seen this 'apparition.'" [3]

Her account is complementary to but independent of Kelman's, and both differ in detail from that of [[miss-margaret-conolly|Miss Margaret Anne Conolly]], who could see only the lower half of the figure from her vantage point. The three partial but consistent observations are treated by Underwood and Tabori as mutual corroboration of an actual three-dimensional form: one witness described a "vaguely human shape," another (Jane) saw the figure from the broad shoulders up, and the third could see only the lower portion.

Notably, whereas most witnesses at Borley reported intense fear when encountering phenomena, Miss Conolly documented a highly positive reaction — she was "not afraid in any way, only delighted."
