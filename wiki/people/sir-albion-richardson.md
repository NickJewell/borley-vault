# Sir Albion Richardson

K.C., C.B.E. Recorder of Nottingham; M.P. for Peckham 1910–1922; a very prominent member of the Society of Gray's Inn, the Inns of Court, the Midland Circuit, and the Grand Committee of the House of Commons; Chairman of many Government judicial committees; member of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s Ghost Club (member Number 414) [3]. His entire career was, according to Price, "devoted to determining what is and what is not evidence" [1, 2].

## Legal Opinion on Borley

After reading Price's *The Most Haunted House in England*, Richardson sent Price a letter on **May 22, 1945**, offering a glowing assessment which Price enthusiastically published in *The End of Borley Rectory*, Chapter 22 [2, 6]:

> "Borley Rectory stands by itself in the literature of psychical manifestation. Wisely discarding theories of causation (which in these matters are little better than conjecture), the author, Mr Harry Price, sets out to prove by the cumulative evidence of eyewitnesses — recorded in a form which would be admissible in evidence in any court of law — the happening of events at Borley Rectory which it is impossible to explain by the operation of natural law."

> "The evidence which he has collected of the phenomena which appeared there is as conclusive as human testimony can ever be, and is admirably marshalled. I have not met anyone who has read *The Most Haunted House in England* (and it is mainly with legal friends of long experience in the weighing and sifting of evidence that I have discussed it) who has not been satisfied that the manifestations therein disclosed are proved by the evidence, to the point of moral certainty. Many of these friends were, like myself, previously sceptical."

Along with [[sir-ernest-jelf|Sir Ernest Jelf]]'s *Law Times* article, Richardson's assessment represents the first time in the annals of psychical research that distinguished jurists publicly expressed opinions as to the validity of phenomena witnessed in a haunted house. Price calls it "legal, as well as psychic, history."

## A Cryptic Later Comment

Despite his glowing written endorsement, Richardson later reportedly seemed reluctant to discuss the case openly. When a medical doctor (a friend of investigator [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]]) tried to get him to talk about the Borley haunting and Harry Price, Sir Albion allegedly refused to elaborate, offering only the cryptic remark: **"An extraordinary man, Price – a most extraordinary man"** [3].

## Skeptical Backlash

Richardson's legal endorsement was heavily utilised by Price and his publishers to legitimise the Borley haunting, making it a prime target for sceptical critics. [[trevor-hall|Trevor H. Hall]] actively disparaged Richardson's conclusions, implying the jurist had failed to make any real critical attempt to examine the evidence on which he was relying [10]. The SPR's *Borley Report* authors — [[eric-dingwall|Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Goldney]], and Hall — argued that lawyers like Richardson had foolishly treated witnesses' subjective accounts as hard "facts" and had simply fallen victim to the **"barrage of suggestion put up by Price"** [11]. Responding directly to Richardson's published statement, the SPR authors sarcastically agreed the case was indeed fascinating, but added that **"its fascination is hardly the kind to which Sir Albion was alluding"** [12] — implying it was fascinating as a study of fraud rather than genuine ghosts.
