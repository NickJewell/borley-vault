# Sir Albion Richardson

K.C., C.B.E. Recorder of Nottingham; M.P. for Peckham 1910–1922; Chairman of many Government judicial committees. Entire career devoted to determining what is and is not evidence.

## Legal Opinion on Borley

Richardson studied the records published in [[harry-price|Price]]'s *The Most Haunted House in England* and provided the following assessment (reproduced in *The End of Borley Rectory*, Chapter 22):

> "Borley Rectory stands by itself in the literature of psychical manifestation. Wisely discarding theories of causation (which in these matters are little better than conjecture), the author, Mr Harry Price, sets out to prove by the cumulative evidence of eyewitnesses — recorded in a form which would be admissible in evidence in any court of law — the happening of events at Borley Rectory which it is impossible to explain by the operation of natural law."

> "The evidence which he has collected of the phenomena which appeared there is as conclusive as human testimony can ever be, and is admirably marshalled. I have not met anyone who has read *The Most Haunted House in England* (and it is mainly with legal friends of long experience in the weighing and sifting of evidence that I have discussed it) who has not been satisfied that the manifestations therein disclosed are proved by the evidence, to the point of moral certainty. Many of these friends were, like myself, previously sceptical."

Along with [[sir-ernest-jelf|Sir Ernest Jelf]]'s *Law Times* article, Richardson's assessment represents the first time in the annals of psychical research that distinguished jurists publicly expressed opinions as to the validity of phenomena witnessed in a haunted house. Price calls it "legal, as well as psychic, history."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Sir Albion Richardson, K.C., C.B.E.**, was an eminent British jurist who provided one of the most famous and highly publicized legal endorsements of Harry Price’s investigation into Borley Rectory. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, quotes, and subsequent controversies regarding his involvement:

**Biographical Details and Professional Status**
*   **Legal and Political Career:** Richardson was a highly distinguished legal mind whose entire career, according to Harry Price, **"has been devoted to determining what is and what is not evidence"** [1, 2]. He was a very prominent member of the Society of Gray’s Inn, the Inns of Court, the Midland Circuit, and the Grand Committee of the House of Commons [3]. He also served as the Recorder of Nottingham, and was the Member of Parliament (MP) for Peckham from 1910 to 1922 [1, 2]. 
*   **Connection to Price:** He was a member of Harry Price’s Ghost Club (specifically, member Number 414) [3]. While he was not mentioned in Price's first Borley book, *The Most Haunted House in England* (*MHH*), Price awarded him "special thanks" and described him as a "brilliant thinker" in his 1946 sequel, *The End of Borley Rectory* (*EBR*) [1, 4, 5]. 

**His Assessment of the Borley Evidence (May 1945)**
After reading *MHH*, Richardson sent Harry Price a letter on **May 22, 1945**, offering a glowing appreciation of the case, which Price enthusiastically published in *EBR* [2, 6]. 
*   **On the quality of the evidence:** Richardson concluded that the paranormal manifestations at Borley were proved **"to the point of moral certainty"** [1, 6, 7]. He famously wrote: **"The evidence which he has collected of the phenomena which appeared there is as conclusive as human testimony can ever be, and is admirably marshalled"** [1, 6, 8]. 
*   **On legal admissibility:** He explicitly stated that Price had succeeded in proving the paranormal events with cumulative eyewitness evidence **"recorded in a form which would be admissible in evidence in any court of law"** [1, 9]. 
*   **On the consensus of his peers:** Richardson noted that he had discussed the book extensively with **"legal friends of long experience in the weighing and sifting of evidence"** and claimed that every single one of them (many of whom, like himself, had previously been skeptical) was satisfied that the manifestations were proven [1, 6]. 
*   **Praise for Harry Price:** He expressed a debt of gratitude to Price, writing that without his **"untiring energy and skilled experience as an investigator... a fascinating chapter in the history of psychical research would have been lost to the world"** [9]. 

**A Cryptic Later Comment**
Despite his glowing written endorsement, Richardson later seemed reluctant to discuss the case openly. When a medical doctor (a friend of investigator Peter Underwood) tried to get him to talk about the Borley haunting and Harry Price, Sir Albion refused to elaborate, offering only the cryptic remark: **"An extraordinary man, Price – a most extraordinary man"** [3]. 

**Skeptical Backlash and Contradictions**
Richardson’s legal endorsement was heavily utilized by Price and his publishers to legitimize the Borley haunting, making it a prime target for skeptical critics. 
*   **Trevor Hall's Criticism:** The skeptical investigator Trevor H. Hall actively disparaged Richardson's conclusions, implying that the jurist had failed to make any real, critical attempt to examine the actual evidence on which he was relying [10].
*   **The SPR's Dismissal:** In the highly critical 1956 *Borley Report*, authors Eric Dingwall, Kathleen Goldney, and Trevor Hall argued that lawyers like Richardson had foolishly treated the witnesses' subjective and distorted accounts as hard "facts" [11]. The SPR authors argued that the jurists possessed little appreciation of the psychological complexities of the material they were judging, and had simply fallen victim to the **"barrage of suggestion put up by Price"** [11]. 
*   **A Sarcastic Rebuttal:** Responding directly to Richardson's published statement that Borley was a "fascinating chapter in the history of psychical research," the SPR authors sarcastically agreed that it was indeed fascinating, but added: **"its fascination is hardly the kind to which Sir Albion was alluding"** (implying the case was a fascinating study of fraud and deception, rather than genuine ghosts) [12].
