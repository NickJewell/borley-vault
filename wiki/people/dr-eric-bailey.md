# Dr. Eric H. Bailey, M.R.C.S., L.R.C.P.

Senior Assistant Pathologist at the County Hospital, Ashford, Middlesex. Bailey was present at the August 17, 1943 excavation of Borley Rectory's cellars and provided the immediate expert identification of the [[bones-excavation|human remains]] found there.

## Identification of the Bones

When Jackson the labourer handed up the jaw-bone at approximately 1:45 p.m. on August 17, 1943, Jackson identified it as belonging to a pig. [[harry-price|Price]] did not know which animal it came from. Dr. Bailey immediately identified it as human.

Five minutes later, when the skull fragment was recovered, Bailey confirmed it too was human. That same evening he returned to Sudbury and **wrote to the Rev. Henning confirming the details in writing** — creating an independent contemporaneous record.

His field opinion: "The jaw-bone is the left mandible, with five teeth in good condition, and probably belonged to a woman. The other bone is from the left side of a human skull and is the parietal and temporal bone."

## Later Expert Consultation

The bones were also subsequently examined by [[leslie-godden|Mr. Leslie J. Godden, L.D.S., R.C.S.]] (dental surgeon) and by the Rev. Father M. J. Moriarty. A detailed dental report (from an unnamed West End dental surgeon) was submitted to Price on May 23, 1945, X-raying the jaw-bone and concluding:

- Female, approximately thirty years old (more probably under than over)
- Severe deep-seated abscess in the jaw, associated with the sixth tooth — a condition that would cause chronic pain ranging from persistent ache to severe neuralgia
- Diet containing "a good amount of roughage and probably some finely divided grit" — consistent with conventual fare of coarse stone-ground bread

The "pale and haggard appearance" described by all nun-apparition witnesses corresponds, Price argues, to the chronic dental pain evidenced by this abscess.

## Significance

Bailey's rapid and confident identification prevented the bones from being dismissed as animal remains and being discarded — the fate of whatever Jackson might have done with "pig bones." His presence at the excavation, and his written confirmation that evening, anchored the discovery with the credibility of a trained forensic pathologist.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Dr. Eric H. Bailey, M.R.C.S., L.R.C.P.**, was the Senior Assistant Pathologist at the County Hospital (also referred to as Staines County Hospital) in Ashford, Middlesex [1-3]. In the Borley sources, he is specifically documented for his role as the medical expert present during Harry Price's wartime excavations of the ruined Rectory's cellars.

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following facts and events regarding his involvement:

**The 1943 Excavations**
Price deliberately invited Dr. Bailey to Borley to assist with the identification of any human remains that might be uncovered during the digging [1]. Dr. Bailey attended the excavations on **August 17 and 18, 1943**, and was accompanied to the site by his brother, Roland F. Bailey, a barrister [1, 3, 4]. 

**The Discovery and Identification (August 17, 1943)**
On the afternoon of the first day of digging, human remains were brought to the surface from the blue clay in the cellar passage [5, 6]. At 1:45 p.m., a local laborer named Jackson unearthed a jawbone and handed it up, suggesting it came from a pig [7]. Price's secretary, Mrs. Ethel English, initially recorded the discovery in her notes as "Jawbone (pig?) found" [7]. However, the sources record that **"immediately Dr Bailey saw it he at once said it was human"**, definitively identifying it on sight [6, 7]. 

Five minutes later, at 1:50 p.m., part of a skull was also dug up [7]. Examining the fragments *in situ* [8], Dr. Bailey provided the following expert opinion on the remains:
> **"The jaw-bone is the left mandible, with five teeth in good condition, and probably belonged to a woman. The other bone is from the left side of a human skull and is the parietal and temporal bone"** [9]. 

Because the skull fragment was found only a few inches from the jawbone, Dr. Bailey's assessment made it highly likely they belonged to the same skeleton [5]. 

**Written Confirmation**
Upon returning to Sudbury on the evening of August 17, 1943, Dr. Bailey wrote a letter to the local clergyman, the Rev. Alfred C. Henning, to officially confirm these medical details in writing [9]. Price subsequently had the remains examined by a dental surgeon, Dr. Leslie J. Godden, but publicly expressed his gratitude to Dr. Bailey in his 1946 book *The End of Borley Rectory* for his expert, on-site examination [2, 8].
