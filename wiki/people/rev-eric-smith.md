# Reverend G. Eric Smith (1885 – 3 August 1940)

Rector of Borley October 1928 – July 1929 (vacating the building, remaining technically incumbent until April 1930). His nine-month residency was the pivot that brought [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and national press attention to the case.

## Background

Born in Calcutta, India, in February 1885, son of Eldred Melville Smith and Ellen Mary Smith [3]. A man of Eurasian extraction — large, kind, bespectacled, and cultured by contemporary description [4–6]. He completed his education at the University of Calcutta in 1907 and was subsequently employed as a clerk in the Indian Civil Service (ICS) [3, 4, 7]. Anglo-Indians were excluded from executive ICS roles and experienced pervasive racism in both India and, on return, in Britain.

On November 1, 1924, he married Sarah Helen Mabel Hart (eldest daughter of a railway clerk from Isleworth; mother and grandmother born in India) in Calcutta [3]. Due to his wife's failing health, the couple left India for England, where Smith decided to take Holy Orders [4, 6, 8]. He studied at Chichester Theological College, was ordained in 1926, and served a curacy at Great Clacton with Little Holland until 1928 [4, 6, 8].

## Arrival at Borley Rectory

Borley was Smith's first living — the post had been offered to **twelve other clergy**, all of whom refused it; Eric Smith was literally the thirteenth candidate [9–11]. The Bishop of Chelmsford ([[dr-guy-warman|Guy Warman]]) did not tell Smith either this fact or that the Rectory had stood empty since Harry Bull's death due to its sinister reputation. (hbrs-ch02)

The Smiths arrived on October 2, 1928, to find the massive twenty-three-room house dilapidated, exceptionally cold, and completely lacking in amenities such as gas, electricity, or an indoor piped water supply [10, 12, 13]. A childless couple, they spent £200 of their own money on refurbishment but confined themselves to living in just a few rooms [6, 8, 14].

### Race and Reception

In 1920s Essex, a non-white parson was "not just unusual, but alien to the vast majority of the villagers." Race riots had occurred in British cities 1919–20. The village had long resented the disruption to their access to the Rectory. Smith also practised Anglo-Catholicism — already resented in the Low Church Bull tradition. The locals' hostility to Smith was thus compounded: race, religion, and the disruption to their informal use of the empty Rectory. (hbrs-ch02)

## Paranormal Experiences During His Incumbency

Almost immediately, the Smiths experienced phenomena they could not logically explain. Price compiled the following from direct interviews with the Smiths:

- **Sibilant whisperings** heard by Smith on the landing, following him, ceasing abruptly at a second arch "as though a wireless set had been switched off" [18–20]
- **A woman's voice** from the arch — *"Don't, Carlos, don't!"* — rising crescendo, dying to a mutter. "Carlos" reputedly Henry Bull's nickname; denied by the Bull family [19, 21, 22]
- **The Hockey Stick Incident:** Smith repeatedly heard slow, dragging footsteps traversing the bare boards of unoccupied rooms [15–17]. Armed with a hockey stick, he hid in wait; when the slippered footsteps approached, he lashed out — "the stick whistled through the empty air, and the steps continued across the room" (witnessed by journalist V. C. Wall) [15–17]
- **Footsteps** patrolling passages and upper rooms; more frequent in winter than summer
- **Keys shooting from locks** — found two or three feet away; happened periodically; Price personally witnessed this
- **Schoolroom window** lit up twice; maid not present; dark on investigation; second occurrence witnessed by the choir after evening practice [26–29]

Smith frequently concealed terrifying incidents from his wife to protect her, indicating a genuine fear of the phenomena [5, 41].

## Role in the Borley Story

Because local parishioners were too fearful to attend meetings at the Rectory, the Smiths contacted the *Daily Mirror* in June 1929 [23, 24]. They hoped to get the address of a psychical research society that could provide an authoritative verdict to debunk the haunting and reassure the locals [23, 24]. Instead, the newspaper sent reporter V. C. Wall, who published sensational articles that made the Rectory nationally famous [24]. The newspaper editor also contacted [[harry-price|Harry Price]], who arrived at the Rectory on June 12, 1929 [23, 25, 26] — the first press report having appeared on June 10, 1929.

Price's arrival coincided with a sudden, violent outbreak of [[poltergeist-activity|poltergeist phenomena]], including stones, mothballs, and keys being thrown [27, 28]. On the night of June 12–13, Price organised a séance in the [[blue-room]]. Raps on a mirror purported to be from the spirit of the late Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]], who communicated that he had been murdered over money troubles [29–31]. Reverend Smith was horrified that libellous family issues were being openly discussed in front of a journalist — **he demanded the séance be stopped at once and refused to allow any further sittings in the house** [29, 32].

Overwhelmed by the complete lack of amenities and the invasion of rowdy, trespassing sightseers brought by the press coverage, the Smiths vacated the Rectory on July 14, 1929 [1, 33, 34]. They moved to lodgings in nearby [[long-melford|Long Melford]] and ran the parish from there until April 1930.

Price's own description of the Smiths: "very intelligent, delightful, and much-travelled people who were, like myself, utterly sceptical as regards 'spirits.' They knew nothing about psychical research, and the subject did not particularly interest them." What drove the Smiths from the Rectory, in Price's words: "the lack of amenities there and not the 'ghosts.'"

## Later Career and Death

Smith later accepted a curacy at Sheringham, Norfolk, then became Rector of Sevington in Kent [35–37]. He died at Sheringham on August 3, 1940 [4, 35, 38].

## The Smith Contradiction

A major controversy in the Borley literature centres on a letter [[mabel-smith|Mrs Mabel Smith]] wrote to the *Church Times* in October 1945, five years after her husband's death [39, 40]. She stated: **"I would like to state definitely that neither my husband nor myself believed the house haunted by anything else but rats and local superstition"** [39, 40].

However, Smith's own contemporary writings flatly contradict this claim:

- In a letter to Lord Charles Hope on November 26, 1929, Smith explicitly confirmed his belief: **"You and the others know the place is haunted, and I am sure you will agree it was no place for my wife to reside in, seeing that no maid would stay there"** [42–44]
- In March 1930, writing to [[harry-price|Harry Price]] about a visit to the empty Rectory, Smith stated, **"we heard the most horrible sounds in the house,"** adding that he no longer allowed his wife to go inside [42, 45]
- A 1937 letter from Smith to [[sidney-glanville|Glanville]] said it "would indeed be a great pleasure to renew the fellowship of sight and hand with Mr Price" — which sits awkwardly with [[mabel-smith|Mabel's]] 1945 claim that Price was "very unwelcome" [46, 47]
- Smith wrote to Price in January 1932 still discussing the phenomena normally
- Close acquaintances confirmed to investigators that Eric Smith believed completely in the paranormal phenomena at Borley, with one associate noting his absolute conviction regarding the sibilant whisperings and the mysterious trajectory of thrown vases [48]

He always maintained that Borley "had an evil atmosphere and was haunted," and letters confirming this belief survive.

> **Contradiction:** Mabel Smith's 1945–49 testimony that neither she nor her husband believed the Rectory haunted is directly contradicted by Eric Smith's own letters of 1929, 1930, and 1932, which express genuine belief. The SPR authors argue the reversal reflects later psychological settlement; Hastings and others argue [[mabel-smith|Mabel Smith's]] testimony is therefore unreliable.

## O'Connor's Analysis (hbrs)

O'Connor argues Smith deliberately exaggerated and possibly fabricated phenomena in order to depreciate the Rectory's market value so that a friend could purchase it cheaply. William Bigg (churchwarden) told Henry Lawton (locum 1933) that he believed this was the case. The Smiths needed an official investigation as cover — hence the letter to the *Daily Mirror*. This would also explain [[mabel-smith|Mabel's]] later reversal: once Eric died, her conscience overrode the original strategy. O'Connor also notes the Church had already secretly decided to amalgamate Borley with [[liston-rectory|Liston]] parish and sell the Rectory — a resolution the Smiths were never told about. (hbrs-ch02, hbrs-ch18)
