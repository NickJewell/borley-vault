# Reverend G. Eric Smith (d. 3 August 1940)

Rector of Borley October 1928 – July 1929. His nine-month incumbency was the pivot that brought [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and national press attention to the case.

## Background

Born and educated in Calcutta; son of a Freemason. Anglo-Indian (mixed race). Graduated from the University of Calcutta 1907 before joining the Indian Civil Service as a clerk. Anglo-Indians were excluded from executive ICS roles and experienced pervasive racism in both India and, on return, in Britain. Married Mabel Hart (eldest daughter of a railway clerk from Isleworth; mother and grandmother born in India). Ordained 1926.

Borley was his first living — the post had been offered to **twelve other clergy**, all of whom refused it. Eric Smith was literally the thirteenth candidate. The Bishop of Chelmsford (Guy Warman) did not tell Smith either this or that the Rectory had stood empty since Harry Bull's death due to its sinister reputation. (hbrs-ch02)

### Race and Reception

In 1920s Essex, a non-white parson was "not just unusual, but alien to the vast majority of the villagers." Race riots had occurred in British cities 1919–20. The village had long resented the disruption to their access to the Rectory. Smith also practised Anglo-Catholicism — already resented in the Low Church Bull tradition. The locals' hostility to Smith was thus compounded: race, religion, and the disruption to their informal use of the empty Rectory. (hbrs-ch02)

## Role in the Borley Story

Smith wrote to a national newspaper for help "quelling the ghosts." The *Daily Mirror* sent reporter V. C. Wall and contacted Price simultaneously. Price's visit (12 June 1929) marked the beginning of the formal investigation. The first press report on Borley was published 10 June 1929.

## His Character and Testimony

Price's own description (from *The Most Haunted House in England*): "very intelligent, delightful, and much-travelled people who were, like myself, utterly sceptical as regards 'spirits.' They knew nothing about psychical research, and the subject did not particularly interest them." Genuinely puzzled rather than a believer. He always maintained that Borley "had an evil atmosphere and was haunted," and letters confirming this belief survive. After leaving, he spent a period at Sheringham, Norfolk, then accepted a curacy in Kent. Died 3 August 1940.

What drove the Smiths from the Rectory: in Price's words, "the lack of amenities there and not the 'ghosts.'"

## Phenomena During His Incumbency (Price's Account)

Price compiled these from direct interviews with the Smiths:

- **Sibilant whisperings** heard by Smith on the landing, following him, ceasing abruptly at a second arch "as though a wireless set had been switched off"
- **A woman's voice** from the arch: *"Don't, Carlos, don't!"* — rising crescendo, dying to a mutter. "Carlos" reputedly Henry Bull's nickname; denied by the Bull family
- **Footsteps** patrolling passages and upper rooms; more frequent in winter than summer
- **Keys shooting from locks** — found two or three feet away; happened periodically; Price personally witnessed this
- **Schoolroom window** lit up twice; maid not present; dark on investigation; second occurrence witnessed by the choir after evening practice
- Smith himself sat in a haunted room armed with a hockey stick, heard footsteps, swung at the source — "the stick whistled through the empty air, and the steps continued across the room" (witnessed by journalist [[vc-wall|V. C. Wall]])

## The Smith Contradiction

His wife [[mabel-smith|Mabel Smith]] became the problem: her 1929 testimony (frightened; reporting genuine phenomena) versus her 1945–49 testimony (denying everything; blaming rats and Price). Price noted that Smith himself wrote in January 1932 still discussing the phenomena normally. A 1937 letter from Smith to Glanville said it "would indeed be a great pleasure to renew the fellowship of sight and hand with Mr Price." This sits awkwardly with Mabel's 1945 claim that Price was "very unwelcome."

## O'Connor's Analysis (hbrs)

O'Connor argues Smith deliberately exaggerated and possibly fabricated phenomena in order to depreciate the Rectory's market value so that a friend could purchase it cheaply. William Bigg (churchwarden) told Henry Lawton (locum 1933) that he believed this was the case. The Smiths needed an official investigation as cover — hence the letter to the *Daily Mirror*. This would also explain Mabel's later reversal: once Eric died, her conscience overrode the original strategy. O'Connor also notes the Church had already secretly decided to amalgamate Borley with Liston parish and sell the Rectory — a resolution the Smiths were never told about. (hbrs-ch02, hbrs-ch18)

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Reverend Guy Eric Smith** (1885–1940) was the Rector of Borley from October 1928 to April 1930. His brief, dramatic incumbency marks a turning point in the history of Borley Rectory, bridging the gap between local folklore and international notoriety, as he was the clergyman who first brought the haunting to the attention of the national press and psychical researcher Harry Price [1, 2].

**Early Life and Career**
*   **Background:** Born in Calcutta, India, in February 1885, he was the son of Eldred Melville Smith and Ellen Mary Smith [3]. A man of Eurasian extraction, he was described as a large, kind, bespectacled, and cultured individual [4-6].
*   **Education and the ICS:** He completed his education at the University of Calcutta in 1907 and was subsequently employed as a clerk in the Indian Civil Service (ICS) [3, 4, 7]. 
*   **Marriage and Return to England:** On November 1, 1924, he married Sarah Helen Mabel Hart in Calcutta [3]. Due to his wife's failing health, the couple left India for England, where Smith decided to take Holy Orders [4, 6, 8]. 
*   **Ordination:** He studied at Chichester Theological College, was ordained in 1926, and served a curacy at Great Clacton with Little Holland until 1928 [4, 6, 8].

**Arrival at Borley Rectory (October 1928)**
Smith accepted the living at Borley after twelve other clergymen had refused it [9-11]. The Smiths were not warned that the Rectory was reputed to be haunted [9, 10]. They arrived on October 2, 1928, to find the massive twenty-three-room house dilapidated, exceptionally cold, and completely lacking in amenities such as gas, electricity, or an indoor piped water supply [10, 12, 13]. A childless couple, they spent £200 of their own money on refurbishment but confined themselves to living in just a few rooms [6, 8, 14]. 

**Paranormal Experiences**
Almost immediately, the Smiths experienced phenomena they could not logically explain:
*   **The Hockey Stick Incident:** Reverend Smith repeatedly heard the sound of slow, dragging footsteps traversing the bare boards of unoccupied rooms [15-17]. Armed with a hockey stick, he hid in wait for the sounds; when the slippered footsteps approached, he lashed out, but his stick merely whistled through empty air while the footsteps continued [15-17].
*   **The Whispering Voices:** While walking across the upstairs landing, Smith heard sibilant whisperings over his head that would inexplicably cease whenever he passed under an archway leading to the chapel [18-20]. 
*   **"Don't, Carlos, don't!":** At dusk one evening, Smith was startled to hear a woman's voice near the same archway. It began with a moaning sound that rose to a crescendo, ending with the specific words, **"Don't, Carlos, don't!"** before dying away into a muttering [19, 21, 22]. 

**Involving the Press and Harry Price**
Because local parishioners were too fearful to attend meetings at the Rectory, the Smiths naively decided to contact the *Daily Mirror* in June 1929 [23, 24]. They hoped to get the address of a psychical research society that could provide an authoritative verdict to debunk the haunting and reassure the locals [23, 24]. Instead, the newspaper sent reporter V.C. Wall, who published sensational articles that made the Rectory nationally famous [24]. The newspaper editor also contacted Harry Price, who arrived at the Rectory on June 12, 1929 [23, 25, 26].

**The Blue Room Seance and Departure**
Price's arrival coincided with a sudden, violent outbreak of poltergeist phenomena, including stones, mothballs, and keys being thrown [27, 28]. 
*   On the night of June 12–13, Price organized a seance in the Blue Room. Raps on a mirror purported to be from the spirit of the late Rev. Harry Bull, who communicated that he had been murdered over money troubles [29-31]. 
*   Reverend Smith was horrified that libelous family issues were being openly discussed in front of a journalist [31]. **He demanded the seance be stopped at once and refused to allow any further sittings in the house** [29, 32].
*   Overwhelmed by the complete lack of amenities and the invasion of rowdy, trespassing sightseers brought by the press coverage, the Smiths vacated the Rectory on July 14, 1929 [1, 33, 34]. They moved to lodgings in nearby Long Melford and ran the parish from there until April 1930 [1, 33, 34]. 
*   Smith later accepted a curacy at Sheringham in Norfolk, and subsequently became Rector of Sevington in Kent [35-37]. He died at Sheringham on August 3, 1940 [4, 35, 38].

**Contradictions Regarding His Beliefs**
A major controversy in the Borley literature centers on a letter Mrs. Mabel Smith wrote to the *Church Times* in October 1945, five years after her husband's death [39, 40]. She stated: **"I would like to state definitely that neither my husband nor myself believed the house haunted by anything else but rats and local superstition"** [39, 40]. 

However, the Borley sources reveal that Mrs. Smith's claim flatly contradicts her husband’s contemporary writings and lifelong attitude:
*   Eric Smith frequently concealed terrifying incidents from his wife to protect her, indicating a genuine fear of the phenomena [5, 41]. 
*   In a letter to Lord Charles Hope on November 26, 1929, Smith explicitly confirmed his belief, writing: **"You and the others know the place is haunted, and I am sure you will agree it was no place for my wife to reside in, seeing that no maid would stay there"** [42-44].
*   In March 1930, writing to Harry Price about a visit to the empty Rectory, Smith stated, **"we heard the most horrible sounds in the house,"** adding that he no longer allowed his wife to go inside [42, 45]. 
*   He consistently maintained to researchers like Sidney Glanville that Borley Rectory possessed an "evil atmosphere" and firmly stated he would refuse to ever live there again [46, 47]. 
*   Close acquaintances later confirmed to investigators that Eric Smith believed completely in the paranormal phenomena at Borley, with one associate noting his absolute conviction regarding the sibilant whisperings and the mysterious trajectory of thrown vases [48].
