# Tony Broughall

Former Ghost Club Society area representative. A long-standing, enthusiastic, and meticulous investigator of spontaneous and induced psychic phenomena who spent many years visiting Borley and examining the evidence. He was married to Georgina Broughall and maintained a close professional friendship with [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] for many years [2–4].

## Discovery of Borley

In **1948**, Broughall first heard of the Borley haunting when a Spiritualist friend showed him a borrowed library copy of [[mhh|The Most Haunted House in England]] [1]. He did not visit Borley until **February 1974** — a gap of over 25 years during which he developed his investigative skills at other locations.

His **first visit** was on **13 February 1974**, when he and his wife Georgina visited the site and took photographs of the Rectory ruins, including the old well mechanism [4]. In December 1974, he sent a piece of Borley Rectory brickwork to fellow researcher Charles Doerrer in Rochester, New York; in return, Doerrer sent Broughall a detailed theory proposing that [[the-nun|the phantom nun]] was Catherine Waldegrave [5].

Beyond Borley, Broughall investigated numerous haunted locations, including Chicksands Priory, where his wife and a party of friends reportedly heard ghostly footsteps while alone inside the locked building [2]. He compiled an unpublished manuscript titled *The Ghosts of Bedfordshire and Hertfordshire* (1976), which he presented to [[peter-underwood|Underwood]] [2].

## Borley Investigations

Broughall conducted major all-night vigils inside [[borley-church|Borley Church]] on **30–31 May 1975** and **28–29 May 1976** alongside his wife, [[alan-roper|Alan Roper]], and Charles Doerrer [6, 7]. Temperature readings during these vigils revealed nothing unusual, and their tape recorders picked up no paranormal sounds [6, 7]. His findings were later featured prominently in [[peter-underwood|Underwood]]'s *Hauntings* (1977).

He became extremely sceptical of [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth]]'s experiences, particularly the lengthy sighting of [[the-nun|the phantom nun]] in 1970 and the genuineness of the audio recordings made by [[denny-densham|Denny Densham]].

Two of the ghostly sounds on the Densham recordings — the opening of a "phantom" door and an eerie sigh — Broughall found he could **replicate easily** inside the church during his own vigil [8]. After his findings were published by [[peter-underwood|Underwood]] in 1977, he received a phone call from [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth|Croom-Hollingsworth]] who **attempted to backtrack on the recordings**, claiming that the BBC had "mistakenly selected sounds of the party themselves walking about, locking and unlocking doors, etc. in error" [8]. Croom-Hollingsworth continued to champion the recordings as late as 1984, despite this apparent backtracking.

During the 1990s, investigators Paul Moss-Kemp and [[wesley-downes|Wesley H. Downes]] gave Broughall an account of a midnight vigil at the grave of the Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] at [[campsey-ashe|Campsey Ashe]]. They claimed to see mist or smoke rise from the grave, travel horizontally, and pass through the locked church door. When Broughall bluntly asked what they did next, they admitted: **"We ran for our lives!"** [9].

## Card Index

During the 1980s, Broughall compiled an **exhaustive card index system** for the Borley case, combining all published literature with his own firsthand experiences [2]. This index was an unparalleled exercise in Borley data gathering and eventually served as the foundation for the extensive chronology published in the [[brc|Borley Rectory Companion]] [2].

## Conclusion

After decades of careful analysis, Broughall ultimately concluded that the Rectory was **probably never haunted** — "only perhaps by a succession of very strange occupants that fate had brought together" [2]. However, he maintained a cautious open mind regarding the surrounding environment, stating he was "not so sure" about the grounds, the [[rectory-cottage|Rectory Cottage]], the road, the [[borley-church|church]], and the churchyard [2]. His interest in Borley remained undiminished after more than thirty years.
