# Tony Broughall

Former Ghost Club Society area representative. Long-standing, enthusiastic and careful investigator of spontaneous and induced psychic phenomena who spent many years visiting Borley and examining the evidence.

## Discovery of Borley

In 1948 Broughall first heard of the Borley haunting when a visiting Spiritualist friend showed him a copy of [[mhh|MHH]] borrowed from his local church library. He did not visit Borley until February 1974 — a gap of over 25 years during which he developed his investigative skills at other locations.

## Borley Investigations

His later work at Borley is detailed in Peter Underwood's *Hauntings* (1977), including precise accounts of an all-night vigil in [[borley-church]] on 28–29 May 1976 with three other investigators. He became extremely sceptical of [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth]]'s experiences, particularly the lengthy sighting of the [[the-nun|phantom nun]] in 1970 and the genuineness of the audio recordings made by [[densham-denny|Denny Densham]].

Two of the ghostly sounds on the Densham recordings — the opening of a "phantom" door and an eerie sigh — Broughall found he could replicate easily inside the church during his own vigil. After his findings were published by [[peter-underwood]] in 1977, he received a phone call from Croom-Hollingsworth saying the BBC had "selected sounds of the party themselves walking about, locking and unlocking doors, etc. in error." Croom-Hollingsworth continued to champion the recordings as late as 1984, despite this apparent backtracking.

Broughall also became the person to whom [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth]] confided accounts of his own changed stories, and he became a key critical source on the reliability of Croom-Hollingsworth's claims.

## Card Index

During the 1980s Broughall compiled an exhaustive card index system for the Borley case, using all published literature plus his own experiences. One of us (PA) used this card index as the basis for the Chronology in the [[brc|Borley Rectory Companion]]. In terms of systematic data gathering on the Borley case, it has never been equalled.

## Conclusion

Broughall eventually concluded that the Rectory was probably never haunted — "only perhaps by a succession of very strange occupants that fate had brought together." However, he was less sure about the church, [[rectory-cottage]], road, and churchyard. He also investigated Chicksands Priory, where his wife and a party heard ghostly footsteps inside a locked building. His interest in Borley remained undiminished after more than thirty years.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Tony Broughall** was a dedicated and careful lay investigator of spontaneous and induced psychic phenomena who served as a former area representative for the Ghost Club Society [1]. His meticulous research, skepticism of sensational claims, and extensive archival work made him a highly respected figure in the post-Price era of Borley Rectory research.

### **Biographical Details and Background**
*   **Personal Connections:** He was married to Georgina Broughall and maintained a friendship and professional association with renowned ghost hunter Peter Underwood for many years [2-4].
*   **Other Investigations:** Beyond Borley, Broughall investigated numerous haunted locations. Notably, he investigated Chicksands Priory, where his wife and a party of friends heard ghostly footsteps while they were alone inside the locked building [2].
*   **Writings:** He compiled an exhaustive, unpublished manuscript titled **_The Ghosts of Bedfordshire and Hertfordshire_ (1976)**, which he presented to Peter Underwood [2]. His specific findings regarding Borley were prominently featured in Underwood's 1977 book, _Hauntings_ [1]. 

### **Introduction to Borley and Archival Contributions**
*   **First Awareness (1948):** Broughall first heard of the Borley haunting in **1948** when a Spiritualist friend showed him a borrowed library copy of Harry Price’s book, _The Most Haunted House in England_ [1]. 
*   **The Card Index System:** During the 1980s, Broughall compiled an **exhaustive card index system** of the Borley case, combining published literature with his own firsthand experiences [2]. This index was an unparalleled exercise in Borley data gathering and eventually served as the foundation for the extensive chronology published in _The Borley Rectory Companion_ [2].

### **Specific Investigations and Dates**
Although he had known of the case since 1948, Broughall did not actually visit Borley until **February 1974** [1]. 
*   **Early Visits:** On **13 February 1974**, he and his wife Georgina visited the site and took photographs of the Rectory ruins, including the old well mechanism [4].
*   **Exchanging Theories and Artifacts:** In **December 1974**, he sent a piece of Borley Rectory brickwork to fellow researcher Charles Doerrer in Rochester, New York [5]. In return, Doerrer sent Broughall a detailed theory proposing that the phantom nun was Catherine Waldegrave [5].
*   **Church Vigils (1975–1976):** Broughall conducted major all-night vigils inside Borley Church on **30–31 May 1975** and **28–29 May 1976** alongside his wife, Alan Roper, and Charles Doerrer [6, 7]. During these vigils, their temperature readings revealed nothing unusual, and their tape recorders picked up no paranormal sounds [6, 7]. 

### **Skepticism and the Croom-Hollingsworth Controversy**
Broughall became a staunch critic of the highly dramatic phenomena reported by Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth and film cameraman Denny Densham during the 1970s.
*   **Debunking the Audio Tapes:** Broughall was highly skeptical of Densham's audio recordings, which allegedly captured a "phantom" door opening and an eerie sigh inside the church. During his May 1976 vigil, Broughall found he was **able to easily replicate these exact noises himself** using the natural environment of the building [8].
*   **The Backtrack:** Broughall published these debunking details in 1977. Shortly afterward, he received a highly unexpected phone call from Croom-Hollingsworth, who **attempted to backtrack on the recordings**. Croom-Hollingsworth claimed that the BBC (who had broadcast the tapes) had mistakenly selected sounds of the investigators themselves walking about and locking doors in error [8]. 

### **Interactions with Other Investigators**
Broughall frequently collected the experiences of other Borley enthusiasts. During the 1990s, investigators Paul Moss-Kemp and Wesley H. Downes gave Broughall an account of a midnight vigil at the grave of the Rev. Lionel Foyster at Campsey Ashe. They claimed to see mist or smoke rise from the grave, travel horizontally, and pass through the locked church door [9]. Broughall bluntly asked them what they did next, to which they admitted: **"We ran for our lives!"** [9].

### **Final Conclusions on the Haunting**
After decades of careful analysis, Tony Broughall ultimately concluded that the Rectory itself was **probably never haunted** [2]. Instead, he believed the house had merely been occupied by a succession of "very strange occupants that fate had brought together" [2]. However, he maintained a cautious open mind regarding the surrounding environment, stating he was **"not so sure" about the grounds, the Rectory Cottage, the road, the church, and the churchyard**, leaving room for the possibility of genuine phenomena in those specific areas [2].
