# Esther Cox

Esther Cox (1860–1912) was a young Canadian woman at the centre of the famous **Great Amherst Mystery** poltergeist case in Amherst, Nova Scotia (1878–79). The SPR report draws eighteen numbered parallels between the Amherst phenomena and those of the [[foyster-phenomena|Foyster incumbency]] at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], and treats the comparison as central evidence that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] consciously modelled the Borley disturbances on the Amherst case.

## The Amherst Phenomena (1878–79)

In August 1878, eighteen-year-old Esther was living in a small wooden house on Princess Street, Amherst, in the home of her sister Olive and brother-in-law Daniel Teed. After an attempted sexual assault by a young man named Bob McNeill (who then fled town), poltergeist phenomena began approximately fifteen days later and lasted about fourteen months:

- Her hair would stand on end, her face turn blood red, her body swell
- Objects flew around the house and furniture was overturned
- Words were scratched deep into the plaster above her bed: *"Esther Cox, you are mine to kill"*
- A doctor had bedclothes violently stripped from the bed in his presence
- Small fires broke out and were always extinguished before serious damage
- Sensational messages addressed to Esther appeared on walls
- Mysterious voices were heard
- The phenomena were largely dependent on Esther's presence — during her absences from the house they ceased

## Convicted and Aftermath

Esther Cox was convicted and sentenced to four months' imprisonment as a thief and incendiary. She was eventually acquitted of the incendiary charges. The phenomena ceased abruptly after an Indian doctor performed "incantations and conjurations." She later married and refused to speak about the case, reportedly afraid the phenomena would return. She died in poverty in 1912 while [[walter-hubbell|Walter Hubbell's]] book continued to sell.

## Why the Foysters Likely Knew the Case

[[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] was rector of Sackville, Nova Scotia, from 1928 to 1930 — five miles from Amherst and the site of the phenomena. Hubbell's book had sold over 55,000 copies in multiple editions. Foyster himself appears to have confirmed his familiarity with the case by using "Teed" (the name of Esther's sister and brother-in-law, [[daniel-and-olive-teed|Daniel and Olive Teed]]) as a pseudonym in his own manuscript *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*.

> **Contested:** The SPR acknowledges the parallels could be coincidental and that they "cannot be regarded as anything but an extraordinary coincidence" if not intentional. The "admixture of truth and fraud" framework from the [[nhf|New Horizons Foundation report]] allows that some genuine phenomena occurred independent of any Amherst influence.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Esther Cox** (1859–1912) was the central figure in the "Great Amherst Mystery," a famous Canadian poltergeist case that took place between September 1878 and July 1879 in Amherst, Nova Scotia [1]. The Borley Rectory sources include extensive profiles of her life and the haunting because researchers believe her case served as the direct inspiration and blueprint for the phenomena later produced by Marianne Foyster at Borley Rectory [2, 3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following specific facts, events, and biographical details regarding Esther Cox:

**Biographical Details and the Teed Household**
Esther is described variably in the texts as a **"simple-hearted village girl"** and as **"dark and sullen"** [2, 4]. The sources contain a minor contradiction regarding her age at the time of the haunting, recording her as being either 18 or 19 years old [1, 5]. 

She lived in a neat, yellow-painted wooden house at Number 6, Princess Street, alongside her sister Olive and Olive's husband, Daniel Teed, who was a 35-year-old shoemaker and foreman at the Amherst Shoe Factory [1]. The household also included their two small sons, Esther’s older sister Jean, her brother William, and Daniel’s brother John [1, 4]. The entire family were devout, active members of the Wesleyan Methodist Church [1, 4].

**The Inciting Incident (August 1878)**
The spectacular display of strange happenings is recorded as having been sparked by a severe emotional and physical trauma [2, 5]. Esther was courting Bob McNeill, a local shoe factory worker [4]. On August 28, 1878, McNeill drove her to some nearby woods in a borrowed carriage, where he threatened her with a pistol and attempted to sexually assault her [2, 4]. McNeill fled the town the following day and was never heard from again [4].

**The "Great Amherst Mystery" Phenomena**
Fifteen days after the attempted rape, bizarre phenomena began to plague Esther, heavily focused on her bedroom and her bedclothes [6, 7]. The manifestations, which lasted for approximately 14 months, were a rare combination of visual apparitions and exceedingly violent physical events [8, 9]. 
*   **Physical Afflictions:** Esther would suffer from sudden attacks of ill health [8]. Witnesses reported that her hair would stand on end, her face would turn blood red, and her body would **"visibly swell"** or expand inexplicably, while she complained of feeling "electric currents" passing through her [7, 10]. 
*   **Poltergeist Activity:** The house was subjected to loud knocking and heavy pounding from the roof [7, 10]. Household objects were transported or lost, chairs were piled up, and Esther's bedclothes were violently stripped off by unseen forces even in the presence of the family doctor [7, 10-12]. Small outbreaks of fire also occurred, though they were always extinguished before causing serious damage [6].
*   **Apparitions and Voices:** Esther heard mysterious voices and claimed to see apparitions that were completely invisible to everyone else [11, 13]. She stated the house was possessed by six ghosts, led by an entity named "Bob Nickle" [12]. 
*   **Physical Assaults:** The phenomena grew violent; Esther was stabbed in the back, pins were found stuck into her flesh, and an attempt was made to cut her throat with a carving knife, though she was never seriously injured [9, 12].
*   **Wall Writings:** One of the most famous aspects of the case was the appearance of sensational messages scrawled on the walls, specifically addressed to her [6]. Words were scratched deep into the plaster above her bed reading: **"Esther Cox, you are mine to kill"** [12]. Other writings were deemed **"too profane even to print in the newspapers"** [12]. 

**Walter Hubbell and the Commercial Tour**
The events turned Esther into a local celebrity and attracted the attention of Walter Hubbell, an actor and theatrical impresario [12]. Hubbell arranged a commercial tour where the traumatized Esther exhibited herself and spoke about her experiences [12]. This exploitation drew fierce criticism; the *Presbyterian Witness* protested the tour, demanding, **"If the girl is sick, should her infirmities be exhibited to the public?"** [12]. Hubbell subsequently published an alleged diary of the events in a highly successful 1879 book, *The Haunted House: A True Ghost Story*, which went through at least ten editions and sold over 55,000 copies [3, 12, 14, 15]. Olive Teed, however, claimed Hubbell greatly embellished and dramatized the events [15]. 

**Aftermath and Criminal Conviction**
The sources record that Esther's involvement with the supernatural came to an abrupt end after about 14 months, following the **"incantations and conjurations of an Indian doctor"** [9]. A lesser-known biographical detail is that Esther was eventually **"tried, convicted, and sentenced to 4 months’ imprisonment as a thief and incendiary"** [9]. She subsequently retired into obscurity and marriage, refusing in her later years to ever speak of the haunting because she was **"afraid they would come back"** [15, 16]. She died poor in 1912 [15]. 

**Connection to the Borley Rectory Case**
Esther Cox is heavily referenced by skeptical psychical researchers (such as Eric Dingwall, Kathleen Goldney, and Trevor Hall) who drew a 19-point comparison between the Amherst mystery and the Foyster incumbency at Borley [5, 6, 8, 9, 11, 14, 17]. They noted that the Rev. Lionel Foyster and his wife Marianne had lived in Sackville, New Brunswick—just five miles from Amherst—immediately before moving to Borley Rectory [3]. The researchers argued that the Foysters must have known of the famous local legend and Hubbell's book, deliberately copying Esther Cox's specific phenomena, such as the wall writings, the bedclothes disturbances, and the small fires [3, 16, 18]. Further cementing this theory, Lionel Foyster even used the pseudonym "Teed" (the surname of Esther's brother-in-law) for a character in his own manuscript about the Borley hauntings [2, 3, 19].
