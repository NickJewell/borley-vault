# Esther Cox

Esther Cox (c. 1859–1912) was a young Canadian woman at the centre of the famous **Great Amherst Mystery** poltergeist case in Amherst, Nova Scotia (1878–79). The SPR report draws eighteen numbered parallels between the Amherst phenomena and those of the [[foyster-phenomena|Foyster incumbency]] at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], and treats the comparison as central evidence that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] consciously modelled the Borley disturbances on the Amherst case.

## Biographical Background

Esther is described variably in the texts as a "simple-hearted village girl" and as "dark and sullen" [2, 4]. The sources contain a minor contradiction regarding her age at the time of the haunting, recording her as either 18 or 19 years old [1, 5].

She lived in a neat, yellow-painted wooden house at Number 6, Princess Street, Amherst, with her sister Olive and Olive's husband Daniel Teed — a 35-year-old shoemaker and foreman at the Amherst Shoe Factory [1]. The household also included their two small sons, Esther's older sister Jean, her brother William, and Daniel's brother John [1, 4]. The entire family were devout, active members of the Wesleyan Methodist Church [1, 4].

## The Inciting Incident (August 1878)

The phenomena are recorded as having been sparked by a severe emotional and physical trauma [2, 5]. Esther was courting Bob McNeill, a local shoe factory worker [4]. On 28 August 1878, McNeill drove her to some nearby woods in a borrowed carriage, where he threatened her with a pistol and attempted to sexually assault her [2, 4]. McNeill fled the town the following day and was never heard from again [4].

## The Phenomena (1878–79)

Fifteen days after the attempted assault, bizarre phenomena began to plague Esther, heavily focused on her bedroom and her bedclothes [6, 7]. The manifestations lasted approximately fourteen months [8, 9]:

- Her hair would stand on end, her face turn blood red, her body swell inexplicably, while she complained of "electric currents" passing through her [7, 10]
- Objects flew around the house and furniture was overturned; bedclothes were violently stripped from the bed even in the presence of the family doctor [7, 10–12]
- Words were scratched deep into the plaster above her bed: *"Esther Cox, you are mine to kill"* [12]. Other writings were deemed "too profane even to print in the newspapers" [12]
- Small fires broke out and were always extinguished before serious damage [6]
- Esther heard mysterious voices and claimed to see apparitions invisible to everyone else; she stated the house was possessed by six ghosts led by an entity named "Bob Nickle" [12]
- The phenomena were largely dependent on Esther's presence — during her absences from the house they ceased
- Esther was stabbed in the back, pins were found stuck into her flesh, and an attempt was made to cut her throat with a carving knife, though she was never seriously injured [9, 12]

## Walter Hubbell and the Commercial Tour

The events attracted [[walter-hubbell|Walter Hubbell]], an actor and theatrical impresario, who arranged a commercial tour where the traumatized Esther exhibited herself [12]. The *Presbyterian Witness* protested: **"If the girl is sick, should her infirmities be exhibited to the public?"** [12]. Hubbell subsequently published *The Haunted House: A True Ghost Story* (1879), which went through at least ten editions and sold over 55,000 copies [3, 12, 14, 15]. Olive Teed, however, claimed Hubbell greatly embellished and dramatized the events [15].

## Aftermath and Criminal Conviction

The phenomena came to an abrupt end after the **"incantations and conjurations of an Indian doctor"** [9]. A lesser-known biographical detail is that Esther was **"tried, convicted, and sentenced to 4 months' imprisonment as a thief and incendiary"** [9], though she was eventually acquitted of the incendiary charges. She retired into obscurity and marriage, refusing in her later years to speak of the haunting because she was **"afraid they would come back"** [15, 16]. She died poor in 1912, while Hubbell's book continued to sell [15].

## The 18 Parallels with Borley

The SPR report presents a systematic comparison between Amherst and the Foyster period at Borley:

| Amherst (Esther Cox) | Borley (Marianne Foyster) |
|---|---|
| Young woman aged 19 | Young woman aged ~31 |
| Phenomena began after emotional disturbance | Emotional outbursts noted by Glanville |
| Sudden alleged ill-health | Frequent alleged fainting fits |
| Phenomena ceased during her absence | Phenomena ceased during her absence |
| Lasted ~14 months | Lasted 15 months |
| Claimed to see apparitions visible to nobody else | Claimed to see apparitions visible to nobody else (except d'Arles) |
| Mysterious voices heard | Mysterious voices heard |
| Objects transported from place to place | Objects transported from place to place |
| Violent throwing of small objects | Violent throwing of small objects |
| Furniture repeatedly upset | Furniture repeatedly upset |
| Many manifestations concerned with her bed and bedclothes | Many manifestations concerned with her bed and bedclothes |
| Small fires, always extinguished | Small fires, always extinguished |
| Sensational messages scrawled on walls addressed to her | Wall-messages addressed to Marianne |
| Injured several times, never seriously | Injured several times, never seriously |
| Rare combination of apparitions and violent objective phenomena | Rare combination of apparitions and violent objective phenomena |
| Tried, convicted as thief and incendiary; phenomena ceased after Indian doctor's "incantations" | Repeatedly accused of responsibility; phenomena ceased after Marks Tey Circle's visit |
| Hubbell wrote an embellished diary expanded into a book | Foyster wrote a diary expanded into a book for publication |
| Analysis showed published account embellished vs. original | SPR investigators demonstrate *Fifteen Months* was much embellished vs. *Diary of Occurrences* |

## Why the Foysters Likely Knew the Case

[[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] was rector of Sackville, Nova Scotia, from 1928 to 1930 — just five miles from Amherst. Hubbell's book had sold over 55,000 copies in multiple editions. Foyster himself appears to have confirmed his familiarity with the case by using "Teed" (the name of Esther's sister and brother-in-law, [[daniel-and-olive-teed|Daniel and Olive Teed]]) as a pseudonym in his own manuscript *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House* [2, 3, 19].

> **Contested:** The SPR acknowledges the parallels could be coincidental and that they "cannot be regarded as anything but an extraordinary coincidence" if not intentional. The "admixture of truth and fraud" framework from the [[nhf|New Horizons Foundation report]] allows that some genuine phenomena occurred independent of any Amherst influence.
