# Dr. J. H. Pafford

*(The page slug follows the original designation "H. P. Pafford," but the Borley sources consistently record his name as **Dr. J. H. Pafford**, occasionally misspelled "Dr. Patford" in the texts.)*

**Goldsmiths' Librarian at the University of London** and official custodian of the massive Harry Price Library of Magical Literature — including the famous [[borley-dossier|Borley Dossier]] — which Price bequeathed to the University upon his death in 1948 [1–3]. He preceded [[ah-wesencraft|A. H. Wesencraft]] in this role. Pafford made a significant allegation about the removal of [[locked-book|Glanville's Locked Book]] from the library.

## Assistance to the SPR Investigators

When [[eric-dingwall|Dr. Eric Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen Goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] were commissioned by the Society for Psychical Research to re-examine the Borley case in the 1950s, it was Pafford who facilitated their research. The authors specifically thanked him in the preface of the 1956 *Borley Report* for **"giving us facilities to remove temporarily some of these documents for extended examination."** [3]

## The *Locked Book* Controversy

Pafford is most notable in the Borley literature for his direct contradiction of [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] regarding the legal ownership of [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]]'s *Locked Book of Private Information*.

In **1948**, Pafford gave Glanville permission to borrow the *Locked Book* from the University of London library so Glanville could reference his own original notes while working on the projected *Borley Symposium* [1, 4]. However, in **1953**, Glanville inscribed the borrowed book and "gave" it to Trevor Hall [1, 4]. Hall later published articles claiming that Price had "oddly" included the book in his university bequest, asserting it was never Price's to give away and that Glanville had legally "withdrawn" it in 1948 [2].

On **June 19, 1959** — three days after Hall visited the library and left a copy of the *International Journal of Parapsychology* containing his assertions — Pafford drafted an internal University memorandum formally recording the Library's opposing view. His definitive quotes:

> **"It is clear that Mr. Price considered that the *Locked Book* had been given to him by Mr. Glanville and that he, therefore, naturally presented it to us—not 'oddly.'"** [2]

> **"It is, furthermore, quite certain that Mr. Glanville did not 'withdraw' the *Locked Book* in 1948 as if it were his property. There is no question of this whatsoever."** [2]

> **"I knew Mr. Glanville and he visited me several times in the Library. He never, at any time, said that the *Locked Book* was his property, and he asked me if he could borrow it. What is accordingly odd about the matter is that Mr. Glanville then gave the book to Mr. Hall."** [2]

> **Contradiction:** Roger Glanville (Sidney's son) felt the ownership rested with his father and understood the gift to Hall was arranged during a lengthy meeting at his home. Pafford, however, stated that Sidney Glanville once *asked to borrow* the book from the library — implying Glanville himself regarded it as library property, not his to give.

## Significance

Pafford's 1959 statement is the earliest documented challenge to Trevor Hall's ownership claim of the Locked Book, establishing that concerns about Hall's conduct arose shortly after the book changed hands — not retrospectively. [[ah-wesencraft|Wesencraft]] (Pafford's successor) later voiced similar doubts: "the current curator... voiced his doubts to me about Hall's claim to the ownership and his right to eventually sell it to an American for over £1000." Banks records Pafford alongside [[ah-wesencraft|Wesencraft]] as one of the key figures who **"raised their eyebrows"** over Hall's possession of the artifact, with Wesencraft going so far as to state outright that Hall had "stolen" the book from the University [5].
