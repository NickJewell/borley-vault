# John Whyard

Seventeen-year-old grocer's son who drowned in the River Stour on **5 August 1862** while working on the construction of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] as a bricklayer's labourer.

## The Drowning

Whyard was employed by builder [[thomas-farrow|Thomas Farrow]] on the Rectory site [1]. After a hot summer day's work, he and several colleagues — including carpenters John Mathoby and John Barber — headed down through the glebelands to the riverside near the Rectory to bathe [1].

Mathoby, noting that the water seemed deep after measuring it with a stick, specifically warned Whyard to take care before they entered the water [1]. As Whyard struck out to swim, his fellow carpenter Barber overheard the boy say that he had cramp [1]. Hearing the shouts that Whyard was in trouble, Mathoby swam towards him and seized him by the hair, but the panicking boy struggled and pulled Mathoby down into the deep water with him [1]. Mathoby was forced to let go and, despite making another attempt to save him, could not reach Whyard in time. The men eventually had to use creepers to drag the boy's lifeless body from the river [1].

At the inquest in Long Melford three days later, the jury returned a verdict of **accidental drowning** [1].

## Significance

Superstitious locals muttered at the time that the boy's death was **"a bad omen for the new rector's grand new rectory"** [1]. This association embedded itself in the local consciousness long before any ghost stories were attached to the Rectory, and [[hbrs-ch04|O'Connor]] cites it as one of the earliest strands of unease that surrounded the building even as it was being erected.

The site of the Rectory on a ridge above the River Stour, where Whyard drowned, would later become the setting for the most famous alleged haunting in British history.
