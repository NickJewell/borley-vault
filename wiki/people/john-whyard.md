# John Whyard

Seventeen-year-old bricklayer's labourer who drowned in the River Stour on 5 August 1862 while working on the construction of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

## The Drowning

Whyard was a grocer's son employed by builder [[thomas-farrow|Thomas Farrow]] on the Rectory site. After a hot summer day's work, he and several colleagues — including carpenters John Mathoby and John Barber — headed down through the glebelands to the riverside near the Rectory to bathe.

Carpenter Mathoby, who was not a strong swimmer, measured the water with a stick and found it deep, warning Whyard to take care. Mathoby jumped in first and swam across to the far meadow. As Whyard entered the water and tried to swim, Barber overheard him say that he had cramp. By the time Mathoby heard shouts and swam back, Whyard had gone under. Mathoby seized him by the hair, but Whyard struggled and pulled Mathoby down with him. Mathoby let go, tried again, but could not save him. Whyard's lifeless body was eventually dragged from the river with the aid of creepers.

At the inquest in Long Melford three days later, the jury returned a verdict of **accidental drowning**.

## Significance

Superstitious locals muttered at the time that the boy's death was "a bad omen for the new rector's grand new rectory." This association embedded itself in the local consciousness long before any ghost stories were attached to the Rectory, and O'Connor cites it as one of the earliest strands of unease that surrounded the building even as it was being erected.

The site of the Rectory on a ridge above the River Stour, where Whyard drowned, would later become the setting for the most famous alleged haunting in British history.
