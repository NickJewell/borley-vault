# Constance Bull

**Constance St. Clere Bull** (born 1871, baptised 14 January 1872) was the ninth child and seventh daughter of [[henry-bull|Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] and Caroline Sarah Foyster [1, 2]. One of the surviving sisters of the late Rev. [[harry-bull]] and one of the children who grew up with the Borley phenomena. Distinct from [[constance-brackenbury]], who was Harry Bull's stepdaughter by his wife Ivy.

## Family Context

Constance is described as having been regarded as "backward" by her family and treated accordingly [3]. However, [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]], who lived at the Rectory during the 1930s, disagreed with this assessment and found Constance "kind of charming" [3].

Her presence in the family occasionally caused confusion for the domestic staff after her brother Harry married Ivy Brackenbury in 1911. Ivy brought her own daughter, [[constance-brackenbury|Constance Brackenbury]], to live with them. The servants respectfully referred to the stepdaughter as "Miss Constance," which caused slight confusion because Constance Bull was also addressed by that name [4].

Given that [[henry-bull]] had fourteen children (twelve of whom survived), multiple daughters were resident at the Rectory for extended periods and would have experienced the same pattern of nightly footsteps, bell-ringing, and other phenomena that [[ethel-bull]] describes in detail. The Bull family, including Constance, reportedly "grew up with the phenomena, of which they had little fear."

## Departure from Borley Rectory

Constance lived at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] for nearly fifty years. In 1920, she and her unmarried sisters were effectively displaced when Harry and his new wife Ivy moved into the Rectory, forcing Constance, [[ethel-bull|Ethel]], [[freda-bull|Freda]], and their brothers to relocate to [[chilton-lodge|Chilton Lodge]] in Great Cornard, Sudbury [7–9]. This eviction reportedly fuelled a bitter, lifelong animosity between the Bull sisters and Ivy [9].

## Hostility Towards Marianne Foyster

Constance harboured a deep dislike for [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]], the young wife of the subsequent rector. The animosity was mutual and well-known; while [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]] described Marianne as "mad as a hatter," Constance bluntly referred to Marianne as "a little beast" [10, 11].

## Connection to the Case

[[harry-price|Harry Price]] and his secretary [[lucie-kaye]] visited the Misses Bull at Chilton Lodge on 13 June 1929 — the day after Price's first arrival at Borley. Price names "the Misses Ethel, Freda, and Constance Bull" as the three sisters he interviewed on this occasion, recording that he found them "very charming and cultured, and quite ready to give any information in their possession concerning Borley Rectory; and willing to relate their personal experiences of the nun and other phenomena" [14, 15]. Constance is not credited with witnessing the famous collective sighting of the phantom nun on [[july-28th|July 28, 1900]] (which was seen by [[ethel-bull|Ethel]], [[freda-bull|Freda]], [[mabel-bull|Mabel]], and [[elsie-bull|Elsie/Dodie]]) [12, 13], but she openly supported her family's claims that the Rectory was haunted.

The account compiled from the June 13 interview was supplemented by later interviews that Price and [[sidney-glanville]] had with the Bull sisters at Chilton Lodge on 29 March 1939.

In the 1950s, Constance was still residing at Chilton Lodge with Ethel and Alfred. She was present when the investigator [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] visited their home on several occasions, during which the surviving siblings continued to maintain the absolute truth of their paranormal experiences [16, 17].

## Later Life and Death

Constance outlived all thirteen of her siblings, making her the last surviving child of [[henry-bull|Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] [5, 6]. She died in 1963 at the age of 91 or 92. Despite her family's reportedly dismissive treatment of her, she left a substantial estate of about £50,000 [6].
