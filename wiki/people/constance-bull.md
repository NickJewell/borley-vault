# Constance Bull

Daughter of [[henry-bull]], the builder of Borley Rectory. One of the surviving sisters of the late Rev. [[harry-bull]] and one of the children who grew up with the Borley phenomena. Distinct from [[constance-brackenbury]], who was Harry Bull's stepdaughter by his wife Ivy.

## Connection to the Case

[[harry-price]] and his secretary [[lucie-kaye]] visited the Misses Bull at Chilton Lodge, Great Cornard on June 13, 1929 — the day after Price's first arrival at Borley. Price names "the Misses Ethel, Freda, and Constance Bull" as the three sisters he interviewed on this occasion. She, along with [[ethel-bull]] and [[freda-bull]], supplied Price with detailed composite accounts of the Borley phenomena as experienced by the Bull family during their decades of residence.

The account compiled from the June 13 interview was supplemented by later interviews that Price and [[sidney-glanville]] had with the Bull sisters at Chilton Lodge on March 29, 1939.

## Family Context

Constance is named in the 1929 interview but does not appear as a witness to a specific paranormal incident in Price's text — her testimony was grouped with that of her sisters. Given that Henry Bull had fourteen children (twelve of whom survived), multiple daughters were resident at the Rectory for extended periods and would have experienced the same pattern of nightly footsteps, bell-ringing, and other phenomena that [[ethel-bull]] describes in detail.

The Bull family, including Constance, "grew up with the phenomena, of which they had little fear," according to Ethel Bull's testimony.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Constance St. Clere Bull** was the ninth child (and seventh daughter) of the Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull and Caroline Sarah Foyster [1, 2]. Born in 1871 and officially baptised on January 14, 1872 [1, 2], she features in the Borley Rectory sources primarily through her interactions with investigators, her attitude towards other key figures in the haunting, and her eventual position as the longest-living member of the immediate Bull family. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, and lesser-known biographical details:

**Lesser-Known Biographical Details and Family Dynamics**
*   **The "Backward" Sister:** Within the large Bull family, Constance was **"regarded as 'backward' by her family and was treated accordingly"** [3]. However, Marianne Foyster (who lived at the Rectory during the 1930s) disagreed with this assessment, noting that Constance was actually **"kind of charming"** [3].
*   **The Name Confusion:** Her presence in the family occasionally caused mix-ups for the domestic staff after her brother Harry married Ivy Brackenbury in 1911. Ivy brought her own daughter, Constance Brackenbury, to live with them. The servants respectfully referred to the stepdaughter as **"Miss Constance,"** which caused slight confusion because Constance Bull was also addressed by that name [4].
*   **Death and Wealth:** Constance outlived all thirteen of her siblings, making her the **last surviving child** of the Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull [5, 6]. She died in 1963 at the age of 91 or 92, and despite her family's allegedly dismissive treatment of her, she left a very substantial estate of **about £50,000** [6]. 

**Departure from Borley Rectory**
Constance lived at Borley Rectory for nearly fifty years. However, in 1920, she and her unmarried sisters were effectively banished from their childhood home. Her brother Harry and his new wife Ivy decided to move into the Rectory, forcing Constance, Ethel, Freda, and their brothers to relocate to **Chilton Lodge** in Great Cornard, Sudbury [7-9]. This eviction fueled a bitter, lifelong hatred between the Bull sisters and Ivy [9].

**Hostility Towards Marianne Foyster**
Constance harboured a deep dislike for Marianne Foyster, the young wife of the subsequent rector, the Rev. Lionel Foyster. The animosity was mutual and well-known; while Ethel Bull described Marianne as "mad as a hatter," **Constance bluntly referred to Marianne as "a little beast"** [10, 11]. 

**Involvement in the Psychical Investigations**
Though Constance is not credited with witnessing the most famous collective sighting of the phantom nun on July 28, 1900 (which was seen by Ethel, Freda, Mabel, and Elsie/Dodie) [12, 13], she openly supported her family's claims that the Rectory was haunted:
*   On **June 13, 1929**, investigator Harry Price and his secretary Lucie Kaye interviewed Constance, Ethel, and Freda at Chilton Lodge. Price recorded that he found the three sisters to be **"very charming and cultured, and quite ready to give any information in their possession concerning Borley Rectory; and willing to relate their personal experiences of the nun and other phenomena"** [14, 15].
*   Decades later, in the 1950s, Constance was still residing at Chilton Lodge with Ethel and Alfred. She was present when the investigator Peter Underwood visited their home on several occasions, during which the surviving siblings continued to maintain the absolute truth of their paranormal experiences [16, 17].
