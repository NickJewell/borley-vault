# Alan Gregson

Son of [[captain-gregson|Capt William Hart Gregson]], the final owner of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]]. At the time of writing (1985) he was living in Australia. He corresponded with the researchers and provided two previously unpublished letters containing the most detailed account of the fire and the family's experiences at Borley.

## Letters of 1985

### First Letter (16 July 1985)

Key revelations:
- Purchase price was **£350** (not £500 as reported elsewhere) — the family's own knowledge should be definitive
- The family **never slept or ate in the rectory** — they lived in the flat above the coach-house while intending to renovate the main house
- Captain's library and furniture were stored in the ground-floor rooms of the rectory
- The fire was "a pure mischance not attributable to Poltergeist, Marianne, elemental or any mortal person"
- **The two dogs:** Peter (a spaniel-retriever cross, "a happy, reliable, uncomplicated dog") panicked in the Borley courtyard and ran howling away toward Long Melford, never seen again; weeks later a second dog suffered the same fate
- **The well cover:** the circular well in the cellar was completely unprotected; the family covered it themselves with a heavy framed door found on the property
- **Carriage tracks in snow:** "Not only footprints in overnight snow, but the tracks of ironshod wheels, as of a carriage, certainly ran from the front of the rectory, down the lawn past the cedar tree and they seemed to lift-off at the shrubbery one winter's morning. The thing to remember is that no physical access for anything bigger than a wheelbarrow led to that part of the grounds."

### Second Letter (18 October 1985)

**The fire in detail:**
> "...my father wanted a book or document from the rectory; apparently he stood the lamp on a table or box while he rummaged about and then a stack of books suddenly keeled over onto the lamp, overturning it, breaking the glass and spilling kerosene which then caught alight and speedily ignited surrounding articles."

The boys were sent to Borley Green (the nearest telephone) to call the Sudbury fire brigade. Alan Gregson himself did **not** witness any apparitions in or around the fire. The "man and lady" seen in the grounds, and the figure at the upstairs window, were reported by others. The fire was centred on the main staircase.

The family opened a visitors' book; Capt Gregson approached coach companies about organizing trips to Borley for profit. In January 1940 the captain wrote to [[harry-price|Harry Price]] saying they would like to return to live at Borley. The war ended any such plans.

## Significance

Alan Gregson's letters effectively clear his father of the arson charge. The captain owned other properties in Essex, had lost irreplaceable books, furniture and valuables in the fire, and his son's detailed account is internally consistent with an accident. The out-of-court settlement with the insurers for £750 reflects the insurers' commercial decision, not proof of guilt.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Alan Gregson** (born 1921) was the eldest son of Captain William Hart Gregson and his first wife, Rita Pattard [1]. He is a crucial, if somewhat contradictory, witness to the final days of Borley Rectory. A thorough reference profile of his life and involvement in the Borley case yields the following details:

**Arrival at Borley and Rectory Experiences**
Alan moved to Borley in December 1938 at the age of eighteen, along with his father and fourteen-year-old brother, Anthony [2, 3]. The family initially lived in the stable cottage pending the redecoration of the main Rectory, stacking a great deal of their furniture and belongings in the entrance hall and stairwell [4]. 

During his brief time living on the estate, Alan experienced at least one notable unexplained event. He recalled observing **"iron-rimmed tracks of some form of, presumably, horse-drawn conveyance—in a part of the garden that was accessible by nothing larger than a wheelbarrow!"** [5]. He noted that the tracks stood out crisply in the freshly fallen snow [5]. 

After the Rectory was destroyed, Alan helped his father conduct "personally conducted tours" of the haunted ruins during the June 1939 Psychic Fête [6]. He also appeared alongside his father and brother in footage shot at the Rectory by British Films Ltd., though Alan later joked in a letter, **"With a cast of us three, you can be assured that it would not have changed the course of cinema history"** [7, 8].

**The Fire and the Contradiction with Anthony**
Alan’s most significant contribution to the Borley literature is his detailed account of the February 1939 fire, which **directly contradicts the accusations of arson and insurance fraud leveled against their father by his younger brother, Anthony** [9, 10]. 

In the 1980s, researcher Richard Lee-Van den Daele tracked Alan down in Australia/Tasmania [11, 12]. In letters dated July 16 and October 18, 1985, Alan defended his father as an "upright citizen" [13] and provided a mundane explanation for the blaze: 
*   He wrote that his father went into the unlit Rectory to fetch a book, using an argand-type brass table-lamp [4]. 
*   According to Alan: **"he stood the lamp on a table or box while he rummaged about and then a stack of books suddenly keeled over onto the lamp, overturning it, breaking the glass and spilling kerosene which then caught alight and speedily ignited surrounding articles"** [4, 14]. 
*   Because they lacked firefighting equipment, his father sent Alan and Anthony to the nearest telephone at Borley Green to call the Sudbury fire brigade [4]. *(Note: Borley researchers have pointed out that sending both boys away, rather than keeping one to help contain the fire, was highly suspicious and perhaps allowed the fire to become unmanageable [10, 15].)*

Alan dismissed Anthony’s dramatic claims that the fire was a deliberate real estate scam, suggesting his brother suffered from **"overexposure to James Cagney"** [10]. Alan also explicitly denied seeing any of the ghostly figures in the blazing ruins that other villagers and a local policeman reported [8]. Lee-Van den Daele found Alan to be a man of "great integrity" with an "immense memory" for minute details [14].

**Later Life and the Bullion Theft Contradiction**
During the Second World War, Alan joined the armed forces and was posted to East Africa, an assignment his brother jealously described as an "easy billet" [16]. In 1947, sharing his father's dislike of the Socialist government, Alan and Capt. Gregson emigrated to Tasmania [13]. 

The sources contain a **major contradiction regarding which of the Gregson brothers was later convicted of a spectacular gold heist in Canada**. 
*   One passage explicitly states that **Alan** became involved in a bullion theft at a gold mine in the Northwest Territories, was apprehended in Tasmania in 1957, and returned to Yellowknife, Canada, to serve a two-year sentence [17]. 
*   However, another passage attributes this exact crime to **Anthony** Gregson, claiming that *Anthony* stowed away to Canada, arrived at the Discovery Mine, and stole the gold shipment by substituting lead for the gold bars, before being caught in Tasmania in 1957 and sentenced to two years in prison [9, 13]. 

The text remains ambiguous on which brother actually committed the Canadian bullion theft, though it definitively places Alan in Tasmania by 1947 [13].
