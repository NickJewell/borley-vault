# Robert O'Neil

American G.I. from Minnesota. Marianne Foyster's third (or fourth) husband, whom she married on August 11, 1945 — approximately four months after [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster's]] death on April 18, 1945. He was stationed at Parham airbase in England during World War II.

Marianne and Robert emigrated to America in 1946 (arriving August), bringing the adopted infant Vincent (later [[vincent-oneil|Vincent O'Neil]]). They settled in various locations including Minnesota, Wisconsin, and North Dakota.

Robert died in a road accident, saving a child. Marianne was left widowed in America with limited means, working variously as a teacher (briefly, at a schoolhouse in Bangor, Wisconsin, 1950) and other occupations.

## Significance

Robert O'Neil's name became Vincent's legal name, giving the author his identity as "O'Neil." The family was unaware of Marianne's Borley history; she never discussed it at home.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Robert O'Neil** (also recorded as **Robert Vincent O'Neil**) was an American soldier stationed in England during the Second World War who became the fourth husband of Marianne Foyster, the controversial figure at the centre of the Borley Rectory hauntings [1, 2]. While he had no direct involvement in the paranormal investigations at Borley, his life with Marianne provides a crucial concluding chapter to her story.

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical facts, events, dates, quotes, and contradictions regarding his life:

**Early Life and Military Service**
*   **Background:** Born on **22 February 1920** in Caledonia, Minnesota, he was the son of an Irish farmer, V. O'Neil, who died when Robert was eleven years old [3-6]. He was raised as a devout Roman Catholic by his mother, Esther, and his grandmother on a remote, primitive farm in the Minnesota hills [4, 7].
*   **Military Career:** He enlisted as a Private at Fort Snelling on **13 January 1941**, under the serial number **47097995** [5]. 
*   **Deployment:** During the war, he served as a ground crew member for the **390th Heavy Bombardment Group** (a Flying Fortress unit) stationed at Parham (Framlingham) in Suffolk [3, 5]. His foreign service dates were recorded as **1 September 1943 to 25 December 1945** [5].

**Meeting Marianne Foyster**
*   **First Contact:** O'Neil met Marianne when she sought help from an army doctor at the nearby American base after her husband, the ailing Rev. Lionel Foyster, collapsed [3, 8]. O'Neil, along with another GI, visited Dairy Cottages to check on the rector [3, 8].
*   **The Affair:** Generous with his wealth relative to the ration-beleaguered British, the 25-year-old O'Neil brought sweets for the children and alcohol for the 46-year-old Marianne, even installing a barrel of beer in her larder [3, 9, 10]. They quickly began a sexual relationship [9, 11]. 
*   **Lionel's Approval:** Despite the 21-year age gap between the lovers, Lionel Foyster not only condoned the affair but actively encouraged Marianne to marry the American after his death so she could start a new life in North America [9, 12]. 

**Marriage and Deception**
*   **The Proposal:** After Lionel died in April 1945, Marianne learned that O'Neil's unit was destined for Europe before returning to the United States [13]. Desperate to escape Britain, she falsely told the "naive and decent Catholic" soldier that she was pregnant with his child, prompting him to propose [13]. 
*   **The Wedding:** They married by special licence on **11 August 1945** at the Ipswich Register Office [2, 13, 14]. O'Neil arrived out of uniform just three minutes before the office closed [13]. 
*   **Certificate Falsehoods:** To bypass US military regulations forbidding unauthorised marriages to foreigners, the marriage certificate was filled with lies [6, 13]. O'Neil's military status was completely omitted; instead, his profession was recorded as an **"engineer’s erector, railway plant and sluice manufacturer"** [2, 6]. Marianne claimed a fictitious address (229 Ranelagh Road) and shaved 14 years off her age, claiming to be 32 [2, 6]. 

**The "War Baby"**
*   Following the wedding, O'Neil was posted to France [13]. In his absence, Marianne acquired an illegitimate baby boy from a local woman and legally adopted him, passing the child off as O'Neil's biological son [7, 15]. 
*   When she sent a photograph of the baby (baptised Robert Vincent O'Neil) to O'Neil's family in Wisconsin, the thrilled GI wrote back: **"I guess the baby is like me in his ways, too Hon"** [7]. 

**Life in America and Downward Spiral**
*   **Settling in Minnesota:** Marianne and the baby joined O'Neil in the United States in August 1946 [16, 17]. They lived initially on his grandmother's primitive farm before O'Neil bought a house 13 miles away in Hokah, Minnesota, where he found work with a plumbing firm and purchased a new Hudson automobile [4]. 
*   **The 1947 Car Accident:** A turning point occurred in 1947 when O'Neil allowed a drunken friend, Sauer, to drive his car [18, 19]. The vehicle was run off Highway 44 and ruined, leaving O'Neil hospitalised with a fractured jaw and clavicle [18, 19]. 
*   **Alcoholism and Abuse:** Following the accident, O'Neil's behaviour drastically changed; he became a feckless, heavy drinker and a reckless driver who suffered multiple arrests [18]. He refused to work, forcing Marianne to become the sole breadwinner as a journalist and uncertified teacher to support the family [18, 20]. 
*   **Separation and Divorce:** O'Neil briefly abandoned the family for a woman named Beverly St Jacques [21]. Though the couple later reconciled and moved to Jamestown, North Dakota, his drinking continued [20, 22]. He became violently abusive, breaking both of Marianne's wrists and beating his son, before tearfully apologising: **"Why do you make me do this? I don’t want to hit you. Why do you make me do it?"** [23]. Marianne finally divorced him in **1959** [23, 24]. 
*   **Later Years:** Despite the divorce, O'Neil was completely disowned by his own family and incapable of supporting himself [24]. Marianne eventually allowed the "hopeless case" to live with her again [24]. To protect them both from credit agencies pursuing his debts, she would often falsely list herself in local directories as a widow [24]. In 1958, private investigator Robert Swanson described O'Neil as a man of medium height and build with reddish hair, a heavy beard growth, and noted he was a drunkard who seldom worked [25, 26].

**Major Textual Contradictions Regarding His Death**
The Borley sources present two completely contradictory accounts of Robert O'Neil's death:
1.  **The Factual Timeline (1981):** The primary historical narrative records that in 1981, following another car crash, O'Neil underwent a routine X-ray which revealed an inoperable tumour [27]. He was given six months to live and died later that year, leaving Marianne deeply aggrieved despite their unstable history [1, 27].
2.  **The "Heroic Accident" Myth:** An anomalous and contradictory account in one source claims that shortly after arriving in America in the 1940s, O'Neil **"died trying to save a child in a road accident, leaving Marianne with a baby son, Vernon O'Neil"** [28]. *(Note: This source also incorrectly identifies the son's name as Vernon rather than Vincent).*
