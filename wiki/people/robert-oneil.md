# Robert O'Neil

(22 February 1920, Caledonia, Minnesota – 1981) American G.I. from Minnesota; [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]]'s third (or fourth) husband. Born in Caledonia, Minnesota, the son of an Irish farmer, V. O'Neil, who died when Robert was eleven years old. He was raised as a devout Roman Catholic by his mother, Esther, and his grandmother on a remote farm in the Minnesota hills. He enlisted as a Private at Fort Snelling on 13 January 1941 (serial number 47097995).

During the war he served as a ground crew member for the **390th Heavy Bombardment Group** (a Flying Fortress unit) stationed at Parham (Framlingham) in Suffolk; his foreign service dates were 1 September 1943 to 25 December 1945.

## Meeting Marianne Foyster

O'Neil met Marianne when she sought help from an army doctor at the nearby American base after her husband, the ailing [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]], collapsed. O'Neil visited Dairy Cottages to check on the rector. Generous with his wealth relative to the ration-beleaguered British, the 25-year-old O'Neil brought sweets for the children and alcohol for the 46-year-old Marianne, even installing a barrel of beer in her larder. They quickly began a sexual relationship. Despite the 21-year age gap, Lionel Foyster not only condoned the affair but actively encouraged Marianne to marry the American after his death so she could start a new life in North America.

## Marriage and Deception

After Lionel died in April 1945, Marianne learned that O'Neil's unit was destined for Europe before returning to the United States. Desperate to escape Britain, she allegedly falsely told the "naive and decent Catholic" soldier that she was pregnant with his child, prompting him to propose. They married by special licence on **11 August 1945** at the Ipswich Register Office — O'Neil arriving out of uniform just three minutes before the office closed.

To bypass US military regulations forbidding unauthorised marriages to foreigners, the marriage certificate was filled with lies: O'Neil's military status was completely omitted and his profession recorded as "engineer's erector, railway plant and sluice manufacturer"; Marianne claimed a fictitious address (229 Ranelagh Road) and shaved 14 years off her age, claiming to be 32.

## The "War Baby"

Following the wedding, O'Neil was posted to France. In his absence, Marianne reportedly acquired an illegitimate baby boy from a local woman and legally adopted him, passing the child off as O'Neil's biological son. When she sent a photograph of the baby (baptised Robert Vincent O'Neil) to O'Neil's family in Wisconsin, the thrilled GI wrote back: "I guess the baby is like me in his ways, too Hon."

## Life in America and Downward Spiral

Marianne and the baby joined O'Neil in the United States in August 1946. They lived initially on his grandmother's primitive farm before O'Neil bought a house 13 miles away in Hokah, Minnesota, where he found work with a plumbing firm and purchased a new Hudson automobile. In 1947, a turning point occurred when O'Neil allowed a drunken friend, Sauer, to drive his car; the vehicle was run off Highway 44, leaving O'Neil hospitalised with a fractured jaw and clavicle.

Following the accident, O'Neil's behaviour drastically changed: he became a heavy drinker and reckless driver who suffered multiple arrests and refused to work, forcing Marianne to become the sole breadwinner as a journalist and uncertified teacher. He briefly abandoned the family for a woman named [[beverly-st-jacques|Beverly St Jacques]], though the couple later reconciled and moved to Jamestown, North Dakota. His drinking continued; he became violently abusive, breaking both of Marianne's wrists and beating his son, before tearfully apologising: "Why do you make me do this? I don't want to hit you. Why do you make me do it?" Marianne finally divorced him in **1959**. Despite the divorce, O'Neil was completely disowned by his own family; Marianne eventually allowed the "hopeless case" to live with her again, and would often list herself as a widow in local directories to protect them both from credit agencies pursuing his debts.

In 1958, private investigator [[robert-swanson|Robert Swanson]] described O'Neil as a man of medium height and build with reddish hair, a heavy beard growth, and noted he was a drunkard who seldom worked.

## Death

> **Contradiction:** The Borley sources present two completely contradictory accounts of Robert O'Neil's death. The primary historical narrative records that in 1981, following a car crash, O'Neil underwent a routine X-ray which revealed an inoperable tumour; he was given six months to live and died later that year. An anomalous and contradictory account in one source claims that shortly after arriving in America in the 1940s, O'Neil "died trying to save a child in a road accident, leaving Marianne with a baby son, Vernon O'Neil." (This source also incorrectly identifies the son's name as Vernon rather than Vincent.)

## Significance

Robert O'Neil's name became [[vincent-oneil|Vincent]]'s legal name, giving the author his identity as "O'Neil." The family was unaware of Marianne's Borley history; she never discussed it at home.
