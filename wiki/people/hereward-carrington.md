# Hereward Carrington

Hereward Carrington (1880–1958) was an American psychical researcher and prolific author. He is cited twice in the SPR's 1956 Borley report:

1. **As author of *Personal Experiences in Spiritualism*** (London, 1913), which on pp. 95–124 gives a summary of the [[great-amherst-mystery|Amherst affair]] — a copy [[harry-price|Harry Price]] owned, meaning Price had access to detailed comparative material when writing about the supposedly "unique" Borley wall-writings.

2. **As co-author, with [[nandor-fodor|Nandor Fodor]], of *The Story of the Poltergeist down the Centuries*** (London, 1953), which the SPR cites to show that the Amherst case (p. 42) and the Borley case (p. 69) were both still being credited as genuine poltergeist cases in recent popular literature — despite [[walter-prince|Dr Walter F. Prince]]'s detailed analysis arguing for trickery in the Amherst case.

## Significance

The SPR investigators used Carrington's continued credulous treatment of both cases to illustrate that Price's success in establishing Borley as a "classic" was entirely typical: poorly-examined cases, once publicised, become entrenched in the literature regardless of critical analysis. The fact that the Amherst case was "regarded as a classic" (like Borley) because Prince's investigation "never reached the public" is the direct model for how the SPR authors understood Price's success with Borley.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

Based on the provided Borley sources, **Hereward Carrington** does not appear as an active participant or on-site investigator in the Borley Rectory case. Instead, he is recorded as a noted author and authority on psychical research whose published works were referenced by Harry Price, his critics, and later historians to contextualize and debate the haunting. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following facts, publication dates, and specific quotes regarding his indirect involvement in the Borley literature:

**Published Works Consulted in the Borley Case**
The sources record two specific books co-authored or written by Carrington that feature in the Borley bibliography:
*   ***Personal Experiences in Spiritualism* (or *Personal Experiences of Spiritualism*):** Published in London by T. Warner Laurie in **1913** [1, 2]. 
*   ***The Story of the Poltergeist Down the Centuries*:** Co-authored with Nandor Fodor and published in London by Rider in **1953** [1, 3]. 

**Connection to the "Amherst Mystery" and the S.P.R. Report**
Carrington's 1913 book became a piece of supporting evidence for the skeptical authors of the 1956 *Borley Report* (Eric Dingwall, Kathleen Goldney, and Trevor Hall). In their attempt to prove that the famous Borley "wall-writings" were fraudulently produced by Marianne Foyster, the investigators noted the striking similarities between the Borley writings and the sensational "messages" that appeared on the walls during the "Great Amherst Mystery" (the Esther Cox poltergeist case) in Nova Scotia in 1878–1879 [2]. 

The investigators specifically pointed out that Harry Price possessed a copy of Carrington's *Personal Experiences in Spiritualism* in his library, and that **pages 95–124** of Carrington's book provided a detailed summary of the Amherst affair [2]. Because the Foysters had lived in Sackville, Nova Scotia—just five miles from Amherst—immediately prior to moving to Borley, the investigators used Carrington's work to bolster their theory that the Borley phenomena were consciously modeled after the Amherst case [2, 3].

**Specific Quotes By and About Carrington**
*   **Regarding the genuineness of Borley:** The authors of the *Borley Report* cited page 69 of Carrington and Fodor's 1953 book to demonstrate how Harry Price's publicity had successfully cemented the Rectory's reputation. They noted: **"The Borley case is regarded as a classic and is quoted today by writers on the subject as genuine. (See *The Story of the Poltergeist down the Centuries*, by Hereward Carrington and Nandor Fodor, London, 1953, p. 69.)"** [3].
*   **Defining Poltergeists:** A direct quote from Carrington's *The Story of the Poltergeist Down the Centuries* is used as a chapter epigraph in the literature to define the very nature of the phenomena reported at Borley:
> **"Poltergeist cases, so-called, are those in which bells are rung, crockery broken, objects thrown about etc., by (seemingly) some invisible entity. It is a compound German word, meaning ‘noisy spirit’... The question is: Do such phenomena ever happen in a genuine supernormal manner? Or are they invariably due to trickery, illusion and fraud?"** [4].
