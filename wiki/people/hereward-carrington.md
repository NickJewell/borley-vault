# Hereward Carrington (1880–1958)

American psychical researcher and prolific author. He does not appear as an active participant or on-site investigator in the Borley Rectory case. Instead, he is recorded as a noted authority on psychical research whose published works were referenced by [[harry-price|Harry Price]], his critics, and later historians to contextualise and debate the haunting.

## Published Works in the Borley Literature

Two specific books by Carrington feature in the Borley bibliography:

1. ***Personal Experiences in Spiritualism*** (T. Warner Laurie, London, 1913) — pages 95–124 give a summary of the [[great-amherst-mystery|Amherst affair]] (the Esther Cox poltergeist case, Nova Scotia, 1878–79). A copy of this book was found in Price's library [1, 2].

2. ***The Story of the Poltergeist down the Centuries*** (co-authored with [[nandor-fodor|Nandor Fodor]], Rider, London, 1953) — page 42 covers the Amherst case; page 69 credits the Borley case as a genuine poltergeist case [1, 3].

## Significance

The SPR investigators cited Carrington's continued credulous treatment of both cases to illustrate that Price's success in establishing Borley as a "classic" was entirely typical: poorly-examined cases, once publicised, become entrenched in the literature regardless of critical analysis. The SPR authors noted:

> **"The Borley case is regarded as a classic and is quoted today by writers on the subject as genuine. (See *The Story of the Poltergeist down the Centuries*, by Hereward Carrington and Nandor Fodor, London, 1953, p. 69.)"** [3]

The fact that the [[great-amherst-mystery|Amherst case]] was "regarded as a classic" (like Borley) because [[walter-prince|Dr Walter F. Prince]]'s investigation "never reached the public" is the direct model for how the SPR authors understood Price's success with Borley.

Because the Foysters had lived in Sackville, Nova Scotia — just five miles from Amherst — immediately prior to moving to Borley, the SPR investigators used Price's ownership of Carrington's 1913 Amherst summary to bolster their theory that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] consciously modelled the Borley phenomena after the Amherst case [2, 3].

A direct quote from *The Story of the Poltergeist Down the Centuries* appears as a chapter epigraph in the Borley literature, defining the phenomena at issue:

> **"Poltergeist cases, so-called, are those in which bells are rung, crockery broken, objects thrown about etc., by (seemingly) some invisible entity. It is a compound German word, meaning 'noisy spirit'... The question is: Do such phenomena ever happen in a genuine supernormal manner? Or are they invariably due to trickery, illusion and fraud?"** [4]
