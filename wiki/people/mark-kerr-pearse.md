# Mark Kerr-Pearse

**Mark C. M. Kerr-Pearse** (born March 1908 in Kensington; died in the 1970s) was ultimately the most dedicated of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s official observers during the 1937–38 Borley tenancy. He first encountered the investigation through [[harry-price|Price]]'s *Times* advertisement while on holiday in England, describing himself in his May 1937 application as "a bachelor, 29 and independent" [3, 4]. He was out of work at the time, making the prospect of staying at the Rectory for practically nothing highly appealing [4].

Price described him as "a very shrewd observer and a model of what a psychical researcher should be." His minute-by-minute protocols were rated "the most interesting report" of all the observers. In November 1937 he took up a post as pro-consul for the British Foreign Office in Geneva [5–7], and after the Second World War was posted to the British Embassy in Prague [8].

## Time at Borley

First visit **26 June 1937**. Subsequent long stays through **1 November 1937** — more time at the Rectory than any other observer [5, 9]. Price affectionately dubbed him an "open air fiend" because he habitually removed his camp bed from the [[base-room|Base Room]] to sleep in the large summer-house, keeping the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] under constant observation [10, 11]. Although initially disappointed — writing to Price "I had no luck so it is most uninteresting" — he soon became convinced he was witnessing genuine phenomena [4, 10].

He was meticulous: submitting long, minute-by-minute reports, systematically tabulating and chalk-numbering every portable object in the house, and mapping all pre-existing pencil marks on the walls [4, 12, 13]. He personally examined the main well in the courtyard, climbing eight feet down the supply pipe, and investigated a shallow sump in the cellar that he and Price referred to as the "well tank" [14–16].

The only other official observer who spent a night entirely alone was J. W. Burden (December 1937).

## Principal Phenomena Experienced

**Pencil marks appearing**: Subjected the walls to "microscopic scrutiny," checked at 7:25 p.m., returned at 8:30 p.m. and found two new marks not there before. New marks appeared on at least a dozen separate occasions during his stays.

**The moved screw (28 June 1937)**: He placed a screw on the mantelpiece of the Base Room, carefully ringing it with chalk. Just five minutes later, while still in the room, he found the screw had moved outside its chalk ring [17, 18].

**The swishing footsteps (16 July 1937)**: While conducting a table-rapping experiment with [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. and Mrs Henning]], the three heard "a most extraordinary noise" from the kitchen — footsteps accompanied by a swishing sound "as of long trailing garments" — that moved slowly down the passage toward them, ceasing the moment they stood up to investigate [19–22].

**Apports (July and August 1937)**: Two bizarre objects appeared inexplicably in the house. On 18 July, a 3-inch-square piece of rotten "touchwood" was found in the Sewing Room hearth [23, 24]. On 5 August, a dry, flattened, petrified frog appeared in the passage outside the drawing-room [23, 25, 26]. Both doors had been closed overnight and neither object had been there on his evening patrol.

**50-lb coal bag moved 18 inches (21 September 1937)**: Standing on the landing with his cousin [[rupert-haig|Rupert Haig]], both heard a rustling from below. Upon descending, they found a 50-lb sack of coal had moved approximately 18 inches along the passageway [17, 29–31].

**Locked in the Base Room (26 October 1937)**: While having supper alone, Kerr-Pearse heard a small click; when he tried to leave, he found himself locked in. Most remarkably, **the key was on the inside of the lock with him**, meaning whatever locked the door must have been inside the room [6, 19, 32–34].

**"Cold spot" corroboration**: Confirmed that his cousin [[rupert-haig|Rupert Haig]] was affected by sudden inexplicable cold at the same landing spot as other independent witnesses.

**Coat pulled twice (29 October 1937)**: On his last long stay, his coat was pulled twice in quick succession by an invisible agency, with an interval of about fifteen seconds [35, 36].

**The wall writings**: He carefully deciphered one message as "Marianne Get Help — well tank bottom me" and wrote his own message underneath: **"I STILL CANNOT UNDERSTAND PLEASE TELL ME MORE"** [4, 27, 28].

## The BBC "Listener" Dispute

On 21 September 1937, hearing the BBC was planning a series on haunted houses, Kerr-Pearse wrote independently to the Director of Talks offering to share his experiences, stating the results were "certainly both interesting and convincing as conditions have been fully controlled" [37, 38]. Price, who was friends with the BBC editor, intercepted this and wrote Kerr-Pearse a severe rebuke, reminding him that the Declaration Form "absolutely prohibited" such publicity [38–40]. A few weeks later, Price made the Borley broadcast himself [41, 42].

## What Price Suppressed from His Reports

The SPR found that several of Kerr-Pearse's observations were omitted from *The Most Haunted House in England* because they pointed toward normal explanations:

- His June 1937 report noted "a rose tree which was repeatedly blown backwards and forwards against the wall causing knocks which 'might provide an excellent ghost for the imaginative'" — entirely omitted
- He noted "found many traces of mice in cupboards" — omitted (Price stated in MHH that "never once has any observer mentioned rats")
- He first suggested the "Well Tank Bottom Me" reading of the wall-message in July 1937, predating [[canon-phythian-adams|Phythian-Adams's]] "discovery" of the same reading by nearly four years; he also explored the well tank himself in August 1937, finding only old tins and broken glass — both facts omitted by Price, who later presented Phythian-Adams's reading as a revelation

## SPR Assessment and Contradictions

The SPR regarded Kerr-Pearse as one of the two most credible official observers (alongside [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]]) and took his evidence seriously. However, it offered normal explanations for his principal observations:

- **Coal bag moved**: No marks on the floor showed whether the bag was lifted or dragged; insufficient detail to treat as genuinely anomalous [29, 43]
- **Locked in Base Room**: More probably he turned the key himself while supping — a habitual locking action not consciously remembered — than that it was locked paranormally [32]
- **Pencil marks**: Given the evidence for Price adding marks when at the rear of touring parties, and the availability of the house to intruders via the cellar trapdoor, fresh marks cannot be attributed to paranormal agency

Kerr-Pearse was not entirely credulous: he theorised that the famous Borley bell-ringing might be caused by rats or mice running along exposed wires in the attic [44], and suspected that a servant might have written some of the pencil messages [45]. Despite his massive contribution to the files and the SPR's intense efforts to debunk the case, the SPR investigators curiously **never interviewed him** for their critical 1956 report [8] (single omission).

## Reliability

A diplomat, accustomed to precise observation and recording. His long and detailed protocols are written with meticulous care and include appropriate qualifications — including explicit uncertainty about whether phenomena were caused by natural means. The SPR considered his original unpublished reports more reliably sceptical than Price's published versions, which selected only the more dramatic elements.
