# Charles A. Boyden

A man who grew up in the village of Borley in the 1870s and later emigrated to Canada. Writing from Sousana P.O., Alberta, Canada, on October 31, 1929, Boyden contacted the then-Rector [[rev-eric-smith|Rev. G. Eric Smith]] after seeing an account of the Borley haunting in the American press — the first time he had heard of the village since leaving as a child.

## Childhood Memories

Boyden wrote that he remembered "the story being told to me over fifty years ago" — placing his first encounter with the ghost legend at approximately 1878 or 1879, when he was about nine years old. More significantly, he stated that he not only heard the story but "experienced a little of it myself."

He attended Borley Church and Bible classes under the late Rev. [[henry-bull|Henry Bull]], and his mother lived in one of the houses down the lane from Borley Green.

A nine-year-old experiencing paranormal phenomena would have been at exactly the age when such an event would make a "deep and permanent impression on his mind and memory," as Price notes — and Boyden's letter, written fifty years later without foreknowledge of the price investigation, is free of any motivation to fabricate.

## Significance

Boyden's letter is one of Price's key pieces of evidence against the assertion (made by a correspondent to *The Spectator* in 1940) that "concrete manifestations were less than five years old" — i.e., post-1928. Boyden's childhood experiences of around 1878–79 push documented Borley phenomena back to within fifteen years of the Rectory's construction in 1863.

His testimony dovetails with that of [[mrs-byford|Mrs Byford]] (nursemaid 1886) and the Misses Bull's accounts of phenomena during the same era, and with [[p-shaw-jeffrey|P. Shaw Jeffrey]]'s 1885–86 Oxford undergraduate visits, all of which collectively establish that the haunting reputation was not a twentieth-century invention.

Price intended to write to Boyden for more details but, given the letter reached him late, does not record whether further correspondence ensued.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Charles A. Boyden** (also recorded in one source’s chronology as **Charles A. Baydon** [1]) was a former childhood resident of Borley whose 1929 correspondence provided investigators with some of the earliest dated claims of paranormal activity at the Rectory. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, quotes, and events regarding his connection to the case:

**Early Life in Borley**
Boyden was brought up in the village of Borley during the first incumbency of the Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull [2, 3]. His mother lived in one of the three houses down the lane from Borley Green [3]. During his youth, Boyden attended Borley Church and participated in Bible classes taught by the Rev. Bull [1, 3]. He left the village of Borley when he was nine years old [3]. 

**The 1929 Letter from Canada**
Decades later, Boyden emigrated and was living at Sousana P.O., Alberta, Canada [3]. In the autumn of 1929, the sensational reporting of the Borley haunting reached North America, prompting Boyden to reach out to the current rector. On **October 31, 1929**, he wrote a letter to the Rev. Guy Eric Smith [2, 3]. 

The letter contained the following specific quote regarding his memories:
> **"Excuse the liberty I take in writing to you, but when I saw the pictures of the church and Rectory of my childhood days, and reading an account of the ghost story in an American paper, I remember the story being told to me over fifty years ago and I experienced a little of it myself. I was brought up in the village of Borley and went to Borley Church and Bible class, under the late Mr [Henry] Bull... I was nine years old when I left Borley, and have never heard of the dear old village until I saw it in the American paper"** [3].

**Significance to the Investigation**
Boyden's letter was passed to investigator Harry Price (either directly by the Rev. Smith or later contributed to the Borley dossier by his widow, Mrs. Mabel Smith) [2, 3]. 

While Boyden unfortunately did not provide any specific details about what exact phenomena he had personally experienced, Price considered his letter highly significant [2, 4]. Because Boyden stated he heard the ghost stories "over fifty years ago" from the time of his 1929 letter, this securely dated the origins of the haunting to **1878 or 1879**—roughly fifteen years after the Rectory was built [2, 4]. 

Price argued that an unusual paranormal experience would leave a "deep and permanent impression" on a nine-year-old boy's mind, and he used Boyden's testimony as independent documentary confirmation of the Misses Bull's claims that manifestations had occurred during their childhood [4]. Price noted in his second book, *The End of Borley Rectory*, that the letter had only recently come into his hands and that he intended to write back to Boyden in Canada to solicit more details of his psychic experiences, provided the man was still living [4].
