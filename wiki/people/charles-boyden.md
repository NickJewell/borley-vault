# Charles A. Boyden

A man who grew up in the village of Borley in the 1870s and later emigrated to Canada. Writing from Sousana P.O., Alberta, on October 31, 1929, Boyden contacted the then-Rector [[rev-eric-smith|Rev. G. Eric Smith]] after seeing an account of the Borley haunting in the American press — the first time he had heard of the village since leaving as a child. (Also recorded in one source's chronology as **Charles A. Baydon** [1].)

## Childhood Memories

Boyden was brought up in the village of Borley during the first incumbency of the [[henry-bull|Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]]. His mother lived in one of the three houses down the lane from Borley Green [3]. He attended Borley Church and participated in Bible classes taught by Rev. Bull, and left the village at the age of nine [3].

Writing to Rev. Smith, Boyden stated:
> **"Excuse the liberty I take in writing to you, but when I saw the pictures of the church and Rectory of my childhood days, and reading an account of the ghost story in an American paper, I remember the story being told to me over fifty years ago and I experienced a little of it myself. I was brought up in the village of Borley and went to Borley Church and Bible class, under the late Mr [Henry] Bull... I was nine years old when I left Borley, and have never heard of the dear old village until I saw it in the American paper"** [3].

He remembered "the story being told to me over fifty years ago" — placing his first encounter with the ghost legend at approximately 1878 or 1879, when he was about nine years old. More significantly, he stated that he not only heard the story but "experienced a little of it myself," though he did not specify what phenomena he had personally encountered.

## Significance

Boyden's letter was passed to [[harry-price|Harry Price]] (either directly by Rev. Smith or later contributed to the Borley dossier by his widow, [[mabel-smith|Mrs. Mabel Smith]]) [2, 3]. Price considered the letter highly significant [2, 4]. Because Boyden stated he heard the ghost stories "over fifty years ago" from the time of his 1929 letter, this securely dates the origins of the haunting to **1878 or 1879** — roughly fifteen years after the Rectory was built [2, 4].

This testimony is one of Price's key pieces of evidence against the assertion (made by a correspondent to *The Spectator* in 1940) that "concrete manifestations were less than five years old" — i.e., post-1928. Boyden's childhood experiences push documented Borley phenomena back to within fifteen years of the Rectory's construction in 1863.

His testimony dovetails with that of [[mrs-byford|Mrs Byford]] (nursemaid 1886) and the Misses Bull's accounts of phenomena during the same era, and with [[p-shaw-jeffrey|P. Shaw Jeffrey]]'s 1885–86 Oxford undergraduate visits, all of which collectively establish that the haunting reputation was not a twentieth-century invention.

Price intended to write to Boyden for more details but, given the letter reached him late, does not record whether further correspondence ensued.
