# Georgina Dawson

Resident of Leavenheath, near Colchester. A stranger to [[harry-price|Price]] until she wrote to him on April 23, 1943, asking for details of the clothing of the Borley "nun." She had been researching the history of Borley independently. Her eventual dossier — 20,000 words — covered the church, all rectors from 1313, the Rectory, the Waldegrave family, and the Borley estate over several centuries.

## The Waldegrave Research

Dawson traced the Waldegrave family's connexion with Borley from Sir Richard Waldegrave (died 1402) through the extinction of the Borley branch with Philip Waldegrave (died 1720–21), when the estates passed to James, Lord Waldegrave.

Key findings:
- The Waldegrave family seat was **Borley Hall** on the Stour (not on the Rectory site).
- **Borley Place** (the manor house opposite the Rectory, still occupied by the Payne family) was the manor house.
- The Rectory site was always a rectory or chaplain's house — no Waldegrave manor ever stood there.
- From 1662, when Rev. William Playne moved to Langenhoo, the rectory at Borley was effectively vacant or absentee for decades — both Playne and his successor Muriell (1681) were moved to Langenhoo, both in the Waldegrave gift.

## The Arabella Waldegrave Theory

Dawson proposed that the [[the-nun|Borley nun-ghost]] was **Arabella Waldegrave** (born 1687), daughter of Henry, first Baron Waldegrave, and Henrietta FitzJames (natural daughter of James II and Arabella Churchill). Taken to France in 1688, educated at the English Benedictine convent at Pontoise. Her name appears in the convent archives — she left or was dismissed, went to Paris, and became a nun. Then she vanishes completely from all family records.

Dawson's theory: Arabella became a spy or agent, was eventually brought to Borley, and murdered there. This would explain her otherwise inexplicable disappearance from a well-documented noble family.

> **Contradiction:** Canon [[canon-phythian-adams|Phythian-Adams]] demolishes the Arabella theory on linguistic grounds: the Pontoise convent was an English house on French soil; Arabella's education was entirely in English. The wall-messages at Borley include the French word 'trompée' written in the flowing hand of one using her own language. An English-educated woman cannot be the author. The Planchette evidence pointing to "Marie Lairre" from Havre remains the stronger candidate. See [[ebr-ch16]].

Dawson also discovered that Arabella's mother, Henrietta Waldegrave, was expelled from the French Court in 1695 on charges of spying against James II — which may have suggested to Dawson the daughter followed a similar path.
