# Georgina Dawson

**Mrs. Georgina Dawson** was a local historian from Leavenheath, near Colchester in Essex, who became a significant contributor to the historical and genealogical research surrounding the Borley Rectory hauntings [1, 2]. She first became interested in the Rectory story after reading [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s *The Most Haunted House in England* [1], and her work heavily influenced Price's second book, *The End of Borley Rectory*, particularly regarding the history of the Waldegrave family and the identity of the legendary phantom nun.

## The 20,000-Word Dossier

On **April 23, 1943**, despite being a complete stranger to Price, Dawson wrote to him asking for particulars about the phantom nun's clothing, explaining that she had been researching the history of Borley to discover "what caused such powerful effects there" [3]. She subsequently provided Price with a massive **20,000-word dossier** (also described as a 55-page typescript titled *Borley – Waldegrave Records*) [4, 5]. This comprehensive document chronicled the history of Borley, its church, twenty rectors dating back to 1313, the Rectory site, and the influential [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]] [5, 6].

Price publicly praised her research, noting that it was **"as complete as they are interesting"** [7]. Although Dawson intended to publish her work, it remained an unpublished typescript; later historian [[ivan-banks|Ivan Banks]] continued the enquiries she began [4].

## The Waldegrave Research

Dawson traced the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]]'s connexion with Borley from Sir Richard Waldegrave (died 1402) through the extinction of the Borley branch with Philip Waldegrave (died 1720–21), when the estates passed to James, Lord Waldegrave.

Key findings:
- The Waldegrave family seat was **Borley Hall** on the Stour (not on the Rectory site).
- **[[borley-place|Borley Place]]** (the manor house opposite the Rectory, still occupied by the Payne family) was the manor house.
- The Rectory site was always a rectory or chaplain's house — no Waldegrave manor ever stood there.
- From 1662, when Rev. William Playne moved to Langenhoo, the rectory at Borley was effectively vacant or absentee for decades.

Dawson forcefully argued against the traditional legends of a local monastery and a walled-up nun: **"As there was no monastery at Borley nor nunnery at Bures those features of the monk and walled-up-alive legend must be discarded. Although it is not possible to say definitely there was no medieval tragedy with a nun at Borley, I have searched most carefully for records of such an event and found none…"** [12]. She did discover that a Benedictine priory existed across the valley from Borley, on the River Stour, and correctly suggested it had "free warren" within Borley's boundaries — a claim later supported by historical archives [13–15].

## The Arabella Waldegrave Theory

Dawson is best known for formulating a detailed theory that directly challenged the popular belief (championed by [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]]) that the phantom nun was a 17th-century French novice named [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] [3, 8]. Instead, Dawson posited that the ghost was **Arabella Waldegrave** (born 1687), daughter of Henry, first Baron Waldegrave, and Henrietta FitzJames (natural daughter of James II and Arabella Churchill), a grandchild of King James II [3].

Dawson's research traced Arabella's early life: she was taken to France in 1688 and placed in a convent school at Pontoise at the age of seven [8, 9]. Dawson discovered that Arabella did not stay at Pontoise but entered a novitiate in Paris [8]. After her early teen years, all trace of the girl completely evaporated from historical records [9, 10]. Dawson theorised that Arabella became a spy for the Stuart Pretenders or the British government, and that her activities became so dangerous she was murdered at Borley [8, 11]. She also discovered that Arabella's mother, Henrietta Waldegrave, was expelled from the French Court in 1695 on charges of spying against James II — which may have suggested to Dawson that the daughter followed a similar path.

> **Contradiction:** [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] demolishes the Arabella theory on linguistic grounds: the Pontoise convent was an English house on French soil; Arabella's education was entirely in English. The [[wall-writings|wall-messages]] at Borley include the French word *trompée* written in the flowing hand of one using her own language. An English-educated woman cannot be the author. The Planchette evidence pointing to "Marie Lairre" from Havre remains the stronger candidate.

## The 1943 Excavations

Dawson transitioned from historical researcher to firsthand witness when invited to attend [[harry-price|Price]]'s excavations of the ruined Rectory's cellars. She was present on the first day of the dig, **Tuesday, August 17, 1943**, alongside Price, [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Alfred Henning]], [[captain-gregson|Captain W.H. Gregson]], and pathologist [[dr-eric-bailey|Dr. Eric Bailey]] [16–19]. Because she lived locally, she did not require the scarce wartime overnight accommodation needed by some of the other attendees [20].

## Contemporary Opinions

While [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] highly valued her contributions [7, 21], she had severe detractors. The author [[james-turner|James Turner]] (who lived in the [[rectory-cottage|Rectory Cottage]] in the late 1940s) warned [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] bluntly: **"Keep away from her; she has verbal and writing diarrhoea and is quite mad!"** [4].
