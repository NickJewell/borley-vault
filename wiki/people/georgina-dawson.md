# Georgina Dawson

Resident of Leavenheath, near Colchester. A stranger to [[harry-price|Price]] until she wrote to him on April 23, 1943, asking for details of the clothing of the Borley "nun." She had been researching the history of Borley independently. Her eventual dossier — 20,000 words — covered the church, all rectors from 1313, the Rectory, the Waldegrave family, and the Borley estate over several centuries.

## The Waldegrave Research

Dawson traced the Waldegrave family's connexion with Borley from Sir Richard Waldegrave (died 1402) through the extinction of the Borley branch with Philip Waldegrave (died 1720–21), when the estates passed to James, Lord Waldegrave.

Key findings:
- The Waldegrave family seat was **Borley Hall** on the Stour (not on the Rectory site).
- **Borley Place** (the manor house opposite the Rectory, still occupied by the Payne family) was the manor house.
- The Rectory site was always a rectory or chaplain's house — no Waldegrave manor ever stood there.
- From 1662, when Rev. William Playne moved to Langenhoo, the rectory at Borley was effectively vacant or absentee for decades — both Playne and his successor Muriell (1681) were moved to Langenhoo, both in the Waldegrave gift.

## The Arabella Waldegrave Theory

Dawson proposed that the [[the-nun|Borley nun-ghost]] was **Arabella Waldegrave** (born 1687), daughter of Henry, first Baron Waldegrave, and Henrietta FitzJames (natural daughter of James II and Arabella Churchill). Taken to France in 1688, educated at the English Benedictine convent at Pontoise. Her name appears in the convent archives — she left or was dismissed, went to Paris, and became a nun. Then she vanishes completely from all family records.

Dawson's theory: Arabella became a spy or agent, was eventually brought to Borley, and murdered there. This would explain her otherwise inexplicable disappearance from a well-documented noble family.

> **Contradiction:** Canon [[canon-phythian-adams|Phythian-Adams]] demolishes the Arabella theory on linguistic grounds: the Pontoise convent was an English house on French soil; Arabella's education was entirely in English. The wall-messages at Borley include the French word 'trompée' written in the flowing hand of one using her own language. An English-educated woman cannot be the author. The Planchette evidence pointing to "Marie Lairre" from Havre remains the stronger candidate. See [[ebr-ch16]].

Dawson also discovered that Arabella's mother, Henrietta Waldegrave, was expelled from the French Court in 1695 on charges of spying against James II — which may have suggested to Dawson the daughter followed a similar path.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mrs. Georgina Dawson** was a local historian from Leavenheath, near Colchester in Essex, who became a significant contributor to the historical and genealogical research surrounding the Borley Rectory hauntings [1, 2]. Her work heavily influenced Harry Price’s second book on the case, *The End of Borley Rectory*, particularly regarding the history of the Waldegrave family and the identity of the legendary phantom nun [1, 2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, theories, quotes, and lesser-known details regarding her involvement:

**Initial Involvement and the 20,000-Word Dossier**
Mrs. Dawson first became interested in the Rectory story after reading Harry Price's *The Most Haunted House in England* [1]. On **April 23, 1943**, despite being a complete stranger to Price, she wrote to him asking for particulars about the phantom nun's clothing, explaining that she had been researching the history of Borley to discover "what caused such powerful effects there" [3]. 

She subsequently provided Price with a massive **20,000-word dossier** (also described as a 55-page typescript titled *Borley – Waldegrave Records*) [4, 5]. This comprehensive document chronicled the history of Borley, its church, twenty rectors dating back to 1313, the Rectory site, and the influential Waldegrave family [5, 6]. Although Dawson intended to publish her work, it remained an unpublished typescript, though later historian Ivan Banks would continue the enquiries she began [4]. Price publicly praised her research, noting that it was **"as complete as they are interesting"** [7].

**The Arabella Waldegrave Theory**
Dawson is best known for formulating a detailed theory that directly challenged the popular belief (championed by Canon W.J. Phythian-Adams) that the phantom nun was a 17th-century French novice named Marie Lairre [3, 8]. Instead, Dawson posited that the ghost was an obscure member of the local Waldegrave family [2, 8]. 

In her initial letter to Price, she stated: **"My inquiries are not yet complete, but I feel I am on the right lines and that the lady [the nun-ghost] was Arabella Waldegrave, born in 1687, daughter of Henry, first Lord Waldegrave and Henrietta FitzJames, and a grandchild of King James II"** [3]. 

Dawson's research traced Arabella's early life: she was taken to France in 1688 and placed in a convent school at Pontoise at the age of seven [8, 9]. Dawson discovered that Arabella did not stay at Pontoise but entered a novitiate in Paris [8]. After her early teen years, all trace of the girl completely evaporated from historical records [9, 10]. Dawson theorized that Arabella became a spy for the Stuart Pretenders or the British government, and that her activities became so dangerous she was murdered at Borley [8, 11].

**Dispelling the Monastery Legend and Other Historical Finds**
Dawson forcefully argued against the traditional legends of a local monastery and a walled-up nun. She is quoted directly on this matter:
> **"As there was no monastery at Borley nor nunnery at Bures those features of the monk and walled-up-alive legend must be discarded. Although it is not possible to say definitely there was no medieval tragedy with a nun at Borley, I have searched most carefully for records of such an event and found none … This seems much the most likely time to have contained a disaster powerful enough to cause the Borley hauntings"** [12].

Despite dismissing the Borley monastery, she did discover that a Benedictine priory existed across the valley from Borley, on the River Stour, and correctly suggested it had "free warren" (access to woodland) within Borley's boundaries—a claim later supported by historical archives [13-15].

**The 1943 Excavations**
Dawson transitioned from a historical researcher to a firsthand witness when she was invited to attend Harry Price's excavations of the ruined Rectory's cellars. She was present on the first day of the dig, **Tuesday, August 17, 1943**, alongside Price, the Rev. Alfred Henning, Captain W.H. Gregson, and pathologist Dr. Eric Bailey [16-19]. Because she lived locally, she did not require the scarce wartime overnight accommodation needed by some of the other attendees [20].

**Contemporary Opinions**
While Harry Price and Canon Phythian-Adams highly valued her contributions to the case [7, 21], she had severe detractors among others connected to Borley. The author James Turner (who lived in the Rectory Cottage in the late 1940s) held a highly unfavorable view of her, bluntly warning investigator Peter Underwood: **"Keep away from her; she has verbal and writing diarrhoea and is quite mad!"** [4].
