# Constance Brackenbury

The stepdaughter of the [[harry-bull|Rev Harry Foyster Bull]], acquired when he married [[ivy-brackenbury|Ivy Brackenbury]] in 1911. She lived at Borley Rectory during the 1920s and is remembered by Margaret Finch, who worked there as a resident maid.

## Background

Constance was the daughter of Ivy Brackenbury from a previous relationship (Ivy was a widow when she married Harry Bull). She became known to the servants at Borley Rectory as "Miss Constance," which caused some confusion, as Harry Bull's sister of the same name was also addressed as "Miss Constance."

## Life at Borley Rectory

According to Margaret Finch's testimony, Constance and her stepfather did not like each other at all. She would make faces at him when his back was turned. On one occasion, in his absence, she picked up one of his waistcoats with tongs, implying it was not fit to be touched with bare hands — a gesture that amused the servants greatly.

Constance worked as a dressmaker. Although she had her own rooms at the rectory, she was frequently absent, spending much of her time visiting a Mrs Mansfield at Eyston Hall, a large Georgian house just outside the western boundary of Borley parish.

## Significance

Constance Brackenbury is one of several figures whose testimony about daily life at [[borley-rectory]] during the 1920s has been preserved through [[margaret-finch|Margaret Finch's]] accounts. Her relationship with Harry Bull sheds light on the domestic tensions within the household in his final years.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Constance Brackenbury** was the daughter of Ivy Johnson Brackenbury from her first marriage to Harold Brackenbury, an electrical engineer [1]. Born on October 7, 1901, she became the stepdaughter of the Rev. Henry "Harry" Foyster Bull when he married her mother on September 12, 1911 [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following specific facts, events, contradictions, and lesser-known details about her life and her connection to the hauntings:

**Life at Borley and a Hostile Relationship**
Constance's relationship with her stepfather, the Rev. Harry Bull, was exceptionally hostile. The sources state that Constance "hated her new step-father like poison," and the animosity was mutual; Harry Bull allegedly struck her on more than one occasion [2, 3]. 

Because of this strained relationship, she was sent away to a Roman Catholic convent school in Surrey [3]. Even when she was home at the Rectory, Harry Bull "would not allow \[her] to live in family with them," and she was confined to her own separate apartment in the schoolroom wing [4, 5]. Constance was a Roman Catholic, and researchers suggest this religious difference may have further exacerbated tensions with her Anglican stepfather [2]. 

**Lesser-Known Quirks and Rebellions**
The sources record several charming and petty rebellions Constance and her mother conducted against the Rector:
*   **The Stolen Cream:** Constance and her mother would secretly skim the cream from the house cow's milk and hide it in the cellar—specifically in direct line with the infamous "Cold Spot"—so that Harry Bull would not get his share [6]. 
*   **The Waistcoat and the Tongs:** Constance made no secret of her disdain for her stepfather in front of the servants. She used to make faces at him behind his back, and on one occasion, she amused the maids by picking up one of his waistcoats with a pair of tongs, implying it was not fit to be touched with bare hands [7].
*   **The Name Confusion:** To the slight confusion of the domestic staff, she was referred to as "Miss Constance," which led to mix-ups, as one of Harry Bull's many sisters was also named Constance [8].
*   **Later Career:** She eventually worked as a dressmaker and spent a great deal of her time visiting a Mrs. Mansfield at nearby Eyston Hall [1, 9].

**The Age Contradiction Regarding Her Marriage**
There is a glaring chronological and mathematical contradiction in the texts regarding her marriage. Multiple sources explicitly state that Constance "married, at eighteen, a Colonel Boothby who was considerably older than herself" [10, 11]. 

However, the specific dates provided in the records make this impossible. The texts state she was born on October 7, 1901, and was married to Colonel Boothby at All Saints Church, St Johns Wood, London, on February 21, 1938 [1, 12]. Therefore, she would have been 36 years old at the time of her wedding, not eighteen [1, 12]. She eventually outlived her husband, moving to South Africa where she died in 1970 [1].

**Paranormal Connections and Theories**
Despite living in the Rectory from 1920 until her stepfather's death in 1927, it is specifically recorded that neither Constance nor her mother ever witnessed any of the classic paranormal phenomena during their residency [1, 13]. 

However, Constance features prominently in several theories regarding the origins of the Borley hauntings:
*   **Hidden Catholic Artifacts:** Because of her tense relationship with her stepfather, it has been theorized that Constance might have secretly hidden Catholic symbols—such as the French Revolution medallion discovered in 1929—around the house to avoid provoking him [2, 14].
*   **The Séance Entity:** During the planchette séances of 1937, researchers evaluating the confused messages noted that the communicating entity strongly suggested the personality of Miss Brackenbury, theorizing that her profound unhappiness at Borley left a residual, entrenched atmosphere in the house [15, 16]. 
*   **Solving the Skull Mystery:** Constance posthumously solved one of Borley's most gruesome mysteries. In a 1945 interview with investigator Norah Walrond in Cheltenham, Constance explained that the infamous "skull in the cupboard" (which had allegedly terrorized the Smiths) was simply a skull that had been turned up by gravediggers and sent by parcel post to her mother Ivy after Harry's death; Ivy had merely returned the package to the Rectory with instructions for it to be reburied in the churchyard [17].
