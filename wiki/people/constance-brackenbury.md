# Constance Brackenbury

The stepdaughter of [[harry-bull|Rev Harry Foyster Bull]], acquired when he married [[ivy-brackenbury|Ivy Brackenbury]] in 1911. Born October 7, 1901, she was the daughter of Ivy Brackenbury from her first marriage to Harold Brackenbury, an electrical engineer [1]. She lived at Borley Rectory during the 1920s and is remembered by [[margaret-finch|Margaret Finch]], who worked there as a resident maid.

## Hostile Relationship with Harry Bull

Constance's relationship with her stepfather was exceptionally hostile — the sources state that she "hated her new step-father like poison," and the animosity was mutual; Harry Bull allegedly struck her on more than one occasion [2, 3]. Because of this strained relationship, she was sent away to a Roman Catholic convent school in Surrey [3]. Even when she was home at the Rectory, Harry Bull "would not allow [her] to live in family with them," and she was confined to her own separate apartment in the schoolroom wing [4, 5]. Constance was a Roman Catholic, and researchers suggest this religious difference may have further exacerbated tensions with her Anglican stepfather [2].

## Life at Borley Rectory

To the slight confusion of the domestic staff, Constance was referred to as "Miss Constance," which led to mix-ups, as one of Harry Bull's many sisters was also named Constance [8].

The sources record several of Constance's petty rebellions against the Rector:

- **The Stolen Cream:** Constance and her mother would secretly skim the cream from the house cow's milk and hide it in the cellar — specifically in direct line with the infamous "Cold Spot" — so that Harry Bull would not get his share [6].
- **The Waistcoat and the Tongs:** She used to make faces at her stepfather behind his back, and on one occasion amused the maids by picking up one of his waistcoats with a pair of tongs, implying it was not fit to be touched with bare hands [7].

Constance worked as a dressmaker and spent a great deal of her time visiting a Mrs. Mansfield at nearby Eyston Hall, a large Georgian house just outside the western boundary of Borley parish [1, 9].

## The Age Contradiction Regarding Her Marriage

> **Contradiction:** Multiple sources state that Constance "married, at eighteen, a [[colonel-boothby|Colonel Boothby]] who was considerably older than herself" [10, 11]. However, the specific dates make this impossible: she was born on October 7, 1901, and was married to Colonel Boothby at All Saints Church, St Johns Wood, London, on February 21, 1938 [1, 12] — making her 36 years old at the time of her wedding, not eighteen. She eventually outlived her husband, moving to South Africa where she died in 1970 [1].

## Paranormal Connections and Theories

Despite living in the Rectory from 1920 until her stepfather's death in 1927, it is specifically recorded that neither Constance nor her mother ever witnessed any of the classic paranormal phenomena during their residency [1, 13].

However, Constance features in several theories regarding the origins of the Borley hauntings:

- **Hidden Catholic Artifacts:** Because of her tense relationship with her stepfather, it has been theorised that Constance might have secretly hidden Catholic symbols — such as the French Revolution medallion discovered in 1929 — around the house to avoid provoking him [2, 14].
- **The Séance Entity:** During the planchette séances of 1937, researchers evaluating the confused messages noted that the communicating entity strongly suggested the personality of Miss Brackenbury, theorising that her profound unhappiness at Borley left a residual, entrenched atmosphere in the house [15, 16].
- **Solving the Skull Mystery:** Constance posthumously solved one of Borley's most gruesome mysteries. In a 1945 interview with investigator [[norah-walrond|Norah Walrond]] in Cheltenham, Constance explained that the infamous "skull in the cupboard" (which had allegedly terrorised the Smiths) was simply a skull turned up by gravediggers and sent by parcel post to her mother Ivy after Harry's death; Ivy had merely returned the package to the Rectory with instructions for it to be reburied in the churchyard [17].
