# Lady Frances Waldegrave

Wife of [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Sir Edward Waldegrave]], Lord of the Manor of Borley; arrested with her husband in April 1561 and interrogated throughout June and July of that year on charges of harbouring the forbidden Catholic Mass.

## Background

Lady Frances was present at Borley Hall when the priest [[john-cox-devon|John Cox / John Devon]] stayed for a month and celebrated the Catholic Mass. During this period she gave Cox **a book of the Sarum liturgy** — the old liturgical form previously used at Salisbury — which was itself an illegal item under Elizabethan Protestant law.

## Arrest and Interrogation

The *Calendars of State Papers Domestic* record that following the betrayal of Cox/Devon and the arrest of Sir Edward, Frances Waldegrave was interrogated alongside her husband at the Commission of Oyer held at Brentwood, Essex, before the Earl of Oxford on June 3, 1561. The interrogations continued through July 1561.

Banks does not provide details of what Frances said under interrogation, or what happened to her after Sir Edward's death in the Tower on September 1, 1561. He notes only the fact of her arrest and her role in providing the Sarum liturgy book to Cox.

## Significance

Lady Frances represents the collateral cost of Sir Edward's religious convictions and political activities. Her gift of the Sarum liturgy book is one of the concrete details from Cox's confession that corroborates the account of organized Catholic worship at Borley Hall.

> **Contested:** Banks notes that the precise cause of Sir Edward's death — illness vs. execution vs. torture — remains unresolved. Lady Frances's own fate after 1561 is not recorded in the sources Banks consulted.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Lady Frances Waldegrave (née Neville)** was the daughter of Sir Edward Neville and the wife of the prominent Tudor politician and staunch Catholic, Sir Edward Waldegrave [1, 2]. Together, the couple had three sons and three daughters [1, 3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, historical events, and quotes regarding her life and legacy:

**Religious Persecution and Imprisonments**
As devout Roman Catholics during the turbulent Reformation, Frances and her husband faced severe persecution from the Protestant establishment. 
*   **The First Imprisonment (1551):** When Sir Edward was sent to the Tower of London in August 1551 for permitting the celebration of Mass in Princess Mary Tudor’s household, he fell ill during his incarceration. As a result, **Frances was allowed to join him in the Tower** to be with him [4]. 
*   **The Second Imprisonment (1561):** Following the accession of Queen Elizabeth I, the Waldegraves continued to illegally practice their faith. After a spy reported that a Mass had been held at Borley Hall, **Frances, her husband, and their family were arrested on April 20, 1561** [4]. The formal interrogation of both Frances and Edward went on throughout June and July of 1561 [5]. 

**The Sarum Liturgy Book**
During the 1561 investigation, a Papist priest named John Cox (alias John Devon) confessed to the authorities that he had spent a month at Borley Hall. He provided a specific piece of evidence against Frances, testifying that he had **received from Lady Frances Waldegrave "a book of the Sarum liturgy, which was the form formerly used at Salisbury"** [6, 7].

**Her Second Marriage**
Sir Edward Waldegrave died a prisoner in the Tower on September 1, 1561 [1, 8]. Frances survived him by thirty-eight years, eventually taking a second husband. The sources record the lesser-known biographical detail that she married **Chedick Paulet**, the Governor of Southampton and the third son of William Paulet, first Marquis of Winchester [1]. The texts note that Paulet was **"an even more fervent Roman Catholic than her first"** husband [9, 10]. 

**Death and the Waldegrave Monument**
Frances died at the age of 70 on **October 18, 1599** [8, 11]. She is memorialized alongside Sir Edward in the magnificent 14-foot-high clunch and marble altar-tomb located in the northeast corner of the nave of Borley Church [12, 13]. The Paulet family arms are also featured on the monument [1].

Her painted, recumbent effigy is described in the sources in fine detail. She is depicted lying beside her first husband in a **"tight-fitting bodice and skirt, a flat cap on her head, wearing a large ruff"** [3, 12, 13]. A highly specific detail of her effigy is that there is **a squirrel at her feet, which served as the "emblem of thrift"** [3, 12].

The translated Latin inscription carved into the black marble edge of the tomb's canopy bears this specific quote regarding her death and resting place:
> **"Frances died in the year of our Lord One Thousand Five Hundred and Ninety Nine, seventy years of age, on the Eighteenth day of October. Lo! within this tomb lies Edward Waldegrave and with him Frances, formerly the companion of his bed, but now of his grave. A family untouched by misfortune."** [8, 11]
