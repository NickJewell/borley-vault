# Lady Frances Waldegrave (née Neville, d. 1599)

Wife of [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Sir Edward Waldegrave]], Lord of the Manor of Borley; daughter of Sir Edward Neville. Together, the couple had three sons and three daughters. Arrested with her husband in April 1561 and interrogated throughout June and July of that year on charges of harbouring the forbidden Catholic Mass.

## Religious Persecution and Early Imprisonment

As devout Roman Catholics during the turbulent Reformation, Frances and her husband faced severe persecution from the Protestant establishment.

**The First Imprisonment (1551):** When Sir Edward was sent to the Tower of London in August 1551 for permitting the celebration of Mass in Princess Mary Tudor's household, he fell ill during his incarceration. As a result, Frances was allowed to join him in the Tower to be with him.

## Arrest and Interrogation (1561)

Following the accession of Queen Elizabeth I, the Waldegraves continued to practise their faith illegally. After a spy reported that a Mass had been held at [[borley-place|Borley Hall]], Frances, her husband, and their family were arrested on **April 20, 1561**. The formal interrogation of both Frances and Edward before the Earl of Oxford at the Commission of Oyer held at Brentwood, Essex, went on throughout June and July of 1561.

## The Sarum Liturgy Book

During the 1561 investigation, a Papist priest named [[john-cox-devon|John Cox]] (alias John Devon) confessed that he had spent a month at Borley Hall. He provided a specific piece of evidence against Frances, testifying that he had **received from Lady Frances Waldegrave "a book of the Sarum liturgy, which was the form formerly used at Salisbury"** — itself an illegal item under Elizabethan Protestant law.

## Later Life and Second Marriage

[[sir-edward-waldegrave|Sir Edward Waldegrave]] died a prisoner in the Tower on September 1, 1561. Frances survived him by thirty-eight years. She eventually took a second husband: **Chedick Paulet**, the Governor of Southampton and the third son of William Paulet, first Marquis of Winchester. Paulet was reportedly "an even more fervent Roman Catholic than her first" husband.

Frances died at the age of 70 on **October 18, 1599**.

## The Waldegrave Monument

Frances is memorialized alongside Sir Edward in the magnificent 14-foot-high clunch and marble altar-tomb located in the northeast corner of the nave of [[borley-church|Borley Church]]. The Paulet family arms are also featured on the monument.

Her painted, recumbent effigy depicts her lying beside her first husband in a **"tight-fitting bodice and skirt, a flat cap on her head, wearing a large ruff."** A highly specific detail is that **a squirrel appears at her feet, serving as the "emblem of thrift."**

The translated Latin inscription carved into the black marble edge of the tomb's canopy reads:

> **"Frances died in the year of our Lord One Thousand Five Hundred and Ninety Nine, seventy years of age, on the Eighteenth day of October. Lo! within this tomb lies Edward Waldegrave and with him Frances, formerly the companion of his bed, but now of his grave. A family untouched by misfortune."**

## Significance

Lady Frances represents the collateral cost of Sir Edward's religious convictions. Her gift of the Sarum liturgy book is one of the concrete details from Cox's confession that corroborates the account of organised Catholic worship at Borley Hall.

> **Contested:** The precise cause of Sir Edward's death — illness vs. execution vs. torture — remains unresolved. Lady Frances's own fate between 1561 and her 1599 death is not fully recorded in the sources consulted.
