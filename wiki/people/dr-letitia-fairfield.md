# Dr Letitia Fairfield, CBE, MD, DPH

A barrister-at-law and medical doctor — notably **one of the first qualified female doctors in Britain** [3] — who served as **Senior Medical Officer of the London County Council** [1, 2]. A member of the [[ghost-club|Ghost Club]], she participated in the May 1960 Ghost Club discussion of the Borley case with [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]]. Her combination of medical and legal qualifications gave her a distinctly forensic approach to evidence.

## The 1939 Correspondence and Critique of the Borley Legend

Following a lantern lecture on Borley Rectory given by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] to the Ghost Club on 18 April 1939, which was subsequently reported in *The Times*, Dr. Fairfield initiated a public correspondence challenging the historical validity of the famous Borley legends [2, 3].

She astutely pointed out major anachronisms and myths within the story:
- The presence of a convent and a nun placed the tale before the Reformation, yet **coaches were unknown in England until after 1550** [3].
- She stated that the idea of **bricking up individuals alive as a punishment had long been proved to be mythical** [3, 4].
- She concluded humorously that the doomed monk and nun "seem to have been singularly ill-fitted for the religious life" [3].

In an amusing and sarcastic remark regarding Price's findings, she expressed fear that the "unscrupulous phantoms" had been pulling Price's leg, protesting that it was unthinkable that Price "has been pulling ours" [3].

Price responded to what he called her "good-natured criticisms" by claiming she had jumped to conclusions, asserting that the legend was not his own invention and that he did not actually believe in spirits [3, 4]. He did, however, concede that Dr. Fairfield was "probably right about the bricking-up of nuns, and undoubtedly right about there being no 'black coaches'" at the time the supposed monastery flourished [4].

## Ghost Club Discussion (11 May 1960)

On **11 May 1960**, Fairfield participated in a Ghost Club discussion about the Borley haunting and the [[lionel-foyster|Foyster]] occupancy in particular, alongside [[miss-christina-hole|Miss Christina Hole]] and [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]].

Fairfield "seemed to be impressed with the critics' case" and cited what she described as "some unpublished evidence regarding Marianne." This mainly involved irregularities in [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]]'s various marriages [5]. She agreed with [[miss-christina-hole|Miss Hole]] that the sceptics' case was largely concerned with putting [[harry-price|Harry Price]] into the dock as "a sort of secondary or unconscious accomplice of Marianne."

When her opponents argued that Marianne's marital irregularities made no difference to the reality of the Borley hauntings, Dr. Fairfield firmly countered that "it certainly made a difference to the acceptability of the Foysters' evidence" [5] — a position consistent with the [[the-borley-report|SPR Report]]'s sceptical framework.

## Assessment

Underwood and Tabori recorded her position without endorsing it, concluding: "by and large, the 'mysteries of Marianne' were recorded without much reliance being placed on them — they form part of the immensely varied and rich human tapestry that is the background of Borley."

Dr. Fairfield is also recorded as having attended an extensive 100-slide lecture on the Borley haunting delivered by [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] at Essex Hall in London [6].
