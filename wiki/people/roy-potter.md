# Roy Potter

An associate of [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth]], described by him as his "Chief Investigator" in the work their group undertook on the former [[borley-rectory|Rectory]] site and in [[borley-church]].

## Phantom Nun Sighting (1970)

Potter is best known for co-witnessing one of the most famous post-war sightings of the [[the-nun|phantom nun]] on 20 June 1970. According to the account:

Potter and colleague Frank Connell were on the roadway between the church and the [[rectory-cottage]] while [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth]] was keeping watch in the back garden of a bungalow on part of the old [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]]. At 1:50 a.m., Croom-Hollingsworth saw a figure proceed across the gardens toward the cottage. He contacted his colleagues; the figure then turned and moved back toward the bungalows. Potter, running down the road, saw what he took to be two people near each other — one was Croom-Hollingsworth and the other was the ghost.

Potter and Connell felt intense coldness when the ghostly figure was under observation. The nun turned, glided toward the field behind the old Rectory grounds, and vanished. She had been under observation for what the investigators claimed was about twelve minutes — one of the longest sightings ever recorded in the case.

Potter's account of the same sighting: during a subsequent investigation he claimed to have thrown a brick through the apparition of the phantom nun.

## Investigations

Potter and Croom-Hollingsworth visited Borley on numerous occasions between 1970 and 1973, employing infra-red photography, walkie-talkie sets and sound-recording apparatus. They were interviewed by [[peter-underwood]] in June and July 1972.

## Assessment

[[tony-broughall]] became sceptical of both Potter's and Croom-Hollingsworth's experiences, particularly the long duration of the nun sighting (which changed between versions from 8 to 12 minutes) and the authenticity of the audio recordings made by [[denny-densham]]. The description of the nun as appearing "elderly" (in her sixties) also conflicts with most earlier accounts which describe a younger woman.
