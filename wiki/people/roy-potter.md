# Roy Potter

An associate of [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth]], described by him as his "Chief Investigator" in the work their group undertook on the former [[borley-rectory|Rectory]] site and in [[borley-church]].

## Phantom Nun Sighting (1970)

Potter is best known for co-witnessing one of the most famous post-war sightings of the [[the-nun|phantom nun]] on 20 June 1970. According to the account:

Potter and colleague Frank Connell were on the roadway between the church and the [[rectory-cottage]] while [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth]] was keeping watch in the back garden of a bungalow on part of the old [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]]. At 1:50 a.m., Croom-Hollingsworth saw a figure proceed across the gardens toward the cottage. He contacted his colleagues; the figure then turned and moved back toward the bungalows. Potter, running down the road, saw what he took to be two people near each other — one was Croom-Hollingsworth and the other was the ghost.

Potter and Connell felt intense coldness when the ghostly figure was under observation. The nun turned, glided toward the field behind the old Rectory grounds, and vanished. She had been under observation for what the investigators claimed was about twelve minutes — one of the longest sightings ever recorded in the case.

Potter's account of the same sighting: during a subsequent investigation he claimed to have thrown a brick through the apparition of the phantom nun.

## Investigations

Potter and Croom-Hollingsworth visited Borley on numerous occasions between 1970 and 1973, employing infra-red photography, walkie-talkie sets and sound-recording apparatus. They were interviewed by [[peter-underwood]] in June and July 1972.

## Assessment

[[tony-broughall]] became sceptical of both Potter's and Croom-Hollingsworth's experiences, particularly the long duration of the nun sighting (which changed between versions from 8 to 12 minutes) and the authenticity of the audio recordings made by [[denny-densham]]. The description of the nun as appearing "elderly" (in her sixties) also conflicts with most earlier accounts which describe a younger woman.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Roy Potter** was a prominent lay investigator active at Borley during the early 1970s. He was a leading member of a Harlow-based psychical research group and served as the close associate of its founder, Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth, who referred to Potter as his **"Chief Investigator"** (mirroring the title Harry Price gave to Sidney Glanville) [1-3].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific investigations, events, and a notable contradiction regarding his experiences:

**Investigation Methods and Equipment (1970–1973)**
*   Between **1970 and 1973**, Potter and his colleagues conducted a series of protracted, overnight vigils at the former Rectory site and Borley Church [4-6].
*   The group utilized what was then modern technology, including **delicate scientific instruments, infra-red cameras, tape recorders, and short-range walkie-talkie sets** [3, 4, 7, 8].
*   During a typical vigil, their six-man team would divide into pairs, placing two men on the Rectory site, two on the roadway, and two in the churchyard, rotating every two hours and communicating via radio [3, 8].

**Sightings of the Phantom Nun (June 20, 1970)**
*   Potter was involved in one of the most detailed modern sightings of the Borley nun. On the night of **June 20, 1970**, after seeing a mysterious light in the chancel window of Borley Church, Potter and investigator Frank Connell were patrolling the roadway [4, 9].
*   At 1:50 a.m., Croom-Hollingsworth radioed that he was watching an apparition approach his position in the back garden of a bungalow built on the former Nun's Walk [4, 9].
*   Potter ran down the road to join him and clearly saw two figures standing about twenty feet away: Croom-Hollingsworth and a motionless figure draped from head to foot in grey material [4, 10].
*   As Potter watched, the figure turned, **walked straight through a fence**, crossed a ditch, and vanished into a pile of rubble [10].

**The "Flying Brick" Contradiction**
Potter's most sensational claim is that he physically interacted with the phantom nun by throwing a projectile at it. Wondering if the figure was a human masquerading as a ghost, **Potter threw a brick at the apparition; the object went clean through the figure, which remained completely visible and unmoved** [11].
*   **Contradiction:** The sources provide conflicting dates for this spectacular event. One entry states this occurred during an encounter in **1970** [2], while another explicitly records that the brick-throwing incident took place on a bright moonlit night in **early 1972** [11, 12].

**Auditory Phenomena and the Car Experiment**
*   During a misty night vigil at 3:00 a.m., the group heard the sounds of "laughter and merriment" resembling a late-night party coming from the lower end of Hall Lane [3, 13].
*   To investigate without warning the presumed revellers, Potter **coasted his car down the hill with the engine switched off**, but found no one there [3, 13].
*   To test the acoustics of the area, Potter drove out onto the Long Melford road and shouted at various intervals; his colleagues back at the site reported via walkie-talkie that they could only hear his voice when he was relatively near, leaving the source of the party sounds a mystery [3, 13, 14].

**Interviews and Documentaries**
Potter provided independent written reports of his experiences to Peter Underwood and was personally interviewed by him in **June and July of 1972** [2, 11]. He also featured in a television documentary about Borley, in which he and Croom-Hollingsworth described their extensive visits and visual encounters with the apparition [12].
