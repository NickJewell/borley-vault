# Harry Marshall

Undergraduate at Trinity College, Oxford; son of Mr. J. R. Marshall, K.C., D.L., a distinguished Scottish jurist. Visited the [[borley-rectory|Borley ruins]] with [[jh-russell-oxford|J. H. Russell]] from January 3 to 6, 1944 — one of the last substantial investigations before demolition work proceeded further.

## January 1944 Visit

The primary purpose of the visit was photographic: Marshall obtained **fifty photographs** of the ruins, some by flashlight in the cellars where daylight now penetrated following the fire damage [1]. His photographic work was exceptionally well-timed, as the demolition of the Rectory ruins had already begun in a modest way by the time they arrived. Marshall's photographs are reproduced in *The End of Borley Rectory*.

Because the pair experienced delays in securing permission from the site's new owners, the two students actually spent a total of only **ten hours** on the premises across the stay [1].

## Phenomena

Four incidents were recorded during the visit:

1. **The displaced camera bag (Tuesday, January 4)**: The students left a bag containing their photographic accessories on a specific shelf in the summer-house. When they returned at 12:25 p.m., the bag was missing; they subsequently found it lying on the grass behind the summer-house [3]. Because the bag had not been under direct observation, they acknowledged the possibility that it had been moved by a mischievous living trespasser.

2. **A crash in the cellar (Tuesday morning, January 4)**: Both Marshall and Russell heard a crash in a cellar below them; a piece of iron gutter appeared to have been struck by a falling brick, though neither was directly above or near any such objects at the time.

3. **Phantom voice (January 5, 5:25 p.m.)**: Russell was walking near the stream in the orchard when he was certain he heard Marshall call to him. Marshall was standing about fifteen yards away on a cart-track and had not spoken. In his independent report, Marshall confirmed the incident with the following quote:

   > **"On Wednesday evening Russell was certain I called him. I did not. At the time (5:25 p.m.) he claimed to have recognized my voice. When he knew I had not called him he naturally began to doubt whether he really had heard a voice. He was by the north-east end of the stream in the garden; I was fifteen yards away on the path through the orchard from the road to the fields."** [6]

   Russell had been near the stream on the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] when he heard what he took to be Marshall's voice. Price notes this is consistent with multiple previous "voice" phenomena at Borley.

4. **Figure in the field (January 5, 5:42 p.m.)**: Seventeen minutes after the voice incident, Marshall was standing by the summer-house opposite the Nun's Walk when he reported a visual anomaly. Demonstrating a cautious and critical mindset, he noted:

   > **"Later, at 5:42, while standing by the summer-house (opposite the Nun's Walk), I turned round and thought I saw someone in the corner of the field. But it is easy to imagine things when turning suddenly and when it is beginning to get dark."** [3]

   The two undergraduates also recorded other minor incidents they classified as "doubtful," including thinking they heard someone moving inside the ruins of the Rectory [3].

## Significance

Marshall's fifty photographs are among the last systematic photographic records of the Rectory structure before its complete demolition. His calm, cautious reporting style — explicitly noting the possibility of imagination in his visual impression — gives the auditory phantom-voice incident greater credibility by contrast.
