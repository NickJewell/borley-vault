# Harry Marshall

Undergraduate at Trinity College, Oxford; son of J. R. Marshall, K.C., D.L., a distinguished Scottish jurist. Visited the Borley ruins with [[jh-russell-oxford|J. H. Russell]] from January 3 to 6, 1944 — one of the last substantial investigations before demolition work proceeded further.

## January 1944 Visit

The primary purpose of the visit was photographic: Marshall obtained fifty photographs of the ruins, some by flashlight in the cellars where daylight now penetrated. Marshall's photographs are reproduced in *The End of Borley Rectory*.

The visit lasted approximately ten hours on the premises across the stay, constrained by the need to obtain permission (the Rectory had just changed hands) and by demolition work already underway.

## Phenomena

Two incidents were recorded:

1. **Crash in cellar (Tuesday morning, January 4)**: Both Marshall and Russell heard a crash in a cellar below them; a piece of iron gutter appeared to have been struck by a falling brick, though neither was directly above or near any such objects at the time.

2. **Phantom voice (January 5, 5:25 p.m.)**: Russell was walking down the orchard path when he was certain he heard Marshall call to him. Marshall denied having spoken. In his independent report, Marshall confirmed the incident — he had not called. Russell had been near the stream on the Nun's Walk when he heard what he took to be Marshall's voice. Price notes this is consistent with multiple previous "voice" phenomena at Borley.

In Marshall's own report, he also records that at 5:42 p.m. (seventeen minutes after the voice incident) he "turned round and thought I saw someone in the corner of the field" near the summer-house, in fading light. He adds: "it is easy to imagine things when turning suddenly."

## Significance

Marshall's fifty photographs are among the last systematic photographic records of the Rectory structure before its complete demolition. His calm, cautious reporting style — explicitly noting the possibility of imagination in his visual impression — gives the auditory phantom-voice incident greater credibility by contrast.

**Harry Marshall** was an undergraduate student at Trinity College, Oxford [1]. A lesser-known biographical detail recorded in the sources is that he was the son of Mr. J. R. Marshall, K.C., D.L., a distinguished Scottish jurist [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, dates, events, and quotes regarding his involvement in the case:

**The January 1944 Visit and Photography**
Accompanied by a fellow Oxford undergraduate, Mr. J. H. Russell, Marshall visited the ruined Borley Rectory between **January 3 and January 6, 1944** [1, 2]. However, because they experienced delays in securing permission from the site's new owners, the two students actually spent a total of only ten hours on the premises [1]. 

A major objective of their trip was to capture photographs of the constructional details of the ruined fabric of the building [1]. Marshall was highly successful in this endeavor, securing **fifty photographs** of the site [1]. He was aided by the fact that the fire damage allowed daylight to penetrate into previously dark areas like the cellars, though he also utilized a flashlight for some pictures [1]. His photographic work was exceptionally well-timed, as the demolition of the Rectory ruins had already begun in a modest way by the time they arrived [1]. 

**Anomalous Experiences and Quotes**
During their brief investigation, Marshall and Russell recorded several puzzling incidents, though they maintained a skeptical and objective approach to their observations:

*   **The Displaced Camera Bag (January 4):** On Tuesday, January 4, 1944, the students left a bag containing their photographic accessories and tools on a specific shelf in the summer-house [3]. When they returned to collect it at **12:25 p.m.**, the bag was missing [3]. After searching, they found it lying on the grass behind the summer-house [3]. Because the bag had not been under direct observation, they acknowledged the possibility that it had been moved by a mischievous living trespasser rather than a paranormal entity [3].
*   **The Disembodied Voice (January 5):** At **5:25 p.m.** on January 5, Russell, who was walking near the stream in the orchard, heard Marshall call out his name [4, 5]. Marshall was standing about fifteen yards away on a cart-track and had not spoken [5, 6]. Marshall confirmed this auditory anomaly in his official report, providing the specific quote: 
> **"On Wednesday evening Russell was certain I called him. I did not. At the time (5:25 p.m.) he claimed to have recognized my voice. When he knew I had not called him he naturally began to doubt whether he really had heard a voice. He was by the north-east end of the stream in the garden; I was fifteen yards away on the path through the orchard from the road to the fields."** [6].
*   **The Figure in the Field (January 5):** Just seventeen minutes after the voice incident, at **5:42 p.m.**, Marshall recorded a visual anomaly while standing near the summer-house opposite the Nun's Walk [3]. Demonstrating a cautious and critical mindset, he noted the event in his report with the following quote: 
> **"Later, at 5:42, while standing by the summer-house (opposite the Nun’s Walk), I turned round and thought I saw someone in the corner of the field. But it is easy to imagine things when turning suddenly and when it is beginning to get dark."** [3].
*   **Other Incidents:** The two undergraduates also recorded other minor incidents they classified as "doubtful," which included thinking they heard someone moving inside the ruins of the Rectory [3].
