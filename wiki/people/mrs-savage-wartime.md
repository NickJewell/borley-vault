# Mrs Savage

A "portly, middle-aged" resident of a cottage near the burnt Borley Rectory during the Second World War. Interviewed by a correspondent for the *American Weekly* (October 11, 1942) for an article entitled "Can't Bomb out Britain's Spooks." She is not related to [[m-savage|M. Savage]] the BBC engineer.

## Interview

Mrs Savage was described by the American reporter as "a hard-headed, reasonable lady" who had not previously believed in ghosts. Her account:

> *"I never did believe in ghosts until I came here. But there is certainly something strange about the Rectory. The only thing that surprises me is that they do no harm to people. They want to be left alone, poor lonely things. Each and every one of them seems to tell us humans to mind our own business."*

She confirmed having "heard sounds from that place. Queer sounds that couldn't be made by man or beast, nor wind nor decay." She had also "seen lights too — often, in fact." This got her into trouble with the ARP (Air Raid Precautions):

> *"Got me into trouble, they have. The A.R.P. thought it was me breaking the black-out. Just imagine that!"*

## Significance

Mrs Savage's testimony, published in an American magazine in 1942, represents independent wartime corroboration of the [[window-lights|window lights]] phenomenon — still continuing in the ruins of a gutted building, years after the fire, without any electrical source. Her frustration with the ARP suggests the lights were visible enough to be mistaken for blackout violations, which requires them to have had some genuine luminosity.

She is a named, local, unconnected adult witness whose matter-of-fact tone and practical complaint about ARP trouble lend her account credibility.

The American reporter also repeats the claim that the Nun was seen on July 28, 1940 and 1941 — for which Price has no first-hand evidence.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mrs. Savage** was a wartime resident of Borley who lived in a cottage near the ruins of the burnt-out Rectory. She features in the sources primarily through an interview she gave to a representative of the *American Weekly* for an article titled "Can't Bomb out Britain's Spooks," published on **October 11, 1942** [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following details regarding her experiences:

**Biographical Details and Demeanor**
*   **Appearance and Personality:** The American journalist described her physically as a **"portly, middle-aged"** woman with **"calm, blue eyes"** [2].
*   **Initial Skepticism:** She was characterized as a **"hard-headed, reasonable lady"** who initially had no patience for the supernatural, sharply telling the reporter, **"I have got no time to mess with things like that"** [2]. 

**Paranormal Experiences and Quotes**
Despite her initial skepticism, Mrs. Savage's proximity to the Rectory ruins completely changed her perspective on the supernatural:
*   **Shift in Belief:** She openly admitted her conversion to the journalist, stating, **"I never did believe in ghosts until I came here. But there is certainly something strange about the Rectory"** [2, 3].
*   **Auditory Phenomena:** She reported hearing bizarre noises emanating from the site, providing the specific quote: **"Queer sounds that couldn't be made by man or beast, nor wind nor decay"** [2, 3].
*   **Her Theory on the Entities:** Unlike those who found the phenomena terrifying or malevolent, Mrs. Savage felt a degree of sympathy for whatever was haunting the grounds. She noted, **"The only thing that surprises me is that they do no harm to people. They want to be left alone, poor lonely things. Each and every one of them seems to tell us humans to mind our own business"** [2, 3].

**The A.R.P. (Blackout) Incident**
Mrs. Savage's most notable contribution to the Borley lore involved visual phenomena that resulted in a uniquely wartime misunderstanding. 
*   She claimed that mysterious lights were often seen shining among the ruins of the Rectory at night [2, 4]. 
*   Because Britain was under strict wartime blackout regulations at the time, the local Air Raid Precautions (A.R.P.) warden investigated these mysterious lights on more than one occasion [3, 4].
*   The warden ultimately accused Mrs. Savage of breaking the blackout laws [3]. With "natural indignation," she recounted the bizarre predicament to the journalist: **"Got me into trouble, they have... The A.R.P. thought it was me breaking the black-out. Just imagine that!"** [2]. 
*   She was only able to clear herself of the warden's accusations by explaining to him that the Rectory site was rumored to be haunted [3].
