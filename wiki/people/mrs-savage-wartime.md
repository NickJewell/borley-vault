# Mrs Savage

A "portly, middle-aged" resident — described by an American journalist as having "calm, blue eyes" — who lived in a cottage near the burnt [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] during the Second World War. Characterised as a "hard-headed, reasonable lady" who initially had no patience for the supernatural. She is not related to [[m-savage|M. Savage]] the BBC engineer.

Interviewed by a correspondent for the *American Weekly* for an article entitled "Can't Bomb out Britain's Spooks," published on **October 11, 1942**.

## Interview

Mrs Savage told the reporter bluntly, "I have got no time to mess with things like that." But despite her initial scepticism, her proximity to the Rectory ruins had completely changed her perspective on the supernatural:

> *"I never did believe in ghosts until I came here. But there is certainly something strange about the Rectory. The only thing that surprises me is that they do no harm to people. They want to be left alone, poor lonely things. Each and every one of them seems to tell us humans to mind our own business."*

She confirmed having "heard sounds from that place. Queer sounds that couldn't be made by man or beast, nor wind nor decay." She had also "seen lights too — often, in fact." This got her into trouble with the ARP (Air Raid Precautions):

> *"Got me into trouble, they have. The A.R.P. thought it was me breaking the black-out. Just imagine that!"*

The local Air Raid Precautions warden investigated the mysterious lights on more than one occasion, accusing Mrs Savage of breaking the blackout laws. She was only able to clear herself by explaining to him that the Rectory site was rumoured to be haunted.

The American reporter also repeats the claim that the Nun was seen on July 28, 1940 and 1941 — for which [[harry-price|Price]] has no first-hand evidence.

## Significance

Mrs Savage's testimony, published in an American magazine in 1942, represents independent wartime corroboration of the [[window-lights|window lights]] phenomenon — still continuing in the ruins of a gutted building, years after the fire, without any electrical source. Her frustration with the ARP suggests the lights were visible enough to be mistaken for blackout violations, which requires them to have had some genuine luminosity.

She is a named, local, unconnected adult witness whose matter-of-fact tone and practical complaint about ARP trouble lend her account credibility.
