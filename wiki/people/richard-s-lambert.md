# Richard S. Lambert

Author and BBC administrator who served as editor of the BBC publication *The Listener* for ten years. He enjoyed a close friendship with [[harry-price|Harry Price]], collaborating with him before the Borley book was written, and is cited in [[alan-wesencraft|Alan Wesencraft's]] introduction to *Borley Rectory: The Final Analysis* as a character witness for Price's personal integrity.

## Collaborations with Harry Price

Lambert had already worked with Price on several media projects before Borley: a radio broadcast from Dean Manor, a programme concerning the famous "talking mongoose" case on the Isle of Man, and a co-authored book on that case, *The Haunting of Cashen's Gap*. These projects established a productive working relationship.

## Involvement in the Borley Book

During Price's year-long tenancy at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], one of his official observers, [[mark-kerr-pearse|Mark Kerr-Pearse]], approached the BBC's Talks Department hoping to feature his Borley experiences in a radio series on haunted houses. Because of his position at *The Listener*, Lambert heard about Kerr-Pearse's unauthorised approach and tipped off Price, prompting Price to send Kerr-Pearse a sharp warning letter reminding him that he had signed a legal declaration prohibiting him from discussing the case publicly.

Having successfully collaborated on the "talking mongoose" book, Price and Lambert planned to co-author a book about the Borley Rectory haunting, jointly attending a meeting with Kenneth Potter, the commissioning editor at Longmans. By the autumn, Potter officially offered Price an advance of £75 for the Borley book. Still eager to participate, Lambert wrote to Price offering to immediately start drafting several chapters using Price's research material, proposing that his drafting work could be used "without acknowledgement as long as Price was willing to share the advance." Despite this evident enthusiasm, Lambert abruptly pulled out of the arrangement — reportedly advised by his wife to withdraw, leaving Price to write what eventually became *The Most Haunted House in England* alone.

## Statement on Harry Price

In his introduction to a book by David Cohen, Lambert wrote:

> "From my personal acquaintance, I can say that Mr. Price was generous, straightforward, loyal, and honest. He possessed a cautious scepticism, a highly developed sense of publicity, and a skilful pen."

This assessment is notable because it concedes two of the critiques most often levelled at Price ("a highly developed sense of publicity") while simultaneously affirming the personal qualities attacked most virulently by the three authors of the SPR's *Haunting of Borley Rectory* — his straightforwardness, loyalty, and honesty. Wesencraft, the then-curator of the [[national-laboratory|Harry Price Library]] at the University of London, cites it as evidence against the characterisation of Price by his critics.
