# Richard S. Lambert

Author and BBC administrator who served as editor of the BBC publication *The Listener* for ten years and during that period enjoyed a close friendship with [[harry-price|Harry Price]]. He is cited in [[alan-wesencraft|Alan Wesencraft's]] introduction to *Borley Rectory: The Final Analysis* as a character witness for Price's personal integrity.

## Statement on Harry Price

In his introduction to a book by David Cohen, Lambert wrote:

> "From my personal acquaintance, I can say that Mr. Price was generous, straightforward, loyal, and honest. He possessed a cautious scepticism, a highly developed sense of publicity, and a skilful pen."

This statement is notable because it comes from someone with a long professional relationship with Price, writing in a context where he had nothing to gain from defending him. Wesencraft, the then-curator of the Harry Price Library at the University of London, cites it as evidence against the characterisation of Price by his critics.

## Significance

Lambert's assessment is particularly valuable as it concedes two of the critiques most often levelled at Price ("a highly developed sense of publicity") while simultaneously affirming the personal qualities attacked most virulently by the three authors of the SPR's *Haunting of Borley Rectory* — his straightforwardness, loyalty, and honesty.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Richard S. Lambert** was the editor of the BBC magazine *The Listener* and a literary associate of the famous psychical investigator Harry Price. While he was never directly involved in investigating the physical site of Borley Rectory, he played an important behind-the-scenes role in the early planning of Price's first book on the case.

A reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific details, events, and collaborations regarding his involvement:

**Early Collaborations with Harry Price**
*   **Prior Projects:** Before becoming involved in the Borley case, Lambert had already collaborated with Price on several media projects [1]. He participated in Price's radio broadcast from Dean Manor, as well as a program concerning the famous "talking mongoose" case on the Isle of Man [1].
*   **Co-Author:** Following their work on the Isle of Man case, Lambert and Price co-authored a book on the subject titled *The Haunting of Cashen’s Gap* [1].

**Involvement in the Borley Case**
*   **The BBC Broadcast Incident:** During Price's year-long tenancy at Borley, one of his official observers, Mark Kerr-Pearse, approached the BBC's Talks Department with the hope of featuring his Borley experiences in an upcoming radio series on haunted houses [1]. Because of his position at *The Listener*, Lambert heard about Kerr-Pearse's unauthorized approach and tipped off Price [1]. This prompted Price to send Kerr-Pearse a sharp warning letter reminding him that he had signed a legal declaration prohibiting him from discussing the case publicly [1]. 
*   **The Book Collaboration:** Having successfully collaborated on the "talking mongoose" book, Price and Lambert planned to co-author a book about the Borley Rectory haunting [2]. To this end, they jointly attended a meeting with Kenneth Potter, the commissioning editor at Longmans [2]. 
*   **The £75 Advance:** By the autumn, Potter officially offered Price an advance of £75 for the Borley book [3]. Still eager to participate, Lambert wrote to Price offering to immediately start drafting several chapters using the research material Price had already amassed [3]. Lambert proposed a ghostwriting arrangement, stipulating that his drafting work could be used **"without acknowledgement as long as Price was willing to share the advance that had been offered by Longmans"** [3].
*   **Withdrawal:** Despite his evident enthusiasm for the Borley project, Lambert abruptly pulled out of the arrangement [3]. The sources record that he was **"advised by his wife to withdraw from the Borley project, leaving Price to write it alone"** [3].

The provided sources contain no further biographical details regarding Richard S. Lambert beyond his aborted involvement in the publication of what would eventually become *The Most Haunted House in England*.
