# Dennis Herbert (Denny) Densham

(Born Putney, 6 March 1921; died South Glamorgan, September 1992) Film cameraman and cinematographer. Worked as a cameraman for the Crown Film Unit and Gaumont British Screen Services during the Second World War, and was Director of Photography on several pictures in the 1950s including *Man of Africa* (1953), *Devil on Horseback* (1954), *Make Me an Offer* (1954) and *Child's Play* (1954).

## Borley Investigations

Densham was interested in ghosts and had previously obtained recordings of alleged phenomena in the haunted squash court at RAF Bircham Newton, broadcast on the Jack de Manio radio show.

In 1972 Densham approached [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth]] with a request to join his investigations at Borley and subsequently took part in several vigils inside [[borley-church]]. These sessions were recorded; when tape recorders were left running inside the locked, empty church, mysterious sounds including footsteps, clicks, knocks, sinister sighing and grunting sounds were picked up. These recordings are described in detail in [[ghost-of-borley-church|*The Ghost of Borley Church*]].

## The Recordings

The results were later featured in the BBC's *Ghost Hunters* television programme. Densham quoted as saying:

> "Frankly, I am at a loss to explain what goes on at Borley. We made every effort to ensure that our legs weren't being pulled, and the tapes were new and untampered with. No theory I have tried to put forward seems to pan out ... One's left with the feeling that whatever causes the phenomena is indifferent to or perhaps unaware of observers."

## Controversy

When an article by Frank Smyth in *The Unexplained* published in the early 1980s described Densham's experiences, [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth]] complained that he had not been informed and that the impression given was that Densham had been in charge of proceedings.

[[tony-broughall]] later demonstrated that two of the supposedly paranormal sounds on the Densham recordings — the opening of a phantom door and an eerie sigh — could easily be replicated inside the church, and Croom-Hollingsworth himself subsequently acknowledged that the BBC may have selected sounds made by the investigation party itself.

## Published Work

Densham also produced an audio programme *Is There Anybody There?* on survival after death, issued by Mindpower Records in the mid-1970s, with EVP experiments and séance recordings. The [[ghost-tapes|Ghost Tapes]] CD (originally *The Ghost of Borley Church*) was reissued by Revenant Recordings UK and remains one of the few audio documents associated with the Borley case.

**Dennis Herbert (Denny) Densham** (born March 6, 1921, in Putney; died September 1992 in South Glamorgan) was a film executive, cameraman, and cinematographer whose audio experiments became a famous component of the later Borley investigations [1, 2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, quotes, and controversies regarding his life and work:

**Lesser-Known Biographical Details**
During the Second World War, Densham worked as a cameraman for the Crown Film Unit and Gaumont British Screen Services [2]. He subsequently served as Director of Photography on several 1950s films, including *Man of Africa* (1953), *Devil on Horseback* (1954), *Make Me an Offer* (1954), and *Child’s Play* (1954) [2]. 

He possessed a deep interest in ghosts and had previously investigated the haunted RAF Bircham Newton aerodrome, where he obtained recordings of alleged phenomena using a locked-off tape machine inside a squash court [3]. He also produced a mid-1970s audio documentary titled *Is There Anybody There?* (issued by Mindpower Records), which explored the spiritualistic hypothesis for survival after death using recordings of séances and Electronic Voice Phenomena (EVP) experiments [4].

**Investigations at Borley Church (1972–1974)**
In 1972, Densham approached Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth and requested to join his investigative team at Borley [3]. Acting on a suggestion from Cambridge researcher David Ellis, Densham began utilizing locked-off tape recorders inside the sealed, empty Borley Church [1, 5]. 

Densham made five specific visits to Borley starting in July 1972 [5]. During his vigils—and notably during a night vigil in July 1974—Densham and the team captured an astonishing array of mysterious audio anomalies [6-8]. The recordings purported to contain footsteps, clicks, knocks, crashing sounds, sinister sighing, and deep grunting noises [3, 6]. The investigators also claimed to see pinpoints of light and captured a specific noise they firmly believed was **"the sound of a ghost stepping forward and opening something that sounds like a door"** [1, 8].

**Densham's Conviction and Quotes**
Densham was utterly baffled by his findings. Regarding his recordings at Borley, he stated:
> **"Frankly, I am at a loss to explain what goes on at Borley. We made every effort to ensure that our legs weren’t being pulled, and the tapes were new and untampered with. No theory I have tried to put forward seems to pan out ... One’s left with the feeling that whatever causes the phenomena is indifferent to or perhaps unaware of observers"** [9].

**Media Releases and Skeptical Contradictions**
Densham's audio recordings gained significant attention and were featured in the BBC television programme *The Ghost Hunters* [3, 10]. The 1974 church recordings were also issued on cassette tape in the mid-1970s under the title *The Ghost of Borley Church*, and later reissued on CD as *The Ghost Tapes* [7].

However, his recordings were met with severe skepticism by other Borley investigators, and his involvement caused friction within the group:
*   **Natural Explanations:** Investigator Tony Broughall became extremely skeptical of Densham's recordings; during his own vigil in the church, Broughall found that he was able to easily replicate the exact sounds of the "phantom door" and "eerie sigh" [11]. Similarly, when Peter Underwood inspected the church with a BBC producer, he quickly located objects that could provide a natural, physical explanation for every single noise on Densham's tapes [1].
*   **Group Friction:** When an article by Frank Smyth was published in the early 1980s, it gave the impression that Densham had brought his own group to Borley and was in charge of the investigation [4]. This prompted a complaint from Croom-Hollingsworth, who contacted the publishers to clarify that he and his team had been misrepresented by this portrayal [4].
