# Dennis Herbert (Denny) Densham

(Born Putney, 6 March 1921; died South Glamorgan, September 1992) Film cameraman and cinematographer [1, 2]. Worked as a cameraman for the Crown Film Unit and Gaumont British Screen Services during the Second World War, and subsequently served as Director of Photography on several 1950s films including *Man of Africa* (1953), *Devil on Horseback* (1954), *Make Me an Offer* (1954), and *Child's Play* (1954) [2]. His audio experiments at [[borley-church|Borley Church]] became a well-known component of the post-war investigations.

## Background and Prior Investigations

Densham possessed a deep interest in ghosts and had previously investigated the reportedly haunted squash court at RAF Bircham Newton aerodrome, where he obtained recordings of alleged phenomena using a locked-off tape machine [3]. He also produced a mid-1970s audio documentary titled *Is There Anybody There?* (issued by Mindpower Records), exploring the spiritualistic hypothesis for survival after death using recordings of séances and Electronic Voice Phenomena (EVP) experiments [4].

## Investigations at Borley Church (1972–1974)

In 1972, Densham approached [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth|Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth]] with a request to join his investigations at [[borley-church|Borley]] and subsequently took part in several vigils inside the church. Acting on a suggestion from Cambridge researcher David Ellis, Densham began utilising locked-off tape recorders inside the sealed, empty church [1, 5].

Densham made five specific visits to Borley starting in July 1972 [5]. During his vigils — notably during a night vigil in July 1974 — the team captured a range of mysterious audio anomalies [6–8]. The recordings purported to contain footsteps, clicks, knocks, crashing sounds, sinister sighing, and deep grunting noises [3, 6]. The investigators also claimed to see pinpoints of light and captured a specific noise they firmly believed was "the sound of a ghost stepping forward and opening something that sounds like a door" [1, 8].

## The Recordings

The results were later featured in the BBC's *Ghost Hunters* television programme. Densham stated:

> "Frankly, I am at a loss to explain what goes on at Borley. We made every effort to ensure that our legs weren't being pulled, and the tapes were new and untampered with. No theory I have tried to put forward seems to pan out ... One's left with the feeling that whatever causes the phenomena is indifferent to or perhaps unaware of observers." [9]

The 1974 church recordings were issued on cassette tape under the title *The Ghost of Borley Church* and later reissued on CD as *The Ghost Tapes* by Revenant Recordings UK — one of the few audio documents associated with the Borley case [7].

## Controversy

When an article by Frank Smyth in *The Unexplained* (early 1980s) described Densham's experiences, [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth|Croom-Hollingsworth]] complained that he had not been informed and that the impression given was that Densham had been in charge of proceedings [4].

[[tony-broughall|Tony Broughall]] later demonstrated that two of the supposedly paranormal sounds on the Densham recordings — the opening of a phantom door and an eerie sigh — could easily be replicated inside the church [11]. Similarly, when [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] inspected the church with a BBC producer, he quickly located objects that could provide a natural, physical explanation for every noise on Densham's tapes [1]. Croom-Hollingsworth himself subsequently acknowledged that the BBC may have selected sounds made by the investigation party itself.
