# Jeronima Waldegrave

One of two daughters of Sir Nicholas Waldegrave of Borley who became nuns in exile on the Continent. She is eliminated by Ivan Banks as a candidate for the ghost nun of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], by virtue of her known cause of death — which in turn focuses Banks's attention entirely on her sister [[barbara-waldegrave|Barbara Waldegrave (Sister Apollonia)]].

## Family

Jeronima was a daughter of Sir Nicholas Waldegrave of Borley (descended from [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Sir Edward Waldegrave]]'s nephew [[nicholas-waldegrave|Nicholas Waldegrave]]). Her mother was Catherine, née Browne of Wellhall, Essex. Her sister was Barbara, who became Sister Apollonia of the Brussels Benedictines.

## Religious Life

Unlike her sister Barbara who joined the Brussels English Benedictine community, Jeronima entered the **nuns of Ghent** — a different exiled English religious house in the Spanish Netherlands [1, 3].

## Significance: Elimination

Banks's logic for identifying the ghost nun rests on a process of elimination. He establishes two key facts:

1. Both Jeronima and Barbara came from Borley itself, making them intrinsically relevant candidates.
2. Jeronima's cause of death is known — she **died young from consumption** (tuberculosis) while with her religious order in Ghent [3].

Because her cause of death is clearly documented and unremarkable — consumption was a common fatal illness — Jeronima does not fit the profile of a troubled or murdered spirit. Banks therefore eliminates her and concludes:

> "Of the two sisters, we do know Jeronima died young from consumption and she belonged to the exiled English community of Ghent. That leaves Barbara."

This leaves [[barbara-waldegrave|Barbara Waldegrave / Sister Apollonia]], who also died young (aged ~36) but whose death is recorded only as "happily deceased" — an ambiguous entry that Banks finds suspicious, and which he argues could conceal inner spiritual conflict or an unusual death [3].
