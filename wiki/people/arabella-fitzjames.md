# Arabella FitzJames / Sister Ignatia

Natural daughter of King James II by [[arabella-churchill|Arabella Churchill]], and sister of [[henrietta-fitzjames|Henrietta FitzJames (Lady Waldegrave)]]. She became a Benedictine nun at the English house at Pontoise, France, under the name **Sister Ignatia** (also recorded as **Dame Ignatia**). Ivan Banks investigates her as a candidate for the ghost nun of Borley Rectory before eliminating her.

## Family

- **Father:** King James II
- **Mother:** [[arabella-churchill|Arabella Churchill]]
- **Sister:** [[henrietta-fitzjames|Henrietta FitzJames]], who married [[henry-lord-waldegrave|Henry Lord Waldegrave]]

Arabella FitzJames is therefore the aunt of **Arabella Waldegrave (the granddaughter)** — the girl who vanished from the records, long considered a candidate for the ghost nun.

> **Note:** There were thus two women named "Arabella" in this family who became nuns: Arabella FitzJames (sister of Henrietta, aunt of the granddaughter Arabella) and the granddaughter [[arabella-waldegrave|Arabella Waldegrave]] herself. Banks's research untangled the confusion between them.

## Religious Life

Arabella FitzJames became a novice at Pontoise at the age of fifteen [2], entering the English Benedictine convent near Paris where she bore the religious name "Ignatia" [1, 2]. According to a footnote by researcher [[mary-lycett|Mary Lycett]], she became a nun with the English Benedictines on April 16, 1689, and was officially professed on April 30, 1690 [3] — placing her as novice at approximately sixteen and professed at seventeen.

Banks's research into the Oulton Abbey archives confirms she was clothed at Pontoise and remained there — she did not go on to Brussels. This means the theory that she was removed from Pontoise to Brussels (to separate her from a suitor) is not supported by the records [3].

The archives further revealed that there were actually *two* Arabellas at the Pontoise convent at the same time: Arabella FitzJames was there as an adult nun, while her young niece, [[arabella-waldegrave|Arabella Waldegrave]], was sent there as a child to be educated between 1693 and 1696 [1].

## Banks's Investigation

Banks considers Arabella FitzJames in the context of [[mary-lycett|Mary Lycett]]'s 1952 theory, which confused the identities of the two Arabellas. Lycett's scenario describes an "Arabella Waldegrave" placed in Pontoise as a child, who then becomes romantically involved with a cousin named Henry Waldegrave. Banks disentangles this by showing:

1. Arabella FitzJames was placed at Pontoise as an adult (novice at approximately fifteen–sixteen, professed at seventeen)
2. The granddaughter [[arabella-waldegrave|Arabella Waldegrave]] (Henry and Henrietta's daughter) was placed at Pontoise much later, as a child of seven

A prominent theory proposed that the Borley nun was an Arabella who had been removed from the Pontoise convent after attracting the unwanted attentions of a suitor [3]. Banks pointed out that if such a romance occurred, the adult Arabella FitzJames (Sister Ignatia) would have been a much more likely subject for a young suitor's interest than her young niece [3]. However, archival records confirm that Arabella FitzJames was officially "clothed" and remained at Pontoise — effectively ruling out theories that she was transferred to the Brussels order or removed to England [3].

The adult Arabella FitzJames/Sister Ignatia is thus eliminated as the ghost nun candidate: she was professed, remained in religious life, and there is no evidence of her disappearing or dying under unusual circumstances.

## Significance

Arabella FitzJames/Sister Ignatia is significant to the Borley research primarily because her presence at Pontoise has caused persistent confusion with her niece the granddaughter Arabella Waldegrave. Untangling the two Arabellas was necessary for Banks's nun-identity investigation. Her elimination from candidacy points attention back toward the granddaughter and toward [[barbara-waldegrave|Barbara Waldegrave / Sister Apollonia]] as the most credible candidates.
