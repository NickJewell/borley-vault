# Miss Cynthia Ledsham

Researcher and archivist attached to the London Bureau of the American pictorial magazines *Time* and *Life* [1–4]. Later known by her married name, **Mrs. Cynthia Thompson**. She accompanied [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and photographer [[david-scherman|David E. Scherman]] on their visit to the Borley ruins on **5 April 1944** to photograph the site for a proposed *Life* article entitled "Haunted England" [2, 3, 6, 7]. Upon arrival, they found the building in the process of being demolished [2, 8].

## The Flying Brick

During the visit, Scherman set up his camera to photograph the ruins. According to Price's published account in *The End of Borley Rectory*, as Scherman released the shutter, a single brick suddenly shot up about four feet into the air in front of the kitchen passage [2, 9, 10]. Price claimed that he, Ledsham, and Scherman were standing at least 100 feet away and that "no workmen were at all on that side of the Rectory" [9–11]. Ledsham was one of three witnesses — with Price and Scherman — to this incident.

Price dubbed the event "the last phenomenon" and published the resulting photograph as possibly the first photograph of a "poltergeist projectile in flight" [2, 9, 10, 12]. He noted that Miss Ledsham laughed at the time and cried out, "Look, poltergeists!" [8, 9, 13].

Without knowing it at the time, Scherman's camera had been open at the precise instant the brick was at the apex of its flight. The resulting photograph — later enlarged — shows a white, rectangular shape against the dark background of the kitchen passage, stationary (no motion blur) and clearly in mid-air. [[david-scherman|Scherman]] confirmed the negatives were not retouched.

## Ledsham's Accusation of "Bare-Faced Hocus Pocus"

Following Price's death, Ledsham (now Mrs. Cynthia Thompson) fiercely contradicted Price's account. In an undated letter written around early 1950 to Dr. D. J. West, Research Officer of the [[spr|Society for Psychical Research]], she wrote:

> "I had firsthand experience of the most bare-faced hocus pocus on the part of the late Harry Price... there is no doubt at all that the flying bricks, several of which came out at regular intervals, were propelled by this workman as part of his demolition work." [5, 14, 15]

She stated that a "brawny workman" was standing behind the wall, and that all three of them (including Price) clearly saw him as they approached the spot [5, 12]. During an interview over lunch with [[kathleen-goldney|SPR's Kathleen M. Goldney]] on **17 October 1950**, she explained that she had jokingly said "Look, poltergeists" when the workman threw the brick, and that Price had joined in the joke, picking up the brick with a large smile and saying, "Yes, indeed; look; no strings, no wires attached" [4, 8, 12, 15]. She was completely astounded to later see Price present the joke as a genuine paranormal event in his book [16].

Her testimony was subsequently used by [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen Goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] in the highly critical 1956 SPR [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] as a "final accusation of flagrant misrepresentation" against Price [2, 17].

## Contradictions and Criticisms of Her Testimony

When investigator [[robert-hastings|Robert J. Hastings]] later re-examined the SPR's *Borley Report*, he highlighted several significant discrepancies and missing links regarding Ledsham's testimony and the way the SPR handled it:

- **One brick vs. several:** Price's account spoke of only *one* brick rising from the ground, whereas Ledsham stated that *several* bricks came out at regular intervals from behind a wall [11].
- **The position of the workman:** Price stated there were no workmen "on *that side* of the building," which did not necessarily rule out a workman operating *behind* the wall [11, 18].
- **A flawed SPR interview:** Hastings heavily criticised Goldney for how she conducted the October 1950 interview — hastily taking notes over lunch and failing to ask Ledsham to indicate on a floor plan exactly where the "brawny workman" had been standing relative to the camera [19, 20]. Without knowing the precise position, it was impossible to prove whether Price genuinely saw the workman or was consciously misrepresenting the facts [21]. Goldney also allowed Ledsham to alter details of her story without noting the inconsistencies [19].

A postscript emerged in 1996 when Michael Coleman contacted [[david-scherman|David Scherman]], who admitted that he had **deliberately positioned the camera to hide the workman** throwing the rubble because he thought it would be fun [22]. Scherman confirmed that Price was completely in on the joke, adding that when he saw Price had the "effrontery" to pass the episode off in his book as genuine poltergeist activity, he was actually delighted by Price's "adventurous spirit" [22].

## Significance

Ledsham's presence as an independent, non-paranormal-research professional with no personal stake in the Borley narrative made her corroboration of the brick incident particularly valuable to the SPR critics. Her subsequent reversal of that position — and the contradictions within her account — remained contested long after the publication of [[the-borley-report|the Borley Report]].
