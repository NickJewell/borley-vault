# Miss Cynthia Ledsham

Researcher and archivist attached to *Time and Life Ltd.*, publishers of *Life* magazine. She accompanied [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and photographer [[david-scherman|David E. Scherman]] on their visit to the Borley ruins on April 5, 1944, for the purpose of photographing the site for a *Life* article on "Haunted England."

## The Flying Brick

Ledsham was one of three witnesses — with Price and Scherman — to the "flying brick" incident: as Scherman pressed the shutter of his camera, a brick or part of a brick suddenly shot approximately four feet into the air in front of the ruins of the kitchen passage, approximately one hundred feet in front of the observers.

All three saw it simultaneously. They walked over and found bricks lying about normally — no wires, no strings, no workmen visible on that side of the building.

Without knowing it at the time, Scherman's camera had been open at the precise instant the brick was at the apex of its flight. The resulting photograph — later enlarged — shows a white, rectangular shape against the dark background of the kitchen passage, stationary (no motion blur) and clearly in mid-air. [[david-scherman|Scherman]] confirmed the negatives were not retouched.

## Significance

Ledsham's presence as an independent, non-paranormal-research professional with no personal stake in the Borley narrative makes her corroboration of the brick incident particularly valuable. She was there for a journalistic purpose; the incident was unexpected and unexplained. See [[flying-brick-1944]] for the full account.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Miss Cynthia Ledsham** (later known by her married name, **Mrs. Cynthia Thompson**) was a researcher and archivist attached to the London Bureau of the American pictorial magazines *Time* and *Life* [1-4]. In the Borley Rectory sources, she is primarily recorded as a central witness to the infamous "flying brick" incident—an event that later became a cornerstone accusation of fraud against the psychical researcher Harry Price [2, 5].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, quotes, and contradictions regarding her involvement:

**The Visit to Borley (April 5, 1944)**
On **April 5, 1944**, Miss Ledsham accompanied Harry Price and David E. Scherman (a *Life* photographer and accredited war correspondent to the US Army) to the ruins of Borley Rectory [2, 3, 6, 7]. The purpose of the trip was to photograph the remains of the house for a proposed *Life* magazine article entitled "Haunted England" [3, 7]. Upon arrival, they found the building in the process of being demolished [2, 8].

**The "Flying Brick" Incident and Price's Version**
During the visit, Scherman set up his camera to take a photograph of the ruins. According to Price’s published account in *The End of Borley Rectory*, as Scherman released the shutter, a single brick suddenly shot up about four feet into the air in front of the kitchen passage [2, 9, 10]. Price claimed that he, Ledsham, and Scherman were standing at least 100 feet away, and that **"no workmen were at all on that side of the Rectory"** [9-11]. 

Price dubbed the event **"the last phenomenon"** and published the picture as the first photograph ever taken of a "Poltergeist projectile in flight" [2, 9, 10, 12]. Price noted that Miss Ledsham laughed at the time and cried out, **"Look, poltergeists!"** [8, 9, 13].

**Ledsham's Accusation of "Bare-Faced Hocus Pocus"**
Following Price's death, Ledsham (now Mrs. Cynthia Thompson) fiercely contradicted Price’s account. In an undated letter written around early 1950 to Dr. D.J. West, Research Officer of the Society for Psychical Research (SPR), she wrote:
> **"I had firsthand experience of the most bare-faced hocus pocus on the part of the late Harry Price... there is no doubt at all that the flying bricks, several of which came out at regular intervals, were propelled by this workman as part of his demolition work."** [5, 14, 15]

She stated that a "brawny workman" was standing behind the wall, and that all three of them (including Price) clearly saw him as they approached the spot to take the photograph [5, 12]. During an interview over lunch with the SPR's Kathleen M. Goldney (KMG) on **October 17, 1950**, she explained that she had jokingly said "Look, poltergeists" when the workman threw the brick, and that Price had joined in the joke, picking up the brick with a large smile and saying, **"Yes, indeed; look; no strings, no wires attached"** [4, 8, 12, 15]. She noted that she was completely astounded to later see Price present the joke as a genuine paranormal event in his book [16].

Her testimony was subsequently utilized by Eric Dingwall, Kathleen Goldney, and Trevor Hall in the highly critical 1956 SPR *Borley Report* as a "final accusation of flagrant misrepresentation" against Price [2, 17]. 

**Contradictions and Criticisms of Her Testimony**
When investigator Robert J. Hastings later re-examined the SPR's *Borley Report*, he highlighted several significant discrepancies and missing links regarding Ledsham's testimony and the way the SPR handled it:
*   **One Brick vs. Several:** Price's account spoke of only *one* brick rising from the ground, whereas Ledsham stated that *several* bricks came out at regular intervals from behind a wall [11].
*   **The Position of the Workman:** Hastings pointed out a carefully worded technicality in Price's account. Price stated there were no workmen "on *that side* of the building," which did not necessarily rule out a workman operating *behind* the wall [11, 18]. 
*   **A Flawed SPR Interview:** Hastings heavily criticized K.M. Goldney for how she conducted the October 17, 1950, interview with Ledsham. Goldney hastily took notes over lunch and failed to properly test Ledsham's memory—such as asking her to indicate on a floor plan exactly where the "brawny workman" had been standing relative to the camera [19, 20]. Hastings argued that without knowing the precise position, it was impossible to prove whether Price genuinely saw the workman or was consciously misrepresenting the facts [21]. Goldney also allowed Ledsham to alter details of her story without noticing the inconsistencies [19].

**A Lesser-Known Postscript**
A lesser-known detail regarding this controversy emerged decades later. In 1996, Michael Coleman contacted the photographer, David Scherman, who admitted that he had **deliberately positioned the camera to hide the workman** throwing the rubble because he thought it would be fun [22]. Scherman confirmed that Price was completely in on the joke, and added that when he saw Price had the "effrontery" to pass the episode off in his book as genuine poltergeist activity, he was actually delighted by Price's "adventurous spirit" [22].
