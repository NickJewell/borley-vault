# Canon Edward Powell (d. 14 November 1994)

Vicar of Belchamp St Paul — a remote hamlet situated about five miles from Borley — until his retirement in 1989 at the age of 80. Rural dean; elected to serve on Braintree District Council; instrumental in securing a community centre and main drainage for Belchamp St Paul. He ran educational courses preparing young people for public schools and self-described as **"the last of the autocratic clergy"** [3]. He was also the person who, during a locum at Belchamp St Paul, first directed the Mathiases toward the Borley living.

## Biographical Details

Canon Powell lived in a massive Victorian vicarage in Belchamp St Paul (now a private residence known as "Mulberry House") [2]. Beyond his ecclesiastical and civic duties, he served as chairman of his parish council and as an elected member of the Braintree District Council — thanks largely to his influence, the isolated hamlet enjoyed main drainage and a fine community centre that most surrounding villages, including Borley, lacked [3]. He was highly active into old age, retiring at 80 in 1989 [3].

## Connection to the Mathias Incumbency (1955)

Following the unexpected death of the [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning]] in January 1955, it was Canon Powell who informed the [[rev-lanfranc-mathias|Rev. Lanfranc Mathias]] of the vacancy at Borley and Liston, setting the stage for Mathias to be offered the incumbency [2].

## Cycling Encounter — Early 1960s

On an afternoon in the early 1960s (described in one passage as "the early part of 1960"), Canon Powell cycled over to the Rodbridge area of Borley to visit a sick friend [1, 4]. His return route took him past Borley church on his right and the old rectory grounds on his left.

When he reached the top of Hall Lane at the point opposite the church, a **"shadowy figure dressed in long robes"** suddenly moved out into the roadway in front of him, coming from his left, and then "**melted back into the hedge** from whence it had appeared" [1, 4]. He stopped, got off his bicycle, and searched the former rectory garden thoroughly — the light was still good, and his careful search revealed absolutely no one there [1, 4]. The texts note that the site offered very little cover at the time, as the grounds had been cleared in preparation for the construction of modern bungalows, and there were no workmen present [4].

Reflecting his formidable character, Canon Powell remained absolutely steadfast about his experience, insisting that **"he saw what he saw"** [4]. Completely at a loss for a logical explanation, he maintained the firm opinion to his acquaintances that Borley was undoubtedly a haunted place [1, 4].
