# Canon Edward Powell (d. 14 November 1994)

Vicar of Belchamp St Paul (only a few miles from Borley) until his retirement in 1989 at the age of 80. Rural dean; elected to serve on Braintree District Council; instrumental in securing a community centre and main drainage for Belchamp St Paul. Ran educational courses preparing young people for public schools — self-described as "the last of the autocratic clergy." He was the person who, during a locum at Belchamp St Paul, first directed the Mathiases toward the Borley living.

## Cycling Encounter — Early 1960s

On an afternoon in the early 1960s, Canon Powell cycled over to the Rodbridge area of Borley to visit a sick friend (not "poaching" in [[rev-lanfranc-mathias|Lanfranc's]] parish). His return route took him past Borley church (on his right) and the old rectory grounds (on his left).

When he reached the top of Hall Lane at the point opposite the church, a **shadowy figure dressed in long robes moved out onto the road** in front of him, coming from his left, and then "**melted back into the hedge** from whence it had appeared."

He stopped, got off his bicycle, and went into the former rectory garden to search thoroughly. No one was there. He noted that the area had been largely cleared in preparation for the bungalows being built, so there was little cover. The light was still good.

He was "definite that he saw what he saw," at a complete loss for a logical explanation, and expressed the firm opinion to his acquaintances that **Borley was indeed a haunted place**.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Canon Edward Powell** (d. 14 November 1994) was a prominent and highly respected clergyman in north Essex who served as the vicar of Belchamp St Paul—a remote hamlet situated about five miles from Borley—and as the Rural Dean of the area [1-3]. A formidable presence both in the church and local government, he became a firsthand witness to the Borley haunting during the early 1960s [1, 4]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, quotes, and biographical details regarding his life and connection to the case:

**Lesser-Known Biographical Details and Personality**
Canon Powell lived in a massive Victorian vicarage in Belchamp St Paul (now a private residence known as "Mulberry House") [2]. He was highly active in local government, serving as the chairman of his parish council and as an elected member of the Braintree District Council [3]. Thanks largely to his recommendations and civic influence, the isolated hamlet of Belchamp St Paul enjoyed main drainage and a fine community centre—amenities that most surrounding villages, including Borley, lacked [3]. 

Beyond his ecclesiastical and civic duties, Powell ran educational courses to prepare youths for entry into public schools, functioning as an old-style "crammer" [3]. Possessing a redoubtable and awesome personality, he famously described himself as **"the last of the autocratic clergy"** [3]. He remained highly active into old age, retiring from his parish duties in 1989 at the age of 80 [3]. 

**Connection to the Mathias Incumbency (1955)**
Canon Powell played a direct, behind-the-scenes role in the ecclesiastical history of Borley. Following the unexpected death of the Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning in January 1955, it was Canon Powell who informed the Rev. Lanfranc Mathias of the vacancy at Borley and Liston, setting the stage for Mathias to be offered the incumbency [2]. 

**The Apparition Sighting (Early 1960s)**
Despite his no-nonsense character, Canon Powell had a dramatic encounter with the paranormal at Borley. One afternoon in the early 1960s (described in one passage specifically as "the early part of 1960"), he cycled over to the Rodbridge area of Borley to visit a sick friend [1, 4]. 

While cycling home up Hall Lane, he passed Borley Church on his right and the old Rectory grounds on his left [4]. At this point, well past Tom Gooch's bungalow at the eastern end of the grounds, a **"shadowy figure dressed in long robes"** suddenly moved out into the roadway in front of him from the left, before melting back into the hedge [1, 4]. 

**Investigation and Conclusion**
Rather than fleeing, the pragmatic Canon got off his bicycle and entered the former Rectory garden to investigate [1, 4]. The light was still quite good, but his careful search revealed absolutely no one there [1, 4]. The texts note that the site offered very little cover at the time, as the grounds had been cleared in preparation for the construction of modern bungalows, and there were no workmen present [4]. 

Reflecting his formidable nature, Canon Powell remained absolutely steadfast about his experience, insisting that **"he saw what he saw"** [4]. Completely at a loss for a logical explanation, he maintained the firm, unwavering opinion to his acquaintances that Borley was undoubtedly a haunted place [1, 4].
