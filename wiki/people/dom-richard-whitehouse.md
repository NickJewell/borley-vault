# Dom Richard Whitehouse OSB (Leslie Edwin Whitehouse, b. 1899 Istanbul)

The most extensively defended witness of the Foyster incumbency. A Benedictine monk; nephew of Sir George and [[lady-whitehouse]] of Arthur Hall, Sudbury. He made approximately thirty visits to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] between June and December 1931. His chapter in *The Most Haunted House in England* (ch. 15) is the most detailed first-person witness account in the entire Borley corpus.

## Background (per O'Connor, hbrs-ch11)

Born 1899 in Istanbul; father Francis Cowley Whitehouse, who served as canon to the British Embassy there; mother daughter of J. W. Whittall (head of a mohair/opium trading empire). Served in the Royal Navy at Gallipoli aged 16 on HMS *Queen Elizabeth*, HMS *Vengeance* and HMS *Lord Nelson*. Invalided out 1917 with nerves/shell shock; hospitalised in Malta. Spent time in Switzerland; rejoined the family business in Istanbul 1919; returned to England 1924. Suffered a broken or unreciprocated romance and heard that a cousin had killed herself.

**Mental health**: by May 1931 he had had "a complete nervous breakdown" and been admitted to Bowdon House nursing home, Harrow (Dr Crichton-Miller's facility). He had quarrelled with his analyst and run away, treatment incomplete. He was "seriously contemplating the priesthood" when his aunt's letter arrived. A "damaged and troubled young man."

O'Connor notes that Edwin and [[lionel-foyster|Lionel]] "became very close friends... 'exceedingly intimate', with the suggestion of an attraction between the two men." Marianne was dismissive: Edwin was "too ascetic" to take interest in women.

## How He Came to Borley

He was staying with his uncle and aunt at Arthur Hall when he received [[lady-whitehouse|Lady Whitehouse's]] letter describing the Foyster phenomena. He had known the Rectory since boyhood. He consulted Father Herbert Thurston (Jesuit; sympathetic to paranormal) before visiting, who warned him that blessings had "little effect" on poltergeist cases. He first visited the Rectory on 6 June 1931.

## What He Witnessed (first-hand, mhh-ch15)

**The brass stiletto incident (June 8, 1931)**: Richard sat with [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] while she lay in bed in Room 7 (beside the Blue Room). He sat at the foot of the bed, could see the whole room; Marianne was lying with hands under bedclothes. After ten minutes of conversation: "I felt something land lightly on my lap, coming apparently from behind me." He picked it up: a brass stiletto (paper knife), approximately 8–9 inches, weighing nearly 8 oz, belonging in the study below. Marianne said she had watched it "rise up from the floor behind me and do one or two curious convolutions in the air before settling on my lap." Richard was certain she could not have thrown it — her hands were under the covers throughout.

He then left the room briefly; heard a piercing shriek; rushed back to find Marianne face-down on the floor with the mattress on top of her. "The whole thing must have happened in an instant, for I am quick on my feet and I was no distance from the room."

**November 13, 1931 — the kitchen bottle materializations**: With Marianne and maid Katie in the kitchen, doors and windows closed:
- A bottle burst under Mrs. Foyster's chair
- Fifteen minutes later, another burst under Richard's chair as he sat down
- A third bottle "poised itself in mid-air within a foot or so of the kitchen ceiling" for one to two seconds before crashing to the floor — all three witnessed this
- A fourth bottle rolled in through a door from the staircase without breaking, circling the floor

**The Novena message (June 16, 1931)**: Richard and Mrs. Foyster completed a nine-day Rosary novena asking for guidance. On the last day, kneeling on the landing near the Chapel, both simultaneously became "aware of a presence." Turning to check a wall, Richard found a fresh pencil message: "Get light mass and prayers. M." — appearing while they were both present in the corridor. He was "sorry afterwards that I did not have the writing photographed."

**Bell-ringing directly witnessed**: Stood in the kitchen passage watching the bells moving "to and fro, the only people in the house often standing alongside me, witnessing the same performance." Cut wires and all.

**Skirting-board fire**: He and the Foysters discovered smoke issuing from a room, and a hole "about the size of a child's fist" burned into the skirting-board, with no flame visible. Fluff and material had apparently been pushed into the aperture. (Also witnessed by [[lady-whitehouse]].)

## Historical Documentation

Richard cites Dugdale's *Monasticon Anglicanum*: "Edward III in the 36th year of his reign gave to the Prior and community of Christ Church, Canterbury, the Manor of Borley" — providing independent documentary evidence for a Benedictine monastic connection at the site.

## His Conclusion

> "I, personally, never had any occasion to suspect any member of the household of producing phenomena artificially or fraudulently."

> "I agree with Mr. Price in asserting that this place, which is now a ruin, was once the most haunted house in England."

## The SPR's Detailed Analysis (spr-v)

The 1956 SPR Report subjects Edwin's testimony to the most thorough critique it received:

- His published account was written approximately 8 years after the events (April 1939 write-up; events June–December 1931)
- He suffered a nervous breakdown in 1931 before his visits and a second "maladjustment of some severity" between 1931 and 1939, the period in which he wrote his account
- His letter of 6 November 1940 correcting errors in MHH admitted he "did not *witness* No. 5" (the tumbler incident) — "arriving as I did just after it had taken place." Price's published version implies direct eyewitness observation, and Price did not pass this correction to readers
- The "tumbler circling round" him before coming to rest was not mentioned by Edwin as an impressive feature in his corrections letter; he focused on the glass not breaking. The SPR suggest the "circling" detail — like the "convolutions" of the stiletto and the "mushroom shape to bottle" of the materializing bottle — may have been supplied by Marianne verbally rather than directly observed
- The "mushroom shape to bottle" description of the materializing bottle (EBR, p. 37) does not appear in Edwin's own account at all; it was added by Price
- Edwin was on terms of "friendship" with Marianne and made a special journey to visit the Smiths in December 1931 to gather evidence "to answer those who very rashly suggest and insinuate that the phenomena are being manufactured by somebody in the house"
- He had not fully recovered from his nervous breakdown when at Borley in 1931

## The SPR Attacks and His Defence

Five charges made in the 1956 SPR report:
1. Wrote from memory eight years later (no notes)
2. Had a nervous breakdown affecting his reliability
3. Got his evidence from Marianne second-hand
4. His November 1940 letter contradicts his published account on the tumbler incident
5. Mrs Smith had no recollection of meeting him

All five charges rebutted (his 6 August 1956 letter):
1. His contribution to Price's book explicitly referenced notes "made within ten days of arrival" — the SPR report omitted mention of these entirely
2. The breakdown was 1924–25, not 1931 — Goldney "acknowledged her memory was at fault"
3. He "utterly repudiates" getting the stiletto story from Marianne — he watched her while it happened
4. Casual language: "just after" = "soon after" — the same incident
5. Mrs Smith also denied meeting [[sidney-glanville|Glanville]], who clearly did meet her

> "I never cast suspicion on Marianne... because I knew after the stiletto incident, bottles, tumbler and so forth... that whatever other things she may for some obscure reason have done herself, she certainly did not manufacture the stiletto landing on my lap."

The GOTB authors find him "impressive as a witness and obviously sincere."

## Marianne's Retrospective Accusation (hbrs-ch20)

In the 1958 Swanson interviews and the 1979 Iris Owen interviews, [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] suggested that Edwin was a **confederate of [[lionel-foyster|Lionel's]]** in producing phenomena — possibly responsible for the wall-writings himself. She noted that the phenomena "suddenly stopped" when Edwin left the district to become a priest (around January 1932, coinciding with the Marks Tey Circle séance that appeared to cleanse the house). She considered this "not a coincidence." 

> **Contradiction:** This directly contradicts Edwin's own repeated denials and his specific account of the stiletto incident (where he was an independent observer with hands occupied). Marianne's accusation was made retrospectively, decades later, under sustained investigative pressure when she was seeking to deflect responsibility from herself.

## Clarke's Account (slbr-10): Edwin as Catalyst

Andrew Clarke offers a more detailed biography and a different interpretive frame. Rather than focusing on Edwin as a witness to evaluate, Clarke analyses Edwin as a **catalyst** who drove the phenomena to their peak:

- **Service history:** Midshipman at the Battle of Jutland (1916); invalided out of the Royal Navy in 1917. Worked at Whittall & Co. in Istanbul; returned to England mid-1920s for treatment at a Harrow nursing home (anxiety neurosis). Drifted into teaching.
- **First visit:** 6 June 1931, nothing happened.
- **The "high water mark" day (June 8):** Clarke's analysis suggests Marianne was deliberately performing — the "flimsy jerry bed" that tipped Marianne out three times was a lightweight occasional bed that collapsed if its occupant jumped out quickly. Edwin's phrase *"I saw her start"* before the stiletto "flew" onto his lap is telling.
- **The close relationship with Lionel:** Owen and Mitchell's Marianne's Story noted they "played a lot of chess together... spent much time in each other's company." Eileen Garrett's letter to Trevor Hall: Marianne *"rather intimated that Whitehouse and Foyster were exceedingly intimate, which suggested a spot of homosexuality."*
- **In "Fifteen Months":** Lionel transformed Edwin into "Miss Edith Greycastle" and recorded only three of his ~thirty visits.
- **Outcome:** Lady Whitehouse eventually banned Edwin from the Rectory, fearing for his mental state. *"Borley Rectory did Edwin no good at all."* He was subsequently **re-admitted to mental hospital**.

Clarke's view: Edwin's intense uncritical belief, his encouragement of spirit communication, and his introduction of Anglo-Catholic rituals (relics, scapulae, novenas) directly shaped the character of the phenomena — including the wall-writings' "Light Mass Prayer" content.
