# Dom Richard Whitehouse OSB (Leslie Edwin Whitehouse, b. 1899 Istanbul)

The most extensively defended witness of the Foyster incumbency. Born Leslie Edwin Whitehouse in 1899 in Istanbul; son of [[francis-cowley-whitehouse|Francis Cowley Whitehouse]] (canon to the British Embassy in Istanbul) and grandson of J. W. Whittall, head of a successful mohair/opium trading empire [1]. By his relatives he was affectionately nicknamed "Boy" [1, 2]. A Benedictine monk; nephew of Sir George and [[lady-whitehouse|Lady Whitehouse]] of Arthur Hall, Sudbury. He made approximately thirty visits to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] between June and December 1931. His chapter in *The Most Haunted House in England* (ch. 15) is the most detailed first-person witness account in the entire Borley corpus. He died in St Hugh's Nursing Home, Cleethorpes, in 1975 [9, 10].

## Background and Mental Health (per O'Connor, hbrs-ch11)

His mental health was fragile — a fact heavily scrutinised by critics. As a fifteen-year-old midshipman, he served in the Royal Navy during World War I and suffered terrible trauma at the Battle of Jutland, invalided out in 1917 with "nerves or shell shock" [3–5]. In 1924–25 he was treated for anxiety neurosis at Bowdon House nursing home, Harrow (Dr Crichton-Miller's facility), but ran away before completing his treatment [5, 6]. He spent time in Switzerland, worked at Whittall & Co. in Istanbul, and returned to England mid-1920s. He suffered an unreciprocated romance and heard that a cousin had killed herself.

By May 1931 he had drifted into teaching and was "seriously contemplating the priesthood" when his aunt [[lady-whitehouse|Lady Whitehouse's]] letter about the Foyster phenomena arrived. O'Connor describes him as "a damaged and troubled young man." He was officially ordained as a Benedictine monk on 18 May 1940, taking the name Dom Richard Whitehouse, and later taught Latin and Physical Education at Assumption House School in Ramsgate [9–11].

O'Connor notes that Edwin and [[lionel-foyster|Lionel]] "became very close friends... 'exceedingly intimate', with the suggestion of an attraction between the two men." [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] was dismissive: Edwin was "too ascetic" to take interest in women.

## How He Came to Borley

He was staying with his uncle and aunt at Arthur Hall when he received [[lady-whitehouse|Lady Whitehouse's]] letter describing the Foyster phenomena. He had known the Rectory since boyhood. He consulted [[father-herbert-thurston|Father Herbert Thurston]] (Jesuit; sympathetic to paranormal) before visiting, who warned him that blessings had "little effect" on poltergeist cases. He first visited the Rectory on 6 June 1931.

## What He Witnessed (first-hand, mhh-ch15)

**The brass stiletto incident (June 8, 1931)**: Richard sat with [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] while she lay in bed in Room 7 (beside the Blue Room). He sat at the foot of the bed, could see the whole room; Marianne was lying with hands under bedclothes. After ten minutes of conversation: "I felt something land lightly on my lap, coming apparently from behind me." He picked it up: a brass stiletto (paper knife), approximately 8–9 inches, weighing nearly 8 oz, belonging in the study below. Marianne said she had watched it "rise up from the floor behind me and do one or two curious convolutions in the air before settling on my lap." Richard was certain she could not have thrown it — her hands were under the covers throughout [15–17].

He then left the room briefly; heard a piercing shriek; rushed back to find Marianne face-down on the floor with the mattress on top of her. "The whole thing must have happened in an instant, for I am quick on my feet and I was no distance from the room."

**November 13, 1931 — the kitchen bottle materializations**: With Marianne and maid [[katie-foyster-maid|Katie]] in the kitchen, doors and windows closed [18, 19]:
- A bottle burst under Mrs. Foyster's chair
- Fifteen minutes later, another burst under Richard's chair as he sat down
- A third bottle "poised itself in mid-air within a foot or so of the kitchen ceiling" for one to two seconds before crashing to the floor — all three witnessed this
- A fourth bottle rolled in through a door from the staircase without breaking, circling the floor

**The Tumbler Contradiction (December 14, 1931)**: Whitehouse claimed in *MHH* that a fragile tumbler dropped near his feet, circled him, and came to rest without breaking [20, 21].

> **Contradiction:** In a November 1940 letter to [[harry-price|Harry Price]] correcting his own account, Whitehouse admitted: "to be strictly accurate I did not *witness* No 5 [the tumbler incident], arriving as I did just after it had taken place" [22, 23]. Price deliberately ignored this crucial retraction and continued to publish the incident as firsthand testimony in the second edition [24, 25].

**The Novena message (June 16, 1931)**: Richard and Mrs. Foyster completed a nine-day Rosary novena asking for guidance. On the last day, kneeling on the landing near the Chapel, both simultaneously became "aware of a presence." Turning to check a wall, Richard found a fresh pencil message: "Get light mass and prayers. M." — appearing while they were both present in the corridor. He was "sorry afterwards that I did not have the writing photographed."

**Bell-ringing directly witnessed**: Stood in the kitchen passage watching the bells moving "to and fro, the only people in the house often standing alongside me, witnessing the same performance." Cut wires and all.

**Skirting-board fire**: He and the Foysters discovered smoke issuing from a room, and a hole "about the size of a child's fist" burned into the skirting-board, with no flame visible. Fluff and material had apparently been pushed into the aperture. (Also witnessed by [[lady-whitehouse]].)

## Catholic Influence and the Wall Writings

Whitehouse was largely responsible for introducing a distinctly Roman Catholic flavour to the Borley hauntings. He supplied the Foysters with Catholic scapulars to pin to their underwear and a relic of St John Vianney (the Curé d'Ars) to unlock doors [26–28]. He also conducted Catholic Novenas (nine days of prayer) in the house. During the Novena of 16 June 1931, the famous [[wall-writings|wall writing]] "Get light mass and prayers" appeared, to which the word "Here" was added when Whitehouse prayed asking where the mass should be offered [27, 29].

Another wall writing heavily associated with him was interpreted by critics as reading "Mrs Foyster," but Edwin excitedly interpreted it as "Ibi Mass by Boy," believing the spirits were addressing him by his childhood nickname [2, 30]. Fascinatingly, during [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s 1937–38 tenancy, the single word "Edwin" appeared on a wall. According to professional graphologist Lewis T. Ackermann, this was the *only* piece of wall writing at Borley not executed by Marianne Foyster [31–33].

## The Foyster Novel Pseudonym

A curious biographical detail: despite Edwin being a tall, ex-Navy man and a frequent guest, [[lionel-foyster|Foyster]] completely changed his identity and gender in the unpublished novel *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*, disguising him as a maiden niece named "Miss Edith Greycastle" [34–37]. Owen and Mitchell's *Marianne's Story* noted they "played a lot of chess together... spent much time in each other's company."

## Historical Documentation

Richard cites Dugdale's *Monasticon Anglicanum*: "Edward III in the 36th year of his reign gave to the Prior and community of Christ Church, Canterbury, the Manor of Borley" — providing independent documentary evidence for a Benedictine monastic connection at the site.

## His Conclusion

> "I, personally, never had any occasion to suspect any member of the household of producing phenomena artificially or fraudulently."

> "I agree with Mr. Price in asserting that this place, which is now a ruin, was once the most haunted house in England."

## The SPR's Detailed Analysis (spr-v)

The 1956 SPR Report subjects Edwin's testimony to the most thorough critique it received:

- His published account was written approximately 8 years after the events (April 1939 write-up; events June–December 1931)
- He suffered a nervous breakdown in 1924–25 (not 1931, as Goldney initially claimed) and a second "maladjustment of some severity" between 1931 and 1939, in which he wrote his account
- His November 1940 letter correcting errors in *MHH* admitted he "did not *witness* No. 5" (the tumbler incident); Price did not pass this correction to readers
- The "tumbler circling round" detail, the "convolutions" of the stiletto, and the "mushroom shape to bottle" of the materializing bottle may have been supplied verbally by Marianne rather than directly observed
- The "mushroom shape to bottle" description (EBR, p. 37) does not appear in Edwin's own account at all; it was added by Price
- Edwin was on terms of "friendship" with Marianne and had not fully recovered from his earlier breakdown

In the 1956 *Borley Report*, [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen Goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] attacked Whitehouse's credibility. Kathleen Goldney even travelled to Ramsgate on the day the report was published and threatened Whitehouse in front of his abbot, warning him that if he defended Marianne, they would publicly expose his mental health history [41, 42].

## The SPR Attacks and His Defence

Five charges made in the 1956 SPR report:
1. Wrote from memory eight years later (no notes)
2. Had a nervous breakdown affecting his reliability
3. Got his evidence from Marianne second-hand
4. His November 1940 letter contradicts his published account on the tumbler incident
5. Mrs Smith had no recollection of meeting him

All five charges rebutted (his 6 August 1956 letter):
1. His contribution to Price's book explicitly referenced notes "made within ten days of arrival" — the SPR report omitted mention of these entirely
2. The breakdown was 1924–25, not 1931 — Goldney "acknowledged her memory was at fault"
3. He "utterly repudiates" getting the stiletto story from Marianne — he watched her while it happened
4. Casual language: "just after" = "soon after" — the same incident
5. Mrs Smith also denied meeting [[sidney-glanville|Glanville]], who clearly did meet her

> "I never cast suspicion on Marianne... because I knew after the stiletto incident, bottles, tumbler and so forth... that whatever other things she may for some obscure reason have done herself, she certainly did not manufacture the stiletto landing on my lap."

The GOTB authors find him "impressive as a witness and obviously sincere."

## Marianne's Retrospective Accusation (hbrs-ch20)

In the 1958 Swanson interviews and the 1979 Iris Owen interviews, [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] suggested that Edwin was a **confederate of [[lionel-foyster|Lionel's]]** in producing phenomena — possibly responsible for the wall-writings himself. She denied the stiletto incident ever happened, accused him of being "squiffy" (drunk or eccentric) and highly emotional, and complained that he was a nuisance who talked obsessively about sin [3, 46–48]. She noted that the phenomena "suddenly stopped" when Edwin left the district to become a priest (around January 1932), considering this "not a coincidence." She also intimated to investigator [[eileen-garrett|Eileen Garrett]] that Whitehouse and her husband Lionel were "exceedingly intimate, which suggested a spot of homosexuality in the relationship" [36, 52].

> **Contradiction:** This directly contradicts Edwin's own repeated denials and his specific account of the stiletto incident (where he was an independent observer with hands occupied). Marianne's accusation was made retrospectively, decades later, under sustained investigative pressure when she was seeking to deflect responsibility from herself.

## Clarke's Account (slbr-10): Edwin as Catalyst

[[andrew-clarke|Andrew Clarke]] offers a more detailed biography and a different interpretive frame. Rather than focusing on Edwin as a witness to evaluate, Clarke analyses Edwin as a **catalyst** who drove the phenomena to their peak:

- The "high water mark" day (June 8): Clarke's analysis suggests Marianne was deliberately performing — the "flimsy jerry bed" that tipped Marianne out three times was a lightweight occasional bed that collapsed if its occupant jumped out quickly. Edwin's phrase *"I saw her start"* before the stiletto "flew" onto his lap is telling.
- Lady Whitehouse eventually banned Edwin from the Rectory, fearing for his mental state. "Borley Rectory did Edwin no good at all." He was subsequently re-admitted to mental hospital.
- Clarke's view: Edwin's intense uncritical belief, his encouragement of spirit communication, and his introduction of Anglo-Catholic rituals (relics, scapulae, novenas) directly shaped the character of the phenomena — including the wall-writings' "Light Mass Prayer" content.
