# Vernon O'Neil

Son of [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] and her fourth husband [[robert-oneil|Robert Vincent O'Neil]]. The couple married in Ipswich on 11 August 1945, emigrated to America almost immediately, and Robert O'Neil died shortly afterwards "trying to save a child in a road accident." As of 1972, when *Ghosts of Borley* was published, Vernon O'Neil was "a member of a religious sect in America."

No further details are given. His existence is noted here primarily as part of the biographical record of [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]]'s post-Borley life.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**"Vernon O'Neil"** is an inaccurate name used in Robert Wood's book _The Widow of Borley_ to refer to **Robert Vincent O'Neil Jr.**, who was better known to Borley researchers as **Vincent O'Neil** (or "Vince") [1, 2]. A self-styled **"Son of Borley,"** he was the adopted child of Marianne Foyster O'Neil, the most controversial resident of Borley Rectory [3, 4]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals a life marked by an obscured identity, a dramatic discovery of his legacy, and a prominent but ultimately aborted role as a Borley archivist:

**Birth, True Identity, and Contradictory Dates**
Vincent was actually born **Peter Richard Howlett** in Ipswich [5, 6]. The sources contain a slight contradiction regarding his exact date of birth: _The Widow of Borley_ records it as **October 8, 1945**, while _The Borley Rectory Companion_ dates it to **October 9, 1945** [5-8]. 
*   His biological mother, Kate Howlett, had an affair with an American serviceman, and her husband agreed to reconcile only on the condition that the child be disposed of or aborted [8]. 
*   Marianne undertook to take the baby in November 1945 [6, 8]. She falsely informed her new American husband, Robert O'Neil, that he was the father, and the boy was baptized Robert Vincent O'Neil Jr. on **July 11, 1946** [9, 10]. 
*   In August 1946, Marianne emigrated to the United States with the baby, somehow bypassing US military bureaucracy without a birth certificate for the child [9, 11].

**Childhood in America and the "Religious Sect" Claim**
Vincent's early life in America was unstable, spent in towns like La Crosse and Bangor in Wisconsin, Hokah in Minnesota, and Jamestown and Fargo in North Dakota [2, 12, 13]. 
*   By early 1951, his adoptive parents had separated, and the five-year-old was placed temporarily in the La Crosse Home for Children, for which his adoptive father paid nothing [14, 15].
*   As a teenager, Vincent struggled with his mother's secrecy regarding his background [16]. After getting into trouble for stealing a car, the court threatened to send him to a correctional center [16]. To avoid this, Marianne sent him to live on a farm with Mormon friends in Utah, where he eventually married and started a family [16, 17]. 
*   It is this move to Utah that likely prompted Robert Wood to assert in 1992 that "Vernon O'Neil" was now **"a member of a religious sect in America"** [1]. 

**Discovering the Borley Connection (1994)**
Vincent grew up completely unaware of his mother's famous past or the "most haunted house in England" [3, 4, 18]. Following Marianne's death in December 1992, he began researching his genealogy, posting a public request for help on the internet on **September 8, 1994** [18-20]. 
*   On **September 22, 1994**, an English historical enthusiast named Nick Rowland contacted him, revealing his true birth name, his stay at a transit camp in Tidworth, Hampshire, and his mother's notorious connection to Borley Rectory [7, 19, 20]. 
*   During this same period, "heir-hunters" from Wisconsin were desperately trying to track Vincent down to prevent his mother's pension from being dissolved [21]. They succeeded only after unmasking a fanciful alias Marianne had used to register at a school district: **"Ena Howlett O'Neil"** [21].

**The 1997 Pilgrimage**
Fascinated by his origins, Vincent planned an extensive trip to England between **September 17 and September 26, 1997** [22]. 
*   The trip was partly funded by a £250 contribution from Joe McNally, the editor of _The Fortean Times_ [23, 24]. 
*   During this visit, Vincent met several prominent researchers, including Peter Underwood, Stewart P. Evans, Alan Wesencraft, Montague Keen, and Guy Lyon Playfair, and also visited the grave of Lionel Foyster at Campsey Ashe [22, 24-27]. 
*   Despite all this, Vincent noted that he was ultimately disappointed by the trip because he experienced absolutely no paranormal phenomena while at Borley [27].

**Writings, Websites, and Debunking Louis Mayerling**
Embracing his history, Vincent established the **borleyrectory.com** website in 1998 and founded the internet-based **Borley Ghost Society**, a group whose members eventually included Uri Geller and Colin Wilson [3, 22, 28]. He published several works based on his mother's and Lionel's unpublished papers, including the e-book _Borley Rectory: The Ghosts That Will Not Die_ (2001), _The Most Haunted Woman in England_, _Things My Mother Tried to Teach Me_, and a combined compilation titled _Fifteen Months in the Most Haunted House in England_ [29, 30].

When Louis Mayerling (alias George Carter) published the notorious 2000 hoax book _We Faked the Ghosts of Borley Rectory_, Vincent vigorously repudiated him. He stated to Peter Underwood: **"I no longer believe that Mayerling knew my mother, and if he met her at all, it was only for a brief period of time... The time spent with my mother happened only in his imagination. Unfortunately, he has lived with these false ideas so long, he probably now believes them to be true. There is no credible evidence to support his claims"** [31, 32].

**The 2004 Withdrawal and Final Stance**
On **November 30, 2004**, Vincent abruptly disbanded the Borley Ghost Society and pulled his website from the internet [33-35]. He cited the ongoing disturbances and vandalism caused by amateur ghost hunters in Borley village as his primary reason for withdrawing, declaring that he no longer wished to promote the case [33]. He also shelved his project to publish Lionel Foyster's original novel manuscript and declined to be involved in the compilation of _The Borley Rectory Companion_ [34].

Despite ending his public involvement, and despite his mother's later claims that she faked much of the phenomena, Vincent remained convinced of the core mystery, concluding: **"My conclusion was that something happened at Borley. How much happened is opened for debate, but something strange did happen there"** [36, 37].
