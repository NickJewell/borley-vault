# Iris Owen

Parapsychology researcher and author, based in Toronto, Canada. Co-author (with Pauline Mitchell) of the 1979 report *Marianne's Story — The Alleged Haunting of Borley Rectory*, published in the *Journal of the British Society for Psychical Research* (September 1979). One of the last researchers to interview Marianne Foyster before her death.

## Background

Grew up in Cambridgeshire, England — "not very far away" from Borley. Read about the Borley haunting in the 1930s as it was happening. After the war, met and married **George Owen** (scholar of poltergeist phenomena) and lived in Cambridge for 20 years, where they met [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] and became interested in psychical research.

Moved to Toronto with her husband. Became co-founder and researcher at the **New Horizons Foundation**, a parapsychology research organisation. George Owen authored *Can We Explain the Poltergeist?*

## The Philip Experiment (1972)

With a group of eight people, Owen conducted a landmark experiment: creating a wholly fictional historical character named "Philip" and attempting to communicate with him through séances.

Philip — invented, with a completely fabricated biography — communicated through rapping, table-moving, and switching lights on and off. He displayed consistent character traits, a sense of humour, and performed on live television. **Yet there never was a real Philip.**

Key implications for Borley:
- Séance communicators identify as whoever participants expect
- Planchette messages reflect participants' subconscious minds
- The Blue Room séance at which "Harry Bull" appeared was most likely this type of communication
- The planchette sessions identifying "Marie Lairre" are similarly explicable

## Contact with Marianne (1977–1986)

Pauline Mitchell tracked down Marianne in La Crosse, Wisconsin in 1977 and enlisted Owen's help. Owen corresponded with and interviewed Marianne over several years under a promise of confidentiality (nothing published until Marianne's death). The report was written in 1986; the full version was circulated privately to a few investigators.

Owen's assessment of Marianne: "I completely believe that her version of what happened is the correct one." She described her as "a delightful person — witty, charming... clever — but someone to whom life had dealt a hard hand."

Her central finding: the dominant factor in the Foysters' lives was not the haunting but **Lionel's terminal prognosis** and their financial anxiety. Lionel conceived a fictional Borley book to make money, as Walter Hubbell had with the Amherst poltergeist. Most Foyster-period phenomena were either this fiction or genuine minor poltergeist activity.

Owen believed Edwin Whitehouse ([[dom-richard-whitehouse]]) was the most likely focus for any genuine poltergeist events during the Foyster period.

## Contact with Vincent O'Neil

In May 1995, O'Neil wrote to Owen through the Parapsychology Foundation; she replied within two weeks. Over subsequent months she became "one of my most ardent supporters, supplying me with vast quantities of research and books." She gave him a first edition of *The Most Haunted House* and all her notes for the New Horizons report.

Owen died before the publication of O'Neil's web book.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Iris May Owen** (née Pepper) was a highly respected British-Canadian psychical researcher. While she was not present during the active hauntings at Borley Rectory, she became a pivotal figure in the case’s later history by successfully tracking down and interviewing its most famous and elusive resident, Marianne Foyster, in the 1970s. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, events, quotes, and research contributions:

**Biographical Details and Psychical Research Background**
*   **Early Life:** Born in Meldreth, Cambridgeshire, in 1916, Owen grew up not far from Borley. She later recalled, "My association with the affairs at Borley goes back a long way. I remember reading about it during the 1930's when it happened, as my childhood home was not very far away" [1, 2].
*   **Marriage and Cambridge Years:** In 1952, she married A. R. George Owen, a Fellow of Trinity College, Cambridge. The couple lived in Cambridge for 20 years, during which time they developed a keen interest in psychical research [1, 2].
*   **Connection to Trevor Hall:** While at Cambridge, her husband was appointed to supervise the ESP research of Trevor Hall—the man who would later become Harry Price’s fiercest critic. Iris Owen noted that Hall "was personally convinced the whole thing [Borley] was a fraud, and everything he did was slanted to that perception" [2, 3].
*   **Move to Canada and "The Philip Experiment":** The Owens eventually moved to Toronto, Canada. Between 1970 and 1984, Iris served as the Assistant Program Director of the New Horizons Research Foundation [1]. She is famous in parapsychology for a 1972 five-year table-tipping experiment where her group successfully "conjured" an entirely fictional ghost named Philip to prove that poltergeist outbreaks can be the result of the living mind. She documented this in her 1976 book, *Conjuring Up Philip* [1, 4, 5].

**The 1977 Marianne Foyster Interviews**
In 1976, Owen received a call from Paulene Mitchell, a Canadian book reviewer who had tracked Adelaide Foyster’s family and wanted an experienced psychical researcher to help her find and interview Marianne Foyster [6]. 
*   **The Meeting:** Supported by a grant from the Perrott-Warrick Electors of Trinity College, Cambridge, Owen and Mitchell met the 79-year-old Marianne at the Stoddard Hotel in La Crosse, Wisconsin, in 1977 [7, 8]. 
*   **The Promise:** Marianne spoke freely to Owen about her life but extracted a strict promise: Owen and Mitchell had to do everything in their power to keep Marianne's past a secret from her adopted American son, Vince O'Neil, and they were forbidden from publishing her full story until after her death [8, 9]. 

**The Owen/Mitchell Report (1979) and Findings**
In March 1979, the New Horizons Research Foundation released Owen and Mitchell's typescript report, *The Alleged Haunting of Borley Rectory*, with an abridged version published in the Journal of the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) in September 1979 [7]. The report relayed Marianne's startling claims:
*   **Lionel Foyster's Trickery:** Marianne claimed that the majority of the events were invented by her husband, Lionel, who threw objects to test visitors' reactions [10, 11]. 
*   **The Novel:** She asserted that Lionel was deliberately trying to replicate the financial success of the famous Amherst Mystery (which occurred near the Foysters' former home in Canada) by fabricating the Borley haunting for a best-selling novel [12, 13].
*   **Harry Price's Complicity:** Marianne suggested that Harry Price was well aware that Lionel's diary was fictional, but "it suited his purpose to pretend to believe it as truth" [12, 13].

**Contradictions and Clashes with Other Researchers**
Owen's completely sympathetic view of Marianne put her at direct odds with other Borley researchers:
*   **Conflict with Trevor Hall:** Owen strongly rejected Trevor Hall's harsh assessment of Marianne. She wrote, "Trevor devoted a lot of time to investigating Marianne's background... put together a picture of Marianne as an adventuress, a bigamist, a baby farmer, and obviously someone who would take part in frauds to suit her own ends" [3, 14]. Owen countered that she found Marianne to be "a delightful person - witty, charming... clever - but someone to whom life had dealt a hard hand" [8, 15].
*   **Conflict with Peter Underwood:** Veteran ghost-hunter Peter Underwood publicly criticized Owen's report, arguing that she had fallen into a trap. He stated, "It is quite obvious to anyone who has simply examined her various statements that Marianne has been leading people up the garden path for years," calling Marianne "unreliable and decidedly unimpressive as a witness" [16, 17].
*   **The Poltergeist Paradox:** A major contradiction in Owen's findings was that despite accepting Marianne's claims that Lionel fabricated the haunting for financial gain, Owen and Mitchell still concluded that "genuine poltergeist activity did occur" at the Rectory [18]. 

**Specific Quotes by Iris Owen**
*   **On Marianne's honesty:** "It is my firm belief that what Marianne told us was true... At eighty years old, with the end of her life in sight, why would she lie?" [14, 15].
*   **On the Borley evidence:** In a June 1995 letter to Marianne's son, Vince O'Neil, Owen expressed her ultimate view on the case's authenticity: **"From a parapsychological point of view we have never felt that it mattered whether or which of the events alleged to have happened at Borley were genuine or the result of trickery, because there are so many [other] well documented up-to-date cases which prove that indeed these phenomena do exist"** [19].
*   **On the Borley Cult:** Acknowledging the public's fascination with the Rectory, she astutely observed: **"the general buying public does not seem to care very much whether the stories are true or not; they are prepared to believe them"** [20].
