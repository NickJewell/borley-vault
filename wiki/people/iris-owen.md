# Iris Owen (née Pepper)

(Born Meldreth, Cambridgeshire, 1916.) Parapsychology researcher and author, based in Toronto, Canada. Co-author (with [[paulene-mitchell|Pauline Mitchell]]) of the 1979 report *Marianne's Story — The Alleged Haunting of Borley Rectory*, published in the *Journal of the British Society for Psychical Research* (September 1979). One of the last researchers to interview [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] before her death.

## Background

Owen grew up in Cambridgeshire, not far from Borley. She later recalled: "My association with the affairs at Borley goes back a long way. I remember reading about it during the 1930's when it happened, as my childhood home was not very far away" [1, 2].

In 1952, she married A. R. George Owen, a Fellow of Trinity College, Cambridge, and the couple lived in Cambridge for twenty years, developing a keen interest in psychical research [1, 2]. While at Cambridge, her husband was appointed to supervise the ESP research of [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] — the man who would later become [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s fiercest critic. Owen noted that Hall "was personally convinced the whole thing [Borley] was a fraud, and everything he did was slanted to that perception" [2, 3].

The Owens eventually moved to Toronto, Canada. Between 1970 and 1984, Iris served as the Assistant Program Director of the **New Horizons Research Foundation** [1].

## The Philip Experiment (1972)

With a group of eight people, Owen conducted a landmark experiment: creating a wholly fictional historical character named "Philip" and attempting to communicate with him through séances.

Philip — invented, with a completely fabricated biography — communicated through rapping, table-moving, and switching lights on and off. He displayed consistent character traits, a sense of humour, and performed on live television. **Yet there never was a real Philip.** Owen documented this in her 1976 book, *Conjuring Up Philip* [1, 4, 5].

Key implications for Borley:
- Séance communicators identify as whoever participants expect
- Planchette messages reflect participants' subconscious minds
- The [[blue-room-seance|Blue Room séance]] at which "Harry Bull" appeared was most likely this type of communication
- The planchette sessions identifying "[[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]" are similarly explicable

## Contact with Marianne (1977–1986)

In 1976, Owen received a call from [[paulene-mitchell|Paulene Mitchell]], a Canadian book reviewer who had tracked [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide Foyster's]] family and wanted an experienced psychical researcher to help find and interview [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] [6]. Supported by a grant from the Perrott-Warrick Electors of Trinity College, Cambridge, Owen and Mitchell met the 79-year-old Marianne at the Stoddard Hotel in **La Crosse, Wisconsin**, in **1977** [7, 8].

Marianne spoke freely but extracted a strict promise: Owen and Mitchell had to do everything in their power to keep Marianne's past secret from her adopted American son, [[vincent-oneil|Vince O'Neil]], and were forbidden from publishing her full story until after her death [8, 9].

## The Owen/Mitchell Report (1979) and Findings

In March 1979, the New Horizons Research Foundation released Owen and Mitchell's typescript report; an abridged version was published in the *Journal of the Society for Psychical Research* in September 1979 [7]. The report relayed Marianne's startling claims:

- **Lionel Foyster's trickery:** Marianne claimed that the majority of the events were invented by her husband, who threw objects to test visitors' reactions [10, 11].
- **The novel:** She asserted that Lionel was deliberately trying to replicate the financial success of the Amherst Mystery (which occurred near the Foysters' former home in Canada) by fabricating the Borley haunting for a best-selling novel [12, 13].
- **Price's complicity:** Marianne suggested that [[harry-price|Harry Price]] was well aware that Lionel's diary was fictional, "but it suited his purpose to pretend to believe it as truth" [12, 13].

Owen's central finding: the dominant factor in the Foysters' lives was not the haunting but **Lionel's terminal prognosis** and their financial anxiety. Most Foyster-period phenomena were either this fiction or genuine minor poltergeist activity. Owen believed [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] was the most likely focus for any genuine poltergeist events during the Foyster period.

Owen's assessment of Marianne: **"It is my firm belief that what Marianne told us was true... At eighty years old, with the end of her life in sight, why would she lie?"** [14, 15] She described her as "a delightful person — witty, charming... clever — but someone to whom life had dealt a hard hand" [8, 15].

## Contradictions and Clashes with Other Researchers

Owen's completely sympathetic view of Marianne put her at direct odds with other Borley researchers:

- **Conflict with Trevor Hall:** Owen strongly rejected Hall's harsh assessment of Marianne, counter-arguing that he had assembled "a picture of Marianne as an adventuress, a bigamist, a baby farmer, and obviously someone who would take part in frauds to suit her own ends" [3, 14].
- **Conflict with Peter Underwood:** [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] publicly criticised Owen's report, arguing that she had fallen into a trap, calling Marianne "unreliable and decidedly unimpressive as a witness" and stating: "It is quite obvious to anyone who has simply examined her various statements that Marianne has been leading people up the garden path for years" [16, 17].
- **The poltergeist paradox:** A major contradiction in Owen's findings was that despite accepting Marianne's claims that Lionel fabricated the haunting for financial gain, Owen and Mitchell still concluded that "genuine poltergeist activity did occur" at the Rectory [18].

## Contact with Vincent O'Neil

In May 1995, [[vincent-oneil|O'Neil]] wrote to Owen through the Parapsychology Foundation; she replied within two weeks. Over subsequent months she became "one of my most ardent supporters, supplying me with vast quantities of research and books." She gave him a first edition of *The Most Haunted House* and all her notes for the New Horizons report.

Acknowledging the public's fascination with the Rectory, Owen astutely observed: **"the general buying public does not seem to care very much whether the stories are true or not; they are prepared to believe them."** [20]

Owen died before the publication of O'Neil's web book.
