# Dr Walter F. Prince

Dr Walter F. Prince was an American psychical researcher who wrote a detailed critical analysis of the [[great-amherst-mystery|Great Amherst Mystery]] — "A Critical Study of 'The Great Amherst Mystery'" — published in Vol. XIII of the *Proceedings of the American Society for Psychical Research* (New York, 1919, pp. 89–130). He argued formidably for trickery by [[esther-cox|Esther Cox]] while in a state of dissociation.

## The Amherst Analysis

Prince's 1919 paper argued that the published account by [[walter-hubbell|Walter Hubbell]] was greatly embellished compared with earlier and shorter original testimony — precisely the same analysis that the SPR's 1956 investigators would apply to [[lionel-foyster|Foyster]]'s manuscripts in the Borley case. The SPR report draws an explicit parallel between Prince's methodology and their own:

| Amherst | Borley |
|---|---|
| Dr Walter F. Prince demonstrated published account was much embellished vs. the earlier shorter Diary | The SPR investigators demonstrate *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House* was much embellished vs. the *Diary of Occurrences* |

Prince also documented a chronological coincidence relevant to Borley: in December 1878 Esther Cox visited her married sister in **Sackville**, the very town where [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] would be rector almost exactly fifty years later.

Prince's analysis was not widely known outside psychical research circles, meaning the Amherst case continued to be cited by credulous writers as genuine — as it was by [[hereward-carrington|Hereward Carrington]] and [[nandor-fodor|Nandor Fodor]] in their 1953 book, which credited the Amherst case on p. 42 as authentic.

## Harry Price's Library

[[harry-price|Harry Price]] owned two volumes containing Prince's work on Amherst: Carrington's *Personal Experiences in Spiritualism* (London, 1913, pp. 95–124) and the relevant volume of the *Proceedings of the American S.P.R.* The SPR investigators note this to establish that the same *Price* who claimed the Borley wall-writings were "unique in the annals of psychical research" actually possessed books demonstrating that almost identical wall-writings had occurred in the Amherst case.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Dr. Walter F. Prince** was an investigator and psychical researcher whose critical analysis of a famous Canadian poltergeist case was used by skeptics to draw damaging parallels to the Borley Rectory hauntings. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, dates, publications, and connections regarding his work:

**The Amherst Mystery Investigation (1919)**
*   **The Publication:** Dr. Prince is best known in the Borley sources for authoring an exhaustive essay titled **‘A Critical Study of “The Great Amherst Mystery”’** [1]. This study was published in **Volume XIII of the *Proceedings of the American Society for Psychical Research*** (New York) in **1919** (pages 89–130) [1].
*   **His Findings:** His analysis focused on the sensational 1878–1879 poltergeist case surrounding a young woman named Esther Cox in Amherst, Nova Scotia [1]. In his detailed study, Dr. Prince **"argued formidably the case for trickery by Esther whilst in a state of dissociation"** [1].
*   **Exposing Embellishment:** He also successfully demonstrated that the bestselling book written about the Amherst case by Walter Hubbell (*The Haunted House*) was **"much embellished"** when compared to Hubbell's earlier, much shorter diary [2].

**Connection to the Borley Rectory Case**
Dr. Prince's work became highly relevant to Borley when the authors of the skeptical *Borley Report* (Eric Dingwall, Kathleen Goldney, and Trevor Hall) used it to argue that the phenomena during the Rev. Lionel Foyster's incumbency at Borley were fraudulently copied from the Amherst events [2, 3].
*   **The Blueprint for Fraud:** Skeptics noted the highly suspicious fact that Rev. Foyster had lived in Sackville, Nova Scotia—just five miles from Amherst—immediately before moving to Borley, suggesting that the Foysters were familiar with the Amherst case and used it as a blueprint for their own phenomena [3]. 
*   **Contradicting Harry Price:** Ghost hunter Harry Price repeatedly claimed that the mysterious Borley wall messages were "unique in the annals of psychical research" [1]. However, the authors of the *Borley Report* pointed out that Price actually owned a copy of the 1919 *Proceedings* containing Dr. Prince's essay in his personal library [1]. Because Dr. Prince's study detailed how sensational wall messages had previously appeared in the Amherst case, critics used Price's ownership of the text to prove he was fully aware that the Borley wall writings were not unique [1].

**Public Awareness**
*   A lesser-known detail about Dr. Prince's work is that despite his thorough debunking of the Amherst manifestations, the results of his investigation **"never reached the public"** [2]. As a result, the Amherst case continued to be regarded as a classic and was still being quoted by writers as a genuine haunting decades later [2].
