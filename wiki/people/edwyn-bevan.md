# Edwyn R. Bevan, O.B.E., F.B.A., M.A., D.Litt., LL.D.

Archaeologist and scholar of Yatscombe Cottage, Boar's Hill, Oxford. A kinsman of the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]]. In a letter of August 24, 1942, Bevan pointed out a significant detail in [[m-savage|M. Savage]]'s sketches of the spontaneous wall-markings that appeared on May 7, 1938.

## The Waldegrave Crest Identification

[[harry-price|Price]] had published Savage's sketches of the markings in *The Most Haunted House in England*, describing them as resembling "Prince of Wales's feathers." [[william-salter|Professor W. B. Stevenson]] had suggested they might be attempts at the *fleur-de-lis*.

Bevan, as a Waldegrave kinsman, recognised a key distinction that neither Price nor Stevenson had caught:

- **Prince of Wales's crest**: Three ostrich feathers
- **Waldegrave family crest**: **Five** ostrich feathers

Savage's sketches showed three sets of **four** strokes and **one set of five** strokes. Bevan pointed out that the set of five — which appeared at the bottom of the sequence — could represent the final, most careful attempt by the "entity" to draw the Waldegrave crest.

If Bevan's reading is correct, the spontaneous markings under close observation were not random, not the Prince of Wales's device, but a specific heraldic symbol pointing to the Waldegrave family — precisely the family that [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] had independently identified as central to the Borley drama.

## Significance

Bevan's contribution is a small but precise piece of evidence in an already complex puzzle. He had no prior connection to the Borley investigation; his expertise in the Waldegrave crest was familial rather than constructed. The coincidence of the five-feather set appearing in the markings, combined with Bevan's independent identification of its heraldic meaning, gave Price another "indicator" pointing toward [[waldegrave-family|the Waldegraves]].
