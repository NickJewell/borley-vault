# Edwyn R. Bevan, O.B.E., F.B.A., M.A., D.Litt., LL.D.

Archaeologist and scholar of Yatscombe Cottage, Boar's Hill, Oxford. A kinsman of the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]]. In a letter of 24 August 1942, Bevan pointed out a significant heraldic detail in [[m-savage|M. Savage]]'s sketches of the spontaneous wall-markings that appeared on 7 May 1938.

## The Waldegrave Crest Identification

[[harry-price|Price]] had published Savage's sketches of the markings in *The Most Haunted House in England*, describing them as resembling "Prince of Wales's feathers." [[william-salter|Professor W. B. Stevenson]] had suggested they might be attempts at the *fleur-de-lis*.

Bevan, as a Waldegrave kinsman, recognised a key distinction that neither Price nor Stevenson had caught:

- **Prince of Wales's crest**: Three ostrich feathers
- **Waldegrave family crest**: **Five** ostrich feathers

Savage's sketches showed three sets of **four** strokes and **one set of five** strokes. Bevan pointed out that the set of five — which appeared at the bottom of the sequence, suggesting the unseen author's final attempt — could represent the most careful attempt by the "entity" to draw the Waldegrave crest.

If Bevan's reading is correct, the spontaneous markings were not random, not the Prince of Wales's device, but a specific heraldic symbol pointing to the Waldegrave family — precisely the family that [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] had independently identified as central to the Borley drama. Price theorised that the communicating entity (supposedly the ghost nun, [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]) was trying to delineate the Waldegrave crest as an "indicator pointing to a Waldegrave" [4].

## Acknowledgement by Harry Price

Harry Price was highly appreciative of Bevan's insight. In the preface to his 1946 sequel, *The End of Borley Rectory*, Price explicitly lists Edwyn R. Bevan among a select group of distinguished academic and clerical correspondents to whom he was **"especially grateful... for suggestions and help"** in his ongoing research [1].

## Significance

Bevan's contribution is a small but precise piece of evidence in an already complex puzzle. He had no prior connection to the Borley investigation; his expertise in the Waldegrave crest was familial rather than constructed. The coincidence of the five-feather set appearing in the markings, combined with Bevan's independent identification of its heraldic meaning, gave Price another "indicator" pointing toward [[waldegrave-family|the Waldegraves]].
