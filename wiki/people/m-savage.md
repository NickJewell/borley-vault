# M. Savage

BBC television engineer; official observer during [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s 1937–38 tenancy of Borley Rectory.

## Spontaneous Wall Markings (May 7, 1938)

On May 7, 1938, Savage and a friend named Bowden visited the Rectory as observers. During their visit, they witnessed something unusual even by Borley's standards: new pencil markings spontaneously appeared on the walls **while they were standing within a yard of them**.

Savage reported two sets of markings in two different rooms: in the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] (Room No. 6) and in Room No. 5, the dressing-room adjacent to it. The marks appeared while the observers were actively watching.

Savage submitted a written report with sketches of the markings. Price initially described them as resembling **"Prince of Wales's feathers"** — a triple-plume design. This interpretation was later challenged and revised.

## The Waldegrave Crest Identification

When *The Most Haunted House in England* was published, [[edwyn-bevan|Edwyn R. Bevan, O.B.E., F.B.A.]] — a kinsman of the Waldegrave family — wrote to Price pointing out a key discrepancy: the Prince of Wales's crest has **three** ostrich feathers, while the **Waldegrave family crest** has **five**. Reviewing Savage's sketches, Bevan noted that they depicted three sets of four-stroke marks and **one set of five strokes**. The set of five, appearing at the bottom of the sequence, appears to be the final and most deliberate attempt.

This reading transforms the markings from a puzzling coincidence into a possible "indicator" of precisely the family [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] had independently identified as connected to the murder of [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]].

Alternatively, [[william-salter|Emeritus Professor W. B. Stevenson]] suggested the marks might be attempts to draw the *fleur-de-lis*, emphasising Marie's French nationality.

## Significance

Savage's account is notable for two reasons: the spontaneous appearance of markings under conditions of close observation (ruling out unnoticed manual production), and the retrospective identification of the markings as possibly the Waldegrave crest — an element no observer at the time would have been looking for.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mr. M. Savage** was an electrical engineer working for the fledgling British Broadcasting Corporation (BBC) Television Service at Alexandra Palace [1, 2]. He was the last official BBC observer recruited during Harry Price's year-long tenancy investigation of Borley Rectory [3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific events, dates, and quotes regarding his two investigations of the haunted house:

**The First Visit (March 12–13, 1938)**
Savage first visited the Rectory over the weekend of March 12–13, 1938, accompanied by a friend, **Mr. Bridgewater** [2, 4]. During this visit, they shared the house with other official observers, Sidney and Roger Glanville, and Alan Cuthbert [2, 4]. 
*   **The Break-in and Gluey Substance:** Upon inspection, the party discovered that the house had been entered and items were missing from the Base Room [4]. They also found some "curious gluey substance" on the Chapel floor, which they scraped up but could not obtain in sufficient quantities to analyze [3-5].
*   **Auditory Phenomena:** The group heard a scraping noise resembling a chair leg being dragged across the floor of the Blue Room [4, 5]. Savage's official report also recorded a specific event that took place at **1:43 a.m.** while the group was resting in the Base Room. He noted an "extremely heavy knock" that seemed to emanate from the top left-hand side of a bookcase [2, 3]. Taking a measured approach, Savage wrote: **"This knock... appeared to be too heavy to be accounted for by normal house movements caused by changes in temperature, but on the other hand I should not like to say that this was not so"** [2]. 

**The Second Visit (May 7–8, 1938)**
Savage returned to Borley with a BBC colleague, **Mr. Bowden**, on May 7, 1938 [6, 7]. This visit is historically significant as it marked the very last observation period of Harry Price's tenancy [7].
*   **Electrical Traps:** Utilizing his engineering background, Savage installed several "electrical traps" for the entities, including an alarm in the kitchen and another fitted over the famous "cold spot" on the landing outside the Blue Room [8]. He noted that the alarms were inadvertently tested during their tours and found to be in "perfect order!" [8].
*   **Spontaneous Wall Writings:** In his May 10, 1938 report, Savage documented witnessing paranormal wall-writings materializing in real-time [9]. At 9:00 p.m., the two men conducted a "microscopic examination" of the Blue Room walls, ringing every visible mark with chalk [3, 8]. When they returned at exactly 11:00 p.m., they noticed a new, 3/16th-inch vertical pencil mark above the right side of the fireplace [8]. After ringing it, they were surprised to see a second mark appear four inches below it, and within minutes, two more marks appeared on the left side [8]. Savage reported: **"I do not think we could have both missed these marks... and it therefore seems almost certain that they must have been produced while we were actually within a yard of the mantelpiece"** [8].
*   **The "Prince of Wales's Feathers":** During this same visit, Savage and Bowden examined Room No. 5 (the dressing-room leading out of the Blue Room) and discovered new, intricate pencil drawings [3, 8]. Savage sent Price a sketch of these, which included a long series of marks resembling the letter "B" and a specific pattern of lines that looked like the **"Prince of Wales's feathers"** [6, 10]. 

**Later Interpretations of Savage's Discoveries**
The "Prince of Wales's feathers" discovered by Savage became a point of academic speculation. Professor W.B. Stevenson of Edinburgh later theorized that the markings were actually a clumsy attempt by the spirit to draw a **fleur-de-lis** [11, 12]. Since the primary suspect for the haunting was supposedly the murdered French nun "Marie Lairre", Stevenson argued that the drawing was specifically emphasizing the French nationality of the communicator [11, 12].
