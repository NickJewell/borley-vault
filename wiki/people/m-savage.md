# M. Savage

Electrical engineer working for the fledgling BBC Television Service at Alexandra Palace; official observer during [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s 1937–38 tenancy of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] and reportedly the last official BBC observer of that investigation.

## First Visit (March 12–13, 1938)

Savage first visited the Rectory over the weekend of March 12–13, 1938, accompanied by a friend, **Mr. Bridgewater**. They shared the house with other official observers — [[sidney-glanville|Sidney and Roger Glanville]] and [[alan-cuthbert|Alan Cuthbert]].

Upon inspection, the party discovered the house had been entered and items were missing from the [[base-room|Base Room]]. They also found a "curious gluey substance" on the Chapel floor, which they scraped up but could not obtain in sufficient quantities to analyse. The group heard a scraping noise resembling a chair leg being dragged across the floor of the [[blue-room|Blue Room]].

At **1:43 a.m.** while the group was resting in the Base Room, Savage recorded an "extremely heavy knock" that seemed to emanate from the top left-hand side of a bookcase. Taking a measured approach, he wrote: **"This knock... appeared to be too heavy to be accounted for by normal house movements caused by changes in temperature, but on the other hand I should not like to say that this was not so."**

## Second Visit — Spontaneous Wall Markings (May 7–8, 1938)

Savage returned to Borley with a BBC colleague, **Mr. Bowden**, on May 7, 1938 — the very last observation period of Harry Price's tenancy. Utilising his engineering background, Savage installed several "electrical traps" for the entities, including an alarm in the kitchen and another fitted over the famous "cold spot" on the landing outside the Blue Room.

At **9:00 p.m.**, the two men conducted a "microscopic examination" of the Blue Room walls, ringing every visible mark with chalk. When they returned at exactly **11:00 p.m.**, they noticed a new, 3/16th-inch vertical pencil mark above the right side of the fireplace. After ringing it, they were surprised to see a second mark appear four inches below it, and within minutes two more marks appeared on the left side.

Savage reported: **"I do not think we could have both missed these marks... and it therefore seems almost certain that they must have been produced while we were actually within a yard of the mantelpiece."** New, intricate pencil drawings also appeared in Room No. 5 (the dressing-room leading out of the Blue Room), which included a pattern Savage described as resembling the **"Prince of Wales's feathers."**

## The Waldegrave Crest Identification

When *The Most Haunted House in England* was published, [[edwyn-bevan|Edwyn R. Bevan, O.B.E., F.B.A.]] — a kinsman of the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]] — wrote to Price pointing out a key discrepancy: the Prince of Wales's crest has **three** ostrich feathers, while the **Waldegrave family crest** has **five**. Reviewing Savage's sketches, Bevan noted they depicted three sets of four-stroke marks and **one set of five strokes**. The set of five, appearing at the bottom of the sequence, appears to be the final and most deliberate attempt.

This reading transforms the markings from a puzzling coincidence into a possible "indicator" of precisely the family [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] had independently identified as connected to the murder of [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]].

Alternatively, Professor W.B. Stevenson of Edinburgh suggested the marks might be attempts to draw the *fleur-de-lis*, emphasising [[marie-lairre|Marie's]] French nationality.

## Significance

Savage's account is notable for two reasons: the spontaneous appearance of markings under conditions of close observation (ruling out unnoticed manual production), and the retrospective identification of the markings as possibly the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave]] crest — an element no observer at the time would have been looking for.
