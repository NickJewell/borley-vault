# Rev Somerset Pennefather

A clergyman who rented [[borley-rectory]] for a six-week holiday in approximately the summer of 1895 — prior to being appointed Vicar of Kensington in 1896 — with his family, experiencing nothing unusual or supernatural throughout their stay. He is significant as an articulate negative witness from the Bull era, predating the haunting legend's public emergence.

## Identity and Stay

[[andrew-clarke]] names him as "Rev Somerset Pennefather" and dates the stay to 1895:

> *We know of a Rev Somerset Pennefather who stayed there for six weeks with his family in 1895 and encountered nothing strange or unusual about the place.*
> — *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, essay 11

The historical record of this visit comes from his son, [[ws-pennefather|Mr. W. S. Pennefather]] of Walton-on-Thames, who sent [[harry-price|Harry Price]] a letter while he was preparing his first book on Borley. W. S. Pennefather provided the following direct account:

> *"My father, a clergyman, rented this house for his six weeks' holiday one summer, previous to 1896. To the best of my knowledge, no incidents occurred during our stay, and it is unlikely that the then incumbent said anything to my father, as he was desirous of letting the house"* [4].

## Harry Price's Dismissal

When Price published this correspondence he explicitly brushed aside the negative evidence, writing: "I do not for one moment question Mr. Pennefather's assertion that there were 'no incidents' during their stay at Borley, but after forty-five years it would be difficult to remember trivial, but unusual happenings, had they occurred" [1]. The [[spr-investigation|SPR]] authors subsequently highlighted Price's dismissal as evidence of methodological bias, concluding: "Had anything been remembered which would have supported the legend, we may be sure that it would have found a place in MHH" [3].

## Significance

Rev Somerset Pennefather's six-week stay is one of the few documented extended visits to the Rectory during the Bull era by someone outside the Bull family network. The complete absence of phenomena during a stay of this length — at a time when [[harry-bull]] was supposedly witnessing nuns, headless men, and phantom coaches — is strong evidence for the view that such stories were personal to Harry Bull and certain of his sisters rather than general to the building.

Clarke uses the Pennefather visit alongside [[henry-lawton]]'s blank 1933 stay, [[ivy-brackenbury]]'s ten years of experiencing nothing, and [[margaret-finch]]'s similar negative account to build the case that the haunting was not a general property of the building.

> **Note:** Clarke is the first to name the clergyman himself; Price's published accounts identified only the son W.S. Pennefather as correspondent.
