# Rev Somerset Pennefather

A clergyman who rented [[borley-rectory]] for a six-week holiday in 1895 with his family and experienced nothing unusual or supernatural throughout their stay. He is significant as an articulate negative witness from the Bull era, predating the haunting legend's public emergence.

## Identity and stay

[[andrew-clarke]] names him as "Rev Somerset Pennefather" and dates the stay to 1895:

> *We know of a Rev Somerset Pennefather who stayed there for six weeks with his family in 1895 and encountered nothing strange or unusual about the place.*
> — *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, essay 11

His son, W.S. Pennefather, later testified that his father had rented Borley Rectory for a six-week holiday "before 1896" with no phenomena experienced (recorded in Harry Price's observation files). The family wiki page for [[ws-pennefather]] covers the son's testimony.

## Significance

Rev Somerset Pennefather's six-week stay is one of the few documented extended visits to the Rectory during the Bull era by someone outside the Bull family network. The complete absence of phenomena during a stay of this length — at a time when [[harry-bull]] was supposedly witnessing nuns, headless men, and phantom coaches — is strong evidence for the view that such stories were personal to Harry Bull and certain of his sisters rather than general to the building.

Clarke uses the Pennefather visit alongside [[henry-lawton]]'s blank 1933 stay, [[ivy-brackenbury]]'s ten years of experiencing nothing, and [[margaret-finch]]'s similar negative account to build the case that the haunting was not a general property of the building.

> **Note:** The family connection means Rev Somerset Pennefather is the father of W.S. Pennefather, who later quoted his father's experience to Harry Price's investigators. The father's name was unknown to Price's books; Clarke is the first to name him.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Reverend Somerset E. Pennefather** was a clergyman who briefly occupied Borley Rectory in the late nineteenth century and later went on to be appointed the **Vicar of Kensington in 1896** [1]. He features in the Borley sources solely because his time at the Rectory completely lacked any paranormal activity, a fact that later became a point of contention between psychic investigator Harry Price and his critics. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following details regarding his involvement:

**The Six-Week Holiday (c. 1895)**
*   Sometime prior to 1896—a date deduced by investigators to likely be the **summer of 1895**, during the early incumbency of the Rev. Harry Bull—Rev. Pennefather rented Borley Rectory for a **six-week summer holiday** with his family [1-4]. 
*   During this entire six-week stay, the family experienced absolutely **"no phenomena"** or anything strange or abnormal [2, 3].

**The Testimony of W. S. Pennefather**
*   The historical record of this visit comes from Rev. Pennefather's son, **Mr. W. S. Pennefather** of Walton-on-Thames [3, 4]. 
*   While Harry Price was preparing his first book on Borley, W. S. Pennefather sent him a letter detailing his family's stay. He provided the following direct quote: **"My father, a clergyman, rented this house for his six weeks’ holiday one summer, previous to 1896. To the best of my knowledge, no incidents occurred during our stay, and it is unlikely that the then incumbent said anything to my father, as he was desirous of letting the house"** [4].

**Harry Price's Dismissal**
*   When Harry Price published this correspondence, he explicitly **brushed aside** the negative evidence [3]. 
*   Price justified his dismissal by arguing that the account was provided over four decades later, writing: **"I do not for one moment question Mr. Pennefather’s assertion that there were ‘no incidents’ during their stay at Borley, but after forty-five years it would be difficult to remember trivial, but unusual happenings, had they occurred"** [1].

**The SPR Critique**
*   The skeptical authors of the Society for Psychical Research's *Borley Report* (Eric Dingwall, Kathleen Goldney, and Trevor Hall) later highlighted the Pennefather holiday to criticize Price’s biased methodology [3]. 
*   They argued that Price deliberately discounted normal, everyday accounts to protect his sensational ghost story, concluding: **"Had anything been remembered which would have supported the legend, we may be sure that it would have found a place in MHH [*The Most Haunted House in England*]"** [3].
