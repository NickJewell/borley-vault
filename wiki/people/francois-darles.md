# François d'Arles (père and fils)

Two generations of the d'Arles family were associated with Borley Rectory during the [[marianne-foyster|Foyster]] incumbency (1930–1935).

## Real Identity (per O'Connor, hbrs-ch10)

"François d'Arles" was a pseudonym. His real name was **Frank Charles Pearless**, born in Bermondsey, son of a family of leather workers. His family lived for a time in Arles, southern France (where his father worked in a shoe shop), and the young Pearless became fluent in French and adopted the town as his surname. The war record he described to Lionel was genuine: Royal Army Service Corps, France and Salonica; awarded the 1914 Star, British War Medal and Victory Medal.

**His "dead wife" story was fabricated.** He was not a widower: he was married to Ada Ewens (married Boxing Day 1918) with two legitimate children (Renée and Leonard), who remained in Stoke Newington. His son "François Junior" (actually called Douglas) was the illegitimate result of an affair with Emily Fernie of Worcestershire (child born 1928); Fernie had abandoned the child to D'Arles's care. The moving "widower with a motherless son" story was invented to secure the job.

## François d'Arles Jr. (Douglas Pearless)

A young boy presented as "Francois Junior" — "big blue eyes and brown curls, and an ever present smile." He came to live at the Rectory after an advertisement in *The Times* for a companion for Adelaide. Marianne "fell in love with him" immediately; he and Adelaide became close friends.

His father came initially for interviews; subsequently took up the cottage.

## François d'Arles Sr.

Answered [[lionel-foyster|Lionel's]] advertisement in *The Times* personals in early 1931, advertised for an odd-job man. Short, dark, thick-set and handsome; boasted an inflated military record. O'Connor: "a con man, womaniser and fantasist." He rapidly dominated the household.

**The affair with Marianne:** within weeks of arriving, he was sleeping with the rector's wife. Marianne admitted it was "no romantic love-match" — she "disliked him intensely" but was addicted to him sexually. They "fought like cats and dogs"; the sex was "violent." His actual black eye at breakfast was from this relationship, not from punching a ghost.

He lived in the Rectory Cottage; increasingly took meals at the main house and spent nights there. Lionel, aware of the situation, merely described Marianne as "a naughty girl."

He lived in the rectory cottage (not the main house), though took his midday meals at the rectory.

Foyster's *Fifteen Months* manuscript (as published in gtwnd) refers to him and his son using the pseudonym "Francois" and "F. de Arles."

### The Blackmail

D'Arles threatened to tell the Bishop about Marianne allowing him to live with her at the rectory. He also threatened to tell the Bishop that "Lionel was responsible for the ghost" — a significant threat to Lionel's position. Marianne eventually went to Lionel's cousin Bernard Foyster (the family lawyer) who removed d'Arles.

### The Fake Ghost

Marianne admitted (Swanson interview) that she, d'Arles, and others made a luminous cardboard figure draped in a sheet, suspended between two pulleys, to frighten d'Arles Sr. when he returned one evening — suspecting him of staging phenomena himself: "We made up a very crude figure and put this sheet around it. We happened to have some luminous paint... this did scare d'Arles and he never did play any more of his tricks again."

### Séance Witness

D'Arles witnessed several poltergeist incidents firsthand. On one occasion, waking in the night and seeing "a shadowy form" in his room, he jumped out of bed and lunged at it — "his hands went right through the apparition and he encountered nothing except the wall." He then received "a terrific smack in the face" and appeared at breakfast with a black eye.

### His Possible Role

Owen/Mitchell raise the possibility that d'Arles could have been a confederate of Lionel's, creating phenomena when Lionel was not present. He and Lionel spent considerable time together.

## Clarke's Account (slbr-14): Full Biography and Phenomena Role

Andrew Clarke provides additional detail on Pearless's origins and role:
- He sold flowers from a **barrow outside a London cemetery** — not a master florist. His move to France was after WWI; he opened a shoe store in Arles and adopted the town name.
- Clarke confirms: **Marianne initiated the affair**, citing the Swanson interview: *"I think it was several weeks before we had sexual relations."*
- Pearless had a **key to the servants' door** and knew of the **external cellar trapdoor** in the courtyard (noted by Price's observers Westland and Ryberg in 1937). From the cellars he could: throw bottles two at a time up the back stairs; simulate footsteps in the library by pressing on the floor from below; exit via the courtyard without being seen.
- He **confessed to Ian Shaw** that he had caused much of the haunting and advised Marianne that the Marks Tey Circle visit should be used as a staged finale to end it. *"Things had got out of hand."*
- The Marks Tey night was a joint production: Marianne the star; Pearless providing the mechanical effects from below.

## Later Life and Death (slbr-14)

After the flower shop failure (1934), Pearless's subsequent life:
- Married Jessie Irene Dorothy Mitchell, August 1934 (she divorced him 1944)
- Worked as a taxi-driver, then motor mechanic
- Emigrated to Australia with third wife (Jessie Pring Steed) in the 1950s
- Returned to London **1966**; went straight into hospital; died approximately six weeks later
- **Buried in an unmarked pauper's grave** — family had no money for a funeral
