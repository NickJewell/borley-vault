# François d'Arles (père and fils)

Two generations of the d'Arles family were associated with [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] during the [[marianne-foyster|Foyster]] incumbency (1930–1935).

## Real Identity

"François d'Arles" was a pseudonym. His real name was **Frank Charles Pearless** (sometimes spelled Peerless), born on **November 10, 1894**, in Bermondsey, South London, from a family of leather workers [1, 2, 7]. He also used the aliases "Black" and "Wade," and was given the pseudonym "Mr. Lawless" in [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]]'s unpublished manuscript, *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House* [4–6].

His family lived for a time in Arles, southern France, where his father worked in a shoe shop and the young Pearless became fluent in French, later deriving his famous pseudonym from the town. The war record he described to [[lionel-foyster|Lionel]] was genuine: he served in the Royal Army Service Corps in France and Salonica and was awarded the 1914 Star, British War Medal, and Victory Medal [7].

**His "dead wife" story was fabricated.** He was not a widower: he was married to Ada Ewens (married Boxing Day 1918) with two legitimate children (Renée and Leonard), who remained in Stoke Newington [1, 2, 7]. His son "François Junior" (actually called Douglas) was the illegitimate result of an affair beginning in 1927 with Emily Fernie, a gamekeeper's daughter, who gave birth in July 1928; Fernie had abandoned the child to Pearless's care [7]. The moving "widower with a motherless son" story was invented to secure the job. In reality, he made a living selling flowers from a barrow outside Stoke Newington and Highgate cemeteries [7, 11].

## François d'Arles Jr. (Douglas Pearless)

A young boy presented as "François Junior" — "big blue eyes and brown curls, and an ever present smile." He came to live at the Rectory after an advertisement in *The Times* for a companion for [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide]]. [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] "fell in love with him" immediately; he and Adelaide became close friends. His father came initially for interviews; subsequently took up the cottage.

## François d'Arles Sr.

Pearless (using the name d'Arles) answered [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]]'s advertisement in *The Times* personals in early 1931 for an odd-job man. Short, dark, thick-set and handsome; he falsely claimed to be a French-Canadian widower who had served in the French Secret Service and was an important figure at the Elstree film studios [5, 8–10]. O'Connor describes him as "a con man, womaniser and fantasist."

> **Contradiction:** Some accounts suggest d'Arles placed the ad himself seeking a home for his son, or that Marianne placed it [10], rather than Lionel advertising for an odd-job man.

D'Arles arrived on a motorcycle with his young son, whom he introduced as "François d'Arles Jr." [15, 16]. He officially rented the adjacent [[rectory-cottage|Rectory Cottage]], though he took most of his meals and frequently slept in the Rectory itself [11, 19, 20].

### The Affair with Marianne

Within weeks of his arrival, d'Arles and [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] began a sexual relationship [21, 22]. According to [[ian-shaw|Ian Shaw]] (Marianne's son from a previous marriage), Marianne initiated the affair, persuading d'Arles to take her for a ride in his motorcycle sidecar to a lonely spot [23, 24]. The relationship was devoid of romance and highly volatile; Marianne admitted she was "desperate" for sex but found d'Arles a "dictatorial man," noting that they "fought like cats and dogs" [25–27]. Despite the obvious affair, [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Foyster]] tolerated the ménage à trois, reportedly chastising Marianne only as a "naughty girl" [28, 29]. Investigators later theorised that the frail rector derived voyeuristic or vicarious pleasure from the arrangement [30, 31].

### Role in the Poltergeist Phenomena

D'Arles's arrival coincided with the most violent period of the Borley hauntings [32, 33].

**The Black Eye:** D'Arles claimed that he awoke one night to find a shadowy form in his bedroom. He leaped out of bed to fight it with his fists, but his hands passed through the figure and "the ghost" delivered a terrific smack to his face [34–36]. He appeared at breakfast with a black eye, though researchers strongly suspect this was actually the result of a violent lovers' quarrel with Marianne [27, 36].

**The Paint Pot:** [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] recorded hearing a noise and finding a paint pot pushed against the inside of d'Arles's bedroom door, which d'Arles denied placing there [37, 38].

**Vouching for the Foysters:** During [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s visit in October 1931, when a bell mysteriously rang, d'Arles intervened to vouch for Marianne's innocence, claiming he had looked into the Foysters' bedroom and seen them both sound asleep [39, 40].

**Confession of Fraud:** [[andrew-clarke|Clarke]] confirms from the Swanson interview that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] initiated the affair. Pearless had a **key to the servants' door** and knew of the **external cellar trapdoor** in the courtyard (noted by Price's observers Westland and Ryberg in 1937). From the cellars he could throw bottles two at a time up the back stairs, simulate footsteps in the library by pressing on the floor from below, and exit via the courtyard without being seen. D'Arles eventually confessed to [[ian-shaw|Ian Shaw]] that he had faked much of the phenomena [41, 42]. He also claimed he advised Marianne that the haunting had gone too far and suggested they use the visit of the [[marks-tey-spiritualist-circle|Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle]] in January 1932 as a convenient excuse to "exorcise" the ghosts and stop the charade [42, 43].

**The Luminous Ghost Prank:** D'Arles's own trickery backfired when Marianne and Ian Shaw rigged a crude cardboard torso covered in a luminous sheet and suspended it between two pulleys [44–46]. When d'Arles returned one evening, they operated the pulleys; it terrified him so badly that he ceased playing his own ghostly tricks [45, 46].

### The Baby (John Evemond Emery)

In May 1932, an infant named John Evemond Emery was brought to the Rectory — officially adopted, though rumours persisted that Marianne had secretly given birth [47, 48]. D'Arles was fiercely jealous and convinced the child was his own [47, 49]. When the baby tragically died at five months old, d'Arles was devastated; locals recalled seeing him weeping openly at the infant's unmarked grave in Borley churchyard [47, 50, 51].

### Jonquille et Cie and Blackmail

In February 1933, financed by [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]], Marianne and d'Arles opened a florist shop called *Jonquille et Cie* (Daffodil & Co.) at 20a Worple Road, Wimbledon [52, 53]. They lived together in the flat above the shop as husband and wife during the week, returning to Borley only on weekends [52, 53]. D'Arles showed little aptitude for the work, and the business was a financial disaster [54].

The relationship ended bitterly in late 1934 when Marianne discovered d'Arles was sleeping with their 16-year-old shop assistant [55]. In retaliation, d'Arles attempted to blackmail the Foysters, threatening to report Marianne's previous marriage and [[lionel-foyster|Lionel's]] mental state to the Bishop of Chelmsford [56, 57]. Lionel's cousin, [[bernard-foyster|Bernard Foyster]] (a solicitor), had to intervene to physically evict d'Arles and end the harassment [12, 58].

### Opinions of Contemporaries

D'Arles was almost universally disliked by those who met him. The Misses Bull described him as "an odd and peculiar man" and "an extraordinary man" [1, 34, 62]. [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] called him "a very strange and frightening man, thick-set and unpleasant" [1]. The Rectory nurse, Miss Dytor, distrusted him deeply, noting that he was manipulative, cruel, and completely dominated the frail rector [63]. [[ian-shaw|Ian Shaw]] later referred to him as a vicious con man, whilst other researchers characterised him simply as the "Rasputin" of Borley Rectory [6].

## Later Life and Death

After the flower shop failure (1934), Pearless married Jessie Irene Dorothy Mitchell on August 8, 1934 (she divorced him October 1944), then married Jessie Steed (née Pring) on November 10, 1944 [1, 2, 12]. He emigrated to Australia with his third wife in the 1950s, returning to London in 1966 [1, 12, 60]. He died on October 10, 1966, at St. Ann's Hospital in Tottenham [12, 60, 61]. Because his family lacked the funds for a funeral, this central figure of the Borley haunting was buried in an unmarked pauper's grave [12].
