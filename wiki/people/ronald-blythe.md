# Ronald Blythe

Writer and former librarian at Colchester, and a frequent visitor to [[james-turner|James Turner]] and [[catherine-turner|Catherine Turner]] at [[rectory-cottage|Borley Priory cottage]]. Blythe was "no more than very slightly interested in alleged psychic happenings at Borley" and had almost forgotten the Turners' mention of a possible phantom cat — making him an ideal unprimed witness when, in late October 1949, he encountered it himself.

## Phantom Cat Sighting

During a pre-supper visit to the bathroom, Blythe went upstairs in unusually bright moonlight and did not trouble to light a candle. As he washed, he saw a distinct feline shape sliding along, cat-fashion, against the bath. Assuming he had been accompanied by one of the Turner cats, he turned to look — and found the room empty. An immediate and thorough search found neither Fred nor Holly (the Turners' two cats) anywhere near the bathroom.

When asked to describe what he saw, his account tallied closely with the Turners' own recollection of the unidentified grey-white, lean, scraggy creature that had earlier terrified their cat Fred. Blythe did not connect his experience to the earlier phantom-cat episodes; he had practically forgotten the Turners had mentioned them.

[[john-dening|John Dening]], who happened to be staying at the cottage at the time (though away at Liston Rectory during the incident itself), recalled:

> "I well remember the quiet, nonchalant way in which Mr Turner, during breakfast the following morning, remarked casually: 'Oh, by the way, Ronnie saw the phantom cat last night,' and proceeded to pass on at once to some other topic of conversation, just as if the incident were quite unimportant!"

## Significance

Blythe's sighting is the strongest independent confirmation of [[phantom-cat|the phantom cat]], because he was: an uninvited witness with no expectation of seeing anything, unable to see clearly in the moonlit room (making misidentification unlikely), and his description was independently corroborated by the Turners' earlier account.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Ronald Blythe** (also referred to by friends as **"Ronnie"**) appears in the Borley sources as an independent witness to phenomena during the time James and Catherine Turner occupied the Rectory Cottage. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following biographical details and specific events regarding his involvement:

**Biographical Details**
*   **Profession:** At the time of his Borley experiences, Blythe was working as a librarian at the **Colchester Library** [1, 2]. He is described as a writer who would later become a **"much-respected writer on East Anglian rural life"** [1, 2].
*   **Stance on the Paranormal:** The texts describe him as a **"fresh and entirely disinterested witness."** Prior to his Borley encounter, he had absolutely **"no previous experience of paranormal phenomena"** and held only a very slight interest in the alleged hauntings [3]. 

**The Phantom Cat Encounter (Late October 1949)**
*   **The Sighting:** During one of his frequent visits to the Turners at the Rectory Cottage in late **October 1949**, Blythe went upstairs to the unlit bathroom before supper. The room was illuminated only by unusually bright moonlight [3]. 
*   **The Apparition:** As he washed his hands, he saw a **"distinct feline shape sliding along, as cats do, against the bath"** [3]. He initially assumed it was one of the Turners' two real cats (named Fred and Holly), but quickly realized the animal didn't remotely resemble either of them [3]. 
*   **Disappearance:** When he turned from the basin to get a better look, he found he was completely alone in the room, and an immediate search found no sign of the real cats anywhere in the vicinity [3]. 
*   **Corroboration:** When Blythe described the animal, his account of its **"leanness and scragginess"** tallied remarkably well with a "spectre" cat that the Turners had seen previously [3]. 
*   **The Turners' Reaction:** The next morning at breakfast, James Turner demonstrated his characteristically nonchalant attitude toward the hauntings by casually telling another guest, **"Oh, by the way, Ronnie saw the phantom cat last night,"** before immediately changing the subject as if the incident were completely unimportant [4].

**The "Face in the Wall" Sketch (September 1950)**
*   Blythe was also a talented artist. When the Turners were digging around the orchard wall and unearthed what appeared to be earlier flint foundations, they found a medieval stone face built into the wall about six feet from the ground [1, 2]. 
*   On **September 10, 1950**, James Turner wrote to investigator Peter Underwood about the discovery and enclosed a sketch of the "Face" that had been **executed by Ronald Blythe** [1, 2]. 
*   On **September 24, 1950**, Underwood visited Borley to examine the relic *in situ*, where he personally met and congratulated Blythe on his **"excellent drawing"** [1, 2].
