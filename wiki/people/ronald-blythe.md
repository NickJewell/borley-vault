# Ronald Blythe

Writer and librarian at Colchester Library; later to become a much-respected writer on East Anglian rural life. A frequent visitor to [[james-turner|James Turner]] and [[catherine-turner|Catherine Turner]] at [[rectory-cottage|Borley Priory cottage]], he is described as "a fresh and entirely disinterested witness" with no previous experience of paranormal phenomena and only slight interest in the alleged hauntings — making him an ideal unprimed witness when, in late October 1949, he encountered the [[phantom-cat|phantom cat]] himself.

## Phantom Cat Sighting (Late October 1949)

During a pre-supper visit to the bathroom, Blythe went upstairs in unusually bright moonlight and did not trouble to light a candle. As he washed, he saw a distinct feline shape sliding along, cat-fashion, against the bath. Assuming he had been accompanied by one of the Turner cats, he turned to look — and found the room empty. An immediate and thorough search found neither Fred nor Holly (the Turners' two cats) anywhere near the bathroom. When he described the animal, his account of its "leanness and scragginess" tallied remarkably well with the spectre cat the Turners had seen previously.

[[john-dening|John Dening]], who happened to be staying at the cottage at the time (though away at Liston Rectory during the incident itself), recalled:

> "I well remember the quiet, nonchalant way in which Mr Turner, during breakfast the following morning, remarked casually: 'Oh, by the way, Ronnie saw the phantom cat last night,' and proceeded to pass on at once to some other topic of conversation, just as if the incident were quite unimportant!"

## The "Face in the Wall" Sketch (September 1950)

Blythe was also a talented artist. When the Turners were digging around the orchard wall and unearthed what appeared to be earlier flint foundations, they found a medieval stone face built into the wall about six feet from the ground. On **10 September 1950**, James Turner wrote to [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] about the discovery and enclosed a sketch of the "Face" executed by Ronald Blythe. On **24 September 1950**, Underwood visited Borley to examine the relic *in situ*, where he personally met and congratulated Blythe on his "excellent drawing."

## Significance

Blythe's phantom cat sighting is the strongest independent confirmation of the phenomenon, because he was: an uninvited witness with no expectation of seeing anything, unable to see clearly in the moonlit room (making misidentification unlikely), and his description was independently corroborated by the Turners' earlier account.
