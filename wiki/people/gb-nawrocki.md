# Lieutenant G.B. Nawrocki

Doctor of medicine, Lieutenant in the Polish Army Medical Corps, stationed at Kirkcaldy. Had read most of [[harry-price|Price]]'s books and taken a long interest in psychical research. Wrote to Price after reading Price's 1943 article on Borley in *Everybody's Weekly*.

## Investigations

Nawrocki and Polish Army colleagues made two overnight visits to the ruins at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in summer 1943, producing minute-by-minute protocols in "perfect English."

**First night (June 28–29, 1943)**: Present — Colonel J. Wroblewski, Lt. W. Kujawa, 2nd Lt. A. Ligaszewski, Lt. Nawrocki.
- 8:10 p.m.: Stone thrown at Nawrocki in kitchen passage on entering.
- 8:15 p.m.: Remaining party enter; stones thrown at Kujawa in kitchen passage (hitting him on shoulder) and more at Nawrocki in bathroom passage and chapel.
- 9:05 p.m.: Kitchen door (shut when they ascended to first floor) opens and closes again with a great noise; no one on ground floor.
- 9:10 p.m.: Party build seats and improvised table from bricks and a half-burnt drawer in the dining-room.
- 11:05 p.m.: Nawrocki sees a black shadow moving slowly between trees on the Nun's Walk; vanished between second and third tree.
- 2:40 a.m.: Half a brick thrown from the ceiling, falling directly in the centre of the makeshift table — immediately after Kujawa jokingly said "a brick could easily be thrown from the ceiling upon our heads."
- 4:30 a.m.: Many whisperings heard by Nawrocki in the kitchen passage; no words distinguishable.

**Second night (July 28–29, 1943)**: Present — Kujawa and Nawrocki, plus two English boys. Nawrocki noted he was on the Rectory on the traditional "nun's anniversary" (July 28), though unaware of the tradition.
- Stones thrown in Blue Room and passage outside chapel on arrival.
- Reconstructed floor of Blue Room with bricks and straw.
- Nawrocki in Room 5 window, Kujawa in Blue Room window, English boys in summer-house.
- 11:00 p.m. to 00:30 a.m.: Scratchings in Blue Room fireplace; twenty or thirty stones thrown in Blue Room and near the Cold Spot; dull thumps from ground floor.
- 11:25 p.m.: Shadow moving between trees on Nun's Walk — stopped between second and third tree, vanished after 5–10 seconds (same location as first visit).
- 00:30 a.m.: Shadow of a man silhouetted on the chapel wall, standing motionless for 10–20 seconds; then vanishing slowly. Seen independently by English boys who had come upstairs after a thump.
- 2:35 a.m. and 3:30 a.m.: Dull thumps from ground floor.

Nawrocki asks whether the shadows were the ghost of the nun and the Rev. Harry Bull respectively. Price doubts the Harry Bull identification but notes the July 28 tradition — the same date the Bull sisters saw the nun in sunlight in 1900.

## SPR Assessment: A Probable Hoax (spr-vii)

The 1956 SPR Report regards the Polish account as "so open to suspicion that it is difficult to resist the impression that it was a hoax of some kind":

- Stone-throwing had previously occurred at Borley only when [[marianne-foyster]] or [[harry-price]] was present — never during the official observer period (1937–38) and not during the Cambridge Commission visits
- The Cambridge Commission made visits on 22 June and 16–17 July 1943 — immediately bracketing the Polish visits on 28 June and 28 July — and "produced no results of any kind at all"
- The party allegedly "rebuilt the floor of the Blue Room in the space of an hour" and maintained a watch from its windows; yet Price had written in 1939 that the Blue Room was entirely inaccessible: "No human being could have climbed up to that window... there was nothing to stand on"
- The Harry Bull apparition had been seen only by [[marianne-foyster]] during the entire history of the case; Price himself wrote "I doubt whether the ghost of Harry Bull has ever been seen at the Rectory"
- Nawrocki heard "whisperings" in the kitchen passage — not reported during the entire official observer period (confirmed by [[sidney-glanville]])
- [[robert-fordyce-aickman]], visiting the ruins on 24 and 31 July 1943, was told by someone that overnight stays cost a fee, with "reference to a group of Polish officers who had paid £5"; he also encountered a noisy group entering the cottage at 1:30 a.m. during one of his overnight visits

The SPR was unable to locate the original documents for this account.
