# Constance Price (née Constance Mary Knight, 1881–c.1969)

Widow of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]. She and Price grew up together at New Cross in south-east London, becoming childhood sweethearts as school pupils [2–4]. She was the only daughter of Robert Hastings Knight [1].

## Early Life, Marriage, and Domestic Arrangement

Her family was wealthy; following the death of her father, Constance inherited property and government bonds, and her mother, Mary Ann Knight, moved to a house called "Riverside" in Pulborough, West Sussex [3, 4].

Constance and Harry were married on **1 August 1908** at St Mary's Church, Pulborough [3, 5].

> **Contradiction:** A lesser-known biographical detail reveals a scandalous background to their union. Constance's strict mother harboured a hostile attitude towards her new son-in-law because he and Constance had slept together as teenagers, thereby — in her parents' eyes — wrecking her prospects of making a "good marriage" [6]. According to one of Price's great friends, Price never slept with Constance again after the marriage and embarked on numerous affairs [6]. *(Note: Constance herself in 1968 claimed they moved into "Arun Bank" in 1907 or 1909, though the marriage is explicitly recorded as 1908 [2].)*

Following a honeymoon in Scarborough, they initially lived with Constance's mother at "Riverside," but the domestic arrangement was so unhappy that Price commissioned the building of a new house, [[arun-bank|"Arun Bank"]], on an adjacent plot [6, 7]. They never had children [6], and Constance found her husband's enthusiasm for psychical research distasteful and took absolutely no part in his work [6, 8, 9]. She stated: "I myself have no interest in and could not carry on his work. In the past I have had too much to do about the house" [9].

## Character and Domestic Life

[[arun-bank|Arun Bank]] contained a workshop with the [[borley-rectory|Borley]] bell over the door. Constance was deaf and used the bell occasionally to summon her brother and sister-in-law next door. At 87 (October 1968) she still lived alone with many relatives nearby. The house still felt as though Price "had just popped out for a moment" — his coats, hats, and walking sticks still at the rack by the front door, twenty years after his death [18, 19].

## Harry Price's Death

Price died on **28 March 1948** in his study chair. He had posted a letter, returned home, and sat down. Constance went to make tea; when she returned he was dead, his freshly lit pipe still smoking in his hand [10, 11]. She was "inundated with letters, literally hundreds of them" in the days after his death.

In a letter to [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] dated 8 April 1948, she wrote: "It was a great shock to me, as you can imagine, to find my husband dead in his chair, as he seemed the same as usual half-an-hour before. I shall miss him terribly..." [12]. When besieged by reporters eager to know if the great ghost-hunter might return from the dead, Constance firmly stated that her husband "never said he believed he would be able to 'come back' in any form after he died" [13].

## On the SPR Attack

Constance had not read the [[the-borley-report|SPR book]] (1956): her Midland Bank trustee advised against it and she complied. On [[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]] and [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]]: "I think Mrs Goldney was a two-faced woman and Eric Dingwall was no better. They had often been there and she had looked on them as friends." On the unnamed man who had been "snooping about" (almost certainly [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]]): "asking the rector and other people questions about what kind of man Harry had been" [16]. She could not understand the attacks, remarking: "These people were our friends. They know it is not true — why do they say these things?" [15]

She always liked [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]].

## *Borley III*

Price had "just written the first chapter for a third book on haunted Borley Rectory and drafted out what the book would contain." She did not know what would happen to it.

## The Borley Bell

Constance used the bell hung over the workshop to summon family. After her death, the University of London and Midland Bank (executors) presented the bell to [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]], who hung it in his garden.

## Underwood's Visit

On **20 October 1968**, [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] and [[steuart-kiernander|Steuart Kiernander]] visited Constance at [[arun-bank|Arun Bank]] [17]. She was described at the time as a deaf, "rather dumpy, tinted-spectacled figure, about five feet and six inches in height," accompanied by a barking Scottie dog [17]. She served exquisite sherry on a silver tray on the balcony overlooking the River Arun and the Sussex Downs. Underwood sat in Price's favourite study chair at Constance's invitation.

> **Contradiction:** A minor age discrepancy exists in the records: during Underwood's visit in October 1968, she claimed she had just turned 87, placing her birth in October 1881 [20]; however, another record suggests she died on April 25, 1973, at the age of 94 [9, 21].
