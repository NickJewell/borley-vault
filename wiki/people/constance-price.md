# Constance Price (née Constance Mary Knight, 1881–c.1969)

Widow of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]. Childhood sweetheart turned long-term wife; they grew up together at New Cross in south-east London. Married at Pulborough Church; lived next door for a year before building "Arun Bank" on the River Arun, moving in around 1907–1909.

## Character and Domestic Life

Harry Price's home: goldfish in an octagonal pond (a heron eventually ate them all), a Scottie dog, a workshop with the [[borley-rectory|Borley]] bell over the door. Constance was deaf and used the bell occasionally to summon her brother and sister-in-law next door. At 87 (October 1968) she still lived alone with many relatives nearby. The house still felt as though Price "had just popped out for a moment" — his coats, hats, and walking sticks still at the rack by the front door.

## Harry Price's Death

Price died on **28 March 1948** in his study chair. He had posted a letter, returned home, and sat down. Constance went to make tea; when she returned he was dead. She was "inundated with letters, literally hundreds of them" in the days after his death.

## On the SPR Attack

Constance had not read the [[the-borley-report|SPR book]] (1956): her Midland Bank trustee advised against it and she complied. On [[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]] and [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]]: "I think Mrs Goldney was a two-faced woman and Eric Dingwall was no better. They had often been there and she had looked on them as friends." On the unnamed man who had been "snooping about" (almost certainly [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]]): "asking the rector and other people questions about what kind of man Harry had been."

She always liked [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]].

## *Borley III*

Price had "just written the first chapter for a third book on haunted Borley Rectory and drafted out what the book would contain." She did not know what would happen to it.

## The Borley Bell

Constance used the bell hung over the workshop to summon family. After her death, the University of London and Midland Bank (executors) presented the bell to [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]], who hung it in his garden.

## Underwood's Visit

Underwood sat in Price's favourite study chair at Constance's invitation. She served exquisite sherry on a silver tray on the balcony overlooking the River Arun and the Sussex Downs.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Constance Mary Price (née Knight)** was the wife of the renowned psychical researcher Harry Price, with whom she shared a marriage lasting almost forty years. Despite her husband's intense involvement in the Borley Rectory case, Constance remained entirely detached from his investigations. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, contradictions, quotes, and events regarding her life:

**Early Life, Marriage, and Lesser-Known Details**
*   **Childhood Sweethearts:** Constance was the only daughter of Robert Hastings Knight [1]. She and Harry Price both grew up in South East London and had been school pupils together at New Cross, becoming childhood sweethearts [2-4]. 
*   **Inheritance:** Her family was very wealthy; following the death of her father, Constance inherited property and government bonds, and her mother, Mary Ann Knight, moved to a house called "Riverside" in Pulborough, West Sussex [3, 4]. 
*   **A Strained Marital Beginning:** Constance and Harry were married on August 1, 1908, at St Mary's Church, Pulborough [3, 5]. However, a lesser-known biographical detail reveals a scandalous background to their union: Constance's strict mother harboured a hostile attitude towards her new son-in-law because he and Constance had slept together as teenagers, thereby—in her parents' eyes—wrecking her prospects of making a "good marriage" [6]. They were effectively obliged to marry, chaining Constance to a man who was not wealthy at the time, and according to one of Price's great friends, **Price never slept with Constance again and embarked on numerous affairs** [6]. 
*   **Arun Bank:** Following a honeymoon in Scarborough, they initially lived with Constance's mother at "Riverside," but the domestic arrangement was so unhappy and tense that Price commissioned the building of a new house, "Arun Bank," on an adjacent plot [6, 7]. *(Note: A contradiction exists in the dates provided by Constance herself; in 1968 she claimed they built the house and moved in 1907 or 1909, though her husband explicitly recorded their marriage as taking place in 1908)* [2].
*   **Childless and Disinterested:** Though their marriage was long, they never had children [6]. Constance found her husband's enthusiasm for psychical research distasteful and took absolutely no part in his work [6, 8, 9]. When interviewed shortly after his death, she stated: **"I myself have no interest in and could not carry on his work. In the past I have had too much to do about the house"** [9].

**Harry Price's Death**
Constance was the sole witness to her husband's sudden death on Easter Monday, March 29, 1948 [10]. Price had been feeling unwell but had gone out to post a letter. When he returned to Arun Bank, Constance offered to make them both a cup of tea. When she returned to his study with the tray, she was shocked to find him dead in his armchair from a massive heart attack, his freshly lit pipe still smoking in his hand [10, 11]. 

In a letter to investigator Peter Underwood dated April 8, 1948, she wrote: **"It was a great shock to me, as you can imagine, to find my husband dead in his chair, as he seemed the same as usual half-an-hour before. I shall miss him terribly..."** [12]. 

When besieged by reporters eager to know if the great ghost-hunter might return from the dead, Constance firmly stated that her husband **"never said he believed he would be able to 'come back' in any form after he died"** [13]. 

**Later Life, Contradictions, and the "Borley Report"**
Following Price's death, his reputation was fiercely attacked by members of the Society for Psychical Research (SPR), particularly Eric Dingwall, Mollie Goldney, and Trevor Hall. Constance was deeply hurt by these allegations [14]. Defending her husband, she could not understand the attacks, remarking: **"These people were our friends. They know it is not true – why do they say these things?"** [15]. 

She came to regard Mollie Goldney as a "two-faced woman" and Eric Dingwall as "no better," and expressed disdain for a man (likely Trevor Hall) who had been "snooping about and asking the rector and other people questions about what kind of man Harry had been" [16]. She explicitly chose never to read the highly critical SPR _Borley Report_, having been advised against doing so by a trustee from the Midland Bank [16].

On October 20, 1968, Peter Underwood and Steuart Kiernander visited Constance at Arun Bank [17]. She was described at the time as a deaf, "rather dumpy, tinted-spectacled figure, about five feet and six inches in height," accompanied by a barking Scottie dog [17]. A poignant and somewhat haunting detail noted during this visit was that, twenty years after his death, **Constance still kept several of Harry Price's coats and hats hanging on the hall rack, and his walking sticks in the corner, "almost as though he had just gone out and would be back again very shortly"** [18, 19]. 

*(Note: There is a minor age contradiction in the records: During Underwood's visit in October 1968, she claimed she had just turned 87, which would place her birth in October 1881 [20]. However, another timeline records that she died on April 25, 1973, at the age of 94 [9, 21].)*
