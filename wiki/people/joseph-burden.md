# Joseph W. Burden

Christ Church, Oxford. Official observer at Borley Rectory, December 15–19, 1937; accompanied initially by [[t-stainton|T. Stainton]] and later remaining alone in the house for approximately 24 hours — one of only two observers ever to do so without a companion (the other being [[mark-kerr-pearse]]). Burden borrowed Mr. Arbon's cat for behavioural experiments, and independently corroborated several phenomena including wailing sounds, dragging noises, and footsteps.

## The Visit with Stainton (December 15–17)

Arrived with T. Stainton; conducted routine sealing and inspection. Between 6:10 and 6:25 p.m. on December 16, heard:

- A "whining or hooting" from the direction of the courtyard;
- Then "the sound of shuffling footsteps coming from the direction of the scullery";
- Then at approximately 6:25, "sound of swishing garments as though someone were walking in upstairs hall."

At 1:30 a.m. on December 17: "Continual wailing sound heard (higher and more continuous note than previous wailing) ... seemed to be coming from the Blue Room, but seemed to penetrate everywhere as it could be heard equally clearly in Base Room, hall, or kitchen passage." It stopped at 1:42 a.m. — an independently-timed 12-minute episode.

At 12:10 a.m.: "Noise heard (too loud to be mistaken) of something being dragged along floor. Two seconds duration, at two second intervals." — confirmed by both Burden and Stainton.

**The cat experiment** (Stainton's watch): Borrowed [[mr-and-mrs-arbon|Arbon]]'s cat and tested its reactions in the house. The cat:
- Was frightened by moonlight on the floor;
- Rubbed her back against the "strange coat" apport as if recognising an old friend;
- Was "particularly interested in Room 7."
- She did not appear to react to the "fields of known psychic activity" in any dramatically abnormal way beyond the above.

## Solo Overnight (December 18–19)

After Stainton left, Burden spent approximately 24 hours alone in the Rectory.

At 6:10 p.m.: heard "talking and footsteps coming from direction of the main hall while sitting in Base Room." They could not be traced and he concluded they might have come from Mr. and Mrs. Arbon (not visible from any window).

During this period, had "an uncanny sense of being watched in Room 2" — one of the few accounts of a subjective "presence" feeling in a specific named room.

At 10:35 p.m.: made his last inspection, found no new marks, and went to sleep — sleeping until 8:30 a.m. Final check found nothing disturbed.

## Corroboration

Burden discussed his experiences with [[sg-welles|S. G. Welles]], who visited two months later in February 1938. Welles confirmed that Burden "clearly remembers a lath of those dimensions being in the scullery" in a different position — corroborating the "appearance" of the lath that Welles's party noted in a new position.

Appendix D lists Burden under: "Footsteps and Similar Sounds," "Dragging Noise," "Wailing Sounds," "Sensation of a Presence," "Reaction by Animals," and "Wall and Paper-writings."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Joseph W. Burden** was an undergraduate student at Christ Church, Oxford, who served as one of Harry Price’s official observers during the year-long tenancy investigation of Borley Rectory [1, 2]. He is notable in the Borley records as an exceptionally meticulous investigator and, with the exception of Mark Kerr-Pearse, **the only observer to spend time in the haunted house entirely by himself** [3].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following specific events, dates, quotes, and lesser-known details regarding his involvement:

**The December 1937 Investigation**
Burden visited Borley Rectory from **December 15 to 19, 1937**, initially accompanied by a fellow Oxford undergraduate, T. Stainton [2, 4]. The pair began their vigil with a thorough inspection of the house and grounds, sealing doors and setting traps, such as a black thread stretched across the passage between Rooms 9 and 10 [2]. 

During their joint watch, they experienced several auditory anomalies. They reported hearing **"shuffling footsteps, swishing garments, faint whining or hooting, and a continuous wailing sound"** that seemed to emanate from the Blue Room but penetrated the entire house [1, 5, 6]. 

Just after midnight on December 17, during Burden's watch, a loud noise resembling something being dragged across the floor was heard in the first-floor passage [7]. The dragging sound lasted for two seconds and repeated at two-second intervals, with the observers emphasizing that the noises were **"much too loud to admit of their having been mistaken"** [7].

**The Cat Experiment**
Burden and Stainton decided to test the theory that animals react to psychic influences [1]. They borrowed a cat belonging to the Rectory's caretaker, Mr. Lionel Arbon, and tempted it with condensed milk into known areas of psychic activity [1]. Burden noted that the cat was frightened by moonlight on the floor, rubbed its back against a "strange coat" in the Blue Room as if recognizing an old friend, and showed particular interest in Room 7, though other rooms did not appear to affect her [1]. 

**The Solo Vigil and the Chapel**
Stainton left the Rectory early, leaving Burden to conduct the final 24 hours of the investigation alone [8, 9]. *(Note: Harry Price's text contains a minor chronological contradiction here, stating Stainton left "early on the morning of December 19" but later describing Burden retiring on the same evening and sleeping "until 8.30 a.m. [December 19]" [9]).*

While alone, Burden found two new pencil marks in the North passage, heard a rattling sound in the dairy (which he rationally put down to loose window bars), and recorded an **"uncanny sense of being watched in Room 2"** [8-10]. He also spent five minutes sitting in the dark in the Rectory Chapel, lighting two candles and hanging a small silver cross between them; he noted that the Chapel **"seemed to have evil atmosphere, but this probably imagination"** [2, 6].

**Acoustic Discoveries and Objective Skepticism**
Burden proved to be a highly objective and critical observer. During his solo vigil, he heard talking and footsteps seemingly coming from the main hall, but upon investigation, he deduced the sounds must actually be coming from the Arbons' cottage [9, 11]. The researcher Robert Hastings later credited Burden with discovering that **the Rectory possessed acoustic properties that acted as an amplifier for voices originating in the stable cottage**—a natural phenomenon Price had previously been unaware of [12].

Burden was openly suspicious of the caretaker's potential involvement in faking phenomena. Writing to Harry Price on **December 21, 1937**, he shared his doubts with this specific quote:
> **"Lastly I would hesitate to place some of the manifestations beyond Arbin [sic] because he might easily realise that ‘Phenomena appearing strong’ would mean more observers and consequently more tips, not forgetting the fact that you would be more inclined to rent or buy the house if the ghosts were active."** [13, 14]

**Attention to Detail**
Burden's meticulous control of the environment was highly valued by later investigators. When S.G. Welles and his party visited the Rectory in February 1938 and found a 42-inch wooden lath in a new position on the floor, they consulted Burden to verify its original placement [15, 16]. Burden explicitly remembered the lath being stretched diagonally across the lead sink of the scullery pump, where he had chalked it into position, and was even able to confirm the exact placement of a slightly crumpled cigarette card located two feet from the wall [16].
