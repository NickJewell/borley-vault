# Rev. Daniel L. Booth

Vicar of St Michael's, Sutton Ings, Hull. Visited the Borley site in September 1945 with his young son while on holiday near Cambridge. His experience closely parallels that of [[gilbert-hayes|Gilbert Hayes]] six years earlier — a phantom companion providing a sense of someone walking alongside when no one was there.

## The September 1945 Visit

Booth and his son cycled over to Borley to examine the site of the demolished Rectory, the summer-house, the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]], and the grounds. While wandering, Booth found himself in the thicket at the bottom of the garden. He heard his son following close behind him, and the footsteps stopped when he stopped. Without turning round, Booth began speaking to his boy about the overgrown state of the grounds.

He received no reply.

> *"As I received no reply, I suddenly turned round and found I had been talking to myself, my boy being elsewhere at the time, though I assumed he was behind me in the undergrowth."*

## Significance

By September 1945, the Rectory had been demolished for a year. The grounds were severely overgrown. This was not a building in use; no one had any reason to produce footsteps among the thickets. The pattern — footsteps accompanying a visitor in the garden, pausing when the visitor pauses, vanishing on direct investigation — is one of the most consistently reported phenomena at Borley, recorded by [[gilbert-hayes]], [[arthur-medcraft]], and [[gerald-bull]] among others.

Booth also wrote to Price on December 2, 1943, suggesting possible locations for the nun's remains — under the fir-tree, in the cats' cemetery, at the "smoke" spot on the lawn, or under the "B" stone. Price respectfully explained why none of these suggestions could be pursued.
