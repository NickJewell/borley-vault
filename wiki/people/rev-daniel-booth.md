# Rev. Daniel L. Booth

Vicar of St Michael's, Sutton Ings, Hull. Visited the Borley site in September 1945 with his young son while on holiday near Cambridge. His experience closely parallels that of [[gilbert-hayes|Gilbert Hayes]] six years earlier — a phantom companion providing a sense of someone walking alongside when no one was there.

## The September 1945 Visit

Booth and his son cycled over to Borley to examine the site of the demolished Rectory, the summer-house, the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]], and the grounds. While wandering, Booth found himself in the thicket at the bottom of the garden. He heard his son following close behind him, and the footsteps stopped when he stopped. Without turning round, Booth began speaking to his boy about the overgrown state of the grounds.

He received no reply.

> *"As I received no reply, I suddenly turned round and found I had been talking to myself, my boy being elsewhere at the time, though I assumed he was behind me in the undergrowth."*

## Significance

By September 1945, the Rectory had been demolished for a year. The grounds were severely overgrown. This was not a building in use; no one had any reason to produce footsteps among the thickets. The pattern — footsteps accompanying a visitor in the garden, pausing when the visitor pauses, vanishing on direct investigation — is one of the most consistently reported phenomena at Borley, recorded by [[gilbert-hayes]], [[arthur-medcraft]], and [[gerald-bull]] among others.

Booth also wrote to Price on December 2, 1943, suggesting possible locations for the nun's remains — under the fir-tree, in the cats' cemetery, at the "smoke" spot on the lawn, or under the "B" stone. Price respectfully explained why none of these suggestions could be pursued.

**Reverend Daniel L. Booth** was the Vicar of St Michael’s, Sutton Ings, Hull. He features in the Borley sources as a witness who reported a specific auditory phenomenon on the former Rectory grounds shortly after the end of the Second World War. 

A reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following specific details, dates, and quotes regarding his experience:

**The September 1945 Visit**
*   In **September 1945**, Rev. Booth and his young son were spending a holiday near Cambridge [1]. 
*   Out of curiosity, they decided to cycle over to Borley specifically to examine the ruins of the Rectory site, the summer-house, and the Nun’s Walk [1].

**The Phantom Footsteps Incident**
*   While wandering around the site, Rev. Booth found himself in the thicket at the bottom of the garden [1]. 
*   As he walked, he clearly heard footsteps following close behind him, which he naturally assumed belonged to his young son [1]. The footsteps perfectly matched his own movements, stopping whenever he stopped walking [1]. 
*   **Direct Quote:** In a communication sent to Harry Price dated **September 22, 1945**, Rev. Booth detailed the climax of the incident: **"As I received no reply, I suddenly turned round and found I had been talking to myself, my boy being elsewhere at the time, though I assumed he was behind me in the undergrowth"** [1].

**Harry Price's Commentary**
*   Harry Price included Rev. Booth's account in *The End of Borley Rectory* to corroborate similar experiences reported by other visitors [1]. 
*   Price noted that while phantom footsteps were one of the most common phenomena associated with Borley Rectory, Rev. Booth's experience was highly unique [2]. Price explicitly claimed: **"I have no other record of footsteps having been heard in the garden"** [2]. *(Note: While other witnesses in the wider Borley literature reported footsteps on the paths or the Nun's Walk, Price singled out Booth's experience in the actual garden thicket as unrecorded elsewhere in his files)*.
