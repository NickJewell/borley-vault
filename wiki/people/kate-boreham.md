# Kate Boreham (d. Easter Sunday 1888, aged 31)

A woman whose death is recorded in Borley's parish register and whose alleged spirit was contacted during séances in the Rectory in October–November 1937. Believed to be the "cook" from the recurring legend that a servant died in [[henry-bull|Henry Bull's]] arms in the Rectory kitchen.

## Parish Register Entry

Sidney Glanville consulted the Borley parish register after table-tipping séances at which the spirit of a cook-servant communicated. He found the entry:

> "Kate Boreham of Sudbury died Easter Day 1888 aged 31"

The name "Kate" or "Katie" had not appeared in any phenomena until Glanville discovered this record.

## Séance Communications (October 1937)

Table-tipping sessions with Glanville, Kerr-Pearse and Cuthbert in the kitchen passage produced communications attributed to her:

> **Did something unfortunate happen in the kitchen?** Yes.  
> **Did a servant girl die in your presence there?** Yes.  
> **Did she die a natural death?** No.  
> **Was there a baby?** Yes.

When Glanville brought the Katie entity's name to a later séance, she confirmed she was a maid and replied to "Will you please spell your message?" with: "Light, Mass, [indistinct]" — the same imperative that appeared in the [[wall-writings|wall-writings]].

## Full Planchette Records (ebr-ch08)

The complete Planchette séance transcripts published for the first time in *The End of Borley Rectory* (1946) show that "Katie Boreham" communicated at multiple sessions in 1937:

- Her name "Katie" first appeared at the October 24, 1937 table-tipping séance in Borley kitchen passage; death date given as "1889" then corrected to "1888."
- At the Streatham séance (October 31, 1937) the name "Boreham" was spelled out, and a further death date given as "April." Since the Sudbury register records Easter Day 1888, and April and Easter Day are approximately coincident, this is a partial verification. However, a second date — June 13 — was also given in the same séance, which cannot be reconciled.
- "Katie Boreham" is described as "a servant here" (at Borley) in the entities' own words.
- The mirror-writing séance (November 20–21, 1937) had an entity claim to know whose skull it was that Mrs Smith found in the library cupboard — but refused to name the owner.

Price notes: "There is the information, wherever it came from, and whoever recorded it." The name, year, and rough month match — but the date of June 13 is irreconcilable with Easter Day.

## Reliability

The parish register entry is the one documented fact. The legend of a servant dying in the rector's arms, possibly having borne him an illegitimate child, circulated among the Bulls' social circle and was repeated to Sidney Glanville by the Smiths during his 1937 visit. Whether the real Kate Boreham corresponds to this legend is unknown. The table-tipping communications were produced by participants who had varying degrees of prior knowledge.

## Banks's Full Investigation (enbr-ch20–21)

[[Ivan Banks]] (1994) conducted the most extensive documented research into Kate Boreham, consulting:
- Parish registers (baptisms, marriages, burials at Borley)
- Suffolk County Records Office for the 1888 electoral register
- St. Catherine's House death registers
- **Professor Bernard Knight** (Cardiff University Medical School) on forensic aspects
- **Dr. David Whittaker** (Knight's colleague) on the bone fragments

### Birth and Marriage

Born **Catherine Barber** in Ballingdon, Sudbury, 1857. Married Walter Boreham at Borley Parish Church, February 19, 1876 — the wedding conducted by Henry Dawson Bull himself. Her first child was baptized at Borley on **June 4, 1876** — just over three months after the wedding, suggesting possible premarital conception. She had further children baptized at Borley subsequently.

### Death Certificate Problems

Kate is recorded as dying in Sudbury (Priory Walk) on Easter Day 1888, cause: "acute cerebritis," age stated as both 31 (in the church burial register) and 38 (on the death certificate) — the discrepancy is unexplained.

**Banks's problem 1 — The house:** The 1888 electoral register for Priory Walk, Sudbury shows the only person owning property there was "Jessie Mitchell." There is no record of the Borehams ever owning or renting a house in Priory Walk. However, "William Henry Bull" appears as owning a house in Market Hill, Sudbury — possibly a relative of the Borley Bulls.

**Banks's problem 2 — The grave:** Richard Lee van den Daele visited Borley churchyard and could not find Kate Boreham's grave, despite the parish register recording her burial there.

**Banks's problem 3 — "Cerebritis":** Professor Knight's assessment: "The cause of death recorded — cerebritis — is meaningless as such and was probably speculative." The modern equivalent (encephalitis or meningo-encephalitis) requires virological testing not available in 1888. A Victorian doctor certifying death without post-mortem could describe almost any brain-related illness as "cerebritis."

### Lead Poisoning Theory

Banks researched **sugar of lead (lead acetate)** — reportedly found as a half-empty bottle in a Rectory cupboard after Henry Bull's death. Sugar of lead was historically used in "diachyton plasters" which could be scraped and swallowed as an **abortifacient**.

Professor Knight's assessment: lead poisoning causes encephalopathy including fits, tremors, and death — all of which "could certainly result in signs that could be mistaken for inflammation of the brain."

**Banks's proposed sequence:**
1. Kate became pregnant by Henry Dawson Bull (c.1887)
2. She took or was given sugar of lead to abort the pregnancy
3. The abortion failed; she gave birth to a dead or dying child
4. Kate herself was now critically ill from lead poisoning
5. The Rectory bell summoned coachman [[joseph-tyler|Joseph Tyler]]
6. Henry Bull and Tyler drove Kate in the family carriage to a Sudbury house after dark
7. A young or compliant doctor issued a death certificate listing "cerebritis"
8. Kate was buried at Borley at Easter 1888 — possibly in an unmarked grave (explaining why van den Daele couldn't find it)

### Connection to the Phantom Coach

Banks argues this sequence explains the [[phantom-coach|phantom coach and horses]]: a Victorian cab (described by one witness as "an old-fashioned cab") driven at speed by two men on the driver's box, carrying a dying Kate away from the Rectory. The voice heard by Guy Smith pleading "No Carlos, Don't" may have been Kate's (or Henry's wife's) protest at the removal.

### Bone Fragment Connection

Dr. David Whittaker's assessment of the 1943 Borley skull fragment dental report: the wear on the first and second molar teeth "suggested an age close to thirty." Kate Boreham was 31 when she died. Banks raises the possibility that the bones buried at Liston in 1945 are not the nun's but Kate Boreham's — and that the nun's remains have never been found.

### Henry's Syphilis as Corroboration

Henry Dawson Bull died in 1892 from Locomotor Ataxia — documented by death certificate as a manifestation of tertiary syphilis. Banks argues this confirms the village tradition about Henry's affairs. Kate Boreham is the most documented case.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Kate Boreham** (born **Catherine Barber** or **Katherine Barba**) was a local woman whose life and death became central to one of Borley Rectory's darkest and most controversial subplots. According to parish census returns, she was employed as a domestic maid at the Rectory during the incumbency of the Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull [1, 2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, contradictions, and sensational theories regarding her life and alleged involvement in the haunting:

**Biographical Details and Contradictions**
*   **Birth and Marriage:** She was born in Ballingdon, Sudbury, in 1857 [3, 4]. On **February 19, 1876**, at the age of 19, she married Walter Boreham, a farm labourer from Acton, at Borley Church [3-5]. The service was conducted by the Rev. Henry D. E. Bull [4]. 
*   **Premarital Conception:** Kate’s first child was baptized on **June 4, 1876**, less than four months after her wedding, which strongly indicates premarital conception [4, 6]. 
*   **Contradictory Death Records:** Official records state she died in **March 1888** of "acute cerebritis" (brain inflammation) at a house in Priory Walk, Sudbury [2, 7]. However, Henry Bull entered her burial in the Borley churchyard as taking place on **Easter Day, 1888** [2]. 
*   **Age and Address Anomalies:** Her death certificate recorded her age as 38, contradicting the church register which listed her as 31 [2, 8]. Furthermore, Sudbury electoral registers from 1888 show that the Priory Walk house was owned by a Jessie Mitchell; there is no evidence that Walter or Kate Boreham ever rented or owned it [5, 8, 9]. 

**The 1937 Séance Revelations**
Kate Boreham’s name entered Borley's paranormal lore during a series of séances conducted in **October 1937** by Sidney Glanville, his children (Helen and Roger), and Mark Kerr-Pearse [1, 10, 11]. Using a planchette and table-tipping methods, the sitters contacted an entity claiming to be Henry Bull, who stated that a servant girl named "Katie Boreham" had died unnaturaly in the Rectory kitchen [10, 11]. The dates given by the spirits were notably confused, citing her death as occurring in **April 1888** and later as **June 13** [10, 12, 13].

**The "Cover-Up" Theory**
The séance results and local village gossip coalesced into a dark theory championed by researchers like Ivan Banks. The theory posits that Kate became pregnant by Henry Bull and went into premature labour in the Rectory kitchen [1, 14, 15]. According to this hypothesis:
*   Kate attempted to abort the pregnancy using **"sugar of lead" (lead acetate)**, a bottle of which was later allegedly found half-empty in the Rectory cellar [16-18]. 
*   To avoid a monumental scandal, the Bulls orchestrated a cover-up. The dying maid was allegedly placed in the family carriage and rushed under the cover of darkness to the obscure address in Sudbury [19, 20]. Researcher Ivan Banks speculated that Joseph Tyler, the Bull family coachman, drove the vehicle—a detail some believe is the true origin of the famous "phantom coach" sightings at Borley [20]. 
*   An inexperienced doctor, W. Nigel Mason, signed the death certificate citing "cerebritis," keeping the true cause of her death hidden from the public [7, 19]. 

**Medical and Skeptical Re-Evaluations**
*   **Forensic Analysis:** Professor Bernard Knight noted that "cerebritis" in the 1880s was an unspecific and speculative diagnosis [21]. He stated that Kate's symptoms could have been eclampsia related to childbirth, or **lead encephalopathy** caused by ingesting lead acetate, which was commonly scraped from diachyton plasters and swallowed as an abortifacient during that era [17, 22, 23].
*   **The Skeptical Dismissal:** Critics of Harry Price and Sidney Glanville, including the authors of the SPR's *Borley Report*, dismissed the Katie Boreham mystery entirely. They pointed out that Sidney Glanville had thoroughly examined the Borley Church registers *prior* to the séances where her name emerged [24]. Skeptics argue that Glanville consciously or subconsciously absorbed the name and reproduced it during the planchette sessions, and that the story derived from local gossip surrounding a separate rape scandal involving a neighbouring vicar [24, 25].
*   **The Cellar Bones:** When Harry Price excavated a female skull and jawbone from the Rectory cellar in 1943, he attributed the remains to a legendary 17th-century French nun, Marie Lairre [26, 27]. However, subsequent dental analysis indicated the bones likely belonged to a woman around 30 years of age, leading some researchers to ask a disturbing question: were the bones buried beneath Borley Rectory actually the remains of Kate Boreham? [27, 28].
