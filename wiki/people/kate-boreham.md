# Kate Boreham (d. Easter Sunday 1888, aged 31)

A woman whose death is recorded in Borley's parish register — born **Catherine Barber** (or **Katherine Barba**) in Ballingdon, Sudbury, in 1857 — and whose alleged spirit was contacted during séances in the Rectory in October–November 1937 [1, 2]. According to parish census returns, she was employed as a domestic maid at the Rectory during the incumbency of the Rev. [[henry-bull|Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] [1, 2]. She is believed to be the "cook" from the recurring legend that a servant died in [[henry-bull|Henry Bull's]] arms in the Rectory kitchen.

## Parish Register Entry and Birth

On **February 19, 1876**, at the age of 19, Catherine Barber married Walter Boreham, a farm labourer from Acton, at [[borley-church|Borley Parish Church]] — the service conducted by the Rev. [[henry-bull|Henry D. E. Bull]] himself [3, 4]. Her first child was baptized on **June 4, 1876**, less than four months after the wedding, which strongly indicates premarital conception [4, 6]. She had further children baptized at Borley subsequently.

[[sidney-glanville]] consulted the Borley parish register after table-tipping séances at which the spirit of a cook-servant communicated. He found the entry:

> "Kate Boreham of Sudbury died Easter Day 1888 aged 31"

The name "Kate" or "Katie" had not appeared in any phenomena until Glanville discovered this record.

## Death Certificate Problems

Official records state she died in **March 1888** of "acute cerebritis" (brain inflammation) at a house in Priory Walk, Sudbury [2, 7]. However, [[henry-bull|Henry Bull]] entered her burial in the [[borley-church|Borley churchyard]] as taking place on **Easter Day, 1888** [2]. Her death certificate recorded her age as 38, contradicting the church register which listed her as 31 [2, 8].

Furthermore, the 1888 electoral register for Priory Walk, Sudbury shows the only person owning property there was "Jessie Mitchell." There is no record of the Borehams ever owning or renting a house in Priory Walk — though "William Henry Bull" appears as owning a house in Market Hill, Sudbury, possibly a relative of the Borley Bulls [5, 8, 9]. Richard Lee van den Daele visited [[borley-church|Borley churchyard]] and could not find Kate Boreham's grave, despite the parish register recording her burial there.

## Séance Communications (October 1937)

Kate Boreham's name entered Borley's paranormal lore during a series of séances conducted in **October 1937** by [[sidney-glanville]], his children ([[helen-glanville|Helen]] and [[roger-glanville|Roger]]), and Mark Kerr-Pearse [1, 10, 11]. Her name "Katie" first appeared at the **October 24, 1937** table-tipping séance in the Borley kitchen passage — death date given as "1889" then corrected to "1888." The sitters contacted an entity claiming to be [[henry-bull|Henry Bull]], who stated that a servant girl named "Katie Boreham" had died unnaturally in the Rectory kitchen. A table-tipping session with Glanville, Kerr-Pearse and Cuthbert in the kitchen passage produced the exchange:

> **Did something unfortunate happen in the kitchen?** Yes.  
> **Did a servant girl die in your presence there?** Yes.  
> **Did she die a natural death?** No.  
> **Was there a baby?** Yes.

When Glanville brought the Katie entity's name to a later séance, she confirmed she was a maid and replied to "Will you please spell your message?" with: "Light, Mass, [indistinct]" — the same imperative that appeared in the [[wall-writings|wall-writings]].

## Full Planchette Records (ebr-ch08)

The complete Planchette séance transcripts published for the first time in *The End of Borley Rectory* (1946) show that "Katie Boreham" communicated at multiple sessions in 1937:

- At the Streatham séance (October 31, 1937) the name "Boreham" was spelled out, and a further death date given as "April." Since the Sudbury register records Easter Day 1888, and April and Easter Day are approximately coincident, this is a partial verification. However, a second date — June 13 — was also given in the same séance, which cannot be reconciled [10, 12, 13]
- "Katie Boreham" is described as "a servant here" (at Borley) in the entities' own words
- The mirror-writing séance (November 20–21, 1937) had an entity claim to know whose skull it was that [[mabel-smith|Mrs Smith]] found in the library cupboard — but refused to name the owner

Price notes: "There is the information, wherever it came from, and whoever recorded it." The name, year, and rough month match — but the date of June 13 is irreconcilable with Easter Day.

## Reliability and the Skeptical Challenge

The parish register entry is the one documented fact. The legend of a servant dying in the rector's arms, possibly having borne him an illegitimate child, circulated among the Bulls' social circle and was repeated to [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] by the Smiths during his 1937 visit.

Critics of [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]], including the authors of the SPR's *[[the-borley-report|Borley Report]]*, dismissed the Katie Boreham mystery entirely. They pointed out that [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] had thoroughly examined the Borley Church registers *prior* to the séances where her name emerged [24]. Skeptics argue that Glanville consciously or subconsciously absorbed the name and reproduced it during the planchette sessions, and that the story derived from local gossip surrounding a separate rape scandal involving a neighbouring vicar [24, 25]. The table-tipping communications were produced by participants who had varying degrees of prior knowledge.

## Banks's Full Investigation (enbr-ch20–21)

[[ivan-banks|Ivan Banks]] (1994) conducted the most extensive documented research into Kate Boreham, consulting:
- Parish registers (baptisms, marriages, burials at Borley)
- Suffolk County Records Office for the 1888 electoral register
- St. Catherine's House death registers
- **Professor Bernard Knight** (Cardiff University Medical School) on forensic aspects
- **Dr. David Whittaker** (Knight's colleague) on the bone fragments

### Lead Poisoning Theory

Banks researched **sugar of lead (lead acetate)** — reportedly found as a half-empty bottle in a Rectory cupboard after [[henry-bull|Henry Bull's]] death. Sugar of lead was historically used in "diachyton plasters" which could be scraped and swallowed as an **abortifacient** [16–18].

Professor Knight's assessment: "The cause of death recorded — cerebritis — is meaningless as such and was probably speculative." Lead poisoning causes encephalopathy including fits, tremors, and death — all of which "could certainly result in signs that could be mistaken for inflammation of the brain." The modern equivalent (encephalitis or meningo-encephalitis) requires virological testing not available in 1888. A Victorian doctor certifying death without post-mortem could describe almost any brain-related illness as "cerebritis." [21, 22, 23]

**Banks's proposed sequence:**
1. Kate became pregnant by [[henry-bull|Henry Dawson Bull]] (c.1887)
2. She took or was given sugar of lead to abort the pregnancy
3. The abortion failed; she gave birth to a dead or dying child
4. Kate herself was now critically ill from lead poisoning
5. The Rectory bell summoned coachman [[joseph-tyler|Joseph Tyler]]
6. [[henry-bull|Henry Bull]] and Tyler drove Kate in the family carriage to a Sudbury house after dark
7. An inexperienced doctor, W. Nigel Mason, signed the death certificate citing "cerebritis," keeping the true cause hidden from the public [7, 19]
8. Kate was buried at [[borley-church|Borley]] at Easter 1888 — possibly in an unmarked grave (explaining why van den Daele couldn't find it)

### Connection to the Phantom Coach

Banks argues this sequence explains the [[phantom-coach|phantom coach and horses]]: a Victorian cab (described by one witness as "an old-fashioned cab") driven at speed by two men on the driver's box, carrying a dying Kate away from the Rectory [20]. The voice heard by [[rev-eric-smith|Guy Smith]] pleading "No Carlos, Don't" may have been Kate's (or [[henry-bull|Henry's]] wife's) protest at the removal.

### Bone Fragment Connection

Dr. David Whittaker's assessment of the 1943 Borley skull fragment dental report: the wear on the first and second molar teeth "suggested an age close to thirty." Kate Boreham was 31 when she died. Banks raises the possibility that the bones buried at [[liston-rectory|Liston]] in 1945 are not the nun's but Kate Boreham's — and that the nun's remains have never been found [27, 28].

### Henry's Syphilis as Corroboration

[[henry-bull|Henry Dawson Bull]] died in 1892 from Locomotor Ataxia — documented by death certificate as a manifestation of tertiary syphilis. Banks argues this confirms the village tradition about Henry's affairs. Kate Boreham is the most documented case.
