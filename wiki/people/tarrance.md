# Mr. Tarrance (real name: Farrance)

Borley man employed by the Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] to repair a well in the garden of [[borley-place|Borley Place]], the old house nearly opposite the Rectory. During the course of that repair work, he made one of the most significant physical discoveries in the entire Borley case: the Tudor-brick tunnel.

> **Contradiction:** Harry Price originally introduced him to the public in *The End of Borley Rectory* (1946) under the misspelled name **"Mr. Tarrance"**. Later Borley historians corrected this, noting his real surname was **Farrance** [1, 3]. A further contradiction concerns who actually found the tunnel: most historical entries state that Farrance himself was employed by the Rev. Harry Bull and made the discovery [1, 4], but a specific chronological entry records that **Frederick James Farrance** died on October 21, 1971, at the age of 80, describing him as "a local man whose *father*, working for Harry Bull, found a tunnel in the garden of Borley Place" [5] — implying the discoverer was his father, not Frederick himself.

## Discovery of the Tunnel

While repairing the well at [[borley-place|Borley Place]], Farrance came across some brickwork a few feet below the ground. With his pick, he forced an entrance through the bricks and found himself in a tunnel described as **"high and dome-shaped"** (or vaulted), built of the old two-inch bricks characteristic of Tudor construction [1–3].

He attempted to explore the newly discovered passage, climbing inside and pushing forward for some distance. His exploration was abruptly halted when he encountered a pocket of **"foul air"** [3, 9] — the stale gas caused his eyes to smart and water, and the candle he was carrying guttered and went out. (Later researchers noted it was fortunate his candle did not ignite a pocket of potentially explosive methane gas [10].) Finding it impossible to go on, Farrance was forced to turn back, and the cavity was subsequently sealed up [9, 11].

The discovery was made "several years ago" before the Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|A. C. Henning]] relayed the account to Price — probably in the early twentieth century during Harry Bull's incumbency (1892–1927). Prior to the publication of *EBR* in 1946, Henning interviewed the ageing labourer about his experience. Farrance reportedly passed no opinion on what the mysterious tunnel could have been used for, but was certain **"that it was not a sewer"** [2]. Henning confirmed to Price that Farrance was "still living" at the time of writing (c.1946).

## Significance

This account corroborates Price's earlier reports of tunnel fragments found near the Rectory and the apparent "hollowness" of the road outside. The tunnel appears to be the same structure later explored in the 1950s excavations and identified by [[andrew-clarke|Andrew Clarke]] as likely connected to the Herringham rectory drainage system. See [[tunnels]] and [[herringham-rectory]] for context.

Farrance's son — referred to by [[rev-ac-henning]] as "Farrance junior" — separately told Henning about [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] being followed from church by the Nun, confirming the family's long local connection to Borley. A daughter is also mentioned in the sources, interviewed by visiting psychical researchers on August 20, 1949 [6].
