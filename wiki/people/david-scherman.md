# David E. Scherman

A photographer on the staff of *Life* magazine and an accredited war correspondent to the United States Army during the Second World War [1]. Scherman took the famous "[[flying-brick-1944|flying brick]]" photograph on 5 April 1944 at [[borley-rectory]], and is remembered in the Borley annals as the man who staged it — knowingly, and as a joke [2–4].

## The Flying Brick

Scherman visited Borley on 5 April 1944, invited by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] because *Life* had asked him to do an article on "Haunted England." Also present was Miss [[cynthia-ledsham|Cynthia Ledsham]] (later Mrs Thompson), the magazine's researcher and archivist. Price met both for the first time.

They journeyed to Borley by car. Scherman used a miniature American camera with a very fast shutter speed [8–10]. Because the camera did not have a wide-angle lens, he had to stand at least one hundred feet back to capture the entirety of the ruins, which were actively being demolished by workmen at the time [6, 8, 11]. He took several photographs that day, including an excellent picture of the [[waldegrave-monument|Waldegrave tomb]] inside [[borley-church|Borley Church]], which Price later published as the frontispiece for *The End of Borley Rectory* [1, 7].

A few days after the visit, Scherman sent Price 2.25-inch square contact prints [9]. Over the telephone, Scherman pointed out a white pin-point against the dark background of the kitchen passage — a brick caught in mid-air [9]. At Price's request, Scherman supplied 10-by-8-inch enlargements, including a heavily cropped version focusing directly on the hovering brick, which Price subsequently published as possibly "the first photograph of a poltergeist projectile in flight" [6, 12, 13].

## Scherman's Exposé

When [[cynthia-ledsham|Cynthia Ledsham]] later accused Price of "bare-faced hocus pocus" — claiming the brick was merely tossed by a demolition workman — investigator [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] wrote to Scherman at the Time and Life Building in New York to ask for his version of events [16]. In a 1956 letter, Scherman entirely corroborated Ledsham's account:

> "Miss Ledsham's statement that there was no doubt at all that the so-called 'flying bricks' were propelled by a workman at Borley Rectory as part of his demolition work is absolutely correct... It is my recollection that the picture was used in our magazine with the tongue-in-cheek, and I was therefore just as astonished as Miss Ledsham when I learned it had been reprinted as photographic proof of supernatural behaviour." [16]

He recalled that they had all been amused watching the bricks fly out from the demolition, noting:

> "I think someone may have said, 'let's get a picture of that poltergeist throwing stones' or some such... The simple truth is that we liked Harry Price, let him use the picture, but had no idea he would turn what we printed as a pleasantry into gospel fact." [16]

Despite his astonishment, Scherman added that when he realised Price had the "effrontery" to pass the obvious joke off as genuine poltergeist activity, he was actually delighted by Price's adventurous spirit [17].

Subsequent correspondence in 1996 confirmed that Scherman had **deliberately positioned the camera to hide the workman** throwing the rubble because he thought it would be fun [16, 17].

## A Photographic Contradiction

Although Scherman claimed he deliberately framed the picture to hide the workman, later analysis of Scherman's uncropped, full-frame photograph revealed that a lone workman *is* actually visible on the extreme left-hand side of the picture [12, 18, 19].

> **Contradiction:** In defending Price, one source points out that this visible workman is actually facing the wrong direction to have thrown the brick in question, and that analysing the trajectory and debris, the brick could not logically have been thrown by him or fallen from the floor above [18]. This analysis raises the ironic possibility that Scherman, while attempting to stage a fake photograph of a "flying brick," may have accidentally captured a genuinely unexplained anomaly on film [18].

## Assessment of Price

Scherman's letter provides a nuanced view of Price: "I couldn't pretend to give you an assessment on the ins and outs of Psychic Phenomena or on whether or not Harry Price was a fraud... It was always my (doubtless incomplete) understanding that he was mainly interested in exposing various spurious manifestations — but that somewhere along the line he found it more profitable or amusing to write about the lore itself rather than its possibly fake nature."

## Personal Life

Beyond his work as a war correspondent, Scherman lived at 21 Downshire Hills in London alongside the surrealist [[alan-cuthbert|Sir Roland Penrose]] and the acclaimed *Vogue* war correspondent and photographer Lee Miller (who had previously been married to the artist Man Ray) [5]. The three of them conducted a "much remarked upon *ménage à trois*" at the residence [5]. When the home was awarded a Blue Plaque in 2003, it only commemorated Miller and Penrose; Scherman's name was deliberately omitted [5].
