# David E. Scherman

A photographer on the staff of *Life* magazine and an accredited war correspondent to the United States Army. Scherman took the famous "[[flying-brick|flying brick]]" photograph on 5 April 1944 at [[borley-rectory]].

## The Flying Brick

Scherman visited Borley on 5 April 1944, invited by [[harry-price]] because *Life* had asked him to do an article on "Haunted England." Also present was Miss Cynthia Ledsham (later Mrs Thompson), the magazine's researcher and archivist. Price met both for the first time.

They journeyed to Borley by car. Scherman photographed the ruins — using a miniature camera with no wide-angle lens, requiring him to stand about a hundred feet away. One photograph, of a house brick apparently poised in mid-air in front of the ruins, was later published by Price in *EBR* as possibly "the first photograph of a poltergeist projectile in flight."

However, in a letter to [[peter-underwood]], Scherman confirmed that the brick was thrown by a demolition workman:

> "Miss Ledsham's statement that there was no doubt at all that the so-called 'flying bricks' were propelled by a workman at Borley Rectory as part of his demolition work is absolutely correct... It is my recollection that the picture was used in our magazine with the tongue-in-cheek, and I was therefore just as astonished as Miss Ledsham when I learned it had been reprinted as photographic proof of supernatural behaviour. I recall at the time that we were amused to see the bricks come flying through the window, and I think someone may have said, 'let's get a picture of that poltergeist throwing stones' or some such..."

Scherman added: "The simple truth is that we liked Harry Price, let him use the picture, but had no idea he would turn what we printed as a pleasantry into gospel fact."

## Assessment of Price

Scherman's letter provides a nuanced view of Price: "I couldn't pretend to give you an assessment on the ins and outs of Psychic Phenomena or on whether or not Harry Price was a fraud... It was always my (doubtless incomplete) understanding that he was mainly interested in exposing various spurious manifestations — but that somewhere along the line he found it more profitable or amusing to write about the lore itself rather than its possibly fake nature."

## Other Work

Scherman also took the excellent photograph of the Waldegrave tomb in Borley Church published in *EBR* (Plate VI). Outside Borley, he was an acclaimed war correspondent and lived with photographer Lee Miller and Roland Penrose in London during and after the war.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**David E. Scherman** was a photographer for the American weekly magazine *Life* and an accredited war correspondent to the United States Army during the Second World War [1]. In the Borley Rectory sources, he is immortalized as the man who took the famous "flying brick" photograph—a piece of evidence that Harry Price heralded as potentially the first photograph of a poltergeist projectile in flight, but which critics later exposed as a deliberate joke [2-4].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, events, quotes, and contradictions regarding his involvement:

**Lesser-Known Biographical Details and the *Ménage à Trois***
Beyond his work as a war correspondent and photographer, the sources note a scandalous detail regarding his personal life. Scherman lived at 21 Downshire Hills in London alongside the surrealist Sir Roland Penrose and the acclaimed *Vogue* war correspondent and photographer Lee Miller (who had previously been married to the artist Man Ray) [5]. The three of them conducted a "much remarked upon *ménage à trois*" at the residence [5]. However, when the home was awarded a prestigious Blue Plaque in 2003, it only commemorated Miller and Penrose; Scherman's name was deliberately omitted from the plaque, prompting the source to note that "David E. Scherman's talents, it seems, did not lie in photography alone" [5].

**The Borley Visit (April 5, 1944) and Photography**
On April 5, 1944, Scherman travelled to the ruins of Borley Rectory with Harry Price and Cynthia Ledsham (a researcher and archivist for *Time* and *Life*) to photograph the site for a proposed article titled "Haunted England" [1, 6, 7]. 

Scherman used a miniature American camera with a very fast shutter speed [8-10]. Because the camera did not have a wide-angle lens, he had to stand at least one hundred feet back to capture the entirety of the ruins, which were actively being demolished by workmen at the time [6, 8, 11]. He took several "magnificent" photographs that day, including an excellent picture of the Waldegrave tomb inside Borley Church, which Price later used as the frontispiece for his book *The End of Borley Rectory* (*EBR*) [1, 7]. 

**The "Flying Brick" and Scherman's Exposé**
A few days after the visit, Scherman sent Price 2.25-inch square contact prints of the negatives [9]. Over the telephone, Scherman asked Price if he had noticed anything unusual, pointing out a white pin-point against the dark background of the kitchen passage—a brick caught in mid-air [9]. At Price's request, Scherman supplied 10-by-8-inch enlargements, including a heavily cropped version focusing directly on the hovering brick, which Price subsequently published [6, 12, 13]. 

When Cynthia Ledsham later accused Price of "bare-faced hocus pocus"—claiming the brick was merely tossed by a demolition workman [14, 15]—investigator Peter Underwood wrote to Scherman at the Time and Life Building in New York to ask for his version of events [16]. In a 1956 letter, Scherman entirely corroborated Ledsham's exposure of the hoax, admitting that he had deliberately positioned the camera to hide the workman tossing the rubble because he thought it would be fun [16, 17].

Scherman provided the following direct quotes in his reply:
> **"Miss Ledsham's statement that there was no doubt at all that the so-called 'flying bricks' were propelled by a workman at Borley Rectory as part of his demolition work is absolutely correct... It is my recollection that the picture was used in our magazine with the tongue-in-cheek, and I was therefore just as astonished as Miss Ledsham when I learned it had been reprinted as photographic proof of supernatural behaviour"** [16]. 

He recalled that they had all been amused watching the bricks fly out from the demolition, noting: 
> **"I think someone may have said, 'let’s get a picture of that poltergeist throwing stones' or some such... The simple truth is that we liked Harry Price, let him use the picture, but had no idea he would turn what we printed as a pleasantry into gospel fact"** [16]. 

Despite his astonishment, Scherman added that when he realized Price had the "effrontery" to pass the obvious joke off as genuine poltergeist activity, he was actually delighted by Price's adventurous spirit [17].

**A Photographic Contradiction**
Although Scherman claimed he deliberately framed the picture so as not to see the workman throwing the bricks, later analysis of Scherman's uncropped, full-frame photograph revealed a glaring contradiction. A lone workman *is* actually clearly visible on the extreme left-hand side of the picture [12, 18, 19]. 

In defending Price, one source points out that in Scherman's photograph, this visible workman is actually facing the wrong direction to have thrown the brick in question [18]. Furthermore, analyzing the trajectory and the debris, the writer argues that the brick could not have logically been thrown by him or fallen from the floor above [18]. Therefore, this analysis suggests the ironic possibility that Scherman, while attempting to stage a fake photograph of a "flying brick," may have accidentally captured a genuinely unexplained anomaly on film [18].
