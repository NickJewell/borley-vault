# Dr Albert von Schrenck-Notzing

Munich-based psychical researcher and physician (also referred to as **Baron von Schrenck-Notzing**); the primary European promoter of the [[willi-schneider|Schneider]] brothers; an Honorary Vice-President of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s [[national-laboratory|National Laboratory of Psychical Research]] [2]. His invitation of Price and [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] to Munich in 1922 shaped Price's entire subsequent career, and his death in February 1929 triggered Price's takeover of [[rudi-schneider|Rudi Schneider]]'s career.

## Background

Schrenck-Notzing was a wealthy, well-connected Munich physician with a private income who devoted considerable resources to psychical research. After hearing sensational stories in the local press about nineteen-year-old Willi Schneider's telekinetic and materialisation abilities, he visited the Schneider family home in Braunau, Austria [3]. He persuaded Willi's father to allow him to take the boy under his wing, having him trained as a dentist in Munich while conducting dozens of sittings in his private apartment [3, 4].

When Willi's powers began to fade and his younger brother Rudi emerged as a medium, Schrenck-Notzing promoted Rudi's skills in Munich and provided him with financial support [5, 6].

## The Munich Invitation

In **May 1922**, Schrenck-Notzing invited Price and Dingwall to Munich to observe Willi Schneider's abilities under controlled conditions [3, 7]. This visit was Price's true introduction to serious study of physical mediumship [8]. The authors of the highly critical *Borley Report* noted that this experience set Price's life course, observing that his thoughts turned towards "making himself in England what he thought Schrenck-Notzing was in Germany" [8]. While Price ultimately went far beyond the German investigator's aims by establishing his own National Laboratory and becoming a prolific psychic journalist, Schrenck-Notzing was the original template for his ambitions [8].

Price was impressed enough to declare Willi genuine, producing one of the key moments in his public career — the so-called "conversion of Harry Price." Dingwall was less enthusiastic.

## Death and Aftermath

Schrenck-Notzing died in **February 1929** [6]. His death had an immediate ripple effect: because he had been financially supporting Rudi Schneider, his death left the young medium without a patron [6]. Price immediately travelled to Munich to secure the twenty-year-old Rudi's services for sittings in London, effectively taking over Schneider's career and turning him into an international media celebrity — "the Austrian psychical superman" [6]. The transition from Munich-based European research to London tabloid celebrity was entirely Price's creation and would not have been possible without Schrenck-Notzing's earlier groundwork.

> O'Connor notes a melancholy irony: the Schneider brothers' hometown of Braunau had, by this period, become famous not for a celebrated medium but as the birthplace of Adolf Hitler.
