# James Turner (and Catherine Turner)

**James Ernest Turner** (6 January 1909 – 1975/76) was a poet, author, broadcaster, and BBC representative for the South West [1–3]. He purchased the Rectory Cottage and site in April or May 1947 for £1,500 (some sources give £5,000) [3, 7, 8], and lived there with his wife Catherine — born Lucy, but known universally as Cathy — until July 1950 [9]. He is one of the most important post-fire witnesses.

## Character and Departure

The Turners came from houses they already regarded as haunted and "took most of the strange happenings as part of the furniture." Neither was frightened by their experiences — the phenomena were "part of the scenery." Publicly they stated they left because of "the constant stream of uninvited and inquisitive visitors" who made work impossible; privately, however, Turner suffered financial ruin after a flax-growing venture failed and two successive mushroom crops were destroyed — partly by vibration from low-flying jet aircraft, for which the Ministry of Defence refused compensation [11]. They subsequently moved to Belchamp Walter, then Grundisburgh in Suffolk, and finally to Cornwall [3, 10].

## Experiences at Borley

### Voices on the Nun's Walk

While clearing brambles from the old orchard in May or June 1947, Turner repeatedly heard "happy and laughing voices" from the direction of the Rectory site each evening [25–27]. Cathy heard them too. "Always, as soon as I stopped work and laid down the sickle, the voices, happy and laughing, fled before me" [25]. Once, he dashed into the bushes to catch them but found no one. After the clearing was done and the path restored, the voices were never heard again. Turner later attempted to rationalise them as "the noise of aeroplanes warming up on a distant aerodrome" [5, 43], though he confided to others that there was "definitely something mysterious about the place" [44].

### Footsteps and Churchyard Sounds

- **2 August 1947** — Before taking ownership, Turner heard **lame footsteps and the swishing of clerical clothing** in the churchyard at about midnight on a bright moonlit night. He saw nothing to account for the sounds [28–31]. The same sound — lame footsteps, clerical gown swishing — was reported by Miss Backhouse the following month (September 1947), and by an unidentified visitor at an unknown date. These are widely attributed to the ghost of [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]], who was lame from rheumatism and died in 1945. *(gtwnd-ch04)*
- **Whit Monday 1947** — All four (James, Cathy, Rev. Henning, Mrs Henning) heard footsteps and muffled talking from inside the locked [[borley-church|Borley Church]]; the sounds ceased the instant the door began to open [50].
- **About 1951** — Turner smelled a strong odour of incense near the church. *(gtwnd-ch04)*

### Crashes and Domestic Phenomena

- On 27 May 1947, a very loud noise from empty upper rooms of the cottage; doors all closed, nothing moved [32–34].
- In September 1948, a crash of breaking crockery so loud that two workmen outside came to the door to ask what had happened; nothing was broken [35–37]. A few weeks later, a noise identical to a pistol shot from within the kitchen [36, 37].

### The Phantom Cat

On 28 August 1949, a thin, scraggy, grey-white cat aggressively chased the Turners' own cat, Fred, under their car. The strange cat vanished completely, despite the surrounding area being open ground [38–40]. Both Turners experienced what became known as the [[phantom-cat]] repeatedly throughout 1947–1950.

### Footsteps from the Turners' Bedroom (March 1950)

[[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] and [[laurence-gafford|Laurence Gafford]], sleeping in the lounge below, heard heavy footsteps and muffled voices from the Turners' bedroom on three occasions during a night vigil — at approximately 1:15, 2:30, and 4:15 a.m. The Turners confirmed they had slept soundly throughout. (See also [[bps-06]])

## The Face in the Wall

On 24 August 1950, after leaving, Turner discovered a medieval carved stone face cemented into the brickwork of the stables at eye level. Digging beneath it, he unearthed earlier flint foundations [21–24]. [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]] later confirmed she remembered it being found and built into the wall during construction.

## Physical Excavations and Alterations

During his time at Borley, Turner dramatically altered the landscape of the haunted grounds:

- **The Sunken Garden:** He completely filled in the rubble-crammed Rectory cellars, outlined the footprint of the vanished house with a low brick wall, and planted a sunken rose garden inside it [7, 12–14].
- **Church Vault Excavation:** On Whit-Monday, 26 May 1947, Turner assisted [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Henning]] in excavating the sanctuary of [[borley-church|Borley Church]]. Turner broke into a brick-lined vault and discovered three skeletons lying in three feet of water, believed to be the 18th-century rector [[rev-humphrey-burrough|Humphrey Burrough]] and his daughters [15–17].
- **Exploring the Wells:** In July 1947, Turner and investigator Tommy Frankland excavated the main Rectory well. Turner descended 30 feet into it to confirm there were no secret [[tunnels|tunnels]] leading off from it [18–20].

## Séances

The Turners hosted a séance on 8 June 1948 at which the entity "Harry Bull" stated he would haunt Borley for "4004 years." When asked to "go to church now," the whole group did so — and found nothing.

## Private Accounts vs. Public Accounts

Turner's public and private stances on the haunting were highly contradictory. During a 1947 BBC broadcast, he stated that the poltergeist influence had "died and gone" and that the atmosphere was one of "utter peace and quiet and happiness" [41, 42]. In his 1970 autobiography, *Sometimes Into England*, he heavily disparaged the haunting, claiming "quite three-quarters — if not more — of the stories told about the house have no foundation in fact" [5].

> **Contradiction:** Privately, Turner confided to Alan Wesencraft and [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] that there was "definitely something mysterious about the place" [44]. In strict confidence, he and Cathy admitted to Underwood that they had glimpsed a nun-like figure near the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] and a tall male clerical figure in the churchyard — but kept it quiet due to Turner's religious scruples and hatred of publicity [45]. Underwood received the Turners' unpublished diaries from Cathy before her death, which confirm a substantially richer paranormal record than anything Turner published in his own name.

Turner also published *My Life With Borley Rectory* (1950) — not a factual account but a satirical novel — and typed the manuscript for [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Henning]]'s 1949 book *Haunted Borley* on the strict condition that Henning omit any mention of the Turners' names or their psychic experiences [47, 48].

Turner's final position (*Stella C*, 1973 introduction): he came to believe Ethel Bull's 1900 sighting was genuine and concluded: "forget the Bulls, the evidence for paranormal activity at Borley is established by a wealth of other evidence."

## On Georgina Dawson

Turner had no patience for the local historian [[georgina-dawson|Georgina Dawson]], warning investigator Underwood: **"Keep away from her; she has verbal and writing diarrhoea and is quite mad!"** [49]

## The Boundary Stone

When he moved, Turner gifted Peter Underwood one of the few surviving Borley relics: a boundary stone recovered from the garden bearing the carved initials "H.B." (for Henry Bull) [52–54].
