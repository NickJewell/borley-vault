# James Turner (and Catherine Turner)

Poet, author, and broadcaster. Purchased the Rectory cottage and site in May 1947; lived there with his wife Catherine until July 1950. Turned the rubble-filled cellars into a sunken rose garden. One of the most important post-fire witnesses.

## Character

The Turners came from houses they already regarded as haunted and "took most of the strange happenings as part of the furniture." Neither was frightened by their experiences — the phenomena were "part of the scenery." They left not because of the hauntings but because of "the constant stream of uninvited and inquisitive visitors" who made work impossible.

## Experiences at Borley

- Heard "happy, laughing voices" from the direction of the Rectory site for over a fortnight while clearing the Nun's Walk; voices ceased when he stopped working and "fled" when he pursued them
- Both Turners experienced the [[phantom-cat]] repeatedly (1947–1950)
- Heard unexplained sounds of crashing crockery, furniture being moved (workmen in the yard came to ask what had happened)
- Catherine Turner felt heavy footsteps approaching and dying away while sitting in the drive — "like someone walking on boards in a house that no longer existed"
- Peter Underwood and Laurence Gafford heard heavy footsteps and muffled voices from the Turners' bedroom on three occasions during a night vigil in March 1950; the Turners confirmed they had slept soundly throughout

## The Face in the Wall

In August 1950, after leaving, Turner discovered a medieval carved stone face built into the stable wall — a curiosity that had been hidden by ivy for years. Ethel Bull later confirmed she remembered it being found and built into the wall during construction.

## Participation in Excavations

Turner took part in the 1947 excavations of Borley church, helping uncover the tomb of Rev. Humphrey Burrough. He explored the wells, cleared the Rectory cellars, and covered the site with a low brick wall.

## Séances

The Turners hosted a séance on 8 June 1948 at which the entity "Harry Bull" stated he would haunt Borley for "4004 years." When asked to "go to church now," the whole group did so — and found nothing.

## Pre-Ownership Experiences at the Church

Even before moving in, Turner had experiences at Borley Church:

- **August 2, 1947** — Before taking ownership, Turner heard **lame footsteps and the swishing of clerical clothing** in the church yard. The same sound (lame footsteps; clerical gown swishing) was reported by Miss Backhouse the following month (September 1947), and by an unidentified visitor at an unknown date. These are believed to be the ghost of [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]], who was lame from rheumatism and died in 1945. *(gtwnd-ch04)*

- **About 1951** — Turner smells a strong odour of **incense** near the church. *(gtwnd-ch04)*

## Private Accounts vs. Public Accounts (bps-08, bps-09)

James published several accounts of his Borley years (*Sometimes into England*, 1970; a fictional satire, 1950) in which he was consistently sceptical or minimising. But in private — in letters to Underwood and in unpublished diaries given to Underwood by Cathy before her death — a different picture emerges:

- **Midnight churchyard (2 November 1947):** Heard "something or somebody with a lame leg and a swishing skirt" approach along the church path and pass toward the porch — the same lame footsteps attributed elsewhere to the ghost of [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]]. Used in a 1951 feature film.
- **Whit Monday 1947:** All four (James, Cathy, Rev. Henning, Mrs Henning) heard footsteps and muffled talking from inside the locked church; sounds ceased the instant the door began to open.
- **Loud furniture noise:** The day after, a very loud noise from empty upper rooms of the cottage. Doors all closed; nothing moved.
- **Happy voices (May–June 1947):** While clearing brambles from the old orchard, Turner heard "happy and laughing" voices from the direction of the Rectory site each evening. Cathy heard them too. "Always, as soon as I stopped work and laid down the sickle, the voices, happy and laughing, fled before me." After the clearing was done and the path restored, the voices were never heard again.
- **Crockery crash (September 1948):** Very loud crash heard by both James and Cathy; nothing disturbed; two workmen outside came to ask what had happened.
- **Spectral cat (summer 1949):** A thin grey-white cat chased their cat Fred under the car; Fred emerged from the other side; the pursuing cat was nowhere to be found. The same cat seen on another occasion; disappeared impossibly.
- **Footsteps from Turners' room (March 1950):** Underwood and Laurence Gafford, sleeping in the lounge below, heard heavy footsteps and muffled voices from above at approximately 1:15, 2:30, and 4:15 a.m.; the Turners confirmed they slept soundly throughout. (See also [[bps-06]])

**Privately confirmed:** Both James and Cathy told Underwood they had "glimpsed a nun-like figure near the Nun's Walk one summer evening and seen a tall male clerical figure in the churchyard" — and that they had both been in contact with an entity claiming to be the Rev. Harry Bull. Price wrote to Turner intending to include these experiences in *Borley III*.

James's final position (*Stella C*, 1973 introduction): he came to believe Ethel Bull's 1900 sighting was genuine and concluded: "forget the Bulls, the evidence for paranormal activity at Borley is established by a wealth of other evidence."
