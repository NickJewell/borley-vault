# Lieutenant W. Kujawa

Polish Army Medical Corps officer; stationed in Britain during the Second World War. Along with [[lt-nawrocki|Lieutenant G. B. Nawrocki]], Kujawa investigated the Borley ruins on two separate nights in 1943, producing one of the most systematic wartime investigation records.

## Two Nights at the Ruins

**First Night (June 28–29, 1943):** The party consisted of [[lt-nawrocki|Lt. Nawrocki]], Kujawa, Second Lt. [[lt-ligaszewski|A. Ligaszewski]], and [[col-wroblewski|Colonel J. Wroblewski]]. At 8:20 p.m., a stone was thrown at Kujawa in the kitchen passage, hitting him on the shoulder — one of the first recorded poltergeist-type events at the ruins.

**Second Night (July 28–29, 1943):** A smaller party: Kujawa, [[lt-nawrocki|Lt. Nawrocki]], and two English boys. They "rebuilt" the floor of the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] with planks and straw to make it habitable for the night. Kujawa took up position in the window of the Blue Room to watch for the Nun, while Nawrocki watched from Room 5. Between 11 p.m. and 12:30 a.m., twenty to thirty stones were thrown in the Blue Room and the landing area near the "Cold Spot."

At 00:30 a.m., Kujawa and Nawrocki saw a shadow of a man silhouetted on the wall of the chapel, which stood without moving for ten to twenty seconds before vanishing "very slowly."

Kujawa is also referenced in Nawrocki's covering letter as corroborating the witness's account of the shadow on the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] on both nights.

## Significance

Kujawa and Nawrocki's two-night investigation is notable for its precision: minute-by-minute protocols, signed reports, and the methodological habit — common in military medical training — of recording exact times and exact positions. The July 28 visit occurred on the traditional "anniversary" night when the nun was said always to appear, though neither officer was aware of this when they planned their visit. See [[lt-nawrocki]] for the full protocol.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Lieutenant W. Kujawa** was an officer in the Polish Army who, alongside his colleague Lieutenant G.B. Nawrocki (a medical doctor), reported experiencing some of the most sensational and violent phenomena in the ruined Borley Rectory during the "Later Borley" period [1-3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following specific facts, dates, events, quotes, and contradictions regarding his involvement:

**First Visit and Stone-Throwing (Night of June 28–29, 1943)**
Lieutenant Kujawa first visited the burnt-out Rectory ruins accompanied by Lieut. Nawrocki, Colonel J. Wroblewski, and Second Lieut. A. Ligaszewski [3]. The officers reported immediate poltergeist activity:
*   At **8:20 p.m.**, while standing in the kitchen passage, **a stone was thrown at Kujawa, hitting him directly on the shoulder** [4, 5].
*   At **2:40 a.m.**, while the men were resting in the darkness of the dining room, Ligaszewski made a remark to Kujawa [6, 7]. Kujawa hushed his colleague, asking him to be quiet with the specific quote: **"because a brick could easily be thrown from the ceiling upon our heads"** [7]. 
*   Kujawa had scarcely finished speaking this warning when half a brick was inexplicably hurled from the ceiling, crashing loudly onto the center of their improvised table [7].

**Second Visit and the Blue Room Vigil (Night of July 28–29, 1943)**
Kujawa and Nawrocki returned to the site exactly a month later on the traditional "anniversary" of the phantom nun's appearance, this time accompanied by two unnamed "English boys" [8, 9]. 
*   **A Timeline Contradiction:** While the vast majority of the texts and Harry Price's official logs record this second visit as occurring on July 28–29, **1943** [8-10], one chronological summary explicitly dates the sighting of the shadowy figure by Kujawa and Nawrocki to July 28–29, **1944** [11].
*   At **8:30 p.m.**, while investigating the ruins, **Kujawa had another stone thrown at him** while standing in the passage outside the Chapel [9]. 
*   At **9:00 p.m.**, the Polish officers decided to spend the night on the dangerous, burnt-out first floor. To do this, **Kujawa and Nawrocki improvised and "rebuilt" the floor of the infamous Blue Room** using loose planks and fallen roof timbers, bringing in straw to sit on [9, 12].
*   At **10:00 p.m.**, the watch for the phantom nun began, with **Kujawa specifically stationed at the window of the Blue Room** [13, 14]. 
*   During this visit, the party reported dozens of falling stones, dull thumps, and the sighting of shadowy figures on the Nun's Walk and inside the house, before Kujawa and Nawrocki finally left the ruins at 6:00 a.m. [14-16]. 

**SPR Skepticism and the "Impenetrable Fog of Mystery"**
The astonishing reports filed by Kujawa and Nawrocki were heavily championed by Harry Price in his book *The End of Borley Rectory*, but they became a major target of criticism by the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) [1, 8].

The SPR authors (Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) bluntly dismissed the Polish officers' claims as an **"elaborate hoax"**, declaring: "Frankly, we cannot take this account seriously" [17, 18]. They pointed out several contradictions and implausibilities surrounding Kujawa's testimony:
*   **The Cambridge Commission Contrast:** The SPR highlighted the absurdity that Kujawa and Nawrocki supposedly experienced the entire spectrum of traditional Borley phenomena in just two nights, whereas the scientific "Cambridge Commission" (who were investigating the ruins during the exact same weeks in June and July 1943) recorded absolutely nothing of interest [17, 18]. 
*   **The Blue Room Window Impossibility:** The SPR strongly criticized the claim that Kujawa kept watch from the Blue Room window [13]. They pointed out that Harry Price himself had written in 1939 that reaching that window was physically impossible due to the burnt-through rafters, making it highly suspicious that Kujawa could sit there a few years later when the house had deteriorated even further [13]. 
*   **The Rumor of Paid Vigils:** The SPR also noted testimony from another investigator, Robert F. Aickman, who had visited the ruins in July 1943 and was told that a group of Polish officers had **paid £5 for the privilege of spending the night in the haunted ruins** [17, 19]. The SPR suggested that the presence of such high fees made the Polish officers' sensational accounts deeply suspicious [19].
