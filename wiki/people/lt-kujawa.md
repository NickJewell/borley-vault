# Lieutenant W. Kujawa

Polish Army Medical Corps officer; stationed in Britain during the Second World War. Along with [[lt-nawrocki|Lieutenant G. B. Nawrocki]], Kujawa investigated the Borley ruins on two separate nights in 1943, producing one of the most systematic wartime investigation records.

## Two Nights at the Ruins

**First Night (June 28–29, 1943):** The party consisted of [[lt-nawrocki|Lt. Nawrocki]], Kujawa, Second Lt. [[lt-ligaszewski|A. Ligaszewski]], and [[col-wroblewski|Colonel J. Wroblewski]]. At 8:20 p.m., a stone was thrown at Kujawa in the kitchen passage, hitting him on the shoulder — one of the first recorded poltergeist-type events at the ruins.

**Second Night (July 28–29, 1943):** A smaller party: Kujawa, [[lt-nawrocki|Lt. Nawrocki]], and two English boys. They "rebuilt" the floor of the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] with planks and straw to make it habitable for the night. Kujawa took up position in the window of the Blue Room to watch for the Nun, while Nawrocki watched from Room 5. Between 11 p.m. and 12:30 a.m., twenty to thirty stones were thrown in the Blue Room and the landing area near the "Cold Spot."

At 00:30 a.m., Kujawa and Nawrocki saw a shadow of a man silhouetted on the wall of the chapel, which stood without moving for ten to twenty seconds before vanishing "very slowly."

Kujawa is also referenced in Nawrocki's covering letter as corroborating the witness's account of the shadow on the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] on both nights.

## Significance

Kujawa and Nawrocki's two-night investigation is notable for its precision: minute-by-minute protocols, signed reports, and the methodological habit — common in military medical training — of recording exact times and exact positions. The July 28 visit occurred on the traditional "anniversary" night when the nun was said always to appear, though neither officer was aware of this when they planned their visit. See [[lt-nawrocki]] for the full protocol.
