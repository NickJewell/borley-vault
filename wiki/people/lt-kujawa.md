# Lieutenant W. Kujawa

Polish Army Medical Corps officer; stationed in Britain during the Second World War. Along with [[gb-nawrocki|Lieutenant G. B. Nawrocki]], Kujawa investigated the [[borley-rectory|Borley ruins]] on two separate nights in 1943, producing one of the most systematic wartime investigation records.

## First Night at the Ruins (June 28–29, 1943)

The party consisted of [[gb-nawrocki|Lt. Nawrocki]], Kujawa, Second Lt. [[lt-ligaszewski|A. Ligaszewski]], and [[col-wroblewski|Colonel J. Wroblewski]]. At **8:20 p.m.**, while standing in the kitchen passage, **a stone was thrown at Kujawa, hitting him directly on the shoulder** — one of the first recorded poltergeist-type events at the ruins.

Later in the early hours, the men were resting in the darkness of the dining room when Ligaszewski made a remark to Kujawa. Kujawa hushed his colleague, warning him to be quiet **"because a brick could easily be thrown from the ceiling upon our heads."** He had scarcely finished speaking when half a brick was inexplicably hurled from the direction of the ceiling, crashing loudly onto the centre of their improvised table.

## Second Night — The Blue Room Vigil (July 28–29, 1943)

> **Contradiction:** While the vast majority of texts and Harry Price's official logs record this second visit as occurring on July 28–29, **1943**, one chronological summary explicitly dates the shadowy figure sighting to July 28–29, **1944**.

A smaller party returned: Kujawa, [[gb-nawrocki|Lt. Nawrocki]], and two unnamed English boys. They "rebuilt" the floor of the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] with planks and straw to make it habitable for the night.

- At **8:30 p.m.**, another stone was thrown at Kujawa while standing in the passage outside the Chapel.
- At **9:00 p.m.**, the officers decided to spend the night on the dangerous, burnt-out first floor.
- At **10:00 p.m.**, watch for the phantom nun began, with **Kujawa specifically stationed at the window of the Blue Room**.
- Between 11 p.m. and 12:30 a.m., twenty to thirty stones were thrown in the Blue Room and the landing area near the "Cold Spot."
- At **00:30 a.m.**, Kujawa and Nawrocki saw a shadow of a man silhouetted on the wall of the chapel, which stood without moving for ten to twenty seconds before vanishing "very slowly."

Kujawa is also referenced in Nawrocki's covering letter as corroborating the witness's account of the shadow on the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] on both nights. The party finally left the ruins at 6:00 a.m.

## SPR Skepticism

The accounts filed by Kujawa and Nawrocki were championed by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] in *The End of Borley Rectory* but heavily attacked by the [[the-borley-report|SPR *Borley Report*]]. The SPR authors (Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) bluntly dismissed the Polish officers' claims as an **"elaborate hoax"**, declaring: "Frankly, we cannot take this account seriously." They raised several specific objections:

- **The Cambridge Commission Contrast:** The SPR highlighted that Kujawa and Nawrocki supposedly experienced the entire spectrum of traditional Borley phenomena in just two nights, whereas the scientific [[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]] — investigating the ruins during the exact same weeks in June and July 1943 — recorded absolutely nothing of interest.
- **The Blue Room Window Impossibility:** Price himself had written in 1939 that reaching that window was physically impossible due to the burnt-through rafters, making it highly suspicious that Kujawa could sit there a few years later when the house had deteriorated further.
- **The Rumour of Paid Vigils:** Investigator [[robert-fordyce-aickman|Robert F. Aickman]], who visited the ruins in July 1943, reportedly heard that a group of Polish officers had **paid £5 for the privilege of spending the night in the haunted ruins** — a detail the SPR suggested made their sensational accounts deeply suspicious.

## Significance

Kujawa and Nawrocki's two-night investigation is notable for its precision: minute-by-minute protocols, signed reports, and the methodological habit — common in military medical training — of recording exact times and positions. The July 28 visit occurred on the traditional "anniversary" night when the nun was said always to appear, though neither officer was aware of this when they planned their visit.
