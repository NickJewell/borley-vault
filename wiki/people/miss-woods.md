# Miss Woods

Daughter of the man who purchased the Borley Rectory ruins and land for demolition after [[captain-gregson|Captain Gregson]]'s sale (at a price mentioned to [[harry-price|Price]] as £560). [[harry-price|Price]] interviewed her on his visit of June 1, 1945, having made the journey from London to witness and document the final state of the site.

## Fate of the Italian Mantelpieces

Miss Woods told Price what had happened to the two ornate Italian inlaid marble mantelpieces that had decorated the dining-room and drawing-room — distinctive features of the Rectory that Price had photographed and written about extensively. The one in the dining-room featured two carved marble monks' heads and had been singled out by [[mrs-c-ryan-baines|Mrs. C. Ryan Baines]] as significant (the nun peering through the window would have been gazing at these monks).

The mantelpieces were carefully removed and put aside. The next day they were found **smashed to pieces**, with one of the monks' heads missing.

Miss Woods attributed the damage to "mischievous boys" — an assessment Price accepted as probable, though he noted the possibility of souvenir-hunters. He expressed regret about the loss of the monks' mantelpiece, to which he admitted "a sort of curious affection."

## Demolition Materials

Miss Woods also told Price that much of the Rectory material had been sold to farmers for hayrick foundations and that the rubble was used for American airfield runways. [[eric-calcraft|Eric Calcraft]], the Bulmer photographer, was building a garage from Rectory materials, including eleven-inch oak beams. Price noted the Rectory had been "exceptionally well built."
