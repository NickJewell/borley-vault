# Miss Woods

Daughter of **Mr Woods**, a local demolition contractor who purchased the burned-out shell of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] from [[captain-gregson|Captain W. H. Gregson]] in late 1943, intending to dismantle the ruins and sell the salvaged materials — slates, red bricks, and timber — then divide and resell the cleared land for profit [1–4].

[[harry-price|Price]] interviewed her on his visit of **1 June 1945**, having made the journey from London to witness and document the final state of the site. She provided him with "the latest information concerning the end of the Rectory" [5].

## Fate of the Italian Mantelpieces

Miss Woods told Price a "curious story" regarding the fate of two ornate Italian inlaid marble mantelpieces that had decorated the dining-room and drawing-room — distinctive features [[harry-price|Price]] had photographed and written about extensively [5]. The dining-room mantelpiece was particularly notable for its carved marble monks' heads, singled out by [[mrs-c-ryan-baines|Mrs C. Ryan Baines]] as significant (the [[the-nun|nun]] peering through the window would have been gazing at these monks).

According to Miss Woods, the workmen had carefully removed both mantelpieces from the ruins and set them aside to be salvaged [5]. When the men returned the next day, however, they found the marble relics had been **"found smashed to pieces and one of the monks' head was missing"** [5].

She attributed the damage to **"mischievous boys"**, though Price personally suspected local souvenir-hunters were the true culprits [5]. He expressed regret about the loss of the monks' mantelpiece, to which he admitted "a sort of curious affection."

## Demolition Materials

Miss Woods also told Price that much of the Rectory material had been sold to farmers for hayrick foundations and that rubble was used for American airfield runways. [[eric-calcraft|Eric Calcraft]], the Bulmer photographer, was building a garage from Rectory materials, including eleven-inch oak beams. Price noted the Rectory had been "exceptionally well built."

The sources contain no further biographical details, events, or quotes regarding Miss Woods beyond her recounting of these demolition-period details.
