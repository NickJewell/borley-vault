# G. S. Taylor

Correspondent who wrote to [[harry-price|Harry Price]] about the Borley wall-writings and, separately, about the origin and early life of the ghost nun [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]. He resided at **Portobello Farm, Watlington, Oxon** [1].

> **Note:** He should not be confused with other individuals named Taylor in the Borley narrative, such as the 1938 official observer Mrs. C. S. Taylor, or the Mass-Observation reporter [[gay-taylor-mass-obs|Mrs. Gay Taylor]].

## The February 1942 Correspondence

Taylor wrote to [[harry-price|Harry Price]] on **February 16, 1942**, having read Price's *The Most Haunted House in England* carefully [1]. Like [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon W.J. Phythian-Adams]], Taylor had independently arrived at the **"Well-Tank-Bottom-Me"** rendering of one of the famous "Marianne" [[wall-writings|wall appeals]] [1].

Taylor made a unique geographical connection between this specific wall-writing and a mysterious deep scratch (shaped like an arrow-shaft and head) that had inexplicably appeared on the ground-floor kitchen passage wall during an observational vigil on July 19, 1937 [1]. He pointed out the proximity of these two markings to the Rectory's well, sending Price the following query:

> **"Having read your book with attention, I note that one of the pencilled 'Ma' messages was written on the wall close to the well, and that the 'scratch' described on p. 223 was close to it. I should very much like to know in which direction that arrow pointed!"** [1]

## The 1947 Letter on Marie Lairre

In a later letter, dated **February 17, 1947**, Taylor asserted that [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] was born on **September 27, 1647** in either **Caudibec** or **Caldibec** (i.e. Caudebec-en-Caux on the Seine between Le Havre and Rouen) and had entered a **nunnery of St. Francois de Sales in Le Havre**.

[[ivan-banks|Banks]] notes that the name "Caidibec" had already appeared in Price's published planchette extracts in both *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940) and *The End of Borley Rectory* (1946). Taylor would have had ample opportunity to read both books before writing his 1947 letter, making his claim to have received the information independently through a séance contact difficult to assess.

## Banks's Investigation

Banks pursued both claims through French archives:

**Caudebec-en-Caux:** The town's archives were largely destroyed during WWII (Normandy fighting), and what survives is now held at the regional archives of Seine Maritime at Cours Clemenceau in Rouen. Banks's enquiries there found "nothing to substantiate Taylor's theory about the nun Marie Lairre having originated from Caudebec in 1647 or at any other time."

**Convent of St. Francois de Sales, Le Havre:** Banks's investigation of Le Havre's municipal archives showed that St. Francois de Sales was a **small parish church**, not a convent. Furthermore, Le Havre had only one order of nuns at the relevant period — the **Sisters of St. Thomas de Villeneuve** — and no order of St. Francois de Sales is documented there.

Both Taylor's claims therefore fail to survive archival scrutiny.

## Significance

Taylor represents a recurring pattern in the Borley literature: well-intentioned correspondents who conflate published details with private spiritual impressions and present the synthesis as independent evidence. Banks's investigation of Taylor's claims is a minor but methodologically important demonstration of why séance material requires corroboration from independent archival sources.
