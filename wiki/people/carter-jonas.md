# Air Commodore R. Carter Jonas

Member of Harry Price's [[ghost-club]], Number 45. One of [[harry-price]]'s first and most enthusiastic investigators at Borley during the [[price-tenancy]].

## First Visit and Lifelong Interest

From the time he answered [[harry-price]]'s 1937 advertisement in *The Times*, until his death in the 1990s, Carter Jonas remained deeply interested in all things psychic, especially the haunting of [[borley-rectory]]. He visited the Rectory several times, meeting members of the [[bull-family]] and others associated with the case.

His personal record of his involvement (which he presented to [[peter-underwood]]) includes details of his first arrival at the Rectory on 29 June 1937. At the end of that report he wrote:

> "The night had passed in absolute silence and I have seldom stayed in a house at night which has less of the usual noises such as wood creaking, etc."

The report also contains four personal letters from Price and eleven relevant newspaper cuttings dating from 1939 to 1942.

## Assessment

Carter Jonas visited a great many haunted houses but for him Borley Rectory stood alone "as by far the most interesting haunted house he ever came across." His unusually measured first-night report — noting the *absence* of the atmospheric noises one might expect — is a significant counterpoint to the SPR's suggestion that the [[blue-book-of-instructions|Blue Book of Instructions]] was designed to generate suggestion-driven phenomena. Carter Jonas observed nothing that first night, and recorded the fact honestly.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Air Commodore R. Carter Jonas, CBE** (sometimes recorded in the sources with the first initial "E") was a Royal Air Force officer and an enthusiastic psychical investigator who served as one of Harry Price’s official observers during the tenancy of Borley Rectory [1-3]. Having responded to Price’s famous 1937 advertisement in *The Times*, he became intricately involved in the case and retained a lifelong interest in the haunting until his death in the 1990s [4]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following specific details regarding his involvement:

**Lesser-Known Biographical Details**
Jonas was **Member Number 45 of Harry Price’s exclusive Ghost Club** [4]. He and his wife, Gina, were long-time friends with investigator Peter Underwood, frequently hosting him at their beautiful home in Fowey, Cornwall [5]. When Jonas eventually moved from Cornwall, he **presented Underwood with his personal Borley logbook, files, 11 relevant newspaper cuttings (dating from 1939 to 1942), and four personal letters from Harry Price** [5, 6]. Jonas visited many haunted houses throughout his life, but he maintained that Borley Rectory stood alone as by far the most interesting case he had ever encountered [6].

**The June 1937 Visit: The Apport and the Absolute Silence**
On June 29–30, 1937, Jonas (then a Flight-Lieutenant) visited the Rectory alongside his friend, Flight-Lieutenant L. F. Caunter [6-8]. During this visit, they experienced a strange materialization: at 7:00 p.m. on the 29th, **they discovered a steel nail file inside a ground-floor cupboard** [8, 9]. Neither officer had noticed it during a careful inspection of the same area earlier in the day, and Jonas noted in his report that it was "inconceivable that two very alert young men, when actually looking for objects, should both have missed the file on their first inspection" [9, 10]. 

Despite the reputation of the house for auditory phenomena, Jonas's primary observation during this visit was the uncanny lack of natural sound. He provided a memorable quote in his official report: 
> **"The night had passed in absolute silence and I have seldom stayed in a house at night which has less of the usual noises such as wood creaking, etc."** [6, 10].

**The March 1938 Visit**
Jonas and Caunter returned to Borley for a two-night stay on March 16–17, 1938. During this trip, they also visited the Bull family at Chilton Lodge [5]. Jonas reported that **"absolutely nothing"** occurred during this watch—no fresh pencil marks, messages, or movements of objects were found [11]. He again remarked on the oppressive quiet of the property, describing the house and grounds at night as being **"as still as a vault"** [5, 11].

**The November 1938 Visit: The Smell of Incense**
Jonas and Caunter made one final, unofficial visit to the Rectory in November 1938. By this time, Price's tenancy had expired eight months previously, and the property had been sold to Capt. W. H. Gregson [3, 11]. The two officers visited on the spur of the moment while on leave, right before they deployed to Egypt [3, 11]. 

While exploring the shut-up, unfurnished house, they experienced a striking olfactory phenomenon. In a letter to Harry Price dated December 12, 1938, forwarded from Ismailia, Egypt, Jonas wrote: 
> **"All seemed to be much the same as before, except in one place upstairs there was an overpowering smell of incense, which we had not noticed on previous visits"** [12]. 
Harry Price considered this highly significant, as the smell of incense pointed towards a Roman Catholic presence, aligning with the legends of the phantom nun and monks [3, 12].

*(Note regarding contradictions: The sources contain minor inconsistencies regarding his military rank and initials, which is typical for documents spanning decades. He is predominantly identified as "R. Carter Jonas" [4] but twice referred to as "E. Carter Jonas" [1, 3]. Depending on the date of the record, his rank is listed variably as Flight-Lieutenant [8], Group-Captain [13], and Air Commodore [2]).*
