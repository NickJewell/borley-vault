# Air Commodore R. Carter Jonas

Member of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s [[ghost-club]] (Number 45); one of Price's first and most enthusiastic investigators at Borley during the [[the-observer-program|Price tenancy]]. He and his wife Gina were long-time friends with investigator [[peter-underwood]]. He visited many haunted houses throughout his life but maintained that Borley Rectory stood alone as by far the most interesting case he had ever encountered [6].

> **Note:** The sources contain minor inconsistencies regarding his initials and rank. He is predominantly identified as "R. Carter Jonas" [4] but twice referred to as "E. Carter Jonas" [1, 3]. His rank is listed variably as Flight-Lieutenant [8], Group-Captain [13], and Air Commodore [2], depending on the date of the record.

## The June 1937 Visit: The Apport and the Absolute Silence

On June 29–30, 1937, Jonas (then a Flight-Lieutenant) visited the Rectory alongside his friend, Flight-Lieutenant [[flight-lieutenant-caunter|L. F. Caunter]] [6-8]. During this visit, they experienced a strange materialisation: at 7:00 p.m. on the 29th, **they discovered a steel nail file inside a ground-floor cupboard** [8, 9]. Neither officer had noticed it during a careful inspection of the same area earlier in the day, and Jonas noted in his report that it was "inconceivable that two very alert young men, when actually looking for objects, should both have missed the file on their first inspection" [9, 10].

Despite the Rectory's reputation for auditory phenomena, Jonas's primary observation was the uncanny lack of natural sound. He recorded a memorable quote in his official report:
> **"The night had passed in absolute silence and I have seldom stayed in a house at night which has less of the usual noises such as wood creaking, etc."** [6, 10]

## The March 1938 Visit

Jonas and Caunter returned for a two-night stay on March 16–17, 1938, and also visited the Bull family at Chilton Lodge [5]. Jonas reported that **"absolutely nothing"** occurred during this watch — no fresh pencil marks, messages, or movements of objects — and again remarked on the oppressive quiet of the property, describing the house and grounds at night as being **"as still as a vault"** [5, 11].

## The November 1938 Visit: The Smell of Incense

Jonas and Caunter made one final, unofficial visit to the Rectory in November 1938. By this time, Price's tenancy had expired eight months previously and the property had been sold to [[captain-gregson|Capt. W. H. Gregson]] [3, 11]. The two officers visited on the spur of the moment while on leave, right before deploying to Egypt [3, 11].

While exploring the shut-up, unfurnished house, they experienced a striking olfactory phenomenon. In a letter to Harry Price dated December 12, 1938, forwarded from Ismailia, Egypt, Jonas wrote:
> **"All seemed to be much the same as before, except in one place upstairs there was an overpowering smell of incense, which we had not noticed on previous visits"** [12].

Price considered this highly significant, as the smell of incense pointed towards a Roman Catholic presence, aligning with the legends of the phantom nun and monks [3, 12].

## Legacy

Jonas's unusually measured first-night report — noting the *absence* of the atmospheric noises one might expect — is a significant counterpoint to the SPR's suggestion that the [[blue-book-of-instructions|Blue Book of Instructions]] was designed to generate suggestion-driven phenomena. Jonas observed nothing that first night and recorded the fact honestly.

When Jonas eventually moved from his home in Fowey, Cornwall, he **presented Peter Underwood with his personal Borley logbook, files, eleven relevant newspaper cuttings (dating from 1939 to 1942), and four personal letters from Harry Price** [5, 6].
