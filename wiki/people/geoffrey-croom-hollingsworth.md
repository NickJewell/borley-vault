# Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth (30 October 1917 – 2 May 1987)

Born Stretford, Greater Manchester; died Harlow, Essex, aged 70 of heart disease. Highways labourer, assistant caretaker and lay paranormal investigator who conducted the most extensive investigations at Borley in the early 1970s and claimed the most spectacular post-demolition sightings of the phantom nun.

> **Contradiction:** Some texts state he died on **May 2, 1987** [1, 3], while another claims he died of a heart condition at Princess Alexandra Hospital in Harlow in **October 1987**, at the age of 70 [4].

Croom-Hollingsworth consciously modelled his public persona and investigative style on [[harry-price|Harry Price]]. He posed for newspaper photographs holding a pipe in a "Price-like close-up," and he referred to his closest associate, [[roy-potter|Roy Potter]], as his "Chief Investigator," directly mirroring Price's relationship with [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] [2]. Following his death, his widow Helena, who wanted nothing to do with his ghost-hunting exploits, completely disposed of his extensive case files, Borley materials, and his unpublished manuscript, *The Ghosts Remain at Borley* [4, 5].

## Investigations

Working with a team including "Chief Investigator" [[roy-potter|Roy Potter]], [[f-connell|Frank Connell]], and later film cameraman [[denny-densham|Denny Densham]], Croom-Hollingsworth visited Borley extensively between **March 1970 and 1980**. His team brought "modern" technology to the site: infra-red cameras, delicate sound-recording apparatus, walkie-talkie communication, and a new kind of "sound scanner" designed to pick up noises within a 500-yard radius [1, 8–10]. During a typical vigil, he would place two men on the Rectory site, two on the roadway, and two in the churchyard, rotating positions every two hours [6].

He was featured in the 1975 BBC programme *The Ghost Hunters* (broadcast December 4, 1975) [24]. For this programme, the producer, Hugh Burnett, deliberately chose to interview him at work in a trench on the construction site of the M11 motorway extension near Epping, **"so that people could see me working at an ordinary job"** [5]. His audio recordings were released on a commercial cassette tape titled *The Ghost of Borley Church* by Mindpower Records in the mid-1970s [16].

In late 1972, his original team disbanded and he shifted his primary focus from the Rectory site to [[borley-church|Borley Church]] [6]. He abandoned his investigations altogether in 1980, citing harassment by **"yobs and such like"** who disrupted night vigils [2].

### The 1970 Nun Sighting

The most significant event from his investigations. On **20 June 1970**, at approximately 1:50 a.m., while stationed in the back garden of a bungalow on the old Nun's Walk, he suddenly felt extremely cold. He watched a figure glide about a foot above the ground, passing clean through an old fence, and contacted [[roy-potter|Potter]] and [[f-connell|Frank Connell]] by walkie-talkie [13]. According to their various accounts, all three saw the figure, which appeared as a woman in a grey habit and cowl; Potter clearly saw two figures in the bright moonlight — one was Croom-Hollingsworth, and the other was the ghost.

At 15 feet from the figure, [[roy-potter|Potter]] could see: eyes shut, a sad expression, a mole or blemish on her left cheek, head and shoulders covered by a cowl, dark grey robes reaching down to and covering her feet.

Croom-Hollingsworth declared: **"The sight of her is a memory I will take to my deathbed"** [4].

> **Contradiction:** The duration of the sighting changed repeatedly in different accounts — 8 minutes, "eight to eleven minutes," then 12 minutes, then back to 8 [14]. Furthermore, Croom-Hollingsworth described the figure as an "elderly woman in her sixties" — at variance with virtually all previous sightings, which described a young woman [14]. Babbs and Mathias suggest she may be a different entity — perhaps a nun associated with the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave household]] rather than [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]].

### The 1972 Sighting

On 13 July 1972, [[roy-potter|Potter]] and Croom-Hollingsworth again reported seeing the same nun near the Rectory site in bright moonlight. Potter threw a brick at the figure; the object passed clean through it, which remained visible and unmoved.

### Borley Church Recordings

[[denny-densham|Denny Densham]] joined the group in late 1972 and began recording allegedly paranormal sounds inside [[borley-church|Borley Church]]. The tapes — featuring footsteps on bare wood, knocks, and a deep human sigh — were featured in *The Ghost Hunters* and released on cassette as *The Ghost of Borley Church*. [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]], during a tour of the church for the BBC programme, located objects that "could have made it possible for every noise in the recording to have a natural explanation."

**The Tape Recorder Incident:** On one occasion the Harlow group set up recording equipment in Borley church porch and kept watch from a distance. Nobody entered. They heard a loud noise from the porch, investigated, and found the tape **removed from its reel and lying in a tangled heap on the floor** — the same problem [[denny-densham|Denny Densham]] experienced with his equipment about ten years later (brfa-ch12).

## Assessment and Controversies

The Companion notes "ample evidence that he changed his stories on several occasions to suit later developments":

- **The Densham Tape:** In 1974, he and Densham recorded what they claimed were ghostly sighs inside [[borley-church|Borley Church]]. Veteran investigator [[tony-broughall|Tony Broughall]] visited the church and easily replicated all the "ghostly" noises naturally. Confronted with this, Croom-Hollingsworth unexpectedly telephoned Broughall and backtracked, claiming the BBC had mistakenly broadcast sounds of the investigators walking around. Yet he continued to champion the recordings as genuine paranormal evidence as late as 1984 [17].
- **Mrs. Baines Contradiction:** Serious researchers like [[mrs-cc-baines|Mrs. C. Ryan Baines]] were deeply distrustful of him [1]. Despite this, in a 1984 letter to fellow enthusiast Richard Lee-Van den Daele, Croom-Hollingsworth falsely boasted that Mrs. Baines had enthusiastically endorsed him, claiming she said: **"I think your investigation is greater than Harry Price's"** [18].
- **Rival Investigators:** He was highly dismissive of rival ghost-hunting factions at Borley. When asked about the Enfield Parapsychical Research Group, he scoffed: **"Other people mentioned I regard as a joke... Most of it was junk, but looks impressive on the photo"** [19].

His unpublished book *The Ghosts Remain at Borley* (co-written with [[roy-potter|Roy Potter]]) had a scheduled December 1975 publication but never appeared. [[mrs-cc-baines|Mrs Cecil Ryan Baines]] — who was initially a supporter — "soon became extremely sceptical of Mr Croom-Hollingsworth" after his investigations began.

## Historical and Genealogical Discoveries

Despite the controversies over his psychic claims, Croom-Hollingsworth contributed to the historical understanding of the Borley residents:

- **The [[dodie-bull-diary|"Dodie" Bull Diary]]:** In 1970, by sheer fluke, he discovered a diary written by [[dodie-bull|Caroline "Dodie" Bull]] (the eldest Bull daughter) covering January 1 – October 28, 1885, in a second-hand shop in Sudbury [18, 20]. He allowed Mrs. Baines to copy it and offered to sell it to the Harry Price Library for **£1000** (~£9,500 in 2002 terms); the Library declined to pay this price [18, 21].
- **The Brackenbury Marriage:** He proved that [[ivy-brackenbury|Ivy Brackenbury]] (Harry Bull's widow) had indeed been married to a man named Harold Brackenbury previously, contradicting local gossip that she had been jilted at the altar [22].
- **The Crypt Entrance:** Long before it was officially found in 1988, Croom-Hollingsworth was firmly convinced that the secret entrance to the [[borley-church-crypt|church crypt]] was located outside in the churchyard [23].
