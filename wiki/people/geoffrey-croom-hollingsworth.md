# Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth (30 October 1917 – 2 May 1987)

Born Stretford, Greater Manchester; died Harlow, Essex, aged 70 of heart disease. Highways labourer, assistant caretaker and lay paranormal investigator who conducted the most extensive investigations at Borley in the early 1970s and claimed the most spectacular post-demolition sightings of the phantom nun.

## Investigations

Working with a team including "Chief Investigator" [[roy-potter|Roy Potter]], Frank Connell, and later film cameraman [[denny-densham|Denny Densham]], Croom-Hollingsworth visited Borley extensively between 1970 and 1980. He used infra-red cameras, walkie-talkies, and tape recorders, and was featured in the 1975 BBC programme *The Ghost Hunters*. He modelled himself consciously on [[harry-price]] — he is photographed pipe in hand in a Price-like close-up.

### The 1970 Nun Sighting

The most significant event from his investigations. On 20 June 1970, at 1.50 a.m., Croom-Hollingsworth was observing the former Nun's Walk area from a bungalow garden when he saw a "nun" drifting about a foot above ground level. He contacted Potter and Frank Connell by walkie-talkie. According to their various accounts, all three saw the figure, which appeared as a woman in a grey habit and cowl. The figure passed through a fence and disappeared towards the cottage.

> **Contradiction:** The duration of the sighting changed repeatedly in different accounts — 8 minutes, "eight to eleven minutes," then 12 minutes, then back to 8. The figure was described as an "elderly woman in her sixties" — at variance with virtually all previous sightings which described a young woman.

### The 1972 Sighting

On 13 July 1972 Potter and Croom-Hollingsworth again reported seeing the same nun near the Rectory site in bright moonlight. Potter threw a brick at the figure; the object passed clean through it, which remained visible and unmoved.

### Borley Church Recordings

Denny Densham joined the group in late 1972 and began recording allegedly paranormal sounds inside Borley Church. The tapes — featuring footsteps on bare wood, knocks, and a deep human sigh — were featured in *The Ghost Hunters* and released on cassette as *The Ghost of Borley Church*. Peter Underwood, during a tour of the church for the BBC programme, located objects that "could have made it possible for every noise in the recording to have a natural explanation."

## Assessment

The Companion notes "ample evidence that he changed his stories on several occasions to suit later developments." His unpublished book *The Ghosts Remain at Borley* (co-written with Roy Potter) had a scheduled December 1975 publication but never appeared; the manuscript was destroyed by his widow after his death. His photographically caught "nun" photograph from 1972 (the [[brazil-borley-photograph|Brazil Borley Photograph]], actually taken by Eddie Brazil that September) was "interpreted" rather than captured by him.

[[mrs-baines|Mrs Cecil Ryan Baines]] — who was a supporter — "soon became extremely sceptical of Mr Croom-Hollingsworth" after his investigations began.

He ended his Borley investigations in 1980 "due to yobs and such like causing problems during night-time vigils." He is buried in Princess Alexandra Hospital, Harlow.

## Additional Details from *The Final Analysis*

Babbs and Mathias (brfa-ch09, brfa-app) provide additional context:

- The 12-minute 1970 sighting is presented by them as one of the most remarkable post-demolition sightings: Croom-Hollingsworth contacted the other two observers by radio, giving one of them time to position himself only ~20 feet from the figure before it disappeared. At 15 feet he could see: eyes shut, sad expression, a mole or blemish on her left cheek, head and shoulders covered by a cowl, dark grey robes reaching down to and covering her feet
- His assessment of the nun as **elderly** conflicts with the Marie Lairre planchette theory (a young novice of about 19 in 1667); Babbs and Mathias suggest she may be a different entity — perhaps a nun associated with the Waldegrave household, who were staunch Catholics
- **Dodie's diary:** Croom-Hollingsworth found this diary (1 January – 28 October 1885) in a second-hand shop in Sudbury and offered it to the Harry Price Library for **£1000** (worth ~£9500 in 2002); the Library declined to pay this price
- **Tape recorder tangled in church porch:** On one occasion the Harlow group set up recording equipment in Borley church porch and kept watch from a distance. Nobody entered. They heard a loud noise from the porch, investigated, and found the tape **removed from its reel and lying in a tangled heap on the floor** — the same problem [[denny-densham]] experienced with his equipment about ten years later (brfa-ch12)

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth** (1917–1987) was a dedicated lay investigator and a major, albeit controversial, figure in the "Later Borley" period. Operating primarily in the 1970s, he led investigations that produced some of the most sensational claims of paranormal activity following the destruction of Borley Rectory. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, quotes, contradictions, and biographical details regarding his life and investigations:

**Biographical Details and Legacy**
*   **Early Life & Career:** Born on October 30, 1917, in Stretford, Greater Manchester, Croom-Hollingsworth was a working-class man employed as a highways labourer and an assistant caretaker [1]. 
*   **Harry Price Influence:** Croom-Hollingsworth consciously modeled his public persona and investigative style on the famous psychical researcher Harry Price. He posed for newspaper photographs holding a pipe in a "Price-like close-up," and he referred to his closest associate, Roy Potter, as his "Chief Investigator," directly mirroring Price's relationship with Sidney Glanville [2].
*   **Contradictory Death Dates and Lost Archives:** There is a distinct contradiction in the sources regarding the exact date of his death. Some texts state he died on **May 2, 1987** [1, 3], while another claims he died of a heart condition at Princess Alexandra Hospital in Harlow in **October 1987**, at the age of 70 [4]. Following his death, his widow, Helena, who wanted nothing to do with his ghost-hunting exploits, completely disposed of his extensive case files, Borley materials, and his unpublished manuscript, *The Ghosts Remain at Borley* [4, 5]. 

**The 1970s Investigations and Technology**
Between March and April 1970, Croom-Hollingsworth began a protracted investigation of the Borley site, leading a six-man team that included researchers Roy Potter, Frank Connell, and later, film cameraman Denny Densham [1, 6, 7]. 
*   **Technological Approach:** His team was notable for bringing "modern" technology to the site, utilizing infra-red cameras, delicate sound-recording apparatus, walkie-talkie communication, and a new kind of "sound scanner" designed to pick up noises within a 500-yard radius [1, 8-10]. 
*   **Investigative Layout:** During a typical overnight vigil, Croom-Hollingsworth would place two men on the Rectory site, two on the roadway, and two in the churchyard, rotating their positions every two hours [6]. In late 1972, his original team disbanded, and he shifted his primary focus from the empty Rectory site to Borley Church [6]. He eventually abandoned his investigations altogether in 1980, citing harassment by **"yobs and such like"** who disrupted their night vigils [2].

**The June 1970 "Phantom Nun" Sightings**
Croom-Hollingsworth is most famous for his extended, highly controversial daylight and moonlight sightings of the Borley "phantom nun." 
*   On the morning of **June 20, 1970**, at 12:20 a.m., he reported seeing a shrouded figure walking down the church path [11, 12]. 
*   An hour and a half later, at 1:50 a.m., while stationed in the back garden of a bungalow on the old Nun's Walk, he suddenly felt extremely cold. He watched a figure glide about a foot above the ground, passing clean through an old fence. He hailed his companions on the walkie-talkie. When Roy Potter ran down the road to assist, Potter clearly saw two figures in the bright moonlight—one was Croom-Hollingsworth, and the other was the ghost [13].
*   Regarding the profound impact of this sighting, Croom-Hollingsworth famously declared: **"The sight of her is a memory I will take to my deathbed"** [4]. 

**Contradictions and Skepticism Surrounding His Claims**
Croom-Hollingsworth’s findings were met with intense skepticism by other investigators, largely due to his tendency to change his stories to suit later developments [4]. 
*   **The Nun's Changing Appearance and Duration:** Croom-Hollingsworth claimed he observed the nun's face and described her as a woman in her sixties. This was in direct contradiction to all historical sightings of the Borley nun, which had consistently described a young woman [14]. Furthermore, he constantly changed the duration of the sighting, initially claiming he watched her for 8 minutes, then changing it to between 8 and 11 minutes, then 12 minutes, before finally reverting to 8 minutes [14]. 
*   **The Densham Tape Controversy:** In 1974, Croom-Hollingsworth and Denny Densham recorded what they claimed were ghostly sighs and the sound of an invisible door opening inside Borley Church [15, 16]. However, veteran investigator Tony Broughall visited the church and easily replicated all the "ghostly" noises naturally. Confronted with this, Croom-Hollingsworth unexpectedly telephoned Broughall and backtracked, claiming the BBC had mistakenly broadcast sounds of the investigators walking around. Yet, despite this admission, he continued to champion the recordings as genuine paranormal evidence as late as 1984 [17].
*   **The Mrs. Baines Endorsement Contradiction:** Serious researchers like Mrs. C. Ryan Baines were deeply distrustful of Croom-Hollingsworth [1]. Despite this, in a 1984 letter to fellow enthusiast Richard Lee-Van den Daele, Croom-Hollingsworth falsely boasted that Mrs. Baines had enthusiastically endorsed him, claiming she said: **"I think your investigation is greater than Harry Price’s"** [18]. 
*   **Rivalry with Other Groups:** He was highly dismissive of rival ghost-hunting factions at Borley. When asked about the Enfield Parapsychical Research Group, he scoffed: **"Other people mentioned I regard as a joke... Most of it was junk, but looks impressive on the photo"** [19]. 

**Historical and Genealogical Discoveries**
Despite the controversies over his psychic claims, Croom-Hollingsworth contributed to the historical understanding of the Borley residents:
*   **The "Dodie" Bull Diary:** In 1970, by sheer fluke, he discovered an 1884–1885 diary written by Caroline "Dodie" Bull (the eldest Bull daughter) in a second-hand shop in Sudbury [18, 20]. He proudly noted: **"After travelling many hundreds of miles and viewing dozens of people, by sheer fluke we bought a diary... and in 1885 the rectory was a very very happy place"** [2]. He allowed Mrs. Baines to copy it and offered to sell it to the Harry Price Library [18, 21]. 
*   **The Brackenbury Marriage:** He corrected local gossip generated by the Bull sisters by proving that Ivy Brackenbury (Harry Bull's widow) had indeed been married to a man named Harold Brackenbury previously, contradicting the rumors that she had been jilted at the altar [22]. 
*   **The Crypt Entrance:** Long before it was officially found in 1988, Croom-Hollingsworth was firmly convinced that the secret entrance to the church crypt was located outside in the churchyard [23].

**Media Appearances**
Croom-Hollingsworth’s exploits drew significant media attention. He was featured in the BBC documentary *The Ghost Hunters* (broadcast December 4, 1975) [24]. For this program, the producer, Hugh Burnett, deliberately chose to interview him at his day job—working in a trench on the construction site of the M11 motorway extension near Epping—because the producer thought **"it was a good idea to show me at work so that people could see me working at an ordinary job"** [5]. His investigations were also featured in a local newspaper article titled *Hunting Ghosts for Seven Years* in *The Gazette* [5], and his audio recordings and interviews were released on a commercial cassette tape titled *The Ghost of Borley Church* by Mindpower Records in the mid-1970s [16].
