# Eileen Garrett (1893–1970)

Famous psychic medium and founder of the **Parapsychology Foundation** in New York. Born in Ireland ("extremely Irish" — her own description of Marianne, suggesting she identified with her). Hired private detective **Robert Swanson** at the instigation of [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] to interview [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] in 1958.

## Role in the Marianne Interviews

Garrett funded both rounds of interviews (Jamestown, North Dakota, February 1958; New York, May 1958). She met personally with Marianne in New York, took her to dinner, and reported back to Hall in extraordinary detail.

Her personal assessment of Marianne (letter to Hall, May 10, 1958):
- "Extremely Irish... filled with all the imagination, superstition, legends... of my race"
- "She quite unblushingly told Mr. Swanson that she had every intention of lying to him... her lying is a game"
- "She lives in a perpetual temperature of romance"
- When asked point-blank if she had killed Foyster: Marianne "answered equally candidly that it had often been in her mind, but that he was too cunning for anything like that to happen to him"
- "I am perfectly willing to believe that if Price wanted some phenomena, and Mrs. Goldney obviously did, she would have no hesitation in producing it"
- She dangled a scholarship for Vincent as incentive for Marianne to "come clean": "She seemed to take great pleasure in this"

Despite her role as Hall's operative, Garrett was more sympathetic to Marianne than Hall: "I felt sorry for her in spite of her willfulness and mischievousness."

Her explanation for Marianne's inconsistencies: "I honestly think that so much more has happened to her that she forgets, funnily or seriously."

The Parapsychology Foundation archives subsequently suffered a basement flood that destroyed some of the Swanson interview transcripts.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Eileen Jeanette Garrett** (1893–1970) was one of the most respected mediums of the twentieth century, as well as a publisher and the founder of the Parapsychology Foundation [1, 2]. While she had no direct involvement with the Borley Rectory hauntings while they were occurring, she played a pivotal role in the late 1950s by helping psychical researchers track down and interview the Rectory's most controversial former resident, Marianne Foyster [3, 4].

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, events, quotes, and contradictions regarding her life and involvement in the case:

**Lesser-Known Biographical Details**
Garrett’s personal life was marked by profound tragedy and illness. She was born in Beaupare, County Meath, Ireland, on March 14, 1893 [1]. Her parents committed suicide shortly after her birth, and she was raised by her aunt and uncle [1]. During her childhood, she suffered from tuberculosis and other respiratory issues, which led to her leaving Ireland for England at the age of 15 [1]. 

She was married three times: her first marriage ended in divorce, and her second husband was killed in action at Ypres during the First World War [1]. She married for a third time a month before the Armistice [1]. Tragically, three of her four children died young [1]. Her surviving daughter, Eileen Coly, and granddaughter, Lisette Coly, later served as President and Executive Director, respectively, of the Parapsychology Foundation [3]. Garrett died of bone cancer on September 15, 1970 [1, 3].

**Mediumship, Publishing, and the R101 Séance**
Garrett’s psychic abilities began to develop after the First World War, and she was trained by J. Hewat McKenzie, the founder of the College of Psychic Studies [2]. In 1932–1933, she toured the United States, conducting sittings with institutions such as Johns Hopkins University and the New York Psychiatric Institute [2]. 

Her most famous association with Borley investigator Harry Price occurred in October 1930 during a séance at his National Laboratory of Psychical Research [5]. During the sitting, Garrett was apparently taken over by the spirit of Flight Lieutenant H. Carmichael Irwin, who had died just two days earlier in the crash of the R101 airship [5]. The entity provided a highly technical and detailed appraisal of what caused the crash, which an Air Ministry official later evaluated as being 70% absolutely correct and 20% highly likely [5]. 

Garrett left France for the United States at the end of 1940 as a refugee [2]. In New York, she established the Creative Age Press and published a monthly magazine devoted to literary and public affairs [2]. In 1951, alongside Francis P. Bolton, she founded the Parapsychology Foundation to provide a worldwide forum for the scientific study of psychic phenomena [2]. She also authored several books, including *Awareness* (1943), *Adventures in the Supernormal* (1949), *The Sense and Nonsense of Prophecy* (1950), *Beyond the Five Senses* (1957), and her autobiography, *Many Voices* (1968) [3]. 

**Tracking Marianne Foyster**
In 1957, the skeptical Society for Psychical Research (SPR) investigator Trevor Hall began a private investigation into Marianne Foyster’s past [3, 6]. In 1958, Hall contacted Garrett and requested her assistance in tracking down Marianne, who had emigrated to America [3]. Garrett agreed and hired a private investigator, A. Robert Swanson, to locate and interview Marianne in Jamestown, North Dakota [3, 4]. Hall and Garrett later had a falling out over their collaboration [7].

**Interviews and Tactics**
Garrett, Hall, and Swanson were dissatisfied with the initial interviews conducted in North Dakota, so Garrett invited Marianne to New York for a second round of questioning [4, 8]. In a letter to Hall dated April 16, 1958, Garrett noted that Marianne was **"not an easy girl to deal with,"** but expressed her intent to **"get her to tell all, and then help her find a decent position where she could live out a more healthy and disciplined life"** [8].

Garrett employed various tactics to try and draw the truth out of Marianne. During a dinner at Garrett's favorite restaurant, she deliberately **"pressed the bottle close to her"** to see if alcohol would loosen Marianne's tongue, but noted that Marianne **"drank like a lady and showed no outside or visible signs of being affected by it"** [9, 10]. Garrett also asked Marianne point-blank if she had ever killed her husband, Lionel Foyster. Marianne candidly replied that **"it had often been in her mind, but that he was too cunning for anything like that to happen to him"** [10]. 

To entice her cooperation, Garrett promised Marianne that if she "came clean" and told the truth about Borley, Garrett would help secure a scholarship for Marianne's young adopted son, Vincent [11, 12]. Garrett noted that Marianne **"seemed to take great pleasure in this"** and unabashedly admitted that she had every intention of lying to the investigator, Robert Swanson [11]. 

**Garrett's Opinions and Contradictions Regarding Marianne**
*   **On Marianne's Psychic Abilities:** Garrett and Marianne fundamentally contradicted each other on the subject of mediumship. Marianne vehemently denied having any psychic power [10]. However, following the interviews, Garrett wrote to Hall stating she firmly believed Marianne possessed mediumistic powers, regardless of what Marianne claimed [13]. 
*   **On Poltergeist Trickery:** Garrett concluded that if Harry Price or other investigators had wanted phenomena, Marianne **"would have no hesitation in producing it"** [10]. She assessed that Marianne's **"mischievous make-up... might well lend itself to a form of poltergeist phenomena"** [10]. 
*   **On Marianne's Inconsistencies:** When addressing Marianne's contradictory testimony regarding the events at Borley, Garrett offered a sympathetic psychological explanation, telling Hall: **"I honestly think that so much more has happened to her that she forgets, funnily or seriously"** [11].
*   **On the Household Dynamics:** Garrett relayed to Hall that Marianne had strongly insinuated her husband Lionel and the young investigator Edwin Whitehouse **"were exceedingly intimate, which suggested a spot of homosexuality in the relationship,"** though Marianne also emphatically denied having any sexual relationship with Whitehouse herself [14, 15].
