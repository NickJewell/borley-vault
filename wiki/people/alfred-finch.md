# Alfred Finch

Lifetime Borley resident, listed on the 1935 electoral roll as living "near the Green" with his wife [[margaret-finch|Margaret]]. He died while the book was in preparation. Alfred attended Sunday school at the rectory as a boy (using the servants' entrance; held in the sewing room); old Mrs Bull dispensed hot cross buns from her chair at the hall doorway on Easter Sundays.

## Bull Family Knowledge

Alfred and Margaret Finch remembered all the relevant families personally and provided the most detailed local testimony in *The Final Analysis*:

- **Rev Henry Dawson Ellis Bull** — "a typical wealthy squarson; an athlete who enjoyed boxing and fox-hunting"
- **Rev Harry Bull** — "a tall and athletic man, but suffered much from bronchitis... wheezy breathing audible from a considerable distance"; "slightly strange man who seemed to live in his own dream world"; took his last cat to church with him; Ivy Bull came downstairs backwards to check dusting
- **Alfred Bull** — schoolmaster; Alfred Finch's patron of the living; described as "the less talkative" at the luncheon with CM
- **Hubert Bull** — remembered for pushing a pram for Alfred's mother up Hall Lane unprompted
- **Rev Lionel Foyster** — confirmed as "a very tall man bent over due to arthritis; hands swollen; walked with a limp and a stick; later confined to a wheelchair; extremely thin and gaunt"
- **Marianne Foyster** — remembered as "dark-haired and attractive, very much one of the 'bright young things' of the inter-war years"
- **Herbert Mayes** — confirmed as "a very delicate man"; subject of Marianne's rumoured designs on him

## Edward and Mary Cooper

Alfred and Margaret Finch confirmed that the Coopers were "sensible and rather religious" people — strongly corroborating their testimony about the phantom coach and nun sightings. The Coopers moved to Sudbury after leaving the cottage.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Alfred Finch** was a lifelong resident of the Borley district whose extensive personal memories provided psychical researchers and historians with invaluable context regarding the daily life, occupants, and atmosphere of Borley Rectory over several decades [1, 2]. 

**Early Life and Personal Background**
Finch grew up in Borley and left school at the age of twelve because he "had a job to go to" working as a herdsman [3]. He later noted pragmatically that his classmates who stayed in school until they were fourteen only did so because they lacked employment [3]. He married Margaret Finch (who lived to the age of 97 and ran the village post office from their house), and for most of their long lives, the couple resided in a semi-detached cottage at Borley Green [4, 5]. Official records place Alfred and Margaret as electors No. 23 and No. 24 on the 1935 electoral roll for the parish [6]. 

**Memories of the Bull Incumbency**
Finch provided investigators with vivid recollections of the Rectory during the era of the Bull family. As a boy, he attended Sunday school classes at the Rectory taught by Miss Freda Bull [7]. The local children were required to use the servants' entrance, and the classes were conducted in the sewing room next to the kitchen [7]. 

He distinctly remembered the Rector's wife, old Mrs. Bull (Caroline Sarah Foyster), during the later years of her life. He recalled that on Easter Sundays, she would sit by the hall doorway with the green baize door kept shut and dispense hot cross buns to the children from a basket [7]. Finch noted that she was so aged and frail that she had to be physically assisted into and out of her chair [7]. He also fondly remembered the annual Christmas parties hosted at the Rectory as a "big treat for the villagers," where the men were gifted tobacco and the children received sweets [8].

Finch shared a touching personal anecdote regarding the Bulls' paternalistic but kindly relationship with the villagers: while walking home from Sudbury as a young boy with his mother, who was pushing his baby sibling in a pram, they were overtaken on the steep ascent of Hall Lane by Hubert Bull, who proceeded to push the pram the rest of the way up the hill for them [8].

**Observations of the Smiths and Foysters**
Finch and his wife were also present in the village during the highly publicized hauntings of the 1920s and 1930s and provided first-hand descriptions of the subsequent Rectors:
*   **The Rev. Guy Eric Smith (1928–1930):** Finch remembered Smith as a "huge man" with spectacles and a dark complexion, noting specifically that the Rector did not visit his parishioners very much [9]. 
*   **The Rev. Lionel Algernon Foyster (1930–1935):** Finch described Foyster as a very tall man who was severely bent over due to chronic arthritis, with noticeably swollen hands [10]. He recalled that Foyster walked with a limp and the aid of a stick, and was sometimes confined to a wheelchair [10]. 
*   **Herbert Mayes:** Finch also offered a description of Herbert Mayes—a local man who did casual gardening at the Rectory and was purportedly the target of Marianne Foyster's romantic designs—remembering him as a "very delicate man" [5]. 

**Legacy**
Alfred Finch died prior to the 2003 publication of Edward Babbs and Claudine Mathias's book, *Borley Rectory - The Final Analysis* [1]. The authors formally dedicated their acknowledgements to Alfred and his wife Margaret, crediting them for supplying "new details and information" and providing "a great deal of information about life at Borley Rectory" that helped ground the sensational ghost story in historical reality [1].
