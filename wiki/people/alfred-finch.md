# Alfred Finch

Lifetime Borley resident, listed on the 1935 electoral roll as electors No. 23 and No. 24 alongside his wife [[margaret-finch|Margaret]], in a semi-detached cottage at Borley Green [4, 5, 6]. He died while *The Final Analysis* was in preparation, and the authors formally dedicated their acknowledgements to Alfred and Margaret, crediting them for supplying "new details and information" that helped ground the sensational ghost story in historical reality [1]. Alfred attended Sunday school at the rectory as a boy (using the servants' entrance; held in the sewing room); old Mrs Bull dispensed hot cross buns from her chair at the hall doorway on Easter Sundays.

## Early Life and Background

Finch grew up in Borley and left school at the age of twelve because he "had a job to go to" working as a herdsman [3]. He later noted pragmatically that his classmates who stayed in school until fourteen only did so because they lacked employment [3]. His wife Margaret lived to the age of 97 and ran the village post office from their house [4, 5].

## Bull Family Knowledge

Alfred and Margaret Finch remembered all the relevant families personally and provided the most detailed local testimony in *The Final Analysis*:

- **Rev Henry Dawson Ellis Bull** — "a typical wealthy squarson; an athlete who enjoyed boxing and fox-hunting"
- **Rev Harry Bull** — "a tall and athletic man, but suffered much from bronchitis... wheezy breathing audible from a considerable distance"; "slightly strange man who seemed to live in his own dream world"; took his last cat to church with him; Ivy Bull came downstairs backwards to check dusting
- **Alfred Bull** — schoolmaster; Alfred Finch's patron of the living; described as "the less talkative" at the luncheon with CM
- **Hubert Bull** — remembered for pushing a pram for Alfred's mother up Hall Lane unprompted
- **Rev Lionel Foyster** — confirmed as "a very tall man bent over due to arthritis; hands swollen; walked with a limp and a stick; later confined to a wheelchair; extremely thin and gaunt"
- **Marianne Foyster** — remembered as "dark-haired and attractive, very much one of the 'bright young things' of the inter-war years"
- **Herbert Mayes** — confirmed as "a very delicate man"; subject of Marianne's rumoured designs on him

As a boy, Finch attended Sunday school classes at the Rectory taught by Miss Freda Bull [7]. The local children were required to use the servants' entrance, and classes were conducted in the sewing room next to the kitchen [7]. He also fondly remembered the annual Christmas parties hosted at the Rectory as a "big treat for the villagers," where the men were gifted tobacco and the children received sweets [8].

Regarding the Rev. [[rev-eric-smith|Guy Eric Smith]], Finch remembered him as a "huge man" with spectacles and a dark complexion who did not visit his parishioners very much [9].

## Edward and Mary Cooper

Alfred and Margaret Finch confirmed that the Coopers ([[edward-cooper|Edward]] and [[edward-cooper|Mary]]) were "sensible and rather religious" people — strongly corroborating their testimony about the phantom coach and nun sightings. The Coopers moved to Sudbury after leaving the cottage.
