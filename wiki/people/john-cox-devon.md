# John Cox / John Devon

A Papist priest who betrayed [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Sir Edward Waldegrave]] to the Protestant authorities in April 1561, triggering Waldegrave's arrest, imprisonment in the Tower of London, and death. Banks identifies Cox/Devon as the proximate cause of Waldegrave's downfall — and thus, under a spiritual interpretation of hauntings, as an indirect cause of whatever atmosphere attaches to the Borley site.

## Identity

The man appears in the historical record under two names: **John Cox** and **John Devon** (also written "John Devon Clarke" in his own confession). He was the **parish priest of Pentlow** — the very village where the Bull family had their seat, and where [[henry-bull|Henry Dawson Bull]]'s father Edward was later Rector.

Banks notes the irony: "How ironic it is that some three hundred years later there should come another Rector of Pentlow, Edward Bull, whose son Henry was to build and occupy a Victorian Borley Rectory upon the site that was perhaps occupied by the Rectory for which Sir Edward Waldegrave had been responsible."

## The Betrayal

On or about April 17, 1561, Edmond Grindall, Bishop of London, informed Sir William Cecil of the Privy Council that John Cox (alias John Devon) had been arrested, and expressed the view that "the Privy Council would surely punish him for his magic and conjuration."

Under interrogation by Hugh Darrell, Justice of the Peace for Kent, Cox/Devon confessed to celebrating the forbidden Mass:

> "The said within John Devon Clarke sayeth that on Tuesday next following the Friday within written, he came to Sir Edward Waldegrave, Knight, to his house at Borley in Essex and there made his abode one month in the company of Doctor Ramberge, late Dean of Lichfield, and daily did eat and drink at the only table of the said Sir Edward Waldegrave."

Cox/Devon also confessed to having celebrated Mass previously at the house of [[sir-thomas-wharton|Sir Thomas Wharton]] of Newhall, Essex.

## At Borley Hall

During his month's residence at Borley Hall, Cox/Devon:
- Celebrated the Catholic Mass (illegal under Elizabeth I)
- Was given money by Sir Edward Waldegrave
- Received from [[lady-frances-waldegrave|Lady Frances Waldegrave]] a **book of the Sarum liturgy** (the old Salisbury liturgical form)

He also resided there alongside **Dr. Ramberge** (late Dean of Lichfield), suggesting organized Catholic activity at Borley Hall rather than informal religious observance.

## Who Betrayed Cox?

Banks speculates that Cox/Devon was himself betrayed — possibly by the manservant **Goldney**, whom a November 1560 letter from Thomas Parker to Sir Edward warned was "a lying, deceitful and cunning individual." Banks suggests Goldney may have reported Cox's activities to agents of the Crown. If so, Cox's betrayal of Waldegrave may itself have been the product of his own betrayal.

## Aftermath

Following Cox/Devon's confession, Sir Edward's house at Borley Hall was searched by the Earl of Oxford, who found treasonable correspondence. Waldegrave was arrested on April 19th or 20th, 1561, and died in the Tower on September 1st of that year.
