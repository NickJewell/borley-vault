# Second Lieutenant A. Ligaszewski

Polish Army officer; present at the first of the two Polish Army Medical Corps investigations of the Borley ruins, on the night of June 28–29, 1943.

## The Half-Brick Incident

Ligaszewski was part of the four-man party that included [[lt-nawrocki|Lt. G. B. Nawrocki]], [[lt-kujawa|Lt. W. Kujawa]], and [[col-wroblewski|Colonel J. Wroblewski]]. At 2:40 a.m., the party was sitting in complete darkness in the dining-room when Ligaszewski made a remark to Kujawa:

> *"He asked him to be quiet 'because a brick could easily be thrown from the ceiling upon our heads.'"*

He had "hardly finished speaking" when a half-brick was thrown from the direction of the ceiling and fell directly in the centre of their improvised table, making a great noise.

The timing — instantaneous response to a casual, low-expectation remark about a possibility — is one of the more striking sequences in the post-fire record. Neither Ligaszewski nor any other member of the party was anywhere near the ceiling, and the ruins were confirmed deserted at the start of the visit.

He was present only for the first night; the second visit (July 28–29) involved a smaller party of Nawrocki, Kujawa, and two English boys.

See [[lt-nawrocki]] for the full protocol of both visits.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Second Lieutenant A. Ligaszewski** was an officer in the Polish Army whose contribution to the Borley Rectory saga is tied to a single, highly dramatic investigation of the burnt-out ruins during the "Later Borley" period.

The Borley sources record the following specific facts and events regarding his involvement:

**The June 1943 Vigil**
Ligaszewski visited the Rectory ruins on the night of **June 28–29, 1943** [1]. He was part of a four-man investigating party of Polish officers that included Colonel J. Wroblewski (O.C. Polish Signal Training Centre), Lieut. W. Kujawa, and Lieut. G.B. Nawrocki [1]. 

**The Falling Brick Incident**
During the vigil, the party decided to spend the night in the ruined ground-floor dining room, improvising a table and seats out of garden bricks and a half-burnt pantry drawer [2]. The sources record a highly specific interaction involving Ligaszewski that took place in the early hours of the morning:
*   At **2:40 a.m.**, Second Lieut. Ligaszewski made a remark to Lieut. Kujawa [3]. 
*   Kujawa hushed Ligaszewski, asking him to remain quiet with the specific warning: **"because a brick could easily be thrown from the ceiling upon our heads"** [3]. 
*   Scarcely had this warning been spoken to Ligaszewski when **half a brick was inexplicably hurled from the ceiling, falling directly into the center of their improvised table with a great noise** [3].

**The SPR Hoax Accusation**
While Harry Price championed the Polish officers' report as vital evidence of ongoing poltergeist activity, Ligaszewski's experience falls under a broader contradiction raised by skeptical investigators. The authors of the highly critical 1956 Society for Psychical Research (SPR) _Borley Report_ dismissed the entire Polish investigation as an **"elaborate hoax"** [4]. They noted that it was highly suspicious for Ligaszewski and his companions to experience such sensational phenomena when members of the scientific "Cambridge Commission" were investigating the ruins during the exact same weeks in June and July 1943 and reporting absolutely nothing of interest [4].
