# Dorothy Lane (née Tabbart)

Interviewed by Babbs and Mathias in November 1995 at her home in a Suffolk village. During WWII she served in the NAAFI and was stationed at Liston Hall, most of which no longer exists. At the time of the incident she was 20 years old.

## The Bedside Figure — Summer 1944

Shortly after D-Day (6 June 1944), Dorothy was sleeping in a Nissen hut in the Liston Hall grounds with three other young women. She awoke suddenly at 5am and saw:

- A **shadowy figure dressed in long grey robes** standing at her bedside
- The figure was of average height
- She felt **no malevolence** from the presence
- She reached out her hand — and felt **nothing**; the figure was not solid
- The figure stood still for approximately **ten seconds**, then **faded gradually** (did not vanish suddenly)
- She screamed, woke the other three women, who found her clearly shocked and believed her account

She was so shaken she remained shocked for several days. She had the feeling it might be a premonition, but nothing bad happened to her.

Years later she learned the bones found under the rectory had been buried in Liston churchyard on **29 May 1945** — nearly a year after her sighting. The authors note the figure has also been reported at various points throughout the district, not only at the rectory site.

## Note on Location

Liston Hall is about a mile from [[borley-rectory]], in the adjacent hamlet of Liston. The [[the-nun]] sightings are not confined to the rectory grounds; they extend throughout the wider district.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Dorothy Lane (née Tabbart)** was a World War II servicewoman whose paranormal experience near Borley has been recorded as a possible extended sighting of the infamous Borley nun phantom. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, and chronological contradictions regarding her experience:

**Lesser-Known Biographical Details**
During the Second World War, Dorothy Lane (then Miss Tabbart) served in the NAAFI (Navy, Army and Air Force Institutes) [1]. In the early summer of 1944, when she was 20 years old, she was stationed at Liston Hall (a building largely no longer in existence), which is located in the parish adjacent to Borley [1]. She shared her sleeping quarters—a Nissen hut erected in the grounds of the Hall—with three other young women [1]. Decades later, in November 1995, she was interviewed about her experiences at her home in a Suffolk village [1].

**The Apparition (Summer 1944)**
Lane's paranormal encounter took place shortly after June 6, 1944, the date of the Allied invasion of Europe [1]. At 5:00 a.m. one morning, she awoke suddenly to find a **"shadowy figure dressed in long grey robes"** standing directly at her bedside [1]. 

The sources record several specific details about the sighting:
*   **Visuals and Black-outs:** Because wartime black-out screens were still in position over the windows, the room was dark, and she was unable to make out any of the figure's facial features [1]. She noted the figure was of average height [1].
*   **Tactile Experience:** Though deeply frightened, she sensed no malevolence from the entity [1]. Demonstrating considerable nerve, she put out her hand and **"would have touched the figure if it had been solid,"** but she felt nothing [1]. 
*   **The Departure:** The figure stood completely still for about ten seconds before it faded away gradually; it **"did not vanish suddenly"** [1]. 

Upon the figure's disappearance, Lane screamed and woke her three roommates, who immediately believed her account because her profound state of shock was obvious [1]. She remained deeply shaken for several days, harboring a fear that the apparition was a premonition, though nothing untoward ultimately occurred to her [1].

**The Borley Connection and a Chronological Contradiction**
A few years after the war, Lane heard a radio broadcast detailing the haunting of Borley Rectory [2]. The broadcast mentioned that human remains, which had been excavated from the Rectory cellars, had been subsequently reburied in the graveyard at Liston by the Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning [2]. Because she had been stationed at Liston, this broadcast led her to wonder if the grey-robed figure she had seen was actually the famous Borley nun [2].

However, investigators pointed out a chronological contradiction regarding the bones: the remains were not actually interred at Liston Churchyard until May 29, 1945, which was nearly a full year *after* she had witnessed the apparition by her bed [2]. Nevertheless, researchers noted that the phantom nun had been sighted in the general district on other occasions, not strictly within the Rectory grounds [2]. 

Whatever the true identity of the figure, Dorothy Lane remained absolutely **"adamant that her experience was real and not imagined,"** maintaining over fifty years later that it was **"something which she will never forget"** [2].
