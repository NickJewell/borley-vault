# Dorothy Lane (née Tabbart)

A World War II servicewoman whose paranormal experience near Borley has been recorded as a possible extended sighting of the infamous Borley nun phantom. Interviewed by Babbs and Mathias in November 1995 at her home in a Suffolk village [1].

## Background

During the Second World War, Dorothy Lane (then Miss Tabbart) served in the NAAFI (Navy, Army and Air Force Institutes) [1]. In the early summer of 1944, when she was 20 years old, she was stationed at [[liston-hall|Liston Hall]] — a building largely no longer in existence — in the parish adjacent to Borley [1]. She shared her sleeping quarters, a Nissen hut erected in the grounds of the Hall, with three other young women [1].

## The Bedside Figure — Summer 1944

Shortly after D-Day (6 June 1944), Dorothy awoke suddenly at 5am and saw:

- A **shadowy figure dressed in long grey robes** standing at her bedside [1]
- The figure was of average height [1]
- Because wartime blackout screens were still in position over the windows, the room was dark and she was unable to make out any facial features [1]
- She felt **no malevolence** from the presence [1]
- She reached out her hand and "would have touched the figure if it had been solid," but felt **nothing** [1]
- The figure stood still for approximately **ten seconds**, then **faded gradually** — it "did not vanish suddenly" [1]
- She screamed, woke the other three women, who found her clearly shocked and believed her account [1]

She was so shaken she remained shocked for several days. She had the feeling it might be a premonition, but nothing bad happened to her [1].

## The Borley Connection and a Chronological Contradiction

A few years after the war, Lane heard a radio broadcast detailing the haunting of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] [2]. The broadcast mentioned that human remains excavated from the Rectory cellars had been subsequently reburied in the graveyard at Liston by the [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning]] [2]. Because she had been stationed at Liston, this broadcast led her to wonder if the grey-robed figure she had seen was actually the famous Borley nun.

> **Contradiction:** The [[bones-excavation|remains]] were not actually interred at Liston Churchyard until **29 May 1945** — nearly a full year *after* she had witnessed the apparition [2]. Nevertheless, researchers noted that the phantom nun had been sighted throughout the general district, not strictly within the Rectory grounds [2].

Years later she learned of these details. The authors note the figure has been reported at various points throughout the district, not only at the rectory site.

Dorothy Lane remained absolutely "adamant that her experience was real and not imagined," maintaining over fifty years later that it was "something which she will never forget" [2].

## Note on Location

Liston Hall is about a mile from [[borley-rectory]], in the adjacent hamlet of Liston. The [[the-nun|nun]] sightings are not confined to the rectory grounds; they extend throughout the wider district.
