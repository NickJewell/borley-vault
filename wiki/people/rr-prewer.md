# Surgeon-Lieutenant R. R. Prewer, R.N.V.R.

Also identified in the texts as Dr. Richard Prewer. Of "Domus," Gore Court Road, Sittingbourne, Kent during the Second World War. He was among the individuals who personally knew [[harry-bull|Rev. Harry Bull]] during his lifetime, giving him a connection to the pre-haunting era as well as the wartime period.

He wrote to [[harry-price|Harry Price]] on December 7, 1941, reporting an early wartime incident at the Borley ruins that Price had not previously heard.

## The Army's Attempted Occupation

Prewer's nephew at Clare College, Cambridge, had written to him saying that a friend had told him the following: [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] had been "partially restored" at some point in the early war years, and the Army attempted to use it, but were so disturbed by "Poltergeister" that "they had to seek quieter billets elsewhere." In Prewer's own words:

> *"My nephew wrote to me some months ago, saying that a friend of his at Clare had told him how the Rectory had been partially restored, and that the Army tried to make use of it earlier in the war; but they were so annoyed by Poltergeister that they had to seek quieter billets elsewhere."* [1]

Price admitted that he had simply filed Prewer's letter away in his Borley dossier and completely forgotten about it. The letter was only recalled when, approximately four months later, [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. A. C. Henning]] confirmed the same story independently. In a letter of 16 April 1942, Henning wrote that an officer had slept at the Rectory "about a year ago" and was "awakened several times in the night by all the bells ringing."

The convergence of these two independent reports — one via Prewer's network, one directly from Henning — establishes the military disruption as likely factual. Price notes that "the ringing of bells had been heard during the present war, by soldiers who slept there whilst on maneuvers" — suggesting the incidents may have been multiple.

## Significance

This is a minor but interesting data point: soldiers who were not expecting a haunted house were driven out by the same phenomena that had disturbed civilian investigators since 1929 — lending weight to the view that the phenomena at Borley were not dependent on expectation or prior knowledge.
