# Surgeon-Lieutenant R. R. Prewer, R.N.V.R.

Of "Domus," Gore Court Road, Sittingbourne, Kent. He wrote to [[harry-price|Harry Price]] on December 7, 1941, reporting an early wartime incident at the Borley ruins that Price had not previously heard.

## The Army's Attempted Occupation

Prewer's nephew at Clare (College, Cambridge) had written to him saying that a friend had told him the following: the Borley Rectory had been "partially restored" at some point in the early war years, and the Army attempted to use it, but were so disturbed by "Poltergeister" that "they had to seek quieter billets elsewhere."

This independent report, received December 7, 1941, preceded by some months a second account of the same event. When Prewer's letter arrived, Price had already filed it; he had forgotten about it until, later, the Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|A. C. Henning]] confirmed the same story independently. In a letter of April 16, 1942, Henning wrote that an officer had slept at the Rectory "about a year ago" and was "awakened several times in the night by all the bells ringing."

The convergence of these two independent reports — one via Prewer's network, one directly from Henning — establishes the military disruption as likely factual. The incident is a minor but interesting data point: soldiers who were not expecting a haunted house were driven out by the same phenomena that had disturbed civilian investigators since 1929.

Price notes that "the ringing of bells had been heard during the present war, by soldiers who slept there whilst on maneuvers" — suggesting the incidents may have been multiple.
