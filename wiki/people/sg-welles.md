# S. G. Welles

Rhodes Scholar, University College, Oxford. Visited [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] on the night of February 16–17, 1938 as part of a University College Oxford party of four. Sole witness of the "luminous phenomenon" on the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] ceiling — the only unambiguously visual apparition recorded during [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s observer-program tenancy. Wrote the most detailed individual observer account of any visual phenomenon in the entire program.

## Background and the 1938 Visit

Welles was a **Rhodes Scholar** studying at **University College, Oxford** [1]. He visited Borley Rectory for an overnight observational vigil on **16–17 February 1938** [1, 2], accompanied by three fellow undergraduates: R. Hawkin (also spelled Hawkins), Samuel J. Milberg (also spelled Millberg), and M. P. Knox [1, 2]. Welles took his duties seriously, sleeping on the floor of the Base Room for an hour or so but otherwise making visits to the highly active [[blue-room|Blue Room]] at half-hour intervals throughout the night [3]. He subsequently compiled a **"long and detailed report"** of the night's events; his companions sent shorter comments explicitly confirming his comprehensive log [1, 4, 5].

## The Luminous Phenomenon

Welles spent 25 minutes alone in the darkened Blue Room between 8:00 and 8:25 p.m., his companions in the Base Room preparing supper. Between approximately 8:15 and 8:20, he observed what [[harry-price|Harry Price]] called a **"phenomenon unique in the Borley annals"** [6]:

> "I became aware of a luminous patch of light on the ceiling, about six inches from the moulding that ran round the top of the walls and opposite the centre of the window. This luminous patch, a roughly rounded rectangle of perhaps a foot by five inches, moved slowly about half-way towards my corner from its original position... poised there a moment, moved as slowly back, disappeared for an instant, again appeared just where it had first done so, and moved forward and back exactly as it had done before, and then disappeared a second and last time at the spot where it had both appeared and disappeared. This whole proceeding took not more than a minute."

He describes it as clearly distinct from phosphene phenomena (the visual noise of strained eyes in darkness): "it was clearly outlined and made its motions in leisurely yet precise fashion," moving **"as of a lambent flame"** [2, 6]. He wrote his full account within twelve hours of the experience.

After the phenomenon, Welles and his companions conducted extensive experiments: they shone torches of varying power, candles, matches, and glowing cigarette ends through the window and from various angles. None of the results **"even remotely suggested what I felt I had seen."** Specifically: any light through the window illuminated a far larger area than the single patch; all external light cast the window's sash-and-pane pattern as dark bars; even the smallest pinprick of light cast shadows throughout the room; none of the experimental lights matched the single isolated patch that **cast no shadows at all** [7]. Even the highly sceptical SPR authors of *The Borley Report* conceded that, based on Welles's record, **"it is certainly difficult to suggest any normal cause for this light,"** though they speculated it might have been hallucinatory [8].

## Auditory Phenomena and Deafness

A significant biographical detail that adds credibility to Welles's honest reporting is his candid admission regarding his hearing. Between 11:40 p.m. and 12:20 a.m., the four investigators separated in complete darkness into different areas of the house. His three companions clearly heard footsteps coming from the kitchen and travelling along the passage, but Welles heard nothing — he was **"slightly deaf"** [3]. Later, around 12:30 a.m., his three friends heard a series of faint thumping noises at regular short intervals while in the Base Room; Welles frankly documented: **"I, who am slightly deaf, heard none of these"** [3, 5].

Welles also noted the "appearance" of a wooden lath (42 inches by 1 inch) on the scullery floor that none of the party could remember seeing on their first inspection, and a piece of dried grass lying alongside it. He corroborated the lath's changed position with [[joseph-burden|J. W. Burden]], who had visited two months earlier.

## Significance and Textual Contradiction

The luminous phenomenon is listed in Appendix D as "Luminous Phenomenon — Mr. S. G. Welles" — the only entry under that heading. As a Rhodes Scholar, Welles was not a credulous witness; his post-experience elimination of normal explanations was systematic and documented. Price reproduces his account as the lengthiest verbatim extract from any single observer in Appendix C.

> **Contradiction:** While the primary timelines and Harry Price's records explicitly place this visit on **16–17 February 1938** [1, 2], the SPR's *Borley Report* erroneously cites the date of his luminous patch sighting as **16 February 1937** [8] — several months before Price even began his tenancy at the Rectory.
