# S. G. Welles

Rhodes Scholar, University College, Oxford. Visited Borley Rectory on the night of February 16–17, 1938 as part of a University College Oxford party of four. Sole witness of the "luminous phenomenon" on the Blue Room ceiling — the only unambiguously visual apparition recorded during Price's observer-program tenancy. Wrote the most detailed individual observer account of any visual phenomenon in the entire program.

## The Luminous Phenomenon

Welles spent 25 minutes alone in the darkened Blue Room between 8:00 and 8:25 p.m. His companions were in the Base Room preparing supper. Between approximately 8:15 and 8:20:

> "I became aware of a luminous patch of light on the ceiling, about six inches from the moulding that ran round the top of the walls and opposite the centre of the window. This luminous patch, a roughly rounded rectangle of perhaps a foot by five inches, moved slowly about half-way towards my corner from its original position... poised there a moment, moved as slowly back, disappeared for an instant, again appeared just where it had first done so, and moved forward and back exactly as it had done before, and then disappeared a second and last time at the spot where it had both appeared and disappeared. This whole proceeding took not more than a minute."

Welles describes it as clearly distinct from phosphene phenomena (the visual noise of strained eyes in darkness): "it was clearly outlined and made its motions in leisurely yet precise fashion." He wrote his full account within twelve hours of the experience.

After the phenomenon, Welles and his companions conducted extensive experiments: they shone torches of varying power, candles, matches, and glowing cigarette ends through the window and from various angles. None of the results "even remotely suggested what I felt I had seen." Specifically:

- Any light through the window illuminated a far larger area than the single patch;
- All external light cast the window's sash-and-pane pattern as dark bars;
- Even the smallest pinprick of light cast shadows and highlights throughout the room;
- None of the experimental lights matched the single isolated patch that cast no shadows.

## Wider Experiences

Later in the night, the three other party members — Mr. R. Hawkin, Mr. Samuel J. Milberg, and Mr. M. P. Knox — heard footsteps coming from the kitchen direction and "a series of faint noises at regular short intervals" while all were in the Base Room. Welles, "slightly deaf," heard none of these. The footsteps were independently confirmed by Hawkin's separate written report.

Welles also noted the "appearance" of a wooden lath (42 inches by 1 inch) on the scullery floor that none of the party could remember seeing on their first inspection, and a piece of dried grass lying alongside it. He corroborated this with [[joseph-burden|J. W. Burden]], who had visited two months earlier and remembered the lath in a different position.

## Significance

The luminous phenomenon is listed in Appendix D as "Luminous Phenomenon — Mr. S. G. Welles" — the only entry under that heading. It is unique in the observer program. As a Rhodes Scholar at the time of the visit, Welles was not a credulous witness; his post-experience elimination of normal explanations was systematic and documented. Price reproduces his account as the lengthiest verbatim extract from any single observer in Appendix C.
