# S. G. Welles

Rhodes Scholar, University College, Oxford. Visited Borley Rectory on the night of February 16–17, 1938 as part of a University College Oxford party of four. Sole witness of the "luminous phenomenon" on the Blue Room ceiling — the only unambiguously visual apparition recorded during Price's observer-program tenancy. Wrote the most detailed individual observer account of any visual phenomenon in the entire program.

## The Luminous Phenomenon

Welles spent 25 minutes alone in the darkened Blue Room between 8:00 and 8:25 p.m. His companions were in the Base Room preparing supper. Between approximately 8:15 and 8:20:

> "I became aware of a luminous patch of light on the ceiling, about six inches from the moulding that ran round the top of the walls and opposite the centre of the window. This luminous patch, a roughly rounded rectangle of perhaps a foot by five inches, moved slowly about half-way towards my corner from its original position... poised there a moment, moved as slowly back, disappeared for an instant, again appeared just where it had first done so, and moved forward and back exactly as it had done before, and then disappeared a second and last time at the spot where it had both appeared and disappeared. This whole proceeding took not more than a minute."

Welles describes it as clearly distinct from phosphene phenomena (the visual noise of strained eyes in darkness): "it was clearly outlined and made its motions in leisurely yet precise fashion." He wrote his full account within twelve hours of the experience.

After the phenomenon, Welles and his companions conducted extensive experiments: they shone torches of varying power, candles, matches, and glowing cigarette ends through the window and from various angles. None of the results "even remotely suggested what I felt I had seen." Specifically:

- Any light through the window illuminated a far larger area than the single patch;
- All external light cast the window's sash-and-pane pattern as dark bars;
- Even the smallest pinprick of light cast shadows and highlights throughout the room;
- None of the experimental lights matched the single isolated patch that cast no shadows.

## Wider Experiences

Later in the night, the three other party members — Mr. R. Hawkin, Mr. Samuel J. Milberg, and Mr. M. P. Knox — heard footsteps coming from the kitchen direction and "a series of faint noises at regular short intervals" while all were in the Base Room. Welles, "slightly deaf," heard none of these. The footsteps were independently confirmed by Hawkin's separate written report.

Welles also noted the "appearance" of a wooden lath (42 inches by 1 inch) on the scullery floor that none of the party could remember seeing on their first inspection, and a piece of dried grass lying alongside it. He corroborated this with [[joseph-burden|J. W. Burden]], who had visited two months earlier and remembered the lath in a different position.

## Significance

The luminous phenomenon is listed in Appendix D as "Luminous Phenomenon — Mr. S. G. Welles" — the only entry under that heading. It is unique in the observer program. As a Rhodes Scholar at the time of the visit, Welles was not a credulous witness; his post-experience elimination of normal explanations was systematic and documented. Price reproduces his account as the lengthiest verbatim extract from any single observer in Appendix C.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**S. G. Welles** was an Oxford undergraduate and Rhodes Scholar who served as one of Harry Price’s "official observers" during the tenancy investigation of Borley Rectory.

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, specific events, quotes, and contradictions regarding his involvement:

**Background and the 1938 Visit**
*   **Identity:** Welles was a **Rhodes Scholar** studying at **University College, Oxford** [1]. 
*   **The Rectory Vigil:** He visited Borley Rectory for an overnight observational vigil on **16–17 February 1938** [1, 2]. He was accompanied by three fellow University College undergraduates: R. Hawkin (also spelled Hawkins), Samuel J. Milberg (also spelled Millberg), and M. P. Knox [1, 2]. 
*   Welles took his duties seriously, sleeping on the floor of the Base Room for an hour or so, but otherwise making visits to the highly active Blue Room at half-hour intervals throughout the night [3]. 
*   Following the visit, Welles compiled a **"long and detailed report"** of the night's events and submitted it to Harry Price; his companions sent shorter comments explicitly confirming Welles's comprehensive log [1, 4, 5].

**The "Luminous Patch" Phenomenon**
Welles's most significant contribution to the Borley literature was witnessing what Harry Price called a **"phenomenon unique in the Borley annals"** [6]. 
*   While on duty in the Blue Room, Welles observed a moving **"luminous patch"** on the ceiling that he described **"as of a lambent flame"** [2, 6]. He reported that it moved slowly to and fro across the ceiling and remained visible for about a minute [6]. 
*   **Failed Replication:** The four Oxford men subsequently conducted numerous experiments attempting to duplicate the phenomenon through normal means (such as shining torches into the room from outside), but they **"utterly failed"** [6]. They noted that any normal light source shined into the room produced clearly defined shadows, whereas the luminous patch Welles saw produced **no shadow at all** [7]. 
*   Even the highly skeptical authors of the SPR's *Borley Report* conceded that, based on Welles's record, **"it is certainly difficult to suggest any normal cause for this light,"** though they speculated it might have simply been hallucinatory [8].

**Auditory Phenomena and Deafness**
A lesser-known but crucial biographical detail that adds credibility to Welles's honest reporting is his candid admission regarding his hearing. 
*   Between 11:40 p.m. and 12:20 a.m., the four investigators separated in complete darkness into different areas of the house, with Welles stationed in Room No. 9 [3]. His three companions clearly heard footsteps coming from the kitchen and traveling along the passage, but Welles heard nothing because he was **"slightly deaf"** [3]. 
*   Later, around 12:30 a.m., while the group was having tea in the Base Room, his three friends heard a series of faint thumping noises at regular short intervals [3, 5]. Welles frankly documented: **"I, who am slightly deaf, heard none of these"** [3]. 

**Textual Contradictions Regarding Dates**
There is a notable chronological contradiction in the sources regarding Welles's experience. While the primary timelines and Harry Price's records explicitly place the visit of the Oxford undergraduates on **16-17 February 1938** [1, 2], the SPR's critical *Borley Report* (authored by Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) erroneously cites the date of his luminous patch sighting as occurring on **16 February 1937** [8]—several months before Price even began his tenancy at the Rectory.
