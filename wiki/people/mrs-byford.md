# Mrs E. Byford (née Martha Seaber Wilson)

Born **Martha Seaber Wilson**; married Ernest Byford in 1891. Nursemaid employed at Borley Rectory in 1886, during the incumbency of the Rev. [[henry-bull|Henry D. E. Bull]]. Writing from Parsonage Farm, Newport, Essex, she provided [[harry-price]] with early first-hand testimony in June 1929 — testimony he later cited as helping to establish that phenomena at the Rectory predated the Smith period by more than forty years.

## Experience at the Rectory

Mrs Byford worked at the Rectory as "under-nursemaid" for only one month before leaving. She describes the place as "so weird" from the beginning. Her direct experience:

> *"When I had been there a fortnight something awakened me in the dead of night. Someone was walking down the passage towards the door of my room, and the sound made suggested that they were wearing slippers. As the head nurse always called me at six o'clock, I thought it must be she, but nobody entered the room, and I suddenly thought of the 'ghost.'"*

She asked all four other maids whether they had come to her room; all denied it. These and other experiences, she wrote, were so unsettling that her grandparents removed her from the Rectory and "would not even let her pass the house after dark."

## Significance

Mrs Byford was a working-class domestic servant with no incentive to fabricate or embellish. Her account is significant for two reasons:

1. **Chronological depth**: Her 1886 experience establishes paranormal footstep phenomena at the Rectory well before the Smiths arrived in 1928 — rebutting later suggestions that the "haunting" was a recent invention.
2. **Corroboration**: Her account tallies with that of [[charles-boyden|Charles A. Boyden]], who also recalled disturbances from approximately the same period, and with the testimony of the Misses Bull about the same era.

She also confirms the general village knowledge that the Rectory had a reputation: "Much of my youth was spent in Borley and district with my grandparents, and it was common talk that the Rectory was haunted."

> **Note:** Price dates her experience as occurring in 1886, placing her as an under-nursemaid about fourteen years after the Rectory was built (1863). Her testimony helps anchor the haunting reputation to the earliest decades of the building's existence.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mrs. E. Byford**, whose maiden name was **Martha Seaber Wilson**, was an under-nursemaid to the Bull family whose brief employment at Borley Rectory in 1886 provided investigators with some of the earliest recorded testimony of the haunting [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following biographical details, specific events, direct quotes, and subsequent skeptical analysis of her experiences:

**Biographical Details and Background**
*   **Early Life:** She spent much of her youth living in the Borley district with her grandparents [2, 3]. 
*   **Marriage and Later Residence:** In 1891, she married a farmworker named Ernest Byford [1]. By the time she came forward with her testimony in 1929, she was residing at Parsonage Farm in Newport, near Saffron Walden, Essex [2, 4].

**Employment at Borley Rectory (1886)**
In 1886, she was hired to work at Borley Rectory as an under-nursemaid for the large family of the Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull [1, 4]. 
*   Upon her arrival, she was assigned a bedroom at the back of the house overlooking the stable yard, a room that had previously been occupied by two of the Bull daughters [1, 4]. 
*   During dinner in the servants' hall, the cook warned the young woman that her assigned bedroom was supposedly haunted [1]. Another maid reprimanded the cook, saying, "Shut up! You will frighten the girl!" [1]. Initially, the new nursemaid took little notice of these warnings [3].

**The "Slippered Footsteps" Incident**
About a fortnight into her employment, Mrs. Byford had a terrifying auditory experience in the dead of night [3]. 
*   She distinctly heard someone walking down the passage toward her bedroom door, noting that the sound suggested the person was **"wearing slippers"** [3]. 
*   Assuming it was the head nurse—who routinely called her at 6:00 a.m.—she waited, but nobody entered the room [3, 5]. 
*   Remembering the cook's warning about the ghost, she pulled the bedclothes over her head and lay trembling in fear for the rest of the night [1]. 

**Departure and Lingering Fear**
The next morning, she asked the other four maids if any of them had come to her room; they all denied it and tried to laugh her out of her fears [5]. Convinced that **"somebody or something in slippers had been along that corridor,"** she became so unnerved that she resigned from her post after having worked there for only a month [5]. Her fear of the property persisted long after she left; she recalled that her grandparents would never let her pass the Rectory after dark, and she would never venture near the garden or the wood at dusk [3, 5].

**The 1929 Letter and Direct Quotes**
Forty-three years after her experience, prompted by Vernon C. Wall's initial articles in the *Daily Mirror*, Mrs. Byford wrote a letter on June 11, 1929, detailing her time at Borley [2, 6]. Her letter provided crucial evidence to Harry Price that the Rectory already possessed a sinister reputation mere decades after it was built [6]. 

She provided the following specific quotes regarding the local lore and her departure:
*   **"Much of my youth was spent in Borley and district, with my grandparents, and it was common talk that the Rectory was haunted. Many people declared that they had seen figures walking at the bottom of the garden."** [5].
*   **"I once worked at the Rectory forty-three years ago, as under-nursemaid, but I only stayed there a month, because the place was so weird."** [5].

**Contradictions and Skeptical Analysis**
Mrs. Byford's testimony was heavily scrutinized in the 1956 *Borley Report* by the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) investigators Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall [7]. 
*   The authors pointed out that Mrs. Byford's experience could easily be explained by natural causes or a practical joke, particularly because **she never actually opened her bedroom door to see who was making the noise** [7]. 
*   They argued her fright was the result of suggestion, noting that the older servants had actively primed the new girl by warning her that her room was haunted [7]. They noted that female servants were known to be the source of many alleged hauntings, often playing practical jokes to frighten newcomers [8].
*   Furthermore, the critics used her account to attack Harry Price's credibility. They highlighted that while Mrs. Byford simply described the sound of "slippers," Price exaggerated her testimony in his 1946 book *The End of Borley Rectory* by describing the sounds as **"persistent 'ghostly footsteps'"**—sensational adjectives that Mrs. Byford herself had never used [7].
