# Mrs E. Byford (née Martha Seaber Wilson)

Born **Martha Seaber Wilson**; married Ernest Byford in 1891. Much of her youth was spent in the Borley district with her grandparents. Under-nursemaid employed at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in 1886, during the incumbency of the Rev. [[henry-bull|Henry D. E. Bull]]. Writing from Parsonage Farm, Newport, near Saffron Walden, Essex, she provided [[harry-price|Harry Price]] with early first-hand testimony on **June 11, 1929** — prompted by Vernon C. Wall's initial articles in the *Daily Mirror* — testimony he later cited as helping to establish that phenomena at the Rectory predated the Smith period by more than forty years.

## Employment at the Rectory (1886)

In 1886, she was hired as under-nursemaid for the large family of the Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull. Upon arrival she was assigned a bedroom at the back of the house overlooking the stable yard — a room that had previously been occupied by two of the Bull daughters. During dinner in the servants' hall, the cook warned the young woman that her assigned bedroom was supposedly haunted. Another maid reprimanded the cook, saying, "Shut up! You will frighten the girl!" Initially the new nursemaid took little notice.

## The "Slippered Footsteps" Incident

About a fortnight into her employment, Mrs Byford had a terrifying auditory experience in the dead of night:

> *"When I had been there a fortnight something awakened me in the dead of night. Someone was walking down the passage towards the door of my room, and the sound made suggested that they were wearing slippers. As the head nurse always called me at six o'clock, I thought it must be she, but nobody entered the room, and I suddenly thought of the 'ghost.'"*

Remembering the cook's warning, she pulled the bedclothes over her head and lay trembling in fear for the rest of the night. The next morning she asked all four other maids whether they had come to her room; all denied it and tried to laugh her out of her fears. Convinced that "somebody or something in slippers had been along that corridor," she became so unnerved that she resigned from her post after only a month.

## Departure and Lingering Fear

Her fear of the property persisted long after she left. Her grandparents removed her from the Rectory and "would not even let her pass the house after dark," and she would never venture near the garden or the wood at dusk. She confirmed in her 1929 letter:

- "Much of my youth was spent in Borley and district, with my grandparents, and it was common talk that the Rectory was haunted. Many people declared that they had seen figures walking at the bottom of the garden."
- "I once worked at the Rectory forty-three years ago, as under-nursemaid, but I only stayed there a month, because the place was so weird."

## Significance

Mrs Byford was a working-class domestic servant with no incentive to fabricate or embellish. Her account is significant for two reasons:

1. **Chronological depth**: Her 1886 experience establishes paranormal footstep phenomena at the Rectory well before the Smiths arrived in 1928 — rebutting later suggestions that the "haunting" was a recent invention.
2. **Corroboration**: Her account tallies with that of [[charles-boyden|Charles A. Boyden]], who also recalled disturbances from approximately the same period, and with the testimony of the Misses Bull about the same era.

## Sceptical Analysis

Mrs Byford's testimony was scrutinised in the 1956 [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] by [[eric-dingwall|Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Hall]]:

- The authors pointed out that Mrs Byford could easily have been the victim of natural causes or a practical joke — she never actually opened her bedroom door to see who was making the noise. Her fright, they argued, was the result of suggestion from the older servants who had actively primed her.
- They also used her account to attack [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s credibility: while Mrs Byford simply described the sound of "slippers," Price exaggerated her testimony in *The End of Borley Rectory* (1946) by describing the sounds as "persistent 'ghostly footsteps'" — sensational adjectives she had never used.

> **Note:** Price dates her experience as occurring in 1886, placing her as an under-nursemaid about fourteen years after the Rectory was built (1863). Her testimony helps anchor the haunting reputation to the earliest decades of the building's existence.
