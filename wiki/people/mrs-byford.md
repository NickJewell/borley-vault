# Mrs E. Byford (née Martha Seaber Wilson)

Born **Martha Seaber Wilson**; married Ernest Byford in 1891. Nursemaid employed at Borley Rectory in 1886, during the incumbency of the Rev. [[henry-bull|Henry D. E. Bull]]. Writing from Parsonage Farm, Newport, Essex, she provided [[harry-price]] with early first-hand testimony in June 1929 — testimony he later cited as helping to establish that phenomena at the Rectory predated the Smith period by more than forty years.

## Experience at the Rectory

Mrs Byford worked at the Rectory as "under-nursemaid" for only one month before leaving. She describes the place as "so weird" from the beginning. Her direct experience:

> *"When I had been there a fortnight something awakened me in the dead of night. Someone was walking down the passage towards the door of my room, and the sound made suggested that they were wearing slippers. As the head nurse always called me at six o'clock, I thought it must be she, but nobody entered the room, and I suddenly thought of the 'ghost.'"*

She asked all four other maids whether they had come to her room; all denied it. These and other experiences, she wrote, were so unsettling that her grandparents removed her from the Rectory and "would not even let her pass the house after dark."

## Significance

Mrs Byford was a working-class domestic servant with no incentive to fabricate or embellish. Her account is significant for two reasons:

1. **Chronological depth**: Her 1886 experience establishes paranormal footstep phenomena at the Rectory well before the Smiths arrived in 1928 — rebutting later suggestions that the "haunting" was a recent invention.
2. **Corroboration**: Her account tallies with that of [[charles-boyden|Charles A. Boyden]], who also recalled disturbances from approximately the same period, and with the testimony of the Misses Bull about the same era.

She also confirms the general village knowledge that the Rectory had a reputation: "Much of my youth was spent in Borley and district with my grandparents, and it was common talk that the Rectory was haunted."

> **Note:** Price dates her experience as occurring in 1886, placing her as an under-nursemaid about fourteen years after the Rectory was built (1863). Her testimony helps anchor the haunting reputation to the earliest decades of the building's existence.
