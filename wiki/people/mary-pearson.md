The second maid employed by [[rev-eric-smith]] and Mrs. Smith at [[borley-rectory]], interviewed by [[harry-price]] on June 12, 1929 — his first day at the Rectory. Price describes her as "a very intelligent girl." The first London maid had stayed only two days, frightened by the nun; Pearson stayed longer and accumulated multiple sightings.

## Phenomena Witnessed

**The nun at the gate**: Pearson saw a nun wearing a hood standing at the gate at the bottom of the garden. This was one of the sightings that frightened the first London maid away.

**The phantom coach (twice)**: From Price's original notes, June 12, 1929:
- First sighting: approximately 12:30 p.m., about three weeks before Price's visit. A coach on the lawn going down the garden with two bay horses. Stopped to look; coach disappeared.
- Second sighting: two days later, from the road. The coach going up the garden; passed through trees.
- Description: "like a big cab," two brown horses. No coachman visible on either occasion.

This account broadly corroborates [[edward-cooper]]'s sighting of the same coach: both describe two horses, a black coach "like a large cab," passage through trees. Cooper saw the coachmen; Pearson did not.

**The headless man**: Saw "a man, headless, behind a tree." She chased it into the garden; it disappeared. She had also heard scratchings at the drawing-room window.

Price's assessment: "There seems little doubt that Mary thought she saw the objects she described to us."

## Significance

Pearson is notable for being the only Smith-period maid who remained long enough to accumulate multiple sightings — and for the fact that her coach account and Cooper's agree on key details (two bay/brown horses, black coach, passage through trees) without the witnesses having compared notes.

## New Details from *The Final Analysis* (brfa-ch04)

Babbs and Mathias researched Mary Pearson's identity and family in depth:
- Born **Christmas Day 1911** in Belchamp Walter (baptised 20 November 1912)
- Her surname appears as both "Pearson" and "Pearsons" in different documents; birth certificate shows the latter
- She was **17 or 18** during her employment at the Smiths — not "15 years old" as flatly stated in the 1956 SPR report *The Haunting of Borley Rectory*
- She married and had **ten children**, all alive at time of writing (2002)
- EB met the eldest daughter and one son; Mary told her children about her experiences in a serious, not flippant, mood — directly contradicting Mrs Smith's claim that Mary was laughing
- What she told her children: she saw the nun always moving away from the rectory along the Nun's Walk; the coach was "like a big cab"; she heard the bell-ringing and footsteps on many occasions; she witnessed keys shooting from their locks
- She "adored" the Smiths despite long working hours; she neither criticised Harry Price nor accused him of trickery
- Mary died tragically young at 55 after falling down stairs at her home
