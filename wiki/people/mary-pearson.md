# Mary Pearson

**Mary Katherine Pearsons** (25 December 1911 – 1966) was born Christmas Day in the neighbouring village of Belchamp Walter and baptised 20 November 1912 [1]. Her surname appears as both "Pearson" and "Pearsons" in different documents; the birth certificate shows the latter. She was the second maid employed by [[rev-eric-smith|Rev. Guy Eric Smith]] and Mrs Smith at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], engaged after the first London maid stayed only two days and fled, claiming to have seen a ghostly nun [3, 4].

[[harry-price|Price]] interviewed her on 12 June 1929 — his first day at the Rectory — describing her as "a very intelligent girl." She was 17 or 18 during the height of the 1929 phenomena — **not** "15 years old" as stated in the 1956 SPR report [1, 2]. While at Borley, her boyfriend was [[fred-tatum|Fred Tatum]], a 25-year-old local man described as a "local bad lad" convicted of stealing rabbits [5, 6]. She married (reportedly secretly) and had ten children, spending her post-Borley life in Sudbury [5, 7]. She died tragically at 55, from injuries sustained after falling down stairs at her home [8–10].

## Phenomena Witnessed

**The phantom coach (twice)**: From Price's original notes, 12 June 1929:

- *First sighting*: approximately 12:30 p.m., about three weeks before Price's visit. A coach on the lawn going down the garden with two bay horses. Stopped to look; coach disappeared.
- *Second sighting*: two days later, from the road. The coach going up the garden, passing through trees.
- Description: "like a big cab," two brown horses. No coachman visible on either occasion [11, 12, 13].

This account broadly corroborates [[edward-cooper|Edward Cooper]]'s sighting of the same coach: both describe two horses, a black coach "like a large cab," passage through trees. Cooper saw the coachmen; Pearson did not.

**The headless man**: Saw a man, headless, behind a tree; chased it into the garden where it disappeared [11, 12]. She had also heard scratchings at the drawing-room window.

**The nun and poltergeists**: She claimed to have seen the [[the-nun|phantom nun]] walking away from the Rectory along the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]], and witnessed [[poltergeist-activity|poltergeist activity]] including inexplicable footsteps, the ringing of servant bells, and keys shooting from their locks [5, 8, 10].

Price's assessment: "There seems little doubt that Mary thought she saw the objects she described to us."

## Contradictions: Pranks and Levity

Much of Mary's testimony was severely undermined in 1949 when her former employer, [[mabel-smith|Mabel Smith]], gave a highly sceptical interview to the SPR. Mrs Smith claimed that Mary did not believe in the legend and that they both "used to laugh at ghosts," and that Mary fabricated the stories as a joke [14]. Specific claims include:

- **The Sugar Incident**: Mrs Smith claimed Mary suspected Price of faking phenomena, allegedly saying: "It was that man threw that coin — so I threw some sugar" [15].
- **Faking the Nun and the Headless Man**: Researchers noted that Mary "played at least one prank at the Rectory when she put her apron over her head and pretended to be the nun" [5]. Her boyfriend, Fred Tatum, was also known to walk around the garden with his coat pulled over his head to mimic a headless spook [6, 16].
- **Profiting from Sightseers**: When hordes of sightseers descended on the Rectory in 1929, Mary reportedly made them drop money into the Rectory's missionary box before she would tell them ghost stories [17, 18].

## Later Interviews and Family Rebuttals

The evidence about Mary's true views is deeply conflicting:

- **The SPR interview (21 August 1952)**: Investigators Eric Dingwall and Trevor Hall tracked her down and she reportedly retracted her story about chasing the headless man, stating it was "quite untrue" and that she could not remember having told it [19, 20].
- **The aunt's denial (1947)**: During a BBC broadcast, an aunt (a Mrs Pearson) stated aggressively: "My sister's girl worked there for four years, but she never heard or saw a thing... the whole thing's a lot of nonsense anyway" [21].
- **The children's testimony**: Decades later, investigator [[edward-cooper|Edward Babbs]] tracked down two of Mary's children. In stark contrast to Mrs Smith's claims that Mary treated it all as a joke, her children insisted she was completely serious when recounting her experiences [10, 16]. They stated she "adored" the Smiths and gave them faithful service, believed genuinely in the phenomena she had witnessed, and **never accused Harry Price of trickery** — directly contradicting Mrs Smith's "sugar" anecdote [10].

Her family confirmed that Mary remained deeply affected by her time at Borley. She maintained there were dark "forces at work" at the Rectory, deliberately avoided the site for the rest of her life, and reportedly passed by it only three times in over twenty years [22].

## Significance

Pearson is notable for being the only Smith-period maid who remained long enough to accumulate multiple sightings, and for the fact that her coach account and Cooper's agree on key details (two bay/brown horses, black coach, passage through trees) without the witnesses having compared notes.

> **Contradiction:** The SPR report states Mary was "15 years old" during the 1929 period; Babbs and Mathias establish from the birth certificate that she was 17 or 18 — a discrepancy that calls into question the SPR's handling of biographical detail in this case.
