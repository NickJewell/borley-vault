# Eric John Dingwall (1890 – 7 August 1986)

Born in Ceylon (now Sri Lanka) into an affluent tea-planting family; died Hastings aged 96. Prominent psychical researcher, sometime Assistant Keeper at the British Museum Reading Room, and Vice-President of the Magic Circle. One of the three co-authors of the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] (1956).

## Background

Graduate of Pembroke College, Cambridge; later gained a doctorate from the University of London. Private income from his family enabled him to pursue psychical research as a career. Joined the SPR in 1920; worked for two years in America as Director of Physical Phenomena at the ASPR. Claimed to have introduced [[harry-price]] to serious psychical research when they investigated Austrian medium Willi Schneider together in 1922. During WWII worked for the Ministry of Information and Foreign Office in propaganda/disinformation.

His personal interests ran to the extreme — cataloguing erotic literature for the British Museum, writing *Male Infibulation* (1925) and *The American Woman* (1956). His *Who's Who* entry listed his hobby as "studying rare and queer customs."

## Relationship with Harry Price

Dingwall and Price had a productive working relationship in the early 1920s but it deteriorated over the following decades. By the 1950s Dingwall was recruited by [[william-salter]] to re-examine the Borley case as part of what amounted to a posthumous prosecution of Price. He contributed two chapters to the Borley Report.

After the Report was published Dingwall continued collaborating with Trevor Hall but eventually broke with him after *Search for Harry Price* (1978), which he felt went too far. In his final years he withdrew from organised psychical research entirely, writing in 1985 that "the great majority [of parapsychologists] wanted to prove something or other" and he had "finished with it."

## Character

"A frosty, arrogant and argumentative but erudite man" (Peter Underwood), who could also be "charming and helpful when he wanted to be." He once told Underwood that he regretted his life in psychical research and wished he had devoted it to "the sex life of insects." His knowledge of psychical research literature was arguably unrivalled.

> **Contradiction with his own actions:** Despite co-authoring a report dismissing virtually all paranormal activity at Borley, Dingwall told his younger collaborator Trevor Hall — in front of a Council member — regarding one over-reaching allegation: "I told Trevor he was claiming too much, but it is too late to do anything about it now."

## Role in the Borley Report

The three authors (Dingwall, [[kathleen-goldney]], [[trevor-hall]]) examined the Borley case between ~1949 and 1955. Dingwall's chapters focused on Price's career and methodology. The Report concluded: the evidence for genuine haunting was "so slender as to be scientifically worthless" and Price had committed "wilful deception and fraud."

The [[hastings-report]] (1969) found "every mistake I noticed tended in a direction unfavourable to Price" and demonstrated specific factual errors in Dingwall and Hall's treatment of incidents.
