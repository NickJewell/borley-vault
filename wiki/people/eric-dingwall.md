# Eric John Dingwall (1890 – 7 August 1986)

Born in Ceylon (now Sri Lanka) into an affluent tea-planting family; died Hastings aged 96. Prominent psychical researcher, sometime Assistant Keeper at the British Museum Reading Room, and Vice-President of the Magic Circle. One of the three co-authors of the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] (1956).

## Background

Graduate of Pembroke College, Cambridge; later gained a doctorate from the University of London. Private income from his family enabled him to pursue psychical research as a career. Joined the SPR in 1920; worked for two years in America as Director of Physical Phenomena at the ASPR. Claimed to have introduced [[harry-price]] to serious psychical research when they investigated Austrian medium Willi Schneider together in 1922. During WWII worked for the Ministry of Information and Foreign Office in propaganda/disinformation.

His personal interests ran to the extreme — cataloguing erotic literature for the British Museum, writing *Male Infibulation* (1925) and *The American Woman* (1956). His *Who's Who* entry listed his hobby as "studying rare and queer customs."

## Relationship with Harry Price

Dingwall and Price had a productive working relationship in the early 1920s but it deteriorated over the following decades. By the 1950s Dingwall was recruited by [[william-salter]] to re-examine the Borley case as part of what amounted to a posthumous prosecution of Price. He contributed two chapters to the Borley Report.

After the Report was published Dingwall continued collaborating with Trevor Hall but eventually broke with him after *Search for Harry Price* (1978), which he felt went too far. In his final years he withdrew from organised psychical research entirely, writing in 1985 that "the great majority [of parapsychologists] wanted to prove something or other" and he had "finished with it."

## Character

"A frosty, arrogant and argumentative but erudite man" (Peter Underwood), who could also be "charming and helpful when he wanted to be." He once told Underwood that he regretted his life in psychical research and wished he had devoted it to "the sex life of insects." His knowledge of psychical research literature was arguably unrivalled.

> **Contradiction with his own actions:** Despite co-authoring a report dismissing virtually all paranormal activity at Borley, Dingwall told his younger collaborator Trevor Hall — in front of a Council member — regarding one over-reaching allegation: "I told Trevor he was claiming too much, but it is too late to do anything about it now."

## Role in the Borley Report

The three authors (Dingwall, [[kathleen-goldney]], [[trevor-hall]]) examined the Borley case between ~1949 and 1955. Dingwall's chapters focused on Price's career and methodology. The Report concluded: the evidence for genuine haunting was "so slender as to be scientifically worthless" and Price had committed "wilful deception and fraud."

The [[hastings-report]] (1969) found "every mistake I noticed tended in a direction unfavourable to Price" and demonstrated specific factual errors in Dingwall and Hall's treatment of incidents.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Dr. Eric John Dingwall** (1890–1986) was a prominent British anthropologist, author, and psychical researcher. Best known in the Borley Rectory case as one of the three co-authors of the highly critical 1956 Society for Psychical Research (SPR) publication, *The Haunting of Borley Rectory* (the "Borley Report"), Dingwall was a formidable and complex figure whose career was marked by his expertise in investigating physical mediumship, his eccentric personal interests, and his volatile relationships with his colleagues. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, quotes, and lesser-known biographical details about his life and his connection to the case:

**Lesser-Known Biographical Details and Quirks**
*   **Early Life and Education:** Born in Ceylon (now Sri Lanka) in 1890, he was the son of Alexander H. Dingwall, a wealthy tea planter. He was raised in a substantial, staffed residence in Weston-super-Mare, England, and was later supported by a private income [1]. He graduated from Pembroke College, Cambridge, worked as an assistant librarian at the Cambridge University Library until June 1918, and later earned a doctorate from the University of London [1].
*   **The British Museum and "Dirty Ding":** Dingwall served as an Assistant Keeper at the British Museum Reading Room, where his duties involved cataloguing and expanding its restricted collection of erotic literature [1, 2]. This specific role earned him the academic nickname **"Dirty Ding"** [3]. 
*   **"Rare and Queer Customs":** A reticent man regarding his own private affairs, he explored normal and abnormal sexual activity in great depth, listing his hobby in *Who's Who* as **"studying rare and queer customs"** [2]. He authored several niche books on the subject, including *Male Infibulation* (1925), *Some Human Oddities* (1947), and *The American Woman* (1956) [2]. 
*   **World War II:** During the Second World War, he worked for the Ministry of Information and the Foreign Office, specializing in propaganda and disinformation work directed at the enemy [2].
*   **Personality:** Known as "Ding" to his acquaintances, but insisting on being addressed as "Dr. Dingwall," he was described as a frosty, arrogant, and argumentative but highly erudite man who could be charming when he chose to be [4, 5]. 

**Early Psychical Career and Collaboration with Harry Price**
Dingwall joined the SPR in 1920 and became a leading expert on the physical phenomena of Spiritualism, investigating famous mediums like 'Margery' Crandon [1]. In 1921, he spent a year in America as the Director of the Department of Physical Phenomena at the American SPR, and he was also a Vice-President of the Magic Circle [1, 6]. 

At the Magic Circle, Dingwall met Harry Price, and the two initially enjoyed a productive collaboration [7]. 
*   **Exposing William Hope (1922):** Dingwall enlisted Price's help to expose the fraudulent spirit photographer William Hope. Together, they covertly used photographic plates secretly marked with the Imperial Dry Plate Company's "rampant lion" trademark to definitively prove Hope was swapping plates during séances [7, 8]. 
*   **Willi Schneider (1922):** In May 1922, Dingwall invited Price to Munich to investigate the young Austrian physical medium Willi Schneider [9, 10]. 
*   **Publications:** The two men also collaborated on a facsimile reprint of *Revelations of a Spirit Medium*, a handbook exposing fraudulent séance tricks [6].

**The Rift with Harry Price**
Despite their early teamwork, Dingwall grew to loathe Price's populist, self-aggrandizing methods. Dingwall felt Price lacked the scientific knowledge required of a serious researcher and criticized his work as **"a new kind of psychic journalism"** [9, 11]. 
When Price began testing the medium "Stella C." at the London Spiritualist Alliance rather than the SPR, Dingwall accused him of **"a frank betrayal of psychical research"** and suspected that if the phenomena were fraudulent, **"Mr Price himself must have been in the fraud"** [12, 13]. Dingwall later dismissed Price's highly publicized sittings with Rudi Schneider as **"a cavalcade of entertainment"** [14]. 

When asked to contribute to Paul Tabori's posthumous biography of Price, Dingwall submitted a piece containing the biting assessment that Price **"never, in my opinion, advanced our real knowledge of the supernatural in any way whatsoever."** The contribution was subsequently withdrawn by the executors to spare Price's widow's feelings [15]. 

**The Borley Report (1956) and Trevor Hall**
Following Price's death, SPR Honorary Secretary William H. Salter recruited Dingwall and Kathleen "Mollie" Goldney to re-examine the Borley case, fully aware of their animosity toward Price [16, 17]. 

*   **Recruiting Trevor Hall:** In 1949, Dingwall attended a Magic Circle lecture by Trevor H. Hall. Impressed, Dingwall invited Hall to his Cambridge flat, took him to Borley, and recruited him into the SPR to co-author the investigation [18-20]. 
*   *The Haunting of Borley Rectory:* Published in 1956, the report accused Price of fraud, deceit, and misrepresentation [21-23]. Dingwall specifically authored the "Introduction" and the chapter evaluating the "Price Tenancy" [22].
*   **Skepticism of Phenomena:** Dingwall ruthlessly dismissed the Borley phenomena. He attributed the famous "swinging blind" to natural draughts [24], dismissed reported lights as reflections from Sudbury [25], and declared the evidence gathered during Price's tenancy to be **"so slender as to be scientifically worthless"** [26]. On a BBC radio broadcast, he confessed he was astonished that Price could take the subject so seriously, viewing him purely as a journalist and storyteller [27].

**Later Life, Contradictions, and Disillusionment**
*   **The Rift with Trevor Hall:** Dingwall collaborated with Hall again on *Four Modern Ghosts* (1958) [28]. However, their friendship permanently ended following the publication of Hall's vicious 1978 book, *Search for Harry Price*, which Dingwall felt went too far in its malice [4, 29]. Dingwall also harbored regrets regarding their earlier work; in 1962, he admitted to an SPR Council member regarding their critique of Price's "Rosalie" séance: **"I told Trevor he was claiming too much, but it is too late to do anything about it now"** [30].
*   **Abandonment of Parapsychology:** In his later years, Dingwall became deeply disillusioned with the field of parapsychology, feeling that modern researchers had lost their objectivity because **"they wanted the phenomena... to serve some purpose in supporting preconceived theories."** He summarized his exit from the field by declaring: **"Anyway, I have finished with it"** [16].
*   **The "Sex Life of Insects":** Reflecting on his sixty-year career, he once confessed to Peter Underwood that he deeply regretted dedicating his life to psychical research, stating that if he had his time over again, he would have devoted it to studying the **"sex life of insects"** [5].

Dr. Eric J. Dingwall died of heart disease at St Helen's Hospital in Hastings on August 7, 1986, at the age of 96 [1, 5, 31].
