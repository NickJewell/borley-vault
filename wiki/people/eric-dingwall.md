# Eric John Dingwall (1890 – 7 August 1986)

Born in Ceylon (now Sri Lanka) into an affluent tea-planting family; died Hastings aged 96. Prominent psychical researcher, sometime Assistant Keeper at the British Museum Reading Room, and Vice-President of the Magic Circle. One of the three co-authors of the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] (1956). His British Museum role earned him the academic nickname **"Dirty Ding"** — his duties involved cataloguing and expanding the restricted collection of erotic literature [1, 2, 3].

## Background

Graduate of Pembroke College, Cambridge; he had worked as an assistant librarian at the Cambridge University Library until June 1918, then earned a doctorate from the University of London [1]. A private income from his family enabled him to pursue psychical research as a career. He joined the [[spr|SPR]] in 1920; in 1921 spent a year in America as Director of Physical Phenomena at the ASPR; was also a Vice-President of the Magic Circle [1, 6]. He listed his hobby in *Who's Who* as **"studying rare and queer customs"** [2], and authored *Male Infibulation* (1925), *Some Human Oddities* (1947), and *The American Woman* (1956) [2]. During WWII he worked for the Ministry of Information and Foreign Office in propaganda and disinformation directed at the enemy [2].

## Early Collaboration with Harry Price

At the Magic Circle, Dingwall met [[harry-price|Harry Price]], and the two initially enjoyed a productive collaboration:

- **Exposing William Hope (1922):** Dingwall enlisted Price's help to expose the fraudulent spirit photographer [[william-hope|William Hope]]. Together they covertly used photographic plates secretly marked with the Imperial Dry Plate Company's "rampant lion" trademark to definitively prove Hope was swapping plates during séances [7, 8].
- **Willi Schneider (1922):** In May 1922, Dingwall invited Price to Munich to investigate the young Austrian physical medium [[willi-schneider|Willi Schneider]] [9, 10].
- **Publications:** The two men also collaborated on a facsimile reprint of *Revelations of a Spirit Medium*, a handbook exposing fraudulent séance tricks [6].

## The Rift with Harry Price

Despite their early teamwork, Dingwall grew to loathe Price's populist, self-aggrandizing methods, criticising his work as **"a new kind of psychic journalism"** [9, 11]. When Price began testing the medium "Stella C." at the London Spiritualist Alliance rather than the SPR, Dingwall accused him of **"a frank betrayal of psychical research"** and suspected that if the phenomena were fraudulent, **"Mr Price himself must have been in the fraud"** [12, 13]. He later dismissed Price's sittings with Rudi Schneider as **"a cavalcade of entertainment"** [14].

When asked to contribute to Paul Tabori's posthumous biography of Price, Dingwall submitted a piece containing the assessment that Price **"never, in my opinion, advanced our real knowledge of the supernatural in any way whatsoever."** The contribution was subsequently withdrawn by the executors to spare Price's widow's feelings [15].

## Character

"A frosty, arrogant and argumentative but erudite man" ([[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]]), who could also be "charming and helpful when he wanted to be." He once told Underwood that he regretted his life in psychical research and wished he had devoted it to the **"sex life of insects"** [5]. He insisted on being addressed as "Dr. Dingwall," not the informal "Ding" [4, 5].

## Role in the Borley Report

Following Price's death, SPR Honorary Secretary [[william-salter|William H. Salter]] recruited Dingwall and [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen Goldney]] to re-examine the Borley case, fully aware of their animosity toward Price [16, 17].

In 1949, Dingwall attended a Magic Circle lecture by [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]]. Impressed, he invited Hall to his Cambridge flat, took him to Borley, and recruited him into the SPR to co-author the investigation [18–20].

*The Haunting of Borley Rectory* (1956) accused Price of fraud, deceit, and misrepresentation [21–23]. Dingwall authored the "Introduction" and the chapter evaluating the "Price Tenancy" [22]. He attributed the famous "swinging blind" to natural draughts [24], dismissed reported lights as reflections from Sudbury [25], and declared the evidence gathered during Price's tenancy to be **"so slender as to be scientifically worthless"** [26]. On a BBC radio broadcast, he confessed he was astonished that Price could take the subject so seriously, viewing him purely as a journalist and storyteller [27].

The [[hast|Hastings Report]] (1969) found "every mistake I noticed tended in a direction unfavourable to Price" and demonstrated specific factual errors in Dingwall and Hall's treatment of incidents.

> **Contradiction with his own actions:** Despite co-authoring a report dismissing virtually all paranormal activity at Borley, Dingwall admitted to an SPR Council member in 1962 regarding their critique of Price's "Rosalie" séance: "I told Trevor he was claiming too much, but it is too late to do anything about it now" [30].

## Later Life and Disillusionment

Dingwall collaborated with Hall again on *Four Modern Ghosts* (1958) [28]. However, their friendship permanently ended following Hall's 1978 book *Search for Harry Price*, which Dingwall felt went too far in its malice [4, 29].

In his later years, Dingwall became deeply disillusioned with parapsychology, feeling that modern researchers had lost their objectivity because **"they wanted the phenomena... to serve some purpose in supporting preconceived theories."** He declared: **"Anyway, I have finished with it"** [16].

Dingwall died of heart disease at St Helen's Hospital in Hastings on 7 August 1986, at the age of 96 [1, 5, 31].
