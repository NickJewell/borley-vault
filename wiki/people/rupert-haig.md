# Rupert Haig

Cousin of [[mark-kerr-pearse|M. Kerr-Pearse]]. Visited Borley Rectory on September 21, 1937, the day after [[sqn-ldr-horniman|Squadron-Leader Horniman]]'s visit, and experienced the cold spot in one of the most vividly described encounters on record.

## Experience at the Cold Spot

Haig wrote to Price on October 2, 1937:

> *"Suddenly, the air surrounding me became ice-cold; my hands became icy, and in fact I became cold all over and my hair stood on end. I was rigid. The sensation lasted, as nearly as I can judge, about twenty seconds."*

The temperature of the air surrounding him was measured at the time: **60°F** — a moderately mild autumn evening. Yet his hands became icy, his whole body felt cold, and he was physically rigid for approximately twenty seconds.

This is the most specifically documented individual cold-spot encounter in terms of the contrast between measured ambient temperature and perceived/physical response. The ambient 60°F was objectively recorded; Haig's hands were objectively icy to the touch (as observed by companions).

## Context and Significance

Haig was introduced to the Rectory through his cousin [[mark-kerr-pearse|Kerr-Pearse]], one of Price's most dedicated observers. He had presumably been informed of the cold spot's general location but his reaction — "hair stood on end," "rigid," lasting a measurable twenty seconds — goes beyond what conscious expectation would typically produce.

The cluster of cold-spot reports in August–September 1937 ([[miss-reid|Miss Reid]] twice in August; [[sqn-ldr-horniman|Horniman]]'s friend on September 20; Haig on September 21) is one of the strongest periods of independent cold-spot corroboration in the entire record. The [[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]]'s 1943 measurements subsequently established the physical reality of the effect.
