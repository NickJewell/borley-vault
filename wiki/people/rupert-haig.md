# Rupert Haig

Cousin of [[mark-kerr-pearse|M. Kerr-Pearse]]. Also spelled **Rupert Haigh** in some texts. Visited Borley Rectory on September 21, 1937, the day after [[sqn-ldr-horniman|Squadron-Leader Horniman]]'s visit, and experienced the cold spot in one of the most vividly described encounters on record.

## Background

At the time of his visit Haig was approximately 33 years old and worked as a **barrister-at-law in the Colonial Legal Service**; in private life he was an amateur big-game hunter [2–4]. Shortly after his visit to the Rectory, his career took him abroad — he departed England aboard the *P. & O. Ranchi* [5].

## Experience at the Cold Spot

Haig joined his cousin [[mark-kerr-pearse|Kerr-Pearse]] for an observation period at the Rectory between September 20 and 22, 1937 [6]. During this visit the two men recorded several distinct phenomena. On the evening of September 21, the weather having turned cold, they bought a large, heavy sack of coal to heat their Base Room and left it outside in the hall. At 8:30 p.m., while standing on the upstairs landing, they heard a rustling noise; going downstairs, they discovered the heavy bag of coal had **moved about 18 inches** across the floor [2]. They also reported hearing a heavy thud that seemed to come from the Blue Room overhead, though a search revealed nothing disturbed [7], and at 2:30 a.m. they heard a sharp rap on the Base Room window — Kerr-Pearse rationally speculated it might simply have been a moth [2].

Haig's most dramatic experience occurred while trying to sleep in the Rectory's Base Room, which was warmed by a fire and illuminated by a paraffin lamp. He documented it in a letter to his cousin dated **October 2, 1937**, posted from Port Said while en route to his colonial posting [5]:

> *"If I did not enjoy my visit to your haunted house, I at least found it most interesting. I entered it with a completely open mind, but left it with the firm conviction that it is haunted. Immediately before waking you, I had been awake for a considerable time... To begin with, I was certainly in a jumpy state. I was expecting footsteps, thumps, knocks, and even to see something, but as time went on and nothing happened I became calmer. Everything was perfectly still and I saw and heard nothing, but suddenly the air surrounding me became ice cold, my hands became icy, and in fact I became cold all over and my hair stood on end. I was rigid."* [5]

He noted that the terrifying sensation lasted for about 20 seconds — so unpleasant he was ultimately compelled to wake Kerr-Pearse. [[harry-price|Harry Price]] later pointed out that the temperature of the air surrounding Haig at the exact time of his sensation was measured at a perfectly normal **60° Fahrenheit** [8]. That contrast — ambient 60°F against objectively icy hands confirmed by his companion — makes this the most specifically documented cold-spot encounter in terms of the gap between measured ambient temperature and physical response.

## Context and Significance

Haig was introduced to the Rectory through his cousin [[mark-kerr-pearse|Kerr-Pearse]], one of Price's most dedicated observers. The cluster of cold-spot reports in August–September 1937 — [[miss-reid|Miss Reid]] twice in August; [[sqn-ldr-horniman|Horniman]]'s friend on September 20; Haig on September 21 — is one of the strongest periods of independent cold-spot corroboration in the entire record. The [[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]]'s 1943 measurements subsequently established the physical reality of the effect.

## Skeptical Assessment

The authors of the 1956 Society for Psychical Research publication *The Borley Report* used Rupert Haig's letter to attack [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s investigative methods. [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen Goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] highlighted Haig's candid admission that he had been in a "jumpy state" and was fully "expecting footsteps, thumps, knocks" [3]. They argued this fearful anticipation was the direct result of a **"barrage of suggestion"** planted by Price's [[blue-book-of-instructions|Blue Book of Instructions]] — the manual given to all observers outlining the house's history — and that Haig simply misinterpreted a normal draught or bodily sensation as something paranormal because his mind had been deliberately primed to do so [3].
