# Rupert Haig

Cousin of [[mark-kerr-pearse|M. Kerr-Pearse]]. Visited Borley Rectory on September 21, 1937, the day after [[sqn-ldr-horniman|Squadron-Leader Horniman]]'s visit, and experienced the cold spot in one of the most vividly described encounters on record.

## Experience at the Cold Spot

Haig wrote to Price on October 2, 1937:

> *"Suddenly, the air surrounding me became ice-cold; my hands became icy, and in fact I became cold all over and my hair stood on end. I was rigid. The sensation lasted, as nearly as I can judge, about twenty seconds."*

The temperature of the air surrounding him was measured at the time: **60°F** — a moderately mild autumn evening. Yet his hands became icy, his whole body felt cold, and he was physically rigid for approximately twenty seconds.

This is the most specifically documented individual cold-spot encounter in terms of the contrast between measured ambient temperature and perceived/physical response. The ambient 60°F was objectively recorded; Haig's hands were objectively icy to the touch (as observed by companions).

## Context and Significance

Haig was introduced to the Rectory through his cousin [[mark-kerr-pearse|Kerr-Pearse]], one of Price's most dedicated observers. He had presumably been informed of the cold spot's general location but his reaction — "hair stood on end," "rigid," lasting a measurable twenty seconds — goes beyond what conscious expectation would typically produce.

The cluster of cold-spot reports in August–September 1937 ([[miss-reid|Miss Reid]] twice in August; [[sqn-ldr-horniman|Horniman]]'s friend on September 20; Haig on September 21) is one of the strongest periods of independent cold-spot corroboration in the entire record. The [[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]]'s 1943 measurements subsequently established the physical reality of the effect.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Rupert Haig** (also spelled **Rupert Haigh** in some texts) was an observer during Harry Price's year-long tenancy investigation of Borley Rectory. He visited the site in September 1937 to assist his cousin, Mark Kerr-Pearse, who was one of Price's most dedicated official investigators [1, 2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, specific paranormal experiences, quotes, and skeptical interpretations regarding his involvement:

**Biographical Details**
*   **Profession and Hobbies:** Haig was a **barrister-at-law in the Colonial Legal Service**, and in his private life, he was an **amateur big-game hunter** [2-4]. 
*   **Age:** He was approximately **33 years old** at the time of his visit to Borley Rectory [2, 4].
*   Shortly after his visit to the Rectory, his career took him abroad, and he departed England aboard the *P. & O. Ranchi* [5].

**The September 1937 Visit and Poltergeist Activity**
Haig joined his cousin for an observation period at the Rectory between **September 20 and 22, 1937** [6]. During this visit, Haig and Kerr-Pearse recorded several distinct phenomena:
*   **The Coal Sack Incident (September 21):** The weather had turned cold, so the men bought a large, heavy sack of coal to heat their Base Room. They left the sack outside in the hall. At 8:30 p.m., while standing on the upstairs landing, they heard a rustling noise. When they went downstairs, they discovered that the heavy bag of coal had **moved about 18 inches** across the floor [2].
*   **Auditory Phenomena:** Haig and his cousin reported hearing a heavy thud in the Base Room that seemed to come from the Blue Room overhead, though a search revealed nothing disturbed [7]. At 2:30 a.m. during the night, they also heard a sharp rap on the Base Room window, though Kerr-Pearse rationally speculated it might simply have been a moth [2]. 

**The "Ice-Cold" Encounter**
Haig's most dramatic experience occurred while trying to sleep in the Rectory's Base Room, which was warmed by a fire and illuminated by a paraffin lamp [2]. Haig documented this experience in a letter to his cousin dated **October 2, 1937**, posted from Port Said while en route to his colonial posting [5]. 
*   **A Firm Conviction:** He began his letter by stating: **"If I did not enjoy my visit to your haunted house, I at least found it most interesting. I entered it with a completely open mind, but left it with the firm conviction that it is haunted"** [5].
*   **The Sensation:** Describing his nighttime ordeal, he wrote: **"Immediately before waking you, I had been awake for a considerable time... To begin with, I was certainly in a jumpy state. I was expecting footsteps, thumps, knocks, and even to see something, but as time went on and nothing happened I became calmer. Everything was perfectly still and I saw and heard nothing, but suddenly the air surrounding me became ice cold, my hands became icy, and in fact I became cold all over and my hair stood on end. I was rigid."** [5]. 
*   He noted that the terrifying sensation lasted for about 20 seconds, and it was so unpleasant he was ultimately compelled to wake Kerr-Pearse [5]. Harry Price later pointed out that the temperature of the air surrounding Haig at the exact time of his "icy" sensation was measured at a perfectly normal **60° Fahrenheit** [8].

**Skeptical Analysis and Contradictions**
The authors of the highly critical 1956 Society for Psychical Research (SPR) publication, *The Borley Report*, used Rupert Haig's letter to explicitly attack Harry Price's investigative methods. 
*   **The Power of Suggestion:** Authors Eric Dingwall, Kathleen Goldney, and Trevor Hall highlighted Haig's candid admission that he had been in a "jumpy state" and was fully "expecting footsteps, thumps, knocks" [3]. 
*   They argued that Haig's fearful anticipation was the direct result of a **"barrage of suggestion"** planted by Price's *Blue Book of Instructions* (the manual given to all observers outlining the house's spooky history and what they might expect to see) [3]. The SPR investigators postulated that it was not unreasonable to conclude that Haig simply misinterpreted a normal draught or natural bodily sensation as something paranormal because his mind had been deliberately primed to do so [3].
