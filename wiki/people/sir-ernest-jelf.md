# Sir Ernest Jelf

Senior Master of the Supreme Court; King's Remembrancer from 1937 to 1943. Studied *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940) and wrote a leading article in the *Law Times* (August 9, 1941): "A Question of Evidence."

## Legal Opinion on Borley

Jelf took [[harry-price|Price]]'s challenge — "it is for the reader to decide as the jury whether in fact the place is haunted" — seriously as a legal exercise.

His position: he and his readers are in the position of counsel perusing proofs before trial, not of a jury that has actually heard witnesses. He distinguishes this carefully — witnesses rarely come fully up to their proofs, and cross-examination modifies accounts. But even with this caveat:

> "They do present a very strong case indeed — stronger than most of us could ever have believed possible before we had read the book. There are more than a hundred witnesses, most of whom are still alive and available, including many persons of position and of intellectual attainment. And many of them were called in as unprejudiced outsiders, on purpose to see whether they would by their own experience corroborate the witnesses who had gone before."

He notes the Poltergeist phenomena (objects moved, wine changed to ink, writings on walls, thermal variations) stand on "a perfectly different level from the ordinary ghost story" — they imply a force capable of producing actual physical and chemical changes in matter. He is "at a loss to understand what cross-examination could possibly shake" the case.

On the dogs: "The reaction of the dogs certainly seemed to show that they too saw some of the strange sights and heard some of the strange sounds of Borley Rectory. Did they imagine them?"

Final verdict: "What would a jury say? They would probably disagree." — the first honest legal assessment in the history of psychical research.

> **Note:** Jelf's opinion was formed solely on the first Borley book. Price remarks that in view of the additional hundred witnesses and the excavation evidence in *The End of Borley Rectory*, the case presented to Jelf's hypothetical jury is substantially stronger than what he evaluated.
