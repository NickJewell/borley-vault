# Sir Ernest Jelf

Senior Master of the Supreme Court; King's Remembrancer from 1937 to 1943; member of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s Ghost Club [3]. Studied *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940) and wrote a leading article in the *Law Times* (August 9, 1941): **"A Question of Evidence."**

## Legal Opinion on Borley

Jelf took Price's challenge — "it is for the reader to decide as the jury whether in fact the place is haunted" — seriously as a legal exercise. He was clear that readers were in the position of a counsel evaluating proofs, not of a jury that had actually heard cross-examined witnesses [6], and speculated that if the truth of the Borley story ever became relevant at a legal trial, a jury would **"probably disagree"** [7] — the first honest legal assessment in the history of psychical research.

His position on the strength of the case:

> "They do present a very strong case indeed — stronger than most of us could ever have believed possible before we had read the book. There are more than a hundred witnesses, most of whom are still alive and available, including many persons of position and of intellectual attainment. And many of them were called in as unprejudiced outsiders, on purpose to see whether they would by their own experience corroborate the witnesses who had gone before."

Price had **"undoubtedly put forward a very strong case – stronger than most of us could have believed possible before we had read the book – and we are at a loss to understand what cross-examination could possibly shake it"** [1, 6].

He notes the Poltergeist phenomena (objects moved, wine changed to ink, writings on walls, thermal variations) stand on "a perfectly different level from the ordinary ghost story" — they imply a force capable of producing actual physical and chemical changes in matter.

On the dogs: "The reaction of the dogs certainly seemed to show that they too saw some of the strange sights and heard some of the strange sounds of Borley Rectory. Did they imagine them?"

> **Note:** Jelf's opinion was formed solely on the first Borley book. Price remarked that in view of the additional hundred witnesses and the excavation evidence in *The End of Borley Rectory*, the case presented to Jelf's hypothetical jury was substantially stronger than what he evaluated [7].

## Price's Reaction

Harry Price was thrilled with this high-profile legal endorsement. In the preface to his 1946 sequel, *The End of Borley Rectory*, Price offered his "especial thanks" to Jelf (and fellow jurist [[sir-albion-richardson|Sir Albion Richardson]]), boasting: **"For the first time in the annals of psychical research the opinions of distinguished jurists have been publicly expressed as to the validity or otherwise of phenomena witnessed in a haunted house. So Borley has made legal, as well as psychic, history"** [8]. Jelf's glowing opinions were ultimately used by the publishers to embellish the promotional blurb on the dust-wrapper of *The End of Borley Rectory* [9].

## Skeptical Backlash

The authors of the 1956 Society for Psychical Research publication *The Borley Report* directly attacked Jelf's conclusions. [[eric-dingwall|Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] argued that Jelf's glowing article revealed a **"strange inability to understand what constitutes valid evidence in cases of this kind"** [10], that he had made **"no real attempt to examine the evidence on which he relied"** [6], and had simply fallen victim to the "barrage of suggestion" put up by Price, treating witnesses' subjective distorted memories as hard facts without any critical appreciation for the psychology of testimony [6, 11].
