# Sir Ernest Jelf

Senior Master of the Supreme Court; King's Remembrancer from 1937 to 1943. Studied *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940) and wrote a leading article in the *Law Times* (August 9, 1941): "A Question of Evidence."

## Legal Opinion on Borley

Jelf took [[harry-price|Price]]'s challenge — "it is for the reader to decide as the jury whether in fact the place is haunted" — seriously as a legal exercise.

His position: he and his readers are in the position of counsel perusing proofs before trial, not of a jury that has actually heard witnesses. He distinguishes this carefully — witnesses rarely come fully up to their proofs, and cross-examination modifies accounts. But even with this caveat:

> "They do present a very strong case indeed — stronger than most of us could ever have believed possible before we had read the book. There are more than a hundred witnesses, most of whom are still alive and available, including many persons of position and of intellectual attainment. And many of them were called in as unprejudiced outsiders, on purpose to see whether they would by their own experience corroborate the witnesses who had gone before."

He notes the Poltergeist phenomena (objects moved, wine changed to ink, writings on walls, thermal variations) stand on "a perfectly different level from the ordinary ghost story" — they imply a force capable of producing actual physical and chemical changes in matter. He is "at a loss to understand what cross-examination could possibly shake" the case.

On the dogs: "The reaction of the dogs certainly seemed to show that they too saw some of the strange sights and heard some of the strange sounds of Borley Rectory. Did they imagine them?"

Final verdict: "What would a jury say? They would probably disagree." — the first honest legal assessment in the history of psychical research.

> **Note:** Jelf's opinion was formed solely on the first Borley book. Price remarks that in view of the additional hundred witnesses and the excavation evidence in *The End of Borley Rectory*, the case presented to Jelf's hypothetical jury is substantially stronger than what he evaluated.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Sir Ernest Jelf** was an eminent British jurist who provided one of the most prominent legal endorsements of Harry Price's investigations into Borley Rectory. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, quotes, and subsequent controversies regarding his involvement:

**Biographical Details and Professional Status**
*   **Legal Career:** Sir Ernest Jelf served as the **Senior Master of the Supreme Court** and held the prestigious position of **King’s Remembrancer from 1937 to 1943** [1, 2]. 
*   **Connections:** He was a member of Harry Price’s Ghost Club [3]. Price highly respected him, explicitly describing him as one of the **"brilliant thinkers"** who had studied the Borley case academically [4].

**His Assessment of the Borley Evidence (August 1941)**
After reading Price’s 1940 book *The Most Haunted House in England*, Sir Ernest Jelf published a leading article titled **"A Question of Evidence"** in the **_Law Times_ on August 9, 1941** [2].
*   **On the book's claims:** He described Price's work as **"one of the most remarkable books of marvels, put forward as being literally true, which was ever published"** [5]. He noted that legal professionals, accustomed to seeking truth, would be **"staggered by the appeal... that we shall believe things more contrary to the ordinary experience of mankind than we could ever have dreamed of before"** [5]. 
*   **On the strength of the case:** Evaluating the case from a legal perspective, Jelf stated that Price **"had undoubtedly put forward a very strong case – stronger than most of us could have believed possible before we had read the book – and we are at a loss to understand what cross-examination could possibly shake it"** [1, 6]. 
*   **On legal admissibility:** Jelf pointed out that from the strict point of view of a legal trial, the book provided no actual "evidence," as readers were not in the position of a jury hearing cross-examined witnesses [6]. Instead, he argued readers were in the position of a counsel evaluating proofs to see if a good case existed to substantiate the claims [6]. Interestingly, he speculated that if the truth of the Borley story ever became relevant in a legal trial, a jury would **"probably disagree"** [7].

**Price's Reaction and the 1946 Publication**
Harry Price was thrilled with this high-profile legal endorsement. 
*   In the preface to his 1946 sequel, *The End of Borley Rectory*, Price offered his "especial thanks" to Jelf (and fellow jurist Sir Albion Richardson), boasting: **"For the first time in the annals of psychical research the opinions of distinguished jurists have been publicly expressed as to the validity or otherwise of phenomena witnessed in a haunted house. So Borley has made legal, as well as psychic, history"** [8].
*   Price noted that Jelf had rendered his verdict based entirely on the first book, emphasizing that the jurist knew **"nothing whatsoever of the vast and important additional evidence"** that Price had subsequently gathered [7].
*   Jelf's glowing opinions were ultimately used by the publishers to embellish the promotional blurb printed on the dust-wrapper of *The End of Borley Rectory* [9].

**Skeptical Backlash and the SPR Report**
The authors of the highly critical 1956 Society for Psychical Research (SPR) publication, *The Borley Report*, directly attacked Sir Ernest Jelf's conclusions.
*   **The SPR's Dismissal:** Authors Eric Dingwall, Kathleen Goldney, and Trevor Hall argued that Jelf's glowing article revealed a **"strange inability to understand what constitutes valid evidence in cases of this kind"** [10]. 
*   **Failure to Investigate:** The SPR investigators questioned how eminent lawyers like Jelf could express such absolute certainty when it was clear they had made **"no real attempt to examine the evidence on which they relied"** [6]. They argued that Jelf had simply fallen victim to the "barrage of suggestion" put up by Price, treating the witnesses' subjective distorted memories as hard facts without any critical appreciation for the psychology of testimony [6, 11].
