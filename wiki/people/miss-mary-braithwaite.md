# Miss Mary E. Braithwaite, J.P.

**Justice of the Peace**; daughter of [[francis-j-braithwaite|Francis J. Braithwaite]], the former rector of All Saints Church in the nearby village of Acton [1]. She had two notable brothers: **[[miles-braithwaite|Miles Braithwaite]]**, town clerk and former mayor of Sudbury, and **[[wj-braithwaite|W. John Braithwaite]]** (later Sir John), a civil servant based in London [1]. She lived with her brother Miles at **Brook House**, situated opposite The Bull Hotel in Long Melford [1, 2]. The Braithwaite family had known Borley Rectory and the Bull family since their childhood, and were good friends with Sir George and Lady Whitehouse of Arthur Hall [1].

## Initiating the SPR Investigation (August 1931)

As rumours of spectacular poltergeist activity at the Rectory swept the district in 1931, Mary Braithwaite became highly concerned. Prompted by her brother [[miles-braithwaite|Miles]], she wrote directly to **[[william-salter|William H. Salter]]** (the SPR's honorary secretary and treasurer) on **15 August 1931**, because she had a friend who was Salter's sister [2–4]. In her letter she vividly described the negative impact the haunting was having on the community, writing that the village was becoming **"almost like a plague spot"** [4].

Despite instigating the investigation, she expressed a desire to keep a polite distance, writing that she **"didn't want to get mixed up in the affair"** but felt compelled to help the Foysters and "get this intolerable nuisance stopped" [5]. She forwarded her brother John's notes directly to Salter and asked him to delay his visit until she and Miles returned from a holiday abroad in September, advising him that he must speak to both the Bulls and the Foysters because "it is almost impossible to explain things, as everyone has a different version" [5].

## The Midnight Rescue Mission — 12 August 1931

Despite living locally and instigating the SPR's involvement, Mary had never actually met the [[lionel-foyster|Foysters]] until the night of **12 August 1931** [6]. Her brother [[wj-braithwaite|John]] returned to Brook House highly unnerved after a four-hour séance at the Rectory with a medium named Mr Johnson, and fearing for [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]]'s mental state, asked Mary to drive up to the Rectory to see if she could help [6]. She found a subdued Lionel Foyster who reported that Marianne and the children were safely in bed [6]. Seeing no immediate crisis, she immediately drove to Arthur Hall to brief the Whitehouses and asked them to check on the Foysters in the morning [6].

## Opinions on Marianne and the Wall Writings

Unlike some more credulous witnesses, Miss Braithwaite took a highly sceptical view of the famous [[wall-writings|Borley wall writings]]. She firmly held that they were faked by [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] herself [3, 7], stating categorically in her 15 August 1931 letter to Salter: **"undoubtedly Mrs Foyster's, as she makes some letters in a funny way"** [3, 8]. The Braithwaite family was at one time in possession of one of the mysterious paper messages addressed to Marianne, which was later acquired for Peter Underwood's Borley collection [2].

## Later Involvement with Harry Price (1945)

During [[harry-price|Price]]'s visit to Borley in May 1945, [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Henning]] introduced him to Miss Braithwaite [9]. She informed Price that her brother John had attended an extraordinary séance at the Rectory in August 1931, and suggested that if he contacted John's widow — living at "Greenwood," Bidborough Ridge, Tunbridge Wells — she might allow him to read John's 5,000-word contemporary report [9]. Price followed her advice, securing what he considered a crucial "missing link" for *The End of Borley Rectory* [9, 10].

She confirmed that "from childhood" her brother had known Borley Rectory and the Bull family, and that he had heard of the haunting "even in those early days" — establishing the family's independent long-standing connection to the Borley story, prior to Price's involvement.

## Significance

Without Mary Braithwaite's introduction at that May 1945 Borley visit, Price would never have known of her brother's séance report or the surviving paper-message — both of which entered the Borley archive only in the last months before he completed *The End of Borley Rectory*.
