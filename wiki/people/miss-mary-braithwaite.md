# Miss Mary E. Braithwaite, J.P.

Sister of [[wj-braithwaite|W. John Braithwaite]]; resident of Brook House, Long Melford, Suffolk. It was Mary Braithwaite who introduced [[harry-price|Harry Price]] to the Braithwaite family's Borley connection, and directed him to his brother's widow.

## Role

During Price's visit to Borley in May 1945, the Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|A. C. Henning]] introduced Price to Miss Braithwaite. She told Price that her brother had attended an extraordinary séance at the Rectory in August 1931, and that if he approached his widow — living at "Greenwood," Bidborough Ridge, Tunbridge Wells — she would perhaps allow him to see the report.

Mary Braithwaite confirmed that "from childhood" her brother had known Borley Rectory and the Bull family, and that he had heard of the haunting "even in those early days" — establishing the family's independent long-standing connection to the Borley story, prior to Price's involvement.

She also held the status of Justice of the Peace, lending social credibility to the chain of introduction.

## Significance

Without Mary Braithwaite's introduction at that May 1945 Borley visit, Price would never have known of her brother's 5,000-word séance report or the surviving paper-message — both of which entered the Borley archive only in the last months before Price completed *The End of Borley Rectory*. She is thus the inadvertent gateway through which the [[braithwaite-report|Braithwaite Report]] became part of the permanent record.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Miss Mary E. Braithwaite, J.P.** (Justice of the Peace) was a highly respected local official and resident of the Borley district. While she was not a primary witness to the majority of the phenomena, her social connections and pragmatic interventions played a pivotal role in initiating the formal Society for Psychical Research (SPR) investigation into Borley Rectory. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, events, quotes, and specific opinions regarding her involvement:

**Biographical Details and Residence**
*   **Family Background:** She was the daughter of Francis J. Braithwaite, the former rector of All Saints Church in the nearby village of Acton [1]. She had two notable brothers: **Miles Braithwaite**, the town clerk and former mayor of Sudbury, and **W. John Braithwaite** (later Sir John), a civil servant based in London [1]. 
*   **Residence:** She lived with her brother Miles at **Brook House**, situated opposite The Bull Hotel in Long Melford [1, 2].
*   **Local Connections:** The Braithwaite family had known the Rectory and the Bull family since their childhood, and they were also good friends with Sir George and Lady Whitehouse of Arthur Hall [1]. 

**Initiating the SPR Investigation (August 1931)**
As rumors of the spectacular poltergeist activity surrounding the Rev. Lionel and Marianne Foyster swept through the district, Mary Braithwaite became highly concerned.
*   Prompted by her brother Miles, Mary took action to bring in expert investigators. Because she had a friend who was the sister of **William H. Salter** (the honorary secretary and treasurer of the SPR), Mary wrote directly to him on **August 15, 1931**, outlining the alarming situation and asking the society to intervene [2-4]. 
*   In her correspondence, she vividly described the negative impact the haunting was having on the community, writing that the village was becoming **"almost like a plague spot"** [4].
*   Despite instigating the investigation, she expressed a desire to keep a polite distance, writing to Salter that she **"didn’t want to get mixed up in the affair"**, but felt compelled to help the Foysters and **"get this intolerable nuisance stopped"** [5]. 
*   She forwarded her brother John's notes on the phenomena directly to Salter and requested that Salter delay his visit until she and Miles had returned from a holiday abroad in September, advising him that he must talk to both the Bulls and the Foysters because **"it is almost impossible to explain things, as everyone has a different version"** [5].

**The Midnight Rescue Mission (August 12, 1931)**
Despite living locally and instigating the SPR's involvement, Mary had never actually met the Foysters until the night of August 12, 1931 [6]. 
*   That evening, her brother John returned to Brook House highly unnerved after enduring a four-hour séance at the Rectory with a medium named Mr. Johnson. Fearing for Marianne Foyster's mental state, John asked Mary to go up to the Rectory to see if she could help [6].
*   Mary drove to Borley late that night and was met by a subdued Lionel Foyster, who informed her that Marianne and the children were safely in bed [6]. 
*   Seeing that there was no immediate crisis, she immediately drove over to Arthur Hall to brief the Whitehouses on what had happened and asked them to check on the Foysters in the morning [6]. 

**Opinions on Marianne Foyster and the "Wall Writings"**
Unlike some of the more credulous witnesses, Miss Braithwaite took a highly skeptical view of the famous Borley "wall writings" (messages supposedly written by a ghostly entity addressing Marianne). 
*   She firmly held the opinion that the wall writings were not produced by poltergeists, but rather were faked by Marianne Foyster herself [3, 7]. 
*   In her August 15, 1931 letter to William Salter, she stated categorically that the writing was **"undoubtedly Mrs Foyster's, as she makes some letters in a funny way"** [3, 8]. 
*   Interestingly, the Braithwaite family was at one time in possession of one of the mysterious paper messages addressed to Marianne, which was later acquired for Peter Underwood's Borley collection [2].

**Later Involvement with Harry Price (1945)**
Mary Braithwaite’s final notable contribution to the Borley saga occurred many years later, in May 1945. 
*   During a visit to Borley, investigator Harry Price was introduced to Miss Braithwaite by the Rev. A. C. Henning [9]. 
*   She informed Price about the extraordinary séance her brother John had attended at the Rectory back in August 1931, and suggested that if Price contacted John's widow, she might allow him to read John's voluminous, 5,000-word contemporary report of the event [9]. Price followed her advice, securing what he considered a crucial "missing link" for his second book, *The End of Borley Rectory* [9, 10].
