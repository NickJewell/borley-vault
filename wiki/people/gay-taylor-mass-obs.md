# Gay Taylor (Mass-Observation reporter)

Journalist working for the **Mass-Observation Archive** — the social research organisation that had been recording everyday life in Britain since 1937 — who investigated the Borley case in the late 1940s and became a useful source for [[eric-dingwall|Dingwall]] and [[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]].

## Background

Mrs Gay Taylor had been corresponding with [[harry-price|Harry Price]] since 1942, discussing the Waldegrave and Catholic backgrounds to the Borley story. She visited the village herself and became acquainted with [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]] and the Turners at the [[rectory-cottage|Rectory Cottage]]. She experienced what she believed to be "a variety of small experiences" at the site, though local opinion was not encouraging — she was repeatedly told that "there waunt no ghosts till Harry Price brought 'um."

## The Christmas 1949 Séance Story

In December 1949, one of Gay Taylor's friends, **Wanda Haines**, took part in a séance in Gloucestershire at which Borley was mentioned. Another sitter at the séance, **Edith Shaw**, had "flared up" at the mention of [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]]'s name, declaring her "a liar, an adulteress and a bigamist." This was significant: Edith Shaw was the sister of **Johnny Fisher** ([[henry-fisher|Henry Francis Fisher]]), the man Marianne had bigamously married. Edith had first-hand knowledge of Marianne's deceptions.

Gay Taylor relayed this information to [[eric-dingwall|Dingwall]] and [[kathleen-goldney|Goldney]], which led them to Edith Shaw and eventually to the full story of Marianne's post-Borley life — a key thread in both the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] and [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]]'s subsequent investigation.

## Significance

Gay Taylor's role is primarily as an investigative intermediary: she connected the Borley researchers with the crucial lead about Edith Shaw, which unlocked the documentation of Marianne's bigamy.

> **Note:** Gay Taylor (Mass-Observation journalist) is distinct from [[mrs-g-taylor|Mrs G. Taylor of Chelsea]], a séance participant mentioned in connection with the Turners.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Gay Taylor** (also referred to in the sources as Mrs. G. Taylor or Mrs. Guy Taylor) was a reporter for the Mass-Observation Archive, an organization that began recording everyday life in Britain in 1937 [1]. She lived in Chelsea, London, and became deeply fascinated by the Borley Rectory case [2, 3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, dates, events, and specific quotes regarding her involvement and her sensational theories:

**Early Involvement and Correspondence with Harry Price**
Mrs. Taylor began corresponding with psychical researcher Harry Price in 1942, discussing the Waldegrave family and the Roman Catholic background of the Borley story [1]. In 1947, she wrote to Price to report that during a séance, she had received a message specifically intended for him: the communicating entity claimed that the reputed ghost nun, Marie Lairre, had been born near Le Havre on **September 27, 1647**, before entering a nunnery [2]. 

**Visits to Borley and Local Skepticism**
Taylor visited Borley village herself, becoming acquainted with Ethel Bull and with James and Catherine Turner, who resided at the Rectory Cottage [1]. Although she claimed to have had **"a variety of small experiences"** while there, she was disappointed by the attitude of the villagers, noting that she was met with local skepticism and was told by residents that **"there waunt no ghosts till Harry Price brought ’um"** [1]. 

**The June 1948 Séance**
Following Harry Price's death, Mrs. Taylor was one of five sitters at a notable planchette séance held in the Rectory Cottage on the evening of **June 8, 1948**. Sitting alongside Mr. and Mrs. Turner, the Rev. John C. Dening, and a Cambridge undergraduate named Tommy Frankland, the group allegedly established contact with entities claiming to be the recently deceased Harry Price, as well as the former rector, Harry Bull [2, 4]. 

**The "Black Magic" and "Satanism" Theories**
Mrs. Taylor held several séances devoted to the Borley site in 1947, which led her to adopt a highly sinister view of the hauntings [3]. In a series of letters sent to Society for Psychical Research (SPR) investigator Trevor Hall in 1957, she outlined her belief that the Rectory's occupants were possessed and that the site was dominated by evil:
*   **The 1480 Origin:** On January 26, 1957, she wrote to Hall: **"I am unalterably convinced that something happened in that countryside in the past that caused it to be disturbed, at times, to this day . . . it was begun by [a priest and two others] who round about 1480 raised something they couldn't control"** [3].
*   **Satanism:** In a subsequent letter on February 22, 1957, she theorized that a local group practiced black magic in Long Melford and Borley, stating: **"When we asked [in a seance] if murder or some such crime had occurred at Borley before the 17th century, we got 'Death - Violent,' and that it was due to Satanism"** [5]. 
*   **The Terrified Medium:** To support her claims of demonic forces, Taylor told Hall about a medium who went into a trance at Borley and **"woke up in a terrible state, declaring that she had seen the devil and that the whole place was reeking in Black Magic. After that, no medium for miles around would go into trance at Borley"** [6].
*   **Harry Price's "Possession":** Taylor went so far as to allege that even Harry Price had fallen under this evil spell, and that it was this demonic possession that caused him to manufacture or misrepresent paranormal evidence about Borley [7].

Trevor Hall ultimately dismissed Taylor's dramatic theories. In his unpublished papers, he concluded: **"I do not at any rate accept Mrs. Taylor's theory that there is evidence to show that certain people behaved criminally because they came to live in the supposedly evil orbit of Borley"** [8].

**Meetings with SPR Investigators and the Marianne Foyster "Exposure"**
As SPR researchers Eric Dingwall and Mollie Goldney were compiling their highly critical *Borley Report*, they arranged to meet Mrs. Taylor at the SPR headquarters in Tavistock Square [1]. During this meeting, she relayed two notable pieces of secondhand information:
1.  **Local Women's Confessions:** She shared a story told to her by the wife of the Rev. A.C. Henning (the rector of Borley). Mrs. Henning had confided that whenever she gave lectures on Borley to the local Women's Institute, women would approach her afterward and, **"under a pledge of secrecy would tell her very similar stories of paranormal happenings in their own households"** [9].
2.  **The Gloucestershire Séance:** Taylor told the investigators that during Christmas 1949, a friend of hers named Wanda Haines attended a séance in Gloucestershire. When Borley was mentioned, another sitter named Edith Shaw suddenly became enraged at the mention of Marianne Foyster's name, declaring the former rector's wife to be a **"liar, an adulteress and a bigamist"** [10]. (Edith Shaw was the sister of Johnny Fisher, the man with whom Marianne Foyster had engaged in a bigamous marriage) [10].
