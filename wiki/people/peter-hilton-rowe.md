# Dr Peter Hilton-Rowe

Member of the Harry Price [[ghost-club]], Number 339, and later Life Member of the Ghost Club Society. Former Bank of England official and enthusiastic visitor to haunted houses.

## Borley Involvement

Hilton-Rowe accompanied [[peter-underwood]] on various investigations and interviews connected with the Borley case, including interviews with [[steuart-kiernander]], [[s-john-saunders|S. John Saunders]], [[ernest-ambrose|Ernest Ambrose]], [[john-osborne-harley]], and [[tony-broughall]]. He also visited the Borley site on multiple occasions as part of his general haunted-house investigation work.

His involvement is primarily in a supporting role alongside Peter Underwood rather than as an independent investigator. The BRC mentions him in the context of several interviews and visits documented in Underwood's files.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Dr. Peter Hilton-Rowe** (frequently recorded in the sources as **Dr. C.C.P. Hilton-Rowe**) was a former Bank of England official and an enthusiastic visitor of haunted houses who contributed firsthand physical and visual evidence to the Borley Rectory investigation [1, 2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following biographical details, specific events, quotes, and chronological contradictions regarding his involvement:

**Biographical Details and Affiliations**
*   **Profession and Residence:** He was a retired Bank of England official who resided at the Wick Farm in Ardleigh, near Colchester [1, 3-5]. 
*   **Affiliations:** He was an active member of Harry Price’s Ghost Club, holding Member Number 339, and later became a Life Member of the Ghost Club Society [2]. 
*   **Relationships:** He was a close friend of paranormal investigator Peter Underwood and publisher Wesley H. Downes [5-7]. 

**The March 1947 Visit and the "Avo" Meter Incident**
Dr. Hilton-Rowe’s first significant contribution to the Borley case occurred in **March 1947**, when he travelled to the site specifically to take photographs on behalf of Harry Price [1, 8]. 
*   He arrived at 10:00 a.m. on a Monday, introduced himself to the Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning, and proceeded to take the requested photographs of the Waldegrave tomb and the grave of the Rev. H.D.E. Bull inside Borley Church [8]. 
*   After finishing in the church, he walked across the road to the old Rectory gateway—the specific spot where the carpenter Fred Cartwright had allegedly seen the phantom nun years earlier [1, 8]. 
*   **The Malfunction Quote:** Intending to take a photograph of the ruins, he experienced a bizarre equipment failure. In a letter dated **March 8, 1947**, he detailed the incident with the following specific quote:
> **"For the purpose of determining correct exposures, I have been accustomed to use an 'Avo' Exposure Meter (photo-electric cell type). This exposure meter I used at the church. After taking the pictures, I walked to the gate... To my surprise, the meter failed to respond, but by tapping the meter very hard I jerked the needle to another position where it was again immobile. It was not until I left the gate that the needle behaved in a normal manner. I have had no trouble with it since."** [8]
*   **The Cats' Cemetery Theory:** In his correspondence regarding this trip, Dr. Hilton-Rowe offered a unique theory regarding the Rectory's mysteries. He suggested that the lost plate of Borley Church might have been buried in the Rectory's cats' cemetery, proposing that this was the real reason an unknown person had hastily and surreptitiously dug up the animal graves in August 1937 [5]. 

**The Phantom Nun Sighting**
Dr. Hilton-Rowe is one of the few named investigators to claim a direct, daylight sighting of Borley's most famous apparition [3].
*   On a fine Saturday evening in **July 1956**, he drove a friend to the village to show them the haunted site [3, 9]. 
*   He parked his car where the old Rectory gate had once stood [3, 9]. His friend was sitting in the car with their back to the Rectory site, listening to Dr. Hilton-Rowe tell the story of the ghost [3, 9]. 
*   Looking past his friend toward the gateway and the Nun's Walk, Dr. Hilton-Rowe suddenly saw the clear figure of a nun dressed entirely in black [3, 9]. 
*   He described her as running past the gateway with **"quick, short steps, her coif flowing behind her"** [3, 9]. Because her habit was not voluminous, he specifically gained the impression that she was a novice [3, 9]. 
*   Both men immediately got out of the car and searched the area, but found absolutely no sign of a nun or any other human being [3, 9]. 

**Investigative Collaborations**
Beyond his independent visits, Dr. Hilton-Rowe frequently partnered with Peter Underwood to conduct formal interviews with key Borley witnesses during the 1950s and 1960s [10-12].
*   In **September 1952**, he and Underwood interviewed James Osborne Harley at his home in Cavendish regarding his boyhood memories of the Rev. Harry Bull [10]. 
*   He assisted in the interview of the lay investigator and journalist S. John Saunders [11]. 
*   In **1969**, alongside Underwood and Underwood's wife, he travelled to Sudbury to interview the 91-year-old Ernest Ambrose, who had been the organist at Borley Church for sixteen years prior to the First World War [12]. 
*   He also accompanied Underwood on visits to Chilton Lodge to interview the surviving Bull sisters [7, 13]. 

**Chronological Contradiction in the Sources**
The texts present a notable contradiction regarding the exact date of Dr. Hilton-Rowe's visual encounter with the phantom nun. 
*   While detailed accounts explicitly date the car-side sighting of the running novice to a Saturday evening in **July 1956** [3, 9], author Peter Underwood offers a contradictory recollection in a separate chapter, mistakenly claiming that Dr. Hilton-Rowe saw the nun while sitting in the car with his friend in **"early in 1947"**—blurring the date of the sighting with the date of the light meter incident [4].
