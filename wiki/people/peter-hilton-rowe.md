# Dr Peter Hilton-Rowe

Frequently recorded in the sources as **Dr C. C. P. Hilton-Rowe**. A retired Bank of England official who resided at the Wick Farm in Ardleigh, near Colchester [1, 3–5]. He was an active member of the [[harry-price|Harry Price]] [[ghost-club]], holding Member Number 339, and later became a Life Member of the Ghost Club Society [2]. A close friend of [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] and publisher Wesley H. Downes [5–7], he contributed both photographic documentation and firsthand visual evidence to the Borley Rectory investigation.

## The March 1947 Visit and the "Avo" Meter Incident

Hilton-Rowe's first significant contribution to the Borley case occurred in **March 1947**, when he travelled to the site specifically to take photographs on behalf of [[harry-price|Harry Price]] [1, 8]. He arrived at 10:00 a.m. on a Monday, introduced himself to the [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning]], and proceeded to photograph the Waldegrave tomb and the grave of the Rev. H.D.E. Bull inside [[borley-church|Borley Church]] [8].

After finishing in the church, he walked to the old Rectory gateway — the spot where the carpenter [[fred-cartwright|Fred Cartwright]] had allegedly seen the phantom nun years earlier [1, 8]. Intending to take a photograph of the ruins, he experienced a bizarre equipment failure. In a letter dated **8 March 1947**, he detailed the incident:

> **"For the purpose of determining correct exposures, I have been accustomed to use an 'Avo' Exposure Meter (photo-electric cell type). This exposure meter I used at the church. After taking the pictures, I walked to the gate... To my surprise, the meter failed to respond, but by tapping the meter very hard I jerked the needle to another position where it was again immobile. It was not until I left the gate that the needle behaved in a normal manner. I have had no trouble with it since."** [8]

In his correspondence regarding this trip, Hilton-Rowe also offered a unique theory regarding the [[cats-cemetery|Rectory's cats' cemetery]], suggesting that the lost plate of Borley Church might have been buried there, and that this was the real reason an unknown person had hastily and surreptitiously dug up the animal graves in August 1937 [5].

## The Phantom Nun Sighting (July 1956)

Hilton-Rowe is one of the few named investigators to claim a direct, daylight sighting of Borley's most famous apparition (single witness) [3].

On a fine Saturday evening in **July 1956**, he drove a friend to the village to show them the haunted site [3, 9]. He parked his car where the old Rectory gate had once stood, and his friend was sitting in the car with their back to the Rectory site, listening to Hilton-Rowe tell the story of the ghost [3, 9]. Looking past his friend toward the gateway and [[nuns-walk|the Nun's Walk]], he suddenly saw the clear figure of a nun dressed entirely in black [3, 9]. He described her as running past the gateway with **"quick, short steps, her coif flowing behind her"** [3, 9]; because her habit was not voluminous, he gained the impression she was a novice [3, 9]. Both men immediately got out of the car and searched the area, but found no sign of a nun or any other human being [3, 9].

> **Contradiction:** While detailed accounts explicitly date the car-side sighting to a Saturday evening in **July 1956** [3, 9], Underwood offers a contradictory recollection elsewhere, mistakenly placing the sighting as **"early in 1947"** — apparently blurring the date of the sighting with the date of the light-meter incident [4].

## Investigative Collaborations

Beyond his independent visits, Hilton-Rowe frequently partnered with [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] to conduct formal interviews with key Borley witnesses during the 1950s and 1960s [10–12].

- In **September 1952**, he and Underwood interviewed James Osborne Harley at his home in Cavendish regarding his boyhood memories of the Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] [10].
- He assisted in the interview of the lay investigator and journalist [[st-john-saunders|S. John Saunders]] [11].
- In **1969**, alongside Underwood and Underwood's wife, he travelled to Sudbury to interview the 91-year-old [[ernest-ambrose|Ernest Ambrose]], who had been the organist at [[borley-church|Borley Church]] for sixteen years prior to the First World War [12].
- He also accompanied Underwood on visits to Chilton Lodge to interview the surviving Bull sisters [7, 13].
