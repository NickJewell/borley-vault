# Basil Payne

Eldest son of Robert and Annie Payne, owners of [[borley-place|Borley Place]]; resident at Borley Place during [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s tenancy at the Rectory; telephoned the Sudbury fire brigade on the night of the 1939 fire.

## Background

Basil was the eldest son of the Payne family, who owned the historic Borley Place manor house directly across the road from the Rectory site, as well as Borley Hall further down the hill by the River Stour. The Paynes were the largest landowners in eastern Borley. Basil and his wife **Edna** (later churchwarden) lived at Borley Place; his parents Robert and Annie lived at Borley Hall.

## Hosting the Rev. Henning

When the [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning]] was appointed rector of the combined parishes of Borley and Liston in 1936, the cavernous and dilapidated Borley Rectory was deemed unsuitable for occupation [4]. Henning and his family consequently lived with Basil and Edna Payne at Borley Place for **nine months** before moving into Liston Rectory [1, 5]. The arrangement began hospitably, but a subsequent **dispute over a right of way** caused a permanent rift between Henning and the Paynes [1]. As a result, the Payne family took little interest in church affairs for the remainder of Henning's incumbency [1].

## Price Tenancy

On **June 2, 1937**, at the very beginning of his one-year tenancy investigation, [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and his colleague [[ellic-howe|Ellic Howe]] were introduced to Mrs. Payne by Rev. Henning [3]. Mr. and Mrs. Basil Payne entertained the two investigators for tea at Borley Place, during which they discussed the Rectory, its occupants, and the alleged phenomena at length [3, 6]. The Paynes confirmed that no paranormal activity had occurred at Borley Place itself — only at the Rectory — which confirmed to Price that the haunting was site-specific. No paranormal activity was ever attributed to any Payne family member or to Borley Place.

Despite witnessing strange lights at the Rectory, the Paynes maintained a strong distaste for the building and frequently referred to it bluntly as **"that horrible old place"** [2].

## Sightings of the Window Lights

Both Basil Payne and his wife personally witnessed the famous unexplained lights in the Rectory windows during the incumbency of the [[rev-eric-smith|Rev. Guy Eric Smith]] (1928–1929), and again in November 1937 during the Price tenancy [6, 7]. Their position directly across the road made them prime observers of external events at the Rectory.

## The Night of the Fire

On the night of 27–28 February 1939, it was Basil Payne who **telephoned the Sudbury fire brigade** after [[captain-gregson|Captain Gregson]] discovered the fire. Gregson had "lost his head" and rushed to get his sons before driving to the fire station himself; meanwhile the residents of Place Farm Cottage were shouting to attract attention. Basil — who had a telephone — made the call at approximately 12:15 a.m., and he and his wife were among the locals who stood and watched the blaze that gutted the Rectory [6].
