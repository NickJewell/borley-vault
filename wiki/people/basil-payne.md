# Basil Payne

Eldest son of Robert and Annie Payne, owners of [[borley-place|Borley Place]]; resident at Borley Place during [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s tenancy at the Rectory; called the fire brigade on the night of the 1939 fire.

## Background

Basil was twenty-nine years old and the eldest son of the Payne family, who owned the historic Borley Place manor house next to the church. When the Biggs' lease on Borley Place expired, Basil and his wife **Edna** moved in. His parents Robert and Annie Payne lived at [[borley-place|Borley Hall]], further down the hill by the River Stour. The Paynes were the largest landowners in eastern Borley.

## Price Tenancy

Price and Ellic Howe visited the Paynes to introduce themselves when setting up the official observer programme in 1937. Over tea, the Paynes confirmed that no such activity had occurred at Borley Place itself — only at the Rectory. This confirmed to Price that the haunting was site-specific.

No paranormal activity was attributed to any Payne family member or to Borley Place itself.

## The Night of the Fire

On the night of 27–28 February 1939, it was Basil Payne who **telephoned the Sudbury fire brigade** after Lionel Arbon of the Rectory Cottage raised the alarm. Captain Gregson, discovering the fire, had "lost his head" and rushed to get his sons before driving to the fire station; meanwhile the Pearsons at Place Farm Cottage had been shouting to attract attention, and it was Basil — who had a telephone — who made the call at approximately 12:15 a.m.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Basil Payne** was a prominent local figure in Borley, belonging to a well-known family of landowners and farmers who owned the Borley estate, Borley Hall, and Borley Place (formerly Borley Manor House) [1]. He and his wife, Mrs. E. E. Payne (who later served as churchwarden), lived at Borley Place, situated directly across the road from the site of Borley Rectory [1-3].

A thorough reference profile of Basil Payne’s connection to the Borley Rectory story includes the following key details:

**Housing the Rev. Alfred Henning and Subsequent Dispute**
When the Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning was appointed rector of the combined parishes of Borley and Liston in 1936, the cavernous and dilapidated Borley Rectory was deemed unsuitable [4]. Instead, Henning and his family temporarily lived with Mr. and Mrs. Basil Payne at Borley Place for nine months before moving into Liston Rectory [1, 5]. While this began as a hospitable arrangement, a subsequent **dispute over a right of way** caused a permanent rift between Henning and the Paynes [1]. As a result of this disagreement, the Payne family took little interest in church affairs for the remainder of Henning’s incumbency [1]. 

**Meeting Harry Price**
On **June 2, 1937**, at the very beginning of his one-year tenancy investigation, Harry Price and his colleague Ellic Howe were introduced to Mrs. Payne by the Rev. Henning [3]. Mr. and Mrs. Basil Payne entertained the two investigators for tea at Borley Place, during which they had a long chat about the Rectory, its various occupants, and the alleged phenomena [3, 6].

**Observations of the "Haunting" and the 1939 Fire**
Living directly opposite the "most haunted house in England", Basil Payne and his wife were prime witnesses to external events at the Rectory.
*   **The Window Lights:** Both Mr. and Mrs. Basil Payne personally witnessed the famous "unexplained lights" in the windows of Borley Rectory during the incumbency of the Rev. Guy Eric Smith (1928–1929), and again later in November 1937 during the Price tenancy [6, 7].
*   **The Rectory Fire:** On the night of **February 27/28, 1939**, Basil Payne and his wife were among the locals who stood and watched the devastating fire that ultimately gutted the Borley Rectory [6]. 

**Opinion of the Rectory**
Despite witnessing the strange lights, Basil Payne and his wife maintained a strong distaste for the building across the road from their home. They frequently referred to Borley Rectory bluntly as **"that horrible old place"** [2].
