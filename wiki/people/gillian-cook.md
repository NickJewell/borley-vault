# Mrs Gillian Cook

A resident of a large town in Essex who reported a curious experience in Borley churchyard on 6 August 1991 — a date she was able to pinpoint precisely because she suffered a family bereavement the following day.

## The Incident

On 6 August 1991, Mrs Cook, a friend, and her two children were driving to Kentwell Hall (one of the two Elizabethan mansions at nearby Long Melford) when they passed through Borley and decided to look at the church and graveyard. During their initial tour of the churchyard, Mrs Cook found herself drawn, for no apparent reason, to a grave on the north side of the church adjacent to the farmland, marked by a rusty iron cross but bearing no name. The group then returned to the car for a picnic lunch.

After eating, Mrs Cook went for a stroll in an adjacent cornfield, was startled by a pheasant flying up, and turned back through the churchyard. At the same grave she noticed that a **gold-coloured bracelet** was lying at the foot of the iron cross. She was certain it had not been there on her first pass. She picked it up, and the group drove on to Kentwell Hall.

## Sequel

About a week later, while walking with her children to school, the delicate chain of the bracelet snapped and it slipped off her wrist unnoticed. On her return journey she found it lying on the pavement — an unusually busy route — apparently unnoticed by anyone else. She retrieved it and still has it.

The bracelet consists of a fine chain with a small heart-shaped lock.

## Significance

The authors of *Borley Rectory: The Final Analysis* note the discovery as a possible **apport** — an object appearing without normal agency — while acknowledging that this claim may be excessive. They regard the appearance of the bracelet on the grave as the more remarkable part of the incident.
