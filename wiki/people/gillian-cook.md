# Mrs Gillian Cook

A resident of a large town in Essex who reported a curious experience in Borley churchyard on 6 August 1991 — a date she was able to pinpoint precisely because she suffered a family bereavement the following day.

## The Incident

On 6 August 1991, Mrs Cook, a friend, and her two children were driving to Kentwell Hall (one of the two Elizabethan mansions at nearby Long Melford) when they passed through Borley and decided to look at the church and graveyard. During their initial tour of the churchyard, Mrs Cook found herself drawn, for no apparent reason, to a grave on the north side of the church adjacent to the farmland, marked by a rusty iron cross but bearing no name. The group then returned to the car for a picnic lunch.

After eating, Mrs Cook went for a stroll in an adjacent cornfield, was startled by a pheasant flying up, and turned back through the churchyard. At the same grave she noticed that a **gold-coloured bracelet** was lying at the foot of the iron cross. She was certain it had not been there on her first pass. She picked it up, and the group drove on to Kentwell Hall.

## Sequel

About a week later, while walking with her children to school, the delicate chain of the bracelet snapped and it slipped off her wrist unnoticed. On her return journey she found it lying on the pavement — an unusually busy route — apparently unnoticed by anyone else. She retrieved it and still has it.

The bracelet consists of a fine chain with a small heart-shaped lock.

## Significance

The authors of *Borley Rectory: The Final Analysis* note the discovery as a possible **apport** — an object appearing without normal agency — while acknowledging that this claim may be excessive. They regard the appearance of the bracelet on the grave as the more remarkable part of the incident.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mrs Gillian Cook** appears in the Borley Rectory sources exclusively as a witness to a peculiar incident in the early 1990s, which she recounted to authors Edward Babbs and Claudine Mathias during the preparation of their book *Borley Rectory – The Final Analysis* [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the text reveals the following specific facts, events, dates, and biographical details:

**Lesser-Known Biographical Details**
The text notes simply that Mrs. Cook was a resident of a **"large town in Essex"** and had two children [1]. 

**The August 1991 Visit to Borley Churchyard**
Mrs. Cook recorded a specific and unusual experience that occurred on **August 6, 1991**—an exact date she remembered precisely because she suffered a tragic family bereavement the very next day [1]. While traveling with a friend and her two children to visit Kentwell Hall in Long Melford, the group routed their trip through Borley to explore the church and graveyard due to its haunted reputation [1]. 

During their stop, Mrs. Cook found herself inexplicably drawn to a specific grave on the north side of the church, adjacent to farmland, which was marked by a **"rusty iron cross"** [1]. Following a picnic lunch by their car, Mrs. Cook took a solitary stroll in a nearby cornfield. She was suddenly frightened when a pheasant flew up directly in front of her; turning back toward the churchyard to return to the car, she found herself standing once again by the grave with the rusty iron cross [1]. 

To her astonishment, she discovered a **gold-coloured bracelet** lying on the ground at the foot of the metal cross [1]. She was absolutely convinced the object had not been there during her first stop at the grave earlier that afternoon [1]. 

**The "Sequel" of the Broken Bracelet**
With no way to identify an owner, Mrs. Cook kept the piece of jewelry, describing it as having a **"fine chain and a small heart-shaped lock"** [1, 2]. About a week later, she experienced a sequel to the strange event. While walking her children to school along a busy route, the delicate chain snapped and slipped off her wrist unnoticed [2]. She realized it was missing upon reaching the school, but on her return journey, she was highly surprised to find the shiny gold bracelet simply lying on the pavement, remarkably untouched by anyone else despite the heavy foot traffic [2]. Mrs. Cook retrieved the item and still possessed it at the time she reported the incident to the authors [2].

**Investigator Opinions**
While documenting her account, the authors noted the peculiarity of the events but remained cautious in their paranormal assessment. They concluded that while the temporary loss and rediscovery of the item made it a "strange business" worth recording, it might be **"excessive to claim that the bracelet was an apport"** (a physical object materialized by supernatural agencies) [2].
