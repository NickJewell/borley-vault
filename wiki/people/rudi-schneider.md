# Rudi Schneider

**Rudi Schneider** (born Braunau-am-Inn, 27 July 1908; died Weyer, Upper Austria, 28 April 1957) was an Austrian physical medium, the youngest of twelve children, and one of the most scientifically tested mediums in history [1]. He is best known in the Borley context as [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s star performer from 1929 to 1932 — and the subject of the **Schneider photograph scandal** that collapsed Price's [[national-laboratory|National Laboratory of Psychical Research]] and drove him back to focus on [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

## Background

Born in Braunau, Austria (also the birthplace of Hitler), Rudi was the younger brother of [[willi-schneider|Willi Schneider]]. When Willi's powers began to fade in 1919, Willi's trance personality **'Olga'** — who claimed to be Lola Montez, the nineteenth-century mistress of the King of Bavaria — demanded that eleven-year-old Rudi replace him [1, 2]. Entering the séance room apparently sleepwalking, Rudi would exhibit the rapid breathing and extreme muscle tension characteristic of 'Olga,' producing phenomena such as levitation, the moving of a coffee table, the ringing of a hand bell, the tying of a handkerchief, and the playing of a toy zither in Munich.

[[harry-price|Harry Price]] met Rudi in 1925 and had his first sitting with him in April 1926. After the death of their promoter [[albert-von-schrenck-notzing|Dr Schrenck-Notzing]] in February 1929, Price travelled immediately to Munich to secure the twenty-year-old medium for a series of sittings at his National Laboratory of Psychical Research (NLPR) in London.

## Price's Promotion

Price effectively took over Rudi's career and turned him into a media celebrity. On his arrival at Liverpool Street he was greeted by the press as **"the Austrian psychical 'superman'"** [3, 4].

During the first series of London sittings (11–22 April 1929), Rudi sat in a curtained cabinet measuring 7ft 3 inches wide, 4ft 10 inches long, and 8ft high. Sitters sat hand-to-hand and foot-to-foot wearing **gloves and socks that were electrically wired**; if the circuit broke, a signal would flash [5]. Among those impressed were Laurence Olivier and Stanley Holloway. [[charles-sutton|Charles Sutton]] of the *Daily Mail* found the materialisation of a "pseudopod" — described as "a paw with thick fingers and a large thumb" — particularly compelling [5]. Price offered £1,000 to any conjuror who could repeat the phenomena and awarded Rudi a certificate declaring him to have produced "absolutely genuine phenomena under conditions never previously imposed on any medium" [5, 6].

A second series of twenty-one sittings (October 1929–January 1930) produced less favourable results. The Rev. Digby Kittermaster noted that while sitters were confined, Price's secretary [[lucie-kaye|Lucie Kaye]] was free to walk around the room. [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] dismissed the sittings as "a cavalcade of entertainment" [6].

## The Photograph Scandal (1932)

The final series of sittings led to a massive scandal that destroyed Price's NLPR.

In early 1932, Rudi annoyed Price by considering a better offer from the Institut Métapsychique in Paris. Enraged, Price declared it a "filthy trick." Compounding his anger, [[lord-charles-hope|Lord Charles Hope]] arranged a further series of sittings at Tavistock Square under the auspices of the rival Society for Psychical Research independently of Price [7, 8].

During a sitting equipped with an automatic camera, a flash went off as a handkerchief lifted. On 28 April 1932, in what his secretary Ethel Beenham later recalled as a moment of calculated spite, Price produced the developed photograph — which showed Rudi lifting his arm at the exact moment the handkerchief was supposedly moved paranormally [9]. Price secretly held onto this incriminating photograph for nearly a year. In the spring of 1933, just as Lord Charles Hope was preparing to publish the findings of his own SPR sittings with Rudi, **Price went public with the photograph in an exclusive exposé in the *Sunday Dispatch*** [10, 11]. Price privately admitted to [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen Goldney]] that this delayed exposure was revenge for Rudi defecting to the SPR [10].

This sensational betrayal caused the most important members of the NLPR council to resign in protest, effectively ending Price's organisation [11]. [[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]], who was "absolutely convinced of Schneider's honesty," warned Price he would never again carry out a major investigation with scientific collaborators.

> **Contradiction:** Anita Gregory, an SPR member, later claimed that Price had faked the photograph himself. This accusation was dismissed by [[alan-gauld|Dr Alan Gauld]] and [[dr-vernon-harrison|Dr Vernon Harrison]], former President of the Royal Photographic Society [10]. Despite deliberately ruining Schneider's reputation in print, Price persistently maintained in later years that he had witnessed absolutely genuine phenomena produced by Schneider [10].

## Significance for Borley

The Schneider scandal is the immediate cause of Price's intensified focus on [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]]. With the Laboratory destroyed, his reputation damaged, and former allies alienated, he needed a project that would restore his standing with the general public. Writing a bestselling book about Borley — following the template of Ada Goodrich Freer's study of [[alleged-haunting-ballechin|Ballechin House]] — was the solution.

[[robert-hastings|Robert J. Hastings]] further argued that Price's near-complete absence from Borley after July 1929 — often cited by critics as evidence he had lost interest after allegedly being "caught" faking phenomena by [[charles-sutton|Charles Sutton]] — was more plausibly explained by the intensity of the Rudi Schneider investigations, which ran at a three-days-a-week schedule through late 1929, making it impossible for Price to actively pursue the Borley case during that period [3, 12].
