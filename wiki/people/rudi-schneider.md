# Rudi Schneider

Younger Austrian medium; [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s star performer from 1929 to 1932; and the subject of the **Schneider photograph scandal** that collapsed Price's National Laboratory of Psychical Research and drove him back to focus on Borley.

## Background

Born in Braunau, Austria (also the birthplace of Hitler), Rudi was the younger brother of [[willi-schneider|Willi Schneider]]. He apparently first exhibited mediumistic abilities in 1919, at the age of eleven, when his brother's spirit guide "Olga" demanded Rudi replace him. Rudi demonstrated raps, knocks, levitation, the moving of a coffee table, the ringing of a hand bell, the tying of a handkerchief, and the playing of a toy zither in Munich.

After the death of their promoter [[albert-von-schrenck-notzing|Dr Schrenck-Notzing]] in February 1929, Price travelled immediately to Munich to secure Rudi's services for London sittings.

## Price's Promotion

Price effectively took over Rudi's career and turned him into a media celebrity. On his arrival at Liverpool Street he was greeted by the press as "the Austrian psychical 'superman'." During the first series of London sittings (11–22 April 1929), Rudi sat in a curtained cabinet while sitters wore electrically-wired gloves and socks; if the circuit broke, a signal would flash. Among those impressed were **Laurence Olivier** and **Stanley Holloway**. [[charles-sutton|Charles Sutton]] of the *Daily Mail* called the materialisation of a "pseudopod" particularly compelling.

Price awarded Rudi a certificate declaring him to have produced "absolutely genuine phenomena under conditions never previously imposed on any medium."

## The Photograph Scandal (1932)

A second series of twenty-one sittings (October 1929–January 1930) produced less favourable results. When [[lord-charles-hope|Lord Charles Hope]] organised a further series of sittings at Tavistock Square in 1932, independently of Price, Price bided his time.

On 28 April 1932, in what [[lucie-kaye|Lucie Kaye]]'s successor as secretary, **Ethel Beenham**, later recalled as a moment of calculated spite, Price produced a photograph from an earlier sitting. It showed Rudi lifting his arm — apparently cheating — at the exact moment the handkerchief was supposedly moved paranormally.

Price published the photograph in an exclusive exposé in the *Sunday Dispatch* in spring 1933. [[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]], who was "absolutely convinced of Schneider's honesty," warned Price that he would never be able to carry out a major investigation with scientific collaborators again. All the more important members of Price's Laboratory council resigned in protest. The scandal effectively **brought an end to the National Laboratory of Psychical Research**.

## Significance for Borley

The Schneider scandal is the immediate cause of Price's intensified focus on [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]]. With the Laboratory destroyed, his reputation damaged, and former allies alienated, he needed a project that would restore his standing with the general public. Writing a bestselling book about Borley — following the template of Ada Goodrich Freer's study of Ballechin House — was the solution.

> **Contested:** [[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]] remained convinced throughout her life that Rudi Schneider was genuine and that Price had fabricated or distorted the incriminating photograph.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Rudi Schneider (born Brannau-am-Inn, 27 July 1908; died Weyer, Upper Austria, 28 April 1957)** was an Austrian physical medium and the youngest of twelve children [1]. He became one of the most scientifically tested mediums in history, and his career—heavily promoted and ultimately derailed by ghost hunter Harry Price—forms a major subplot in the history of psychical research surrounding the Borley Rectory investigators. 

**Early Life and Mediumship**
*   **Origins:** Rudi's mediumistic career followed that of his older brother, Willi. When Willi's powers began to fade in 1919, Willi's trance personality, **'Olga'** (who claimed to be Lola Montez, the nineteenth-century mistress of the King of Bavaria), demanded that eleven-year-old Rudi become a medium [1, 2]. 
*   **Phenomena:** Entering the séance room apparently sleepwalking, Rudi would exhibit the rapid breathing and extreme muscle tension characteristic of 'Olga,' producing phenomena such as levitation, the moving of a coffee table, and the tying of a handkerchief [2].
*   **Price's Involvement:** Harry Price met Rudi in 1925 and had his first sitting with him in April 1926. Following the death of Rudi's mentor, Dr. Albert von Schrenck-Notzing, in February 1929, Price traveled to Munich to secure the twenty-year-old medium for a series of sittings at his National Laboratory of Psychical Research (NLPR) in London. Rudi arrived at Liverpool Street to be greeted by the press as **"the Austrian psychical 'superman'"** [3, 4]. 

**The NLPR Sittings (1929–1930)**
*   **First Series (11–22 April 1929):** Rudi was bound to a chair within a curtained cabinet measuring 7ft 3 inches wide, 4ft 10 inches long, and 8ft high. Sitters sat hand-to-hand and foot-to-foot wearing **gloves and socks that were electrically wired**; if the circuit was broken, a signal would flash [5]. The sittings were attended by celebrities like Laurence Olivier and Stanley Holloway [5]. Journalist Charles Sutton was particularly impressed by the materialization of a **"pseudopod,"** which he described as **"a paw with thick fingers and a large thumb"** [5]. Price confidently **offered £1,000 to any conjuror who could repeat the phenomena** and issued Rudi an absolutely clean certificate from the NLPR [5, 6]. 
*   **Second Series (October 1929–January 1930):** During these twenty-one sittings, criticisms arose. Rev. Digby Kittermaster noted that while sitters were confined, Price’s secretary, Lucie Kaye, was free to walk around the room [6]. Eric Dingwall dismissed the sittings as **"a cavalcade of entertainment"** [6].

**Contradictions, Feuds, and "The Exposure"**
The final series of sittings led to a massive scandal that destroyed Price's NLPR:
*   **The Defection:** In early 1932, Rudi annoyed Price by considering a better offer from the Institut Métapsychique in Paris. Enraged, Price declared it a **"filthy trick"** and said, **"If a son of mine did such a thing, I would thrash him within an inch of his life"** [7]. Compounding Price's anger, Lord Charles Hope arranged to test Rudi under the auspices of the rival Society for Psychical Research (SPR) without informing Price [7, 8].
*   **The Photograph (28 April 1932):** During a sitting equipped with an automatic camera, a flash went off as a handkerchief lifted. Developing the photo in his darkroom, Price pointed out to his secretary, Ethel Beenham, that **Rudi had lifted his arm at exactly the moment the 'paranormal' movement took place** [9]. 
*   **The Delayed Sabotage:** Price secretly held onto this incriminating photograph for nearly a year. In the spring of 1933, just as Lord Charles Hope was preparing to publish the findings of his own SPR sittings with Rudi, **Price went public with the photograph in an exclusive exposé in the *Sunday Dispatch*** [10, 11]. Price privately admitted to Kathleen Goldney that this delayed exposure was revenge for Rudi defecting to the SPR [10].
*   **The Backlash:** This sensational betrayal caused the most important members of the NLPR council to resign in protest, effectively ending Price's organization [11]. **Anita Gregory, an SPR member, later claimed that Price had faked the photograph himself**, though this accusation was dismissed by Dr. Alan Gauld and Dr. Vernon Harrison, former President of the Royal Photographic Society [10]. 
*   **A Curious Contradiction:** Despite deliberately ruining Schneider's reputation in print, Price persistently maintained that **he had witnessed absolutely genuine phenomena produced by Schneider and continued to champion him** in later years [10].

**Connection to the Borley Haunting**
Rudi Schneider directly impacted the Borley Rectory investigation. Price's sudden and complete absence from Borley after July 1929—which his critics often pointed to as proof that he had lost interest after allegedly being "caught" faking phenomena by Charles Sutton—was heavily defended by Robert J. Hastings. Hastings argued that **the intense, three-days-a-week schedule of the Rudi Schneider investigations in late 1929 demanded most of Price's attention**, making it impossible for him to actively pursue the Borley case during that period [3, 12].
