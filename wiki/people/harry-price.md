# Harry Price (17 January 1881 – 29 March 1948)

Psychical researcher and principal investigator of [[borley-rectory]]. Author of *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940) and *The End of Borley Rectory* (1946). The most contested figure in the Borley case.

## Background (per O'Connor, hbrs-ch07)

Born 17 January 1881 to Edward Ditching Price (grocer/paper salesman, Shropshire) and Emma Meech (journalist's daughter). Grew up in New Cross, south-east London. Left school at 15; studied photography, chemistry and mechanical engineering evenings at Goldsmiths College. Father secured him work at Edward Saunders & Son, the paper manufacturer — Price worked as a commercial traveller for this firm for most of his career, leading a double life as ghost hunter and paper salesman.

He was **deeply insecure** about his lack of university education; wrote out life goals as a young man including "To appear in *Who's Who*" and "To possess a Rolls-Royce car." His pretensions to Sussex archaeology collapsed when Professor Haverfield exposed his silver ingot as a fake copy (1910). His marriage to Constance Knight was long but sexually estranged; he reportedly "never slept with Constance again" after the wedding and had numerous affairs. No children.

"Arun Bank," his Pulborough home on the River Arun, was where he did most of his writing and kept his extensive esoteric library. He loved goldfish, breeding them in two garden ponds. His typical week: commercial traveller Mon–Fri; psychical researcher Wed/Fri in London.

## Character (per Underwood & Tabori and O'Connor)

"A maverick, quick-tempered, self-assertive, vain and never averse to publicity." A lone wolf; never comfortable within the SPR's institutional discipline. Made enemies easily. Biographied by [[paul-tabori]] (his literary executor for 25 years). O'Connor's summary: he "outstripped all other investigators in the variety and sensational quality of his work" — but his primary objective was often the bestselling book rather than the scientific truth.

## Price in His Own Words

Price's firsthand account (*The Most Haunted House in England*) projects a different persona: empirical, methodical, self-deprecating as narrator. He positions the book as a documentary collection, explicitly disclaiming responsibility for incidents outside his personal experience. His framing of the case: *"I little dreamed that there was ten years' work ahead of me probing the mystery of what was to become the best-authenticated case of haunting in the annals of psychical research."*

He describes arriving not through personal initiative but because a newspaper editor telephoned him — [[vc-wall|V. C. Wall]] was already on site with independent press coverage.

## Methodology

Price's standard investigation kit included: felt overshoes for silent movement, sealing equipment for rooms, hidden electrical contacts, a transmitting thermograph, a cinematograph camera with remote control, a mercury bowl for tremor detection, and equipment for infrared photography in near-darkness. This level of instrumentation — documented in 1940, before the controversy — directly contradicts the SPR portrait of him as a showman who created effects rather than recording them.

Price personally witnessed phenomena at Borley: he saw keys shoot from their locks during the Smith incumbency, and he was present when a half-brick came through the verandah roof on his first visit (June 1929).

## Role in the Borley Case

- Arrived 12 June 1929 at the Smiths' invitation, with secretary [[lucie-kaye|Miss Lucie Kaye]]
- Investigated across multiple periods; most intensively during his tenancy 1937–38
- Conducted a formal observer programme (48 volunteers in shifts; results in the *Locked Book* compiled by [[sidney-glanville]])
- Planchette sessions produced the [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] name and the fire prediction (March 1938)
- Published two books: *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940); *The End of Borley Rectory* (1946)
- Died 28 March 1948 in his study chair at Arun Bank, Pulborough — returned from posting a letter, sat down; [[constance-price|Constance]] found him dead when she returned with tea
- Had just written the first chapter of *Borley III* and drafted the outline; it was never completed

## First Day's Phenomena (June 12, 1929)

On his first day, Price and [[vc-wall]] conducted a vigil in the octagonal summer-house:
- Wall saw a shadowy figure on the [[nuns-walk]]; Price thought he saw something but was not certain
- On returning to the house: a thick glass pane from the veranda roof crashed at their feet (brick found as missile)
- A glass candlestick was hurled down the main stairs past their heads — both *saw it in flight* — it hit the iron stove and shattered
- A mothball followed; then pebbles, slate, more pebbles down the stairs
- Two keys fell simultaneously from two door locks as Price watched
- That night: the Blue Room séance with the Misses Bull, entity claiming to be Harry Bull

These first-night experiences Price describes as "a day to be remembered, even by an experienced investigator. Sixteen hours of thrills!"

## The Observer Program (1937–38)

Price's methodological centrepiece: his deliberate self-exclusion from the formal inquiry. He rented the Rectory at £30/year from [[rev-ac-henning]] (May 1937) and advertised for observers in *The Times*, receiving ~200 replies. Selected ~40: university graduates, doctors, engineers, army officers, BBC staff. All strangers to him, to the Rectory, and (mostly) to each other. All signed the Declaration Form ([[mhh-app-a]]); all received the Blue Book of instructions ([[mhh-app-b]]). 48 observers eventually took part; almost all reported phenomena. Price established the Base Room in the library; ringed all objects; set up control measures. The *Times* advertisement's wording — "Haunted House. Responsible persons of leisure and intelligence, intrepid, critical, and unbiased" — became the most quoted phrase from the investigation.

## Personal Evidence

Price lists the phenomena he personally witnessed: candlestick in flight (June 1929, with Wall); keys falling simultaneously from two doors; wine turning to ink in the glass (October 1931); claret bottle hurled down stairs. In his final chapter he asks: "The reader has not had a glass candlestick hurled at him from above when he knew there was no one above to hurl it!" His personal verdict: "Yes, decidedly" haunted.

## Analytical Contributions

Price's own analysis ([[mhh-ch31]], [[mhh-ch32]]): poltergeist theory for physical disturbances; "persisting egos" theory for apparitions and footsteps. Found phenomena cyclic and recurrent with determinable periodicity, growing weaker at each recurrence. Noted the consistent Roman Catholic character throughout. Counted 100 witnesses over 70 years; argued hallucination and lying could not account for all of them.

## Marianne Foyster's Account of Price

[[marianne-foyster|Marianne's]] autobiographical notes and Swanson interviews (1958) offer a sharply critical perspective on Price that was not available to earlier commentators:

**Physical appearance:** "He gave me the creeps. He had pointed ears, a balding head with high forehead, and eyes which were startling. They were not polite eyes."

**The wine-to-ink incident (October 1931):** Marianne explicitly states this was "Price pulled his wine into ink trick" — a magician's illusion, not a paranormal event. She places it on his second unannounced visit (with "a picnic hamper and wine"), not necessarily his October 1931 official visit.

**Affair with Mrs. Goldney:** In her autobiography Marianne writes: "Price and Mrs. Goldney were constantly running out together... I sneaked out of the garden door and slipped through the side door. They got a surprise. So did I. **They were in the throes of an affair.**" Eileen Garrett's letter to Hall (May 10, 1958) corroborates: Marianne had "enough to say about Mrs. Goldney and her affairs with Price some of which took place in the oddest places in Borley."

**Price's knowledge of the fictional manuscript:** Marianne believed Price knew the Foyster documents were fictional but "found it useful to pretend otherwise" — useful because it would allow him to present the material as documentary evidence in his own books.

**"Blew hot and cold":** "He was a Magician and a member of the Magician's Society... He blew hot and blew cold and say that he did not believe it one minute and then next breath, he didn't. Anyway, he thought that he was going to make a lot of money."

These allegations are Marianne's own account and cannot be independently verified. The affair allegation in particular has never been corroborated from other sources.

## Constance Price's Assessment (bps-11)

[[constance-price|Constance Price]], visited by Underwood in October 1968 (nearly 20 years after Price's death), was categorical: "I think Mrs Goldney was a two-faced woman and Eric Dingwall was no better. They had often been there and she had looked on them as friends." She had not read the 1956 Report; her bank trustee advised against it.

She confirmed Price had "occasionally felt pain over his heart" but "had not even consulted a doctor." He had just written the first chapter of *Borley III* and drafted its outline. After his death she received "literally hundreds of letters" from members of the Ghost Club asking about its future.

## The Borley Bell

The large iron bell from Borley Rectory's courtyard was given to Price after the fire (1939) and hung at Arun Bank during his lifetime. After Constance's death, the University of London and Midland Bank (executors) presented it to [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]], who hung it in his garden at Bentley, Hampshire. (bps-01, bps-11)

## Price's Health: Migraines

Price suffered from severe migraines that turned the skin around his eyes almost black — a detail confirmed by [[lucie-kaye|Lucie Kaye]] in her account of his first night at Borley, when she initially mistook his dark-ringed eyes for something sinister. (bps-04)

## The Controversy

Accusations of "bad faith, duplicity and downright deception" did not surface until nine months after Price's death. The first critical survey (*The Haunting of Borley Rectory*, Dingwall, Goldney, Hall) was published eight years after his death (1956). [[peter-underwood]] and [[paul-tabori]] find the posthumous timing significant.

The SPR's Robert J. Hastings produced a counter-report (1969) partially rehabilitating Price.

See [[harry-price-controversy]] for the full evidence tracker.

## Death and Posthumous Controversy

Died Easter Monday 29 March 1948 of a heart attack in his armchair at Arun Bank, freshly lit pipe still smoking in his hand, having just returned from posting a letter to [[mollie-goldney]]. Within 24 hours of his death, Magic Circle vice president Douglas Craggs claimed Price had "bamboozled the public into believing things in which he placed no credence whatsoever." The SPR investigation led by Dingwall, Goldney and Hall culminated in the [[borley-report|Borley Report]] (1956).

Robert Hastings's 1969 counter-examination found the Report to be a prosecutor's brief. [[peter-underwood]] and [[paul-tabori]] consistently defended his reputation.

Price's hat, coats and walking sticks remained on the hall stand at Arun Bank for twenty years after his death, as if he had "just stepped out but very shortly would return home." (hbrs-ch19)

## Clarke's Analysis (slbr-02, slbr-12, slbr-15)

Andrew Clarke (drawing on Fred Tatum's testimony via Mrs C. Baines) provides additional local evidence for Price's conjuring during the Smith period:
- Mary Pearson *"distinctly saw Price throw a condiment"* at his first lunch with the Smiths
- Mary told Mrs Smith she *"had distinctly seen Price throw a coin"*
- Fred Tatum: Price manipulated a storm lantern wick in the scullery
- A dinner guest saw water turn to ink at Price's elbow — *"he wasn't a man they would like to offend"*

**Price's private Foyster conclusion** (letter to Dr D. F. Frazer-Harris, 15 October 1931): *"we were convinced that the many phenomena that we saw were fraudulent because we took steps to control various persons and rooms, [and] the manifestations ceased. We think that the rector's wife is responsible for the trouble."* To the rector: *"of course, no word of this will appear in our report."*

Clarke also documents prior suppressed investigations: **Professor Cook (Cambridge)** investigated the Foyster phenomena while they were active and concluded there was "nothing in it" after a thorough inquiry. Cook's investigation was not mentioned in Price's books.

**The "Adelaide" writing:** During Price's third visit (with Lord Charles Hope), Major Douglas-Home noted "Adelaide" written in similar handwriting to the "Marianne" messages on the kitchen wall, at the same height. Price *"brushed aside the question."* The housekeeper confirmed Adelaide *"was a terror for scribbling."*

**The 1943 bones dig:** Clarke raises the possibility (accepted by local people) that Price performed a sleight of hand, switching the farm worker Jackson's pig jawbone for a planted human bone when handing it to the pre-positioned pathologist. Jackson maintained his pig-bone identification for his entire life.

## The SPR Report's Specific Charges (spr-iv, spr-vi, spr-vii)

The 1956 SPR Report (Dingwall, Goldney, Hall) brings multiple specific accusations beyond the Sutton incident. See [[the-borley-report]] and [[harry-price-controversy]] for full analysis. Key points:

### The Medal Substitution
The SPR documents a probable deliberate substitution of the 1929 "apport" medal to fit the evolving narrative:

- **1929 (actual):** A single brass St Ignatius Loyola medal — monk-like figure holding the rule of the Society of Jesus — "appeared" on 5 July 1929. Confirmed by Lord Charles Hope's contemporary notes, by [[lucie-kaye]]'s 1951 statement ("the only medal I remember appearing in the early days"), and by Price's own 1929 article in the *Journal of the American S.P.R.* ("a brass Romish medallion, which the rector could not identify"). The St Ignatius medal was found in Price's Borley papers after his death
- **1940 (MHH):** Price described two entirely different medals: (1) "a small gilt medallion, such as are presented to Roman Catholic children on their Confirmation" and (2) "another medallion or badge, dated 'AN VIII' (i.e. A.D. 1799) issued in Paris after the French Revolution." Neither medal appears anywhere in Price's files before MHH
- **The substitution motive:** In 1929 Price was promoting the English "monastery and monk" legend, fitting a medal with a monk's head. After the 1937 planchette sessions created the "French Roman Catholic nun" story and the monastery legend was demolished in 1938, a monk-head medal became inconvenient; two medals supporting a French/RC narrative were far more useful. Price was an enthusiastic numismatist with a large collection, well placed to supply them

### The "Flying Brick" Confirmed as Normal (April 1944)
Price's famous "levitated brick" photograph (EBR Plates XXII–XXIII) — which he offered £1,000 to any charity to prove was faked — is explained in full by [[cynthia-thompson]]'s signed statement to the SPR (1950): "there was a brawny workman still at work behind the wall. All three of us saw him... There is no doubt at all that the flying bricks, several of which came out at regular intervals, were propelled by this workman as part of his demolition work." Thompson recounted joking "Look, poltergeists" and Price responding "Yes, indeed; look — no strings, no wires attached" in what she took as an obvious joke — before she discovered he had published it as a paranormal event in EBR

### The Cellophane and Pencil Marks (1937)
[[major-douglas-home]], visiting in 1937, independently documented two patterns: (1) new pencil wall-marks appeared only on tours when Price was at the rear, ceasing when Douglas-Home positioned himself behind Price; (2) the mysterious "rustling" sounds attributed to the Blue Room matched the sound of cellophane paper unrolling — and on the way home Douglas-Home "managed to peep into Price's suitcase and there was a long roll of cellophane paper with a jagged edge"

### The 1943 Excavation — Suspicious Circumstances
The SPR finds the bone discovery suspicious (see [[bones-excavation]] for full analysis): Price excluded [[sidney-glanville]] from the excavation entirely; electrical wiring was found in the cellar during demolition that no one had reported during the investigation period; the "well tank" Price claimed to remember had "disappeared" between 1939 and 1943 despite the rectory being uninhabited ruins; the bones were only 3 feet down, inconsistent with being "dropped into a deep well" in 1667

## The Companion's Assessment (brc-ii-op)

The Borley Rectory Companion (Adams, Brazil & Underwood, 2009) offers a balanced synthesis after six decades of investigation. Three key points:

**On the Sutton accusation**: Price probably did throw a stone to help Wall get a story on a night when no phenomena obligingly occurred — "amusing himself by playing the ghost." This was careless but not systematic fraud. It should not be used as a template for everything else.

**On the bones**: The [[well-tank-controversy|well tank never existed]]; the brick floor was undisturbed since 1863; Price could not have planted the bones. He was genuinely confused when writing from memory, and the journalist in him presented the discovery in its most dramatic light.

**On Price's duality**: He operated on two levels throughout his career — the serious psychical researcher and the maverick publicist. The former produced 100 witnesses over 70 years and pioneered the on-site extended investigation; the latter produced the flying-brick photograph, the wine-to-ink incident, and the somewhat misleading well-tank narrative. These two modes are separable. The failures of the publicist should not automatically invalidate the findings of the researcher.

Sir Albion Richardson KC, CBE — former President of the Law Society — read *The Most Haunted House in England* and wrote: "I have not met anyone who has read it who has not been satisfied that the manifestations therein disclosed are proved by the evidence, to the point of moral certainty."

## The End of Borley Rectory (1946)

Price's sequel, written from the Reform Club and published April 1946, covers 1939–1945. New material in his own voice:

**The 1943 excavation** (August 17–18): Price organised and directed the dig at the cellar site where the [[well-tank-controversy|well-tank]] had been. Found human remains (left mandible, 5 teeth; temporal/parietal skull fragment) at exactly 3 feet depth — confirmed as a young woman by two pathologists. Found the cream-jug and the pendants. Price's Planchette records received in January 1945 (17 months after the dig) confirmed that the séance messages had been pointing to exactly this spot.

**The last photograph** (April 5, 1944): Life magazine photographer David Scherman photographed the ruins with Price present; a brick was seen by all three observers to shoot c.4 feet into the air in front of the kitchen passage. Scherman's photograph captures the brick in mid-air. Price: "the first photograph ever taken of a Poltergeist projectile in flight."

**The burial** (May 29, 1945): Price brought the remains to Liston Churchyard; Rev. Henning performed the service using prayers from The Missal (including the Gelasian Sacramentary prayer). The casket was lowered at 5:42 p.m. BDST. Price noted the exact time in case any phenomena were reported at that moment.

**The demolition**: By June 1945, the Rectory was razed. Site sold for £560. The large yard-bell — allegedly rung paranormally many times — hangs in Price's garden at Pulborough.

**Legal opinions**: Price secured assessments from [[sir-ernest-jelf|Sir Ernest Jelf]] (Senior Master of the Supreme Court) and [[sir-albion-richardson|Sir Albion Richardson K.C., C.B.E.]] (Recorder of Nottingham) — the first jurists ever to pronounce publicly on the evidence in a psychical research case. Richardson: "as conclusive as human testimony can ever be, to the point of moral certainty."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Biographical Background**
Harry Price was born on January 17, 1881, at 37 Red Lion Square, Holborn, London, to Emma Meech and Edward Ditcher Price, a grocer who later became a commercial traveller [1-3]. Though Price later cultivated a persona of originating from wealthy Shropshire stock—a claim fiercely criticized by his posthumous detractors—he actually grew up in South East London [3-5]. He attended Haberdashers’ Aske’s Hatcham Boys School, leaving at age fifteen, and subsequently took evening classes in photography, chemistry, and mechanical engineering at Goldsmiths College [6]. For most of his working life, he was a commercial traveller and sales manager for Edward Saunders & Son, a large firm of paper manufacturers [6-8]. On August 1, 1908, he married Constance Mary Knight, a woman with independent means, and they resided together at Arun Bank in Pulborough, West Sussex for the rest of his life [6, 7, 9]. He died of a sudden heart attack at his home on March 29, 1948, sitting in his favourite armchair with a freshly lit pipe just after posting a letter [10-12]. 

**Personality and Professional Profile**
Price was an intensely complex and ambitious man; stocky and bald-headed with pointed ears, he had a penetrating stare often ringed by dark circles due to chronic migraines, and spoke with a slight stammer developed in his schooldays [13-15]. He suffered from angina from 1928 onward, which increasingly curtailed his physical activities [16, 17]. He possessed a brilliant flair for self-promotion and journalism, frankly admitting: **"To work successfully in this field, one has to be absolutely fearless and independent – with a hide like a rhinoceros"** [18]. 

A highly skilled amateur conjurer and a member of the Magic Circle, Price used his knowledge of sleight-of-hand to expose fraudulent mediums, notably the spirit photographer William Hope and the materializing medium Helen Duncan [2, 19-21]. He was also an avid numismatist and a passionate bibliophile, amassing the world's largest library of magical literature, which he eventually bequeathed to the University of London [19, 22, 23]. 

Price joined the patrician Society for Psychical Research (SPR) in June 1920, but chafed under their conservative methods [7, 8, 24]. In 1925, he founded his own rival organization, the National Laboratory of Psychical Research, equipped with state-of-the-art instruments [25-27]. He later reorganized this into the grandly titled University of London Council for Psychical Investigation in 1934, despite it having no official connection to the university [28, 29]. 

**First Visits to Borley and the Sutton Affair (1929)**
Price was introduced to Borley Rectory on June 11, 1929, when the editor of the *Daily Mirror* asked him to investigate strange occurrences reported by the Rev. Guy Eric Smith [30-32]. Price arrived on June 12 with his secretary, Lucie Kaye, and reporter V.C. Wall [14, 33]. His arrival coincided with a sudden, violent escalation of poltergeist activity, including the throwing of stones, a half-brick crashing through a glass roof, and the shattering of a red glass candlestick [32, 34, 35]. That night, Price led a seance in the Blue Room where a heavy mahogany mirror tapped out messages purportedly from the late Rev. Harry Bull [36, 37]. 

This sudden onset of physical phenomena led to severe accusations against Price. On July 25, 1929, *Daily Mail* reporter Charles Sutton visited Borley with Price and later claimed—in an article published nine months after Price's death—that after a pebble hit him on the head, he grabbed Price and found his pockets full of bricks and pebbles [38-41]. Price's defenders point out that Sutton never published this while Price was alive, and suggest that Price's stunned silence during the alleged physical confrontation was likely the result of an angina attack brought on by shock [42-44]. 

**The Foyster Period Contradictions (1930–1935)**
When Price visited the Rectory on October 13-14, 1931, during the incumbency of the Rev. Lionel Foyster and his young wife Marianne, he witnessed a barrage of phenomena, including a glass of wine turning into ink, bell-ringing, and bottle-throwing [45-47]. Convinced it was a hoax, Price bluntly accused Marianne of faking the phenomena, which infuriated Foyster and caused a rift that kept Price away from Borley for over five years [46, 48, 49]. In a private letter at the time, Price explicitly stated: **"we are convinced that the many phenomena we saw were fraudulent... psychically speaking there is nothing in it"** [50]. 

However, in a striking contradiction, when Price published his bestseller *The Most Haunted House in England* in 1940, he suppressed his accusations against Marianne, utilized Foyster's diary, and presented the phenomena from this period as some of the most dangerous and convincing evidence of the haunting [51-53]. 

**The Price Tenancy (1937–1938)**
After the Rectory stood empty, Price rented it for a year beginning May 19, 1937, for the sum of £30 [54-56]. Seeking objective data, he placed an advertisement in *The Times* on May 25, 1937, recruiting a team of "intrepid, critical and unbiased" amateur observers [57-59]. He selected 48 investigators and issued them a *Blue Book of Instructions* [60-62]. Critics later assailed this booklet as a "barrage of suggestion" that pre-conditioned the observers to hallucinate phenomena like footsteps, bells, and apparitions [63-65]. 

Price himself rarely stayed overnight during the tenancy [66]. On the final day of his lease, May 19, 1938, Price and an associate made a final sweep of the empty house and inexplicably found a 22-carat gold wedding ring lying on the Blue Room floor, which Price presented as a "golden apport" [67, 68]. 

**Excavations, "The Flying Brick," and Later Controversies**
Influenced by Canon W.J. Phythian-Adams’s theory that the wall writings ("Well Tank Bottom Me") pointed to a murdered French nun, Price supervised a dig in the Rectory cellars on August 17, 1943 [69-72]. The excavation unearthed a human jawbone and a skull fragment [70, 73]. Sceptical SPR investigators (Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) heavily implied that Price had fraudulently planted these bones to provide a sensational climax for his 1946 sequel, *The End of Borley Rectory* [73, 74]. 

Price also faced accusations over a photograph taken by *Life* magazine photographer David Scherman on April 5, 1944, which showed a brick suspended in mid-air amidst the demolition ruins [75, 76]. Journalist Cynthia Ledsham later accused Price of "bare-faced hocus pocus," stating the brick was simply thrown by a demolition workman standing just out of frame, and that Price knowingly passed a joke off as paranormal [77-80]. Outraged by the rumours, Price offered £,1000 to charity to anyone who could prove the photograph was faked [81]. Price was similarly accused of planting foreign Catholic medallions during his 1929 visits to artificially bolster the nun legend [82, 83]. 

**Final Stance and Legacy**
Following his death, critics like Trevor Hall attempted to entirely dismantle Price's reputation, publishing works filled with "unmitigated malice" describing him as a scoundrel and a fabricator [84-86]. In contrast, friends and researchers like Peter Underwood, Robert Hastings, and Sidney Glanville fiercely defended his underlying integrity, arguing he was a brilliant, if careless, journalist whose primary goal was to bring psychical research to the masses [87-89]. Despite the numerous allegations of trickery and exaggeration levelled at him, Price remained publicly adamant about the core of the Borley hauntings, asserting: **"if you cut out the Foysters, the Bulls, the Smiths, etc., something still remains"** [52, 90]. He frequently summarized his life's most famous case by declaring that **"Every person who has resided in the rectory since it was built in 1863... has sworn to incidents that can only be described as paranormal"** [91].
