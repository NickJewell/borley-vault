# C. J. Cave, M.A., J.P., F.S.A.

Meteorologist of Stoner Hill, Petersfield. Listed in the acknowledgements section of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s *The End of Borley Rectory* among the correspondents who provided "suggestions and help" following the publication of *The Most Haunted House in England* [1]. Writing on October 14, 1941, Cave offered a natural explanation for one of the more unusual reported phenomena at Borley Rectory: the "smoke on the lawn."

## The Smoke-Column Explanation

The incident in question: a foreman painter named Hardy was working on the Rectory exterior when his colleague called him into the library to observe a column of smoke rising vertically from the centre of the lawn. The two men watched it for about two minutes before it disappeared. On investigation, there was no fire, no scorching, and no explicable source.

Cave's explanation: **a dancing column of gnats**. Certain species of gnats "dance" in a column that looks exactly like smoke. Cave had personally observed this in Italy and once in England:

> *"Little columns of what looked exactly like smoke were rising over fields and gardens; I took them for smoke at first, but they were in fact clouds of gnats."*

Price acknowledged Cave's explanation as "perhaps the correct one" but noted a complication: the smoke "disappeared instantly" as Hardy and his colleague looked at it, and when they investigated immediately there was no sign of gnats. A real gnat-column would not typically vanish instantly and leave no trace.

No further biographical details about Cave appear in the Borley sources.

## Significance

Cave's contribution represents the category of expert natural-explanation responses that Price received from readers. Unlike many such explanations, Cave's is specific, drawn from personal observation, and belongs to a field of expertise. Price's balanced response — acknowledging it without dismissing the anomaly — is characteristic of his approach to potentially explainable phenomena.
