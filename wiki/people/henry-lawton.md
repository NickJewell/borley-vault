# Reverend Henry Lawton

A clergyman who served as locum tenens at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] during August 1933, while [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] was in Long Melford hospital with heart trouble. By the time [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] interviewed him, Lawton was vicar of St Luke's, Rochdale, and later near Manchester.

## Time at Borley

In August 1933, the 40-year-old Lawton moved into Borley Rectory for a month with his Welsh wife [[irene-lawton|Irene]] and their two children (a boy of nine and a girl of seven) [3]. Marianne Foyster was occupied with running a flower shop. Irene was heavily pregnant at the time; in September 1933, shortly after their stay, she gave birth to their younger daughter, [[esme-lawton|Esme]] — with [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]] as godmother [4, 5].

On his very first day at the Rectory, Lawton discovered [[foyster-manuscripts|Lionel Foyster's manuscript]], *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House* [3, 6]. Alarmed by its contents and protective of his pregnant wife, he deliberately kept the manuscript a secret from his family and asked neighbours not to discuss the local ghost legends with them [3].

**He experienced nothing unusual.** The SPR interviewed Canon Lawton directly and considered him "an extremely reliable and level-headed person and not at all suggestible." Key observations:

- He slept in the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] every night — supposedly the most haunted room — **"not a solitary rap was heard"** [5]
- He frequently went downstairs alone at night by candlelight to the kitchen at the far end of the house, along the "haunted kitchen passage" — and experienced nothing [8, 9]
- [[irene-lawton|His wife]] sat by the open window near the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] at twilight on most evenings, in precisely the conditions under which apparitions had been repeatedly reported — and saw nothing [10, 11]
- The enclosed courtyard created strong acoustic effects: **"sounds in the courtyard echoed loudly inside the rectory"** when children played outside — a key explanation for many observer-period "footsteps" and "raps" [9, 12, 13]
- He noted that the hot, dry weather likely meant rats and mice were living outside in the fields rather than inside the house [9]
- **The cottage was untenanted during Lawton's residency** — and the SPR noted that the frequent "mysterious sounds" reported by official observers in 1937–38 (when the Arbons occupied the cottage) were entirely absent; strong circumstantial evidence that the Arbon household's acoustic signature was being misidentified as paranormal

## The *Spectator* Letter and Price's Reaction

On October 18, 1940, Dr. C. E. M. Joad published a glowing review of Harry Price's *The Most Haunted House in England* in *The Spectator* [1, 14]. Lawton read it and immediately wrote a rebuttal letter, published on November 8, 1940, providing the definitive quote:

> **"During the whole of the time we lived in the house neither I nor my family saw or heard anything out of the ordinary."** [14]

Harry Price was irritated by this public contradiction. In his 1946 sequel, *The End of Borley Rectory*, Price addressed the letter with "asperity," arguing that a month was too short a time to expect phenomena [7, 15]. Curiously, Price completely suppressed Lawton's name in the book and never made any attempt to interview him — an omission the SPR later highlighted as evidence of Price ignoring inconvenient facts [7, 16]. Lawton noted the irony: Price himself had said the best conditions were when a family was living there — and here was a family with children living there for a full month with nothing.

## Lawton's Theories

A keen tennis player who spent much of his Borley vacation at local tournaments, Lawton had socialised heavily with the locals and developed several specific theories regarding the major Borley players [4, 11]:

- **The Foysters:** Lawton only met the Foysters once [17]. He described Lionel as badly crippled by arthritis but respected locally [18]. In direct contradiction to claims of her frailty, he judged [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] to be physically attractive and in **"excellent health"** [19]. He heard village gossip that Marianne had admired jewellery in London that later mysteriously "appeared" on the Borley mantelpiece, leading him to believe she used the poltergeist phenomena as a smokescreen to conceal gifts from secret admirers and other illicit behaviour from her husband [17].

- **The Smiths:** Lawton became friendly with former churchwarden William Bigg of Borley Place [5]. Bigg believed the Rev. Guy Eric Smith had deliberately engineered the haunting rumours to depreciate the Rectory's market value so a friend could buy the land cheaply, with Smith earning a commission [5, 20]. Lawton came to agree, concluding that the Smiths lied to the Bishop, the press, and Harry Price to tarnish the house's reputation — which explained [[mabel-smith|Mrs. Mabel Smith's]] later "about-face" when her religious conscience compelled her to confess there were no ghosts [20].

- **The Bulls:** Lawton became very close to the surviving Bull family [5, 21]. He felt the sisters were "mildly proud" of the family ghost tradition but did not take it seriously in private. He also spoke with their brother, [[gerald-bull|Gerald Bull]], who explicitly stated he was entirely sceptical, had never seen anything abnormal, and viewed the ghostly nun purely as a product of **"feminine imagination"** [21].

## Interviews with the SPR

[[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] met Lawton in January 1942, and Trevor Hall later interviewed him near his parish in Manchester, finding the clergyman shrewd, highly intelligent, level-headed, and possessing a first-rate memory [1].
