# Reverend Henry Lawton

A clergyman who served as locum tenens at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] during August 1933, while [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] was in Long Melford hospital with heart trouble. By the time Trevor Hall interviewed him, Lawton was vicar of St Luke's, Rochdale, and later near Manchester.

## Time at Borley

Lawton, his pregnant wife Irene and two children (boy of 9, girl of 7) lived at the Rectory for the month of August. Irene gave birth to a daughter, Esme, in September — with [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]] as godmother.

**He experienced nothing unusual.** The SPR interviewed Canon Lawton directly and considered him "an extremely reliable and level-headed person and not at all suggestible." Key observations:

- He slept in the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] every night — supposedly the most haunted room — without a single rap
- He frequently went downstairs alone at night by candlelight to the kitchen at the far end of the house, along the "haunted kitchen passage" — and experienced nothing
- His wife sat by the open window near the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] at twilight on most evenings, in precisely the conditions under which apparitions had been repeatedly reported — and saw nothing
- The enclosed courtyard created strong acoustic effects: "sounds in the courtyard echoed loudly inside the rectory" when children played outside — a key explanation for many observer-period "footsteps" and "raps"
- **The cottage was untenanted during Lawton's residency** — and the SPR noted that the frequent "mysterious sounds" reported by official observers in 1937–38 (when the Arbons occupied the cottage) were entirely absent. This is strong circumstantial evidence that the Arbon household's acoustic signature was being misidentified as paranormal

Price dismissed Lawton's *Spectator* letter (1940) with "asperity" in EBR (p. 93) while suppressing his name — the only correspondent whose testimony undermined the case whose name was withheld. Price acknowledged Lawton had stayed a month but argued he "had to wait for phenomena to occur." Lawton noted the irony: Price himself had said the best conditions were when a family was living there — and here was a family with children living there for a full month with nothing.

## Lawton's Analysis

William Bigg (churchwarden) told Lawton privately that he believed Eric Smith had engineered the haunting story to **depreciate the Rectory's value** so that a friend could purchase it cheaply, with Smith earning a commission. The press furore was deliberately inflated to achieve this. Smith's persistent requests for Price's official report, even after leaving, fit this theory.

Lawton himself concluded the Foysters' phenomena was genuine only in so far as Marianne had used the existing legend as a smokescreen for her affair with D'Arles.

He wrote a letter to *The Spectator* (1940) in response to Cyril Joad's review, stating he had found "nothing to fear" at the Rectory — but his letter went unanswered and was quickly forgotten due to the Blitz.
