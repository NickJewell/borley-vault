# Reverend Henry Lawton

A clergyman who served as locum tenens at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] during August 1933, while [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] was in Long Melford hospital with heart trouble. By the time Trevor Hall interviewed him, Lawton was vicar of St Luke's, Rochdale, and later near Manchester.

## Time at Borley

Lawton, his pregnant wife Irene and two children (boy of 9, girl of 7) lived at the Rectory for the month of August. Irene gave birth to a daughter, Esme, in September — with [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]] as godmother.

**He experienced nothing unusual.** The SPR interviewed Canon Lawton directly and considered him "an extremely reliable and level-headed person and not at all suggestible." Key observations:

- He slept in the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] every night — supposedly the most haunted room — without a single rap
- He frequently went downstairs alone at night by candlelight to the kitchen at the far end of the house, along the "haunted kitchen passage" — and experienced nothing
- His wife sat by the open window near the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] at twilight on most evenings, in precisely the conditions under which apparitions had been repeatedly reported — and saw nothing
- The enclosed courtyard created strong acoustic effects: "sounds in the courtyard echoed loudly inside the rectory" when children played outside — a key explanation for many observer-period "footsteps" and "raps"
- **The cottage was untenanted during Lawton's residency** — and the SPR noted that the frequent "mysterious sounds" reported by official observers in 1937–38 (when the Arbons occupied the cottage) were entirely absent. This is strong circumstantial evidence that the Arbon household's acoustic signature was being misidentified as paranormal

Price dismissed Lawton's *Spectator* letter (1940) with "asperity" in EBR (p. 93) while suppressing his name — the only correspondent whose testimony undermined the case whose name was withheld. Price acknowledged Lawton had stayed a month but argued he "had to wait for phenomena to occur." Lawton noted the irony: Price himself had said the best conditions were when a family was living there — and here was a family with children living there for a full month with nothing.

## Lawton's Analysis

William Bigg (churchwarden) told Lawton privately that he believed Eric Smith had engineered the haunting story to **depreciate the Rectory's value** so that a friend could purchase it cheaply, with Smith earning a commission. The press furore was deliberately inflated to achieve this. Smith's persistent requests for Price's official report, even after leaving, fit this theory.

Lawton himself concluded the Foysters' phenomena was genuine only in so far as Marianne had used the existing legend as a smokescreen for her affair with D'Arles.

He wrote a letter to *The Spectator* (1940) in response to Cyril Joad's review, stating he had found "nothing to fear" at the Rectory — but his letter went unanswered and was quickly forgotten due to the Blitz.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Reverend Henry Lawton** (often referred to in the literature as Canon H. Lawton) was the vicar of St Luke’s in Rochdale, Manchester, who served a brief but highly significant tenure as a locum tenens at Borley Rectory [1]. Because his peaceful stay directly contradicted the claims of violent poltergeist activity, he became a crucial witness for the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) in their skeptical 1956 _Borley Report_ [1, 2].

A comprehensive reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following facts, events, quotes, and theories regarding his involvement:

**Biographical Details and the 1933 Borley Stay**
In August 1933, the 40-year-old Lawton moved into Borley Rectory for a month with his Welsh wife, Irene, and their two children (a boy of nine and a girl of seven) [3]. He was filling in for the Rev. Lionel Foyster, who was suffering from heart trouble in Long Melford hospital, while Marianne Foyster was occupied with running a flower shop [3]. Irene Lawton was heavily pregnant at the time, and in September 1933, shortly after their stay, she gave birth to their younger daughter, Esme [4, 5]. 

On his very first day at the Rectory, Lawton discovered Lionel Foyster’s manuscript, _Fifteen Months in a Haunted House_ [3, 6]. Alarmed by its contents and protective of his pregnant wife, he deliberately kept the manuscript a secret from his family and asked neighbors not to discuss the local ghost legends with them [3].

**A Complete Lack of Phenomena**
Despite the Rectory's terrifying reputation, the Lawtons' stay was entirely normal [7]. Lawton later confirmed several key details that undermined the ghost story:
*   **The Blue Room:** The Lawtons slept undisturbed in the notoriously haunted Blue Room for the entirety of their stay [8, 9]. 
*   **Nocturnal Wanderings:** Lawton frequently walked down the "haunted" kitchen passage at the far end of the house in darkness or by candlelight in the middle of the night to fetch drinks for his children, but he encountered nothing untoward [8, 9].
*   **The Nun's Walk:** It was a hot, dry August, and Irene Lawton frequently sat resting and sewing by the open drawing-room window in the twilight. Although she had an unrestricted view of the Nun's Walk at the exact time the phantom was reputed to appear, she never saw anything [10, 11]. 
*   **Environmental Observations:** Lawton noted that the hot weather likely meant rats and mice were living outside in the fields rather than inside the house [9]. He also noticed that the enclosed Rectory courtyard created a peculiar acoustic effect, amplifying outside sounds so that they echoed loudly inside the building [9, 12, 13]. 

**The Spectator Letter and Harry Price's Reaction**
On October 18, 1940, Dr. C. E. M. Joad published a glowing review of Harry Price’s _The Most Haunted House in England_ in _The Spectator_ [1, 14]. Lawton read it and immediately wrote a rebuttal letter, which was published on November 8, 1940 [7, 14]. He provided the definitive quote regarding his stay:
> **"During the whole of the time we lived in the house neither I nor my family saw or heard anything out of the ordinary."** [14]

Harry Price was irritated by this public contradiction. In his 1946 sequel, _The End of Borley Rectory_, Price addressed the letter with "asperity," arguing that a month was too short a time to expect phenomena [7, 15]. Curiously, Price completely suppressed Lawton's name in the book and never made any attempt to interview him, an omission the SPR later highlighted as evidence of Price ignoring inconvenient facts [7, 16].

**Interviews with the SPR and Lawton's Theories**
Eric Dingwall met Lawton in January 1942, and Trevor Hall later interviewed him near his parish in Manchester, finding the clergyman to be shrewd, highly intelligent, level-headed, and possessing a first-rate memory [1]. 

A keen tennis player who spent much of his Borley vacation at local tournaments, Lawton had socialized heavily with the locals and developed several specific, skeptical theories regarding the major Borley players [4, 11]:
*   **The Foysters:** Lawton only met the Foysters once [17]. He described Lionel as badly crippled by arthritis, but respected locally [18]. In direct contradiction to claims of her frailty, he judged Marianne to be physically attractive and in **"excellent health"** [19]. Lawton heard village gossip that Marianne had admired jewellery in London that later mysteriously "appeared" on the Borley mantelpiece, leading him to believe she used the poltergeist phenomena as a smokescreen to conceal gifts from secret admirers and other illicit behavior from her husband [17].
*   **The Smiths:** Lawton became friendly with former churchwarden William Bigg of Borley Place [5]. Bigg believed the Rev. Guy Eric Smith had deliberately engineered the haunting rumors to depreciate the Rectory's market value so a friend could buy the land cheaply [5, 20]. Lawton came to agree, believing the Smiths lied to the Bishop, the press, and Harry Price to tarnish the house's reputation, which explained Mrs. Mabel Smith's later "about-face" when her religious conscience compelled her to confess there were no ghosts [20].
*   **The Bulls:** Lawton became very close to the surviving Bull family; Miss Ethel Bull even became baby Esme's godmother [5, 21]. Lawton felt the sisters were "mildly proud" of the family ghost tradition but did not take it seriously in private [5, 21]. He also spoke with their brother, Gerald Bull, who explicitly stated he was entirely skeptical, had never seen anything abnormal, and viewed the ghostly nun purely as a product of **"feminine imagination"** [21].
