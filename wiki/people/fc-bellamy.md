# F. C. Bellamy (Francis C.)

Son of [[dr-hf-bellamy|Dr. H. F. Bellamy]]; co-observer at Borley Rectory on two occasions: October 16–17, 1937 and May 26, 1938. He officially signed the Haunted House Declaration Form to join Price's rota of observers on July 12, 1937 [2, 3]. Listed as an independent named witness in both Appendix D and Appendix E of *The Most Haunted House in England*, he wrote the covering letters to Price describing the experiments, adding technical detail that his father's observational log alone did not supply.

## October 1937 Visit

Francis accompanied his father and their friend [[hugh-fee-smith|Hugh E. Fee-Smith]] on an overnight vigil on **October 16–17, 1937**. He constructed or helped install an electric bell contact-breaker alarm on the dining-room mantelpiece — a device built by [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] — rigged so that if any book in the supporting pile was displaced, two pieces of metal would close the circuit and ring the bell.

At 12:50 a.m. on October 17, while all three observers were resting quietly in the moonlit Blue Room, the bell started ringing [4]. It rang for about a minute before abruptly stopping [4]. On arriving in the dining-room, they found all the books completely displaced, "not one of them occupying its original position, and the bottom one was right off the contact-breaker." During this same visit, they also noted two fresh pencil marks and heard faint knocking coming from the Base Room [1].

Francis reported this in his covering letter:

> **"The ringing was a very startling experience, occurring as it did during our vigil in the Blue Room. Knowing something about the vagaries of electrical contacts, I should not have attached much importance to the incident had the books not been so greatly displaced and the apparatus moved out of the chalk marks. The fact that it ceased so suddenly also added to our bewilderment. We have omitted to mention in the report that we tested it before we left at 6 a.m. and we found that everything was in working order."** [5]

## May 1938 Visit

The Bellamys returned with Fee-Smith for another overnight stay on **May 26–27, 1938** [6]. Around 1:00 a.m., they found and marked a fresh pencil line in Room No. 7, next to the Blue Room [7].

During this visit, Francis documented tactile phenomena experienced by Fee-Smith. While standing in a short dark corridor leading to the side garden door — the door through which the phantom nun was reputed to enter — Fee-Smith felt someone lightly ruffle his hair at around 7:00 p.m., and later, at around 10:00 p.m., felt someone touch his face [7].

Francis concluded: "Other than this, we got much less 'feeling' in the house than on our previous visit."

## Skeptical Controversy

The October 1937 electric bell incident became a point of contention when skeptical researchers (the authors of the Society for Psychical Research's *Borley Report*) reviewed the case [8]. The critics highlighted a detail that Price had omitted from his published book: a stray cat had entered the house and had been ejected by the men at 6:20 p.m. that evening [9]. The skeptics argued that the cat might have re-entered the house and caused the displacement of the books [9].

> **Contradiction:** The skeptics' cat theory was contested by the Bellamys themselves. When the alarm rang, Bellamy and his party immediately went to the dining room and did not report seeing the animal again [8]. In later years, Dr. Bellamy and his son were noted to have **"totally rejected"** the explanation that a cat was responsible for the phenomenon [10].
