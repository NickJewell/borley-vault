# F. C. Bellamy (Francis C.)

Son of [[dr-hf-bellamy|Dr. H. F. Bellamy]]; co-observer at Borley Rectory on two occasions: October 16–17, 1937 and May 26, 1938. Listed as an independent named witness in both Appendix D and Appendix E of *The Most Haunted House in England*. He wrote the covering letters to Price describing the experiments, adding technical detail that his father's observational log alone did not supply.

## October 1937 Visit

Francis accompanied his father and their friend [[hugh-fee-smith|Hugh E. Fee-Smith]]. He constructed or helped install the electric bell contact-breaker apparatus on the dining-room mantelpiece — a device that would close a circuit and ring a bell if the pile of books supporting it was disturbed.

At 12:50 a.m. on October 17, the bell started ringing while all three observers were resting in the Blue Room. On arriving in the dining-room, they found all the books displaced from their original positions, "not one of them occupying its original position, and the bottom one was right off the contact-breaker." Francis reported this in his covering letter: "The ringing was a very startling experience, occurring as it did during our vigil in the Blue Room. Knowing something about the vagaries of electrical contacts, I should not have attached much importance to the incident had the books not been so greatly displaced and the apparatus moved out of the chalk marks. The fact that it ceased so suddenly also added to our bewilderment."

He also confirmed that the apparatus was tested before leaving at 6 a.m. and found in perfect working order.

## May 1938 Visit

The Bellamys returned in spring 1938 with Fee-Smith. Francis's covering letter documents:

- A pencil line found in Room 7 at about 1 a.m. that "we found and marked."
- A "distinct feeling that someone had ruffled his hair lightly" experienced by Fee-Smith while standing outside two large cupboards in the corridor — and then the same sensation at the same spot two hours later.

He concluded: "Other than this, we got much less 'feeling' in the house than on our previous visit."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Francis C. (F. C.) Bellamy** was an official observer during Harry Price's year-long tenancy investigation of Borley Rectory [1, 2]. The son of Dr. Henry F. Bellamy (who was also an investigator), Francis officially signed the Haunted House Declaration Form to join Price's rota of observers on July 12, 1937 [2, 3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following specific facts, events, dates, quotes, and contradictions regarding his involvement:

**The October 1937 Visit and the Electric Bell Incident**
Francis Bellamy is best known for his participation in an overnight vigil on **October 16–17, 1937**, alongside his father and a friend named Hugh E. Fee-Smith [1, 2]. During this visit, they utilized an electric bell contact-breaker alarm constructed by Sidney Glanville, which they set up on the dining room mantelpiece underneath a pile of books [2]. The apparatus was rigged so that if a book was displaced, two pieces of metal would close the circuit and ring the bell [2]. 

At 12:50 a.m. on October 17, while the three men were resting quietly in the moonlit Blue Room, the bell suddenly started ringing [4]. It rang for about a minute before abruptly stopping [4]. When the party went to investigate the sealed room, they found the books completely displaced, with no book in its original position and the bottom book having been moved entirely off the contact breaker [4]. During this same visit, they also noted two fresh pencil marks and heard faint knocking coming from the Base Room [1]. 

In a covering letter sent to Harry Price accompanying their joint report, Francis Bellamy provided the following specific quote regarding his reaction:
> **"The ringing was a very startling experience, occurring as it did during our vigil in the Blue Room. Knowing something about the vagaries of electrical contacts, I should not have attached much importance to the incident had the books not been so greatly displaced and the apparatus moved out of the chalk marks. The fact that it ceased so suddenly also added to our bewilderment. We have omitted to mention in the report that we tested it before we left at 6 a.m. and we found that everything was in working order."** [5].

**The May 1938 Visit and Tactile Phenomena**
Francis returned to the Rectory with his father and Fee-Smith for another overnight stay on **May 26–27, 1938** [6]. Around 1:00 a.m., they found and marked a fresh pencil line in Room No. 7, which was located next to the Blue Room [7]. 

During this visit, Francis documented tactile phenomena experienced by their companion, Fee-Smith. While standing in a short dark corridor leading to the side garden door (the door through which the phantom nun was reputed to enter), Fee-Smith felt someone lightly ruffle his hair at around 7:00 p.m., and later, at around 10:00 p.m., he felt someone touch his face [7]. 

Francis Bellamy submitted the report on these events, dated May 26, 1938, noting:
> **"We also had a peculiar experience which oddly enough made very little effect on us at the time... Other than this, we got much less 'feeling' in the house than on our previous visit."** [7].

**Skeptical Explanations and Contradictions**
The October 1937 electric bell incident became a point of contention when skeptical researchers (the authors of the Society for Psychical Research's *Borley Report*) reviewed the case [8]. The critics highlighted a detail that Price had omitted from his published book: a stray cat had entered the house and had been ejected by the men at 6:20 p.m. that evening [9]. The skeptics argued that the cat might have re-entered the house and caused the displacement of the books [9]. 

However, the sources note a contradiction to this skeptical theory: when the alarm rang, Bellamy and his party immediately went to the dining room and did not report seeing the animal again [8]. In later years, Dr. Bellamy and his son were noted to have **"totally rejected"** the explanation that a cat was responsible for the phenomenon [10].
