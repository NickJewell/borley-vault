# Rev Edward Lanfranc Morgan Mathias MA

The last rector of Borley and Liston (1955–1967), and husband of co-author [[claudine-mathias]]. He died before the book was written; CM's account provides the only record of his incumbency at this level of detail.

## Background

Born in Hampshire of Welsh descent. Educated at Cambridge (two degrees); reputation as a sportsman (rugby union, badminton). Served curacies at the Church of the Holy Redeemer, Lamorbey, Sidcup, and the Church of St Michael and All Angels, Barnes, Surrey. First living: St Philip's, Sydenham, south-east London, patron being King George VI.

In early 1955, Lanfranc underwent major surgery at Beckenham Hospital, making another busy London living impossible. Canon Edward Powell of Belchamp St Paul suggested the vacancy at Borley and Liston; the Bishop of Southwark recommended the appointment.

## Incumbency at Borley and Liston (1955–1967)

Inducted at Borley Church on **28 July 1955** — the exact 55th anniversary of the famous [[the-nun|Nun]] sighting by the four Bull sisters, and the traditional "appearance date." The bishop was unaware of the significance; a crowd of journalists was waiting outside after the service.

Before the induction, Bishop Faulkner Allison of Chelmsford gave Lanfranc firm but friendly advice: his first loyalty should be pastoral care of his parishioners, not investigation of the paranormal. Lanfranc agreed entirely.

He never initiated discussions about the haunting. However, he said that on occasions when he was "alone" in Borley church he sometimes felt **another presence** there — which he attributed to the cumulative effect of centuries of prayer and worship, declining to claim anything supernatural. He was never a cyclist before Borley; he and CM both learned to cycle for the rural parish.

When the local relief organist Maud Stearn described hearing the organ playing in an empty and locked church (1955), Lanfranc's response was: "It would be best if the children did not hear about this."

He handled many requests from psychical researchers and television companies for access to the church, granting permission to responsible groups and declining to sensation-seekers.

After 12½ years at Borley and Liston, Lanfranc moved to his last living at Gestingthorpe, another nearby north Essex village. He was always in delicate health following his 1955 surgery. After his departure, the church authorities combined Borley and Liston with Foxearth and Pentlow; [[canon-leslie-pennell]] became the first incumbent of the four-parish group.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Reverend Edward Lanfranc Morgan Mathias, MA** was a clergyman who served as the final rector of the combined parishes of Borley and Liston from 1955 to 1967. [1-3] His tenure was marked by a delicate balancing act between managing the intense public interest in the "most haunted house in England" and maintaining normal pastoral duties. [4, 5] Much of the biographical record concerning him comes from his widow, Claudine Mathias, who co-authored the book *Borley Rectory – The Final Analysis*. [2, 6]

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, facts, dates, and quotes:

**Early Life and Career**
*   **Background and Education:** Born in Hampshire, he was descended from a Welsh family. [3] His lifelong ambition was to join the clergy. [3] He studied at Cambridge, where he earned two degrees and built a well-deserved reputation as a sportsman, specifically excelling in rugby union and badminton. [3] He subsequently attended Cheshunt Theological College. [3]
*   **Marriage and Family:** He married Claudine on **July 3, 1946**. [3] They had a son and two daughters. [3, 7]
*   **Clerical Career:** Following his ordination, his first curacy was at the Church of the Holy Redeemer in Lamorbey, Sidcup, Kent, followed by a second curacy at the Church of St Michael and All Angels in Barnes, Surrey. [3] He received his first living as the vicar of St Philip’s, Sydenham, a busy parish in south-east London, where the patron of the living was King George VI. [7]

**The Move to Borley**
*   **First Contact:** Mathias and his wife first became acquainted with Borley during a month-long locum at nearby Belchamp St Paul. [8] Having read Harry Price's book *The Most Haunted House in England* at the local library, they visited the tiny church and the remains of the Rectory. [8] 
*   **Severe Illness (1955):** In early 1955, while the Bishop of Southwark was considering offering him a second living, Mathias became extremely ill and underwent major surgery at Beckenham Hospital. [8] Though he survived, he was left in delicate health and required lifelong medication, making another busy London parish impossible. [8]
*   **Induction:** Seeking a quieter life, he was recommended for the vacancy at Borley and Liston. [8] He was formally inducted by Dr. Faulkner Allison, Bishop of Chelmsford, on **July 28, 1955**. [3, 9] Ironically, the Bishop selected this date blissfully unaware that it was exactly the **55th anniversary** of the famous phantom nun sighting by the Bull sisters in the Rectory garden in 1900. [9] 

**Life at Borley and Liston**
*   **Residence:** Like his predecessor, Rev. A. C. Henning, Mathias chose not to live in Borley but resided in the older Liston Rectory. [10-12] The house had no mains electricity and relied on an old generator; Mathias took personal responsibility for cleaning and maintaining the family's collection of oil lamps. [12] 
*   **Pastoral Life:** He never learned to drive a car and was a keen cyclist. [13] After completing his pastoral visits, he would often rest in Borley Church before cycling home. [13] Despite his dedication, he was pettily barred from giving voluntary religious instruction at the nearby Foxearth Church of England Primary School by its authoritarian headmistress, Miss Edna Backhouse. [14]

**Attitude Towards the Haunting**
*   **The Bishop's Warning:** At his induction, the Bishop of Chelmsford firmly warned Mathias that his primary loyalty must be to his parishioners and not to investigating the paranormal, noting that the previous incumbent had spent too much time on the supernatural. [9]
*   **Personal Beliefs:** Mathias fully agreed with the Bishop and kept his thoughts on the haunting "close to his chest" to avoid controversy. [4] However, he did privately believe in the haunting, telling his wife that on several occasions when he was "alone" in Borley Church, he felt the distinct sensation of another presence. [4]
*   **Managing Investigators:** Mathias acted as a gatekeeper for the haunted site. He used his discretion to grant permission to genuine researchers wanting to hold night vigils or take photographs, while trying to turn away rowdy sensation-seekers who were increasingly causing vandalism to the church and the graves of the Bull family. [5]

**Specific Incidents and Quotes**
*   **The Organ Incident (1955):** Shortly after Mathias took the living, a relief organist named Mrs. Maud Stearn arrived at Borley Church to find the organ inexplicably playing by itself. When she told local residents about the empty church filled with music, Rev. Mathias's protective reaction regarding his family was to state: **"It would be best if the children did not hear about this."** [15, 16]
*   **The Parchment Discovery:** Sometime in the late 1950s, a visitor, Mrs. Phyllis Cowan-Douglas, found an old piece of parchment with mysterious writing lying directly on top of the Waldegrave tomb in an otherwise empty church. When she wrote to Rev. Mathias about the strange object, he confessed that he **"had not heard of such a happening in the church before."** [17]

**Departure and Legacy**
*   After serving for twelve and a half years, Rev. Mathias resigned from the Borley living in **1967** to take up a new post at Gestingthorpe. [1, 18] 
*   Because the Church authorities subsequently combined Borley and Liston with the parishes of Foxearth and Pentlow (appointing Canon Leslie Pennell to oversee all four), Rev. Mathias holds the historical distinction of being **the last rector to administer Borley and Liston as their own independent combined parish**. [1, 18]
