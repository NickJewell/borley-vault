# Geoffrey H. Motion

Geoffrey H. Motion was a neighbour and friend of [[harry-price]] [1, 2], and the father of Barry Motion, who later provided testimony to researchers regarding his father's views on the haunting [1]. Motion is most notable for accompanying Price on the final day of his rented tenancy of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], and for co-discovering one of the haunting's most striking claimed [[apports]].

## The Final Visit (May 9–10, 1938)

Price asked Motion to assist with moving belongings from the Rectory specifically because Motion owned a "particularly large and roomy" car [2]. On 9 May 1938, Motion and Price travelled to Borley, arriving at approximately 4:00 p.m. to collect Price's equipment, tidy up the [[base-room]], and hand back the keys [2].

Throughout the evening, the two men searched the house from top to bottom several times to ensure nothing was left behind, paying special attention to the floors. They reportedly searched the notoriously active [[blue-room]] half a dozen times [2].

## The Gold Wedding Ring Discovery

At midnight (officially recorded as 12:05 a.m. on May 10), they made their final inspection of the Blue Room using a powerful lamp and a torch [2, 3]. Price noted that the first thing they saw was something shining on the floor. **Motion ran over and picked it up** [2].

It was a nearly new twenty-two-carat gold woman's wedding ring, hallmarked 1864 — the year after the Rectory was built. Because it had inexplicably appeared in a room they had just searched, Price classified it as a "golden apport" [3, 4]. Price made many inquiries about the ring but never found an owner.

> **Cross-reference:** The Foyster diary records a wedding ring in the bathroom as early as 10 March 1931 — that ring disappeared the next morning. Whether the two rings were the same object cannot be established.

Following the discovery, Motion and Price made another minute search for further apports but found none. The rest of the night was "deadly still and quiet" with no other phenomena. They left Borley shortly after 4:00 a.m. and arrived in London around 6:00 a.m. [5].

The ring was later psychometrized by [[ian-aitchison|Lieutenant Ian Aitchison]], whose reaction was immediate and violent: he dropped it and shouted "Murder!" refusing to touch it again.

## Views on Price and the Haunting

During the posthumous controversy surrounding Harry Price, critics alleged that Price deliberately faked phenomena at Borley. Geoffrey Motion firmly believed in Price's integrity. According to a personal communication from his son Barry Motion to researcher [[robert-hastings|Robert J. Hastings]], Geoffrey Motion was "convinced that he [Price] did not fake phenomena" [1].

The authors of the critical 1956 *Borley Report* ([[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], and [[trevor-hall]]) used the incident with Motion to highlight what they viewed as the "slender" and "worthless" nature of Price's evidence — pointing out that even regarding this "golden apport," Price was forced to admit that "the ring found its way into the Blue Room in a perfectly normal manner" [6].

## Significance

Motion's involvement is a footnote to Price's investigation, but his presence at the ring discovery makes him one of the two witnesses (with Price) to what Price considered among the most striking apports in the Borley record — appearing at the precise moment of departure, after an exhaustive search, in the most-investigated room in the house.
