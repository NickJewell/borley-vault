# Annie Pearson

Daily cleaning woman at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] during the [[foyster-phenomena|Foyster period]]; mother of Winifred Arbon (née Pearson) who was caretaker during the Price tenancy; and aunt of [[mary-pearson|Mary Pearson]], the Smiths' maid and early witness. She lived at **Place Farm Cottage** across the road from the Rectory for many decades.

> **Contradiction:** Some sources describe Mary Pearson as Annie's daughter [2, 3]; others describe her as Annie's niece [2, 3]. The *Borley Rectory Companion* gives the aunt relationship. Note also that in the 1947 BBC radio play by [[alan-burgess|Alan Burgess]], the character representing Mary Pearson was inexplicably renamed "Annie" [4].

## Role at the Rectory

When [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]] and his young wife [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] moved into the Rectory in October 1930, their income had been diminished by the Depression and they could not afford live-in servants [2]. Instead, they hired Annie Pearson as a "daily woman" who came in three or four afternoons a week to undertake the scrubbing and harder domestic work [2]. The rigid social etiquette of the era dictated that when the well-to-do matrons of the parish left their calling cards between 3 p.m. and 6 p.m., they had to leave them with Mrs. Pearson at the door, rather than hand them directly to Marianne [6].

## Encounters with the Phenomena

During the worst periods of poltergeist activity, Lionel relied on Annie Pearson as a trusted neighbour. When Marianne collapsed after the failed blessing of the house, Lionel rushed to Borley Place to get brandy from Mrs Bigg while Mrs Mitchell came to sit with Marianne. Lionel worried that Pearson might "spread more gossip around the village."

On one occasion, Mrs Mitchell (the Rectory Cottage tenant) was sitting with Marianne in the sewing room when she commented on the footsteps she could clearly hear overhead: "How plainly this house does sound. You can hear Mr Foyster walking about upstairs." Marianne, knowing Lionel was in the library, said nothing.

**The apparition of Harry Bull**: During the Foyster incumbency, Marianne claimed to have seen an old man in the Rectory garden. When Marianne described the man's distinctive clothing to Annie Pearson, Mrs. Pearson's face reportedly turned "a kind of greenish white," as she immediately recognised the description as matching the late [[harry-bull|Rev. Harry Bull]] [7].

## Keeper of the Keys

When [[harry-price|Harry Price]] rented the empty Rectory for a year of investigation from 1937 to 1938, Annie Pearson's daughter and son-in-law (the [[mr-and-mrs-arbon|Arbons]]) acted as the primary keyholders [8]. Whenever the Arbons were away, Price entrusted one of the three sets of Rectory keys to Mrs. Pearson to provide access to his official observers [8].

## Churchwarden Duties and Paranormal Experiences at the Church

In her later years, Annie Pearson served as churchwarden for [[borley-church|Borley Church]] [9]. Despite her scepticism regarding the Rectory, she personally experienced several inexplicable incidents inside the locked church:

- **The moving wick and book**: It was Annie's duty to light the small wick of the altar's sanctuary lamp each morning and extinguish it at night [10, 11]. For about a fortnight (around April 1942), she found the wick had been frequently moved [10, 11]. The Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|A.C. Henning]] suggested she cover the lamp glass with a psalter, and later a detached book cover. Despite the church being locked, she found these heavy covers inexplicably thrown to the floor the next morning [10, 11]. Henning suspected a bird or bat, but Annie kept a strict lookout and never found one inside the building [11].
- **The smashed lamp and moving register**: She and Mrs. Gooch found the altar lamp inexplicably smashed on two separate occasions [9]. They also noticed a church register had been moved several times; to test this, they hid the book before locking up for the night, only to find it moved several yards away from its hiding place the following morning [9].
- **The phantom bell (Low Sunday, April 24, 1949)**: The Rev. Henning had cancelled the 8:00 a.m. Communion service at Borley [1]. Unaware of this, Annie and her husband distinctly heard the church bell ring at that exact time, prompting Annie to remark, "It's Communion morning" [1, 12]. Assuming the Rector had rung it, she left the church locked [1]. When Henning arrived that evening and asked why the doors were locked, Annie was astounded, insisting, "But you were there at eight o'clock, both my husband and I heard the bell!" [1].

## Opinion on the Rectory Haunting

When interviewed by [[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]] and [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] during their investigation for the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]], Annie Pearson expressed a very dim view of the entire Borley story. In all her years there, neither she nor her husband had ever seen or experienced anything paranormal at the Rectory itself [3]. She maintained that the Smiths left because the house was too big and too expensive, and that Mrs Foyster had produced much of the phenomena herself [3]. She also confirmed that Marianne was often absent from the Rectory for weeks at a time — running the flower shop [[jonquille-et-cie|Jonquille et Cie]] with [[francois-darles|Francois D'Arles]] in Wimbledon [3].

The authors of the highly critical *Borley Report* explicitly highlighted Annie Pearson's complete absence from Harry Price's books, arguing that Price deliberately ignored her testimony — despite the fact that she worked in the Rectory every day for years and lived directly across the street — because she fundamentally questioned the notion of paranormal activity at the site [13].
