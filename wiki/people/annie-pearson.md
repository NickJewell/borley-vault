# Annie Pearson

Daily cleaning woman at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] during the [[foyster-phenomena|Foyster period]]; mother of Winifred Arbon (née Pearson) who was caretaker during the Price tenancy; and mother of [[mary-pearson|Mary Pearson]], the Smiths' maid and early witness. She lived at **Place Farm Cottage** across the road from the Rectory for many decades.

## Role at the Rectory

Annie Pearson came in three or four afternoons a week to do the heavier domestic work — scrubbing and cleaning — that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] needed help with, as the Foysters could not afford live-in servants. This regular presence made her an intimate witness to daily life at the Rectory during 1930–35.

## Encounters with the Phenomena

During the worst periods of poltergeist activity, Lionel relied on Annie Pearson as a trusted neighbour. When [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] collapsed after the failed blessing of the house, Lionel rushed to Borley Place to get brandy from Mrs Bigg while Mrs Mitchell came to sit with Marianne. Annie Pearson was aware of the strange happenings but did not appear to be alarmed by them. When Lionel raised the concern about how Marianne would appear if she sent for Pearson, he worried she might "spread more gossip around the village."

On one occasion, Mrs Mitchell (the Rectory Cottage tenant) was sitting with Marianne in the sewing room when she commented on the footsteps she could clearly hear overhead: "How plainly this house does sound. You can hear Mr Foyster walking about upstairs." Marianne, knowing Lionel was in the library, said nothing.

## Opinion on the Haunting

When interviewed by [[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]] and [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] during their investigation for the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]], Annie Pearson expressed a very dim view of the entire Borley story. In all her years there, neither she nor her husband had ever seen or experienced anything at all. She maintained that the Smiths left because the house was too big and too expensive, and that Mrs Foyster had produced much of the phenomena herself. She also confirmed that Marianne was often absent from the Rectory for weeks at a time — running the flower shop [[jonquille-et-cie|Jonquille et Cie]] with [[francois-darles|Francois D'Arles]] in Wimbledon.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Annie Pearson** (frequently referred to in the sources as **Mrs. Pearson**) was a local Borley resident who lived at Place Farm Cottage, situated near the church and directly opposite the Borley Rectory site [1-3]. She was a key, albeit highly skeptical, witness to the events at the Rectory and the church. 

A thorough reference profile of her involvement in the Borley saga includes the following specific facts, events, and contradictions:

**Family Connections and the BBC Broadcast**
Annie Pearson was the aunt of Mary Pearson, the teenage maid who famously claimed to see a phantom coach and a headless man during the incumbency of the Rev. Guy Eric Smith [2, 3]. *(Note: In a confusing 1947 BBC radio play by Alan Burgess about the haunting, the character representing Mary Pearson was inexplicably renamed "Annie" [4]).* Annie’s daughter, Winifred Arbon, also became intertwined in the Borley story when she and her husband lived in the Rectory Cottage and acted as caretakers during Harry Price's tenancy [5].

**Employment by the Foysters**
When the Rev. Lionel Foyster and his young wife Marianne moved into the Rectory in October 1930, their income had been diminished by the Depression, and they could not afford live-in servants [2]. Instead, they hired Annie Pearson as a "daily woman" [2]. She came in three or four afternoons a week to undertake the scrubbing and harder domestic work [2]. 

The rigid social etiquette of the era dictated that when the well-to-do matrons of the parish left their calling cards between 3 p.m. and 6 p.m., they had to leave them with Mrs. Pearson at the door, rather than hand them directly to Marianne Foyster [6]. 

**Reaction to the Apparition of Harry Bull**
During the Foyster incumbency, Marianne claimed to have seen an old man in the Rectory garden. When Marianne described the man's distinctive clothing to Annie Pearson, Mrs. Pearson's face reportedly turned "a kind of greenish white," as she immediately recognized the description as matching the late Rev. Harry Bull [7].

**Keeper of the Keys**
When Harry Price rented the empty Rectory for a year of investigation from 1937 to 1938, Annie Pearson's daughter and son-in-law (the Arbons) acted as the primary keyholders [8]. However, whenever the Arbons were away, Price entrusted one of the three sets of Rectory keys to Mrs. Pearson to provide access to his official observers [8].

**Churchwarden Duties and Paranormal Experiences**
In her later years, Annie Pearson served as the churchwarden for Borley Church [9]. Despite her skepticism regarding the Rectory, she personally experienced several inexplicable incidents inside the locked church:
*   **The Moving Wick and Book:** It was Annie's duty to light the small wick of the altar's sanctuary lamp each morning and extinguish it at night [10, 11]. For about a fortnight (around April 1942), she found the wick had been frequently moved [10, 11]. The Rev. A.C. Henning suggested she cover the lamp glass with a psalter, and later a detached book cover. However, despite the church being locked, she found these heavy covers inexplicably thrown to the floor the next morning [10, 11]. Henning suspected a bird or bat, but Annie kept a strict lookout and never found one inside the building [11]. 
*   **The Smashed Lamp and Moving Register:** She and Mrs. Gooch found the altar lamp inexplicably smashed on two separate occasions [9]. They also noticed that a church register had been moved several times; to test this, they hid the book before locking up for the night, only to find it moved several yards away from its hiding place the following morning [9].
*   **The Phantom Bell (Low Sunday, April 24, 1949):** The Rev. Henning had cancelled the 8:00 a.m. Communion service at Borley [1]. Unaware of this, Annie and her husband distinctly heard the church bell ring at that exact time, prompting Annie to remark, "It's Communion morning" [1, 12]. Assuming the Rector had rung it, she left the church locked [1]. When Henning arrived that evening and asked why the doors were locked, Annie was astounded, insisting, "But you were there at eight o'clock, both my husband and I heard the bell!" [1]. 

**Skepticism, Contradictions, and the S.P.R. Investigation**
Despite her experiences in the church, Annie Pearson was a staunch and vocal skeptic of the Rectory hauntings, and her testimony became a vital weapon for critics attempting to debunk Harry Price [1, 13]. 

When interviewed by Society for Psychical Research (S.P.R.) investigators Mollie Goldney and Eric Dingwall, she expressed a "very dim view of the whole Borley story" [3]. She insisted that in all the years she and her husband lived directly opposite the Rectory, they had **never seen or experienced anything paranormal** [3]. 

Her testimony directly contradicted the claims of both the Smiths and the Foysters:
*   **The Smiths:** She insisted that the Rev. Eric Smith and his wife left the house simply because it was far too big and too expensive for them to run, not because they were chased out by ghosts [3].
*   **The Foysters:** Annie believed that Marianne Foyster was desperate to get away from Borley and therefore **produced much of the ‘phenomena’ herself** [3]. She noted that Marianne frequently neglected her ailing husband and children, disappearing to London for weeks at a time to run a flower shop with her lodger, François d'Arles [3].

**The Omission by Harry Price**
The authors of the highly critical *Borley Report* explicitly highlighted Annie Pearson's complete absence from Harry Price's books. They argued that Price **deliberately ignored her testimony**—despite the fact that she worked in the Rectory every day for years and lived directly across the street—because she fundamentally questioned the notion of paranormal activity at the site, which did not fit Price's sensational narrative [13].
