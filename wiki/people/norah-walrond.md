# Norah Walrond

(Born Bedford, 2 August 1907; died 1980s) Born Norah Aileen Burke. A Member of Harry Price's [[ghost-club]], Number 453. Contributed to many magazines and published several novels; in 1954 she won a New York World Short Story contest.

## Connection to Borley

Walrond knew [[borley-rectory]] and almost all of its occupants for more than twenty years — a rare long-standing personal connection spanning the key periods of the haunting.

Despite this extensive familiarity, she never had any paranormal experience until 1947 when, visiting [[borley-church]] accompanied by the rector [[rev-ac-henning|Alfred Henning]] — who knew there should be no one inside the church — they both heard organ music when halfway up the path.

On investigation the building was silent and empty. They experimented by walking up the path several times but heard nothing. It was literally seconds between the time they heard the music and the rector entering the church to investigate; certainly insufficient time for anyone to have escaped without being seen.

## The Ivy Brackenbury Story

Norah Walrond also interviewed Constance (née Brackenbury) Boothby, Harry Bull's stepdaughter, at the Ellenborough Hotel, Cheltenham in 1945. In a letter dated 29 June 1945, Walrond recorded Constance's account of the skull found in the Rectory library — explaining it as a burial bone from an overcrowded Borley churchyard that had been sent to Harry Bull for reinterment, and had simply been overlooked after his death.

## Residence

Norah Walrond lived for many years at Thorne Lodge, Cockfield Green, only a few miles from Borley, which explains her sustained personal knowledge of the case participants over decades.

## Significance

Walrond's organ music experience at Borley Church in 1947 — heard independently and simultaneously with [[rev-ac-henning|Henning]] — is one of the better-attested post-war phenomena reports, coming from a long-term local resident with no prior expectation of experiencing anything unusual at a church she knew well.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mrs. Norah Walrond** (who also wrote and published under her maiden name, **Norah Aileen Burke**) was an accomplished novelist and a member of Harry Price's Ghost Club. Although she was familiar with Borley Rectory and almost all of its occupants for more than twenty years, she had absolutely no paranormal experiences of her own until a striking incident in 1947 [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the texts reveals the following biographical details, specific events, dates, and quotes regarding her involvement:

**Biographical Details and Background**
*   **Life Dates:** She was born in Bedford on **August 2, 1907**, and passed away in the 1980s [1]. 
*   **Residence:** For many years, she lived at **Thorne Lodge, Cockfield Green** (sometimes spelled Cookfield), which was situated only a few miles from Borley [1, 2]. 
*   **Career and Achievements:** As a successful author, she contributed to many magazines and published a number of novels [1]. Notably, she was the winner of a **New York World Short Story Contest in 1954** [1, 3]. 
*   **Affiliations:** She was an active member of Harry Price's Ghost Club, holding membership number 453 [1].

**The 1945 Interview and the "Skull in the Cupboard"**
Mrs. Walrond played a key role in documenting the mundane explanation for one of the Rectory's morbid curiosities: the human skull discovered in a library cupboard by Mabel Smith. 
*   In 1945, Mrs. Walrond interviewed **Constance Boothby** (the stepdaughter of the late Rev. Harry Bull) at the Ellenborough Hotel in Cheltenham [4]. 
*   In a letter dated **June 29, 1945**, Mrs. Walrond recorded Mrs. Boothby's highly practical explanation for the skull's presence, providing the following quote: 
> **"I asked her for the history of the skull sent by post to her mother. Here it is and there is nothing particularly remarkable about it. Borley Churchyard, being old, contains many ancient unmarked graves, and human bones are sometimes turned up by grave-diggers. These used to be sent to Harry Bull for re-interment. The skull in question was brought to the Rectory when he was very ill. In consequence it got put out one side. Then he died, and when Constance and her mother left the Rectory, it got left behind in a cupboard. Later it was found and forwarded to Mrs Harry Bull by parcel post! She returned it, with instructions that it was to be buried again in the churchyard. That is the whole story."** [4]

**The Phantom Organ Music (October 1947)**
Mrs. Walrond's own firsthand paranormal experience occurred entirely across the street from the ruined Rectory, centering instead on Borley Church. 
*   On the afternoon of **October 15, 1947** (just before 3:00 p.m.), she visited the church with the local rector, the **Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning**, specifically to view the recently re-dedicated stone altar (the *mensa*) [3, 5]. 
*   As they walked about halfway up the path to the church door, Mrs. Walrond suddenly hung back and stated, **"Someone is playing the organ"** (or, in another account, **"The organ is playing!"**) [2, 3, 5]. The rector also clearly heard the music, noting that it sounded like a voluntary [6].
*   Knowing that no service was scheduled and the regular organist was away teaching school, the rector went to the door. Upon entering, they found the building completely deserted, totally silent, and the old hand-pumped organ securely locked [2, 3, 5]. 
*   To test for acoustic anomalies, they walked up and down the church path several times but heard nothing except the hum of two distant tractors and an aeroplane [2]. They agreed that if someone had been inside playing, they could not have possibly escaped unseen in the few seconds it took the rector to enter the building [1, 3].

**Direct Quote Regarding the Music**
The event was reported in the *East Anglian Daily Times* on October 24, 1947, which prompted Mrs. Walrond to write a formal independent account of her experience in a letter dated **October 27, 1947** [2, 7]. She described the auditory phenomenon with the following specific quote:
> **"It was a warm, still afternoon. Half-way up the path to the church door I heard organ music... The sound was just like the diapason of an organ. It sounded like an organ being played, as it might while the collection is being taken, or waiting for a bride, very soft and indistinct... When we got inside the church, it was absolutely quiet. If the music had been inside, someone could not have got out of the building without our seeing him."** [2]
