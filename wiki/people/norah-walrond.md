# Norah Walrond

(Born Bedford, 2 August 1907; died 1980s) Born **Norah Aileen Burke**; also published under her maiden name. A Member of Harry Price's [[ghost-club]], Number 453. Contributed to many magazines and published several novels; won a New York World Short Story contest in 1954. For many years she lived at Thorne Lodge, Cockfield Green (a few miles from Borley), which explains her sustained personal knowledge of case participants over decades.

## Connection to Borley

Walrond knew [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] and almost all of its occupants for more than twenty years — a rare long-standing personal connection spanning the key periods of the haunting. Despite this extensive familiarity, she never had any paranormal experience until 1947.

## The 1945 Interview and the Skull in the Cupboard

Mrs Walrond played a key role in documenting the mundane explanation for one of the Rectory's morbid curiosities. In 1945, she interviewed **Constance Boothby** (née Brackenbury; stepdaughter of the late Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]]) at the Ellenborough Hotel in Cheltenham. In a letter dated **June 29, 1945**, Mrs Walrond recorded Mrs Boothby's practical explanation for the skull found in the Rectory library cupboard by [[mabel-smith|Mabel Smith]]:

> "I asked her for the history of the skull sent by post to her mother. Here it is and there is nothing particularly remarkable about it. Borley Churchyard, being old, contains many ancient unmarked graves, and human bones are sometimes turned up by grave-diggers. These used to be sent to Harry Bull for re-interment. The skull in question was brought to the Rectory when he was very ill. In consequence it got put out one side. Then he died, and when Constance and her mother left the Rectory, it got left behind in a cupboard. Later it was found and forwarded to Mrs Harry Bull by parcel post! She returned it, with instructions that it was to be buried again in the churchyard. That is the whole story."

## The Phantom Organ Music (October 1947)

On the afternoon of **October 15, 1947** (just before 3:00 p.m.), Walrond visited [[borley-church|Borley Church]] with the local rector, the **Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|Alfred Clifford Henning]]**, to view the recently re-dedicated stone altar. As they walked about halfway up the path to the church door, Mrs Walrond suddenly hung back and stated, **"Someone is playing the organ"** (or, in another account, "The organ is playing!"). The rector also clearly heard the music, noting it sounded like a voluntary.

Knowing that no service was scheduled and the regular organist was away teaching school, the rector went to the door. Upon entering, they found the building completely deserted, totally silent, and the old hand-pumped organ securely locked. To test for acoustic anomalies, they walked up and down the church path several times but heard nothing except the hum of two distant tractors and an aeroplane. They agreed that if someone had been inside playing, they could not have possibly escaped unseen in the few seconds it took the rector to enter the building.

The event was reported in the *East Anglian Daily Times* on October 24, 1947, prompting Mrs Walrond to write a formal independent account in a letter dated **October 27, 1947**:

> "It was a warm, still afternoon. Half-way up the path to the church door I heard organ music... The sound was just like the diapason of an organ. It sounded like an organ being played, as it might while the collection is being taken, or waiting for a bride, very soft and indistinct... When we got inside the church, it was absolutely quiet. If the music had been inside, someone could not have got out of the building without our seeing him."

## Significance

Walrond's organ music experience at Borley Church in 1947 — heard independently and simultaneously with [[rev-ac-henning|Henning]] — is one of the better-attested post-war phenomena reports, coming from a long-term local resident with no prior expectation of experiencing anything unusual at a church she knew well (multiple witnesses).
