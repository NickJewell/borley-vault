# Arthur Henry Foyster

Brother of [[lionel-foyster|Revd Lionel Algernon Foyster]]. Interviewed by [[peter-underwood]] at his home in Pinner when he was 86 years old.

## Knowledge of the Haunting

Arthur Foyster said he had no personal knowledge of the alleged haunting at [[borley-rectory]], but had been kept up to date by frequent "newsletters" — the [[diary-of-occurrences]] — which Lionel sent round the family. These were the documents Lionel initially wrote to share with relatives, and Arthur confirmed this purpose of the *Diary of Occurrences* to Underwood.

He had no doubt, in view of everything Lionel and [[marianne-foyster]] told him, that the Rectory was indeed haunted.

## Post-Borley Contact

After Lionel's death, Arthur and one or two other family members kept in touch with Marianne, whom he had always liked. He believed — as she herself stated in a letter to Peter Underwood — that she had been "in the wrong place at the wrong time."

## Family Connection

Arthur possessed items that came from [[borley-rectory]], including a wooden inkwell carved by [[harry-bull]].

He acted as an intermediary for an original letter from Underwood to Marianne (Foyster) O'Neil, who at that time did not wish to discuss Borley with anyone.

## Significance

Arthur Foyster's testimony is important as independent corroboration of the *Diary of Occurrences*' original purpose (family newsletters, not published diaries), and as confirmation that the wider Foyster family took the haunting seriously based on Lionel's personal communications. His charitable view of Marianne stands in contrast to [[trevor-hall]] and [[robert-wood]]'s more hostile assessments.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Arthur Henry Foyster** was the brother of the Rev. Lionel Algernon Foyster. He lived in Pinner, Middlesex, and was interviewed by psychical researcher Peter Underwood at his home when he was 86 years old [1, 2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, and biographical details about his connection to the case:

**Lack of First-Hand Experience but Firm Belief**
Arthur freely admitted that he had no personal, first-hand knowledge of the alleged hauntings at Borley Rectory [1, 3]. He was, however, kept meticulously informed of the events through frequent "newsletters" that Lionel circulated among the family (these notes later formed the basis of Lionel's *Diary of Occurrences*) [1, 4]. Based entirely on the testimony provided by Lionel and Marianne Foyster, Arthur maintained **"no doubt" that the Rectory was indeed haunted** [1]. 

**Support for Marianne Foyster**
In stark contrast to the severe criticism levelled at Marianne by many investigators and neighbors, Arthur always liked his sister-in-law [1]. Following Lionel’s death, Arthur and one or two other family members were among the few who kept in touch with her [1]. Arthur sympathized with her position, believing—as Marianne herself stated in a letter to Peter Underwood—that she had simply been **"in the wrong place at the wrong time"** [1]. Years later, when Marianne was living in America (as Mrs. O'Neil) and refusing to discuss Borley with anyone, Arthur acted as a trusted intermediary to pass an original letter to her from Peter Underwood [1].

**The Professor Cook Contradiction and Investigation**
Arthur’s correspondence provides a fascinating behind-the-scenes look at how his brother Lionel reacted to skeptics. In late August 1937, investigator Sidney Glanville wrote to Arthur seeking information about the Foyster incumbency [3]. In his reply on August 28, Arthur agreed to forward the request to Lionel, but also shared a revealing lesser-known detail [3, 5]. 

Arthur confided that he had met a Professor Cook of Cambridge in Aldeburgh. Professor Cook had conducted a very thorough investigation at Borley during the period when the phenomena were supposedly highly active, but came away with "great disappointment," concluding that **"there was nothing in it"** [5, 6]. Arthur added a secretive postscript to Glanville: **"I never mentioned to my brother that I met Prof. Cook as I understand that Cook’s conclusions very much annoyed him"** [5]. 

Despite his lack of first-hand haunting experiences, Arthur did take a proactive role in investigating one specific sighting. In 1936, in the company of Professor Cook, Arthur made a point of personally meeting a woman acquaintance of his brother's to discuss her experience [7]. This woman had visited Borley and been told by Marianne that Lionel was out of the house. The visitor replied, "You must be mistaken; I saw him in an upstairs room – he was wearing a dressing-gown" [7]. (This figure in the dressing-gown was frequently identified by Marianne as the ghost of the late Rev. Harry Bull [7]).

**Preservation of Borley Artifacts**
Arthur possessed several historical items from Borley Rectory, which he eventually presented as gifts to Peter Underwood [1]. Most notably, these included:
*   **Lionel Foyster's manuscript for *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*** [8].
*   **A wooden inkwell that had been hand-carved by the Rev. Harry Bull** [1].

**Lesser-Known Family Connection**
The sources also record a minor biographical detail regarding Arthur's children: his daughter was friends with Helen Carter (née Glanville) [9]. Helen was the daughter of investigator Sidney Glanville and the person who operated the planchette board that produced the famous "Sunex Amures" and "Marie Lairre" séance messages [9, 10].
