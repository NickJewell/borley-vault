# Arthur Henry Foyster

Brother of [[lionel-foyster|Revd Lionel Algernon Foyster]]. Lived in Pinner, Middlesex; interviewed by [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] at his home when he was 86 years old [1, 2].

## Knowledge of the Haunting

Arthur freely admitted that he had no personal, first-hand knowledge of the alleged haunting at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] [1, 3]. He was, however, kept meticulously informed of the events through frequent "newsletters" that Lionel circulated among the family — these notes later formed the basis of Lionel's *[[foyster-manuscripts|Diary of Occurrences]]* [1, 4]. Arthur confirmed to Underwood that this was the *Diary of Occurrences*' original purpose: family newsletters, not published diaries. Based entirely on the testimony provided by Lionel and [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]], Arthur maintained "no doubt" that the Rectory was indeed haunted [1].

## The Professor Cook Contradiction

Arthur's correspondence provides a fascinating behind-the-scenes look at how his brother Lionel reacted to sceptics. In late August 1937, investigator [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] wrote to Arthur seeking information about the Foyster incumbency [3]. In his reply on August 28, Arthur confided that he had met a **Professor Cook of Cambridge** in Aldeburgh [5]. Professor Cook had conducted a very thorough investigation at Borley during the period when the phenomena were supposedly highly active, but came away with "great disappointment," concluding that "there was nothing in it" [5, 6]. Arthur added a secretive postscript to Glanville: "I never mentioned to my brother that I met Prof. Cook as I understand that Cook's conclusions very much annoyed him" [5].

Despite his lack of first-hand haunting experiences, Arthur took a proactive role in investigating one specific sighting. In 1936, in the company of Professor Cook, Arthur personally met a woman acquaintance of his brother's to discuss her experience. This woman had visited Borley and been told by Marianne that Lionel was out of the house. The visitor replied, "You must be mistaken; I saw him in an upstairs room — he was wearing a dressing-gown" [7]. (This figure in the dressing-gown was frequently identified by Marianne as the ghost of the late [[harry-bull|Rev. Harry Bull]] [7].)

## Support for Marianne Foyster

In stark contrast to the severe criticism levelled at Marianne by many investigators and neighbours, Arthur always liked his sister-in-law [1]. Following Lionel's death, Arthur and one or two other family members were among the few who kept in touch with her [1]. Arthur sympathised with her position, believing — as Marianne herself stated in a letter to Peter Underwood — that she had simply been "in the wrong place at the wrong time" [1].

Years later, when Marianne was living in America (as Mrs. O'Neil) and refusing to discuss Borley with anyone, Arthur acted as a trusted intermediary to pass an original letter to her from Peter Underwood [1].

## Family Connection

Arthur possessed items from [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], including:
- Lionel Foyster's manuscript for *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House* [8]
- A wooden inkwell carved by [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] [1]

He presented these as gifts to Peter Underwood. His daughter was also friends with Helen Carter (née Glanville) — the daughter of investigator [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] and the person who operated the planchette board that produced the famous "[[sunex-amures|Sunex Amures]]" and "[[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]" séance messages [9, 10].

## Significance

Arthur Foyster's testimony is important as independent corroboration of the *Diary of Occurrences*' original purpose (family newsletters, not published diaries), and as confirmation that the wider Foyster family took the haunting seriously based on Lionel's personal communications. His charitable view of Marianne stands in contrast to [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] and [[robert-wood|Robert Wood]]'s more hostile assessments.
