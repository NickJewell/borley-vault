# Ivan Banks (8 August 1950 – May 2000)

Author of *The Enigma of Borley Rectory* (1994/1996), published from Maidstone, Kent; later residing in Maidenhead. A lay researcher (by his own description "without a scientific education") who conducted approximately ten years of original archival research into the historical background of the [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] case. Described by his peers as a friendly and likeable man who unfortunately **never enjoyed the best of health** [1, 4]. Generally pro-[[harry-price|Price]], strongly influenced by the [[hast|Hastings Report]] of 1969.

## Research

Banks's research involved:
- Harry Price Library, London University (with [[ah-wesencraft|A.H. Wesencraft's]] assistance)
- Institute of Historical Research, London
- National Archives and Public Record Office
- Essex County Archives, Chelmsford
- Suffolk Record Office, Bury St Edmunds
- Department of Paleography and Diplomatic, Durham University
- Archives in France: Seine Maritime (Rouen), Mairie du Havre, Département de l'Eure (Evreux), Archive du France (Paris)
- Belgian National Archives and Rijksarchief te Antwerpen
- Multiple Catholic conventual archives including Stanbrook Abbey, Downside Abbey, Darlington Carmel, Oulton Abbey, Kylemore, St Scholastica's Teignmouth, Colwich
- Canterbury Cathedral Library and Archives
- Consulted Professor Bernard Knight (Cardiff University Medical School) on bone fragments and [[kate-boreham|Kate Boreham]]'s possible death from lead poisoning
- Countess Mary Waldegrave for [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]] conventual records

The editor of his book praised his **"sheer tenacity, persistence, and dedication"** in completing Harry Price's quest to investigate the conventual records of the haunting [5].

## Key Original Findings

1. Henry Dawson Bull died from **Locomotor Ataxia** (a manifestation of tertiary syphilis) — documented by death certificate
2. **[[kate-boreham|Kate Boreham's]]** death was probably due to lead poisoning (sugar of lead as abortifacient) rather than the stated "cerebritis" — Banks stated emphatically: **"the Katie Boreham episode is at the root of psychic disturbances at the Rectory"** [7]
3. [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Sir Edward Waldegrave]] is a plausible ghost candidate — betrayed to Elizabeth I's authorities in 1561, died in the Tower; séance "Tfismong" = "Forster the fishmonger" from the *Calendars of State Papers*
4. The likely identity of the ghost nun is **[[barbara-waldegrave|Barbara Waldegrave (Sister Apollonia)]]** — born at Borley 1603, died Brussels 1638 aged 36, whose age matches the jawbone excavated by Price in 1943 [6]
5. Captain Gregson's son Anthony confirmed that his father torched the Rectory for the insurance

## Additional Contributions

**Floor plans:** Banks prepared new drawings of the Rectory for *The Enigma*, including detailed elevations for the first time [12]. Modern researchers note a contradiction: his floor plans are described as **"somewhat simplistic and less detailed"** than Sidney Glanville's original 1937 plans on which they were based [12].

**Rectory gate posts:** Banks tracked down the last surviving original timber driveway gate posts from Borley Rectory [4]. In 1984, he purchased them for **£20** from the owner of a bungalow built on the former rectory lawn, took the 7-foot posts back to Maidenhead, gave them a liberal coat of preservative, and **"they took pride of place in his garden for some years"** [4]. Following his death in 2000, their current whereabouts are unknown [4, 13].

**The Herringham theory:** Banks suggested that the previous rector, Rev. John Herringham, chose to live at Borley Place instead of the older rectory building because **the old Rectory had already become the site of paranormal activity** [9].

## Limitations

Banks is not an academic historian and his theories, while well-researched, remain speculative. His framework interpreting séance results as factual evidence of historical events is unverified. He strongly favours Price and Hastings throughout.

## Stance on the Borley Controversy

Banks was firmly in the "pro-paranormal" camp. He ardently defended Harry Price against the accusations of fraud levelled by the 1956 SPR *Borley Report*, strongly supporting [[hast|Robert J. Hastings']] earlier defence of Price [2]. He was highly critical of SPR investigator [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]], and is recorded as one of the key figures who "raised their eyebrows" and openly questioned the ethics of Hall taking personal possession of [[locked-book|Sidney Glanville's *Locked Book of Private Information*]] [14].
