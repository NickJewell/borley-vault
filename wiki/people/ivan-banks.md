# Ivan Banks

Author of *The Enigma of Borley Rectory* (1994), published from Maidstone, Kent. A lay researcher (by his own description "without a scientific education") who conducted approximately ten years of original archival research into the historical background of the [[Borley Rectory|Borley Rectory]] case. Generally pro-[[Harry Price|Price]], strongly influenced by the [[Robert Hastings|Hastings Report]] of 1969.

## Research

Banks's research involved:
- Harry Price Library, London University (with A.H. Wesencraft's assistance)
- Institute of Historical Research, London
- National Archives and Public Record Office
- Essex County Archives, Chelmsford
- Suffolk Record Office, Bury St Edmunds
- Department of Paleography and Diplomatic, Durham University
- South Tyneside Metropolitan Borough Council archives
- Archives in France: Seine Maritime (Rouen), Mairie du Havre, Département de l'Eure (Evreux), Archive du France (Paris)
- Belgian National Archives and Rijksarchief te Antwerpen
- Multiple Catholic conventual archives including Stanbrook Abbey, Downside Abbey, Darlington Carmel, Oulton Abbey, Kylemore, St Scholastica's Teignmouth, Colwich
- Canterbury Cathedral Library and Archives
- Consulted Professor Bernard Knight (Cardiff University Medical School) on bone fragments and Kate Boreham's possible death from lead poisoning
- Mrs. D. Davies of Shrewsbury Meole Brace for Harry Price's Shropshire connections
- Countess Mary Waldegrave for Waldegrave family conventual records

## Key Original Findings

1. Henry Dawson Bull died from **Locomotor Ataxia** (a manifestation of tertiary syphilis) — documented by death certificate
2. **[[kate-boreham|Kate Boreham's]]** death was probably due to lead poisoning (sugar of lead as abortifacient) rather than the stated "cerebritis"
3. [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Sir Edward Waldegrave]] is a plausible ghost candidate — betrayed to Elizabeth I's authorities in 1561, died in the Tower; séance "Tfismong" = "Forster the fishmonger" from the *Calendars of State Papers*
4. The likely identity of the ghost nun is **[[barbara-waldegrave|Barbara Waldegrave (Sister Apollonia)]]** — born at Borley 1603, died Brussels 1638 aged 36
5. Captain Gregson's son Anthony confirmed that his father torched the Rectory for the insurance

## Limitations

Banks is not an academic historian and his theories, while well-researched, remain speculative. His framework interpreting séance results as factual evidence of historical events is unverified. He strongly favors Price and Hastings throughout.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Ivan Banks (August 8, 1950 – May 2000)** was a dedicated lay researcher and author whose extensive investigations into the historical aspects of the Borley hauntings culminated in his major 1996 book, *The Enigma of Borley Rectory* [1-3]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following biographical details, research theories, and specific contributions to the Borley literature:

**Biographical Details and Authorship**
*   **Personal Life:** Described by his peers as a friendly and likeable man who unfortunately **never enjoyed the best of health**, Banks resided in Maidenhead [1, 4]. 
*   **The Enigma of Borley Rectory (1996):** His comprehensive survey of the Borley saga took him **ten years to research**, relying heavily on original material from the Harry Price papers at the University of London [2]. The editor of his book praised his **"sheer tenacity, persistence, and dedication"** in completing Harry Price's quest to investigate the conventual records of the haunting [5]. 

**Key Historical Theories and Conclusions**
Banks focused heavily on the historical and genealogical roots of the haunting, utilizing seance transcripts and public records to formulate several unique theories:
*   **The Identity of the Phantom Nun:** Rejecting the popular "Marie Lairre" theory, Banks postulated that the famous phantom nun was actually **Barbara Waldegrave** (born 1603), the daughter of Sir Nicholas Waldegrave of Borley [6]. She entered a Brussels community in 1622 and took the name Apolonia [6]. Because the jawbone excavated by Harry Price in 1943 belonged to a woman in her thirties, Banks reasoned she was a candidate, as Barbara Waldegrave died in Belgium in 1638 at the age of 38 [6].
*   **The Katie Boreham Mystery:** Banks did considerable work on the mystery of Kate Boreham (a maid to the Bull family who died in 1888), concluding there was a cover-up regarding her death and burial [7]. He stated emphatically: **"the Katie Boreham episode is at the root of psychic disturbances at the Rectory"** [7]. He believed the troubles of the Bull family and her fate were intrinsically linked [8]. 
*   **The Herringham Rectory:** When considering why the previous rector, Rev. John P. Herringham, chose to live at Borley Place instead of the older rectory building, Banks suggested it was because **the old Rectory had already become the site of paranormal activity** [9]. He also concluded that a chaplain's house or rectory existed before Borley Place was built, stating it **"seems almost certain to have been PULLED DOWN to make way for Herringham's Rectory"** [10]. 
*   **The Rev. Harry Bull:** Banks suggested that Harry Bull's outwardly cheerful and ebullient countenance **"may have concealed an inner sense of disillusionment"** [11].

**Drawings and Preservation of Borley Relics**
*   **Rectory Floor Plans:** For *The Enigma of Borley Rectory*, Banks prepared new drawings of the house, which included detailed elevations for the first time [12]. While he used Sidney Glanville's original 1937 plans as a basis, modern researchers note a contradiction in their quality, describing Banks's floor plans as being **"somewhat simplistic and less detailed than the originals"** [12].
*   **The Rectory Gate Posts:** Banks successfully tracked down the last surviving original timber driveway gate posts from Borley Rectory [4]. In 1984, he purchased them for **£20** from the owner of a bungalow built on the former rectory lawn [4]. He took the 7-foot posts back to Maidenhead, gave them a liberal coat of preservative, and they **"took pride of place in his garden for some years"** [4]. Following his death in 2000, their current whereabouts are unknown [4, 13].

**Stance on the Borley Controversy**
Banks was firmly in the "pro-paranormal" camp. He ardently defended Harry Price against the accusations of fraud leveled by the 1956 SPR *Borley Report*, strongly supporting Robert J. Hastings' earlier defense of Price [2]. He was highly critical of SPR investigator Trevor Hall; the sources specifically record Banks as one of the key figures who **"raised their eyebrows"** and openly questioned the ethics and legality of Hall taking personal possession of Sidney Glanville's *Locked Book of Private Information* [14].
