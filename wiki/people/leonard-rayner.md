# Leonard G. Rayner

Borley investigator active during the 1950s. A photographer's assistant who lived in Long Melford, he participated in excavations in the company of [[len-sewell]] and [[philip-paul]].

## Role at Borley

Rayner took many photographs during the time [[philip-paul]] was digging at Borley (1954–56) and at times supervised much of the work. [[steuart-kiernander]] regarded him as "the best man involved in the Paul diggings."

## Significance

Rayner represents the category of dedicated local investigators who provided logistical and practical support to the more well-known figures like Paul and Sewell. His photographic record of the 1950s excavations forms part of the visual documentation of this phase of the Borley investigation, though the whereabouts of these photographs today is unknown.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Leonard G. Rayner** was a photographer's assistant and a dedicated investigator during the "Later Borley" period in the 1950s [1]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following biographical details, contradictions, and specific contributions regarding his involvement in the excavations of the haunted site:

**Biographical Details and Contradictions**
*   **Residence:** The sources present a contradiction regarding where Rayner lived during his investigations. One text records that he was a photographer's assistant who lived in **Long Melford** [1], while another specifically identifies him as **"Mr. L. G. Rayner of Ipswich"** [2]. 
*   **Reputation:** He was highly regarded by his peers. The veteran investigator Steuart Kiernan specifically considered Rayner to be **"the best man involved in the Paul diggings"** [1]. 

**The Philip Paul Excavations (1954–1956)**
Rayner was one of the three main participants—alongside the journalist Philip Paul and Leonard (Len) Sewell—in the extensive excavations of the ruined Rectory site that began in 1954 [3]. Rayner took many of the photographs documenting the dig between 1954 and 1956, and at times he personally supervised much of the excavation work [1].

**The Independent Cellar Excavations (1956–1957)**
When Philip Paul abandoned his investigation in August 1956, Rayner and Sewell took over the project [4]. 
*   Working methodically at weekends, Rayner and Sewell spent the next year completely excavating the Rectory cellars [4, 5]. 
*   They finally concluded their "painstaking and patient labours" in the autumn of 1957 [6]. 
*   While they did not discover any human remains or the legendary missing church plate [5, 6], their archaeological efforts were highly successful. They unearthed old walls, drains, and pipes of varying ages, providing solid physical evidence that an older building had previously stood on the site [5, 6].

**The 1957 Tunnel Exploration**
Rayner played a very direct, hands-on role in exploring the subterranean tunnel that was accidentally uncovered by rural contractors digging a trench in Hall Lane on September 12, 1957 [7]. 
*   On the day after the discovery, Rayner and Sewell traced the tunnel into the adjacent farmyard, finding the roof just inches below the surface [8]. 
*   The texts note that **"Mr. Rayner quickly removed some bricks and inspected the interior"** [8]. 
*   Finding a collapsed section blocking their way, they cleared out the earth and stones, which allowed them to physically traverse the length of the tunnel under the road [8]. 
*   Rayner and Sewell carefully examined the tunnel's construction (noting it was built of red Tudor bricks) and discovered a distinct dip that followed the contour of the land above, leading them to firmly rule out the theory that the tunnel was merely a large storm drain [8, 9]. 
*   They also dug on the opposite side of the farmyard wall into the garden of Borley Place, uncovering more bricks and confirming the tunnel's trajectory toward the cellars of that house [10]. 
*   Following their thorough exploration of the passage, Rayner and Sewell covered the opening and filled in the hole [11].
