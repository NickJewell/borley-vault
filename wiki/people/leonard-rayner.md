# Leonard G. Rayner

Borley investigator active during the 1950s. A photographer's assistant who participated in excavations in the company of [[len-sewell|Len Sewell]] and [[philip-paul|Philip Paul]].

> **Contradiction:** The sources present conflicting information on where Rayner lived during his investigations. One text records him as a photographer's assistant who lived in **Long Melford**, while another specifically identifies him as **"Mr. L. G. Rayner of Ipswich."**

## Role at Borley

Rayner took many photographs during the time [[philip-paul|Philip Paul]] was digging at Borley (1954–56) and at times personally supervised much of the excavation work. [[steuart-kiernander|Steuart Kiernan]] regarded him as "the best man involved in the Paul diggings."

When Paul abandoned his investigation in August 1956, Rayner and [[len-sewell|Sewell]] took over the project. Working methodically at weekends, they spent the next year completely excavating the Rectory cellars, finally concluding their "painstaking and patient labours" in the autumn of 1957. While they did not discover any human remains or the legendary missing church plate, their archaeological efforts were highly successful — they unearthed old walls, drains, and pipes of varying ages, providing solid physical evidence that an older building had previously stood on the site.

## The 1957 Tunnel Exploration

Rayner played a very direct, hands-on role in exploring the subterranean tunnel accidentally uncovered by rural contractors in Hall Lane on September 12, 1957. On the day after the discovery, Rayner and Sewell traced the tunnel into the adjacent farmyard. The texts note that **"Mr. Rayner quickly removed some bricks and inspected the interior."** Finding a collapsed section blocking their way, they cleared out the earth and stones, allowing them to physically traverse the length of the tunnel under the road. Rayner and Sewell carefully examined the construction (noting it was built of red Tudor bricks), discovered a distinct dip that followed the contour of the land above — leading them to firmly rule out the theory that the tunnel was merely a large storm drain — and dug on the opposite side of the farmyard wall into the garden of [[borley-place|Borley Place]], confirming the tunnel's trajectory toward the cellars of that house. Following their thorough exploration, they covered the opening and filled in the hole.

## Significance

Rayner represents the category of dedicated local investigators who provided logistical and practical support to the more well-known figures like Paul and Sewell. His photographic record of the 1950s excavations forms part of the visual documentation of this phase of the Borley investigation, though the whereabouts of these photographs today is unknown.
