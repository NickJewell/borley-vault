# Johnnie Palmer

A farm worker at Borley Place employed by the [[payne-family]] who witnessed the August 1943 excavation in the Borley Rectory cellars and maintained throughout his life that the bones Jackson dug up were from a pig, not a human being.

## Witness to the 1943 excavation

Johnnie Palmer was present when [[jackson-labourer]] dug the bones from the cellar floor on 17 August 1943 — the excavation that produced the fragment of skull and jawbone later identified as human by pathologist [[dr-eric-bailey]] and subsequently reburied as [[marie-lairre]] at Liston Churchyard in May 1945.

[[andrew-clarke]] records Palmer's testimony in essay 15:

> *Johnnie Palmer, who worked at Borley Place for the Paynes, observed the dig. He saw the bones and maintained the unshakeable view that what he saw were 'awd Sows Bunz' (old sows bones).*
> — *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, essay 15

## The pig-bones controversy

Palmer's view aligns with that of Jackson himself, who initially identified the jawbone as a pig's jawbone before it was corrected by Dr Bailey. Clarke notes that Jackson "was 'ribbed' quite a bit by his colleagues about mistaking a human jawbone for a pigs' jawbone, but maintained all his life that what he picked out of the ground was from a pig."

Both Palmer and Jackson were experienced farm workers from an agricultural community. As Clarke observes: "For a farmworker, mistaking pigs' bones for human bones would have been an elementary mistake" — and yet both made the same identification independently. Clarke uses this to raise the possibility that the switch from pig to human bones occurred when Harry Price passed the bone from Jackson to Dr Bailey, the pre-arranged pathologist.

## Significance

Palmer is one of two eyewitnesses to the 1943 excavation (alongside Jackson) who disputed the identification of the bones as human. His testimony is central to Clarke's analysis of whether Harry Price planted the bones or took advantage of a fortuitous find. Neither the SPR Report nor Ivan Banks gives his testimony.

> **Contested:** Dr Eric Bailey formally identified the jawbone as human. The SPR investigators accepted this identification. Clarke presents Palmer and Jackson's consistent alternative view as evidence consistent with — though not proof of — planting.
