# F. A. Mansbridge (and Mrs Mansbridge)

Official of the Bank of England [1, 2]; one of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s official observers during the 1937–38 [[borley-rectory|Borley]] tenancy. Both Mansbridge and his wife visited the Rectory and made independent contributions to the record. A minor typographical inconsistency appears in the sources: while official reports identify them clearly as "Mr. and Mrs. F. A. Mansbridge," Harry Price's comprehensive appendix list mistakenly records their names as **"Mansfield, Mr. F. A."** and **"Mansfield, Mrs. F. A."** [6].

## The September 1937 Visit

The Mansbridges arrived at 7:30 p.m. on Sunday 5 September 1937, departing in the early morning of Tuesday 7 September 1937 [2–4]. During this time, they shared the house with another of Price's principal observers, [[mark-kerr-pearse|Mark Kerr-Pearse]], who showed them around upon their arrival [3, 5].

## Mrs Mansbridge at the Cold Spot

On the evening of 5 September, while the couple was standing on the first-floor landing talking with Kerr-Pearse near the famous "cold spot" outside the [[blue-room|Blue Room]], **Mrs Mansbridge felt the end of her coat belt lifted and dropped again by an unseen hand** [1, 3, 5].

In his official log, Mr Mansbridge recorded:

> "The material is too heavy to be lifted by any ordinary draught, and the movement was so definite as to make her look down at it." [3]

Harry Price found this detail highly significant. He classified it as a "tactual clue" and argued that the action — adjusting a piece of clothing — **"suggests that a feminine entity was trying to attract the attention of a human of her own sex,"** characterising it specifically as a "woman's gesture" [1]. He notes that this type of physical contact was specifically characteristic of the Borley entities' interactions with women (see also [[lady-whitehouse]], whose parasol and gloves were moved).

## The Open Car Door

Approximately half an hour after the coat belt event, the couple returned to their car — parked in the drive and out of sight from the main road — and found **the car door standing open, even though they were "quite sure" they had shut it** [3].

## The "Spirit Photo" Sequel (January 1938)

Mr Mansbridge had taken several photographs inside the Rectory but did not develop them for several months [4]. On 9 January 1938, he sent Price a letter containing a negative and prints of the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] [4]. In the centre of the wall, within a recess to the right of the mantelpiece and about 18 inches from the floor, **the photograph appeared to show a "face" on the distemper** [4].

Harry Price dismissed this pragmatically: the "face" was not visible when looking at the actual wall, concluding it was caused by **"normal irregularities on the Blue Room wall, plus a certain subconscious elaboration of details in the mind of the person looking at the picture"** — a phenomenon now known as pareidolia [4].

## Significance

The Mansbridge case is a small but useful double-entry: the "spirit photograph" is a typical example of negative evidence (Price's refusal to accept pareidolia as paranormal), while Mrs Mansbridge's physical experience is a minor positive datum in the record of tactual phenomena.
