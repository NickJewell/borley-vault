# F. A. Mansbridge (and Mrs Mansbridge)

Official of the Bank of England; one of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s official observers during the 1937–38 Borley tenancy. Both Mansbridge and his wife visited the Rectory and made independent contributions to the record.

## The Blue Room "Spirit Photograph"

Before the publication of *The Most Haunted House in England*, F. A. Mansbridge wrote to Price saying that while printing films he had taken of the [[blue-room|Blue Room]], he noticed "a distinct 'face'" on the wall of the recess to the right of the mantelpiece. He sent both print and negative.

Price's verdict: the "face" was due to normal irregularities on the Blue Room wall — "primarily to normal irregularities ... plus a certain subconscious elaboration of details in the mind of the person looking at the picture." He examined the actual wall and found no face visible. This is Price's standard treatment of pareidolic face-recognition in photographs.

## Mrs Mansbridge at the Cold Spot

Mrs F. A. Mansbridge's experience is separately noted in Chapter XIII. While standing at the cold spot outside the Blue Room on September 5, 1937:

> *"My wife felt the end of the belt of her coat lifted and dropped again. The movement was so definite as to make her look down at it."*

Price regards this as a characteristic "feminine gesture" — an entity touching a woman's garment to attract attention — and notes that this type of physical contact was specifically characteristic of the Borley entities' interactions with women (see also [[lady-whitehouse]], whose parasol and gloves were moved). Price suggests this tactual phenomenon supports the inference that the communicating entity was itself female.

## Significance

The Mansbridge case is a small but useful double-entry: Mansbridge's "spirit photograph" is a typical example of negative evidence (Price's refusal to accept pareidolia as paranormal), while Mrs Mansbridge's physical experience is a minor positive datum in the record of tactual phenomena.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Mr. F. A. Mansbridge and Mrs. Mansbridge** were a husband and wife who served as "official observers" during Harry Price’s year-long investigative tenancy of Borley Rectory. While their time at Borley was brief, it produced one of the case's most specific and personal "tactual" (tactile) phenomena, as well as a curious photographic anomaly. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following facts, events, dates, quotes, and minor contradictions regarding their involvement:

**Biographical Details and the September 1937 Visit**
A lesser-known biographical detail recorded in the texts is that **Mr. F. A. Mansbridge was an official of the Bank of England** [1, 2]. He and his wife visited the Rectory over a single weekend, arriving at 7:30 p.m. on **Sunday, September 5, 1937**, and departing in the early morning of **Tuesday, September 7, 1937** [2-4]. During this time, they shared the house with another of Price's principal observers, Mark Kerr-Pearse, who kindly showed them around upon their arrival [3, 5]. 

**The "Tactual Clue" (The Coat Belt Incident)**
The Mansbridges are best known in the Borley records for a physical interaction Mrs. Mansbridge experienced shortly after arriving. On the evening of September 5, while the couple was standing on the first-floor landing talking with Kerr-Pearse near the famous "cold spot" outside the Blue Room, **Mrs. Mansbridge felt the end of her coat belt lifted and dropped again by an unseen hand** [1, 3, 5]. 

In his official log of the visit, Mr. Mansbridge recorded the specific nature of the event:
> **"The material is too heavy to be lifted by any ordinary draught, and the movement was so definite as to make her look down at it."** [3]. 

Harry Price found this detail highly significant. He classified it as a "tactual clue" and argued that the action—adjusting or pulling a piece of clothing—**"suggests that a feminine entity was trying to attract the attention of a human of her own sex,"** characterizing it specifically as a "woman's gesture" [1]. 

**The Open Car Door**
A secondary, lesser-known incident occurred approximately half an hour after the coat belt event. Mr. Mansbridge recorded in his log that when the couple returned to their car—which had been parked in the drive and out of sight from the main road—**they found the car door standing open, even though they were "quite sure" they had shut it** [3]. 

**The "Spirit Photo" Sequel (January 1938)**
There was a curious sequel to the Mansbridges' visit involving a potential "spirit photograph." Mr. Mansbridge had taken several photographs inside the Rectory but did not develop them for several months [4]. On **January 9, 1938**, he sent Price a letter containing a negative and prints of the Blue Room [4]. 

In the center of the wall, within a recess to the right of the mantelpiece and about 18 inches from the floor, **the photograph appeared to distinctly show a "face" on the distemper** [4]. However, Harry Price pragmatically dismissed this anomaly as a visual illusion [4]. Price noted that the "face" was not visible when looking at the actual wall, concluding that it was caused by **"normal irregularities on the Blue Room wall, plus a certain subconscious elaboration of details in the mind of the person looking at the picture"** (a phenomenon known as pareidolia) [4]. 

**Contradictions in the Record**
A minor contradiction or typographical error exists in the Borley records regarding their name. While their official reports and references in the main text identify them clearly as "Mr. and Mrs. F. A. Mansbridge" [2, 3, 5], Harry Price's comprehensive list of witnesses in the appendix mistakenly records their names as **"Mansfield, Mr. F. A."** and **"Mansfield, Mrs. F. A."** [6].
