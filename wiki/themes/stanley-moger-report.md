# Stanley Moger's Surveyor's Report on Borley Rectory (1938)

A professional condition and valuation report on [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], prepared by [[stanley-moger|Stanley Moger OBE FAI FCIA]], a local surveyor based at the Estate Office, Halstead, Essex. Dated 26 May 1938 and addressed to the Ecclesiastical Commissioners at 1 Millbank, Westminster. Commissioned by the Commissioners via the [[rev-ac-henning|Rev Alfred Clifford Henning]], who was attempting to sell the property on their behalf following the conclusion of [[harry-price|Harry Price's]] year-long investigative tenancy on 19 May 1938 [1, 2].

## Physical Description of the Rectory

The report provides a systematic specification of the rectory's condition at a date nine months before it burned down:

**Exterior:**
- Well-built red brick and slated residence
- Drive of a semi-circular nature with gates in and out; trees on both sides rendered the building dark
- Verandah on east side: "much dilapidated, part down and dangerous"
- Greenhouse, heating and potting sheds on south side: same condition

**Interior (ground floor):** Ceiling height 10ft 9in. Rooms: entrance porch, inner hall, dining room, study/library (Price's "[[base-room|base room]]"), drawing room, two storerooms and pantry, servants' hall, kitchen, larder, dairy, scullery.

**Interior (first floor):** Ceiling height 9ft 6in. Rooms: main landing with two side corridors, ten bedrooms, a dressing room, a chapel with a stained glass window in the small tower with a boxroom above it, a bathroom, a lavatory.

**Courtyard:** Another lavatory, knife house, coal store, refuse pit, and a "geared hand pump for forcing water up into the storage tanks... very old pattern and quite out of date."

**Outbuildings:** Including the rectory cottage (described as a "garage or coach house with a first floor flat" in the stable yard), in fairly good external condition but "sadly needs bringing up to date." Cold frames "without lights, in a poor state of repair and hardly worth the cost to put it right."

**Drainage:** Into a cesspit "some distance from the main building, which appeared to be satisfactory." Water from a deep well.

The report also included a rare photograph taken from the lawn showing both the Rectory and the tower of [[borley-church|Borley Church]] in the same view [4]. Modern researchers, such as Paul Adams, have used the dimensions recorded in Moger's inventory to help produce accurate floor plans of the Rectory [5].

Authors Edward Babbs and Claudine Mathias characterise the ethos of the report as being "as depressing and gloomy as the building which it described," noting that it would be impossible to accuse Moger of employing "hard selling" tactics [2, 6].

## The Haunting Reference

The most extraordinary feature of the report is its explicit mention of the paranormal — Babbs and Mathias point out that it "must be rare indeed for a surveyor's report to refer to an alleged haunting" [6]. The report contains an unusual passage under a section headed "Local Rumour":

> "The Rectory is supposed to be HAUNTED and a few years ago was the Hunting Ground of many hundred of Spiritualists and Inquisitive persons, in fact, so many visited the site that the Police had to handle the matter. This, as you may imagine, is a considerable detriment to selling, as many view but turn away on this account." [7]

## Valuation and Recommendation

- Rateable value: £24 gross / £17 net (described as "sympathetic... owing to being a Rectory")
- Reserve recommended: **£450**
- Prediction: "If not disposed of within a reasonable time, I fear the end will be unfortunately as is the case today with large, out-of-date Residences — Demolition for the value of the Materials after cost of pulling down" [7]

The rectory was ultimately sold by private sale to [[captain-gregson|Capt. Gregson]] for approximately £500 in December 1938 (sources differ on the exact figure, with some citing £350). It was destroyed by [[fire-1939|fire]] less than nine months after the report was written. The grim prediction proved remarkably accurate: after the Rectory was gutted, it was sold to a demolition contractor who dismantled the ruins and sold off salvageable bricks and timber [7, 8].

## Significance

The Moger report is unique as the only professional surveyor's assessment of the building made before its destruction. It is rare in surveying history for such a document to mention a building's alleged paranormal reputation as a factor affecting value. The report also confirms the absence of modern utilities: no electricity, no gas, a hand pump for water, and drainage to a cesspit.
