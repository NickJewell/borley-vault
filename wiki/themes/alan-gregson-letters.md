# Alan Gregson Letters (1985)

Two letters written from Australia (Tasmania) in 1985 by [[alan-gregson|Alan Gregson]], one of the two sons of [[captain-gregson|Capt William Hart Gregson]] (the owner of Borley Rectory when it was destroyed by [[fire-1939|fire]] in 1939). Written in response to inquiries from researcher Richard Lee-Van den Daele, the letters provide the most direct account from the Gregson family of their time at Borley and are the primary evidence against [[robert-wood|Robert Wood]]'s claim that Capt Gregson committed arson.

## First Letter (16 July 1985)

Key facts:
- The family purchased the rectory because "the prospect of a 'proper' house was appealing" after years in cottages and bungalows
- The purchase price was **£350** — not the £500 reported in many other accounts
- They **never slept or ate in the rectory itself** but lived in the flat above the coach-house
- On being warned by [[rev-ac-henning|Henning]] that the house was "supposed to be very haunted," they laughed
- **The fire was accidental**: Capt Gregson was in the hall sorting books when "a stack of books suddenly keeled over onto the lamp, overturning it, breaking the glass and spilling kerosene which then caught alight." He sent the boys to the telephone at [[borley-green|Borley Green]] to call the Sudbury fire brigade.
- **Carriage tracks in snow** were found one winter morning, running from the front of the rectory down the lawn past the cedar tree to the shrubbery, appearing to "lift-off" there; no physical access for any vehicle larger than a wheelbarrow led to that part of the grounds.
- **Two dogs**: Peter (a spaniel-retriever cross) panicked in the courtyard and ran away toward [[long-melford|Long Melford]], never to be seen again. Some weeks later they obtained a second dog; it also vanished.
- Capt Gregson received 3 guineas for a 3-minute BBC broadcast about the fire

## Second Letter (18 October 1985)

Expanding on the fire:
- The family lived in the flat above the coach-house pending redecoration of the rectory; most furniture was stacked in the rectory entrance hall and stairwell
- The oil lamp in use was "an argand-type brass table-lamp... not a pressure lamp"
- The fire centred on the main staircase
- Mysterious figures seen in the fire: Alan Gregson confirms these were widely reported but **he himself did not see them**. He notes the fire brigade captain pointed out a human-like form in an upstairs window where no floor remained — but adds "I don't know how much subjective thinking may have prompted that belief"
- The family planned to start a [[psychic-fete-1939|"Psychic Fête"]] in the grounds of the ruined rectory in 1939 with [[harry-price|Harry Price]] as a guest
- Capt Gregson later approached coach companies about organised Borley tours for his own profit

## Significance

The Gregson letters resolve the most contentious practical question about the fire: whether it was started deliberately for insurance fraud, as [[robert-wood|Robert Wood]] asserts in *The Widow of Borley*. Alan Gregson's detailed and specific account — the overturning lamp, the choice to live in the coach-house flat, the valuable irreplaceable possessions lost — provides strong circumstantial evidence that the fire was accidental. Richard Lee-Van den Daele felt that Alan came across in his letters as "a man of great integrity" possessing an "immense memory" capable of recalling minute details from over fifty years prior [5]. Edward Babbs and Claudine Mathias use the letters to argue that if Gregson had planned to burn the Rectory, he would not have left his valuable library, furniture, and other irreplaceable possessions inside to be destroyed [15].

> **Contradiction:** Alan's account is entirely contradicted by his brother, Anthony Hart Gregson, who bluntly stated in his own correspondence that their father bought the Rectory as a real estate venture and, when that failed, deliberately "torched it for the insurance" [7, 9, 10]. Alan countered by claiming Anthony suffered from an "overexposure to James Cagney" — implying he was prone to cinematic dramatization [11].

> **Contested:** [[robert-wood|Robert Wood]] and insurance records state definitively that the insurers believed Gregson had "fired the place himself." Sir William Crocker (the insurance company's lawyer) reportedly described it as "an impudent claim" for £7,356 — and loss adjuster Colonel Cuthbert Buckle branded Gregson a "man of straw" whose supposedly valuable ruined clothes were merely threadbare rags [3, 12, 13]. An out-of-court settlement of £750 was reached. The matter was never tested in court.

Author Ivan Banks argues that Alan's letters actually highlight suspicious behaviour: Banks questions why *both* boys were sent to the telephone — a trip taking roughly 20 minutes — leaving the Captain alone, and points out that stacking books and furniture next to a wooden stairwell was a "monumentally risky" fire hazard in the first place [11, 16, 17]. Banks concludes the evidence for deliberate arson is "hard to avoid," though he philosophically suggests that Gregson's alleged insurance fraud may have unwittingly acted as a paranormal instrument to destroy the house and force the eventual excavation of the [[bones-excavation|cellar bones]] [16, 18].

The letters also confirm several phenomena: the carriage-wheel tracks in snow in a physically inaccessible area, and the two dogs' panicked disappearances.
