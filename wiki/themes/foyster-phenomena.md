# The Foyster Phenomena

General title for paranormal incidents alleged to have occurred at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] between October 1930 and autumn 1935, during the incumbency of the [[lionel-foyster|Revd Lionel Foyster]] and his wife [[marianne-foyster]]. The most contested period in the entire case. [[harry-price]] described it as the time "which has been regarded as affording most proof of the alleged hauntings," and later dubbed the period the apex of "The Most Haunted House in England."

## Scale and Nature

[[harry-price]] reckoned over 2,000 separate paranormal incidents in the first fifteen months alone — a dramatic escalation from previous years, shifting from traditional ghostly sightings to violent physical disturbances. The phenomena included:

- **Auditory and visual anomalies**: On their very first day, a voice was reportedly heard calling "Marianne, dear" [5, 7–9]. Marianne repeatedly reported seeing the apparition of the late [[harry-bull]] in his plum-coloured dressing-gown on the main staircase, as well as a bat-like "monstrosity" near the kitchen door that touched her with a hand "like iron" [10–13]
- **Physical assaults**: Marianne allegedly thrown from bed repeatedly; given a black eye by a thrown object; struck on the head with metal and pelted with stones [14, 19–21]. [[lionel-foyster]] was hit by a flying hairbrush and a piece of brick [22, 23]
- **Poltergeist projection**: Stones, bottles, and other objects hurled across rooms; one stone came through a staircase window from *inside* the house while three people watched
- **Wall writings**: Messages addressed to "Marianne" appearing on walls and scraps of paper, often pleading for "Light, Mass, Prayers" and help (see [[wall-writings]]) [29–32]
- **Apports**: Objects materialising and dematerialising — hymn books appearing over the kitchen range; a watch bracelet, lavender bag, tin trunk, china powder box, and a wedding ring variously appearing and disappearing; bottles appearing in mid-air in the kitchen [14–18]

The peak of disturbances lasted approximately fifteen months, then reduced after the visit of the [[marks-tey-spiritualist-circle|Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle]] (January 1932), though isolated incidents continued until the Foysters' departure in 1935.

## Key Independent Witnesses

The case for genuine phenomena in this period does not rest on [[marianne-foyster]] alone:

- **[[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]]**: Made approximately thirty visits June–December 1931 during the worst period. Witnessed the [[stiletto-incident|stiletto levitation]] (a heavy brass paper knife landing on his lap while Marianne's hands were visible under the bedclothes); the tumbler incident; and bottles materialising in the kitchen in the presence of himself, Marianne, and her maid Katie. He told [[peter-underwood]] he "had never had any occasion to suspect any member of the household of trickery" across six months of observation [24, 25, 43–45]
- **Lady Florence Whitehouse**: Saw a medicine bottle leave the mantelpiece and float through the air in the [[blue-room]], coming to rest by the bed; offered to swear an affidavit [33, 40–42]
- **[[rev-clive-luget|Revd Clive Luget]]** (rector of Middleton): Frequent visitor; witnessed bell-ringing, bottles, writing, and apparitions — "not the slightest shadow of a doubt" the phenomena was real
- **Guy L'Estrange**: Visit January 1932 — saw bell-ringing, footsteps, bottle materialisations, crockery movements; the rector told him directly "I saw a pencil rise from my desk and scribble words on the wall. No hand was visible"
- **[[captain-deane|Capt. V.M. Deane]]** and the Marks Tey group: January 1932 visit; three to five witnesses simultaneously saw and handled materialised bottles [46–48]
- **Canon H. Lawton's *absence* of phenomena** (August–September 1933): Used by the SPR as evidence of fakery; but phenomena had already reduced greatly after the Marks Tey visit, making a quiet period in mid-1933 unsurprising [34, 69]

## The Explanatory Debate

### SPR Position (1956)

[[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Hall]] concluded: [[marianne-foyster]] was responsible for nearly all phenomena, either alone or with the assistance of François d'Arles and possibly Ian Shaw. They pointed out that **99 out of 103 recorded incidents depended entirely on Marianne's presence and good faith** [52, 63]. Motives proposed: boredom; desire to force [[lionel-foyster|Lionel]] to leave a house she hated; desire for excitement and attention; and — according to [[robert-wood]] (*The Widow of Borley*) — the need for a smokescreen to cover an intense, illicit affair with d'Arles, a lodger who dominated the household [54–56].

Local neighbours, such as Sir John Braithwaite, reportedly concluded she was hysterical and personally responsible for faking the [[wall-writings]] [33, 57, 58].

### Harry Price's Private and Public Positions

While [[harry-price]] promoted the Foyster period as the most convincing evidence of the haunting in his published books [49–51], he privately believed [[marianne-foyster]] was a fraud. During a visit in October 1931, where he saw wine inexplicably turn to ink, Price directly accused Marianne of cheating. The accusation caused a furious rift with [[lionel-foyster]], and Price wrote to colleagues stating he was convinced the rector's wife was "just fooling us" [59–62]. He was also repeatedly barred from visiting Borley by the Foysters — a fact suppressed in the SPR report.

### Counter-arguments

1. Multiple independent witnesses saw phenomena when [[marianne-foyster]] was not near or in a position to have caused them
2. Phenomena predated the Foysters' arrival (and Marianne's birth)
3. Canon Lawton's null experience only proves the phenomena was not constant — not that it was fabricated
4. The stiletto, tumbler, and bottle incidents have never been satisfactorily explained as fraud

### Lionel Foyster's Alleged Complicity

Sceptical author [[robert-wood]] argued that [[lionel-foyster]] was not a credulous victim, but a willing accomplice. Facing severe financial difficulties after the Wall Street Crash and deteriorating health, Foyster allegedly tolerated his wife's affairs and helped fake the phenomena because he hoped to write a lucrative bestselling book about the haunted house — modelled, critics argue, on the commercial success of Walter Hubbell's account of the [[great-amherst-mystery]] [54, 70–72].

### Marianne's Own Account (1958 Swanson Interviews; 1978 Owen/Mitchell Interviews)

Marianne confirmed that she, Lionel, and Ian "answered" the wall writings by writing underneath them. She said Lionel threw objects to observe people's reactions for his book, and that local children played pranks [73, 76, 77]. However, she maintained that a core of genuine, minor poltergeist activity — including unexplained footsteps, the [[wall-writings]], and the bottle and tumbler incidents — did occur and genuinely puzzled her [78, 79]. In 1978 she reportedly told researchers [[iris-owen]] and Pauline Mitchell that "by far the majority [of events] were completely invented by Lionel" for his intended book [73–75] — though she also admitted "there were a great many things which he and I went through which no one can explain away."

### Owen/Mitchell Finding (1979)

The most careful independent analysis: "three main strands weave through the story of the Borley haunting. First of all, there were some inexplicable happenings of a poltergeist nature; secondly, there was undoubtedly an amount of trickery; and thirdly, there was misrepresentation and misinformation." They identified genuine [[poltergeist-activity|poltergeist activity]] with centres in Edwin Whitehouse (Foyster period) and Mary Pearson (Smith period).

### The Amherst Parallel

Critics note parallels with the 1878–79 [[great-amherst-mystery|Amherst, Nova Scotia case]] (Esther Cox): [[wall-writings]], mattress disturbances, stone throwing, fires, physical assaults, a young woman at the centre — and a duration of approximately fourteen to fifteen months in both cases. The Foysters had lived near Amherst in Canada; [[lionel-foyster]] knew Walter Hubbell's book about the case and intended to write something similar. The SPR authors documented a further detail: when Foyster wrote his novelised account, *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*, he used the pseudonym "Teed" for one character — the surname of Esther Cox's brother-in-law [2, 4, 6]. Some critics use this to suggest the Foyster events were modelled on Amherst; defenders dismiss the pseudonym as coincidental, noting that "Teed" is a common Canadian surname and Foyster used many other equally unusual pseudonyms [8]. See [[great-amherst-mystery]].

## The Manuscripts

[[lionel-foyster]] wrote four accounts of the Foyster period at various times — see [[foyster-manuscripts]] for the full analysis. The core documents are the *Diary of Occurrences* (1931), *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House* (novel with pseudonyms, c.1932–34), and the *Summary of Experiences* (compiled 1938 for Price).
