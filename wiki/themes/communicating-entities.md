# Communicating 'Entities'

From the time of the Smiths and the Foysters, through [[harry-price]]'s investigations in the late 1930s and early 1940s, and on into the 1950s, the records of the séances carried out both at Borley and elsewhere are full of messages, warnings, and communications from a wide range of alleged discarnate spirits and 'entities' who have become part of the popular presentation of the Rectory haunting. These communications were obtained through various methods — wall writings, mirror-tapping, table-tipping, trance mediums, and planchette boards [1-3].

## Major Communicators

### 'Marie Lairre'

The most famous communicator. She claimed to be a 19-year-old French Catholic novice from a convent in Le Havre, brought to England by a member of the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]] in the seventeenth century, subsequently murdered (strangled on 17 May 1667) and buried in the cellars of a building that once occupied the site of [[henry-bull]]'s house (see [[the-nun|Phantom Nun]]). She came to prominence in planchette sittings conducted by [[sidney-glanville]] during the [[price-tenancy]] in 1937, frequently pleading for "Light, Mass, Prayers," and holy water [4, 5, 7]. Her murderer was later identified as 'Henry Waldegrave' acting on the orders of 'Dominic Drossy'; both entities communicated later (Drossy during [[len-sewell]]'s 1950s sittings where 'Leslie Waldegrave' was also named as the killer).

Despite being the primary operator who gathered the planchette data, [[sidney-glanville]] placed no faith in the material. He believed the messages originated entirely from the subconscious minds of the operators and did not believe [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] ever existed [29].

### 'Evangeline Westcott'

During a visit by the [[marks-tey-spiritualist-circle]] in January 1932, a trance medium named Guy L'Estrange reportedly channelled a different nun entity named Evangeline Westcott [8, 9]. She allegedly pleaded for forgiveness and cried out about being trapped in a "living tomb" where she was tantalised with empty water bottles [10].

### 'Henry Bull' and 'Harry Bull'

Father and son from the Bull incumbencies. 'Harry Bull' was a frequent communicator at many Borley séances, beginning with the [[blue-room-seance]] on 12–13 June 1929 when he apparently answered questions via taps on a mirror and acknowledged responsibility for footsteps the Smiths had heard [2, 11, 12]. Later planchette sittings produced more dramatic — and contradictory — communications, including the claim that Harry Bull had been poisoned by his wife, Ivy, and in another session that he had murdered his brother and killed the nun [13, 14]. The BRC treats this later material as interesting Borleyana rather than serious evidence.

### 'Katie Boreham'

An entity who identified herself as a maid who died in April 1888 [15, 16]. Her appearances in the séances were linked by researchers to local rumours that [[henry-bull|Rev. Henry Bull]] had an illicit affair with a village girl [17, 18].

### 'Sunex Amures'

See the dedicated entry at [[sunex-amures]]. This cryptic entity communicated via planchette on 27 March 1938, prophesying that he and his men would burn down the Rectory that very night and reveal the bones of a murdered victim. While the Rectory did not burn that night, it was destroyed by fire exactly eleven months later [1, 20, 21]. One theory: Latin "Senex Taurus" = "Old Man Bull."

### Other Price Tenancy Communicators (Glanville Séances)

- 'Joseph Glanvil': the English writer/philosopher (1636–80)
- 'Caldecote': either Henry Bull's wife or Marie Lairre herself
- 'Mère Beaumaras': a French Mother Superior

### 'Joe Miles'

During the Foyster period, a sitting held at the Rectory on 11 August 1931 (see [[august-1931-seance|'Joe Miles' Séance]]) produced contact with a drunken publican who claimed responsibility for the haunting — later dismissed as a mistake [3, 19]. Other entities that evening: John Wesley and Cardinal Newman.

### Len Sewell's Communicators (1950s)

[[len-sewell]] held many planchette sessions at Borley between 1955 and 1957. Communicators included:

- 'Dominic Drossy' (most prolific — a Benedictine monk who claimed to have ordered the murder of Marie Lairre) [1, 22, 23]
- 'J.O. Steed'
- 'Percy Long'
- 'Ronald James Hewitt'
- 'Stanley Gardiner'
- 'Harry Doy' (a butcher)
- 'Alfred Henning' (died 1955 — the year Sewell began)
- 'Harry Price' (who "assured Sewell and his friends that he did not fake phenomena at Borley")
- 'Marie Lairre'

### John Deeks

A former Borley cleric (rector from 1642) apparently communicated at a séance organised by [[rev-ac-henning|the Revd John Dening]] in Borley Church in 2004, claiming to have been the cause of the haunting over the years.

## Canon Phythian-Adams and the Nun Theory

Canon W.J. Phythian-Adams of Carlisle took the entity communications very seriously. He combined the planchette statements of 'Marie Lairre' with a linguistic analysis of the wall writings — interpreting one scrawl as the French word *trompée*, meaning "deceived" — to build a comprehensive theory that the haunting was driven by the murdered nun's plea for a Catholic burial [39-41]. He used these entity clues to direct [[harry-price]] to excavate the cellar, where human bones were subsequently found [42, 43].

## Assessment

[[harry-price]] openly warned that certain parts of the séance data "is demonstrably untrue" and that results were probably "due to unconscious muscular action on the part of the Planchette sitters, who... drew on their subconscious minds and automatically recorded the 'messages.'" Price nevertheless heavily publicised the entity communications in his books, using the 'Sunex Amures' prophecy and the 'Marie Lairre' data to provide a sensational narrative backbone to the haunting [42, 44]. He privately acknowledged that planchette writing was likely due to unconscious muscular action or "lying spirits" [31, 45, 46]. Regarding the wall writings, Price proposed that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] might have physically written them herself, but argued she did so unconsciously as a "secondary personality," asserting that this psychological mechanism would not invalidate the paranormal content of the messages [47, 48].

The BRC observes that séance information obtained where responses are the products of leading questions can be interpreted in many ways. [[ivan-banks]] subjected the séance data to detailed analysis in *The Enigma of Borley Rectory*, republishing several transcripts in full and filling in blanks Price had omitted.

[[iris-owen]] and Pauline Mitchell compared the Borley séances to their famous "Philip" experiment, in which a group successfully manifested physical poltergeist phenomena by inventing a completely fictional ghost. They argued that the Borley entities simply claimed to be whoever the participants expected them to be, drawing the details directly from the subconscious expectations and localised legends known to the sitters [30, 31].

The [[spr-investigation|SPR authors]] ([[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], and [[trevor-hall]]) pointed out that the amateur spiritualists operating the planchette boards asked highly leading questions based on local legends, which naturally produced the desired answers [34, 35]. [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]]'s own denials regarding the wall writings variously blamed village children, local pranksters, or the highly strung Edwin Whitehouse [36-38].

[[robert-wood]] dismissed the entire séance material as "rubbish," calling the 'communications' from the Bull family "nothing but village gossip and the Misses Bulls' slanders of their sister-in-law" [32, 33].
