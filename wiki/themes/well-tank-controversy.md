# The Well Tank Controversy

One of the most persistent charges against [[harry-price]] in the Borley case centres on the "well tank" — a shallow rectangular concrete trough Price described as having seen in the Rectory cellar passage. When he excavated that location in 1943 and found human bones, sceptics argued the well tank had been invented post-hoc to justify the digging at a location where Price had secretly pre-planted the remains.

The [[brc|Borley Rectory Companion]] (2009) provides the most thorough analysis of this controversy and reaches a definitive conclusion.

## What Price Said

In *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940), Price described descending the cellar stairs on his 12 June 1929 first visit and noting a water-filled trough — 2ft 6in long, 2ft wide, 9in deep — in the passage at the foot of the stairs. He later described this as a "well tank" that became significant when Mark Kerr-Pearse translated Wall Writing No. 3 as "well tank bottom me" (July 1937). Canon Phythian-Adams amplified this in January 1941.

When Price excavated the cellars in 1943, the "well tank" had apparently vanished — the brick floor was continuous. Price concluded it had been bricked over between 1939 and 1943, perhaps by [[captain-gregson|Captain Gregson]] or workmen.

> **Contradiction:** Price's 1940 book offered "Repentant" as the likely word in Wall Writing No. 3. Only after Phythian-Adams proposed the murdered-nun theory in 1941 did Price enthusiastically adopt the "Well Tank Bottom Me" interpretation for his 1946 sequel, *The End of Borley Rectory* [5, 10]. The [[spr-investigation|SPR]] investigators highlighted this as evidence of a shifting narrative.

## The Wall-Writing Message and Its Interpretations

The controversy begins with the wall-writing itself, which admits of sharply divergent readings.

**The "Bother Me" reading:** During the incumbency of [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]] (around May 1931), he recorded in his *Diary of Occurrences* that the passage message read: *"Marianne get help (something undecipherable) bother me"* (or "bothers me") [1, 2]. Living in the house and able to examine the original wall directly, Foyster's contemporaneous reading never included "well" or "tank."

**The "Well Tank Bottom Me" reading:** During [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s tenancy in July 1937, observer Mark Kerr-Pearse suggested the faint scrawl actually read: "Marianne get help, well tank bottom me" [1, 3, 4]. Price wrote to [[sidney-glanville|Glanville]] on 20 July 1937 that the message "appears to direct investigators to look in the well," and Kerr-Pearse duly searched the well in August 1937 — finding only old tins, broken glass, and brick. This disappointing result, and Kerr-Pearse's own reading, were both suppressed in *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940). Nearly four years later, in January 1941, Canon W.J. Phythian-Adams independently reached the same "Well Tank Bottom Me" reading and used it to construct an elaborate theory that a murdered French nun, "[[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]," had been thrown down a well subsequently filled in to form a tank beneath the cellar floor [6, 7].

**The retouching problem:** At Price's request, [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] had traced over the faint pencil marks with a heavy black pencil so they could be photographed [8, 9]. The [[spr-investigation|SPR]] authors argue that Glanville, already aware of Kerr-Pearse's theory, may have been subconsciously influenced to emphasise the words "Well Tank Bottom Me" when he overwrote the original scribbles [1, 8]. The photograph in *MHH* and *EBR* therefore shows a retouched version, not the original.

**The graphologist's disagreement:** Lewis T. Ackermann, the professional graphologist engaged to analyse the wall-writings, stated that he "totally disagreed" with Phythian-Adams's interpretation and that the Canon "did not understand the mechanics of handwriting." Ackermann produced an entirely different reading, including the word "tombs" rather than "well tank."

The [[spr-investigation|SPR]] concluded that "Well Tank Bottom Me" was by no means clearly apparent in the *untouched* wall-writing and may not have been what was written at all — Foyster's contemporary reading ("bother me") may have been the correct one.

## The Physical Existence of the Well Tank

There was significant confusion over what and where the "well tank" actually was.

**Price's claim:** Price wrote that during his 1929 first visit, an observer put his foot through a rotten wooden hatch-cover in the cellar passage, revealing a shallow, water-filled rectangular trough about 2ft 6in long and 9in deep [4, 11, 12].

**The disappearing tank:** When Price returned in August 1943 to excavate the spot indicated by Phythian-Adams's theory, the rectangular trough had vanished, replaced by undisturbed brickwork [13, 14]. Price concluded it had been mysteriously filled in and bricked over between 1939 and 1943 [13, 15, 16].

**An architectural absurdity:** Modern researchers point out that a "well tank" is never located in a cellar [17]. In the Rectory's plumbing system, the "well tank" was actually a galvanised steel water cistern positioned high in the attics [18–20]. Taken literally, the wall-writing would place the remains in the roof space rather than the cellar floor [21].

## The Companion's Resolution

The [[brc|Borley Rectory Companion]] examined the original documents at the University of London — specifically Mark Kerr-Pearse's contemporaneous report and sketch plan from his excavation visit on 1 November 1937.

**The key finding:** Kerr-Pearse's sketch plan, drawn on the day of the dig, shows only the *round sump* in the corner of the cellar room (the courtyard well) — there is no rectangular trough drawn in the cellar passage. When Kerr-Pearse reported that he had "partially emptied well tank in cellar, which has a smooth concrete bottom," he was describing his cleaning of a small, 6-foot-deep *round sump* in a completely different corner of the cellar [23–25]. His own 1937 sketch map of the cellar shows no rectangular trough in the passage [25]. For a meticulous, precise observer like Kerr-Pearse to have omitted a large rectangular feature in the adjacent passage is impossible.

**Conclusion:** The "well tank" in the cellar passage never existed. Price was genuinely confused when writing from memory ten years after his 1929 visit. What he remembered seeing — possibly the wooden hatch cover over the round courtyard sump — became misremembered as a second water feature [22, 25].

## The Fraud Accusation

The [[spr-investigation|SPR]] investigators (1956) suggested: if the well tank had been invented by Price to fit the Phythian-Adams theory, then the 1943 digging at that invented location was a set-up designed to produce the bones Price had secretly buried there. The "disappearance" of the well tank was too convenient.

The SPR authors argued that Price created an "impenetrable fog of mystery and confusion" around the cellars to cover up the fact that he was planting evidence [26, 27]. Because the bone fragments were found only three feet down — a matter of inches below the supposed bottom of the "missing" tank — they argued this made nonsense of Phythian-Adams's theory of a deep filled-in well [7]. They strongly suspected that Price, desperate for a sensational climax for his second book, used the excuse of the "bricked over" tank to justify digging up a perfectly solid floor to "discover" bones he had secretly buried himself [7, 27, 28].

Robert Wood (1992) went further: "Price had almost certainly put them [the bones] there himself."

## Implications and Defences of Price

[[robert-hastings|Robert Hastings]] and [[edward-babbs]] (Edward Babbs) argue that the accusation of planting the bones is completely unsustainable [29, 30]. Because the "well tank" in the passage never actually existed, Price could not possibly have planted bones in an existing trough [22]. Furthermore, in 1943 Price was 62 years old and suffering from severe angina pectoris, making him physically incapable of breaking through a brick floor and digging into heavy, compacted clay to secretly bury remains [30–32]. A man attempting deliberate fraud would not have invited a professional pathologist (Dr. Eric Bailey) to be present at the dig, nor would a devout churchgoer like Price have arranged a solemn Christian burial for a fabricated hoax [30, 33, 34].

If there was no well tank, there was no pre-prepared cavity in the passage floor. The brick floor had lain undisturbed since 1863. For Price to have planted bones there:
- He would have had to make a secret visit to [[borley-rectory|Borley]], break up 80 years of undisturbed brickwork, deposit bones at the correct depth, and repair the floor convincingly
- All while in his early sixties and in poor health
- Without the knowledge of the local rector who lived opposite

None of the fraud scenarios survive scrutiny. The most likely explanation is that the bones were part of an unrecorded historical burial, possibly from the plague pit that local tradition held to have been somewhere in the Rectory grounds.
