# The Well Tank Controversy

One of the most persistent charges against [[harry-price]] in the Borley case centres on the "well tank" — a shallow rectangular concrete trough Price described as having seen in the Rectory cellar passage. When he excavated that location in 1943 and found human bones, sceptics argued the well tank had been invented post-hoc to justify the digging at a location where Price had secretly pre-planted the remains.

The [[brc|Borley Rectory Companion]] (2009) provides the most thorough analysis of this controversy and reaches a definitive conclusion.

## What Price Said

In *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940), Price described descending the cellar stairs on his 12 June 1929 first visit and noting a water-filled trough — 2ft 6in long, 2ft wide, 9in deep — in the passage at the foot of the stairs. He later described this as a "well tank" that became significant when Mark Kerr-Pearse translated Wall Writing No. 3 as "well tank bottom me" (July 1937). Canon Phythian-Adams amplified this in January 1941.

When Price excavated the cellars in 1943, the "well tank" had apparently vanished — the brick floor was continuous. Price concluded it had been bricked over between 1939 and 1943, perhaps by Captain Gregson or workmen.

## The Fraud Accusation

The SPR investigators (1956) suggested: if the well tank had been invented by Price to fit the Phythian-Adams theory, then the 1943 digging at that invented location was a set-up designed to produce the bones Price had secretly buried there. The "disappearance" of the well tank was too convenient.

Robert Wood (1992) went further: "Price had almost certainly put them [the bones] there himself."

## The Companion's Resolution

The Companion examined the original documents at the University of London — specifically Mark Kerr-Pearse's contemporaneous report and sketch plan from his excavation visit on 1 November 1937.

**The key finding**: Kerr-Pearse's sketch plan, drawn on the day of the dig, shows only the *round sump* in the corner of the cellar room (the courtyard well) — there is no rectangular trough drawn in the cellar passage. Kerr-Pearse had cleaned out the round sump on 3 August 1937, noting it had "a smooth concrete bottom" about 4 feet deep. For a meticulous, precise observer like Kerr-Pearse to have omitted a large rectangular feature in the adjacent passage is impossible.

**Conclusion**: The "well tank" in the cellar passage never existed. Price was genuinely confused when writing from memory ten years after his 1929 visit. What he remembered seeing (possibly the wooden hatch cover over the round courtyard sump) became misremembered as a second water feature.

## Implications

If there was no well tank, there was no pre-prepared cavity in the passage floor. The brick floor had lain undisturbed since 1863. For Price to have planted bones there:
- He would have had to make a secret visit to Borley, break up 80 years of undisturbed brickwork, deposit bones at the correct depth, and repair the floor convincingly
- All while in his early sixties and in poor health
- Without the knowledge of the local rector who lived opposite

None of the fraud scenarios survive scrutiny. The most likely explanation is that the bones were part of an unrecorded historical burial, possibly from the plague pit that local tradition held to have been somewhere in the Rectory grounds.

## The SPR's Analysis of the "Well Tank Bottom Me" Reading (spr-vii)

The 1956 SPR Report adds crucial context to the message's interpretive history that Price suppressed:

- **Kerr-Pearse read "Well Tank Bottom Me" in July 1937** — nearly four years before Phythian-Adams's "discovery" of the same reading in January 1941. Price wrote to Glanville on 20 July 1937 that the message "appears to direct investigators to look in the well." Kerr-Pearse duly searched the well in August 1937 — finding only old tins, broken glass, and brick. This disappointing result, and Kerr-Pearse's own reading, were both suppressed in MHH (1940), which instead offered "Repentant" as the likely word
- **The retouching problem:** Glanville, asked by Price to "strengthen carefully the marks so that they are photographable," would unavoidably have been influenced by Kerr-Pearse's already-known interpretation when strengthening the faint writing. The photograph in MHH and EBR shows a retouched version
- **Foyster's original reading:** In the *Diary of Occurrences* (c. May 1931) Foyster recorded: *"'Marianne get help (something undecipherable) bother me' (or bothers me)."* He was living in the house and could examine the original wall; his reading never included "well" or "tank"
- **The graphologist's disagreement:** Lewis T. Ackermann, the professional graphologist engaged to analyse the wall-writings, stated that he "totally disagreed" with Phythian-Adams's interpretation and that the Canon "did not understand the mechanics of handwriting." Ackermann produced an entirely different reading, including the word "tombs" rather than "well tank"

The SPR concluded that "Well Tank Bottom Me" was by no means clearly apparent in the *untouched* wall-writing and may not have been what was written at all — Foyster's contemporary reading ("bother me") may have been the correct one.
