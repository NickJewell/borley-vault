# The *Suffolk and Essex Free Press* Article (June 1929)

An editorial commentary published in *The Suffolk and Essex Free Press* on 13 June 1929 under the heading "The Borley Ghost." It is one of the earliest recorded pieces of journalism about the Borley haunting and is cited by Babbs and Mathias as evidence that the alleged haunting was already common local knowledge before [[harry-price|Harry Price]] arrived on the scene.

## Significance of the Timing

The article appeared only three days after V.C. Wall's first sensational report in *The Daily Mirror* (10 June 1929) and only one day after Price's first ever visit to Borley (12 June). Price was still at Borley on 13 June. This timing suggests strongly that the local paper did not first learn of the haunting from the national press; rather, the story was already part of the local consciousness [3, 4].

A special correspondent for the paper had visited the village on the preceding Tuesday, interviewing the Rector ([[rev-eric-smith|Rev. G. Eric Smith]]), the gardener (Charles Pilgrim), and the village postmistress [5].

## Content

The editorial opens with a quotation from Frederick W.H. Myers's *Human Personality and its Survival of Bodily Death* (London, 1903), signalling a serious rather than dismissive approach. Key passages:

> "This week we record certain strange experiences at Borley Rectory and after weighing all the facts which are in our possession, we do not propose, as some no doubt will, to treat this matter with ridicule or turn it down as the phantasm of disordered mental processes."

The article outlined the "ghostly part of the history of Borley," recounting the tradition that a monastery or convent once stood on the site and the legend of a nun who attempted to elope with a coachman in a coach drawn by two bay horses — they were caught, the coachman was beheaded, and the nun was returned to the convent [6]. It noted that since that time the nun had been seen walking among the trees, and that the late [[harry-bull|Rev. Harry Bull]] had often spoken of seeing the old-time coach and headless men while walking along the road at night [6].

The article specifically mentions the case of a London domestic (identified elsewhere as Mary Pearson) who, only two days after entering the Rectory and knowing nothing of past legend, almost went into a dead faint when she reported seeing a nun dressed in black [1] — a witness not traceable in most other Borley accounts.

The reporter noted a distinct split in local reactions. The Rector stated that some villagers were too frightened to pass the spot at night, while other residents told the reporter they "ridiculed the whole affair" [7]. The Rectory gardener smiled at the idea of ghosts, stating he had never seen anything and believed the old tales were actually just "couples sweethearting" in the dark [8].

The editorial concludes:

> "It is an accepted fact that there is some phenomena, not understood, which for want of a better definition is called a 'ghost'. There is nothing extraordinary in it, the eeriness comes in our lack of knowledge of what it is and the terror of the unknown and mysterious still grips us humans."

Declaring that there was a "mass of corroborative evidence" that phenomena had occurred at the Rectory for years, the editorial called for a "thorough investigation from the scientific and spiritual points of view" [2, 5]. The editorial also discussed the history of early witchcraft and hypnotism, suggesting that the naïveté of country people played a role in historical supernatural beliefs [9].

## Assessment

Babbs and Mathias consider the article "a most interesting piece of work" and note that despite some grammatical issues and a digression into medieval witchcraft, it represents a thoughtful engagement with the subject by a local paper with direct knowledge of the area.

The Borley sources use the June 13th article to argue vastly different perspectives on the authenticity of the haunting:

**The pro-paranormal perspective:** Babbs and Mathias argue the editorial proves that the haunted reputation of Borley "was common knowledge in the Essex/Suffolk border area and had been for a long time" [4]. Because the local press was so "quick off the mark" — publishing immediately after the *Daily Mirror* and right as Price arrived — they argue this refutes the sceptical claim that the haunting was entirely a media-driven hoax orchestrated by Price [3, 4].

**The sceptical counter-argument:** Conversely, sceptical historian Andrew Clarke uses the article to demonstrate how poorly the ghost story was received by actual locals. Clarke notes that the reporter's "extraordinarily ponderous story" failed to ignite much local interest, as very few people who actually knew the place gave the sensational claims any credence [5, 10]. Furthermore, in the very next issue of the *Suffolk and Essex Free Press*, a reader's letter completely dismantled the "nun and coach" legend by pointing out a massive historical anachronism: horse-drawn coaches were not invented until long after the dissolution of the monasteries [10].
