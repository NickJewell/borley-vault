# Ley Lines at Borley

A hypothesis proposed by Babbs and Mathias in *The Final Analysis* (brfa-ch12) that the intersection of multiple ley lines at Borley may account for some of the minor or mechanical phenomena reported there — specifically the repeated malfunction of electrical and mechanical equipment.

## What Are Ley Lines?

Re-discovered in 1921 by [[alfred-watkins|Alfred Watkins]] of Herefordshire (an amateur archaeologist and photographer). Watkins noticed that ancient monuments — churches, bridges, crossroads, barrows, stone circles — frequently align in straight lines across the landscape. He theorised that neolithic peoples deliberately laid out these alignments to guide movement of flints, salt, and clay across forested country. Roman roads often follow ley lines.

The crucial secondary property: ley lines are said to follow **lines of magnetic force**. Early peoples, like migrating birds, may have possessed a sixth sense for such lines. The convergence of multiple ley lines in one location would create a local concentration of magnetic energy.

Watkins's criterion: if at least **five ancient monuments and/or named places** align within a total distance of **25 miles**, it qualifies as a ley line.

## The Four Borley Ley Lines

Frank Smyth first identified two ley lines through Borley (in a chapter contributed to a book called *Ghosts*). Babbs and Mathias, using a large-scale map of East Anglia and a transparent ruler, identified **two more**, for a total of four. All four meet Watkins's criteria:

1. **West–east:** Sturmer → Wixoe → Stoke-by-Clare → **Borley** → Hadleigh → Chattisham
2. **NW–SE:** Cavendish → [[pentlow|Pentlow]] → Foxearth → **Borley** → Great Cornard → Honey Tye (crossroads) → Nayland → Boxted Cross → Ardleigh → Little Bentley
3. **SW–NE:** Great Saling → Church End → Sible Hedingham → [[gestingthorpe|Gestingthorpe]] → **Borley** → Rodbridge → Thorpe Morieux
4. **SW–NE:** Belchamp Walter → **Borley** → Rodbridge → Lavenham → Preston St Mary → Stowmarket

## The Mechanical Interference Catalogue

A recurring pattern at Borley: electrical and mechanical equipment malfunctions at the site but works normally elsewhere:

- **Dr Alec Ritchie (1930–35):** car engine cut out while driving past the church/rectory section of Hall Lane; no problems before or after. His son Ted had the same experience years later. (brfa-ch10)
- **Godfrey Thurston Hopkins (1954):** photograph of the rectory gate included a shadowy figure not visible in the viewfinder; photographic department confirmed negative was genuine. (brfa-ch10)
- **Denny Densham and BBC team (1974):** one recording machine jammed; tape removed from its reel and found in a tangled heap; this was under observation with only engineers present. (brfa-ch12)
- **Croom-Hollingsworth Harlow group (c.1964):** tape recorder left in church porch; loud noise heard from porch; found tape tangled in a heap on the floor; no one entered. (brfa-ch12)
- **Thomas Musk and friends (1987):** tape recorder refusal; also heard the organ "playing itself" from outside the locked church. (brfa-ch09)
- **Phantom organ music generally:** the repeatedly reported self-playing of the church organ (Henning/Walrond 1947; Jephcott 1947; Stearn 1955; Musk 1987) — after an electric pump was fitted to the organ, the phenomenon continued
- **Paul Kemp (undated):** tape recorder refused to operate properly at Borley; nothing mechanically wrong with it
- **Dr C. Hilton-Rowe (March 1947):** light meter refused to function while near the rectory gate where [[fred-cartwright|Cartwright]] had seen the nun; functioned normally elsewhere. (brfa-ch12)

## The Authors' Hypothesis

Babbs and Mathias propose:
- The intersection of four (possibly more) ley lines at Borley creates a local concentration of magnetic/electromagnetic energy
- This may account for the **minor mechanical/electrical phenomena** and photographic anomalies, including dead batteries, jammed tape recorders, and stalling car engines [1, 7]
- It does **not** explain the apparitions or the violent poltergeist activity, which they attribute to psychic echoes of historical tragedy

They are careful to distinguish: "We feel that it is unlikely that they would be responsible for the apparitions and the sometimes violent poltergeist activity which so many intelligent people claim to have witnessed at Borley. Surely if these latter phenomena are genuine, then they are much more likely to be a psychic echo of a former tragedy or unhappiness."

## Controversy

Dr Simon Broadbent (statistician; died 15 March 2002) argued in a paper to the Royal Statistical Society that the number of alleged ley lines was no greater than random distribution of locations would predict — indeed, that the figure was actually *fewer* than a purely random distribution would produce [11]. Babbs and Mathias note he did not address the secondary claim (ley lines as magnetic force lines), and they invoke Disraeli's sardonic remark about "lies, damned lies and statistics."
