# Waldegrave Family

Along with the Herringhams and the Bulls, the Waldegraves were the third family of importance associated with Borley, being Lords of the Manor for approximately 300 years. Their family history is intertwined with the Borley haunting through historical, legendary and séance-derived narratives.

## Origins and Early History

Originally hailing from Walgrave in Northamptonshire, the Waldegraves long held the manor of Smallbridge at Bures St Mary in Suffolk, close to Borley. **Sir Richard Waldegrave** (d. 1402) was Member of Parliament for Lincolnshire in 1335 and Speaker of the House of Commons under Richard II.

There is evidence that Edward's grandfather (also called Edward) had settled at Borley before 1500. The first Waldegrave formally associated with Borley was **Sir Edward Waldegrave** (1517–61), who came into possession of the Borley estate on the death of his father John in 1543, or possibly received it from Henry VIII in 1546.

## Sir Edward Waldegrave

[[sir-edward-waldegrave]] was attached to the household of Princess Mary (Mary Tudor) and was sent to the Tower of London for refusing to enforce an order forbidding the celebration of Mass in her household. During Mary's reign he was knighted and received the manor of Chewton Mendip in Somerset (still the main Waldegrave family base today).

When Elizabeth I succeeded Mary in 1558, Waldegrave chose to remain loyal to Catholicism. He refused the oath of supremacy. On 20 April 1561 he was arrested for having held Mass at Borley Hall with his wife and others, and was sent to the Tower where he died of ill health on 1 September 1561. He left a wife, Francis Neville, and six children.

His tomb in [[waldegrave-monument|Borley Church]] remains one of the finest examples of early English Renaissance funerary architecture.

## Later Waldegraves

- **Sir Nicholas Waldegrave** (d. 1621): inherited the manor of Borley Hall, 10 acres of woodland in Bulmer, and a share in the church and manor of [[langenhoe]]. In 1583 he acquired Langenhoe by marriage, connecting the two haunted sites.
- **Philip Waldegrave**: Sir Nicholas's eldest son, married twice. 
- **James Waldegrave**: became 1st Earl Waldegrave in 1729; an illegitimate son of King James II and Arabella Churchill (sister of the Duke of Marlborough); later Ambassador to Austria.

The estates passed through the title of Earl Waldegrave. **William Waldegrave MP, Baron Waldegrave of North Hill**, served in both Margaret Thatcher's and John Major's cabinets 1990–1997. **Sir Winston Churchill** was a collateral descendant.

## Waldegrave Women as Nun Candidates

Several Waldegrave women entered holy orders:

- **[[barbara-waldegrave|Barbara Waldegrave]] (Sister Apollonia)**: born Borley 1603; professed as a Benedictine nun in Brussels 1624; died 18 April 1638 aged ~36. [[ivan-banks]]' candidate for the ghost nun.
- **Arabella Waldegrave**: born 1687, daughter of Henry, first Lord Waldegrave; taken to France as a baby; placed in convent school at Pontoise; later disappeared from the record. [[georgina-dawson]]'s candidate for the ghost nun.

## Connection to Borley Place

The Waldegraves' actual home at Borley was **[[borley-place|Borley Hall]]** — a building about a mile from the church, not on the site of the Rectory. The family were Lords of the Manor and held the advowson (right to appoint rectors) from the time of Henry VIII until the death of [[henry-bull]] in 1892. The 1839 and 1841 tithe maps show "John James Henry Waldegrave" still owning land that would become the rambling garden of Borley Rectory. In 1890 Borley Hall passed to the [[payne-family|Payne family]] and the 300-year Waldegrave connection with Borley ended.

## In the Séances

The Glanville [[seance-data|planchette transcripts]] named "Henry Waldegrave" acting on the orders of "Dominic Drossy" as the murderer of [[marie-lairre]]; [[len-sewell]]'s séances in the 1950s named "Leslie Waldegrave." [[canadian-psychometrist|Lt. Ian Aitchison]] during a 1944 visit psychometrised objects and said "Waldegrave did it." None of these séance attributions can be corroborated historically.
