# Waldegrave Family

Along with the Herringhams and the Bulls, the Waldegraves were the third family of importance associated with Borley, being Lords of the Manor for approximately 300 years. Their family history is intertwined with the Borley haunting through historical, legendary and séance-derived narratives. Because of their Catholic faith, their political prominence, and their timeline in Borley, various researchers and séance sitters have heavily implicated the Waldegraves in theories of murder, espionage, and religious persecution.

## Origins and Early History

Originally hailing from Walgrave in Northamptonshire, the Waldegraves long held the manor of Smallbridge at Bures St Mary in Suffolk, close to Borley. **Sir Richard Waldegrave** (d. 1402) was Member of Parliament for Lincolnshire in 1335 and Speaker of the House of Commons under Richard II.

There is evidence that Edward's grandfather (also called Edward) had settled at Borley before 1500. The first Waldegrave formally associated with Borley was **Sir Edward Waldegrave** (1517–61), who came into possession of the Borley estate on the death of his father John in 1543, or possibly received it from Henry VIII in 1546 [2].

## Sir Edward Waldegrave

[[sir-edward-waldegrave|Sir Edward Waldegrave]] was attached to the household of Princess Mary (Mary Tudor) and was sent to the Tower of London for refusing to enforce an order forbidding the celebration of Mass in her household. During Mary's reign he was knighted and received the manor of Chewton Mendip in Somerset (still the main Waldegrave family base today).

When Elizabeth I succeeded Mary in 1558, Waldegrave chose to remain loyal to Catholicism. He refused the oath of supremacy. On 20 April 1561 he was arrested for having held Mass at Borley Hall with his wife and others, reportedly betrayed to the authorities by a fellow Catholic priest and a deceitful servant, and was sent to the Tower where he died of ill health on 1 September 1561 [4–6]. He left a wife, Francis Neville, and six children. He suffered the posthumous indignity of being buried in his own church under Protestant rites — an anathema to a devout Papist [10–12].

His tomb in [[waldegrave-monument|Borley Church]] remains one of the finest examples of early English Renaissance funerary architecture [5, 7]. The tomb has itself been the site of alleged phenomena, such as loud unexplained knocks heard by [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. A.C. Henning]] during a confirmation class in 1949 [15].

**Ivan Banks's haunting claim:** Banks strongly argues that [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Sir Edward]] is one of Borley's central "lost ghosts," suggesting that the apparition of a tall, unhappy man in old-fashioned clothes and a tall hat (seen by [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]]) was Sir Edward [8, 9]. Banks further analyses a bizarre séance message — *"Tfismong"* — connecting it to a 1561 state paper regarding Waldegrave that mentions "Forster the fishmonger," arguing that Sir Edward was trying to communicate the name of the man who betrayed him [13, 14].

## Later Waldegraves

- **Sir Nicholas Waldegrave** (d. 1621): inherited the manor of Borley Hall, 10 acres of woodland in Bulmer, and a share in the church and manor of [[langenhoe]]. In 1583 he acquired Langenhoe by marriage, connecting the two haunted sites.
- **Philip Waldegrave**: Sir Nicholas's eldest son, married twice.
- **James Waldegrave**: became 1st Earl Waldegrave in 1729; an illegitimate son of King James II and Arabella Churchill (sister of the Duke of Marlborough); later Ambassador to Austria.

The estates passed through the title of Earl Waldegrave. **William Waldegrave MP, Baron Waldegrave of North Hill**, served in both Margaret Thatcher's and John Major's cabinets 1990–1997. **Sir Winston Churchill** was a collateral descendant.

## Waldegrave Women as Nun Candidates

Several Waldegrave women entered holy orders:

- **[[barbara-waldegrave|Barbara Waldegrave]] (Sister Apollonia)**: born Borley 1603; professed as a Benedictine nun in Brussels 1624; died 18 April 1638 aged ~36. Ivan Banks's candidate for the ghost nun — a "ghost of conscience" perhaps troubled by an unresolved spiritual conflict [32–35].
- **[[arabella-waldegrave|Arabella Waldegrave]]**: born 1687, daughter of Henry, first Lord Waldegrave; taken to France as a baby; placed in convent school at Pontoise. Georgina Dawson's candidate for the ghost nun, on the theory that Arabella became a spy for the exiled Stuarts, returned to Borley, and was murdered there for her espionage [24–26].

> **Contradiction:** Ivan Banks later contacted Countess Mary Waldegrave, who completely debunked the mystery of Arabella's disappearance: she never vanished at all. She became a nun in Paris and maintained a regular correspondence with her family, destroying the premise that she was murdered at Borley [31]. Canon Phythian-Adams had also noted that Arabella was educated by English nuns and therefore would not have communicated via the broken English and French words (*"trompée"*) found in the Rectory's [[wall-writings|wall-writings]] [30].

## Connection to Borley Place

The Waldegraves' actual home at Borley was **[[borley-place|Borley Hall]]** — a building about a mile from the church, not on the site of the Rectory. The family were Lords of the Manor and held the advowson (right to appoint rectors) from the time of Henry VIII until the death of [[henry-bull]] in 1892. The 1839 and 1841 tithe maps show "John James Henry Waldegrave" still owning land that would become the rambling garden of Borley Rectory. In 1890 Borley Hall passed to the Payne family and the 300-year Waldegrave connection with Borley ended.

## The "Marie Lairre" Murder Theory

The most famous legend of Borley — that of a murdered French nun named [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] — is explicitly tied to the Waldegrave family through séance data generated by [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville's]] family in 1937 [16, 17]. Through planchette writing, an entity claiming to be a 19-year-old French novice communicated that she was strangled to death at Borley on May 17, 1667, and explicitly blamed "Waldegrave" for her murder [16, 17]. During another séance, the murderer was specifically identified as "Henry Waldegrave" (and in later 1950s séances as "Leslie Waldegrave" acting on orders from a monk) [18–20].

Canon Phythian-Adams enthusiastically accepted this séance data, theorising that a young Waldegrave on his European travels had enticed the French novice away from her convent with false promises of marriage. Once at Borley, finding her an impediment to a more profitable match, he strangled her and threw her body down a well beneath what would later become the Rectory [21, 22].

> **Contradiction:** [[harry-price|Harry Price]] pointed out a major historical flaw. The only known "Henry Waldegraves" of the era were the 1st Lord Waldegrave (born in 1660, making him only seven years old when the murder supposedly happened in 1667) and his son, who had not yet been born [23].

Mary Lycett blended the theories further, claiming that [[arabella-waldegrave|Arabella Waldegrave]] literally took the religious name "Soeur Marie Lairre," secretly married her cousin Henry Waldegrave, acted as a courier for secret documents, and was murdered by the Waldegrave family to protect their honour and political standing [27–29] — a synthesis which the debunking of the Arabella theory renders untenable.

## In the Séances

The Glanville planchette transcripts named "Henry Waldegrave" acting on the orders of "Dominic Drossy" as the murderer of [[marie-lairre]]; Len Sewell's séances in the 1950s named "Leslie Waldegrave." Lt. Ian Aitchison during a 1944 visit psychometrised objects and said "Waldegrave did it." None of these séance attributions can be corroborated historically.

## The Secret Tunnels

The Waldegraves are frequently invoked to explain the persistent rumours and physical discoveries of underground [[tunnels|tunnels]] at Borley. Investigators such as Len Sewell and Ivan Banks argue that because the Waldegraves were prominent and wealthy Catholics during the dangerous Tudor and Elizabethan periods, they were uniquely positioned to construct such tunnels as "priest holes" or concealed escape routes from Protestant reformers [36–39].
