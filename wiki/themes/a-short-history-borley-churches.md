# *A Short History of Borley and Liston Churches* (J. M. Bull, 1937)

A booklet by [[jm-bull|J. M. Bull]] published in 1937, providing the primary architectural and historical description of [[borley-church|Borley Church]] and Liston Church. It is the source cited by Underwood and Tabori for most of the physical description of the church building in Chapter 6 of *Ghosts of Borley*.

## Contents (as cited)

The booklet describes [[borley-church|Borley Church]] as:

- Small, consisting of chancel and nave with south porch and west tower
- Composed chiefly of flint rubble
- Probable twelfth-century origin; rebuilt chancel and north wall c.1500
- Tower and brick south porch added c.1500 (one source dates the red-brick south porch to the sixteenth century rather than "about 1500" [3])
- Original small windows replaced during the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries
- The middle window in the north wall of the nave is the earliest surviving, dating from the fourteenth century — likely preserved when the wall was rebuilt c.1500
- The three-stage tower has gargoyles projecting from each of the four sides
- The winding tower stairs are "worn by many feet"

[[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Alfred C. Henning]] (Rector in 1936) fully agreed with Bull's architectural timeline, sharing his confidence that the initial chancel and nave were twelfth-century and that the tower and brick porch were added around 1500 [2]. Henning extended the timeline further back, using the *Domesday Book* (which recorded a Borley Manor prior to 1066) to conclude that a wooden church probably existed on the site before the twelfth-century stone structure [2, 3].

One source contextualises Bull's description by noting that while the "medieval bones" of the church he described remain intact, the interior layout does not predate 1840 [4]. The nineteenth-century Oxford Movement led to a "Victorian gothic" restoration that stripped the church of its Puritan bare-box pews and three-decked pulpit, replacing them with stained glass and pseudo-medieval tiles — significantly altering the internal appearance from its original medieval state [4].

The booklet also covers the [[waldegrave-monument|Waldegrave monuments]], noting these as "good examples of sixteenth-century sculpture."

## The Waldegrave Family

Bull asserts that it is "quite certain" that the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]] played a major role in Borley for some three hundred years, holding the manor and serving as patrons of the church [5]. The broader Borley sources firmly validate this claim. The Waldegraves acquired the manor of Borley after Henry VIII requisitioned it during the Dissolution of the Monasteries, granting it to [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Edward Waldegrave]] in 1546 [6, 7]. They held the right to appoint Borley's rectors (the patronage) from the time of Henry VIII until the death of the Rev. [[henry-bull|Henry D. E. Bull]] in 1892 — a span of over three centuries [8]. Their ownership of Borley Hall also lasted until the end of the 1890s when it passed to the Payne family [9].

While Bull is accurate about the Waldegraves' 300-year patronage, earlier medieval records dispute who held the patronage *before* them. A 1308 document ("The Extent of the Manor of Borley") claims the Lord of the Manor was the true patron, but researchers point out this is contradicted by *Newcourt's Repertorium*, which states patronage remained with the King, and *Morant's History of Essex*, which notes the Crown held the advowson until 1364 when Edward III gave it to Christ Church, Canterbury [10–12].

## Status

The booklet is described as rare. It remains the most detailed pre-war architectural description of the church available. Its author's relationship to the [[henry-bull|Bull family]] of Borley Rectory is unconfirmed but plausible given the shared surname.
