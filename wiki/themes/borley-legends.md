# Borley Legends

The popular traditional stories that became attached to the Borley haunting, most of which cannot be historically verified and some of which are demonstrably false. The legends form the narrative framework through which the phenomena were interpreted, but must be distinguished from the phenomena themselves.

## The Monastery Legend

The foundational legend, circulating in the village when [[harry-price]] first arrived in 1929, held that a 13th- or 14th-century monastery once occupied the site of [[borley-rectory]], with a nunnery located some seven miles away in the village of [[bures-en-bray|Bures]] [1, 2]. In 1938, [[sidney-glanville]] consulted the Essex Archaeological Society, which confirmed definitively that no religious house or ecclesiastical building other than the 12th-century parish church ever existed at Borley, and that there is no evidence of a nunnery at Bures either [4, 6, 7]. [[andrew-clarke]], drawing on local records in his *Sidelights*, demonstrated that 15 years of systematic newspaper research from 1780 onward found no mention of any haunting whatsoever at Borley before 1929.

The "monastery" confusion is historically traceable: in 1362, King Edward III granted the Manor of Borley to the Prior and Community of Christ Church, Canterbury [12, 13]. A Benedictine Priory ([[st-bartholomews-priory|St Bartholomew's]]) also existed across the River Stour and held "Free Warren" in Borley [14, 15]. These administrative links to distant monastic institutions — not a local house — reportedly mutated into the rumour of a physical monastery on the site.

## The Elopement Legend

A monk (or groom) at the monastery fell in love with a novice from [[bures-en-bray|Bures]]. According to the tale, the lovers held clandestine meetings in the woods and eventually arranged to elope in a coach drawn by two bay horses, driven by a fellow lay-brother [1–3]. The elopement was discovered; the men were hanged or beheaded, and the young nun was bricked up alive within the walls of her convent [1–3]. This tragic narrative was used to explain the Rectory's most famous apparitions: [[the-nun|a phantom nun]] wandering the grounds with her head bowed in sorrow, and a [[phantom-coach|spectral coach driven by headless coachmen]] [2, 4, 5].

The BRC notes this origin story cannot explain the haunting. Coaches were unknown in England before 1569; the Dissolution of the Monasteries occurred in 1538. If one accepts the elopement tale, the lovers could not have escaped in an "old-fashioned coach." [[dr-letitia-fairfield|Dr Letitia Fairfield]] pointed out this anachronism explicitly [9, 10]. [[father-herbert-thurston|Father Herbert Thurston]] conducted an extensive historical analysis (published in *The Month* in 1892) and concluded that there is absolutely no record of nuns ever being immured or walled up alive in Britain; the concept was a popular Gothic fiction trope [9, 11].

The legend of the phantom coach and headless coachman was almost certainly borrowed from the neighbouring parish of Acton, where an identical story was published in the *Bury and Norwich Post* in March 1852 — 77 years before Borley's first public haunting report [19, 20].

## The Marie Lairre Theory

The séance data of 1937–38 introduced a more specific narrative: a French Roman Catholic novice named [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]], aged 19, from Le Havre, was brought to England by a member of the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]], married, and ultimately murdered and buried in the cellars of a building that preceded [[borley-rectory]] [21–23]. Her date of death was given as 17 May 1667. Canon W. J. Phythian-Adams built an elaborate theory around this information, combining it with his reading of the [[wall-writings]], concluding that her body was thrown down a well in the cellar [24–28]. [[harry-price]] championed this theory in *The End of Borley Rectory*, especially after discovering bone fragments and French Catholic medallions in the cellar in 1943 [24, 29, 30].

> **Contradiction:** The authors of the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] — [[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], and [[trevor-hall]] — attacked this theory as an elaborate fiction [24, 31]. They accused Price of deliberately planting the French medals and the bones in the Rectory to fraudulently validate the Marie Lairre story and secure a sensational climax for his book, noting that he only promoted the French nun theory *after* his initial English monastery legend was debunked [6, 27, 31–33].

## Alternative Identities for the Nun

- **[[arabella-waldegrave|Arabella Waldegrave]]**: [[georgina-dawson|Georgina Dawson]] proposed that the ghost nun was Arabella Waldegrave (born 1687), taken to France as an infant, placed in a convent at Pontoise, who eventually returned to England and was murdered at Borley [34–36].
- **Mary Lycett's synthesis**: Combined the Phythian-Adams and Dawson theories, proposing that Arabella Waldegrave and Marie Lairre were the same person — "a story concerning Arabella's mother, Henrietta Waldegrave, who is a Stuart spy, and some secret documents."
- **[[barbara-waldegrave|Barbara Waldegrave (Sister Apollonia)]]**: [[ivan-banks|Ivan Banks]]' candidate — born at Borley 1603, died in Brussels 1638 aged ~36; the jawbone dated ~30 years is consistent [37, 38].
- **Katie Boreham**: Other séance data introduced "Katie Boreham," a maid who allegedly died at Easter 1888 [39, 40]. Banks speculated she died following an illicit affair with [[henry-bull|the Rev. Henry Bull]] and a botched abortion, suggesting the hauntings were rooted in a 19th-century cover-up by the Bull family [41–43].
- **Simon of Sudbury**: Archbishop of Canterbury beheaded on Tower Hill in 1381; his head is still preserved in St Gregory's church, Sudbury.

## Other Legends

- **The Screaming Girl**: Alleged story of a girl in a blue or white dress who fell to her death from the [[blue-room]] window sill through the verandah roof. Sceptics note the architectural pitch of the verandah roof directly below the window makes such a dangling fall impossible, rendering the story highly dubious [44–46].
- **The Headless/Legless Man**: The Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] supposedly saw a headless (or legless) man walking in the garden [47, 48]. Price's original contemporary notes reveal that Bull actually just saw a poacher's legs hidden behind some fruit trees [49–51]. Critics use this to demonstrate how Price and others exaggerated mundane events into spectacular supernatural legends [52–54].

## Chicksands Parallel

The BRC notes a telling parallel with Chicksands Priory in Bedfordshire: a very similar legend (monk beheaded, nun walled up, ghost walks on the 17th of each month) only appears in written form in the 1950s — after *MHH* and *EBR* were published. With the eleven-day calendar change of 1752, the 17th would become the 28th — the famous July 28th date at Borley. This suggests Price's publications may have inspired local legends elsewhere, raising a larger question about the direction of influence.

## The Media's Role in Cementing the Legend

The local folklore officially transformed into the legend of "The Most Haunted House in England" in June 1929, when the Rev. [[rev-eric-smith|Guy Eric Smith]] and his wife [[mabel-smith|Mabel]] contacted the *Daily Mirror* — reportedly partly because Mabel was gathering material for a supernatural thriller she was writing, titled [[murder-at-the-parsonage|*Murder at the Parsonage*]] [57, 58]. The *Daily Mirror* sent reporter V. C. Wall and [[harry-price]] to the scene, and their sensationalised daily dispatches permanently elevated localized village fables into the legend of "The Most Haunted House in England" [59–62].

## Assessment

The legends are largely the creation of an evolving oral tradition within the Bull family and the village, elaborated by [[harry-price]]'s journalistic instincts and reinforced by the séance data. The [[borley-legend-origins]] page discusses the specific mechanisms by which these stories were constructed. The BRC emphasises that the presence or absence of the legends should not be confused with the presence or absence of genuine phenomena — the two questions are independent.
