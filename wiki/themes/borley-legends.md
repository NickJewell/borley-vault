# Borley Legends

The popular traditional stories that became attached to the Borley haunting, most of which cannot be historically verified and some of which are demonstrably false. The legends form the narrative framework through which the phenomena were interpreted, but must be distinguished from the phenomena themselves.

## The Monastery Legend

The foundational legend, circulating in the village when [[harry-price]] first arrived in 1929, held that a monastery once occupied the site of [[borley-rectory]]. This supposed monastery formed the backdrop to the romantic elopement story (see below). [[andrew-clarke]], drawing on local records in his [[andrew-clarke|*Sidelights*]], demonstrated that no documentary evidence exists for a monastery at Borley — the site's ecclesiastical associations were through ownership by distant monastic institutions (Barking, Canterbury), not through a local house. [[ivan-banks]] confirmed this finding.

## The Elopement Legend

A groom at the monastery fell in love with a nun at a "nearby convent." Their elopement ended in tragedy: in one version the couple quarrelled and the nun was strangled while the grooms were beheaded; in another, they were caught, the grooms beheaded and the nun bricked up alive.

The BRC notes this origin story cannot explain the haunting. Coaches were unknown in England before 1569; the Dissolution of the Monasteries occurred in 1538. If one accepts the elopement tale, the lovers could not have escaped in an "old-fashioned coach" — they would have used an ox-drawn cart.

## The Marie Lairre Theory

The séance data of 1937–38 (see [[seance-data]]) introduced a more specific narrative: a French Roman Catholic novice named [[marie-lairre]], aged 19, was brought to England by a member of the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]], married, abandoned, and ultimately murdered and buried in the cellars of a building that preceded [[borley-rectory]]. Her date of death was given as 17 May 1667. [[canon-phythian-adams]] built an elaborate theory around this information, combining it with his reading of the [[wall-writings]].

## Other Legends

- **Arabella Waldegrave**: [[georgina-dawson]] proposed that the ghost nun was Arabella Waldegrave (born 1687), taken to France as an infant, placed in a convent at Pontoise, who eventually returned to England and was murdered at Borley
- **Mary Lycett's synthesis**: Combined the Phythian-Adams and Dawson theories, proposing that Arabella Waldegrave and Marie Lairre were the same person — "a story concerning Arabella's mother, Henrietta Waldegrave, who is a Stuart spy, and some secret documents"
- **[[barbara-waldegrave|Barbara Waldegrave (Sister Apollonia)]]**: [[ivan-banks]]' candidate — born at Borley 1603, died in Brussels 1638 aged ~36; the jawbone dated ~30 years is consistent
- **Simon of Sudbury**: Archbishop of Canterbury beheaded on Tower Hill in 1381; his head is still preserved in St Gregory's church, Sudbury
- **The Screaming Girl**: Alleged story of a girl who fell to her death from the [[blue-room]] window sill through the verandah roof — almost certainly fictional, the physical layout of the verandah makes the story implausible

## Chicksands Parallel

The BRC notes a telling parallel with Chicksands Priory in Bedfordshire: a very similar legend (monk beheaded, nun walled up, ghost walks on the 17th of each month) only appears in written form in the 1950s — after *MHH* and *EBR* were published. With the eleven-day calendar change of 1752, the 17th would become the 28th — the famous [[july-28th]] date at Borley. This suggests Price's publications may have inspired local legends elsewhere, raising a larger question about the direction of influence.

## Assessment

The legends are largely the creation of an evolving oral tradition within the [[bull-family]] and the village, elaborated by [[harry-price]]'s journalistic instincts and reinforced by the séance data. The [[borley-legend-origins|origins of the Borley legend theme]] discusses the specific mechanisms by which these stories were constructed. The BRC emphasises that the presence or absence of the legends should not be confused with the presence or absence of genuine phenomena — the two questions are independent.
