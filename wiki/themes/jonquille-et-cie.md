# Jonquil et Cie

"Jonquille et Cie" (Daffodil and Co.) was a florist shop at 20a Worple Road, Wimbledon, South London, co-owned and operated by [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] and [[francois-darles|François d'Arles (Frank Pearless)]] during 1932–34, while Marianne was nominally the rector's wife at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]]. It became the base for their affair and the operational headquarters from which Marianne spent most of her time away from Borley.

## Operation

The shop opened in late 1932 or early 1933 and lasted approximately eighteen months before failing in November 1934. During the week, Marianne and d'Arles lived as man and wife in the flat above the premises — informing their neighbours in Worple Road that they were a married couple — returning to Borley only at weekends. Marianne's son [[ian-greenwood|Ian Shaw]] also moved to Wimbledon to help them run the shop, and lived above the premises for a period after the Foyster rectory years.

## Lionel Foyster's Role

Skeptical investigators, particularly [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] and [[robert-wood|Robert Wood]], argue that [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] was not a credulous, innocent dupe ignorant of his wife's activities. Two pieces of evidence bear on this:

- **Financing the shop:** Lionel Foyster actually financed the Wimbledon florist business for his wife and her lover [2, 6].
- **The "father" deception:** When Lionel visited Marianne in Wimbledon, he actively participated in a deception — concealing his true relationship from neighbours such as Mr. and Mrs. Fenton (who ran the chemist's shop in the same parade) by pretending to be Marianne's father, calling himself "Leon Alphonse" [2, 7, 8]. Wood argues this is conclusive evidence of collusion, proving Lionel was a willing accomplice to her deceptions [7].

## Significance for the Borley Case

The flower shop bears on the Borley case in several ways:

1. It explains Marianne's frequent and prolonged absences from [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], leaving the frail [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] alone to look after the two children.
2. It establishes the nature and depth of Marianne's relationship with d'Arles — which either developed into, or had already become, a full affair before the shop opened.
3. It offers a partial explanation of why d'Arles agreed to live at Borley as the cottage tenant: he and Marianne were already commercially and personally entangled.

Robert Wood (*The Widow of Borley*) doubted whether d'Arles would have read *The Times* personal columns through which the supposed adoption arrangement for his son François junior was made, arguing Marianne met d'Arles casually in London. Babbs and Mathias note that whether the connection began at the shop or preceded it, the shop served as the locus of the relationship during the Borley years.

## Demise and Aftermath

The business ultimately failed and closed in November 1934 [3, 9]. According to the sources, the final split came when Marianne discovered that d'Arles was sleeping with a sixteen-year-old girl they had hired to help in the shop [10]. Marianne ended the relationship; d'Arles subsequently set up a new business elsewhere in Wimbledon with his new girlfriend [10]. The breakup was reportedly fraught with recriminations, with d'Arles threatening to blackmail the Foysters by exposing their domestic arrangements to the Bishop of Chelmsford [4, 11].

## The Haunting Debate

The Jonquille et Cie episode features prominently in the debate over the authenticity of the Borley phenomena during the Foyster incumbency:

- **The smokescreen argument:** Skeptics argue that Marianne used the poltergeist phenomena as a smokescreen to cover up her illicit behaviour with d'Arles, or actively faked the hauntings to force Lionel to leave Borley so she could pursue her London life [12, 13].
- **The pro-paranormal counter-argument:** Conversely, defenders of the haunting point out that the shop provides evidence *in favour* of the paranormal. Marianne noted in a 1956 letter that unexplained things were still happening at Borley Rectory during the week while she was miles away in London at Jonquille et Cie — which suggests she could not have been responsible for all the reported phenomena [14].
