# Jonquil et Cie

A flower shop at 20a Worple Road, Wimbledon, co-owned and operated by [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] and [[francois-darles|François d'Arles (Frank Pearless)]] during 1932–34, while Marianne was nominally the rector's wife at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]]. It became the base for their affair and the operational headquarters from which Marianne spent most of her time away from Borley.

## Operation

The shop was opened roughly in 1932 and lasted approximately 18 months before failing in 1934. During the week, Marianne and d'Arles were at the shop, returning to Borley only at weekends. They lived in the flat above the premises and informed their neighbours in Worple Road that they were a married couple.

Marianne's absent son [[ian-greenwood|Ian Shaw]] also lived above the shop for a period after the Foyster rectory years.

## Significance for the Borley Case

The flower shop explains:
1. Marianne's frequent and prolonged absences from Borley Rectory, leaving the frail [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] alone to look after the two children
2. The nature of Marianne's relationship with d'Arles, which developed into an affair — or may have been established before Borley
3. A partial explanation of why d'Arles agreed to live at Borley as the cottage tenant: he and Marianne were already commercially and personally entangled

Robert Wood (*The Widow of Borley*) doubted whether d'Arles would have read *The Times* personal columns through which the supposed adoption arrangement for his son François junior was made. He argues Marianne met d'Arles casually in London. Babbs and Mathias note that whether the connection began at the shop or preceded it, the shop served as the locus of the relationship during the Borley years.

The failure of the business in 1934 — with "an atmosphere of considerable bitterness" at the parting — contributed to d'Arles's departure from the Borley scene and the end of the most turbulent period of the Foyster incumbency.
