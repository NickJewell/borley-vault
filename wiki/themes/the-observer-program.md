# The Price Observer Program (1937–38)

The most systematically organised psychical investigation in the Borley case, conducted by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] during his one-year tenancy of [[borley-rectory]] (May 1937 – May 1938). The first time in the history of psychical research that formal printed instructions were produced for investigating a specific haunted house.

## Origins and Structure

**Tenancy**: Price rented the Rectory from [[rev-ac-henning]] at £30 per year (Henning paying the rates), becoming the first lay tenant since the building was erected in 1862. His primary motivation for the formal program was to *exclude himself* from the inquiry: "I reiterate that I wanted, as far as possible, to eliminate myself and my friends, and all previous occupiers, from taking any active part in my proposed inquiry." [1]

**Recruitment**: On 25 May 1937, Price placed an advertisement in *The Times* seeking "responsible persons of leisure and intelligence, intrepid, critical and unbiased" [2–4]. From over 200 applicants, Price selected 48 "official observers" [5, 6]. Accepted observers were required to sign a Declaration Form and received the printed [[blue-book-of-instructions|Blue Book]] of instructions.

**Selection criteria**: Price deliberately chose people with a scientific or professional background — Oxford and Cambridge undergraduates, BBC engineers, military officers, and doctors — specifically with *no* prior knowledge of psychical research or spiritualism, believing this would make their testimony more legally and scientifically credible [7, 8]. Key observers included the consulting engineer [[sidney-glanville|Sidney H. Glanville]], his son [[roger-glanville|Roger]], and diplomat Mark Kerr-Pearse [9].

**The Base Room**: Price and his colleague [[ellic-howe|Ellic Howe]] established a "Base Room" in the Rectory's library, furnishing it with a camp bed, lamps, and provisions. All movable objects throughout the house were chalk-ringed to detect paranormal movement [10–12].

**Total participants**: 48 official observers; almost all reported at least some phenomena.

## Methodology

- Every portable object "ringed" with chalk; any movement to be noted
- All walls checked for pencil markings; new markings ringed and dated
- All external doors and windows sealed on entry
- Hourly patrols of the house
- Half-hour watch from the octagonal summer-house before and after dusk
- Written reports submitted immediately after each observational period
- Price appointed Arbon (cottage tenant) as key-keeper; only authorised observers could access the house

## The Blue Book

Price called the Blue Book "for the first time in the history of psychical research, a book of instructions... for the guidance of those investigating a particular haunted house." It includes:
- 20 formal instructions covering search procedures, sealing, patrols, meals, rest
- Specific protocols for each phenomenon type (bells, objects, footsteps, apparitions, raps)
- For apparitions: "do not approach the figure"; ask name, age, sex, origin, cause of visit
- For raps: use alphabet and yes/no to communicate
- Final caution: check all phenomena for normal explanations (rats, small boys, wind, wood, birds)

The Monotype Corporation asked to place a copy in their museum.

The Blue Book also included a comprehensive list of all phenomena alleged to have occurred at Borley over the past forty years, including bell-ringing, footsteps, apparitions (specifically instructing observers to watch the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]]), and raps [14–16].

## Phenomena Recorded

All types of phenomena previously reported at the Rectory recurred during the observer program:

**Objects moving**: Mark Kerr-Pearse found a 50-lb coal bag moved 18 inches; a tobacco tin moved upstairs and placed precisely over a secretly pencilled cross (Glanville); multiple control objects moved millimetres to feet from chalk rings; the mysterious round blue box appeared in a cupboard at midnight after thorough earlier search. Dr. H.F. Bellamy and his son reported an incident where a pile of books on a mantelpiece shifted, triggering an electrical contact-breaker bell they had set up [28–30].

**Wall writings appearing**: Most dramatic systematic confirmation — Kerr-Pearse, [[sidney-glanville|Glanville]] (photographically proved), Savage (watched three marks appear within a yard of the mantelpiece), Bailey and Wintour, Drinkwater. BBC engineer M. Savage and his friend reported seeing marks, including one resembling the "Prince of Wales's feathers," appear almost before their eyes [26, 31, 32]. All in locked, sealed houses.

**Sounds**: Footsteps, thuds, door-closings, clicks, scrabbling — across virtually all observers' reports. On November 20, 1937, [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] and three others reported a window blind in the sealed Base Room waving with a "palpitating action." They blew cigarette smoke at it to test for drafts, but the smoke did not disperse, leading them to view it as unexplained [33, 34].

**Locking**: Kerr-Pearse locked in the Base Room with the key on the inside, alone in the house.

**The nun**: Mrs. Lloyd Williams (BBC) saw "a round dark object" move on the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] [38, 39].

**Luminous phenomenon**: S. G. Welles, a Rhodes Scholar, reported a rectangular luminous patch moving on the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] ceiling for about a minute [40, 41] — all subsequent experiments failed to reproduce it.

**Cold spot**: Three independent observers (Horniman's friend, Miss Reid, Rupert Haig) experienced sudden intense cold at the same spot on the landing outside the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] at different times [35–37].

**Strange appearances**: The "strange coat" appearing in the Blue Room; the gold wedding ring (1864 hallmark) appearing after six thorough searches on the same day.

## Price's Self-Exclusion

Key point: observer-period phenomena — unlike [[foyster-phenomena|Foyster-period]] phenomena — cannot be attributed to [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]]. The 48 observers had no connection to any previous occupant.

Price ([[mhh-ch32]]): "The evidence submitted by our witnesses for the haunting of Borley Rectory would be acceptable in a court of law in consideration of their number, their integrity, their unemotionalism, their disinterestedness, and their skill."

## Criticisms of Price's Methods and Integrity

Sceptical investigators levelled heavy criticisms against how Price managed the program and reported its findings in his bestseller, *The Most Haunted House in England*.

- **The Blue Book as suggestion:** The authors of the SPR [[the-borley-report|*Borley Report*]] ([[eric-dingwall|Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Hall]]), as well as researcher Andrew MacKenzie, strongly condemned the Blue Book. They argued it acted as a "barrage of suggestion" that actively conditioned inexperienced observers to expect supernatural events, leading them to misinterpret natural house noises, drafts, or their own fears as paranormal [3, 17–21].
- **Suppression of natural explanations:** The SPR authors accused Price of deliberate deception by editing out observers' natural explanations to make the book more sensational. Observer Mark Kerr-Pearse noted that "scrabbling" sounds were likely mice, and observer M. Knox noted that thuds were likely caused by bushes hitting the window in the wind; Price reportedly suppressed both explanations [42–45].
- **Lack of central control:** Critics, including [[sidney-glanville|Glanville]] himself, noted the investigation lacked a central logbook in the Rectory, meaning each batch of observers arrived completely ignorant of what the previous group had done or rearranged [19, 46, 47].
- **Intruders and contamination:** Sceptics argued the house was not secure. The caretaker, Lionel Arbon, gave the keys out readily [48, 49]. Unauthorised persons were known to have entered the grounds (e.g., digging up the [[cats-cemetery|cats' cemetery]] and entering through windows), meaning "paranormal" displacements could easily have been the work of intruders [50].
- **Accusations of fakery:** Observer Major Henry Douglas-Home suspected Price of faking phenomena, claiming fresh pencil marks only appeared on the walls when Price was walking at the rear of the group, and accusing Price of creating a ghostly "rustling" noise by secretly crinkling cellophane paper in his pocket [51, 52].

> **Contradiction:** The Blue Book explicitly contained an "Important Note" warning observers that the "greatest effort should be made to ascertain whether such manifestations are due to normal causes, such as rats, small boys, the villagers, the wind, wood shrinking, the Death Watch beetle, farm animals nosing the doors..." [22, 23]. The SPR's characterisation of it as pure suggestion omits this caveat.

## Defences of the Observer Program

- **The open-files argument:** [[robert-hastings|Robert J. Hastings]], who re-examined the SPR's allegations, provided the strongest defence. Hastings pointed out that Price deposited all his unedited observer reports and correspondence in the University of London library for public inspection *during his own lifetime*. If Price were a deliberate fraudster, he would never have preserved and made available the very documents that exposed his editorial discrepancies [53, 54].
- **Glanville's defence of site security:** [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] strongly pushed back against the idea that villagers or tricksters were breaking in to fool them. He stated that the Rectory was meticulously sealed during their visits and that locals were actually terrified of the place and avoided it after dark [55].
- **Scientific journalism:** Defenders argue that Price's book was intended to be "scientific journalism" rather than a strictly academic paper. His goal was to present a readable, compelling narrative to the public, while leaving the raw data in his university files for serious researchers to consult [56–59].

The [[locked-book|Locked Book]] (compiled by [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]]), preserved in Price's archive, is the primary documentary record of the observer program.
