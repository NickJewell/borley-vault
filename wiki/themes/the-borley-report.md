# The Borley Report (1956)

The formal title is *The Haunting of Borley Rectory: A Critical Survey of the Evidence* (1956), authored by [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]]. Published simultaneously in the SPR's *Proceedings* and as a standalone Duckworth volume. The most significant formal examination of the Borley case — and the most disputed.

## Background

After [[harry-price|Harry Price's]] death in March 1948, [[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]] and [[william-salter|William Salter]] (SPR honorary secretary) began a systematic investigation of Price's archive at the University of London. The project was framed from the outset as an exposure of Price rather than a neutral investigation.

[[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] was recruited in 1949 after a Magic Circle lecture by [[eric-dingwall|Dingwall]]. O'Connor describes Hall as "a supreme egotist, inflated to the breaking point with self-importance." His involvement skewed the Report toward outright hostility.

The sources strongly suggest the SPR's attack was fuelled by a long-standing rivalry between the patrician, academic SPR and Price's own populist organisation, the [[national-laboratory|National Laboratory of Psychical Research]] [25]. O'Connor's analysis holds the Report as Salter's vehicle for settling his longstanding grievance against Price (the two had been rivals since 1929). [[eric-dingwall|Dingwall]] had worked with Price for decades and resented him. Hall had never met Price but developed a near-fanatical hostility. [[kathleen-goldney|Goldney]] was the most objective of the three, but was guided by Salter. The society was also motivated by protecting its own prestige: Price had excluded it from the Borley tenancy investigation.

## Key Findings (Primary Source)

The report concluded that the evidence for a genuine haunting was "so slender as to be scientifically worthless" [1, 5], and charged Price with wilful deception and fraud [1]. Specific allegations:

1. **Price faked phenomena**: [[charles-sutton]] (Daily Mail) claimed to have caught Price with pockets full of stones in July 1929; [[lord-charles-hope]]'s *contemporary* handwritten notes allegedly corroborate Sutton's account; Major Douglas-Home (1937) independently documented pencil marks appearing only when Price was at the rear — ceasing when he positioned himself behind Price; cellophane paper found in Price's suitcase explaining "rustling" phenomena [1, 6, 7].
2. **The medal substitution**: The 1929 "apport" was a St Ignatius Loyola medal (monk's head, confirmed by Hope's notes, Lucie Kaye, Price's own 1929 article). In *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940) Price allegedly substituted two different medals supporting the now-dominant "French Catholic nun" theory. The original medal was found in his files after his death but never mentioned in either book [14–17].
3. **Price primed observers**: The [[blue-book-of-instructions|Blue Book]] planted detailed suggestions; observers were instructed to watch from the summer-house at dusk for "the black draped figure" and to expect footsteps, bell-ringing, apparitions etc. as a matter of forty years' documented evidence. Mrs Lloyd Williams's "nun sighting" was entirely a product of these suggestions — her companion dismissed it but Price listed her among 14 nun-witnesses [16–22].
4. **Price manipulated evidence**: Observer reports systematically edited — mice and rat evidence removed; rose-tree knocking explanation removed; Kerr-Pearse's "Well-Tank-Bottom-Me" reading (1937) suppressed so Phythian-Adams could "discover" it in 1941; [[sidney-glanville|Glanville's]] statement that no second photograph existed contradicted by Price's invention of a second photograph in *The Most Haunted House in England*; all positive mentions of Canon Lawton's name suppressed despite Lawton living at the rectory a full month without phenomena [42–45].
5. **The acoustic effect**: Major Douglas-Home documented that the Arbons' footsteps crossing the 18-foot-distant courtyard "sounded as if they were in the corridor of the house"; "words spoken outside the pantry were strongly heard in the Base"; the hand-pump at night produced "thumps, groans-galore" — the single most important explanation for observer-period "footsteps" and "knocking," entirely ignored by Price.
6. **The bones were suspicious**: Price excluded [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] (chief observer, near neighbour, loyal friend) from the 1943 excavation; electrical wiring found in the cellar during demolition had never been seen during any investigation; bones were only 3 feet down (inconsistent with Phythian-Adams's "deep well" theory); the "well tank" had allegedly been bricked up by unknown persons between 1939 and 1943 despite the rectory being uninhabited ruins [9–11, 47–49].
7. **The "flying brick" confirmed normal**: Cynthia Thompson's signed statement (1950) to the SPR: demolition workmen were actively at work behind the wall; all three present saw them; she had joked "Look, poltergeists"; Price turned this into a paranormal claim in *The End of Borley Rectory* [8–10].
8. **Marianne Foyster: 99/103 incidents**: A systematic analysis of [[lionel-foyster|Foyster's]] own *Diary of Occurrences* shows 99 of 103 documented incidents depend entirely on [[marianne-foyster|Marianne's]] good faith. The Amherst parallel (the Foysters had lived near the Esther Cox case in Sackville, Nova Scotia) and the graphological evidence on the [[wall-writings|wall-writings]] both support normal causation [27–29].
9. **Price's private view**: Four letters spanning 1931–1946 show Price privately believed Marianne was responsible; he told Foyster "no word of this will appear in our report" in 1938 while simultaneously planning to publish the Foyster period as the centrepiece of his haunting evidence.

## Public Reception

At the time of publication, the report was widely accepted by the mainstream press. Professor Anthony Flew wrote in *The Spectator* that the document offered satisfaction at last, asserting that "Harry Price was able to erect and maintain for years the house of cards which he built out of little more than a pack of lies" [30–32].

Renée Haynes, author of the official history of the SPR, noted that the report was presented with the "cold, controlled, unremitting fury of a road drill" [29, 30]. She observed that every incident was interpreted to put Price in the worst possible light, operating on the presumption of guilt — what she likened to "the French system of criminal procedure, in which the accused is presumed to be guilty until he can prove himself innocent."

The BBC made a programme (*The Haunted Rectory*) based on the Report; it was cancelled before broadcast by BBC legal due to potential libel from [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster's]] lawyers. **It has never been broadcast.**

## Counter-Criticism

**[[robert-hastings|Robert Hastings]]** (1969, funded by the SPR): found that "every mistake I noticed tended in a direction unfavourable to Price." The Report was a prosecutor's brief with no attempt to find arguments in Price's defence.

- **The open-archive argument:** Price openly deposited his archive at the University of London — not the act of a man who thought it incriminating [34, 35]. Hastings argued that a deliberate fraudster would never preserve and expose the very documents that could be used to incriminate him [34].
- **Rebutting the Sutton affair:** Hastings and Dr. Alan Gauld re-interviewed [[charles-sutton|Charles Sutton]] and exposed major contradictions. Sutton admitted he never actually *saw* Price throw any stones, nor did he look for them on the floor [42, 43]. Price's secretary, Lucie Kaye, vehemently denied Sutton's account, and correspondence proved Price and Sutton remained on friendly terms for years after the supposed "exposure" [44–46].
- **Rebutting the planted bones:** Authors like Edward Babbs pointed out that Price suffered from severe angina and was physically incapable of digging up the heavy clay cellar floor to plant bones secretly [47–49]. Price arranged a Christian burial for the remains — an act of sacrilege a devout churchgoer like Price was highly unlikely to commit for a hoax [50].
- **Selective editing:** Hastings demonstrated that Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall frequently omitted testimony that supported the paranormal or contradicted their sceptical theories [41].
- **Character defences:** Dr. [[nandor-fodor|Nandor Fodor]], a psychoanalyst who personally disliked Price, publicly defended him, calling the *Borley Report* a "scandalous piece of writing" and accusing [[eric-dingwall|Dingwall]] of trying to wreck psychical research for decades [51–53]. R.F. Buckley wrote a critique arguing that the report "seethes with spleen like a suppurating ulcer" and was inspired by "envy, hatred and malice" [35, 54].

[[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] and [[paul-tabori|Paul Tabori]] (Price's literary executor) consistently defended Price's reputation, characterising the Report as "the case for the prosecution with no allowance for the defence."

## The Report's Internal Collapse

[[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]] told [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] at an SPR meeting — in front of witnesses — that she had "no doubt whatsoever that genuine paranormal activity did take place at Borley," directly contradicting the Report she had co-authored. She grew to regret her association with it.

The Report's authors fell out publicly: Goldney and Dingwall were not on speaking terms with Hall within months of publication. [[eric-dingwall|Dingwall]] told an SPR Council member in 1962: "I told Trevor he was claiming too much, but it is too late to do anything about it now."

## The Hastings Report (1969)

The SPR's own Research Advisory Committee in 1965 authorised [[robert-hastings|Robert J. Hastings]] to reexamine all the evidence. His *An Examination of the "Borley Report"* (SPR *Proceedings* Vol. 55, March 1969) is the most systematic counter-argument. Hastings examined each accusation in turn and found:

- The Sutton accusation rested entirely on an uncorroborated account written 20 years later in a journalistic article
- Mabel Smith's testimony was contradictory across multiple interviews
- The Douglas-Home "cellophane" allegation was physically impossible as described
- The flying brick photo involved no claim of paranormality by Price; it was simply incautious publishing
- The bones were almost certainly not planted — the well tank never existed, so there was no pre-prepared cavity (see [[well-tank-controversy]])

## The Companion's Assessment

The *Borley Rectory Companion* examines the Report "with a suitable distance of time from the events in question, and without the personal involvement that affected much of the re-examination." Its conclusion: the Report was the product of people who were unable to be impartial — Salter's personal grievance, Dingwall's long rivalry with Price, Hall's "unmitigated malice" (John Randall's phrase).

What survived the Report's scrutiny: [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville's]] evidence, acknowledged even in the Report as "one of the most puzzling features of the whole case."

## Randall's Academic Assessment

John Randall (Price's *New Dictionary of National Biography* author; 4-year in-depth study) published "Harry Price: The Case for the Defence" in the SPR Journal (July 2000). Key points:

- **Salter's 1931 visit** to Borley to persuade Foyster to sever links with Price was "a clear example of unprofessional conduct" — comparable to a doctor trying to steal a colleague's patients.
- **Hall's profile**: "an estate agent, local magistrate, amateur conjurer and writer of popular books on Sherlock Holmes... conceived an almost fanatical hatred for the famous ghost-hunter" despite never having met Price.
- Hall's involvement gave the Report its "uncompromisingly hostile tone."
- The Report was "badly flawed and very heavily biased."
- Final verdict: "the Borley case remains the most fully documented example of a haunting in the annals of psychical research."

## Constance Price's View

Price's widow Constance Price, interviewed by [[peter-underwood|Underwood]] in October 1968, on the Report's co-authors: "I think Mrs Goldney was a two-faced woman and Eric Dingwall was no better. They had often been there and she had looked on them as friends." She had not read the book: her bank trustee advised against it.
