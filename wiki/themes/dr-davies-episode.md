# The Dr Davies Episode (1941–42)

A six-month relationship between [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] and an unnamed Midlands doctor ([[dr-davies|Dr Davies]]) conducted at and around the **Grosvenor Hotel, Victoria**, during the Second World War. One of the more sinister episodes in Marianne's biography, and a clear case-study in the pattern of exploitation Wood identifies across her adult life.

## Background

[[dr-davies|Dr Davies]] was a general practitioner who suffered a complete emotional collapse after his wife [[mabel-davies|Mabel]] died of cancer. He abandoned his practice, moved to the Grosvenor Hotel, and took up spiritualism with the specific aim of contacting Mabel. He read [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s *The Most Haunted House in England* (published 1940) and concluded that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] was "clearly the most powerful medium who had ever lived." He wrote to Price for her address, and a meeting was arranged in London in autumn 1941.

Davies was forty-five; Marianne was forty-two. He described her as exceedingly attractive, with luminous dark eyes, graceful movements, and the ability to converse across a wide range of subjects. He also noticed "something satanic" about her regard when she thought he wasn't watching.

## The Relationship

Davies paid Marianne's train fare from [[dairy-cottage-rendlesham|Wickham Market]] to London for their meetings. They attended the Society for Psychical Research offices together. An SPR official remembered them as on "very friendly terms." Marianne occasionally stayed overnight at the hotel.

The defining feature of the relationship was Marianne's use of **fake spiritualist trances**: she fell into apparent trances in the hotel lounge and embraced Davies, allowing him to believe it was the spirit of his dead wife. This stratagem gave both parties cover — for Davies, it was religious devotion; for Marianne, it was professional mediumship. Wood remarks dryly that "this called for a good deal of imagination on everyone's part."

Marianne also told Davies truthfully that her husband [[lionel-foyster|"Lion"]] was not expected to live long — positioning herself as a prospective widow. She enquired about Davies's financial situation.

## The Break

In March 1942, after Davies told Marianne his capital was running low, she wrote ending the relationship. She requested the return of her letters; he returned them, but she never returned his, leaving him to fear blackmail for years thereafter.

Her final communication was extraordinary:

> "Marianne's final touch is astounding. Davies had given her many of his dead wife's clothes, and she returned these to him twisted into strange doll-like shapes, with mysterious pieces of cardboard upon which what Davies described as 'cabbalistic symbols' were inscribed. Not unnaturally he gained the impression that Marianne was trying to place a curse on him."

## Significance

### Pattern of Exploitation
The Davies episode fits the pattern Wood traces across Marianne's male victims: all were men with mental, emotional, or physical vulnerabilities — [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] (shell-shock, religious crisis), [[henry-fisher|Henry Fisher]] (head injury, mental instability), [[dr-davies|Dr Davies]] (grief). Marianne never sought out stable, healthy partners.

### Anticipation of Foyster's Death
By telling Davies that "Lion" was not expected to live long and asking about his finances, Marianne was clearly planning her next move. The timing — 1941–42 — precedes Foyster's death by three years, suggesting she was already looking for an exit from the Suffolk household when the opportunity arose (which eventually came in the form of [[robert-oneil|Robert O'Neil]]).

### The Curse
The cabbalistic symbols sent in Mabel Davies's clothes are Wood's strongest evidence for something cold and deliberate in Marianne's cruelty. The fake trance-embrace could be read as pathetic; the cursed clothes sent after a clean break cannot. Wood: "This incident, more than any other, shows Marianne's cruel and vindictive nature."

## Swanson's Interview

Marianne told Swanson:

> **Swanson:** Do you recall Dr Davies?  
> **Marianne:** Yes.  
> **Swanson:** Did you ever have sexual relations with him?  
> **Marianne:** No, I did not. The matter never came up in any way.

Wood considers this a lie. Davies insisted nothing improper occurred; Wood considers this unlikely given his years of feared blackmail. He lived for a long time afraid that Marianne would use his love-letters against him.
