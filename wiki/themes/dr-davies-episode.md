# The Dr Davies Episode (1941–42)

A six-month relationship between [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] and an unnamed Midlands doctor ([[dr-davies|Dr Davies]]) conducted at and around the **[[grosvenor-hotel-victoria|Grosvenor Hotel, Victoria]]**, during the Second World War. One of the more sinister episodes in Marianne's biography, and a clear case-study in the pattern of exploitation [[robert-wood|Wood]] identifies across her adult life.

## Background

[[dr-davies|Dr J.R.A. Davies]] was a general practitioner who suffered a complete emotional collapse after his wife [[mabel-davies|Mabel]] (affectionately known as "Tweatie") died of cancer. He abandoned his practice in the Midlands, moved to the Grosvenor Hotel, Victoria, and took up spiritualism with the specific aim of contacting Mabel [1, 2]. He read [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s *The Most Haunted House in England* (published 1940) and concluded that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] was "clearly the most powerful medium who had ever lived" [3]. He subsequently contacted Price for her address at [[dairy-cottage-rendlesham|Wickham Market]] and arranged a meeting in London in autumn 1941 [3, 4].

Davies was forty-five; Marianne was forty-two. He described her as exceedingly attractive, with luminous dark eyes, graceful movements, and the ability to converse across a wide range of subjects. He also noticed "something satanic" about her regard when she thought he wasn't watching.

## The Relationship

Davies paid Marianne's train fare from Suffolk to London for their meetings. They attended the [[spr-investigation|Society for Psychical Research]] offices together; an SPR official remembered them as on "very friendly terms" [6]. Marianne occasionally stayed overnight at the hotel.

The defining feature of the relationship was Marianne's use of **fake spiritualist trances**: she allegedly fell into apparent trances in the hotel lounge, attempted automatic writing, and embraced Davies while claiming she was channelling his dead wife, Tweatie [5]. Davies admitted that his scruples about remaining faithful to his wife were overcome by this stratagem, and the relationship reportedly escalated to physical intimacy when Marianne was not in a trance [5]. [[robert-wood|Wood]] remarks that "this called for a good deal of imagination on everyone's part."

Marianne also told Davies truthfully that her husband [[lionel-foyster|"Lion"]] was not expected to live long — positioning herself as a prospective widow — and enquired about Davies's financial situation.

## The Break

In March 1942, after Davies told Marianne his capital was running low, she abruptly ended the relationship [7]. She requested the return of her letters; he returned them, but she never returned his, leaving him to fear blackmail for years thereafter [7].

Her final communication was extraordinary:

> "Marianne's final touch is astounding. Davies had given her many of his dead wife's clothes, and she returned these to him twisted into strange doll-like shapes, with mysterious pieces of cardboard upon which what Davies described as 'cabbalistic symbols' were inscribed. Not unnaturally he gained the impression that Marianne was trying to place a curse on him." [7]

## Marianne's Account

When interrogated by private investigator Robert Swanson in 1958, Marianne presented a completely different version of events:

> **Swanson:** Do you recall Dr Davies?
> **Marianne:** Yes.
> **Swanson:** Did you ever have sexual relations with him?
> **Marianne:** No, I did not. The matter never came up in any way. [8]

She claimed Davies approached her merely because she was a "dead ringer" for his late wife, that she thought he was "mentally retarded" due to grief, and that she had refused to accompany him to see a medium in Hampstead because "That sort of thing always makes me feel ill" [8].

[[robert-wood|Wood]] entirely dismisses Marianne's defence as a lie [6]. He points out the massive contradiction in her claim that spiritualism made her "feel ill," questioning what she was doing meeting Davies in the first place and continuing to accept his paid train fares if she found his spiritualist interests so repulsive — and noting she was perfectly willing to visit the SPR offices with him [6].

## Dr Davies's Final Conclusion on Borley

Despite his initial belief that Marianne was a powerful medium, Dr Davies eventually completely rejected the Borley phenomena. In a signed statement submitted to the authors of the [[spr-investigation|*Borley Report*]], **Davies declared it was his "convinced opinion" that the famous Borley wall-messages were executed in "a thinly disguised version of Mrs Foyster's own handwriting"** [2].

## Significance

### Pattern of Exploitation

The Davies episode fits the pattern [[robert-wood|Wood]] traces across Marianne's male victims: all were men with mental, emotional, or physical vulnerabilities — [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] (shell-shock, religious crisis), [[henry-fisher|Henry Fisher]] (head injury, mental instability), and Davies (grief). Marianne never sought out stable, healthy partners.

### Anticipation of Foyster's Death

By telling Davies that "Lion" was not expected to live long and asking about his finances, Marianne was clearly planning her next move as early as 1941–42 — three years before Foyster's actual death — suggesting she was already looking for an exit from the Suffolk household when the opportunity arose (which eventually came in the form of [[robert-oneil|Robert O'Neil]]).

### The Curse

The cabbalistic symbols sent in Mabel Davies's clothes are [[robert-wood|Wood]]'s strongest evidence for something cold and deliberate in Marianne's cruelty. The fake trance-embrace could be read as pathetic; the cursed clothes sent after a clean break cannot. Wood: "This incident, more than any other, shows Marianne's cruel and vindictive nature."
