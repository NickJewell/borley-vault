# The Polstead Haunting

A series of alleged paranormal disturbances at [[polstead-rectory|Polstead Rectory]], a former Church of England rectory of approximately 400 years' age located about ten miles from [[borley-rectory|Borley]], over the county boundary in Suffolk. Babbs and Mathias discuss it at length as the closest parallel case to the Borley haunting, using it to argue both that Borley is not wholly unique and that the Church Commissioners handled it far more decisively than they had handled Borley. [[harry-price|Harry Price]] was likely unaware of the Polstead case, as it did not receive major national media attention until the late 1970s, long after his death [2].

## Parallels with Borley

The Borley sources deploy the Polstead case primarily as a counter-argument to Price's claim that Borley Rectory was entirely unique in the duration and violence of its poltergeist activity [3]. Both cases share:
- A rural rectory with a long history (decades to centuries) of alleged disturbances
- Paranormal footsteps as a common reported phenomenon
- The experience eventually forcing resident clergy out
- Ultimate disposal of the property by the Church Commissioners

The differences: the Polstead case received far less press coverage, was never investigated by a major psychical research organisation, and was resolved by a sale in 1979 — forty years after the Borley fire.

## Timeline of Phenomena

- **Early 19th century**: The Rev John Whitmore (rector 1795–1840) experienced phenomena severe enough to carry out a service of exorcism at the beginning of his incumbency — before the famous Red Barn murder of 1827 took place in the same parish [1].
- **1963–1976**: The Rev and Mrs Neads. Mrs Mary Neads described "unusual but not unpleasant spiritual activity" and informed the local bishop that she and her husband simply got used to the phenomena, which included hearing footsteps when nobody was about [1, 4].
- **Pre-1978**: The wife of Neads's predecessor, the Rev Paul Biddlecombe, told the *Church Times* in 1978 that an unseen force frequently pushed her out of bed, while her young daughter woke repeatedly in distress from screaming noises emanating from the bedroom hearth and flue [1].
- **1979**: The Rev Hayden Foster, his wife Margo, and their son Gerard moved in. On their fifth night, Mrs Foster awoke at 3:00 a.m. to the sound of screaming. She reported that the newly decorated room suddenly appeared dirty with peeling wallpaper, and she experienced a terrifying sensation of being strangled and suffocated [4]. The family packed their bags the next morning and refused ever to return.
- **1979**: The Church Commissioners put the rectory on the market, instructing agents to mention the haunting in the brochure. It was sold as a private residence.

## The Church's Response

Following the Fosters' abrupt departure in 1979, the Church Commissioners acted decisively. Archdeacon Jeremy Walsh admitted that the history of the haunting and the Fosters' recent experiences had hastened the decision to sell [4]. The sources suggest the Church authorities acted with such speed because they had learned from the Borley affair and deliberately wanted to avoid having "another Borley" on their hands [5]. The Archdeacon offered a theological perspective:

> "As Christians believe that there is a spiritual dimension to our lives, then they should not be surprised if they find that spiritual forces break through occasionally."

Babbs and Mathias cite this statement with approval in their own conclusions about Borley.

## The Red Barn Murder Connection

Polstead is also the site of the famous Red Barn murder (1827) — the killing of Maria Marten by William Corder, discovered reportedly after her stepmother dreamed of the location of the buried body. This detail carries competing interpretations:

- **Skeptical Argument:** The historian Andrew Clarke uses the Red Barn murder to argue that the Borley haunting was purely a psychological construct. He suggests that sensational local gossip regarding lurid crimes like the Polstead murder was absorbed by the Bull sisters and [[mabel-smith|Mrs Mabel Smith]], contributing to a "fantastic soup" of legends subsequently projected onto Borley Rectory [8].
- **Counter-Argument:** Babbs and Mathias note that the Polstead Rectory haunting and the Red Barn murder do not appear to be linked in any way. They point out that the Rev Whitmore's exorcism of the rectory took place well before the 1827 murder occurred, ruling out any suggestion that post-murder hysteria generated the haunting tradition [7].

## Significance

The Polstead case demonstrates that the broad outlines of the Borley haunting — a rural rectory with long-standing paranormal disturbances severe enough to force out occupants — are not wholly unique in England. However, the Borley case remains extraordinary in its duration, its documentation, and the scale of its public attention.
