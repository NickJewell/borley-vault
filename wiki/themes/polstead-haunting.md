# The Polstead Haunting

A series of alleged paranormal disturbances at [[polstead-rectory|Polstead Rectory]], a former Church of England rectory located about ten miles from Borley, over the county boundary in Suffolk. Babbs and Mathias discuss it at length as the closest parallel case to the Borley haunting, and use it to argue both that Borley is not wholly unique and that the Church Commissioners handled it far more decisively than they had handled Borley.

## Parallels with Borley

Both cases share:
- A rural rectory with a long history (decades to centuries) of alleged disturbances
- Paranormal footsteps as a common reported phenomenon
- The experience eventually forcing resident clergy out
- Ultimate disposal of the property by the Church Commissioners

The differences: the Polstead case received far less press coverage, was never investigated by a major psychical research organisation, and was resolved by a sale in 1979 — 40 years after the Borley fire.

## Timeline

- **Early 19th century**: The Rev John Whitmore (rector 1795–1840) carried out a service of exorcism.
- **1963–1976**: The Rev and Mrs Neads. Mrs Neads described "unusual but not unpleasant spiritual activity."
- **Pre-1978**: The wife of Neads's predecessor, the Rev Paul Biddlecombe, described being pushed out of bed by an unseen force; her daughter woke repeatedly in distress from screaming noises from the hearth.
- **1979**: The Rev Hayden Foster, his wife Margo, and their son Gerard moved in. Five days later they left, never to return. Mrs Foster had experienced what appeared to be a sudden change in the room's appearance (clean wallpaper becoming dirty and peeling) and a sensation of being strangled.
- **1979**: The Church Commissioners put the rectory on the market, instructing agents to mention the haunting in the brochure. It was sold as a private residence.

## The Archdeacon's Statement

The Ven Jeremy Walsh, then archdeacon, commented:

> "As Christians believe that there is a spiritual dimension to our lives, then they should not be surprised if they find that spiritual forces break through occasionally."

Babbs and Mathias cite this statement with approval in their own conclusions about Borley.

## The Red Barn Murder

Polstead is also the site of the famous Red Barn murder (1827) — the killing of Maria Marten by William Corder, discovered reportedly after her stepmother dreamed of the location. This is coincidental and unconnected to the rectory phenomena.

## Significance

The Polstead case demonstrates that the broad outlines of the Borley haunting — a rural rectory with long-standing paranormal disturbances severe enough to force out occupants — are not wholly unique in England. However, the Borley case remains extraordinary in its duration, its documentation, and the scale of its public attention.
