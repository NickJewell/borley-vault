# The Locked Book of Private Information

Formal title: *The Haunting of Borley Rectory — Private & Confidential Report*. A 360-page typescript dossier compiled by [[sidney-glanville]] following his involvement in the [[the-observer-program|Price tenancy investigation]] (1937–38). The single most important primary document on the Price investigation period.

## Contents

The Locked Book contains:
- Correspondences and notes from Sidney Glanville's many visits to [[borley-rectory]]
- Detailed floor plans of the Rectory (Glanville's first and second versions)
- Tracings and notes on the [[wall-writings]]
- Transcripts of the planchette and table-tipping séances (see the séance section below)
- Observer reports from other observers that Glanville collected
- Various photographs taken during the investigation

[[harry-price|Harry Price]] had it bound in three-quarter calf leather and fitted with a Bramah lock — hence the name. According to Price, the book was locked to protect from prying eyes the names and intimate details of living persons (such as members of the Bull family), as well as to conceal séance "information" that was demonstrably untrue or potentially libellous [5–7]. Price used it as the primary source for *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940).

## The Séance Data in the Locked Book

The *Locked Book* contains the detailed transcripts of the planchette séances conducted by Glanville's family and friends, which introduced the most famous elements of the Borley legend. The scripts introduced the story of the murdered French nun [[marie-lairre|"Marie Lairre"]] and the prophecy by an entity named "Sunex Amures" that the Rectory would burn down — a prediction that appeared to come true eleven months later [8–10].

Skeptical investigators dismiss this data outright. [[robert-wood|Robert Wood]] describes the séance transcripts as "rubbish," arguing that sitters asked leading questions based on malicious village gossip regarding the Bull family, effectively guiding the planchette to produce the answers they desired [11–13]. Significantly, [[sidney-glanville|Glanville]] himself later stated that he placed no faith in his own planchette data, believing it originated entirely in the subconscious minds of the operators and that "Marie Lairre" never existed [14].

## Full Publication of Séance Records (ebr-ch08)

*The End of Borley Rectory* (1946) published the full and verbatim records of the table-tipping and planchette séances from the Locked Book for the first time — records previously only partially quoted in *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940). Price also received in January 1945 additional transcripts of planchette sessions (November 5, 1937 – March 27, 1938) that he had never seen, filling gaps in the record.

Key séance content newly published:
- The full October 23–25, 1937 table-tipping records at Borley (in kitchen passage, Base Room, and several bedrooms)
- [[helen-glanville]]'s solo October 28, 1937 planchette session at Streatham (first mention of "Lairre," the well in the cellar, May 17 1667 date)
- October 30–31, 1937 sessions: "Henry" murdered "Mary" / "Waldeggrave" strangled her
- November 20–21, 1937 (at Borley with Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|Henning]]): "Henry Waldegrave confesses": "Killed. How? Hands. Where? Crypt." A chalice also promised.
- December scripts: "Look under the brick floor in the cellar — Well — Borley"; "P-e-n" (pendants); "Bones from garden and pond, reburied under cellar floor; bones = Lairre"
- The complete Sunex Amures fire-threat (March 27, 1938): "under the ruins you will find bone of murdered…"

Price notes: Many names of living persons, sensitive private allegations, and ambiguous information were still withheld in 1946 — "in fifty years' time" a full unredacted edition might be possible.

[[ian-aitchison|Lt. Aitchison]] (Canadian psychometrist) was later allowed to psychometrize the actual Locked Book objects and relics in July 1944 at Glanville's home.

## The Controversy over Harry Price's Use

[[harry-price|Harry Price]] deposited the *Locked Book* in the University of London library in January 1940 and relied heavily on it to write *The Most Haunted House in England* [1, 15]. Its contents became a primary weapon used by critics to attack Price's integrity.

**The case against Price:** The authors of the *Borley Report* ([[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen Goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]]) compared Price's published accounts directly against Glanville's original notes [16]. They found that Price had altered or suppressed details to make the haunting seem more sensational — for example, Glanville's notes clearly recorded that Mrs. [[mabel-smith|Mabel Smith]] experienced a smashed vase *before* Price arrived at the Rectory, but Price wrote that it happened during his own visit to heighten the drama [17, 18]. Critics also noted that Price ignored Glanville's advice to discard certain observer reports that Glanville (who drew the floor plans) knew were physically impossible [16].

**[[robert-hastings|Hastings]]' counter-argument:** Researcher [[robert-hastings|Robert J. Hastings]] used the *Locked Book* to defend Price. He argued that the discrepancies were due to Price's carelessness or his desire to write "scientific journalism" rather than a strict academic text [19, 20]. More importantly, Hastings pointed out a massive flaw in the critics' logic of fraud: **if Price were deliberately faking or misrepresenting evidence, he would never have deposited the *Locked Book* and his files in a public university library for inspection during his own lifetime** [21, 22]. Hastings also used Glanville's 1937 notes of his interview with the Smiths in the *Locked Book* to prove that the Smiths *did* genuinely believe the house was haunted at that time, directly contradicting Mrs. Smith's later public denials [23].

## Ownership History and Theft

The Locked Book's history is one of the more troubling sub-stories of the Borley case:

1. **1937–38**: Glanville compiled the report and presented it to Price
2. **1940**: Price transferred his entire library to the University of London, including the Locked Book
3. **1948**: After Price's death, Glanville borrowed the Locked Book from the University (with the curator's permission) for use in a projected *Borley Symposium*
4. **1953**: [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] visited the dying and "not thinking clearly" Glanville at his cottage in Fittleworth, West Sussex. On 27 July, Glanville inscribed a dedication to Hall and *presented* the Locked Book to him — under Hall's sustained pressure during a series of visits. Hall claimed Glanville stated he had only "lent" the book to Price and was therefore free to give it away [26, 27].
5. **Post-1953**: Hall sold the original to an American dealer for £1,000. He had four typescript copies made. One copy went to H. Adrian Smith's conjuring collection at Brown University Library (Providence, Rhode Island).
6. **1959**: The University of London filed a formal internal memorandum: "It is clear that Mr. Price considered that the *Locked Book* had been given to him by Mr. Glanville... There is no question of this whatsoever. I knew Mr. Glanville and... he never, at any time, said that the *Locked Book* was his property, and he asked me if he could borrow it."
7. The University never pursued legal recovery of the original.

The curators of the Harry Price Library disputed Hall's version strongly. [[alan-wesencraft|Alan Wesencraft]] (curator of the Harry Price Library for 42 years) bluntly accused [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] of employing "devious" and "bulldog tactics" against a sick man to acquire the manuscript, stating outright that Hall had stolen the *Locked Book* from the University of London [29, 31, 32]. Wesencraft described the inscription Hall forced Glanville to write as "a pathetic inscription" written by a sick man under "Hall's insistent browbeating."

> See also the inscription Hall obtained from Glanville: "To Trevor with my kindest regards and appreciation of his help and encouragement at a time when the making of a re-statement of the history of Borley Rectory seemed to be beyond my capacity." — Sidney, 27 July 1953.

## Hall's Use of the Locked Book

[[trevor-hall|Hall]] used Glanville's material extensively in the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] (1956) and his later book *Search for Harry Price* (1978). Ironically, the same material Hall used to discredit Price was also the most detailed and reliable record of genuine phenomena observed during the Price tenancy — including phenomena Glanville himself insisted the SPR was obliged to acknowledge as "the most puzzling features of the whole case."

## Digital Preservation

[[vincent-oneil|Vincent O'Neil]] included a complete scanned copy of the Locked Book in his 2001 e-book *Borley Rectory: The Ghosts That Will Not Die*, ensuring its contents remained accessible even after the original entered private hands.
