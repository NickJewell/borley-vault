# The August 1931 Séance (Braithwaite)

A professional medium séance held at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] on August 11, 1931, during the [[lionel-foyster|Foyster]] incumbency. Organised at the behest of the Braithwaite family — prominent local residents concerned about the sensational rumours emanating from the Rectory — the séance was long regarded by [[harry-price|Price]] as a "missing link" in the Foyster-period record. The primary documentation is a 5,000-word verbatim report compiled by W. John Braithwaite from notes taken on the night, supplied to Price by Mrs. Braithwaite in 1945.

## Participants

- W. John Braithwaite (organiser; friend of the Bull family from childhood)
- Ernest Meads (fellow sitter; pseudonymously called "Teed" in Foyster's novelised account)
- Mr. Johnson (medium, aged 67; referred to as "Mr. Thompson" in Foyster's novel)
- Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] (present throughout)
- [[marianne-foyster|Mrs. Foyster]] (initially absent; joined the group afterwards)

## Duration

6 p.m. to 10 p.m., four hours. The men assembled in the Rectory library.

## Communications

Johnson operated by successive entity-impersonation, using a Red Indian guide (described as a "chucker-out") and "Dr Haslam of Harley Street" as his primary controls. Entities who communicated:

1. **"Dr. Haslam" (deceased Harley Street physician)**: Diagnosed Foyster with rheumatism and depression (the latter confirmed by Foyster); told him to avoid potatoes and starchy foods such as sago and tapioca [9, 11].

2. **"Joe Miles" (drunken ex-publican)**: Called for drinks; refused to believe he was dead; associated with the Rectory's disturbances. When asked if he was the cause of the disturbances, Miles offered an "inebriated grunt" which was taken as an admission of guilt [3, 12]. A "Silly Billy" (village idiot) also reportedly communicated [11].

3. **"Mary"**: The most significant entity. A young woman who said: *"I went to London and I had a baby, and I murdered it."* She described returning to the village and being turned out at night in the rain, sleeping under a hedge. She "felt very rebellious" because of the cruelty with which she was met [12]. Meads and the sitters attempted to persuade her toward peace.

4. **"John Wesley"**: Five minutes described by Braithwaite as "much better than the ordinary sermon, and much simpler, and in good language and taste."

5. **"Cardinal Newman"**: The closing entity, delivering prayers [13–15].

## The "Mary" Entity

"Mary" represents a fourth claimed identity for the Borley nun-ghost beyond [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]], [[arabella-waldegrave|Arabella Waldegrave]], and "Evangeline Westcott" (Warren's Marks Tey entity). She is not a nun but a woman who committed infanticide and suffered social ostracism. Whether she has any connection to any historically documented person at Borley has not been established.

## Marianne's Collapse

Following the four-hour séance, [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] joined the men as they inspected the mysterious wall writings on the staircase [15]. She suddenly stopped, claiming she sensed the presence of the late Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]], then went into a fit or trance and collapsed down the stairs [17]. When Braithwaite carried her to a bed, he found her pulse perfectly normal [17]. Upon waking, Marianne claimed she had been possessed by the spirit of a Canadian friend named Agnes [17]. Mr. Johnson concluded that Marianne was a highly advanced medium and the primary door through which the spirits were entering the house [17, 18].

## The Paper-Message

Among Braithwaite's effects, his widow found the sole surviving **paper-message** from the Foyster period — an envelope in [[marianne-foyster|Marianne's]] handwriting, bearing the word "Marianne" (identical to the wall-message hand), her own written query "What do you want?", and the response "Rest." Price reproduces this as Plate XXVI.

## Divergent Perspectives

The sources record sharply divergent views about the séance's significance:

**The Braithwaites' sceptical conclusions:** Sir John Braithwaite and his sister Mary wholly rejected the supernatural claims. They concluded that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] was hysterical and was personally manufacturing the phenomena [19]. The Braithwaites compiled reports for [[william-salter|William H. Salter]] at the SPR, in which Sir John stated the phenomena were produced by Mrs. Foyster when she was "not in a normal condition" due to dissatisfaction with her isolated life and her elderly husband [20, 21]. Miss Mary Braithwaite explicitly informed the SPR that the "paranormal" wall writings were "undoubtedly Mrs Foyster's, as she makes some letters in a funny way" [20].

**Foyster's reaction:** In his *Summary of Experiences*, Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Foyster]] recorded the séance but later dismissed its findings, noting that while "Joe Miles" was initially declared responsible for the disturbances, "it appeared subsequently that this was a mistake" [22, 23]. He heavily fictionalised the séance in his unpublished novel, changing the names of participants to Mr. Buttle, Thompson, and Teed [6, 24].

**Harry Price's assessment:** Price eventually published excerpts of Braithwaite's lengthy report in *The End of Borley Rectory* (1946) [3, 9]. While he used the dramatic account to bolster the documented history of the Rectory's phenomena, Price's critics note that he privately agreed with the Braithwaites' counter-arguments, having famously accused [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] of faking the phenomena to her face during his own visit to the Rectory in October 1931 [25, 26].

## Reference in Price's Earlier Book

In *The Most Haunted House in England*, Price had noted this séance with the entry: '"Joe Miles" is among those "spirits" tackled'. He had no details at the time. The Braithwaite report, received in 1945 as Price was completing *The End of Borley Rectory*, filled this gap — making the séance available in the Borley record for the first time.
