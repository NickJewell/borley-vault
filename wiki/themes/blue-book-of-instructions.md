# Blue Book of Instructions

Title given to a slim 8-page pamphlet of printed instructions prepared by [[harry-price]] with the assistance of [[ellic-howe]] and issued to each of the official observers during the Price tenancy of [[borley-rectory]]. Full title: *Private and Confidential — The Alleged Haunting at B— Rectory — Instructions for Observers*. To give the document a façade of academic authority, Price emblazoned the cover with the imprint of his own organisation, the "University of London Council for Psychical Investigation" [5, 6].

## Creation

Soon after becoming tenant of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] on 19 May 1937, Price supervised the drafting and printing of guidelines for his investigators. Copies were ready for the first observers — [[sidney-glanville]] and [[roger-glanville]] — to use at Borley when they arrived on 19 June 1937; Glanville received his copy on 10 June.

Price described it in *MHH*:

> "For the first time in the history of psychical research, there was a printed book of instructions, with a blue cover, for the guidance of those investigating a particular haunted house ... It was a sort of handbook to the 'ghosts' of Borley Rectory, in addition to instructions how to deal with them."

## Contents

The *Blue Book* was divided into two sections:

**Section 1** — Instructions on conduct: what observers had to do at Borley, procedures for watching and recording phenomena, who to contact if strong phenomena occurred, where meals and rest breaks could be taken (including Sudbury), where to send completed reports. Two areas were given special attention: the [[blue-room]] (spend at least part of the day and night there, in complete darkness) and the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] (observed from the large [[summer-houses|summer-house]] for one half-hour before and after dusk) [7, 9].

**Section 2** — A list of all varied phenomena alleged to have occurred over the preceding 40 years, with instructions for handling each type. Covered: bell-ringing, movement of objects, footsteps, [[apparitions]], raps and knockings, lights and [[apports]]. For apparitions:

> "If seen, do not move and on no account approach the figure. Note exact method of appearance... If figure speaks, do not approach, but ascertain name, age, sex, origin, cause of visit, if in trouble, and possible alleviation. Inquire if it is a spirit..."

The *Blue Book* ended with an *Important Note* urging observers to make "the greatest effort" to ascertain if manifestations were actually due to normal causes — such as rats, the wind, the Death Watch beetle, or local villagers — before considering paranormal ones [11, 12].

## SPR Criticism

The [[spr-investigation|SPR investigators]] ([[eric-dingwall|Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Hall]]) seized on the *Blue Book* as providing evidence that Price's intentions were to exploit Borley rather than carry out genuine investigation [5, 13]. They argued that by issuing this document to largely inexperienced laymen, Price subjected them to a **"barrage of suggestion"** that would influence visitors to report experiences that would not otherwise have occurred [13, 14]:

- **The Rupert Haig case:** Observer Rupert Haig woke cold in the night, admitted to being in a "jumpy state," and acknowledged he was actively expecting to hear footsteps and thumps because the manual had told him they were likely [14].
- **The Mrs. Lloyd Williams case:** When Mrs. Lloyd Williams saw a "round dark object" resembling a stooping figure near the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]], the SPR authors claimed this was directly caused by the *Blue Book* instructing her to sit in the summer-house at dusk specifically to watch for a "black draped figure" [15].

Canadian researchers Iris Owen and Pauline Mitchell echoed these criticisms in 1979, concluding that the *Blue Book*, while perhaps well-meaning, "probably had the effect of stimulating imagination and producing exactly the apparitions and stories that were expected" [16].

## Defences

Defenders of [[harry-price|Price]] push back against the suggestion that the *Blue Book* invalidated the investigation:

- [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]], Ghost Club president, praised the *Blue Book* and its accompanying Declaration Form as "particularly sound protocols" for ghost-hunting [17].
- Defenders point out that the SPR critics often downplay the manual's *Important Note*, which strictly mandated testing for mundane causes — demonstrating Price did not simply want his team to imagine ghosts [11, 12].
- Researcher [[robert-hastings|Robert J. Hastings]] explicitly dismantled the SPR's argument regarding Mrs. Lloyd Williams, pointing out that the contemporary report written by her companion, C. Gordon Glover, stated that the group only ascertained *after* the sighting that the Nun was traditionally seen in the exact spot where Mrs. Williams saw the dark figure — making nonsense of the claim that her sighting was induced by prior reading of the manual [18, 19].

## Assessment

The [[the-borley-report|BRC]] takes a measured view: whether the *Blue Book* was a useful practical tool or an instrument of suggestion depends entirely on whether one views Price's activities at Borley as genuine psychical research or money-making deception. Its *Important Note* urging natural explanations first is often overlooked by critics. The document's unprecedented nature in the history of psychical research — the first printed investigation guide for a specific haunted building — is uncontested.

The full text of the *Blue Book* is reproduced as Appendix 1 of the [[the-borley-report|Borley Rectory Companion]].
