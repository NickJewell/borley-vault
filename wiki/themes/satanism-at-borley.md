# Satanism and Black Magic at Borley

A theory, peripheral to the mainstream haunting literature, that some of the phenomena at Borley Rectory were connected to Satanic activity or black magic in the area — not conventional haunting by the spirits of identifiable historical persons, but something darker and less amenable to psychical research methods.

The theory was privately researched by [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] — the most prominent debunker of the Borley legend — but never published. It was also independently investigated by [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. A.C. Henning]] in the late 1950s.

## Marianne Foyster's "Tantrabobous"

[[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] was a firm believer in Satan's power, and believed that "bad" spirits existed whose influence was mischievous and "devilish" [2]. She refused to speak his name, using her personal code-word "Tantrabobous" (pronounced with hushed respect), fearing that to name him would invite his presence or subvert positive plans [1, 2]. She believed Satan:
- Caused her second husband Robert O'Neil's alcoholism
- Could be attracted by violent or abusive language (she crossed herself when it occurred)
- Was present at Borley in some form

She wore a religious scapular throughout her life for protection. Though she later admitted that some phenomena were faked, she "never elaborated about the dark side for fear that Tantrabobous would overhear and react," and maintained that an unexplained evil force was genuinely responsible for some events at Borley [1, 3].

## Mrs. Guy Taylor's Séances (1947)

Mrs. Guy Taylor of London held several séances devoted to Borley in 1947, becoming convinced the site was dominated by evil [4]. Her letters to [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] (1957) described her conclusions:

**January 26, 1957:** "I am unalterably convinced that something happened in that countryside in the past that caused it to be disturbed, at times, to this day... it was begun by [a priest and two others] who round about 1480 raised something they couldn't control."

**February 22, 1957:** "My... theory was that a local group practiced some form of black magic at [Long] Melford and Borley... When we asked if murder or some such crime had occurred at Borley before the 17th century, we got 'Death — Violent,' and that it was due to Satanism." [5]

A medium who went into trance at Borley reportedly "woke up in a terrible state, declaring that she had seen the devil and that the whole place was reeking in Black Magic. After that, no medium for miles around would go into trance at Borley." [6]

Taylor alleged that various persons associated with Borley (including [[harry-price|Harry Price]]) became "possessed" and acted criminally or dishonestly as a result — a claim [[trevor-hall|Hall]] specifically rejected as failing to demonstrate any causal link [14].

## Reverend Henning's Investigation (1959)

[[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Henning]] wrote in 1959: "It has been suggested to me that the phenomena at Borley are due either to the performance of the Black Mass or to witchcraft or possibly both. The Black Mass (it was contended) had taken place within living memory. I find it difficult to believe... There seems, however, to be some foundation for the suggestion of witchcraft." [8]

He was told by local informants of:
- A warlock who "could stop a wagon and horses with his eye"
- Witch trials that had been "notorious" in Essex
- Families in the Stour Valley who reported paranormal happenings in their own homes (told to Rev. Henning's wife in confidence, at Women's Institute meetings)

The Essex Record Office responded to Henning's enquiry: "The question of Black Magic has also been mentioned... while I have no proof of this, it would not — to be quite honest — surprise me to learn that it had been practiced... I feel that an atmosphere created as a result of evil practices is as strong as that created by good ones." [9]

Henning's conclusion: "there is still something strange and inexplicable lingering in haunted Borley."

## Covens, Black Masses, and Witchcraft

Several sources claim that occult rituals actively took place in and around Borley. M.D. Baker wrote to *The Unexplained* in 1983, stating that colleagues visiting Borley at midnight on the anniversary of the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] ran directly into a "witches' coven meeting in the graveyard" [5]. Local investigator Paul Moss-Kemp claimed that Black Masses were actually said inside [[borley-church|Borley Church]] between 1985 and 1990 [7] — one of several incidents that intensified local hostility to paranormal tourists.

## "Elementals" and Evil Presences

Some witnesses reported encountering entities that felt demonic or inhuman rather than like traditional human ghosts. In the late autumn of 1939, Mrs. Violet Sorfleet and two friends visited the ruins and reportedly were surrounded by a "moist, misty, *something*" [10]. She described an "evil, filthy presence" that caused a sensation of infinite corruption, which paranormal author Joan Forman suggested was a type of psychic manifestation known as an "elemental" [10].

## Captain Gregson's Hoof Marks

During an April 15, 1939 BBC broadcast, [[captain-gregson|Capt. Gregson]] described finding strange footprints in the snow that were "not human, nor were they animal." [6] These have been referred to as "the Devil's hoof marks" by some commentators.

## The Scrapfaggot Green Incident (1944)

Nearby, at Scrapfaggot Green (Great Leighs, Essex), a US bulldozer widening a road overturned a 2-ton stone marking a witch's grave from the 17th century. Immediately following:
- The church tenor bell tolled in the early morning
- The church clock lost an hour
- Chickens drowned in water butts
- Sheep got through unbroken hedges
- A large boulder appeared in front of the Dog and Gun

On Friday October 13, 1944 at midnight, villagers restored the stone — and the manifestations ceased.

**Significance:** Suggests the wider area had active witch-burial traditions and associated beliefs that were still part of local culture in the 1940s.

## Colin Wilson's Analysis

Paranormal author Colin Wilson called Borley "the kind of place that *would* be chosen for a monastery, and that probably held some pagan site of worship long before that." He described it as a "place of power" where "the 'ground' itself is haunted."

## Counter-Arguments and Scepticism

The diabolic theories were firmly rejected or minimised by several key investigators:

- **[[harry-price|Harry Price's]] dismissal:** Price acknowledged "the diabolic theory of the old Roman Catholic theologians," but argued it was useless to speculate about devils and elementals [11, 12]. He countered the Satanic theory by arguing that the entities at Borley were largely "benevolent, rather than malevolent" [11, 12], and viewed incidents like Marianne receiving a black eye as the work of a "too-playful Poltergeist with an exuberance of spirits" rather than a demonic attack [12].
- **The Cambridge Commission's failed conjuring:** In the early 1940s, investigators from Cambridge University attempted to test if black magic could stimulate poltergeist activity, reading aloud a magical formula for conjuring the Devil from Grillot de Givry's *Witchcraft, Magic, and Alchemy* and exhibiting a pentacle designed to conjure infernal spirits [13]. The experiment yielded absolutely no results, with no temperature fluctuations or responses obtained [13].
- **[[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall's]] rejection:** Although Hall privately wondered if an evil force was at work and investigated the claims of black magic, he ultimately left the question open [1, 14]. He specifically rejected the idea that individuals were driven to behave criminally simply because they lived in the "supposedly evil orbit of Borley" [14].

## Hall's Private Conclusion

Despite devoting years to debunking the Borley legend, Hall never dismissed the area's unusual history entirely. He wrote privately: "The number of reported cases of haunting and poltergeist activity in the Borley district... is a little surprising." He also wondered whether "a year would be a sufficient period to ascertain whether any spiritual danger exists at Borley."

## Assessment

The black magic theory is unsupported by hard evidence. It explains nothing that conventional explanations (collective suggestion, genuine poltergeist, local legend, and deliberate fabrication) do not also explain. Its significance is as a datum about how the Borley legend functioned in local culture: by the 1940s–50s, the site had attracted genuinely disordered activity (black masses, vandalism, occult practices) as a consequence of its notoriety, which then fed back into accounts of ongoing phenomena.
