# Marie Lairre

The name given by Planchette communications to the spirit believed to haunt [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]]. A claimed seventeenth-century French nun, murdered at Borley. The complete identification emerged in stages: the wall-writings requesting Mass and prayers during the Foyster period, then [[helen-glanville]]'s solo Planchette sessions in 1937, then the table-tipping sessions naming the burial location.

## The Claimed History

According to Planchette sessions ([[helen-glanville]], October 1937; [[mhh-ch26]]):

**Helen Glanville's solo session (October 28, 1937)** — conducted alone at home in Streatham, with *no prior information about the Rectory*:
- Entity wrote name as "Lairre" (confirmed as Mary Lairre)
- "Are you buried in the garden?" → Yes
- "When did you pass over?" → *1667*, month *May*, day *17*
- "Did they hurt you?" → Yes; "What did you want most before you passed over?" → *Water*
- "Was the monk's order that of St. Benedict?" → Yes; monk buried at Borley

**Second session (October 31, 1937)** — with family and [[mark-kerr-pearse]]:
- Name confirmed: *Mary Lairre*
- Age at death: *19*; status: *novice*
- "Were you murdered?" → Yes; "How?" → *Stran...* (strangled confirmed)
- "Whose fault?" → *Waldegrave*
- "Are you French?" → Yes; "Where from?" → *Havre*
- Nunnery: *Bure* (Bures); "Did Waldegrave take you from Bures?" → Yes
- Requests: Requiem Mass (by a Roman Catholic priest), burial, holy water, incense

**Glanville table-tipping sessions (October 1937, [[mhh-ch25]])** — entity claiming to be Harry Bull:
- Nun buried in garden, south-west corner, within 2 yards of a large fir-tree, within 2 yards of the path, under a stone marked "B" (an 1863 boundary marker), less than 3 feet deep
- A second entity named "Fadenoch" — a monk — also buried in the grounds

**The wall-writing connection**: The "Get light mass and prayers" messages on the walls during the Foyster period (1930–35) are consistent with a Catholic entity requesting the same things as Mary Lairre in the Planchette sessions. [[dom-richard-whitehouse]]'s Novena produced "Get light mass and prayers. M." on a wall during a kneeling prayer — appearing while he and Mrs. Foyster were both on the landing.

The complete claimed biography: French novice from Havre; Benedictine connection (monastery at Borley site); brought to England by a Waldegrave; murdered by strangulation on May 17, 1667; buried unsanctified in the Rectory garden; seeks Christian burial and Requiem Mass to rest.

## The Physical Evidence

In August 1943, [[harry-price|Price]] excavated the cellars and found:
- Human bone fragments (skull parts) 3 feet below the floor
- A Miraculous Medal (French, first struck 1832)
- An older gold pendant of probable French origin

A dental surgeon identified the bones as probably belonging to a young woman with a chronic abscess — which the authors note might explain the Nun's invariably pale, sad appearance.

The bones were buried in [[liston-rectory|Liston churchyard]] on 29 May 1945 by [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Henning]].

## Criticisms

- The Miraculous Medal dates from 1832 — cannot belong to a seventeenth-century nun; its presence is unexplained
- Bone fragments found only 3 feet down are suspiciously accessible — critics suggested Price planted them
- The authors counter: the clay patina on the pendants "would be extremely difficult if not impossible to fake"; the floor had clearly not been disturbed for many years; and Price could not have anticipated Canon Phythian-Adams's analysis when he supposedly "planted" them in 1929

## Historical Verification

[[harry-price|Price]] believed he had established the historical existence of such a novice. No independent historical evidence has been published verifying this.

> **Open question:** Has any serious historical research (outside the Borley investigation) verified the existence of Marie Lairre, a Waldegrave murder at Borley, or a French nun in this context?

## The 1955–57 Séances

New planchette sessions at the cottage (organised by Mr Sewell) introduced new details: the murderer was named "Leslie Waldegrave"; an accomplice "Dominic Drossy" was involved. Marie Lairre's final planchette words: "Must I walk this earth for ever?"

## The Library Skull (1928–29)

Early in the Smith incumbency — before [[harry-price|Price]] had any involvement — Mrs Smith found in a library cupboard a parcel containing the skull of "apparently, a young woman. It was in perfect condition, and the teeth were also perfect." [[rev-eric-smith|Eric Smith]] and the sexton buried it in the churchyard in the presence of the churchwarden and the widow of the previous rector.

> **This skull is not the same as the 1943 bones.** The 1943 excavation found bone *fragments* (skull parts and other bones); the library skull was a complete, well-preserved skull found fifteen years earlier. The two physical finds may be entirely unrelated. Neither the SPR's 1956 report nor Underwood and Tabori's *Ghosts of Borley* mention the library skull — it appears only in Price's own 1940 account. Its connection, if any, to the Marie Lairre narrative is unknown.

## The Well Cavity

[[sidney-glanville|Glanville's]] 1937 investigation of the well found a cavity at 61.9 feet below ground level — Price speculated this could lead to one of the subterranean passages discussed in [[gotb-ch05]]. If a passage connects the well to the cellar area, it is a potential route for physical evidence (including the bones) to have been deposited or disturbed. This remains entirely speculative.

## Clarke's Analysis (slbr-15, slbr-20)

Andrew Clarke undermines two central pillars of the Marie Lairre narrative:

**The "well tank" message:** The wall-writing read by Kerr-Pearse and Glanville as "Well tank bottom me" — the clue directing excavation of the cellars — was based on Glanville's pencil tracing of faded marks. The original Foyster "Diary of Occurrences" (June 30, 1931) recorded the text as *"Marianne get help [something undecipherable] **bother me**"*. Clarke also points out that a **"Well Tank"** in a house of this period was in the **attic**, not the cellar — it was the galvanised storage tank fed by the courtyard pump. Any burial "beneath the well tank" would be in the roof space, not the cellar.

**The 1943 bones:** See [[harry-price-controversy]] for Clarke's detailed argument that Price switched Jackson's pig jawbone for a planted human bone when passing it to the pre-positioned pathologist Dr Bailey. Key evidence: Jackson (farm worker) maintained his pig-bone identification his entire life; Johnnie Palmer independently identified *"awd Sows Bunz"*; three years of subsequent excavation (Rayner/Sewell) with a mechanical digger found no further human bones.

**The Plague burial ground:** Price himself noted that human skulls were occasionally turned up by the spade at Borley, from plague victims of the 1654–55 epidemic buried in the garden. Any human remains found at the site may be unrelated to a 17th-century nun.

**The Marks Tey identification:** When the Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle held their séance, the entity identified herself as **"Evangeline Westcott"** — not Marie Lairre. The Marie Lairre identification came later, through the Glanville planchette sessions.

## The Full Planchette Record (ebr-ch08, ebr-ch13)

*The End of Borley Rectory* (1946) published for the first time the complete Planchette and table-tipping séance records from the [[locked-book|Locked Book]], covering October 1937 – March 1938. Price also received in January 1945 a folder of typed transcripts from additional séances (November 1937 – March 1938) that he had never previously seen.

Key additional communications:
- **October 30, 1937 (Borley)**: "Mary" confirms she was murdered by "Henry" and her "remains are near the wall."
- **November 20–21, 1937 (Borley)**: "Henry Waldegrave" communicates directly: "What did you do to the nun? Killed. How? Hands. Where? Crypt." He identifies the crypt as the cellar.
- **December 14, 1937 (Lancaster Gate)**: "Joseph Glanvill" entity instructs: "Look under the brick floor in the cellar — Well — Borley." Writes "P-e-n" (later interpreted as an abbreviation for pendants).
- **December 19, 1937 (Streatham)**: "Henry" writes: "The heap of bones will be found from the garden and the pond... From whose body are the bones? Lairre." Instructed to "look under the cellar floor for the cup and the missal."
- **March 27, 1938 (Streatham)**: "Sunex Amures" threatens to burn the Rectory and instructs: "under the ruins you will find bone of murdered… Under the ruins mean you to have proof."

These séances had not been read by Price when he organised the 1943 excavation; he did not receive them until January 1945 — 17 months after the dig. Their detailed confirmation of the excavation findings gives them enhanced evidential weight. See [[ebr-ch18]] for Price's systematic comparison of predictions versus actual finds.

## Phythian-Adams's Causation Theory (ebr-ch10, ebr-ch16)

[[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]]'s essay (letter January 8, 1941; revised 1946) proposes the following reconstruction of the Marie Lairre story:

1. A young Waldegrave, probably a descendant of Nicholas Waldegrave (died 1621), met a French novice on his Continental travels. She was at a convent in Havre or at Bures.
2. He brought her to Borley — to the chaplain's house / rectory site, which was effectively empty after 1662 when Rev. Playne moved to Langenhoo.
3. He murdered her there ("by hands" according to the Planchette; rape then strangulation according to Aitchison psychometry), and buried her under the cellar floor. He then arranged for the house to remain vacant by moving rectors to his other living (Langenhoo).
4. Her remains lay there for over two centuries, until the foundations were disturbed when Henry Bull built the new Rectory in 1863.
5. Her spirit subsequently manifested through all the subsequent occupancies, appealing for Requiem Mass, prayers, and Christian burial.

The Planchette date (1667) coincides exactly with the period when the Borley Rectory was empty: William Playne moved to Langenhoo in 1662.

## The Arabella Waldegrave Alternative (ebr-ch12, ebr-ch16)

Mrs [[georgina-dawson|Georgina Dawson]] proposed that the nun-ghost was **Arabella Waldegrave** (born 1687), granddaughter of James II, who disappeared from all family records after leaving a Paris convent. Her theory: Arabella became a spy and was murdered at Borley.

> **Demolished by Phythian-Adams (ebr-ch16)**: The Pontoise convent where Arabella was educated was an English convent on French soil; her entire education was in English. The wall-messages include *'trompée'* and *'Répond ici'* — French in the fluent hand of a native speaker. An English-educated woman cannot be the author. The candidate remains Marie Lairre.

## Aitchison's Psychometric Impressions (ebr-ch17)

[[ian-aitchison|Lt. Ian Aitchison]] (Canadian psychometrist, July 1944) psychometrized the excavation finds and other Borley relics without prior knowledge:
- On the gold pendant: scene of a small room (~9 × 7 feet); a girl lying on boards; "someone is on top of me... I pull it off and throw it away."
- On the jaw-bone: "It wasn't strangulation, but she was right when she said strangulation: it was rape. Waldegrave did it." She fought; broke her vow; tore off the medal; felt shame.
- She "seems to remember Queen Anne" (aged 2 in 1667 — consistent with the date).
- She wants her remains buried "not necessarily in churchyard — but out in the open away from the Rectory."

## The Requiem Masses

As [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] repeatedly requested via Planchette and wall-writings, Requiem Masses were eventually said for her:
- **October 29–30, 1943**: Rev. Father John Wright, S. Philip Neri, Arundel, Sussex. The first, Price believes.
- **Oxford**: A Requiem Mass also at Oxford (no date given).

After these Masses, Price observes that phenomena "became weaker and less frequent."

## The Burial (May 29, 1945)

Price carried the cedarwood casket — containing the skull fragment, jaw-bone, gold pendant, Miraculous Medal, and other objects — to [[liston-rectory|Liston churchyard]] (1½ miles from Borley). Rev. Henning performed the service using prayers from *The Missal* (Gelasian Sacramentary and Queen Elizabeth's Primer, 1559):

> "Almighty God, with whom do live the spirits of just men made perfect... we humbly commend the soul of Thy servant into Thy hands... that whatsoever defilements she may have contracted in this miserable and naughty world being purged and done away, she may be presented pure and without spot before Thee."

The casket was lowered at 5:42 p.m. BDST, May 29, 1945. Present: Rev. and Mrs Henning, Eric Calcraft (photographer), the sexton, and Price. Price noted the exact minute in case anyone reported a phenomenon at that moment.

> **Note on the missal**: *The Missal* (the title of the book from which Henning read the prayers) had not been mentioned to him as the object the Planchette scripts urged them to find "in the cellar." Price considered this a coincidence — or another "indicator."

## Banks's Research and Conclusion (enbr-ch19)

[[Ivan Banks]] (1994) conducted the most extensive archival research into the identity of the Borley nun of any source, contacting:
- Archives in France: Seine Maritime (Rouen), Mairie du Havre, Département de l'Eure, Archive du France (Paris)
- Belgian National Archives and Rijksarchief te Antwerpen
- Multiple Catholic conventual orders (Stanbrook, Downside, Darlington Carmel, Oulton, Kylemore, Colwich, etc.)
- Countess Mary Waldegrave (for Waldegrave family conventual records)

**Key findings:**
- Caudebec-en-Caux (Normandy) archives: largely destroyed in WWII; no trace of a St François de Sales convent or "Lairre" family confirmed, though the name "Lairre" was commonplace in 17th-century Normandy
- **Bures-en-Bray (Normandy):** Documented by Dom Cottineau as having a **Benedictine Priory of Saint Etienne** — making this a more plausible source for the "Bures" reference in planchette sessions than Bures in Suffolk
- **Joanna Waldegrave** (possible nun candidate): traced as **Sister Theodosia**, died 1719 (not 1667); ruled out
- **Two Arabellas:** Arabella Fitzjames (James II's daughter) = Sister Ignatia at Pontoise (alive as a professed nun 1689–90); Arabella Waldegrave (granddaughter of James II) — entered Pontoise as a child of 7; Countess Mary Waldegrave confirmed she became a Paris Benedictine nun and kept correspondence with her mother and brother — did not disappear
- **1929 French Revolution medal:** Struck in Paris in 1799 — five years after the French Revolution expelled English convents from France; Banks raises possibility the "nun" dates from the late 18th century, not 1667
- **Brussels Benedictine nuns:** Re-settled at East Bergholt, Suffolk (18 miles from Sudbury) after fleeing Belgium in 1794

**Banks's conclusion:** The most likely candidate for the ghost nun is **[[barbara-waldegrave|Barbara Waldegrave (Sister Mary Apollonia)]]** — daughter of Sir Nicholas Waldegrave *of Borley*, born 1603, professed Brussels Benedictine 1624, died April 18, 1638, aged about 36. The Brussels chronicle describes her death as "happily deceased" — which Banks reads as potentially ambiguous. Banks applies the Holmesian argument: of all Waldegrave nuns investigated, Barbara is the one born at Borley, who died young, and whose circumstances are unexplained.

> **Contradiction with séance data:** The planchette consistently described the nun as "Marie Lairre," French, from Le Havre, murdered 1667. Banks argues the séance contacts were fragmentary and multiple entities overlapping, with place-names misinterpreted. If Barbara Waldegrave is the nun, she died c.1638 in Brussels (not Borley, not 1667).
