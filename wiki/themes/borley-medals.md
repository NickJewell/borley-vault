# Borley Medals

Name given to specific metallic objects that allegedly appeared by paranormal means at [[borley-rectory]] during [[harry-price]]'s early association with the case. The "curious matter of the medals" was treated by the authors of the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] as prime evidence of Price's alleged dishonesty.

## The Three Medals

**Medal A — St Ignatius Medal**: An eight-sided brass medallion minted in Rome by the firm of Harari, bearing on one side the head of St Ignatius in pilgrim's habit; on the reverse the word "ROMA" and a Holy Trinity design.

**Medal B — French Roman Catholic Confirmation Medal**: A small oval gilt medallion similar to those presented to Roman Catholic children on Confirmation.

**Medal C — French Revolution Medal**: An eight-sided metal badge dated "ANVIII" (1799) issued in Paris after the Revolution — identified as a pass issued to members of the French National Assembly.

## The 1929 Appearance

During visits in June/July 1929, Price was with a party at Borley (accompanied by [[lord-charles-hope]] and [[lucie-kaye]]) when a shower of door keys was found on the hallway floor. Among them was a medallion. Price wrote about it in the *ASPR Journal* in August 1929 as "a Brass Romish medallion which the rector could not identify" — consistent with Medal A (the St Ignatius medal).

However, in *MHH* (1940), Price states that *two* French medals appeared on that occasion — now described as Medals B and C. This internal inconsistency — one medal in 1929, two medals in 1940 — was seized upon by the [[spr-investigators|SPR investigators]], who concluded that Price had substituted the French medals for the St Ignatius medal to fit the developing [[marie-lairre]] narrative.

## 1943 Cellar Medals

Two additional medals were found during the [[bones-excavation|1943 cellar excavations]]:
- A thin oval pendant made of poor-quality gold: on one side the Virgin Mary with "Pax" underneath; on the reverse twelve stars
- A Roman Catholic medallion of Sister Catherine (the [[miraculous-medal]])

The BRC notes that no accusations of subterfuge have been levelled at Price for these 1943 finds.

## The SPR Accusation

Dingwall, Goldney and Hall argued that Price had planned to reinforce the "English monastery" story of Borley with the St Ignatius medal (the monkish appearance lending itself to the Borley monastery legend), but that when the séances later introduced the French nun [[marie-lairre]], he revised his story to replace Medal A with the more overtly French Medals B and C.

## The Hastings Counter

[[robert-hastings]] gave several reasons against this conclusion: no evidence exists that Price collected foreign medals; the word "monk" is not used in the 1929 ASPR article; Price later denied believing in the Borley legends; Price attached no importance to séance information unless confirmed by other sources. However, Hastings acknowledged that he could not entirely explain why contemporaries recalled only one medal.

## Missing Medals

Medals B and C were later stolen from the [[borley-dossier|Borley Dossier]] at the University of London — discovered by [[ah-wesencraft|Alan Wesencraft]] after the SPR investigators returned the files in 1951. This has led to suspicions about who took them. Whether the 1943 cellar medals were buried with the Borley bones (as Price stated in *EBR*) or also ended up missing is unclear from the evidence.

Medal A (the St Ignatius medal) was given by Price as a gift to [[lucie-kaye]]; she returned it before his death and he wore it on his watch chain for the remaining weeks of his life. Its current whereabouts are unknown.

## Conclusion

The BRC offers two opposed interpretations: either Price made a genuine mistake in his recollection when writing *MHH* (confusing or conflating the 1929 medal incident); or the medals incident is another clear example of manipulation. The case remains genuinely unresolved.
