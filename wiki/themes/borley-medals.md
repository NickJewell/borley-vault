# Borley Medals

Name given to specific metallic objects that allegedly appeared by paranormal means at [[borley-rectory]] during [[harry-price]]'s early association with the case. The "curious matter of the medals" was treated by the authors of the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] as prime evidence of Price's alleged dishonesty.

## The Three Medals

**Medal A — St Ignatius Medal**: An eight-sided brass medallion minted in Rome (circa 1630, 1657, or 1700), bearing on one side the head of St Ignatius in pilgrim's habit holding a book; on the reverse the word "ROMA" and a Holy Trinity design [1].

**Medal B — French Roman Catholic Confirmation Medal**: A small oval gilt medallion similar to those presented to French Roman Catholic children at their confirmation [2, 3].

**Medal C — French Revolution Medal**: An eight-sided metal badge dated "ANVIII" (1799) issued in Paris after the Revolution — identified as a pass issued to members of the French National Assembly [2, 3].

According to investigators, these medals materialised as "apports" (objects paranormally transported) alongside a shower of keys and pebbles during a period of [[poltergeist-activity|violent poltergeist activity]] [2, 3].

## The 1929 Appearance and the Substitution Controversy

During visits in June/July 1929, Price was with a party at Borley (accompanied by [[lord-charles-hope]] and Lucie Kaye) when a shower of door keys was found on the hallway floor. Among them was a medallion. Price wrote about it in the *ASPR Journal* in August 1929 as "a Brass Romish medallion which the rector could not identify" — consistent with Medal A (the St Ignatius medal). This was corroborated by the contemporary notes of [[lord-charles-hope]] and his secretary Lucie Kaye [4, 6, 7].

However, in *MHH* (1940), Price states that *two* French medals appeared on that occasion — now described as Medals B and C — with no mention of the St Ignatius medal in the main text [3, 8]. This internal inconsistency — one medal in 1929, two medals in 1940 — was seized upon by the [[spr-investigators|SPR investigators]], who concluded that Price had substituted the French medals for the St Ignatius medal to fit the developing [[marie-lairre]] narrative.

**The SPR accusation:** [[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], and [[trevor-hall]] argued that Price, an avid numismatist, fraudulently planted the medals to validate his evolving theories. They claimed he initially introduced the St Ignatius medal to support the "guilty-monk-and-nun" legend of an English monastery, but that after the 1937 planchette séances introduced the theory of a *French* nun named "Marie Lairre," he deliberately substituted the two French medals in his 1940 book to retroactively manufacture evidence supporting the new French Catholic narrative [9, 10, 11].

**The Hastings counter:** [[robert-hastings]] gave several reasons against this conclusion. If Price had intentionally swapped the medals to bolster the French nun theory, he oddly failed to mention in *MHH* that the Confirmation medal was even of French origin — defeating the supposed purpose of the fraud [12]. Hastings also noted that Price did not collect foreign medals, explicitly stated he did not believe the Borley legends, and crucially, left all three medals in his files at the University of London for anyone to examine — an unlikely action for a guilty man [13–15]. Hastings concluded the discrepancy was merely the result of Price's careless writing from memory after his early files were scattered at the outbreak of WWII [16–18].

## 1943 Cellar Medals

Two additional medals were found during the [[bones-excavation|1943 cellar excavations]]:
- **The Gold Pendant**: A thin, oval-shaped piece of poor-quality gold, heavily corroded, bearing the figure of the Virgin Mary and the word "Pax" on one side and twelve stars on the reverse [19–21].
- **The Miraculous Medal**: A small, thick copper plaque coated in green patina, identified when cleaned as a "Miraculous Medal" of the Blessed Sister Catherine Labouré — an item first struck in Paris in 1832 [19, 21, 22].

The BRC notes that no accusations of subterfuge have been levelled at Price for these 1943 finds. Price did not claim they belonged to the 17th-century nun, Marie Lairre [23, 24]; instead he proposed a mundane physical origin — theorising they were lost in the cellar marl by an Italian or French Catholic workman (noting the name "Mercur" pencilled on the Rectory rafters) during the building of the house in 1863 [24–26]. In a psychical sense, however, Price believed the medals functioned as paranormal "indicators" placed by spirits to guide investigators toward the French Catholic identity of the haunting entity [23, 24].

## Missing Medals

Medals B and C were later stolen from the [[borley-dossier|Borley Dossier]] at the University of London — discovered by Alan Wesencraft after the [[spr-investigators|SPR investigators]] returned the files in 1951. The two 1943 cellar medallions also went missing at the same time, presumed stolen [27, 28]. This has led to suspicions about who took them.

Medal A (the St Ignatius medal) was given by Price as a gift to Lucie Kaye; she returned it before his death and he wore it on his watch chain for the remaining weeks of his life. Its current whereabouts are unknown.

## Conclusion

The BRC offers two opposed interpretations: either Price made a genuine mistake in his recollection when writing *MHH* (confusing or conflating the 1929 medal incident); or the medals incident is another clear example of manipulation. The case remains genuinely unresolved.
