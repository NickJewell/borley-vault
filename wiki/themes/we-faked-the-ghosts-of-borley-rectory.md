# *We Faked the Ghosts of Borley Rectory* (Mayerling, 2000)

A book published in autumn 2000, written by "Louis Mayerling" (real name: George Carter), claiming that all the alleged phenomena at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] were fraudulent and that Mayerling/Carter had personally participated in faking them. Sources across the Borley literature are unanimous in dismissing it as a complete hoax and an outright fantasy. Babbs and Mathias devote an appendix to refuting it comprehensively.

## The Author's Identity

The author claimed to be the son of an Austrian lady — reportedly Amelia Romanov — born in Vienna on 4 September 1913. [[alan-roper|Alan Roper]] unearthed public records proving that "Louis Mayerling" was actually George Carter, born at Mayes Road, Wood Green, London, to a kitchen porter and his wife on that same date. The Genealogical Society confirmed this, and Babbs and Mathias conclude the "Vienna birth" story is invented. Carter changed his name to Louis Mayerling by deed poll only in 1953, some forty years later [5, 10].

"Mayerling" also claimed to have been a child prodigy violinist, to have played piano in London jazz bands under the name Lee Lennox, and to have played banjo in a wartime Berlin night club while impersonating Marlene Dietrich — escaping from occupied Europe by walking over the Pyrenees. Two professional jazz musicians consulted by the authors had never heard of Lee Lennox.

Alan Wesencraft, curator of the [[harry-price]] Library at the University of London, stated that in his 42 years of studying the vast files of [[harry-price|Harry Price]], [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]], and [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]], the name Louis Mayerling or George Carter never once appeared [11, 12]. None of the surviving Rectory residents, observers, or investigators ever mentioned him [6, 11].

## Claims Made

The book claims Mayerling/Carter:
- Stayed frequently at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] as a boy and young man, asserting he was personally invited to accept the place as a second home [5, 6]
- Was responsible for the apparent piano playing through a missing back panel and a grille in the wall — denounced as absurd by the authors, who note [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]]'s plans show no such grille and the well-maintained Bull family would have repaired any missing panel
- Arranged for the June 1929 [[blue-room-seance|Blue Room séance]] sounds to be produced by cottage tenants tapping on the window with a pole — also denounced as architecturally absurd, since the cottage was on the far side of the rectory and the tenants could not have known when to tap [13]
- Attended a cellar séance in the early 1930s at which Montague Norman (then Governor of the Bank of England), George Bernard Shaw, Sir Bernard Spilsbury, Maurice Barbanell, and Lawrence of Arabia were present — Lawrence leaving because he objected to Shaw's presence [9]

Mayerling additionally claimed to have lived with T.E. Lawrence (Lawrence of Arabia) and to have been present in 1919 reporting ghost stories — when he would have been only six years old [14].

## Refutation

Key points made by Babbs, Mathias, and by [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] (in *Borley Postscript*):

- No local witness who genuinely remembered the Bulls, Smiths, or Foysters had any recollection of a "Louis Mayerling" or "George Carter" ever staying at the rectory
- No mention of him appears in any of the extensive documentation
- He described the dining room as "small and dark" — but it was one of the two largest and best-lit rooms in the house, with large bay windows
- The illustrations in the book are largely of poor quality; one purporting to show the author's mother with [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] shows a man who is definitively not Foyster
- [[peter-underwood|Underwood]] notes that in all his personal interviews with the Bull family, they responded to questions about the ghost nun with serious and definite assurances that their experiences were entirely factual — directly contradicting Mayerling's claim that the sisters "professed a smiling petulance" and almost confessed to a hoax [14]
- Mayerling depicted [[mabel-smith|Mabel Smith]] as highly gullible, which entirely contradicts the known evidence of her character [14]
- His claim of dangling a nail from the front porch to tap the kitchen window is mocked by Underwood, as the kitchen was several rooms away [14]

## Mayerling's Own Admissions

The sources note that Mayerling effectively condemned his own book out of his own mouth:

- In a BBC television interview in December 2000, he admitted: "I have exaggerated here and there" [15]
- During a 2001 interview with former police officer and [[ghost-club|Ghost Club]] member Stewart P. Evans, Mayerling made a Freudian slip, admitting that it was actually reading [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s 1940 book that got him "interested in the first place" [16, 17]
- In his own book, he casually admitted that he used to pass the time by telling "completely fictitious tales of Borley Rectory" which he "wildly exaggerated whenever I felt like it" [10, 16]

## Verdicts from Researchers and Associates

**Vincent O'Neil** ([[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]]'s adopted son) concluded that Mayerling never knew his mother, stating: "The time spent with my mother happened only in his imagination. Unfortunately, he has lived with these false ideas so long, he probably now believes them to be true" [15, 18].

**Andrew Clarke** similarly suggested that in his advancing years, Mayerling had come to believe the fantasies he had written [19].

**Colin Wilson**, Vice-President of The Ghost Club, concluded that it was "fairly clear that the book is intended either as fiction or as a hoax" [3, 12].

**Edward Babbs and Claudine Mathias** dismissed the book entirely, calling it a "badly conceived and executed spoof" that belongs strictly in the realms of fantasy [4].

The one area on which sources agree is that the exposure of Mayerling's fabricated identity — the deed-poll name change, the invented Vienna birth, the unverifiable jazz career — makes his central claim of intimate knowledge of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] impossible to sustain.
