# National Laboratory of Psychical Research (NLPR)

Institution devoted to the scientific study of paranormal phenomena, established by [[harry-price]] in 1923. Later reorganised as the University of London Council for Psychical Investigation (ULCPI). Price founded the NLPR because he felt the older Society for Psychical Research (SPR) was over-cautious, overly conservative, and allowed its sympathy for mediums to cloud its critical judgment [3, 4].

## Foundation

Price's first solo success as a psychical researcher was his investigation of the medium Stella Cranshaw (*Stella C.*), whom he met in spring 1923. He booked the séance room of the London Spiritualist Alliance at Queen Square, WC1, and later acknowledged that the NLPR began its work at this time.

In spring 1925, the LSA chose 16 Queensberry Place in South Kensington as its new headquarters — the building was purchased by Sir Arthur Conan Doyle for the LSA, which agreed to fund Price's venture [2, 6, 7]. Through an agreement, Price rented the entire top floor for his new organisation.

On 25 March 1925, Price held an inaugural meeting of the NLPR at the Royal Societies Club. The basic structure was laid down: Price as Honorary Director; Lt Col W. W. Hardwick as Honorary Secretary; J. Arthur Findlay as Honorary Editor. The Laboratory officially opened on 1 January 1926 [2].

## Structure

Price intended the NLPR to be a "model psychic laboratory" — a suite of six rooms comprising chemical and physical laboratory, darkroom, workshop, séance room, and office, valued at £3,000 worth of state-of-the-art apparatus including cameras, microscopes, recording devices, and X-ray and infra-red equipment [6, 9]. His book collection became the NLPR's Research Library.

Publications included:
- *British Journal of Psychical Research* (bi-monthly): short reports and news
- *Proceedings*: detailed reports on specific investigations (replaced in 1931 with the *Bulletin*)

Annual membership subscription: one guinea — unlike the exclusive SPR, membership was open to the general public. The Laboratory grew from 250 initial members to 995, making it nearly as large as the SPR, which had existed for over forty years [7].

## Honorary Officers

Among Vice-Presidents: Baron von Schrenck-Notzing, Dr Eugene Osty (Institut Métapsychique International), Prof. Hans Thirring (Vienna University), Lord Charles Hope, and [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen "Mollie" Goldney]], who served as Honorary Treasurer [10–12].

## Notable Investigations

The NLPR largely specialised in testing physical mediums under controlled conditions [13]. Notable cases included:

- **Stella Cranshaw ("Stella C"):** Price tested her telekinetic abilities and claimed her powers were genuine [14, 15].
- **[[eileen-garrett|Eileen Garrett]]:** In October 1930, Garrett held a famous séance at the NLPR where she allegedly channelled the spirit of Flight Lieutenant H. Carmichael Irwin just days after he died in the R101 airship crash, providing highly accurate, classified technical details about the disaster [16].
- **[[rudi-schneider|Rudi Schneider]]:** The young Austrian medium was brought to the NLPR by Price for several series of highly publicised séances between 1929 and 1932 [17–19].

## The Rivalry with the SPR

The NLPR was the focal point of a fierce rivalry with the Society for Psychical Research [20, 21]. Price deliberately antagonised the SPR by giving his organisation's publication the title *British Journal of Psychical Research*, confusingly similar to the SPR's own journal [7]. Price sought to wrest psychical research away from the patrician SPR and make it accessible to the tabloid-reading public, creating a "new kind of psychic journalism" [7, 22].

The SPR establishment loathed Price's showmanship, his interactions with the popular press, and his practice of paying mediums, which they felt encouraged fraud [7]. Critics like [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] argued that the NLPR's activities were less about serious science and more about gratifying Price's love of the limelight and personal adulation [23].

## Decline and Reorganisation

The NLPR collapsed largely due to the scandal surrounding [[rudi-schneider|Rudi Schneider]]. During a 1932 séance, Price used an automatic camera to capture a photograph that he claimed proved Schneider was cheating [18, 24]. Price sat on the evidence for a year and published it in a Sunday newspaper just as [[lord-charles-hope|Lord Charles Hope]] was about to publish his own positive findings on Schneider for the SPR [18, 25]. Appalled by Price's spiteful behaviour, many important members of the NLPR council resigned in protest, effectively bringing the Laboratory's operations to an end [25]. See [[rudi-schneider-affair]] for the full account.

In June 1934, Price dissolved the NLPR and reorganised it as the **University of London Council for Psychical Investigation** [1, 26, 27].

> **Contradiction:** Skeptics and critics consistently point out that despite its grand new title, the reorganised council had no official connection with the University of London whatsoever, although Price did permanently loan his equipment and library to the university [26–28].

## Operations

Price operated the NLPR from Queensberry Place until December 1930, when his lease with the LSA expired. In February 1931 it relocated to 13 Roland Gardens, South Kensington. [[eileen-garrett|Dennis Wheatley]] was shown around the NLPR at Roland Gardens while researching *The Devil Rides Out* (1934).

## Significance for Borley

The NLPR/ULCPI was the institutional framework for [[harry-price]]'s investigation of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]]. The price-tenancy was conducted under the ULCPI banner; the [[blue-book-of-instructions|Blue Book of Instructions]] was printed under the Council's name; observers' reports were submitted to the Council's address. The Harry Price Library was Price's enduring legacy to the University that housed his investigations.

Two specific Borley connections stand out:

- **The Initial Contact (1929):** When the [[rev-eric-smith|Rev Guy Eric Smith]] and his wife wanted an investigation into the Rectory, they naively asked the *Daily Mirror* for the address of a psychical research society [29, 30]. The newspaper editor immediately bypassed the SPR and contacted Price at the NLPR because he knew Price would provide "good copy" and a sensational media story [30]. The Smiths had no idea they had become unwitting pawns in the rivalry between the SPR and the NLPR [30].
- **The October 1931 Visit:** At the height of the [[foyster-phenomena|Foyster incumbency]], Price visited Borley accompanied by two NLPR council members, [[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]] and Mrs Henry Richards [31, 32]. Following this visit, Price and the council members formally declared their opinion to the Rector that his wife, [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]], was fraudulently producing the phenomena — an accusation that severely angered the Rector and caused a years-long rift between Price and the Foysters [33].
