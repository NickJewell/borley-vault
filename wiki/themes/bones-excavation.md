# The Borley Bones and Excavations

The discovery of human remains in the cellars of the ruined [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] on 17 August 1943 was the most dramatic event in the case's later history — and the most contested. It was used by [[harry-price]] as evidence supporting Canon Phythian-Adams's theory that a murdered French nun lay buried under the Rectory, and used by the [[spr-investigation|SPR investigators]] as evidence of Price's fraud.

## Pre-History

### Previous Skulls

Three skulls had come to light at Borley before 1943:
1. A human skull unearthed by [[henry-bull|Henry Bull's]] gardener and reburied in [[borley-church|Borley churchyard]] (date unknown)
2. A skull found by [[mabel-smith]] in a cupboard in the ground-floor library, c.1929 — also reburied in the churchyard
3. The 1943 jawbone and skull fragment

[[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]] believed the first skull came from a plague pit thought to be somewhere in the Rectory grounds — a belief Price had recorded in his first book, before Canon Phythian-Adams's theory was ever advanced [36–38].

### The Canon's Theory

During Christmas 1940, Canon Phythian-Adams read *The Most Haunted House in England* and in January 1941 sent Price an essay interpreting the wall writings and planchette transcripts. His conclusion: Writing No. 3's phrase "well tank bottom me" (as Kerr-Pearse had translated it) pointed to human remains buried near a water feature in the Rectory cellars. He hypothesised the remains belonged to [[marie-lairre|a French nun]] — possibly murdered by a member of the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]] in the seventeenth century — and urged Price to dig there to prove the theory [1, 4, 5].

## The 1943 Excavations

### Organisation

Price arranged with [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Henning]] to dig in the Rectory ruins on August 17–18 and 30, 1943. Full list of participants: Price, Henning, labourer Jackson, Price's former secretary Mrs Ethel English (note-taker), Georgina Dawson, Dr Eric H. Bailey M.R.C.S., L.R.C.P. (Senior Assistant Pathologist, County Hospital Ashford), his brother Roland Bailey (barrister), Flying-Officer A. A. Creamer D.F.C., LL.B., and [[captain-gregson|Captain Gregson]] with two nieces. [[sidney-glanville]] and [[roger-glanville]] attended on Day 3. The excavations had been delayed by wartime shortages of labour and accommodation [6–8].

### Cellar Description

Three connected compartments below library, dining-room, hall, and kitchen passage; partly conforming to foundations of an older building (ancient two-inch bricks). Four wells: (1) main courtyard well, 80 feet deep, with a discovered tunnel at 61.9 feet; (2) small round bricked well in cellar extension, 6 feet deep; (3) soft-water well; (4) the "well-tank" — a shallow rectangular trough seen by Price in 1929 at the foot of the cellar stairs but completely vanished by 1943 — bricked over, leaving no trace. (See [[well-tank-controversy]].)

### Day 1 (August 17, 1943)

Excavation began at the site of the former well-tank, broken through the hard semi-glazed cellar bricks into "blue vinney" — blue clay marl. At 1:45 p.m., at 3 feet depth, a jawbone was handed up by Jackson (who thought it was a pig) [13, 19]. Dr Bailey immediately identified it as human. Five minutes later: a portion of skull.

Price's note-taker Mrs English recorded in sequence: "1.45. Jawbone (pig?) found." Then "1.50. Part of a skull dug up."

Dr Bailey that evening confirmed in writing to Henning: "*left mandible, with five teeth in good condition, and probably belonged to a woman. The other bone is from the left side of a human skull — the parietal and temporal bone.*"

**Round well** (same morning, before the cellar-floor dig): Jackson excavated 6 feet down. At 5'6" depth: a fine antique cream-jug, chased and fluted, 4½" × 3½", no hallmarks, consistent with Sheffield plate, c.1863. Also recovered: a brass preserving pan [8, 14, 15]. Both objects were probably lost by a workman when the Rectory was built.

### Day 2 (August 18, 1943)

Extended the excavation area without further human bones. Two pendants found:
- **Gold pendant**: thin, oval, worn "Pax" inscription with a figure (probably Virgin Mary), very old, probably French
- **Copper plaque**: identified as a *Miraculous Medal* — a Catholic devotional object struck 1832 to the Virgin Mary's design (apparitions to Catherine Labouré, Paris, 1830). Obverse: Virgin crushing a serpent, rays from fingers, legend "O Mary, conceived without sin, pray for us who have recourse to Thee." Reverse: "M", two hearts, 12 stars. Pronounced c.60 years old (c.1883) by Catholic medallists [16–18].

Price's theory: both pendants were lost by a Catholic workman — possibly "Mercur" (named in the 1863 bell-hanging inscription) — when the Rectory was built. They are "indicators" pointing to the French Catholic identity of the entity, not direct relics of [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]].

### Day 3 (August 30, 1943)

No further significant finds. Marl and bricks replaced.

### Expert Dental Report (May 23, 1945)

Price submitted the bones to West End dental surgeon [[leslie-godden|Leslie J. Godden]], who X-rayed them [20, 21]. Godden produced two detailed reports:

**External**: Left mandible with five well-formed teeth (premolars and molars); no decay; marked wear increasing toward the sixth tooth (oldest in the mouth); diet "contained a good amount of roughage and probably some finely divided grit." Strong attrition suggests coarse stone-ground food — consistent with 17th-century convent diet. Slender ascending ramus: jaw is a woman's. Age at death: approximately 30, probably under [20, 22].

**X-ray**: Large area of infected bone below and around the sixth tooth, "extensive... indicates an infection of some severity." This infection, in close association with the mandibular nerve, "could be responsible for anything from a persistent mild ache to... a raging toothache; persistent severe neuralgia; pain in and anterior to the ear." The owner of this jaw was in significant chronic pain [20, 23].

Price's observation: every witness who described the apparition of [[the-nun|the nun]] used words like "sad," "pale," "miserable," "as if she had been crying," "drawn face" — never cheerful or laughing. The jaw-bone's abscess would, he argued, explain this haggard appearance across a century of sightings [20, 24].

### Burial (May 29, 1945)

The skull fragment, jaw-bone, gold pendant, Miraculous Medal, and other objects were placed in a dovetailed cedar-wood casket with a brass identification plate and [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Henning's]] visiting card. Buried at [[liston-rectory|Liston churchyard]] (1½ miles from Borley) at 5:42 p.m. BDST — just after VE Day — at a simple ceremony attended by Alfred Henning, his wife, his son Stephen, Price, and local photographer [[eric-calcraft|Eric Calcraft]]. Henning performed the service using prayers from *The Missal* — including the Gelasian Sacramentary prayer for the dead. The grave is unmarked [44–46].

Price noted the exact minute "in case some one connected with the Borley hauntings recorded a phenomenon as having occurred at that precise moment." No incident was reported.

[[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] later attempted to have the bones exhumed for carbon dating (1975–1977) but the Home Secretary refused the licence, leaving the true age and identity of the Borley bones a permanent mystery [47, 48].

## The Controversy

### The SPR's Specific Suspicions

The [[the-borley-report|1956 SPR Report]] raises several specific suspicious circumstances beyond the general "bones planted" hypothesis:

- [[sidney-glanville]] was not present on Day 1. He told the SPR he was "surprised and disappointed" not to have been invited — given he was Price's principal observer, had drawn all the Rectory's plans, and lived only two miles away. Price had given him no notice of the excavation.
- **Electrical wiring found in the cellar:** [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Henning]] wrote to Price in January 1945 that the demolition contractor had been "mystified by some wires and electric switches he found in the cellar." The Rectory had no electricity supply. Glanville confirmed nothing of the sort was in the cellars during his visits and his "continued scrutiny of the rectory." The SPR noted that anyone wishing to use electric light in the cellar after the investigation period had ended could have installed a battery set.
- The **Phythian-Adams pressure dilemma**: The Canon had in 1941 demanded Price excavate, saying it would be "a striking triumph for psychical research." By early 1943 Price faced a dilemma: failure to excavate would alienate the Canon and mean no second book; excavation without finding bones would prove the theory valueless and also mean no second book. The SPR argued this dilemma had "only one practicable resolution."
- The bones were only **3 feet down** — inconsistent with having been "dropped into a deep well" in 1667 as Phythian-Adams's theory required [28, 29]. Proximity to the surface might suggest more recent burial.
- The "well tank" had allegedly "disappeared" — bricked over by "some person unknown" while the rectory was uninhabited ruins — yet Price "was quite positive as to the exact position" despite not having seen it since 1929 [10, 11].

### Were the Bones Planted?

The [[the-borley-report|SPR authors]] (1956) strongly hinted — without making an outright accusation — that Price had planted the remains. [[robert-wood|Robert Wood]] stated outright in 1992 that "Price had almost certainly put them there himself" [4, 25–27].

Two scenarios were advanced:

**Scenario 1: Pre-planted.** Price (or an accomplice) had buried bones in advance. But Price was 62 years old and suffered from **severe angina** that made even walking painful — the idea that he could navigate dangerous ruins and single-handedly dig through hard clay to plant bones was, defenders argue, physically absurd [32–34]. Any accomplice could also have blackmailed him once *The End of Borley Rectory* was published.

**Scenario 2: Sleight of hand on the day.** Using his conjuring skills, Price palmed a human jawbone (possibly from a local churchyard, such as his home village of Pulborough [30, 31]) and substituted it for the animal bone that Jackson actually found. Local historian Andrew Clarke argued this in *The Bones of Borley*, noting that Jackson and the Borley locals maintained they had seen a pig's jaw. Defenders counter that executing a sleight-of-hand trick with fragile, clay-caked bones under the scrutiny of an expert pathologist, a barrister, and a clergyman would have been "incredibly risky and almost impossible to pull off" [33, 35].

### The Companion's Resolution

The BRC's analysis (2009) resolves the "well tank" confusion and effectively eliminates the fraud scenarios:

1. **The "well tank" never existed.** Mark Kerr-Pearse's contemporaneous sketch plan and reports prove he examined only the round cellar sump (the courtyard well) — he never mentioned a rectangular trough in the passage, which a meticulous observer could not have missed. Price was genuinely confused when writing from memory ten years later. This means there was no pre-prepared cavity in the passage floor.

2. **The brick floor had never been disturbed.** The floor over which the bones were found had been in place since the Rectory was built in 1863. If Price had planted bones in advance, he would have had to break up and then reconstitute 80 years of undisturbed brickwork — impossible without detection.

3. **The "pig's jaw" testimony is not decisive.** Jackson's initial identification was immediately corrected by the pathologist present. Price's own note-taker's entry documents the sequence. Price's account includes — creditably — the uncertainty at the moment of discovery.

4. **The absence of Glanville from Day 1 is explicable.** Price had extreme difficulty arranging the dig at all due to wartime labour shortages. Glanville was not in "constant touch" with Price; their surviving correspondence shows only two letters between 1940 and 1943. Glanville was present on Days 2 and 3.

**The most probable explanation**: the bones were part of an unrecorded pre-Rectory burial — most likely from a communal plague pit, as the Borley locals had long believed existed in the Rectory grounds. Price the psychical researcher knew this was possible; Price the journalist presented the discovery in its most dramatic light.

## Alternative Identities for the Bones

Later forensic experts, including Professor Bernard Knight, pointed out that **dating bones based purely on soil conditions is notoriously inaccurate** [40, 41], meaning Price was overly optimistic in officially dating the remains to the 17th century. Because tooth wear indicated an age of around 30, some researchers — notably Ivan Banks — have hypothesised that the bones did not belong to a nun at all, but to "Katie Boreham," a 31-year-old local woman whose name appeared in the séances and who allegedly died following an illicit affair with the Rev. [[henry-bull|Henry Bull]] [42, 43].
