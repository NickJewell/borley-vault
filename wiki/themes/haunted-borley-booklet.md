# *Haunted Borley* (Henning's Booklet, 1949)

A short booklet written and published by the [[rev-ac-henning|Rev Alfred Clifford Henning]], rector of Borley and Liston from 1936 to 1955. Privately printed by E.N. Mason & Sons Ltd of Colchester, the 56-page pamphlet is now a rare collector's item [1, 2]. It includes a foreword by the well-known novelist **Elizabeth Goudge**. Copies are now extremely rare; Babbs and Mathias obtained one and draw on it extensively in *Borley Rectory: The Final Analysis*.

## Structure and Collaborators

The booklet contains ten short chapters, of which only three are directly concerned with the alleged haunting [2]. Despite the title, Henning covers broader aspects of parish life and local history — a brief history of Borley village, the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]], and an architectural and historical appraisal of [[borley-church]] [1].

**[[james-turner|James Turner]]**, a poet and author who was living in the [[rectory-cottage|Rectory Cottage]] at the time, typed and edited the manuscript for Henning and likely contributed some of the descriptions of the local countryside and rural life [1]. Turner agreed to assist only on the strict condition that Henning omit the Turners' names and refrain from publishing any of the paranormal happenings that the Turners had personally experienced [3].

## Key Contents

### Phenomena at Borley Rectory

Henning uses the booklet to record:

- The July 1937 evening with Mark Kerr-Pearse when the three heard footsteps approaching down the stone-flagged kitchen passage, accompanied by a swishing sound of trailing garments; the door flung open revealed no one
- The mysterious coats incident: his wife found a woman's tattered coat on a bedroom door; Mayes was asked to burn it; later [[harry-price]] found a different blue jacket in the [[blue-room]] — both coats apparently appearing in a locked house
- Alice Reid's experience of the "cold spot" on the landing outside the Blue Room in August 1937, observed to be 11°F colder than the surrounding area

### Phenomena at Borley Church

- The occasion when Sunday school children were locked inside [[borley-church]] with the key turned from outside, footsteps heard approaching but not departing
- The footsteps in the church porch on 4 June 1947 while Henning, Mrs Henning, and [[harry-price]] were inside (birds in the trees screeching, then quiet, then footsteps — no one found)
- Three days later: same phenomenon with two university student friends
- **Organ music**: On 15 October 1947, Henning and the novelist Norah Walrond (Norah Burke) heard the organ playing as they walked toward the church — music ceased on entry, organ locked, church empty; Henning quotes E.W. Jephcott's letter as independent corroboration of the same phenomenon [4, 5]
- **The ghost hand**: Unexplained sightings of a ghost hand reported by the Henning family [6, 7]

### Herbert Mayes's Experiences

- March 1939: phantom horses trotting down Hall Lane at night; Mayes pulled off road, nothing visible; Henning cross-questioned him repeatedly and concluded he was telling the truth
- 1937: Mayes saw lights in rectory windows, confirmed by Mr and Mrs Payne

### The Langenhoe Haunting

The booklet also contains notes extracted from Henning's correspondence with the Rev. E.A. Merryweather regarding a separate, highly active haunting at [[langenhoe|Langenhoe Church]] in Essex [1, 8].

## Significance and Perspectives

*Haunted Borley* is the principal source for Henning's own firsthand testimony. Babbs and Mathias regard it as important partly because [[rev-ac-henning|Henning]] was neither an outsider nor a professional paranormal investigator, but the resident rector who had lived with these phenomena as part of his daily life. His observations were supported by those who knew him as being a reliable if obsessive witness.

Authors Babbs and Mathias argue that what Henning had to say is "most important" to the overall case [2]. Because Henning personally recorded these phenomena and published them, his booklet acts as strong, independent corroboration of [[harry-price]]'s claims, directly countering sceptical arguments that the haunting was nothing more than an elaborate hoax engineered by Price [2].

### The Dennis Wheatley Anecdote

The famous occult and thriller writer Dennis Wheatley owned a copy of *Haunted Borley* in his personal library [9]. He used the inside cover of the booklet to record an otherwise unpublished anecdote about the journalist Kenneth Allsop, who allegedly took a photograph of the Borley ruins that clearly showed the figure of a nun — a photograph his editor subsequently refused to print [9, 10].
