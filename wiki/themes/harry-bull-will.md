# The Will of Rev Harry Foyster Bull (1912)

The will of [[harry-bull|the Rev Harry Foyster Bull]], dated 31 January 1912, examined by Edward Babbs and Claudine Mathias. It is a typewritten document with more straightforward provisions than [[henry-bull-will|his father's will]]. In the Borley sources the will is presented not merely as a legal document, but as the primary catalyst for a vicious family feud that sceptics argue directly shaped — and in part inspired — many of the alleged hauntings at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

## Executors and Trustees

Three executors:
1. **Alfred Richard Graham Bull** — Harry's brother, a retired schoolmaster
2. **Bernard Brereton Foyster** — a London solicitor and one of [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Algernon Foyster's]] brothers; the Foyster family again named as trustees of the Bull estate
3. **Henry White** — also a solicitor, with offices at Winchester

## Key Provisions

The will was drawn up shortly after Harry Bull's controversial marriage to Ivy Brackenbury [1, 2]:

- **£100 to his widow** ([[ivy-brackenbury|Ivy Bull]]) to be paid as quickly as possible
- All assets to be sold and converted to money
- A **trust** to be formed from the proceeds; the three executors to act as trustees
- The investment income from the trust to be paid to Mrs Ivy Bull for the rest of her life, **unless she should remarry** — in which case the income would be paid in equal shares to Harry's surviving siblings [2, 3]
- Upon Harry's death, the Rectory itself was sold to the Ecclesiastical Commissioners — effectively leaving his siblings nothing, not even their childhood home [3]

## The Estate

- Gross value: **£6,643**
- Net value: **£5,983**

The authors note this is larger than his father's reported estate — confirming their view that [[henry-bull|Henry Bull]]'s comparatively small declared estate reflected deliberate use of trusts to keep assets out of probate.

**[[ivy-brackenbury|Ivy Bull]] did not remarry**; she survived until 1955, dying in a nursing home in Hastings.

## The Bull Sisters' Claims and Perspectives

The terms of the will infuriated Harry's unmarried sisters, particularly [[ethel-bull|Ethel]], [[freda-bull|Freda]], and Constance.

According to local history researchers, when Harry's mother died she had left her money to her four surviving sons with the understanding that they would provide an allowance for their unmarried sisters [4]. The sisters had heavily pressured Harry into promising to alter his will to ensure they were provided for, but upon his death in 1927 they discovered he had never done so, leaving [[ivy-brackenbury|Ivy]] with a life interest in everything [4, 5].

Driven by resentment, the Misses Bull spread malicious village gossip claiming that Harry had intended to make a fresh will in their favour, but that Ivy had murdered him (by poisoning him) before he could sign it [1, 6]. Alternatively, it was rumoured that Harry actually *did* write a new will, but that Ivy had deliberately "mislaid" or suppressed it [7].

## The Will in the Paranormal Record

The anxieties surrounding [[harry-bull]]'s will frequently surfaced during the alleged paranormal activity at the Rectory.

### The 1929 Blue Room Séance

During [[harry-price]]'s first visit, a séance was held in the [[blue-room]] where the Bull sisters aggressively questioned an entity claiming to be Harry Bull. Through a series of mirror taps, the entity confirmed that there was "money trouble" and that he had been "killed" [8, 9].

### The Ghost with the Scroll

During the subsequent Foyster incumbency, [[marianne-foyster]] reported seeing the apparition of [[harry-bull]] on the stairs, always wearing his plum-coloured dressing-gown and carrying a "roll of paper or scroll" [10]. [[lionel-foyster]] documented this in his diary, suggesting: "...possibly he wanted to communicate with her about his will, about which he might well be uneasy, since it is said that he talked about making another and possibly did so and it has been mislaid" [7].

[[ethel-bull]] was firmly convinced that her brother's unquiet spirit walked the stairs of the Rectory carrying a manuscript case or book containing his will [3, 11].

## Sceptical Counter-Arguments

Sceptical investigators — most notably [[robert-wood]], Andrew Clarke, and the authors of the SPR's *Borley Report* — argue that the entire haunting of "Harry Bull" was a fabrication rooted in the dispute over this will.

Sceptics point out that the 1929 séance was manipulated by the Bull sisters to push their slanderous narrative. As [[robert-wood|Robert Wood]] notes, the Misses Bull were "putting words into their dead brother's mouth" to validate their claims that they had been cheated out of their inheritance and that [[ivy-brackenbury|Ivy]] was a murderer [12, 13]. Andrew Clarke adds that the sisters "were determined to provide a sensational séance to provide publicity for the suggestion that Harry had been murdered, and had wanted to change his will in their favour" [4].

Wood further argues that [[marianne-foyster]] opportunistically adapted the village gossip regarding the will to suit her own ends. Rather than making use of the traditional "phantom nun" legend, Marianne capitalised on the recent, highly charged gossip about Harry Bull's suppressed will, inventing sightings of him holding a rolled-up document to draw attention to herself and validate the Misses Bull's slander [14, 15].

## Significance

The will confirms the Bull-Foyster family connection: Bernard Brereton Foyster's involvement as a trustee — alongside [[lionel-foyster]]'s father having been a trustee of [[henry-bull-will|Henry Bull's will]] — shows the two families had intertwined financial and ecclesiastical arrangements across at least two generations before Lionel took the Borley living in 1930.
