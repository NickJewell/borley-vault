# Ghost Club

Founded in 1851 in Cambridge, the Ghost Club was a prestigious psychical research organisation that progressed through several incarnations: 1862–1936, 1938–48, and 1954–93. In 1993 the Ghost Club Society emerged and continues today, with [[peter-underwood]] as Life President and [[colin-wilson]] as Vice-President.

## Connection to Borley

The Borley haunting has been the subject of several lectures at this famous London Club. [[harry-price]] revived and ran the Ghost Club as its Chairman for many years, limiting its exclusive membership to 500 people [2, 3], and used it as a prominent platform to promote the case [1]:

- **18 April 1939**: [[harry-price]] spoke on "Ten Years Investigation of Borley Rectory," illustrated with 35 lantern slides, at the 495th meeting at Over-Seas House, Park Place, St James's Street. Attendance: 141 members and guests including [[kathleen-goldney]], [[helen-glanville]], [[ellic-howe|Ellic Howe]] and others connected with Borley. The lecture effectively teased the contents of his forthcoming bestseller, *The Most Haunted House in England* [1, 4, 5]
- **21 June 1939**: Twenty-five members attended a [[psychic-fete-1939|"Psychic Fête"]] held amidst the burned-out ruins of the Rectory to help raise funds for the restoration of [[borley-church]] [4, 10]
- **21 May 1970**: [[peter-underwood]] gave a lecture "The Story of Borley Rectory" at Essex Hall, Strand
- Later, at the Caxton Hall, Westminster: Underwood spoke to an audience of 400 people
- **November 1980**: Underwood presented a "Pictorial History of the Borley Rectory Hauntings" at a crowded Ghost Club meeting
- **1987**: Alistair J. Wilson spoke on his "Battle of Borley Rectory" at the Oriental Club, London

## Ghost Club Members Connected to Borley

Ghost Club membership involved many Borley figures. Members included: [[cem-joad|Dr C.E.M. Joad]], [[leslie-godden|Dr Leslie Godden]], [[sidney-glanville]], [[roger-glanville]], [[paul-tabori|Dr Paul Tabori]], [[steuart-kiernander]], [[p-shaw-jeffrey]], Dr Peter Hilton-Rowe, Air Cdre [[carter-jonas|R. Carter Jonas]], Sir Ernest Jelf, [[captain-deane|Captain V.M. Deane]], and [[peter-underwood]] (President for thirty-nine years).

Ironically, even [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen "Mollie" Goldney]], who later co-authored the highly critical SPR *Borley Report* attacking [[harry-price]], served as the Ghost Club's Honorary Secretary [1, 8]. [[peter-underwood]] joined in 1947 at Price's personal invitation [1, 3, 7, 9].

## Revival History

Following [[harry-price]]'s death in 1948, the Ghost Club was revived in 1954 with the involvement of [[philip-paul]], [[peter-underwood]] and others. Friction developed and Paul left; Underwood became the dominant figure for decades. [[paul-tabori]] became Vice-Chairman in 1958 at Underwood's invitation and remained until his death.

## Perspectives and Arguments Presented Through the Club

### The SPR vs. the Ghost Club

The sources highlight a deep-seated rivalry between [[harry-price]] and the Society for Psychical Research [6]. When the SPR published its obituary of Price, it dismissively referred to him as a "free-lance" investigator who utilised incompatible methods [6]. Proponents of Price counter that the SPR deliberately ignored his prominent role in the Ghost Club, an organisation founded long before the SPR itself [6].

### Dr Vernon Harrison's Defence of Price

[[dr-vernon-harrison|Dr Vernon Harrison]] used his experiences at pre-war Ghost Club meetings to counter the sceptical argument that [[harry-price]] had fraudulently produced the Borley phenomena [11, 12]. Harrison noted that while Price was a poor speaker, he was an enthusiastic and fair chairman [11]. More importantly, Harrison spoke directly to several of Price's official observers at the Club; based on their independent testimonies that Borley was genuinely a "strange place," he concluded Price did not invent the phenomena and had presented the case in good faith [12].

### Attacking the SPR Report

At a Ghost Club meeting in February 1959, member [[leslie-godden|Leslie Godden]] openly attacked the SPR's *Borley Report* [13]. He argued the report was published primarily for the notoriety and royalties of its authors (particularly [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]]) and described it as a "vicious, unprovoked and dastardly attack on a man no longer in a position to reply" [13].

### Sceptical Counter-Arguments at Club Meetings

Not all perspectives presented at the Club supported the haunting. Following [[harry-price]]'s 1939 lecture, the barrister and doctor [[dr-letitia-fairfield|Letitia Fairfield]] challenged the historical accuracy of the "monk and nun" legend he had presented [14]. She pointed out fatal anachronisms, arguing that coaches did not exist in England before 1550 and that the bricking-up of nuns was a myth [14]. Price countered by claiming he did not personally believe the legend, but rather the physical phenomena [14].

### The Robert Wood Controversy

In 1992, [[peter-underwood]] invited [[robert-wood|Robert Wood]], the sceptical author of *The Widow of Borley*, to give a talk to the Ghost Club Society [15]. However, after reading an advance copy of the book — which accused [[marianne-foyster]] of murder and fraud — Underwood abruptly withdrew the invitation [15]. Underwood argued that Wood's book was full of mistakes and unjustly abused respected individuals who had been dedicated members of the Ghost Club [15].

### Exposing the Louis Mayerling Hoax

In 2000, an author named "[[louis-mayerling|Louis Mayerling]]" (real name George Carter) published a book claiming he had lived at [[borley-rectory]] and personally faked the ghosts [16, 17]. During an interview conducted on behalf of the Ghost Club Society by Stewart P. Evans, Mayerling absurdly claimed that he had "started The Ghost Club with [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] in 1938" [18, 19]. Researchers used this blatant historical fabrication as definitive proof that Mayerling was a fantasist and his entire book was a literary hoax [19].

## Significance

The Ghost Club provided the social infrastructure through which many Borley investigators knew each other — meetings, dinners, and lectures at which first-hand testimonies were exchanged and comparative notes taken. [[peter-underwood]]'s long presidency (1960–1993) made the Club effectively a custodian of the Borley case during the decades following Price's death, with Underwood gathering accounts from survivors and witnesses at Ghost Club events.
