# *Confessions of a Ghost Hunter* (Harry Price, 1936)

*Confessions of a Ghost Hunter* was published by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] in London in February 1936, four years before *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940). It was his second book aimed at the popular market, recounting his various adventures in psychical research [1]. Chapter II of the book, entitled "The Most Haunted House in England," is a heavily disguised account of Price's involvement at Borley between June 1929 and October 1931 [1].

## The Disguised Borley Account

To protect the identities of those involved, Price changed all names: Borley Rectory became "K— Manor," the Bull family was renamed "Percival" (with Harry Bull becoming "Walter Percival"), the Smiths became the "Robinsons," and the Foysters were introduced as "Mr and Mrs Morrison" [3]. The chapter reaffirmed Price's belief in the genuineness of the early phenomena — those occurring during the Smith incumbency — but contained veiled hints that he doubted the paranormality of the events he witnessed later in 1931 [4]. The text also shows that in 1936 Price still accepted the legend that the Rectory was built on the site of a 13th-century monastery, a theory only debunked two years later [5].

## The Deleted Sentence

The book's first edition contained a final sentence in the Borley chapter that read:

> "But we came to the conclusion that the supernormal played no part in the 'wonders' we had witnessed."

This sentence — Price's private verdict, expressed publicly in 1936 — was quietly deleted from all subsequent editions [2, 6]. When asked about it in 1938 by C. Gordon Glover of the BBC, Price confirmed the deletion and directed him to the first-edition passage, writing explicitly: "If you will consult my *Confessions of a Ghost Hunter*, first edition, page 35 you will see what I think of Mrs Foyster... In the second and other editions, however, the passage was omitted at the request of my publishers. I had no objection to it" [9, 11].

## Price's Private and Public Views

The [[spr-investigation|SPR investigators]] found four letters spanning 1931–1946 in which Price privately expressed his real opinion — that [[marianne-foyster|Mrs Foyster]] was responsible for the phenomena. The 1936 *Confessions* sentence is the one occasion when this private view briefly surfaced in print before being suppressed. These four documents, at roughly equal intervals over fifteen years, were cited as demonstrating "a lack of sincerity in his presentation of the evidence" [8].

When confronted in 1938 by [[lionel-foyster|Foyster]]'s reminder that Price's 1931 party had given the opinion that Mrs Foyster "was responsible for the phenomena," Price replied revealingly: "of course no word of this will appear in our Report."

## The Controversy: Duplicity or Honest Evolution?

The [[spr-investigation|SPR authors]] ([[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], and [[trevor-hall]]) argued that Price deleted the sentence because he wanted to write a bestselling book entirely about Borley Rectory, which required him to present the spectacular Foyster phenomena as genuine [7, 8]. By hiding his initial conclusion that the events were fraudulent, they charged, he demonstrated a complete lack of sincerity.

Defenders of [[harry-price]], particularly researcher [[robert-hastings]], strongly counter these accusations by arguing the deletion reflected an honest evolution in Price's views combined with diplomatic necessity [2, 9]. Hastings and others note that Price omitted the sentence either at his publishers' request or to avoid offending the Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]], with whom Price had recently repaired a fractured relationship — mediated by [[sidney-glanville]] — and whose permission he needed to quote from the Foyster diary [2, 10]. Hastings argues that if Price were trying to fraudulently cover up his early scepticism, he would not have openly directed other researchers to the first edition [9, 11].

Hastings concludes that Price's views expressed in 1936 accurately reflected his beliefs at that time. It was only later, in 1939, after reviewing the meticulous investigations of [[sidney-glanville]] and the compelling testimony of Edwin Whitehouse, that Price genuinely changed his mind and came to believe that at least some of the Foyster phenomena were authentically paranormal [10, 13].
