# The Picture Post Photograph (1954–55)

A photograph taken by [[godfrey-thurston-hopkins|Godfrey Thurston Hopkins]] during a *Picture Post* assignment at Borley in late 1954 that showed an unexplained shadowy figure at the former rectory gate — a figure not visible in the viewfinder when the shot was taken. The photograph was published in *Picture Post* on 1 January 1955, alongside a clean comparison photograph taken from the same position.

## Background

In late 1954, the editors of *Picture Post* decided to investigate the Borley story. They sent journalist [[kenneth-allsop|Kenneth Allsop]] as reporter and Hopkins as photographer. Hopkins's assignment was to photograph one of the two former entrances to the gravel driveway that had passed the front door of the rectory — specifically, the gate at which [[fred-cartwright|Fred Cartwright]] claimed to have seen the phantom nun on four occasions in 1927. Hopkins used a Rolleiflex camera on a clear, sunny mid-afternoon.

## The Photograph

Hopkins aimed to capture the outline of a gatepost and a short section of the picket fencing (stakes of three different lengths forming a pattern, joined by horizontal wire strands) against a clear sky. It was mid-afternoon, visibility was good. The shadow or figure was **not visible in his viewfinder** at the time of taking.

When the film was developed, the photograph showed:
- The gatepost and fencing as intended
- A **wispy, black and cloud-like shape** or "dark shadow" hovering just inside the gateway, appearing to stand outside the fencing at the precise point where the phantom nun had reportedly been seen

Hopkins's statement: "I wanted to emphasize the outline of the gatepost and the fencing against the clear sky and when the film was developed the dark shadow was found in the centre. I state definitely that the dark mass was not visible in the viewfinder and that I could not understand why it had appeared in the picture." Hopkins subsequently suggested that this shadow was the apparition of the Borley Nun [3].

## Editorial Response

The *Picture Post* photographic department confirmed that the negative was genuine and had not been tampered with [2, 4]. The editors commissioned a second photographer to take a picture from exactly the same position under the same conditions. The second photograph showed only the gatepost and fencing with no unexplained figure [2, 4].

The article, "Is this the Borley ghost?", was published 1 January 1955 with both photographs reproduced side by side.

## Assessment

Babbs and Mathias, who examined a copy of the original photograph, note the shadowy figure appears to be superimposed upon or standing in front of the picket stakes — outside the fencing, at the historically significant gate location.

> **Contested:** The authors of *The Borley Rectory Companion* argue that to the eye of a neutral observer, the image does not clearly depict a nun at all, dismissing Hopkins's interpretation and stating that the apparent ghost is "nothing more than an indistinct black shape, which could in fact be anything" [3]. Babbs and Mathias acknowledge the reputable nature of *Picture Post* and its photographic experts, but urge extreme caution, pointing out that the history of "spirit photography" is rife with fraud — drawing a direct comparison to the Cottingley fairies hoax of the 1920s, which successfully fooled prominent researchers before being exposed as a fake [6].

A separate incident from 1947 adds a parallel note: Dr C. Hilton-Rowe attempted to photograph the exact same gatepost and experienced an inexplicable, temporary malfunction of his light meter, suggesting environmental or mechanical interference at that location rather than a visual apparition [7]. Babbs and Mathias similarly question whether the 1954 anomaly was truly supernatural or merely a mechanical "hiccup" with the camera — consistent with [[ley-lines-at-borley|the ley-lines hypothesis]] of equipment anomalies at Borley [6].

## Theoretical Perspective on Camera and Apparition

Proponents of the paranormal have offered a theoretical explanation for how cameras might capture what human eyes cannot. The suggestion is that apparitions may exist in a faster time dimension than humans can perceive; therefore, the fraction of a second that a camera shutter opens might capture an image that the human eye and brain are too slow to process and record [8].
