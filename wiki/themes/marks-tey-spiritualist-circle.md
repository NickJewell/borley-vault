# Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle

An Essex Spiritualist group led by grocer and postmaster George Warren and secretary H.H. Frost, who lived at Layer-la-Haye near Colchester. The group was centred at Marks Tey, five miles west of Colchester and about eight miles from Borley. One of their mediums was C.W. Warren. During an all-night visit to [[borley-rectory]] on 23–24 January 1932, the group reportedly witnessed some of the most spectacular phenomena of the Foyster incumbency.

## The January 1932 Visit

[[lionel-foyster]], hearing of Guy L'Estrange's long interest in haunted houses through press accounts, invited him to visit. L'Estrange accepted and brought the Marks Tey Circle. The group included L'Estrange, Warren, [[captain-deane|Captain V.M. Deane]], and others. Phenomena reported included:

- A barrage of bottles crashing down the back stairs
- Servant bells ringing wildly and simultaneously, which L'Estrange was apparently able to control by commanding the spirits to be quiet: "instantly every bell became silent... as though each bell had been seized and held by a human hand"
- Materialisation in mid-air of glass bottles
- [[wall-writings|Wall writing]]
- Other disturbances

The rector sought relief in prayer and all members adjourned to the Rectory chapel, taking with them a relic of the Curé d'Ars. They proceeded from room to room making the sign of the cross with the relic. The phenomena practically ceased. The group then held a séance in the freezing [[blue-room|Blue Room]], where L'Estrange's spirit control ("Zenith") engaged with an "intensely evil" black shadow.

The communicating entity identified itself as a demented nun named **"Evangeline Westcott"** — notably contradicting later séance data that would name the nun "[[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]" [10–12].

The group reported: "The atmosphere of the house was heavy and supernormally cold," and the disturbing entities were formally asked not to trouble the Rectory any more. After further prayers at 5:00 a.m. the party left.

## Aftermath

[[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] maintained that following the visit the whole atmosphere of the house changed and became more or less normal — the violent demonstrations stopped, and the house remained entirely different for the rest of their tenancy [11, 13–15] — though odd incidents continued, and later "there were indications of the trouble starting again." The rector contacted the Circle about two years after their visit and was told their medium had seen the ghost nun and prayed for her to leave.

## Captain Deane's Defence

Captain Deane vehemently defended the authenticity of the January 1932 events. He argued there was "not the slightest shadow of doubt" that the phenomena were genuine, stating that in full lamplight, showers of bottles and stones fell among batches of three to five witnesses who directly saw, heard, and handled them [6, 7].

## Sceptical Interpretation

[[andrew-clarke|Andrew Clarke]] and others who maintain that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] (with assistance from [[francois-darles|Frank Pearless]]) fabricated the Foyster phenomena cite the Marks Tey visit as the moment Marianne realised her ghost-playing games were getting out of hand and used the visiting Spiritualists as a convenient reason to "lay the ghost" and wind down the staged activities [16–18].

Regarding the spectacular simultaneous bell-ringing witnessed by the Circle, skeptics suggest that Pearless orchestrated the trick by standing in the courtyard and pulling an exposed string hidden in the ivy that was connected to the bell wires [19, 20].

## Marianne Foyster's Own Account

Adding another layer of complexity, [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] herself provided a different explanation in 1978 interviews with researchers [[iris-owen|Iris Owen]] and Pauline Mitchell. She claimed it was actually [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] who deliberately threw objects during the Marks Tey Circle's visit — specifically to observe the spiritualists' reactions and see what they would say — and that the throwing ceased the minute the group left the house [21, 22].

## L'Estrange and the SPR

The [[spr-investigation|SPR investigators]] severely criticised L'Estrange's published account of the visit (appearing in the *Norwich Mercury*, 26 December 1942), and were forced to publish an apology in the *SPR Journal* in December 1959 for some statements against L'Estrange personally which he considered libellous.

## Internal Disputes within the Circle

The January 1932 visit caused a bitter falling out among the spiritualists themselves. In 1942, the medium Guy L'Estrange published a sensationalised newspaper account of the night in which he effectively wrote the rest of the Circle out of the story, claiming sole credit for stopping the haunting [23–25]. This greatly angered Mr. Warren and the other members, who felt their ongoing séances deserved the credit. Following the dispute, Mr. Warren reportedly fell ill and ultimately died, attributing his declining health to the evil spirits from Borley [25].

## Significance

The January 1932 Marks Tey Circle visit represents a key pivot point in the Foyster period — after it, the most intense phase of activity subsided. Whether this reflects a genuine laying-to-rest of unquiet spirits, a social performance whose audience had changed, a staged exit strategy for a hoax, or simply the natural end of a poltergeist cycle, continues to be debated. The entity's self-identification as "Evangeline Westcott" — rather than "Marie Lairre" — is also significant: it demonstrates that the Marie Lairre narrative was not yet established at this point, emerging only through the Glanville planchette sessions of 1937.
