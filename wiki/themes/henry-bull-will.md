# The Will of Rev Henry Dawson Ellis Bull (1877)

The will of [[henry-bull|the Rev Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]], drawn up on 22 March 1877 — fifteen years before his death in 1892 at the age of fifty-nine — and examined by Edward Babbs and Claudine Mathias. It is a three-page document in elaborate copper-plate handwriting, covering an estate considerably smaller than the actual wealth of the Bull family would suggest.

## Executors and Trustees

Three executors and trustees were named:
1. **Mrs Caroline Sarah Bull** — his wife
2. **The Rev George Alfred Foyster** of All Saints Rectory, Hastings — [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster's]] father; this appointment reveals the long Foyster–Bull family connection before Lionel took the Borley living in 1930
3. **Walter Scadding** — a London solicitor

## Key Provisions

- The contents of the house were bequeathed to Mrs Bull outright, with £500 to be paid to her "as quickly as possible"
- Mrs Bull had the right to continue to live at the rectory
- £100 was bequeathed to the eldest daughter (Dodie/Caroline Sarah)
- The **patronage of the living** and the rectory, cottage, and grounds passed to [[harry-bull|the Rev Harry Bull]], who was "clearly the main beneficiary" — with detailed provisions for managing the estate if Henry died before Harry attained the age of 21

## The Codicil (1887)

A codicil dated 5 June 1887 specifically altered Mrs Bull's inheritance to her disadvantage: instead of outright ownership of the house's contents, she was now granted only the *use* of them. Babbs and Mathias speculate this restrictive change may have been the result of an undisclosed family disagreement, or alternatively may have been rooted in a documented belief among some that Mrs Bull was "slightly simple-minded," prompting her husband to limit her direct control over the assets [2, 3].

## The Estate Valuation and Hidden Wealth

The gross value of the personal estate was **£3,132** — equivalent to approximately £150,600 in 2002 terms. Babbs and Mathias analyse this figure critically, arguing that it comes "nowhere near" the true value of Henry Bull's assets [2]. He maintained a large, antique-filled country rectory, a cottage, extensive grounds, and a staff of five or six resident servants; the Bull family was known to own multiple properties and farms around Borley, Lavenham, and Sudbury [2, 4].

**Their conclusion: Henry Bull deliberately kept a massive portion of his wealth out of probate, most likely by funnelling his money into private trusts** [2]. This is further supported by the fact that when [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] died in 1927, his gross estate was valued at £6,643 — more than double what his wealthy father had officially left behind [5].

## The Foyster Family Connection

The appointment of the Rev. George Alfred Foyster as executor provides documentary evidence of the deep, pre-existing ties between the actors in the Borley drama. Babbs and Mathias point out that the Rev. George Alfred Foyster was the father of [[lionel-foyster|the Rev Lionel Algernon Foyster]] [2]. This demonstrates that the Bulls and the Foysters were closely related and financially intertwined decades before Lionel and his wife [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] arrived to experience the most turbulent period of the Rectory's poltergeist phenomena in 1930 [2].

## The Anne Boleyn Connection

The authors mention a local legend that the Bulls' wealth descended from Anne Boleyn (who spelled her name "Bullen" and lived at the Great Lodge, Great Bardfield in north Essex, linked to the village church by an underground tunnel). They make no definitive claim but note the family had lived in north Essex since at least the mid-18th century.
