# The Will of Rev Henry Dawson Ellis Bull (1877)

The will of [[henry-bull|the Rev Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]], dated 22 March 1877, examined by Edward Babbs and Claudine Mathias. It is a three-page document in copper-plate handwriting, covering an estate considerably smaller than the actual wealth of the Bull family would suggest.

## Executors and Trustees

Three executors and trustees were named:
1. **Mrs Caroline Sarah Bull** — his wife
2. **The Rev George Alfred Foyster** of All Saints Rectory, Hastings — [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster's]] father; this appointment reveals the long Foyster–Bull family connection before Lionel took the Borley living in 1930
3. **Walter Scadding** — a London solicitor

## Key Provisions

- The contents of the house were bequeathed to Mrs Bull, with £500 to be paid to her "as quickly as possible"
- Mrs Bull had the right to continue to live at the rectory
- £100 was bequeathed to the eldest daughter (Dodie/Caroline Sarah)
- The **patronage of the living** and the rectory, cottage, and grounds passed to [[harry-bull|the Rev Harry Bull]], who was "clearly the main beneficiary"
- Detailed provisions for managing the estate if Henry died before Harry attained the age of 21

## The Codicil (1887)

A codicil dated 5 June 1887 reduced Mrs Bull's rights: she was given the *use* of the contents of the house rather than outright ownership. The authors wonder whether this reflected a disagreement, or the belief (reported reliably) that Mrs Bull was "slightly simple-minded."

## The Estate Valuation

The gross value of the personal estate was **£3,132** — equivalent to approximately £150,600 in 2002 terms. The authors note this is far below what would be expected for a man who owned the large rectory (well-maintained and fully furnished), the rectory cottage, extensive grounds, and was said to have owned other property in the Borley and Sudbury district. Their conclusion: "a lot more money was kept out of probate, probably by means of a trust, or trusts."

## The Anne Boleyn Connection

The authors mention a local legend that the Bulls' wealth was descended from Anne Boleyn (who spelled her name "Bullen" and lived at the Great Lodge, Great Bardfield in north Essex, linked to the village church by an underground tunnel). They make no definitive claim but note the family had lived in north Essex since at least the mid-18th century.
