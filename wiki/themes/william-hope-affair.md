# The William Hope Affair (1922)

The exposure of Crewe-based spirit photographer [[william-hope|William Hope]] by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] in February 1922; a defining moment in Price's career that established him as a fraud-exposer, intensified his rivalry with [[sir-arthur-conan-doyle|Arthur Conan Doyle]], and shaped the public persona he brought to Borley in 1929.

## Background

[[william-hope|William Hope]] had practised spirit photography in Crewe since at least the 1900s, producing images that appeared to show auras and apparitions of the dead. Despite repeated doubts — a *Daily Mail* committee had concluded his photographs were fake as early as 1909 — he retained a devoted spiritualist following, notably including [[sir-arthur-conan-doyle|Conan Doyle]].

## The Sting

In February 1922, Price and [[eric-dingwall|Dingwall]] devised a controlled test. They obtained specially prepared photographic plates from the **Imperial Dry Plate Company at Cricklewood**, marked invisibly with the company's rampant-lion trademark. The plan: supply these to Hope for a sitting; if he substituted different plates, the trademark would be absent from the developed images.

At the sitting in Holland Park on 28 February, Hope photographed Price and produced an image of "a charming female form over his shoulders" — supposedly Price's mother. But when the plates were developed by another photographer, the rampant-lion trademark was absent. Hope had switched the plates.

The exposure was published in the SPR's *Journal* in May 1922.

## The Doyle Rupture

[[sir-arthur-conan-doyle|Arthur Conan Doyle]] immediately responded, claiming that Price and Dingwall had set Hope up. He published a vigorous defence of Hope's work and later confronted Price directly at a dinner, publicly upbraiding him for his attitude to mediums and spiritualists.

Doyle then wrote to Price suggesting his solicitor find means "to get rid of Mr Price as a tenant" from Queensbury Place (the National Laboratory's address, leased from the spiritualist-aligned London Spiritualist Alliance). Price replied with characteristic insolence.

The rupture between Price and Doyle never fully healed, though their relationship oscillated between "comradeship and hostility from month to month." When Doyle died in 1930, Price acknowledged losing his "primary antagonist." Beneath the bluster, he wrote, there had been "mutual admiration and respect."

## Significance for Borley

The Hope affair established the paradox that defines Price's Borley involvement: he built his reputation as the rigorous fraud-exposer, yet the very sceptical-hero persona this gave him would later be used against him. The method of using apparently scientific protocols while potentially manipulating results — which the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] would later allege at Borley — could be seen as the Hope sting's dark shadow, run in reverse.

More immediately, the affair gave Price his public standing as a credible investigator. When the *Daily Mirror* called him to Borley in June 1929, it was this reputation — the man who caught Hope — that they were buying.
