# Borley Dossier

Collective title given by [[harry-price]] to his case notes on [[borley-rectory]], frequently mentioned throughout both his books on the haunting. The mass of correspondence, photographs, drawings, and notes that Price accumulated from 1929 onwards formed part of his many papers deposited at the University of London, where they form one of the named Special Collections.

## Physical Description

For his Borley material Price organised a specially made leather-bound case containing 37 numbered envelopes, each marked with a general description of its contents. The accumulated documents included the minute-by-minute protocols of official observers, graphs, tables, sketches, floor plans, and original uncropped photographs [1]. Bizarrely, the human remains — a skull fragment and jawbone — discovered during the 1943 excavations of the Rectory cellars were even temporarily kept inside the Dossier before they were given a Christian burial in 1945 [6].

## History

Price began depositing material at the University of London in 1937. After his death, the case was borrowed from the University and passed through several hands. It was consulted by [[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], and [[trevor-hall]] during the early 1950s when the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] was being prepared, after which it was returned to the then Librarian, [[ah-wesencraft|Alan Wesencraft]], who first examined the file in March 1951. The whole of Price's papers, including the Borley Dossier, were extensively catalogued by Gillian Murphy and Stefan Dickers in 2004, and are available for consultation by prior appointment with the Special Collections staff at Senate House Library, University of London [7].

## Contents Include

- [[observers-reports|Observers' reports]] from the [[price-tenancy]] (dozens of reports, several with sketches and temperature readings)
- The [[locked-book|Locked Book]] (originally, before it was removed by Glanville then transferred to Hall)
- Signed statements from witnesses
- Correspondence spanning 20,000+ letters
- Photographs including the key [[sidney-glanville]] series from 1937
- Price's extensive press-cutting books
- Séance transcripts
- Draft material for *Borley III*

## The Dossier as Evidence: Perspectives on Price's Integrity

The Dossier is the primary battleground between Price's defenders and his critics, each drawing on the same archive for diametrically opposed conclusions.

**The SPR case against Price:** [[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], and [[trevor-hall]] argued that by comparing the raw data in the Dossier against Price's published books they could demonstrate systematic discrepancies, omissions, and exaggerations — using the archive's own contents to accuse Price of manipulating evidence and fabricating phenomena for sensationalism and profit [8, 9].

**The Hastings counter:** [[robert-hastings]], in his re-examination of the case, argued that the Dossier — not Price's popular books — should be treated as the "ultimate authority" on the phenomena [8]. He pointed out that the books were written as "scientific journalism" for a general readership, whereas the Dossier contained the unvarnished scientific record [9]. Most crucially, Hastings maintained that if Price had been dishonest and had deliberately faked phenomena, he would never have made his raw files available for public inspection at a university library during his own lifetime [8]. Hastings attributed any discrepancies between Dossier and books to Price's carelessness as a writer, the overwhelming mass of complex documents gathered over many years, and the misplacement of certain early files — rather than wilful deception [8, 10].

## Missing Materials

When Alan Wesencraft examined the returned files in March 1951, he found several key items missing, presumed stolen, including:
- [[borley-medals|Medals B and C]] (French Roman Catholic Confirmation medal and French Revolution medal)
- The 1943 excavation medallions (the [[miraculous-medal]] and gold pendant)
- Two further medals: one of the Young Pretender, one depicting the "Battle of Flodden"
- The gold wedding ring apport and the steel nail file from the Price tenancy

Wesencraft and Dr Pafford later stated that [[trevor-hall]] stole the [[locked-book]] from the Library. Whether he also took the medals is not recorded, but the correlation between their disappearance and the SPR investigation period is notable.
