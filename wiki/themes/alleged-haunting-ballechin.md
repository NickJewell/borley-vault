# *The Alleged Haunting of B---- House* (1899)

Published report of the investigation of Ballechin House, Strathtay, Perthshire, co-authored by [[lord-bute|John, third Marquess of Bute]] and [[goodrich-freer|Miss A. Goodrich-Freer]]. Cited by [[harry-price|Price]] as the closest parallel to Borley Rectory in the entire literature of psychical research.

## Background

Ballechin House was owned by Captain J. M. S. Steuart. Built on the site of an older house, it had a modern structure with a tradition of hauntings. In 1892, [[father-hayden-sj|Father Hayden, S.J.]] spent nine disturbed nights there and reported to Lord Bute. In 1896, a Spanish family rented Ballechin and was so disturbed they abandoned their twelve-month lease after eleven weeks. Their butler, Mr Harold Sanders, later wrote a detailed letter to *The Times* (June 21, 1897) describing phenomena including footsteps, knockings, bedclothes lifted by invisible hands, and his bed "lifted up and carried round towards the window."

In February 1897, Bute rented Ballechin and commissioned Goodrich-Freer and Colonel Lemesurier Taylor to conduct a full investigation with a staff of servants. They stayed approximately three months. F. W. H. Myers and Sir Oliver Lodge both visited during the investigation.

## The Phantom Nun

[[goodrich-freer|Goodrich-Freer]] and others saw a phantom nun at Ballechin multiple times — including gliding up a snow-covered glen, mounting "a slope apparently too steep for a human to climb," and weeping passionately. Via Ouija board the nun identified herself as "Ishbel" (a Scottish Gaelic form of Isabella). The parallel with the Borley Nun is exact: at both sites, the nun haunted the *grounds only*, was seen many times, and appeared in distress.

## The *Times* Controversy (1897)

When the investigation results became known, *The Times* (June 8–24, 1897) published a series of letters from sceptics who had not been present — dismissing the phenomena and imputing fraud or suggestion to the investigators. Lord Bute and the investigators published replies. The controversy is a direct precursor to the SPR's treatment of Price and Borley decades later.

## Price's Parallel Column

Price draws up a detailed column-by-column comparison:

| Feature | Borley | Ballechin |
|---------|--------|-----------|
| Reporter to investigator | Priest (Smith → Price) | Priest (Father Hayden → Bute) |
| Investigator rented house | Price (12 months) | Bute (3 months) |
| Panel of independent observers | Yes | Yes |
| Phantom nun | In grounds only | In grounds only |
| Exorcism by RC and Anglican priests | Failed | Failed |
| Dogs' reaction | Two spaniels bolted | "Scamp" terrified |
| Maids refused to remain | Yes | Yes |
| Blue Room phenomena focus | Yes | Yes |
| House abnormally cold | Yes (11°F cold spot) | "Like an ice-house" |

The parallels extend to: bed phenomena, auditory footsteps in locked rooms, voices, Poltergeist effects, and the involvement of notable figures (Price at Borley; Myers, Lodge at Ballechin).

## Significance

The book is important in the Borley context because it establishes that the Borley pattern of phenomena is not unique — it recurs at another well-documented case, investigated by independent qualified researchers decades before Price. This pre-empts the argument that the Borley phenomena were entirely a product of Price's own personality or methods.

The Ballechin mystery was never resolved. Phenomena abruptly ceased at some point after the investigation.
