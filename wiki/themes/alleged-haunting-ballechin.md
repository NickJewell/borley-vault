# *The Alleged Haunting of B---- House* (1899)

Published report of the investigation of Ballechin House, Strathtay, Perthshire, co-authored by [[lord-bute|John, third Marquess of Bute]] and [[goodrich-freer|Miss A. Goodrich-Freer]] (also known as Ada Goodrich-Freer, a clairvoyant and medium). Cited by [[harry-price|Price]] as the closest parallel to Borley Rectory in the entire literature of psychical research.

## Background

Ballechin House was owned by Captain J. M. S. Steuart. Built on the site of an older house, it was widely known as "the most haunted house in Scotland" [4–7] and had a tradition of disturbances. In 1892, Father Hayden, S.J. spent nine disturbed nights there and reported to [[lord-bute|Lord Bute]]. In 1896, a Spanish family rented Ballechin and was so disturbed they abandoned their twelve-month lease after eleven weeks. Their butler, Mr Harold Sanders, later wrote a detailed letter to *The Times* (June 21, 1897) describing phenomena including footsteps, knockings, bedclothes lifted by invisible hands, and his bed "lifted up and carried round towards the window."

In February 1897, Bute rented Ballechin and commissioned [[goodrich-freer|Goodrich-Freer]] and Colonel Lemesurier Taylor to conduct a full investigation with a staff of servants and independent observers. They stayed approximately three months. F. W. H. Myers and Sir Oliver Lodge both visited during the investigation. Goodrich-Freer's diary formed the basis of the book.

## Reported Phenomena

In her diary, [[goodrich-freer|Goodrich-Freer]] recorded numerous phenomena that she and other observers claimed to experience. The reported manifestations included loud clanging sounds, voices, dragging of heavy objects, explosive bangs, footsteps in locked rooms, and a continuous monotone sound resembling a priest "saying his office" [1, 8, 9]. Witnesses also reported physical interactions — bedclothes pulled from sleepers, beds moved — as well as apparitions of a phantom black dog and a nun known as the "Grey Lady" [1, 9, 10]. Bute and Goodrich-Freer concluded that the phenomena were genuinely paranormal [6].

## The Phantom Nun

[[goodrich-freer|Goodrich-Freer]] and others saw a phantom nun at Ballechin multiple times — including gliding up a snow-covered glen, mounting "a slope apparently too steep for a human to climb," and weeping passionately. Via Ouija board the nun identified herself as "Ishbel" (a Scottish Gaelic form of Isabella). The parallel with [[the-nun|the Borley Nun]] is exact: at both sites, the nun haunted the *grounds only*, was seen many times, and appeared in distress.

## The *Times* Controversy (1897)

When the investigation results became known, *The Times* (June 8–24, 1897) published a series of letters from sceptics who had not been present — dismissing the phenomena as "inept and unscientific" [3, 20] and imputing fraud or suggestion to the investigators. Lord Bute and the investigators published replies. Despite being challenged by scientific and medical professionals of the era, Goodrich-Freer's book became a bestseller — a fact critics noted demonstrated that "the myth of Ballechin had more power — and greater popular appeal — than the truth," a phenomenon they argue [[harry-price|Harry Price]] recognised and successfully replicated with his own Borley books [3, 22].

The psychiatrist Sir James Crichton-Browne later derided the Ballechin affair in his memoirs as a complete fiasco, upbraiding the Society for Psychical Research for ever giving the case credence and arguing it was nothing more than "a myth and the magnification and misrepresentation of a few, simple natural phenomena by sensitive minds in quest of the supernatural" [3].

## Price's Parallel Column

[[harry-price|Price]] strongly defended the conclusions of *The Alleged Haunting of B—— House* and devoted an entire chapter of *The End of Borley Rectory* to the parallels between Ballechin and Borley, which he described as "amazing correspondences" [5]. He consciously mirrored Goodrich-Freer's methodology: just as she had invited dozens of witnesses to Ballechin to record their experiences, Price rented [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], recruited independent "official observers," and issued them the [[blue-book-of-instructions|Blue Book of Instructions]] — directly intended as a homage to Goodrich-Freer's study [11–13].

| Feature | Borley | Ballechin |
|---------|--------|-----------|
| Reporter to investigator | Priest (Smith → Price) | Priest (Father Hayden → Bute) |
| Investigator rented house | Price (12 months) | Bute (3 months) |
| Panel of independent observers | Yes | Yes |
| Phantom nun | In grounds only | In grounds only |
| Exorcism by RC and Anglican priests | Failed | Failed |
| Dogs' reaction | Two spaniels bolted | "Scamp" terrified |
| Maids refused to remain | Yes | Yes |
| Blue Room phenomena focus | Yes | Yes |
| House abnormally cold | Yes (11°F cold spot) | "Like an ice-house" |

The parallels extend to: bed phenomena, auditory footsteps in locked rooms, voices, [[poltergeist-activity|poltergeist]] effects, strong Roman Catholic associations, and the involvement of notable figures (Price at Borley; Myers, Lodge at Ballechin). Price asserted the major phenomena were of "typical Poltergeist origin" [6, 9], validating the Ballechin haunting as authentic. He noted one major difference: Ballechin was investigated for only three months before phenomena abruptly ceased, whereas the Borley hauntings persisted for nearly a century [19].

## Sceptical Assessment

The [[the-borley-report|SPR report]] authors ([[eric-dingwall|Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Hall]]) disputed Price's claim that Ballechin was a true poltergeist case, arguing the phenomena partook "rather of the nature of hallucination than of clean-cut interference with the material world" (unless the pulling of bedclothes was accepted as objective) [4]. They further criticised the investigation for failing to employ any instrumental registration, meaning it was never resolved whether the tremendous thumps and crashes were objective sounds or subjective hallucinations [21].

## Significance

The book is important in the Borley context because it establishes that the Borley pattern of phenomena is not unique — it recurs at another well-documented case, investigated by independent qualified researchers decades before Price. This pre-empts the argument that the Borley phenomena were entirely a product of Price's own personality or methods.

The Ballechin mystery was never resolved. Phenomena abruptly ceased at some point after the investigation.
