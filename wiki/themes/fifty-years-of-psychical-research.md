# *Fifty Years of Psychical Research* (Harry Price, London, 1939)

*Fifty Years of Psychical Research: A Critical Survey* was published by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] in 1939 (Longmans, Green & Co.), one year before *The Most Haunted House in England*. Price considered it his "magnum opus" — a comprehensive summary and "epitome of all the important developments and experiments which have taken place during the past half-century" [1-3]. He wrote the book concurrently with his year-long tenancy investigation of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] (1937–38), and because the Borley case was slated to be the exclusive subject of his next major work, he mentioned the haunted Rectory only briefly here [1, 4].

The book became a central piece of evidence for both sceptics and defenders evaluating Price's integrity, primarily because it allowed his critics to contrast his stated standards of evidence with his actual practice.

## On Apports

Price stated in the book (pp. 29 and 201):

> "Like 'spirit' photographs, the evidence for apports is bad."
> "Very few serious investigators of today accept 'apports'."

The [[spr-investigation|SPR report]] notes that this directly contradicts Price's treatment of the [[borley-medals|Borley medals]] and other alleged apports, which he presented in *MHH* and *EBR* as paranormal phenomena worthy of serious consideration. The contradiction indicates that Price knew the apport evidence was weak yet continued to use it to bolster the Borley case [6].

## On the Nun Sighting

The book records (p. 297) that the Borley nun had been seen by "many people, including... the wife of a BBC director." This claim originated from [[c-gordon-glover|C. Gordon Glover]]'s February 1938 report that Mrs Lloyd Williams thought she "might" have seen "a round dark object" which could have been "a short, stooping figure" at dusk — a sighting Glover himself attributed to imagination and described as "flimsy" in a letter to Price. Price promoted this doubtful observation through correspondence and published it in *Fifty Years* without Glover's qualifications, exaggerating it as the 'Nun' seen at Borley [7].

## On the Petit Trianon Witnesses

The book also contains Price's severe criticism (p. 280) of Miss Moberly and Miss Jourdain for not writing up their experiences at the Petit Trianon until a week after the event: "their reports were written just a week too late to be of any real value" (his italics). The [[spr-investigation|SPR report]] cites this as acutely ironic given Price's failure to ensure his own observers prepared reports promptly or contemporaneously — and his championing of Edwin Whitehouse's account, written a full *eight years* after the events [5].

## The "Rosalie" Séance Controversy

*Fifty Years of Psychical Research* also included Price's account of a materialisation séance featuring a little girl named "Rosalie" [8]. Emboldened by their work on the [[the-borley-report|*Borley Report*]], critics [[trevor-hall]] and [[eric-dingwall]] used this account as their next target, devoting twenty pages of their 1958 book *Four Modern Ghosts* to dismissing the "Rosalie" incident as another of Price's "literary publicity stunts" [8, 9].

Pro-Price researcher [[robert-hastings]] later took Dingwall and Hall to task for their debunking of "Rosalie," demonstrating that the critics had made "unwarranted assumptions" about the house where the séance took place and had selectively quoted [[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]] without her permission [9]. The Borley sources note that despite Hall and Dingwall's efforts, the "Rosalie" case remains "something of an enigma" [8].

## Pro-Paranormal Perspectives

Defenders of the Borley haunting also drew upon the book. Canon W.J. Phythian-Adams — who theorised that a murdered French nun haunted the Rectory — referenced *Fifty Years of Psychical Research* regarding Price's earlier attempts to use infra-red light to photograph "ectoplasmic arms" issuing from mediums. He used Price's data to theorise that the Borley nun was not a human soul but rather "tenuous matter" borrowed from the living observers in the house [10].
