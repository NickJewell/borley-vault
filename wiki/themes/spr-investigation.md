# The SPR Investigation and Controversy

The Society for Psychical Research (SPR) sits at the centre of the Borley controversy — as both the body that investigated the case and the institution that prosecuted [[harry-price|Harry Price]] eight years after his death.

## The 1956 Report: The Prosecution

*The Haunting of Borley Rectory* by Dr. [[eric-dingwall|E. J. Dingwall]], Mrs. [[kathleen-goldney|K. M. Goldney]], and Mr. [[trevor-hall|T. H. Hall]]. Published simultaneously in the SPR *Proceedings* and as a book (Duckworth & Co., 1956). Its aim: "to establish Price's guilt and to destroy, once and for all, the 'Borley Legend.'"

The report concluded that the evidence for a genuine haunting was "so slender as to be scientifically worthless" and charged Price with wilful deception and fraud [1]. [[peter-underwood|Underwood]] and [[paul-tabori|Tabori]] characterise it as "the case for the prosecution with no allowance for the defence or for any independent judgement." They do not question the authors' good faith but accuse them of "excessive severity and total scepticism."

### Specific Claims Made by the SPR Investigators

- **Active fakery by Price:** The report relied on testimony from *Daily Mail* reporter [[charles-sutton|Charles Sutton]], who alleged that during a 1929 visit he caught Price faking poltergeist phenomena with stones and pebbles thrown from his coat pockets [1, 4, 5]. The SPR also used testimony from *Time-Life* journalist Cynthia Ledsham (later Thompson) and photographer David Scherman, who stated that a supposedly levitating "flying brick" photographed in 1944 was thrown by a hidden demolition worker — a fact Price allegedly knew but deliberately misrepresented [1, 6–8].
- **Planted evidence:** The investigators accused Price of secretly planting the human bone fragments discovered in the Rectory cellars in 1943, arguing he needed physical proof of the "murdered nun" theory for his second bestselling book, *The End of Borley Rectory* [9–11]. They similarly alleged he "substituted" a French Catholic medal in place of an Italian one originally found, altering evidence to match later séance data [12–15].
- **The Blue Book as suggestion:** The SPR authors argued that Price's [[blue-book-of-instructions|*Blue Book of Instructions*]], given to inexperienced amateur observers, was a "barrage of suggestion" that explicitly told them to expect footsteps, apparitions, and ringing bells, effectively ensuring their imaginations would misinterpret natural house noises [16–19].
- **Dismissal of rectors' testimony:** The authors argued that the Rev. [[rev-eric-smith|G. Eric Smith]] and his wife were merely victims of local superstition and rat infestations, and that violent phenomena only broke out when Price arrived [20–22]. Regarding the Foyster period, [[trevor-hall|Hall]] argued that the phenomena were consciously faked by [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] out of boredom, frustration, and a desire to force her elderly, ailing husband to leave the isolated parish [23, 24].

## The Motives Behind the SPR Investigation

The sources strongly suggest the SPR's attack was fuelled by a long-standing, bitter rivalry between the patrician, academic SPR and Price's own populist organisation, the [[national-laboratory|National Laboratory of Psychical Research]] [25].

Price deliberately antagonised the SPR with his showmanship, his tabloid press connections, and his ability to generate massive public interest [25, 26]. SPR officials, particularly [[william-salter|William H. Salter]], intensely disliked Price. In 1931, Salter visited the Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Foyster]] to try to persuade him to sever links with the "publicity-mongering" Price in favour of the SPR — a move John L. Randall likened to a doctor unethically stealing a colleague's patients [27, 28].

Renée Haynes, author of the official history of the SPR, admitted that the *Borley Report* was presented with the "cold, controlled, unremitting fury of a road drill" [29, 30]. It operated on the presumption of guilt, interpreting every incident to put Price in the worst possible light, a method that outraged many because the accused was dead and unable to defend himself [29–31].

## The Timing Problem

Accusations against Price first surfaced nine months after his death (March 1948). The first critical survey was published eight years later. [[peter-underwood|Underwood]] and [[paul-tabori|Tabori]] find this timing significant — Price could not answer, and his character was not established in readers' minds alongside the charges.

## The SPR's Epistemological Position

The introduction raises a pointed epistemological challenge. The SPR's declared object — investigation "without prejudice or prepossession" — is respectable but not the only valid approach. The authors argue that scepticism can itself be a fixed cognitive trait, not a neutral stance. Heisenberg's principle: the act of observation changes the observed.

## The 1969 Counter-Report: The Defence

[[robert-hastings|Robert J. Hastings]] undertook a point-by-point response to the 1956 report, commissioned by the SPR itself (1965 authorisation, grant-aided). Published March 1969. The SPR's willingness to fund a counter-argument is cited as proof of "the Society's fair-mindedness."

Hastings found that "every mistake I noticed tended in a direction unfavourable to Price," arguing the Report was a prosecutor's brief with no attempt to find arguments in Price's favour.

- **The open-archive argument:** Hastings pointed out that Price voluntarily deposited his entire, unedited Borley archive (including contradictory observer reports) at the University of London library for public inspection *during his own lifetime* [34, 35]. A deliberate fraudster would never preserve and expose the very documents that could be used to incriminate him [34].
- **Rebutting the Sutton affair:** Hastings and Dr. Alan Gauld re-interviewed [[charles-sutton|Charles Sutton]] and exposed major contradictions. Sutton admitted he never actually *saw* Price throw any stones, nor did he see the stones he claimed to have found in Price's pockets [36–38]. Correspondence proved that Price and Sutton remained on highly friendly terms for years after the supposed "exposure" [39–41].
- **Rebutting the planted bones:** Defenders, including Mrs. Henning (the Rector's wife), pointed out that Price suffered from severe angina and was physically incapable of digging up the heavy clay cellar floor [42–44]. The digging was done by a local labourer in front of multiple witnesses, and Price arranged a Christian burial for the remains — an act of sacrilege a devout churchgoer like Price was highly unlikely to commit for a hoax [9, 45, 46].
- **Selective editing by the SPR:** Hastings demonstrated that Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall were guilty of the very suppression of evidence they accused Price of. They frequently omitted testimony that supported the paranormal [47, 48]. For example, they claimed Dom Richard Whitehouse suffered a nervous breakdown *during* the phenomena to discredit his testimony, when in reality his illness occurred several years prior [49, 50].

Some said the Hastings report vindicated Price completely; the 1956 authors denied this. The argument continues.

## The Verdict of Contemporaries

Dr. [[nandor-fodor|Nandor Fodor]], a psychoanalyst and psychical researcher who personally disliked Price, nonetheless publicly defended him against the SPR, calling the *Borley Report* a "scandalous piece of writing" and accusing [[eric-dingwall|Dingwall]] of trying to wreck psychical research [51–53]. [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] and [[philip-paul|Philip Paul]] similarly condemned the report as a vicious, biased attack [54].
