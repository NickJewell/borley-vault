# The SPR Investigation and Controversy

The Society for Psychical Research (SPR) sits at the centre of the Borley controversy — as both the body that investigated the case and the institution that prosecuted [[harry-price|Harry Price]] eight years after his death.

## The 1956 Report: The Prosecution

*The Haunting of Borley Rectory* by Dr. E. J. Dingwall, Mrs. K. M. Goldney, and Mr. T. H. Hall. Published simultaneously in the SPR *Proceedings* and as a book (Duckworth & Co., 1956). Its aim: "to establish Price's guilt and to destroy, once and for all, the 'Borley Legend.'"

[[peter-underwood|Underwood]] and [[paul-tabori|Tabori]] characterise it as "the case for the prosecution with no allowance for the defence or for any independent judgement." They do not question the authors' good faith but accuse them of "excessive severity and total scepticism."

## The 1969 Counter-Report: The Defence

Robert J. Hastings undertook a point-by-point response to the 1956 report, commissioned by the SPR itself (1965 authorisation, grant-aided). Published March 1969. The SPR's willingness to fund a counter-argument is cited by the authors as proof of "the Society's fair-mindedness."

Results: some said it vindicated Price completely; the 1956 authors denied this. The argument continues.

## The Timing Problem

Accusations against Price first surfaced nine months after his death (March 1948). The first critical survey was published eight years later. [[peter-underwood|Underwood]] and [[paul-tabori|Tabori]] find this timing significant — Price could not answer, and his character was not established in readers' minds alongside the charges.

## The SPR's Epistemological Position

The introduction raises a pointed epistemological challenge. The SPR's declared object — investigation "without prejudice or prepossession" — is respectable but not the only valid approach. The authors argue that scepticism can itself be a fixed cognitive trait, not a neutral stance. Heisenberg's principle: the act of observation changes the observed.

This theme will recur throughout the book and is central to evaluating all evidence.
