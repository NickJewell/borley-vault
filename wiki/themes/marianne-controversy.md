# The Marianne Controversy

The question of [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster's]] role in the Foyster-period phenomena is the central unresolved dispute of the Borley case — separate from, though connected to, the [[harry-price-controversy|Price controversy]].

## The Prosecution Case (SPR, 1956)

The SPR report (Dingwall, Goldney, Hall), now available in full, argued:

**The 99/103 count:** Of 103 incidents in Foyster's own *Diary of Occurrences*, 99 depended entirely on Marianne's sincerity (see [[marianne-foyster]] for the breakdown by class)

**The Amherst parallel (19 specific parallels):** The Foysters had lived in Sackville, Nova Scotia (5 miles from the site of the 1878 Esther Cox / "Great Amherst Mystery" poltergeist case) from 1928–30, immediately before coming to Borley. Foyster used the pseudonym "Mr Teed" in *Fifteen Months* — the surname of Esther Cox's sister in whose house the Amherst phenomena occurred. The Amherst case ran to 55,000 copies in 10+ editions; its "textbook" was in Price's own library

**Price's private verdict (four letters, 1931–1946):** He told Fraser-Harris in 1931 "we are convinced that the many phenomena we saw were fraudulent... we think that the rector's wife is responsible"; told Foyster directly "no word of this will appear in our report"; suppressed his suspicions entirely in both books

**The wall-writings:** Miss Braithwaite (1931) independently identified the writing as "undoubtedly Mrs Foyster's"; Sir John Braithwaite concurred. The "i" in "Marianne" is dotted with a downward dash matching Marianne's normal signature

**Motives (12 specific points):** Physical discomfort (no gas/electricity/central heating); isolation; age and health gap with Lionel; attention from Edwin Whitehouse, the Whitehouses, various spiritualists; phenomena ceased exactly when (1) she had been accused from all sides and (2) the flower-shop alternative life was secured; the Amherst model available

**Edwin Whitehouse as unreliable corroborator:** His account was written 8 years after the events; he suffered two nervous breakdowns in the relevant period; he admitted he "did not *witness* No. 5" (the tumbler); the "circling" tumbler, "stiletto convolutions," and "hovering bottle" may all have been Marianne's verbal descriptions accepted uncritically

## The Defence Case (Underwood & Tabori)

- At least 12 people witnessed visual phenomena during the Foyster period — not just Marianne
- The smouldering-room fire incident (Sir George and Lady Whitehouse) could not plausibly have been staged by her
- [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard Whitehouse]] witnessed the stiletto levitate onto his lap while watching Marianne "who had not moved hand or foot"
- Phenomena pre-dated her (the Bulls had reported disturbances since 1863); they also continued post-her (at Borley, not following her to Ipswich, Snape, or America)

## The Owen/Mitchell Framework (1979) — The Key Revision

[[iris-owen|Iris Owen]] and Pauline Mitchell's 1979 report (*Marianne's Story — The Alleged Haunting of Borley Rectory*, SPR Journal September 1979) — based on extended interviews with Marianne from 1977 until her death — offers the most analytically sophisticated framework:

**Lionel Foyster was writing a fictional best-seller**, modelled on Hubbell's *Amherst Mystery*, to solve the Foysters' financial crisis. Most of the "phenomena" he documented were:
1. Deliberate fabrication for the book
2. Genuine minor poltergeist activity (primarily associated with Edwin Whitehouse)
3. Natural explanations missed or exaggerated

Marianne threw nothing. Lionel threw objects deliberately "to observe guests' reactions and to note what they would say." When confined to a wheelchair, "the phenomena ceased completely." The wall writings appeared when Edwin Whitehouse was present; Marianne believes he wrote them (consciously or unconsciously).

**Their happiness:** Far from wishing to leave, "they were happy there." Marianne loved the garden and grounds. They left solely because Lionel's health made ministry impossible.

Owen's personal verdict: "I completely believe that her version of what happened is the correct one." Her account "has the ring of truth."

## The 1958 Swanson Interviews

Private detective [[eileen-garrett|Robert Swanson]] (funded by Eileen Garrett, commissioned by [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]]) interviewed Marianne in Jamestown, North Dakota (February 1958) and New York (May 1958). The transcripts, reproduced in full in *Ghosts That Will Not Die* Ch. 5, reveal:

**Admissions:**
- Made a fake luminous ghost to frighten d'Arles
- Bells could be made to ring by tampering with wires in attic (confirmed by Ian)
- Lionel could have thrown stones; throwing stopped when he could no longer walk
- The manuscript was "supposed to be more or less fiction"
- She "answered" the wall writings by writing underneath them

**Denials:**
- Never saw Harry Bull as an apparition (but does describe seeing a man she later connected to Harry Bull)
- Never saw "the monstrosity" in the passage
- Has no memory of the stiletto incident; never even saw a stiletto
- Never consciously initiated the wall writings
- Never wrote wall messages; only responded to them

**Admitted residual puzzlement:**
- "I think there was something at Borley that wasn't a human being"
- "There were unexplained drops in temperature"
- "There did hear footsteps from time to time, when there really did not seem to be any cause"
- "There were phenomena that were not easy to explain away, and which I haven't tried to explain away. There used to be a light appear in one of the rooms — there is something there."

**Eileen Garrett's assessment** (letter to Hall): Marianne "quite unblushingly told Mr. Swanson that she had every intention of lying to him." She attributed Marianne's inconsistency partly to genuine memory failure across 25 years, partly to protective lying to discourage further persecution.

## Marianne's Unpublished Autobiography

O'Neil considers her unfinished autobiography (written without interrogation pressure) the most reliable source. Key sentences:
- "Many strange incidents occurred at Borley, including the ghost lights, the bells, and the **apparitions**" — the word "apparitions" used voluntarily
- "The house had been quiet enough for **over three and one-half years** when we left" — confirming 18 months of genuine activity before the Marks Tey séance quietened things

These statements directly contradict her Swanson denials while being consistent with the Owen/Mitchell finding that *some* genuine phenomena occurred.

## The "Passive Focus" Theory

A third option: that Marianne was a genuine *focus* for poltergeist activity — not consciously faking, but not the origin either. Price in his final letter (March 1948) described her as potentially "the instrument through which the scripts appeared — as a secondary personality." Owen/Mitchell's Philip Experiment research shows that poltergeist activity can cluster around emotionally stressed individuals without conscious intent.

Owen raises the possibility that Adelaide Foyster — an "emotionally confused little girl" separated from her original family and exposed to constant ghost-talk — might also have been a poltergeist focus.

## The Contradictions Explained

Vincent O'Neil (in gtwnd-ch11) argues Marianne's contradictions make sense in context:
1. **25-year gap**: First Swanson interview was 25 years after events
2. **Hostile interviewers**: Marianne knew Mrs. Goldney had her address; wrote to Ian: "I live in the shadow of a volcano"
3. **Protective lying**: Telling investigators nothing happened was a way to make them go away
4. **Selective memory**: Some events she genuinely could not remember accurately

Nandor Fodor (*Tomorrow*, Winter 1956) on Goldney: "a steam-roller personality, capable of crushing any witness... I am still haunted by the pitiful state in which she left her victim."

## The Verdict

The most defensible current assessment synthesises all sources:
- Most Foyster-period phenomena were Lionel's deliberate fabrication for a fictional book
- Some genuine poltergeist-type activity occurred, probably associated with Edwin Whitehouse
- Marianne was neither the villain (SPR's portrayal) nor wholly passive
- The wall writings remain the most puzzling element — appearing both during the Foyster period and during the later observer programme when Marianne was absent
- "Something" at Borley was real; separating it from the fabricated layers is now impossible

## Clarke's Synthesis (slbr-12, slbr-13, slbr-14, slbr-20)

Andrew Clarke adds the critical ingredient missing from all previous accounts: **[[francois-darles|Frank Pearless]]'s role as confederate and physical agent.**

Clarke's framework:
- **Early phenomena (to spring 1931):** Largely accidental misattribution by a superstitious Lionel, plus some pranking between Marianne and Lionel as private jokes.
- **Pearless phase (spring 1931 – January 1932):** Pearless provided physical mechanisms — cellar trapdoor access, string in ivy for bells, bottles thrown from cellars. Marianne organised the performances; Pearless enacted them.
- **The Marks Tey finale (January 1932):** Both agreed it had gone too far. The Circle visit was deliberately staged as a spectacular ending. *"Things had got out of hand."* After this night, phenomena effectively ceased.
- **Wall-writings:** In Marianne's handwriting (multiple graphologist analyses). Timeline: early scribbles (by children, spring 1931) → Marianne's own writings (encouraged by Lady Whitehouse and Edwin Whitehouse, May–June 1931) → ceased when Edwin's visits became unwelcome.

Clarke's conclusion on the wall-writings: *"There is little doubt at all that Marianne wrote the messages"* — but this may have begun unconsciously (as a response to spiritualist encouragement) and escalated.

**Price's private conclusion** also supports this: in his letter of 15 October 1931, Price wrote *"we were convinced that the many phenomena that we saw were fraudulent."* He never published this.

## Wood's Analysis: Foyster as Voyeuristic Accomplice

Robert Wood (*The Widow of Borley*, c. 1990s) advances the most radical interpretation of Lionel's role:

**Not a dupe, but an active accomplice.** Foyster knew d'Arles's real name (Pearless) from the beginning, as his own writings reveal. Ian Shaw (Marianne's abandoned son) testified that Foyster scattered ashes from the hall stove over the floor, then blamed "The Things." Wood concludes Foyster derived "vicarious sexual satisfaction from his wife's sexual relationships with other men" and was essentially a voyeur who colluded in attracting d'Arles to Borley for both sexual and financial reasons.

**D'Arles characterization:** Multiple sources describe Frank Pearless (d'Arles) as "huge sexually," "Rasputin," "a thoroughly evil man." In *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House* Foyster gave him the pseudonym **"Mr Lawless"**. D'Arles later tried to blackmail the Foysters by threatening to expose domestic arrangements to the Bishop of Chelmsford.

**Village confederates:** Ian Shaw testified that **Willy Palmer**, a village boy, was Marianne's confederate — rigging a collapsing shelf in the pantry that deposited pots and pans on the floor at the tug of a string.

**Wood's limiting factor:** He concentrates entirely on the Foyster period and offers no serious treatment of the Bull-period testimonies or the post-fire sightings. His dismissal of Dom Richard Whitehouse is inadequate given Whitehouse's own detailed rebuttal of every SPR charge.

## The New Horizons Foundation Assessment (1986)

Iris Owen and Pauline Mitchell's 1986 published report (based on their 1978 conversation with Marianne) offers the most balanced institutional verdict:

**"Borley is unique in its complications and admixture of truth and fraud."** This statement — from researchers who began their Borley inquiry alongside Trevor Hall with the hypothesis that it was *entirely* fraudulent — is significant. Owen's research convinced her that the case was more complex than either the SPR prosecution or the Price defence admitted.

Their framework distinguishes:
1. A genuinely unusual *place* with pre-Price Bull-period tradition
2. A *deliberate staging layer* (Foyster's book project; Marianne's pranks; d'Arles's and village children's participation)
3. A *genuine residual* — acknowledged by Marianne herself: the bottle, tumbler, and stiletto incidents; footsteps with no cause; the wall writings that "initially appeared, apparently from nowhere"

This three-strand framework is the current scholarly consensus on the Foyster period.
