# *Murder at the Parsonage* (Mabel Smith's Novel)

An unpublished novel written by [[mabel-smith|Mrs Mabel Smith]], wife of the [[rev-eric-smith|Rev Guy Eric Smith]] who was rector of Borley from October 1928 to April 1930. It has been suggested that Mabel's desire for publicity to help place the novel with a publisher was a factor in the Smiths' decision to write to the editor of *The Daily Mirror* about the alleged haunting.

## Contents and Inspiration

Rather than a straightforward ghost story, Mabel Smith described the work as a "lurid" murder mystery set in a creepy, isolated rural village, opening with the discovery of a skull in a cupboard. The narrative was heavily inspired by the Smiths' own experiences at Borley and by the malicious village gossip fed to them by the Bull sisters — tales of a cook dying in the kitchen, a servant giving birth to [[henry-bull|Henry Dawson Bull]]'s illegitimate child, and the alleged murder of [[harry-bull|the Rev. Harry Bull]] by his wife [2, 4, 5].

The only known copy of the manuscript was once held in the Harry Price Collection at the University of London before it went missing. It was explicitly dated 1941 [1].

## The Publication Theory

Two related claims have been made about the novel:
1. Mrs Smith hoped that the sensational coverage generated by [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s arrival and the newspaper reports would attract the attention of publishing companies when she completed the book.
2. Mrs Smith became hostile toward Harry Price in later years partly because he failed to use his influence to help get the novel published.

Skeptical authors, notably Robert Wood (*The Widow of Borley*), argue that the Smiths deliberately contacted the *Daily Mirror* not to rid the house of ghosts, but to attract investigators and generate a media frenzy that would provide realistic background material for the novel and make it easier to secure a publisher [5–7]. This theory is bolstered by the testimony of [[nancy-goodale|Nancy Goodale]], an acquaintance who recalled Mrs Smith confidently declaring she was "going to make Borley hum!" [8].

Babbs and Mathias note that the first suggestion "may be some truth in it from the point of view of motivation, but the experiment was not at all successful and it backfired horribly on the Smiths" — the rectory was invaded by sightseers and required a police presence.

## Interactions with Harry Price

Following her husband's death in 1940, Mabel Smith found herself in financial distress and hoped a successful novel would pay for his headstone [3]. She explicitly solicited Price's help to place the manuscript with a publisher, sending him fifteen pages and suggesting it could be advertised as an "entertaining novel conjured up by a resident of Borley" or even as a sequel to Price's own bestseller, *The Most Haunted House in England* [3, 9].

Price allegedly attempted to persuade the Smiths to allow the story to be published as a true account within his own work, but Mabel refused, insisting in a 1940 letter that the manuscript was "sheer fiction" and stating: "Had I really believed in ghosts I could never have remained in that house" [4, 10]. Harry Price did give the novel a brief mention at the end of chapter three of *The Most Haunted House in England* — which Babbs and Mathias consider quite sufficient.

## Explaining Mrs Smith's Later Denials

In 1945, Mrs Smith publicly declared that neither she nor her husband had ever believed Borley was haunted by anything other than "rats and local superstition" [11]. Several sources point to *Murder at the Parsonage* to explain this dramatic about-face and her ensuing bitterness toward Price:

- **The "Hijacking" Theory:** Robert Wood argues that Mrs Smith developed an antipathy toward Price because he essentially hijacked her literary project; it was Price, not Mrs Smith, who ultimately wrote and profited from the bestseller about Borley [12].
- **The "Insult" Theory:** The investigator [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] theorised that Price had read the manuscript, deemed it "absolute rubbish," and likely sent her an insulting letter. Glanville believed that her resentment over this rejection, combined with disappointment that Price failed to use his influence to help her financially, motivated her subsequent attacks [13].

> **Contradiction:** Mabel Smith's 1945 letter to *The Church Times* (in which she denied the haunting and placed Borley "in Sussex") and her statements to [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen Goldney]] in 1949 are in sharp contrast to her apparent enthusiasm for publicity in 1929. The authors consider her later statements unreliable.

## Counter-Arguments to the Novel Theory

Proponents of the haunting push back against the idea that the Smiths orchestrated the entire Borley affair just to sell a novel:

- **A Backfiring Plan:** Babbs and Mathias point out that if publicity was the goal, it backfired horribly. The resulting media circus brought hordes of rowdy sightseers and police interventions, making the Smiths' lives utterly miserable [14].
- **Timeline Discrepancies:** Paul Adams, Eddie Brazil, and [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] (*The Borley Rectory Companion*) highlight a major flaw in the skeptical timeline: the dated manuscript (1941) postdates Price's first Borley book by a full year, leaving the theory that the novel was the primary motivation behind the 1929 press appeal "unproven" [6, 15].

## Parallel

The situation parallels that of [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]], who also tried to persuade Harry Price to use his influence to get his own novel, *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*, published. Price was unwilling in both cases.
