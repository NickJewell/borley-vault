# *Murder at the Parsonage* (Mabel Smith's Novel)

An unpublished novel written by [[mabel-smith|Mrs Mabel Smith]], wife of the [[rev-eric-smith|Rev Guy Eric Smith]] who was rector of Borley from October 1928 to April 1930. It has been suggested that Mabel's desire for publicity to help place the novel with a publisher was a factor in the Smiths' decision to write to the editor of *The Daily Mirror* about the alleged haunting.

## The Publication Theory

Two related claims have been made about the novel:
1. Mrs Smith hoped that the sensational coverage generated by Harry Price's arrival and the newspaper reports would attract the attention of publishing companies when she completed the book.
2. Mrs Smith became hostile toward [[harry-price|Harry Price]] in later years partly because he failed to use his influence to help get the novel published.

Babbs and Mathias note that the first suggestion "may be some truth in it from the point of view of motivation, but the experiment was not at all successful and it backfired horribly on the Smiths" — the rectory was invaded by sightseers and required a police presence. With regard to the second suggestion, the authors feel Price was under no obligation to act as an intermediary, and that "if its title was any indication, then the balance of probability is that it was an amateurish effort."

Harry Price did give the novel a mention at the end of chapter three of *The Most Haunted House in England* — which the authors consider quite sufficient.

## Parallel

The situation parallels that of [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]], who also tried to persuade Harry Price to use his influence to get his own novel, *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*, published. Price was unwilling in both cases.

## Significance

The novel and the motives surrounding it contribute to the complex question of whether the Smiths had an ulterior reason for inviting maximum publicity to Borley in 1929. [[nancy-goodale|Nancy Goodale]] recalls Mabel Smith saying she was "going to make Borley hum!" — which, if accurate, suggests she saw the situation as an opportunity.

> **Contradiction:** Mabel Smith's 1945 letter to *The Church Times* (in which she denied the haunting and placed Borley "in Sussex") and her statements to Kathleen Goldney in 1949 are in sharp contrast to her apparent enthusiasm for publicity in 1929. The authors consider her later statements unreliable.
