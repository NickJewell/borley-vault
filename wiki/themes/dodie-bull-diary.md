# Dodie Bull's Diary (1885)

A diary kept by [[dodie-bull|Caroline Sarah Elizabeth Bull ("Dodie")]], the eldest daughter of the [[henry-bull|Rev. Henry D.E. Bull]], covering a ten-month period from late December 1884 to 28 October 1885, when she was 20–21 years old. Written in an exercise book, it was reportedly discovered by [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth|Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth]] (the leader of the Harlow psychical research group) in a second-hand shop in Sudbury in 1970 [1, 2].

## Discovery

Croom-Hollingsworth found the diary in a Sudbury junk shop and offered the original manuscript to the Harry Price Library at the University of London for £1,000 (worth approximately £9,500 in 2002 terms), but the authorities declined to pay this amount [2]. In November 1970, he lent the diary to Mrs Cecil Ryan Baines, who made a handwritten or typescript copy of extracts [3, 4]. The current whereabouts of the original are unknown, though it is believed to have ended up in a private collection in America [3].

## Contents

The diary gives a vivid picture of the social life of the Bull family at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in the 1880s — a continuous round of balls, tennis parties, taking tea, and "leaving cards" [4, 6]:

- Fancy balls (Dodie attended one at [[liston-hall]] on 7 January, returning at 3:45 a.m.)
- Tennis parties and reciprocal dinner parties with neighbouring families
- A dance at Borley Rectory where Dodie noted the "nursery was intended for spooning" and that she enjoyed "a hot flirtation with Charlie" [6-8]
- Lessons resumed on 22 January; Dodie and her sister [[freda-bull|Freda]] (Winifred) taught the younger girls at home
- Harry Bull and his brothers attended Felstead School near Dunmow in Essex

On 11 January, Freda's 20th birthday, Dodie wrote: "How old one begins to feel. What a crop of old maids are growing up." (Dodie was not yet 21.) The diary also reveals her frustrations over her lack of opportunities compared to her brothers (who were sent to university and allowed to travel), and her anxieties about her appearance: "Why wasn't I pretty and fascinating? Damn my ugly face..." [11].

The diary confirms that [[p-shaw-jeffrey|Percy Shaw Jeffrey]] — the man who would later provide [[harry-price]] with spectacular accounts of 1880s poltergeist activity — visited the Rectory in June 1885. However, Dodie merely records him as the object of a girlish crush, noting his "lovely dark gray eyes" and writing, "I mean to go in for him. Freda had better not thwart me for I mean to have him" [12]. This entry is significant (see below).

## Paranormal Reference

The diary contains a single paranormal reference: on 8 October 1885 — shortly after the sudden death of a neighbour, Mary Ann Coker — Dodie, her sister Freda, and a visiting Swiss governess named Marie Rolt experimented with "chair moving" [14]:

> "I tried chair moving in the evening. I made a chair walk all over the room, and answer questions, some of which were quite right... It is great fun and I seem to have a great deal of electricity in me as I can make it walk quite fast." [3]

The authors dryly observe she "appears to have been more interested in landing one of them [eligible young men] than in the supernatural." Proponents of the haunting have nevertheless pointed to this incident as evidence that Dodie possessed genuine psychokinetic ability and that there was a latent "psychic energy" within the young Bull household [3, 16].

## Significance for the Haunting Debate

The diary ends on 28 October 1885, well before the famous 28 July 1900 sighting. It provides no firsthand paranormal testimony and is invaluable primarily as a contemporaneous social document of the Bull household — confirming the lively, affluent lifestyle that Margaret Finch's testimony also supports. It counters the image of Borley Rectory as perpetually gloomy.

Despite chronicling daily events, the weather, and the health of farm animals, **the diary contains absolutely no mention of any haunting, ghostly nuns, or unusual experiences at the Rectory** [5]. Sceptics including Andrew Clarke argue this critically undermines [[p-shaw-jeffrey|P. Shaw Jeffrey]]'s later claims that stones were thrown and dictionaries teleported during his 1885 visits. If such spectacular poltergeist events had actually occurred, they would surely have warranted an entry [5]. The diary is also seen as documentary confirmation of the later statements of the Bull sisters ([[ethel-bull|Ethel]] and [[mabel-bull|Milly]]) and their brother [[felix-bull|Alfred]], who insisted to investigators in the 1950s that no physical or objective poltergeist manifestations ever occurred during the Bull era [5, 15].

## Authenticity Concerns

Authors Edward Babbs and Claudine Mathias caution that the handwriting in the surviving copy of the diary may not actually be Dodie's [4]. Because Croom-Hollingsworth likely lent the original to Mrs Baines before it disappeared into private hands, it is probable that the surviving text is merely Mrs Baines's longhand copy, leaving the original unavailable for modern historical verification [4]. The first page of the diary (reproduced in *Borley Rectory: The Final Analysis* as Fig. 93) raises doubts about the handwriting on this basis.

> A separate diary kept by the [[henry-bull|Rev Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] covering 25 May 1858 to 1 August 1860 was discovered at the Essex County Records Office, Chelmsford. There is also the diary referred to by [[marie-poore|Marie Poore]], which was shown to her by Eileen Livock and recorded [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]]'s sightings of the nun in the garden. These are distinct documents.
