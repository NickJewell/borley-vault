# Dodie Bull's Diary (1885)

A diary kept by [[dodie-bull|Caroline Sarah Elizabeth Bull ("Dodie")]] covering the period 1 January to 28 October 1885, when she was 20–21 years old. It was discovered by [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth|Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth]] (the leader of the Harlow psychical research group) in a second-hand shop in Sudbury.

## Discovery

Croom-Hollingsworth found the diary in a Sudbury second-hand shop during the Harlow group's investigations in the 1970s. He offered it to the Harry Price Library at the University of London for £1,000 (worth approximately £9,500 in 2002 terms), but the authorities declined to pay this amount. The diary's subsequent fate is uncertain; Babbs and Mathias have a copy.

The first page of the diary (reproduced in *Borley Rectory: The Final Analysis* as Fig. 93) raises doubts about the handwriting — the authors suggest that [[croom-hollingsworth]] may have been in contact with a Mrs C.C. Baines, who wrote an unpublished book about Borley with frequent references to Dodie's diary, and that Baines may have copied it out in longhand.

## Contents

The diary gives a vivid picture of the social life of the Bull family at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in the 1880s:
- Fancy balls (Dodie attended one at Liston Hall on 7 January, returning at 3:45 a.m.)
- Tennis parties and reciprocal dinner parties with neighbouring families
- Dances at Borley Rectory and elsewhere (the "nursery was intended for spooning" on one occasion)
- Lessons resumed on 22 January; Dodie and her sister Freda (Winifred) taught the younger girls at home
- Harry Bull and his brothers attended Felstead School near Dunmow in Essex

On 11 January, Freda's 20th birthday: "How old one begins to feel. What a crop of old maids are growing up." (Dodie was not yet 21.)

## Paranormal Reference

The diary contains a single paranormal reference: an amateur séance with her sister Freda and a guest, with Dodie as medium, attempting to make chairs move. Her comment: "I seem to have a lot of electricity in me." The authors dryly observe she "appears to have been more interested in landing one of them [eligible young men] than in the supernatural."

## Significance

The diary ends on 28 October 1885, well before the famous 28 July 1900 sighting. It provides no firsthand paranormal testimony but is invaluable as a contemporaneous social document of the Bull household — confirming the lively, affluent lifestyle that Margaret Finch's testimony also supports. It counters the image of Borley Rectory as perpetually gloomy.

> A separate diary kept by the [[henry-bull|Rev Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] covering 25 May 1858 to 1 August 1860 was discovered at the Essex County Records Office, Chelmsford. There is also the diary referred to by [[marie-poore|Marie Poore]], which was shown to her by Eileen Livock and recorded Ethel Bull's sightings of the nun in the garden. These are distinct documents.
