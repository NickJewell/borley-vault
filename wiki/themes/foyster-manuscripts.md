# The Foyster Manuscripts

[[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] produced three separate written accounts of life at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] during the 1930–35 incumbency. These documents form the primary evidence base for the Foyster period — and are simultaneously the most detailed and the most contested records in the entire Borley archive. They are also the subject of fierce controversy among researchers who regard them as either sincere (if retrospective) testimony or as the framework for a deliberate fictional bestseller [3, 4].

## The Manuscripts Now Available in Full

The complete texts of Writing B (Diary of Occurrences) and Writing C (Fifteen Months in a Haunted House) are now in the archive at `raw/15-months-in-a-haunted-house/`, alongside [[peter-underwood]]'s foreword and Vincent O'Neil's introduction. See [[15mhh]] for the full source summary. Key findings from reading the actual texts:

**"The Horror" / "The Shadow":** Writing C describes a distinct entity separate from the [[the-nun|Nun]] — a shadowy, "gigantic bat-like" figure that appeared in the kitchen passage and touched [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] on the shoulder with "a hand of iron." Foyster: "It seemed to her more like a gigantic bat than anything else." Seen by others in the house before and after this occasion. Foyster speculates it may have been responsible for the blow to Marianne's eye that opened the "campaign of frightfulness." This entity is categorically distinct from the Nun.

**Marianne's scapula protection:** A consistent feature: Marianne was hurt "four times, but never when wearing her scapula. Twice she forgot or neglected putting them on, and once they were on a few minutes before, but the fastening... was mysteriously broken." This detail appears in both DO and Writing C.

**Lionel's four exculpatory instances:** The DO's July P.S. explicitly lists instances where Marianne clearly could not have thrown objects: (1) Sellwood witnessed stone from back stairs while Marianne was outside; (2) things thrown while Marianne was in a different room; (3) Sir George and Lady Whitehouse present in the fire room when stone was thrown in; (4) doctor present, throwing over banisters while Marianne was in bed in the spare room.

**Foyster's signed oath:** "I could declare, if it was necessary, on oath, that the foregoing is to the best of my knowledge an absolutely true statement of the facts." Signed L.A. Foyster, Thursday March 23rd, 1931.

## The Hastings Classification System

[[robert-hastings|R.J. Hastings]] (*An Examination of the 'Borley Report'*, 1969) provides the definitive four-document taxonomy, accepted by most subsequent scholars:

| Label | Description | Location |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| Writing A | Supposed original diary covering full 1930–35 period | Not in HPL; existence inferred from references; Clarke argues never existed |
| Writing B | "Diary of Occurrences" — three-instalment family circular, Feb–July 1931 | HPL; S.P.R. files |
| Writing C | "Fifteen Months in a Haunted House" — novelized, pseudonyms, for publication | HPL (acquired after EBR) |
| Writing D | Tabulated "Summary of Experiences" compiled at Price's request, 1938 | HPL |

Clarke's analysis (slbr-07) demonstrates Writing A almost certainly never existed as a discrete document.

## The Three Documents

### Diary of Occurrences (DO)

Probably written **March–July 1931**, though the events it covers begin October 1930. Lionel's own cover sheet states those dates; [[trevor-hall]] confirmed the manuscript describes events from that time period.

31 typewritten pages. Sent to [[harry-price]] October 3, 1931 with a covering letter saying it was written "chiefly to send round to members of my family." Intended for private circulation among Foyster's family [5-7].

Hall's analysis: "It is manifestly not a diary... It is clearly the first account of the alleged haunting written by Mr. Foyster, and much of the preliminary section is evidently written from unaided memory." Events were recorded months after they occurred.

Price claimed in *The Most Haunted House in England* that Foyster kept "a diary of the strange events that occurred from the very first day I entered upon my duties" — Hall described this as "deliberately misleading."

Price later told Marianne he had lost this manuscript; Marianne believed he stole it to use material in his own books.

### Fifteen Months in a Haunted House (FM)

Probably completed **after May 1934** (the last chapter confirms the date). A 180-page novelised account written with a view to commercial publication [5, 7, 8]. Much longer than the DO; contains pseudonyms for all persons involved — Borley became "Cromley Hall," and observer Edwin Whitehouse became "Edith Greycastle" [7, 9, 10]. Hall concluded this was the version Foyster was attempting to get published.

Uses the pseudonym "Fijis" for the poltergeist entities. The title deliberately echoes the most dramatic period of activity.

Hall believed Price did not acquire this manuscript until after *The End of Borley Rectory* was published in 1946; Price could not therefore have used it while writing his own books.

### Summary of Experiences (SE)

Written **January 24 – February 11, 1938** at Price's request for inclusion in *The Most Haunted House in England*. Seven pages [11, 12]. Hall notes: Price reproduced it in MHH and called it "The Rev. L.A. Foyster's Diary" — which it was not. The [[spr-investigation|SPR authors]] accused [[harry-price]] of deliberately misleading the public by publishing Foyster's 1938 Summary under the title "Leaves from the Foyster Diary," falsely implying it was a contemporary day-to-day record [12-14].

## Sceptical Arguments

### The SPR Accusations

The authors of the [[the-borley-report|*Borley Report*]] ([[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], and [[trevor-hall]]) attacked the validity of the manuscripts and Price's use of them on multiple fronts [3, 4]:

- Out of 103 phenomena recorded in the Diary of Occurrences, 99 depended entirely on the good faith of [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]], who was frequently suspected of staging the events [22, 23]
- Foyster's accounts were "wholly uncritical," absurdly attributing natural occurrences (such as cooking smells or his own forgetfulness) to "goblins" [19-21]

### The *Amherst Mystery* Connection

The [[spr-investigation|SPR authors]] pointed out striking similarities between Foyster's manuscripts and Walter Hubbell's bestselling book *The [[great-amherst-mystery|Great Amherst Mystery]]* [15, 16]. They noted that Foyster had previously lived in [[sackville-new-brunswick|Sackville]], just five miles from Amherst, Nova Scotia, and even used the unusual pseudonym "Teed" (the name of the Amherst family) in *Fifteen Months*, suggesting Foyster used the famous Canadian haunting as a blueprint to manufacture a copycat case [16-18].

## The "Fictional Book" Theory

The Owen/Mitchell report (1979) — based on [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]]'s own testimony — presents a radically different framework for these documents:

**Lionel Foyster was writing a fictional bestseller**, not a factual record.

Key points:

1. Lionel was well aware that Walter Hubbell had made significant money from *The [[great-amherst-mystery|Great Amherst Mystery]]* (a book about a poltergeist case)
2. He conceived a similar project: a "Fifteen Months in a Haunted House" that would solve the Foysters' severe financial problems [27-29]
3. The DO and SE were "the bones of a story" — plot outlines for the fictional manuscript
4. Everyone in the household knew this, including Marianne, and probably Price

Supporting evidence:

- The pseudonyms in FM confirm its novelistic intent
- Marianne told Swanson the manuscript "was supposed to be more or less fiction — the whole thing was"
- Marianne told Owen/Mitchell that Lionel "would relate, with great relish, stories of phenomena that were alleged to have happened, and which the family members present knew were not true, in order to observe his visitor's reactions"
- Lionel threw objects deliberately to observe guests' reactions — testing material for the book
- When confined to a wheelchair and unable to stage phenomena, the events ceased

[[robert-wood|Robert Wood]] expands on this further, arguing that Foyster was a willing accomplice to his wife's deceptions and used the sensational "poltergeist" manuscripts as a smokescreen to cover up [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]]'s blatant infidelities with a lodger named François d'Arles (Frank Pearless), even incorporating d'Arles's own fake "ghost" encounters into the text to legitimise his presence in the house [30-32].

### Lionel's Own Statements

Lionel stated in FM: "It is a record of facts and therefore is true." In DO: "I could declare, if it was necessary, on oath, that the foregoing is to the best of my knowledge an absolutely true statement of the facts."

But Owen/Mitchell note that Lionel "was not bothering too much with minor details" in the later FM, since he knew it was largely fictional and the DO was lost. The later manuscript contains inconsistencies and fictionalisations consistent with novelistic enhancement.

> **Contradiction:** Lionel explicitly swore on oath that the DO was "an absolutely true statement of the facts," yet Marianne flatly told Owen/Mitchell that the majority of events were "completely invented by Lionel" for a fictional mystery story [24-26].

## Price's Use of the Documents

Price reproduced large sections of the SE (calling it "The Rev. L.A. Foyster's Diary") in MHH. He represented it as a contemporaneous diary — which Hall demonstrated it was not. Whether Price knew of the fictional intention remains unclear. Marianne believed he did but found it useful to pretend otherwise. The matter was never resolved before Price's death in 1948 or Lionel's in 1945.

## Defences and Counter-Arguments

Despite severe scepticism, several researchers defend the integrity of the manuscripts:

- **Foyster's own defence:** In a 1940 letter to [[harry-price]], Foyster insisted that despite the pseudonyms in *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*, the psychic phenomena reported were "all exactly as they happened to the best of my knowledge and belief," and were recorded soon after their occurrence [7, 33]
- **[[robert-hastings|Hastings]]'s defence of Price:** Hastings argued that Price's introduction was not misleading because the 1938 Summary did not differ in substance from the earlier Diary of Occurrences [34]. He also noted that Price viewed his books as "scientific journalism" and made all the original Foyster files freely available in the University of London library during his lifetime — an action incompatible with deliberate fraud [35, 36]
- **Corroborative testimony:** Many of the spectacular incidents recorded by Foyster were independently corroborated by other witnesses — Edwin Whitehouse, Guy L'Estrange, and Sir George and Lady Whitehouse — rendering the theory of pure fiction unlikely [3, 37, 38]. Even [[sidney-glanville]], a highly respected and meticulous observer, read *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House* and described it as "the most astonishing document I have ever read" [33, 39]

## Reliability Assessment

| Manuscript | Written | From Memory? | Intended Use |
|-----------|---------|-------------|--------------|
| DO | Mar–Jul 1931 | Largely yes — events from 1930 onwards | Family circulation; later sent to Price |
| FM | After May 1934 | Substantially yes — from DO and memory | Commercial publication (fictional) |
| SE | Jan–Feb 1938 | Yes — 3–8 years after events | MHH inclusion (at Price's request) |

All three share a core problem: they were written retrospectively, from memory, by a man who was also (on Marianne's account) consciously fictionalising the events.

## Clarke's Additions (slbr-07)

Andrew Clarke's analysis in "The Foyster Diaries" (slbr-07) provides several corrections to the standard account:

**"Writing A" almost certainly never existed.** Clarke demonstrates this from Lionel's own language in the DO (*"no exact record"* of post-June events; things *"lost in oblivion"*), incompatible with having a diary in hand. [[robert-hastings|Robert Hastings]]'s defence of Price — positing a lost original diary — is rejected.

**Four documents, not three.** Clarke counts: "Writing A" (inferred, probably non-existent); the DO (three installments, March–June 1931); FM (c.1933–34 onwards); and the SE (January–February 1938). Previous analyses conflate these.

**The SE, not the DO, is what Price printed.** The SE was written in 1938 — seven years after the events — yet presented in MHH as a contemporaneous diary. The DO exists but was never published by Price.

**The Canon Lawton observation:** Henry Lawton (locum August 1933) came across a typed manuscript "accounting very fully" for the psychic history of the rectory. Clarke identifies this as probably FM (though FM does not cover the psychic history of the rectory before the Foysters' arrival). This confirms FM was substantially complete by August 1933, earlier than previously thought.

**The healthy short-term memory defence:** Clarke notes that Lionel's medical condition (cardiac insufficiency affecting short-term memory) would cause trivial "missing objects" incidents — but would not produce hallucinations or deliberate fabrication. The fabrication question depends on distinguishing what Lionel genuinely believed happened from what he wrote for an audience.
