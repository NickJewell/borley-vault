# The Psychic Fête (21 June 1939)

A public event held in the grounds of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] on 21 June 1939 — the longest day of the year, and just forty days before the outbreak of the Second World War — four months after the [[fire-1939|fire that destroyed the Rectory]] on 27 February. It was organised by the [[rev-ac-henning|Reverend A. C. Henning]] and his church council with the purpose of raising funds for the restoration of [[borley-church|Borley Church]].

## Organisation and Activities

The initial idea for the fête came from Captain Williams of Borley Lodge [1]. It was formally arranged by Henning, his Church Council, the Rectory's final owner, Captain William Gregson, and with the active cooperation of [[harry-price|Harry Price]] [1, 2]. The primary goal was to raise funds for the restoration of Borley Church; the event ultimately raised £24, though half went toward expenses [4, 5].

The fête was opened with a speech by [[mrs-ra-butler|Mrs R. A. Butler]], wife of the Under-Secretary of State for Foreign Affairs (and future Chancellor of the Exchequer and Master of Trinity) [1, 3]. [[harry-price|Harry Price]] attended, as did twenty-five members of the old [[ghost-club|Ghost Club]], including his secretary, Ethel English (formerly Beenham), making the occasion the Ghost Club's Summer Meeting [1, 6, 7].

Posters and flyers deliberately advertised the ruins as the "Most Haunted House in England" and offered conducted tours personally led by Captain Gregson and his two sons, Alan and Andrew [7–9]. Despite the grim setting, the fête featured traditional English village activities including a coconut shy, skittles, clock-golf, a "white elephant" stall, and a pig-pelting competition [3, 4].

## Claims Regarding Phenomena

Harry Price had light-heartedly proposed organising a "procession of historical ghosts" for attendees, but [[rev-ac-henning|the Rev Henning]] advised against it, noting it would not be effective in broad daylight [6]. Ultimately, **absolutely no supernatural phenomena occurred** [7]. *The Times* reported the following day: "No ghosts were seen. No bricks were hurled at visitors, and no one received a black eye. There were no psychic manifestations or strange phenomena..."[10].

In one pseudo-paranormal twist, former official observers Lt. Col. [[colonel-westland|Westland]] and his wife attended and discovered that a mysterious "blue box" — which they had originally found inside the Rectory during their 1937 vigil — was still sitting exactly where they had left it in the ruins two years earlier. They took it away as a souvenir [11, 12].

## Perspectives and Significance

- **The SPR Perspective:** The authors of the highly critical SPR *Borley Report* ([[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen Goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]]) viewed the 1939 Psychic Fête as a turning point that degraded psychical research. They argued that it ushered in the "Borley silly season" and "[[borley-hysteria|Borley hysteria]]" — a lengthy period characterised by "extravagant theory, unrestrained assumption, and extreme credulity" [13, 14]. Critics also note that the fête successfully cemented Price's label of "The Most Haunted House in England" as a lucrative, commercialised brand [8].
- **Local Scepticism:** The fête provided a platform for local dismissal. Mrs Pearson, who had previously been the Foysters' maid and a key witness to early phenomena, attended and openly dismissed the entire haunting, telling the press: "I have lived in my cottage for eighteen years and never a ghost have I seen yet. I don't believe a word of it" [7].
- **Price's Own Perspective:** Price himself viewed the event simply as a "pleasant day out" [5]. However, the sources note a sombre undertone: standing by the ruins watching attendees play games, Price worried deeply about the deteriorating international situation and the impending war with Germany, suspecting this might be his last visit to the sleepy Essex hamlet [5].

The Psychic Fête represents the last major gathering of key Borley figures while the ruins still stood as recognisable structure. Within a few months, the site and shell passed to a Mr Woods (a contractor who levelled the ruins and sold bricks — some for runways at nearby American Air Force bases), then to Captain Russell. The fête was thus an endpoint for the period of active investigation centred on the building itself. The fact that a church-fund fête was held in the Rectory grounds so shortly after the fire, and was sufficiently credible to attract Mrs Butler as opener, illustrates that the Rectory's haunting reputation was by this point mainstream enough to be commercially usable without stigma.

[[james-wentworth-day|James Wentworth Day]] recalls visiting the ruins "some four months after the fire" — approximately the same period.
