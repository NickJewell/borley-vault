# Rectors of Borley

Borley Church has had fifty-eight clerks officiate since its founding in the thirteenth century. The BRC provides the most comprehensive published list, based on parish records and the Babbs/Mathias *Final Analysis*. While the full list dates to Peter de Cacheporc in 1236 [1], the famous saga of "the most haunted house in England" centres almost entirely on the final four resident rectors of Borley Rectory (the Bulls, Smith, and Foyster), as well as the immediate successors who oversaw the parish after the house was abandoned (Henning and Mathias).

## The Bull Dynasty (1862–1927): The Genesis of the Legend

The rectory's haunted reputation began during the sixty-five-year period when the living was held by a father and son.

- [[henry-bull|Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] 1862–1892 (built [[borley-rectory]]; died of Locomotor Ataxia; thirteen children)
- [[harry-bull|Harry Foyster Bull]] 1892–1927 (died in the [[blue-room]])

**The Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull (1862–1892):** A wealthy, athletic "squarson" (squire-parson) who built the sprawling Borley Rectory in 1863 to accommodate his fourteen children and large staff [2, 3]. He was aware of the local legend of a phantom nun and even built a large summerhouse in the garden for the express purpose of watching for her [4, 5]. Historical researcher Ivan Banks theorised that Henry Bull had affairs with village girls, and that the death of a maid named Kate Boreham following an illegitimate pregnancy during his tenure may be the true historical catalyst for the haunting [6–8].

**The Rev. Harry Foyster Bull (1892–1927):** Henry's son Harry succeeded him and became Borley's most famous ghost-seer. He explicitly believed the rectory was haunted and frequently claimed to see [[the-nun|the ghost nun]], a [[phantom-coach|phantom coach with headless coachmen]], and the spirit of a former gardener known as "Old Amos" [9–11]. An eccentric man, he told a friend that if he was discontented after his death, he would return as a poltergeist and throw mothballs [12]. His late marriage to a widow, Ivy Brackenbury, caused a vicious family feud, leading his sisters to later falsely claim Ivy had murdered him [13, 14].

**Skeptical Counter-Arguments:** Skeptics such as [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] and Andrew Clarke argue that no violent [[poltergeist-activity|poltergeist activity]] occurred during the Bull years, only traditional "Gothic" ghost sightings [15]. They suggest the haunting was entirely a product of the Bulls' eccentricities, Harry's profound interest in spiritualism, and the lively imaginations and malicious gossip of the Bull sisters [16–18].

## Smith and Foyster Period (1928–1935)

- [[rev-eric-smith|Revd Guy Eric Smith]] 1928–1930 (left July 1929; ran parish from Long Melford until April 1930)
- [[lionel-foyster|Revd Lionel Algernon Foyster]] 1930–1935 (October 1930 to October 1935)

**The Smith Incumbency (1928–1930): The Media Catalyst:** After the rectory sat empty, it was occupied by the Rev. Guy Eric Smith, a Eurasian former civil servant from India, and his wife [[mabel-smith|Mabel]] [19–21]. The Smiths were deeply unsettled by the gloomy house. They reported hearing sibilant whisperings, the sounds of a horse-drawn coach, dragging footsteps, and loud [[bell-ringing|bell-ringing]], as well as finding keys inexplicably moved and discovering a human skull in a cupboard [22]. Hoping to dispel the local superstitions, Rev Smith contacted the *Daily Mirror* to find a psychical research society — unwittingly bringing reporter V.C. Wall and investigator [[harry-price|Harry Price]] to the rectory, sparking a media frenzy that attracted hordes of rowdy sightseers [23–25].

Skeptical author Robert Wood argues that the Smiths deliberately invited the press not to solve a haunting, but to generate publicity to help Mabel Smith publish a sensational, Borley-inspired novel she had written titled [[murder-at-the-parsonage|*Murder at the Parsonage*]] [26]. In 1945, Mabel Smith publicly retracted all claims of the paranormal, writing to the *Church Times* that neither she nor her husband believed the house was haunted by anything other than "rats and local superstition" [27–29]. However, researchers note this directly contradicted contemporary letters written by Rev Smith, who firmly believed the house had an evil atmosphere [30–32].

**The Foyster Incumbency (1930–1935): The Poltergeist Peak:** The Rev. Lionel Algernon Foyster, a cousin of the Bulls, returned from missionary work in Canada to take the living with his young wife, [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] [33, 34]. Lionel presided over what [[harry-price|Harry Price]] called the "noisiest, most violent, and most dangerous period" of the haunting [35]. Over 2,000 poltergeist incidents were reported, including physical assaults on Marianne, thrown bottles, mysterious fires, and desperate messages pleading for "light, mass, prayers" scrawled on the walls [36, 37]. Foyster meticulously recorded these events in a *Diary of Occurrences* and an unpublished manuscript titled *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House* [38, 39].

The highly critical SPR [[the-borley-report|*Borley Report*]] argued that the phenomena were consciously faked by [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] to manipulate her husband into moving, or as a smokescreen to cover up an illicit affair with a lodger [40, 41]. Skeptic Robert Wood goes further, arguing that Lionel Foyster was not a credulous victim, but a voyeur and willing accomplice who helped his wife fake the haunting so he could write a lucrative bestselling thriller modelled after the famous Canadian [[great-amherst-mystery|Great Amherst Mystery]] [42–45]. [[marianne-controversy|Marianne]] herself later gave contradictory interviews, at times admitting she and Lionel manufactured phenomena, and at other times insisting genuine poltergeists were present [46, 47].

## Post-Foyster (1936–present)

In 1936 the living of Borley was combined with that of [[liston]] (a village one mile north-east).

- [[rev-ac-henning|Revd Alfred Clifford Henning]] 1936–1955 (died 14 January 1955; lived at Liston Rectory)
- Revd Edward Lanfranc Morgan Mathias 1955–1967 (last rector of Borley and Liston; inducted 28 July 1955; resigned 1967)
- James Henry Leslie Pennell 1967 (combined the parishes of Borley, Liston, Foxearth and [[pentlow]])
- Keith Finnimore 1973
- Ernest Frederick Leonard Brown 1977
- Christopher Cook 1984
- Joseph Thomas Davies 1989
- Capt. Brian Sampson 1996

**The Henning Incumbency (1936–1955):** [[rev-ac-henning|The Rev Alfred Clifford Henning]] lived at [[liston-rectory|Liston Rectory]] but was deeply fascinated by the Borley haunting. He rented the empty rectory to [[harry-price|Harry Price]] in 1937, oversaw its sale to Capt. W. H. Gregson, and witnessed its destruction by [[fire-1939|fire in 1939]] [49, 50]. Henning personally claimed to experience phenomena, including unexplained footsteps and phantom organ music inside [[borley-church|Borley Church]], which he detailed in his booklet *Haunted Borley* [51, 52]. Following Harry Price's death, Henning and his wife vigorously defended Price against accusations of fraud levelled by the SPR [53].

**The Mathias Incumbency (1955–1967):** Revd Edward Lanfranc Morgan Mathias succeeded Henning but was explicitly warned by the Bishop of Chelmsford that his first loyalty must be to the pastoral care of his parishioners, not to investigating the paranormal [54]. Consequently, Mathias kept a low profile regarding the haunting, though he privately admitted to feeling an unexplained "presence" when alone in the church [54]. Parishes in the area were later further amalgamated, and subsequent rectors no longer resided at Borley [55, 56].

## John Deeks

The BRC mentions **John Deeks** (rector from 1642) as a spirit who apparently communicated at a séance organised by the Revd John Dening in Borley Church in 2004, where a medium reportedly contacted and "laid to rest" the spirit of this former clergyman, who claimed to have been the cause of the haunting over the years.

## Humphrey Borough

The vault of **Humphrey Borough** (rector 1722) was discovered during excavations under Borley Church in May 1947 (see [[bones-excavation|Excavations]]). Three skeletons were visible in about 5 feet of water — presumably Borough and his daughters, with the wood of the coffins eaten away. The hole was sealed and new flooring laid.
