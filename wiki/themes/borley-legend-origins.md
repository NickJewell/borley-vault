# The Origins of the Borley Legend

O'Connor's *The Haunting of Borley Rectory* provides the most thorough analysis of how the legend was assembled from pre-existing cultural raw materials before any paranormal claims were made. The key components are traced below; see also [[borley-legends]] for the legends themselves and their historical debunking.

## 1. Structural and Environmental Causes

The Rectory was built in 1862–63 on clay soil, using hard cement (not flexible lime mortar) that cracked easily. The carpenters fitted the floorboards tightly with no allowance for movement; the woodwork would **creak and groan** with every temperature change — most audible at night. The enclosed courtyard produced unusual acoustics: sounds echoed throughout the house when windows facing it were open. Deathwatch beetles (tapping to attract mates) were prevalent in the old timbers. The isolated ridge position meant north-easterly winds rattled windows constantly.

## 2. The Anti-Catholic Context of Essex and Suffolk

The area had fierce anti-Catholic sentiment since the Reformation: the 1862 Ipswich riots, Matthew Hopkins's witch-hunts. The Gothic novel tradition of "doomed nuns" and "sinister monks" — from *Awful Disclosures of Maria Monk* to Julia McNair Wright's *Almost a Nun* — was in full circulation by the 1860s [38]. A legend of a nun romantically involved with a monk, bricked up alive as punishment, was a cultural trope perfectly fitted to the period and place in which the Rectory was built. The 19th century was rife with anti-Catholic hysteria, and the Borley legend perfectly mirrors these popular literary tropes.

## 3. Dodie Bull's Imagination

[[dodie-bull|Caroline "Dodie" Bull]] (b. 1864) was the eldest daughter at the Rectory and its dominant imaginative force. Her 1885 diary reveals:
- A "strong imagination" and ability to write "rather good short stories."
- Active experimentation with spiritualism ("I made a chair walk all over the room").
- Frustration at the limited horizon available to a rector's daughter — no profession, no vote, no property rights.

She and her brother [[harry-bull|Harry]] *"encouraged the notion of the Rectory being haunted by the tragic nun and it soon became part of family lore."* Their guest [[p-shaw-jeffrey|Percy Shaw Jeffrey]]'s reported experiences (1885–86) — stones falling, boots on wardrobes, French dictionary disappearing — are more consistent with adolescent pranks between competitive sisters than with [[poltergeist-activity|poltergeist activity]]. The diary confirms no physical manifestations in the Bull era — only chair-walking experiments and storytelling, entirely consistent with legend construction rather than genuine haunting.

[[andrew-clarke]] additionally suggests that the Rectory's builder, [[henry-bull|Rev. Henry D. E. Bull]], installed a dining room fireplace carved with monks' heads to indulge this Gothic atmosphere [39, 40]. The Bull children — particularly the highly imaginative daughters and the spiritually obsessed Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] — wove local murders, village scandals, and ghost tropes into a grand "family joke" and folklore tapestry [39, 41, 42].

## 4. The Servant Culture

The Rectory employed large numbers of female servants who lived on the premises, separated from their families. The elder servants routinely frightened newcomers with the haunting legend. Martha Seaber Wilson (later Mrs Byford), who entered service c. 1885 aged about 15, heard slippered footsteps in the corridor and left after a month — then spent the rest of her life believing she had had a paranormal experience. Her account appeared in the *Daily Mirror* in 1929 as apparently independent corroboration; in fact it was the legend circulating back.

## 5. Harry Bull's Cultivation

[[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] (rector 1892–1927) was genuinely interested in spiritualism and "not averse to discussing it with the locals." He cultivated the legend: he watched for the [[the-nun|nun]] from the summer house he had built for the purpose; told his pupil John Osborne Harley that he planned to "pelt 'em with mothballs" after death; and used the legend for what O'Connor delicately suggests was a kind of spiritual companionship during a deteriorating marriage.

[[andrew-clarke]] notes that Harry Bull spent nine years (1911–1920) living across the road at [[borley-place|Borley Place]], not in the Rectory, after his marriage. For much of his incumbency he also served other parishes. His personal involvement in the legend was therefore more limited than Price implied.

## 6. The 1900 Sighting and Its Transmission

The four-sister sighting of a "dark figure" in the garden (summer 1900) became the foundation event. By the time it was recalled by [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]] in 1929, the figure had been transformed into the [[the-nun|nun]] legend through fifty years of family retelling. Clarke's itinerant-traveller explanation notes the sighting happened at twilight, on a lane used by itinerant workers who dressed entirely in black (including women hoeing beet in black scarves). Clarke knew the last East Anglian knife-sharpeners — dressed in black, hair covered — within living memory [slbr-05].

## The Legend by 1928

By the time [[rev-eric-smith|Eric Smith]] arrived in 1928, the legend was complete: phantom nun, headless coach, bricked-up wall, monastery, murdered lover. The physical house (cold, dark, creaking, isolated) provided the atmospheric foundation. The Bull family had 65 years of investment in the story. The servants and villagers had inherited it as local knowledge.

O'Connor's conclusion: **"There is no evidence of a ghost story specifically relating to Borley until the nineteenth century."** The story grew as a cultural product of its times and place — and then attracted witnesses who experienced its setting as genuinely uncanny.

## Clarke's Additions: Borrowed Legends Specifically Identified

[[andrew-clarke]] contributes several specific findings from local research:

**The Foxearth Historical Society's search:** 15 years of systematic newspaper research from 1780 onward found **no mention of any haunting whatsoever at Borley before 1929**. The legend was never publicly circulating in the area.

Borrowed legends specifically identified:
- **The headless coachman and ghostly coach:** An essentially identical legend was published in the *Bury and Norwich Post* in March 1852, attached to Acton (adjacent parish), a full 77 years before Borley's first public haunting report [20, 23, 24].
- **The nun/tunnel/skeleton legend:** Borrowed from [[monks-hall-glemsford|Monks Hall, Glemsford]] (across the Stour Valley), where tunnel stories connecting hall to church were taught in local schools. In 1850, the skeletons of a male and female were discovered in a shallow grave near Glemsford; Clarke argues that local speculation over these bones is the true origin of the doomed monk-and-nun romance [20–22].
- **"The Nun's Walk":** Named after a road in Great Yeldham, where the local rectory was also "haunted" and which the Bulls visited occasionally.
- **Local crime legends ("Katie Boreham" etc.):** Drawn from real nearby events — Rev Foster's rape case (Foxearth, 1871), Catherine Foster's arsenic poisoning (Acton, 1846), Maria Marten murder (Polstead).

## The "Monastery" Confusion: Historical Origins

The claim of a monastery at Borley is historically traceable to two genuine ecclesiastical connections. In 1362, King Edward III granted the Manor of Borley to the Prior and Community of Christ Church, Canterbury [14–16]. Additionally, a Benedictine Priory ([[st-bartholomews-priory|St Bartholomew's]]) existed across the River Stour and held "Free Warren" (access to woodland) in Borley [17–19]. These administrative links to monastic orders likely mutated into the rumour of a physical monastery on the Rectory site. In 1938, [[sidney-glanville]] consulted the Essex Archaeological Society, which confirmed definitively that no religious house or ecclesiastical building other than the 12th-century parish church ever existed at Borley [3, 6–8].

## The Marie Lairre Theory: A Second Origin Story

During [[harry-price]]'s tenancy of the Rectory in 1937, planchette séances conducted by the Glanville family produced a new origin story [8, 25, 26]. The "communicating entity" identified herself as [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]], a 19-year-old French Catholic novice from Le Havre [8, 26, 27]. Canon W. J. Phythian-Adams analysed the séance transcripts and [[wall-writings]], constructing a theory that Marie Lairre had been tricked into marrying a member of the powerful [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]], brought to Borley, and strangled to death on 17 May 1667 [26–29]. Phythian-Adams believed her body was thrown down a well in the cellar, prompting her spirit to write messages on the walls begging for Catholic prayers and a Requiem Mass [26, 30, 31]. Price championed this theory in *The End of Borley Rectory*, especially after discovering bone fragments in the cellar in 1943 [25, 32, 33].

> **Contradiction:** The SPR authors accused [[harry-price]] of deliberately planting French Catholic medallions in the Rectory to fraudulently validate the Marie Lairre story, noting that he only promoted the French nun theory *after* his initial English monastery legend was debunked [7, 34, 35]. [[robert-wood]] dismisses the séance data that birthed the Marie Lairre legend as complete "rubbish," generated entirely by the subconscious minds of the sitters and fuelled by leading questions based on village gossip [36, 37].

## The Media's Role in Cementing the Legend

The local folklore officially transformed into the legend of "The Most Haunted House in England" in June 1929 [45, 46]. Frustrated and frightened by the Rectory's atmosphere and local gossip, the Rev. [[rev-eric-smith|Guy Eric Smith]] and his wife [[mabel-smith|Mabel]] contacted the *Daily Mirror* [42, 46]. Mabel Smith was actually gathering material for a supernatural thriller she was writing, titled [[murder-at-the-parsonage|*Murder at the Parsonage*]] [47, 48]. The *Daily Mirror* sent reporter V. C. Wall and investigator [[harry-price|Harry Price]] to the scene, and their sensationalised daily dispatches permanently etched the Borley legend into the public consciousness [46, 49].
