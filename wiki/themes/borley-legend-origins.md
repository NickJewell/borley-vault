# The Origins of the Borley Legend

O'Connor's *The Haunting of Borley Rectory* provides the most thorough analysis of how the legend was assembled from pre-existing cultural raw materials before any paranormal claims were made. The key components:

## 1. Structural and Environmental Causes

The Rectory was built in 1862–63 on clay soil, using hard cement (not flexible lime mortar) that cracked easily. The carpenters fitted the floorboards tightly with no allowance for movement; the woodwork would **creak and groan** with every temperature change — most audible at night. The enclosed courtyard produced unusual acoustics: sounds echoed throughout the house when windows facing it were open. Deathwatch beetles (tapping to attract mates) were prevalent in the old timbers. The isolated ridge position meant north-easterly winds rattled windows constantly.

## 2. The Anti-Catholic Context of Essex and Suffolk

The area had fierce anti-Catholic sentiment since the Reformation; the 1862 Ipswich riots; Matthew Hopkins's witch-hunts. The Gothic novel tradition of "doomed nuns" and "sinister monks" — from *Awful Disclosures of Maria Monk* to Julia McNair Wright's *Almost a Nun* — was in full circulation by the 1860s. A legend of a nun romantically involved with a monk, bricked up alive as punishment, was a cultural trope perfectly fitted to the period and place in which the Rectory was built.

## 3. Dodie Bull's Imagination

[[dodie-bull|Caroline "Dodie" Bull]] (b. 1864) was the eldest daughter at the Rectory and its dominant imaginative force. Her 1885 diary reveals:
- A "strong imagination" and ability to write "rather good short stories."
- Active experimentation with spiritualism ("I made a chair walk all over the room").
- Frustration at the limited horizon available to a rector's daughter — no profession, no vote, no property rights.

She and her brother [[harry-bull|Harry]] *"encouraged the notion of the Rectory being haunted by the tragic nun and it soon became part of family lore."* Their guest Percy Shaw Jeffrey's reported experiences (1885–86) — stones falling, boots on wardrobes, French dictionary disappearing — are more consistent with adolescent pranks between competitive sisters than with poltergeist activity.

## 4. The Servant Culture

The Rectory employed large numbers of female servants who lived on the premises, separated from their families. The elder servants routinely frightened newcomers with the haunting legend. Martha Seaber Wilson (later Mrs Byford), who entered service c. 1885 aged about 15, heard slippered footsteps in the corridor and left after a month — then spent the rest of her life believing she had had a paranormal experience. Her account appeared in the *Daily Mirror* in 1929 as apparently independent corroboration; in fact it was the legend circulating back.

## 5. Harry Bull's Cultivation

[[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] (rector 1892–1927) was genuinely interested in spiritualism and "not averse to discussing it with the locals." He cultivated the legend: he watched for the nun from the summer house he had built for the purpose; told his pupil John Osborne Harley that he planned to "pelt 'em with mothballs" after death; and used the legend for what O'Connor delicately suggests was a kind of spiritual companionship during a deteriorating marriage.

## 6. The 1900 Sighting and Its Transmission

The four-sister sighting of a "dark figure" in the garden (summer 1900) became the foundation event. By the time it was recalled by Ethel Bull in 1929, the figure had been transformed into the nun legend through fifty years of family retelling. The sighting may have been a villager using the garden as a shortcut at dusk.

## The Legend by 1928

By the time [[rev-eric-smith|Eric Smith]] arrived in 1928, the legend was complete: phantom nun, headless coach, bricked-up wall, monastery, murdered lover. The physical house (cold, dark, creaking, isolated) provided the atmospheric foundation. The Bull family had 65 years of investment in the story. The servants and villagers had inherited it as local knowledge.

O'Connor's conclusion: **"There is no evidence of a ghost story specifically relating to Borley until the nineteenth century."** The story grew as a cultural product of its times and place — and then attracted witnesses who experienced its setting as genuinely uncanny.

## Clarke's Additions (slbr-04, slbr-05, slbr-11, slbr-17)

[[andrew-clarke|Andrew Clarke]] contributes several specific findings from local research:

**The Foxearth Historical Society's search:** 15 years of systematic newspaper research from 1780 onward found **no mention of any haunting whatsoever at Borley before 1929**. The legend was never publicly circulating in the area.

**Borrowed legends specifically identified:**
- **The headless coachman and ghostly coach:** An essentially identical legend was published in the *Bury and Norwich Post* in March 1852, attached to Acton (adjacent parish), a full 77 years before Borley's first public haunting report.
- **The nun/tunnel/skeleton legend:** Borrowed from Monks Hall, Glemsford (across the Stour Valley), where tunnel stories connecting hall to church were taught in local schools.
- **"The Nun's Walk":** Named after a road in Great Yeldham, where the local rectory was also "haunted" and which the Bulls visited occasionally.
- **Local crime legends ("Katie Boreham" etc.):** Drawn from real nearby events — Rev Foster's rape case (Foxearth, 1871), Catherine Foster's arsenic poisoning (Acton, 1846), Maria Marten murder (Polstead).

**The itinerant-traveller explanation (slbr-05):** The 1900 four-sister sighting happened at twilight, on a lane used by itinerant workers who dressed entirely in black (including women hoeing beet in black scarves). Clarke knew the last East Anglian knife-sharpeners — dressed in black, hair covered — within living memory.

**Dodie Bull's diary:** Confirms no physical manifestations in the Bull era — only chair-walking experiments and storytelling. Entirely consistent with legend construction rather than genuine haunting.

**Harry Bull's absence:** Harry Bull spent nine years (1911–1920) living across the road at Borley Place, not in the Rectory, after his marriage. For much of his incumbency he served other parishes. His personal involvement in the legend was therefore more limited than Price implied.
