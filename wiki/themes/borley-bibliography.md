# Borley Bibliography

[[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] is one of the most studied and written-about cases of haunting in the history of psychical research. Since first coming to public attention in 1929 it has been the subject of seven full-length books (not including the BRC) and substantial chapters in many others. The sources present the Borley bibliography as a battleground of conflicting perspectives, generally divided into works defending the haunting and [[harry-price|Harry Price's]] methods, and highly sceptical works accusing him of fraud. An early bibliography was compiled by Price in 1946, listing over 100 newspaper and magazine articles [2], while researcher [[vincent-oneil|Vincent O'Neil]] later maintained an extensive online bibliography cataloguing worldwide references up to 2004 [1, 3].

## Annotations on Key Works

### Harry Price's Foundational Works

***The Most Haunted House in England* (1940):** Price's first full-length book on Borley, which made the Rectory world-famous [4, 5]. Price claimed to be a dispassionate narrator, stating the book was largely a verbatim editing of firsthand oral and written testimony from his official observers [6]. Sceptics — particularly the authors of the [[the-borley-report|SPR's *Borley Report*]] — argued the book was sensationalised "psychic journalism" and accused Price of deliberately manipulating testimony, discarding normal explanations, and exaggerating commonplace happenings to produce a profitable bestseller [7, 8].

***The End of Borley Rectory* (1946):** Price's sequel, covering the 1939 fire, the Cambridge Commission's investigations, and the [[bones-excavation|1943 cellar excavations]] [9–11]. Price used it to outline Canon Phythian-Adams's theory that the Rectory was haunted by a murdered French nun, [[marie-lairre|"Marie Lairre,"]] pointing to excavated bones as physical proof [11, 12]. Sceptics — particularly [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] and [[robert-wood|Robert Wood]] — argued Price had deliberately planted the bones himself to secure a sensational climax for the book [13].

### Sceptical Works

***The Haunting of Borley Rectory* / *The Borley Report* (1956):** Commissioned by the Society for Psychical Research and written by Dr [[eric-dingwall|Eric J. Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen M. Goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Trevor H. Hall]] [14, 15]. This highly critical report accused Price of willful deception, fraud, and misrepresentation in virtually every aspect of the case, concluding the evidence for the haunting was "scientifically worthless" [14, 16, 17]. Defenders of Price argued the report was a biased "case for the prosecution" that distorted evidence to fit a preconceived agenda, noting the authors waited until Price was dead and unable to defend himself before publishing [18–20].

***Search for Harry Price* (1978):** By [[trevor-hall|Trevor H. Hall]]. A scathing biographical attack aiming to permanently destroy Price's reputation, characterising him as dishonest [21, 22]. Reviewers including John Randall described the book as "infused with unmitigated malice," and Colin Wilson argued Hall produced no actual evidence that Price was anything other than a dedicated, if publicity-hungry, investigator [21, 23].

***The Bones of Borley* (2004):** An online essay series by East Anglian local historian Andrew Clarke. Clarke dismisses all paranormal phenomena at Borley as the natural settling of old timbers, pranks, or a hysterical joke exploited by Price, and views the haunting as a "put up job by Londoners" fuelled by superstition and snobbery [24–27].

***We Faked the Ghosts of Borley Rectory* (2000):** A self-published autobiography by [[we-faked-the-ghosts-of-borley-rectory|Louis Mayerling]] (real name George Carter), claiming to have lived at the Rectory and personally orchestrated the hauntings as a hoax [28, 29]. Researchers entirely exposed the book as a literary hoax: there is absolutely no record of Mayerling/Carter ever visiting Borley or knowing any of its residents [28, 30, 31].

### Defences of the Haunting

***An Examination of the "Borley Report"* (1969):** A comprehensive rebuttal by SPR member [[robert-hastings|Robert J. Hastings]]. Hastings spent years re-examining the SPR's allegations and concluded that all major charges of fraud against Price could be dismissed — demonstrating that Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall had selectively edited and distorted documentary evidence to build their case [20, 32–34].

***The Ghosts of Borley* (1973):** By [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] and Paul Tabori. Using Price's archives, the authors provided an updated assessment of the case that defended Price's legacy and concluded that genuine paranormal activity did occur at Borley [35].

***The Enigma of Borley Rectory* (1996):** By Ivan Banks. Supports Hastings' defence of Price, focusing heavily on the historical origins of the haunting, analysis of séance transcripts, and examination of [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]] connections [36].

***Borley Rectory — The Final Analysis* (2003):** By Edward Babbs and Claudine Mathias. Utilises local knowledge and firsthand interviews with surviving residents to challenge sceptical assertions and present new background material on the complexity of the hauntings [37].

### Fictional and Novelised Manuscripts

The bibliography also includes unpublished manuscripts written by former Rectory inhabitants, which critics argue prove the hauntings were exaggerated for financial gain:

***Murder at the Parsonage*:** An unpublished novel by [[mabel-smith|Mabel Smith]] (wife of the Rev. Guy Smith). [[robert-wood|Robert Wood]] argues the Smiths originally contacted the *Daily Mirror* not because they were frightened, but to generate publicity to help Mabel sell her supernatural thriller [38–41].

***Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*:** A novelised account written by the Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]]. [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] later told investigators that Lionel deliberately invented the majority of the "phenomena" to write a profitable bestseller, modelling his efforts on the famous [[great-amherst-mystery|*Great Amherst Mystery*]] by Walter Hubbell [42–44].

## Note on the BRC Bibliography

The Borley Rectory Companion (2009) contains the most comprehensive bibliography in print, including rare papers and articles in specialist publications. [[vincent-oneil|Vincent O'Neil's]] comprehensive bibliography compiled for borleyrectory.com was considered probably the most complete ever assembled before the site went offline in 2004.
