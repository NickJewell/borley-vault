# Borley Hysteria

General term coined by the BRC to cover the many sensational, "silly season"-type incidents and fabrications that accumulated around Borley over the years following [[harry-price]]'s publications from 1940 onwards. The sources describe this as a protracted period of "extravagant theory, unrestrained assumption, and extreme credulity" [1, 2]. Price himself was not immune to this phenomenon.

## Origins

The [[spr-investigators|SPR investigators]] — [[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], and [[trevor-hall]] — felt that Borley hysteria began in earnest following the psychic fête of June 1939 and, when it apparently ushered in a lengthy period of "extravagant theory, unrestrained assumption, and extreme credulity," they regarded their [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] as the appropriate antidote. They also argued that [[harry-price]]'s [[blue-book-of-instructions]] subjected inexperienced observers to a "barrage of suggestion," priming them to misinterpret normal drafts, animal noises, or settling timbers as paranormal events [11–13].

## Representative Examples

Following the publication of *EBR* in 1946, correspondents sent Price reports that even he must have found too far-fetched for his "psychic journalism." In 1947 alone:

- **The Anonymous Letter**: An elderly lady and her brother saw the [[phantom-coach]] arrive at the Rectory, watched the occupants descend and return, then saw the coach "rise in the air, disintegrate, the various limbs, wheels, etc. falling in all directions." The knocking needle that reportedly shattered during the same year's BBC radio broadcast was subsequently sent to Price for preservation [2, 6].
- **The exploding knitting needle**: Broke into pieces in a Hendon front room during the broadcast of *The Haunted Rectory* radio programme [2, 6].
- **The Mackintosh Ghost**: A ghost "sat on" a visitor's mackintosh in Borley churchyard.
- **The Lost Pencil**: Mr Croft lost a pencil while visiting Borley and wrote to Price describing it in case it "appeared mysteriously" or was "involved in any phenomena."

Later examples:
- **1949 mushroom story**: *Suffolk Free Press* reported that poltergeists had destroyed [[james-turner]]'s mushroom crops; Turner denied all knowledge [1].
- **1950 "cloud of white steam"**: A visitor saw a cloud rise from the chancel floor, writhe into shapes, and vanish in the roof — and "kept it to myself."
- **1950 Phantom Hitchhiker**: A headless man in the passenger seat of a motorist who became lost near Borley; the figure gestured the way to Sudbury then passed through the car door.
- **Jinxed pebble** (1954): A visitor took a pebble as a souvenir; his car broke down and his house was burgled; he sent the pebble back.
- **Keith Wright** (late): Claimed to have been thrown against his car door by a mysterious force, and the car was then "lifted a few feet off the ground and moved twelve feet along the road" [7].

Canon Phythian-Adams's theory that a 17th-century ghost nun used a misplaced French-English dictionary in the 1880s to learn enough English to write the famous [[wall-writings]] in the 1930s is also cited as hovering "closely to what the present authors have described as Borley hysteria" [8, 9].

## The Phantom Vicar of the Isle of Dogs

The BRC discusses an instructive parallel: Frank Smyth invented the "Phantom Vicar" of Ratcliff Wharf, Isle of Dogs for a magazine article, then BBC's *A Leap in the Dark* took a film crew to investigate — and found genuine witness testimony from a publican and his wife who described seeing a ghost. Under hypnosis, a woman also "saw" the invented apparition. This demonstrates that the general public's desire for ghost stories, combined with a known "haunted location," can generate apparently sincere testimony even about entirely fictional phenomena.

## Arguments in Favour of the Hysteria Reading

Several contemporaries and subsequent commentators endorsed a wholesale dismissal of the Borley phenomena as self-generated hysteria:

- **Andrew Clarke (*The Bones of Borley*)** views the entire haunting as a "hysterical, fabricated joke exploited and encouraged by Harry Price" [14, 15]. Clarke argues that sightings of the [[the-nun|nun]] were likely misidentified trespassers, courting couples, or pranks played on gullible housemaids, and that auditory phenomena were merely the natural creaks and groans of an old house [15].
- **Sir John Braithwaite**, a neighbour, concluded that [[marianne-foyster]] was hysterical and manufactured the phenomena herself during her husband's incumbency due to dissatisfaction with her life [16, 17].
- **[[mabel-smith]]** (wife of the Rev. [[rev-eric-smith|Guy Smith]]) later claimed the house was only haunted by "rats and local superstition," stating her mind had been negatively influenced by village gossip and a fear of the unknown [18, 19].

## Counter-Arguments

Defenders of the Borley case have pushed back on a blanket hysteria explanation on several grounds:

- **Physical evidence:** The Rev. [[lionel-foyster]] argued that one cannot simply "imagine" stones, bricks, and pictures lying on the floor, or a broken window, when the physical damage remains in evidence the next day [20].
- **Pre-existing activity:** Proponents point out that genuinely unexplained phenomena — such as the 1900 sighting of the [[the-nun|nun]] by the four Bull sisters — occurred decades before [[harry-price]] arrived and before the massive wave of newspaper-driven hysteria began [22–24]. Even if one strips away the obvious exaggerations and the "silly season" claims, defenders maintain that a core residue of inexplicable, documented activity remains [4, 25].
- **[[peter-underwood]] and Edward Babbs** acknowledge that much of the hype was driven by newspaper editors creating sensational headlines to sell copy [4], but argue it is "short-sighted" and unscientific to dismiss the entire 80-year haunting as mere hysteria or suggestion [4, 21].

## Assessment

The BRC explicitly warns against dismissing all Borley evidence as hysteria, while acknowledging that much of what accumulated after 1940 is self-perpetuating legend. The test they apply is: did the report come from a person with no prior knowledge of Borley's reputation, in conditions where they could not have been influenced by knowledge of expected phenomena? Reports that meet these criteria — such as [[fred-cartwright]]'s nun sightings in 1927, or Dr Margaret Abernethy's 1949 sighting — carry more weight than reports generated from within the established Borley tourist/investigation circuit.
