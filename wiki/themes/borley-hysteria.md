# Borley Hysteria

General term coined by the BRC to cover the many sensational, "silly season"-type incidents and fabrications that accumulated around Borley over the years following [[harry-price]]'s publications from 1940 onwards. Price himself was not immune to this phenomenon.

## Origins

The [[spr-investigators|SPR investigators]] felt that "Borley hysteria" began in earnest following the psychic fête of June 1939 and, when it apparently ushered in a lengthy period of "extravagant theory, unrestrained assumption, and extreme credulity," they regarded their [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] as the appropriate antidote.

## Representative Examples

Following the publication of *EBR* in 1946, correspondents sent Price reports that even he must have found too far-fetched for his "psychic journalism." In 1947 alone:

- **The Anonymous Letter**: An elderly lady and her brother saw the phantom coach arrive at the Rectory, watched the occupants descend and return, then saw the coach "rise in the air, disintegrate, the various limbs, wheels, etc. falling in all directions"
- **The exploding knitting needle**: Broke into pieces in a Hendon front room during the broadcast of *The Haunted Rectory* radio programme
- **The Mackintosh Ghost**: A ghost "sat on" a visitor's mackintosh in Borley churchyard
- **The Lost Pencil**: Mr Croft lost a pencil while visiting Borley and wrote to Price describing it in case it "appeared mysteriously" or was "involved in any phenomena"

Later examples:
- **1949 mushroom story**: *Suffolk Free Press* reported that poltergeists had destroyed James Turner's mushroom crops; Turner denied all knowledge
- **1950 "cloud of white steam"**: A visitor saw a cloud rise from the chancel floor, writhe into shapes, and vanish in the roof — and "kept it to myself"
- **1950 Phantom Hitchhiker**: A headless man in the passenger seat of a motorist who became lost near Borley; the figure gestured the way to Sudbury then passed through the car door
- **Jinxed pebble** (1954): A visitor took a pebble as a souvenir; his car broke down and his house was burgled; he sent the pebble back
- **Keith Wright** (late): Claimed to have been thrown against his car door by a mysterious force, and the car was then "lifted a few feet off the ground and moved twelve feet along the road"

## The Phantom Vicar of the Isle of Dogs

The BRC discusses an instructive parallel: Frank Smyth invented the "Phantom Vicar" of Ratcliff Wharf, Isle of Dogs for a magazine article, then BBC's *A Leap in the Dark* took a film crew to investigate — and found genuine witness testimony from a publican and his wife who described seeing a ghost. Under hypnosis, a woman also "saw" the invented apparition. This demonstrates that the general public's desire for ghost stories, combined with a known "haunted location," can generate apparently sincere testimony even about entirely fictional phenomena.

## Assessment

The BRC explicitly warns against dismissing all Borley evidence as hysteria, while acknowledging that much of what accumulated after 1940 is self-perpetuating legend. The test they apply is: did the report come from a person with no prior knowledge of Borley's reputation, in conditions where they could not have been influenced by knowledge of expected phenomena? Reports that meet these criteria — such as [[fred-cartwright]]'s nun sightings in 1927, or [[dr-margaret-abernethy]]'s 1949 sighting — carry more weight than reports generated from within the established Borley tourist/investigation circuit.
