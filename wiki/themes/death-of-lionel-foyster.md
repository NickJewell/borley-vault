# Death of Lionel Foyster (18 April 1945)

[[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Algernon Foyster]] died on **18 April 1945** at [[dairy-cottage-rendlesham|Dairy Cottage, Rendlesham]], Suffolk, and was buried in the nearby churchyard at [[campsey-ashe|Campsey Ashe]] [1, 2, 3]. The death certificate was signed by [[kjt-keer|K.J.T. Keer MRCS]], with [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] as the informant present at death. Multiple investigators, medical professionals, and family members have questioned whether the death was natural.

Two days after Lionel's death, Marianne placed a brief death notice in *The Times*, citing his Canadian appointments but conspicuously omitting any mention of Borley Rectory. She ended it with the poetic quote, "There is no death" [3].

## The Death Certificate

**Stated causes of death:** *"Exhaustion, bedsores, and rheumatoid arthritis."*

**Place of death:** Dairy Cottage, Rendlesham, Essex [sic — actually Suffolk]
**Informant present at death:** Marianne Foyster
**Doctor signatory:** K.J.T. Keer MRCS, Wickham Market
**Burial:** Campsea Ashe churchyard. Stone inscription: *"Lionel Algernon Foyster, Priest. 1878–1945. The Lord is my Shepherd."*

[[robert-wood]] consulted several doctors about these causes: "all of them considered the causes to be insufficient." Specifically:

- "No one dies from bedsores" as a standalone cause — bedsores cause septicaemia, which would be the stated cause of death
- "'Exhaustion' is stranger still. Most invalids who die are exhausted, but exhaustion cannot, without qualification, be given as a cause of death."

Wood implies that [[kjt-keer|Dr Keer]] may have been compromised or blackmailed by Marianne into signing an obfuscated certificate [4]. He notes: "The insufficient causes may have been [Keer's] equivalent of a child's crossing its fingers when making a promise it does not intend to keep."

## Murder Allegations

Multiple parties independently concluded that Marianne had hastened Foyster's death:

- **[[trevor-hall]]**: "on the basis of circumstantial evidence... was convinced that she had killed him" [5]
- **A. Robert Swanson** (ex-US law enforcement): "had the distinct impression that Marianne did in fact murder Foyster, and that the memory of this greatly disturbed her" [5]
- **Fisher's sister**: claimed Foyster was poisoned [6]
- **Foyster's relatives**: "not at all happy about the circumstances of his death"; Marianne had deliberately kept them away from him in his final days [6]. Demands for exhumation in the late 1940s were reportedly made [5]
- **Spiritualist séances** (late 1940s): the "shade of Foyster" was said to appear claiming he had been murdered

Wood theorises that Marianne either locked the bedridden Lionel away to die of neglect or actively smothered him [7, 6, 5]. He argues her motive was a desperate need to free herself: she was involved with a 25-year-old American GI, Robert O'Neil, and knew she had to marry him and escape to America before the end of the war separated them [8, 5, 9].

## Paranormal Allegations

The suspicions of murder even carried into the psychical investigations. During a planchette séance at the [[rectory-cottage]] on 8 June 1948, an entity claiming to be the late 'Harry Bull' allegedly foul play in connection with Lionel's death [10, 11]. The entity also claimed that Lionel's ghost, complete with clerical skirts and a rheumatic limp, now walked the churchyard [1, 10, 12].

## Eileen Garrett's Interview

When [[eileen-garrett|Eileen Garrett]] met Marianne in New York (1958), she asked directly:

> "I asked her point blank whether she had killed Foyster. She answered equally candidly that it had often been in her mind, but that he was too cunning for anything like that to happen to him." [13, 14]

Wood reads this as close to an admission: an innocent person, he argues, does not answer a murder question by saying the victim was "too cunning" to be caught.

## Wood's Analysis

Wood is convinced Marianne "did hasten Foyster's end" but allows for ambiguity in motive:

> "I believe that although Marianne helped him on his way, she might have done this at his own request." [5]

His reasoning: Foyster too must have realised that the Fisher "pension plan" had failed, that their old way of life was over, and that if cared for by relatives he would be parted from Marianne. Foyster is therefore a possible participant in his own death, if he asked to be released from a life of pain and exposure.

The pressures on Marianne at this point were extreme: Fisher was near breakdown and his family suspected the truth; [[robert-oneil|O'Neil]]'s unit was likely to be sent to Europe soon; Foyster was incontinent and bedridden; the Suffolk newspapers would shortly report his death and connect it to Borley.

## Defenders of Marianne

Researchers [[iris-owen]] and Pauline Mitchell strongly push back against the accusations of murder and neglect. They argue that Marianne was a devoted wife who did the best she could under terrible circumstances, staying with the bedridden Lionel and nursing him until the very end [15, 16].

## The Doctor and Solicitor Question

Wood raises the possibility that both [[kjt-keer|Dr Keer]] and [[cj-parry|solicitor Parry]] had been compromised by Marianne — possibly through sexual entanglement — making them unable or unwilling to pursue their professional duties:

- Keer signed an insufficient death certificate
- Parry, hired to find Marianne after she abandoned John Fisher, made no progress despite having just helped her prove Foyster's will

Wood cannot prove this.

## Aftermath

Following Foyster's death:

- Suffolk newspapers reported the death and recalled the Borley haunting, triggering exposure
- Fisher's sister contacted the Misses Bull, who confirmed Marianne and Foyster were husband and wife
- Marianne married O'Neil on 11 August 1945 — less than four months after Foyster's death — bigamously [17, 18]
- She obtained probate of Foyster's will in February 1946, receiving £850
- She departed England in August 1946

Decades later, her adopted son Vince O'Neil visited Lionel's grave, talking aloud to the headstone and asking if his mother had truly murdered him, desperate to know the truth [19, 20].
