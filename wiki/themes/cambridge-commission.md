# The Cambridge Commission (1939–1944)

The systematic investigation of the Borley ruins organised by [[ajb-robertson|A.J.B. Robertson]] of St John's College, Cambridge, from Easter 1939 to July 1944. The Commission covered 25 overnight visits by 58 persons — almost all Cambridge undergraduates, nearly all sceptical on arrival. It is the second major organised investigation of the case after [[the-observer-program|Price's Observer Program]], and the first to operate entirely at the ruins post-fire.

## Organisation

Robertson convened the Commission immediately after the fire, after reading Price's *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940). He prepared a comprehensive summary report from each visitor's individual protocols and submitted it to [[harry-price]].

**Structure:**
- 25 separate overnight visits, Easter 1939 – July 1944
- 58 persons total; Robertson personally attended only 8 visits
- Almost entirely Cambridge undergraduates; a small number of older academics
- No payment, no prior coaching on what to expect; most were avowed sceptics

**Significant disadvantages acknowledged by Robertson:**
- Working in open ruins, not an occupied building — no shelter, no heating, no kitchen
- Phenomena were weakening year by year
- Irregular visiting pattern (months between some visits); no continuity of personnel
- No Base Room; the [[blue-room]] was open to the sky

## Key Findings

### Auditory Phenomena

The most common class, and the most evidentially interesting. Robertson distinguished footsteps (traversing sounds, as of someone moving through rooms) from static knockings. Footsteps were heard primarily when investigators were stationary in the sewing-room, separated from the sound by one wall — a regularity he found difficult to attribute to random structural causes.

**Triple-knock responses**: Knockings frequently came 5–10 minutes after a spoken request to "any entity" to manifest. Robertson calculated that three-knock patterns arising from independent random events would have a probability of the order of 10⁻⁷.

### Selected Visit Highlights

| Visit | Date | Finding |
|-------|------|---------|
| 2 | Oct 31, 1941 | Heavy footsteps on pantry stairs (both observers certain); dark outline moving from moonlight into shadow; luminous patch on wall for ~1 second |
| 3 | Dec 20, 1941 | 18 knocks in a regular double-knock pattern × 3 — heard simultaneously by Robertson and Angelbeck; no mechanical explanation found |
| 4 | Feb 27, 1942 | Three regular knocks from front door; rumblings as of furniture moved upstairs; luminous flash seen independently by Grantham (not told of the Visit 2 patch) |
| 9 | Mar 21, 1943 | Five observers simultaneously heard horse-stamping on the road near the summer-house — twice. None of the five knew the [[phantom-coach]] tradition |
| 14 | Jun 19, 1943 | Cold Spot measured at 11°F below surrounding air — confirmed by Grasshof's criterion and the heat-conduction equation |
| 25 | Jul 22–23, 1944 | After a staged hoax ended at 3 a.m., five legitimate investigators established rapping communication with an entity for ~45 minutes. The entity claimed to be a nun, died c.1250, and said it had communicated with [[helen-glanville]] via Planchette |

### The Cold Spot

Temperature readings at Visit 14 showed an 11°F difference between an enclosed thermometer and an adjacent exposed thermometer at the [[blue-room]] Cold Spot location. Robertson and colleague E.D. Low applied Grasshof's criterion and the heat-conduction equation to test whether heat removal at 0.006 cal/cm³/sec could account for the reading — the maths proved consistent, but could not explain the source of the heat removal.

### Robertson's Conclusion

> "There appears to be something at the Rectory which cannot be at all easily explained away."

He noted phenomena were "fragmentary versions" of those reported in Price's 1940 book — consistent with an independently declining haunting, as observers and Price both noted. His full protocols, with graphs, tables, and sketches, were deposited in the Harry Price Library at the University of London.

## Significance

The Commission is the closest the Borley case comes to a controlled, repeating experiment: the same location, a rotating population of sceptics, a written protocol for each visit, and a single summarising investigator. Its limitations — ruins, seasonality, weakening phenomena — are acknowledged. Its modest but persistent findings (22 of 58 persons reported something curious or strange) make it the hardest body of evidence for a simple "nothing happened" sceptical account.

Unlike the [[the-observer-program|Price Observer Program]], the Commission was independently organised and reported to Price from outside, giving its findings greater independence from the case's most contested figure.
