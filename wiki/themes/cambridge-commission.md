# The Cambridge Commission (1939–1944)

The systematic investigation of the Borley ruins organised by A. J. B. Robertson of St John's College, Cambridge, from Easter 1939 to July 1944. The Commission covered 25 overnight visits by 58 persons — almost all Cambridge undergraduates, nearly all sceptical on arrival. It is the second major organised investigation of the case after [[the-observer-program|Price's Observer Program]], and the first to operate entirely at the ruins post-fire.

Led by a young physical chemist (by 1975 an Emeritus Professor of Chemistry), the Commission sought to apply scientific rigour to the haunting [3, 4]. Robertson prepared a comprehensive summary report from each visitor's individual protocols and submitted it to [[harry-price]].

## Organisation

**Structure:**
- 25 separate overnight visits, Easter 1939 – July 1944
- 58 persons total; Robertson personally attended only 8 visits
- Almost entirely Cambridge undergraduates; a small number of older academics
- No payment, no prior coaching on what to expect; most were avowed sceptics
- Equipment included controlled thermometers, trigger objects, and stretched-spring apparatuses designed to detect changes in potential energy [3, 4]

**Significant disadvantages acknowledged by Robertson:**
- Working in open ruins, not an occupied building — no shelter, no heating, no kitchen
- Phenomena were weakening year by year
- Irregular visiting pattern (months between some visits); no continuity of personnel
- No Base Room; the [[blue-room]] was open to the sky

## Key Findings

### Auditory Phenomena

The most common class, and the most evidentially interesting. Robertson distinguished footsteps (traversing sounds, as of someone moving through rooms) from static knockings. Observers regularly reported heavy, measured footsteps padding around the ruins, occasionally tracking across the room directly above them [6, 7]. They also documented thuds, rumblings of heavy furniture moving, whistling, and on one occasion the distinct sound of a horse stamping its hooves on the road outside [4, 8, 9]. Footsteps were heard primarily when investigators were stationary in the sewing-room, separated from the sound by one wall — a regularity Robertson found difficult to attribute to random structural causes.

**Triple-knock responses**: Knockings frequently came 5–10 minutes after a spoken request to "any entity" to manifest. Some knockings appeared to show intelligence; during Visit 3, Robertson and E. N. J. Angelbeck received 18 knocks in a regular double-knock pattern × 3 that seemed to respond to their own tapping signals [10]. Robertson calculated that three-knock patterns arising from independent random events would have a probability of the order of 10⁻⁷.

### Visual Phenomena

On multiple visits, luminous patches of light were seen on the walls (notably in the sewing room) which observers ruled out as being caused by moonlight or flashlights [8, 11]. During Visit 14, a six-foot white shape was seen by a tree [12], and on Visit 22, observers watched a pale, indistinct light appear and disappear in the upstairs bedrooms [13].

### The Cold Spot

Temperature readings at Visit 14 showed an 11°F difference between an enclosed thermometer and an adjacent exposed thermometer at the [[blue-room]] Cold Spot location [12, 14]. Robertson and colleague E. D. Low applied Grasshof's criterion and the heat-conduction equation to test whether heat removal at 0.006 cal/cm³/sec could account for the reading — the maths proved consistent, but could not explain the source of the heat removal. Despite over a thousand temperature readings in total, this was the Commission's only significant thermal anomaly.

### Robertson's Conclusion

> "There appears to be something at the Rectory which cannot be at all easily explained away."

He noted phenomena were "fragmentary versions" of those reported in Price's 1940 book — consistent with an independently declining haunting, as observers and Price both noted. His full protocols, with graphs, tables, and sketches, were deposited in the Harry Price Library at the University of London. About one-third of the 58 participants experienced nothing unusual, another third noted events they thought might not occur normally, and the remaining third described definitely unusual, seemingly paranormal events [1, 5].

## Counter-Arguments and Sceptical Perspectives

The authors of the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] — [[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], and [[trevor-hall]] — largely dismissed the Cambridge Commission's findings as the misinterpretations of inexperienced youths [2, 17, 18]:

- **The practical joke (Visit 25):** The sceptics placed heavy emphasis on the final visit in July 1944. Unbeknownst to the official five-man team, four other Cambridge students arrived secretly to fake phenomena: one draped himself in a sheet to act as an "apparition" on the lawn, while others produced poltergeist knockings. The official observers were initially fooled. The SPR authors argued that if practical jokers could successfully infiltrate the site on this occasion, similar pranks may have gone undetected on other visits [18, 19].
- **Misidentified normal sounds:** The SPR suggested many "paranormal" sounds had mundane origins. They proposed the mysterious "eighteen knocks" were actually just the occupants of the neighbouring cottage operating a noisy hand-pump for water [20, 21].

## Defences of the Commission

- **[[harry-price|Price's defence]]:** Price praised the Commission for its meticulous protocols and for providing independent confirmation of phenomena that previous observers had recorded, despite the severe handicap of investigating a roofless, weather-beaten ruin [22–24].
- **The demolition correlation:** Edward Babbs and Claudine Mathias point out that the phenomena recorded by the Commission systematically grew weaker over the five years, ceasing altogether once the building was totally dismantled — exactly how a genuine haunting tied to a physical structure would be expected to fade [25].
- **Robertson's rebuttal:** At a 1975 Society for Psychical Research meeting where [[kathleen-goldney]] lectured against the Borley case, Robertson — by then an Emeritus Professor of Chemistry — publicly defended his Commission's work. Speaking with "great authority and precision," he rejected Goldney's emphatic dismissals and maintained that at least some of the phenomena reported by his students could not be explained away by normal means [26].

## Significance

The Commission is the closest the Borley case comes to a controlled, repeating experiment: the same location, a rotating population of sceptics, a written protocol for each visit, and a single summarising investigator. Its limitations — ruins, seasonality, weakening phenomena — are acknowledged. Its modest but persistent findings (22 of 58 persons reported something curious or strange) make it the hardest body of evidence for a simple "nothing happened" sceptical account.

Unlike the [[the-observer-program|Price Observer Program]], the Commission was independently organised and reported to Price from outside, giving its findings greater independence from the case's most contested figure.
