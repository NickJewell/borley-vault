# The Great Amherst Mystery

A famous Canadian poltergeist case centred on **Esther Cox** of Amherst, Nova Scotia (1878–79), directly referenced in the Borley story as background to both [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]]'s understanding of poltergeists and [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s theoretical framework.

## The Case

In August 1878, eighteen-year-old **Esther Cox** was living in a small wooden house on Princess Street, Amherst, in the home of her sister Olive Teed and her husband Daniel. On 28 August, a young man named **Bob McNeill** attempted to sexually assault Esther in nearby woods, then fled town and was never seen again.

Fifteen days later, strange phenomena began:
- Esther's hair would stand on end
- Her face would turn blood red and her body would swell inexplicably
- She experienced fits and heard knocking and banging throughout the night
- Objects would fly around the house and chairs would be stacked by unseen forces
- Above her bed, words were scratched deep into the plaster: *"Esther Cox, you are mine to kill"*

A doctor who was called had the bedclothes violently stripped from the bed by an unseen force in his presence. The activity lasted approximately fifteen months before subsiding as suddenly as it had started.

## Hubbell's Book and the Commercial Template

**Walter Hubbell**, an actor and theatrical impresario, arrived to observe Esther and arranged a commercial lecture tour. He then published a book, *The Haunted House* (claiming to be "A True Ghost Story"), which became a bestseller selling over 55,000 copies. Esther Cox died poor in 1912; Hubbell's book has never been out of print.

Price noted that the peaks of the phenomena occurred every twenty-eight days, mirroring the menstrual cycle — a detail he used in his theorising about the role of the psychic focus.

## Relevance to Borley

**Marianne Foyster** first heard the story of the Amherst Mystery while convalescing at Amherst Hospital in [[sackville-new-brunswick|Sackville]], Nova Scotia. She later told [[iris-owen|Iris Owen]] and [[paulene-mitchell|Paulene Mitchell]] that her understanding of what was happening at Borley was informed partly by this case.

In the [[afterword|afterword]] to O'Connor's book, Marianne is quoted as saying that some of the phenomena at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] were inspired by her knowledge of the Amherst case: *"What set out as a bit of fun can surely get one into trouble."*

**Harry Price** also referenced the Amherst case in his theoretical writings, as one of the best-documented poltergeist outbreaks and a parallel to Borley in terms of the role of a young female focus.

The commercial template of Hubbell's book — a sensational first-hand account that sold 55,000 copies and never went out of print — is precisely the model that Price himself attempted to replicate with *The Most Haunted House in England*.

> **Contested:** Esther Cox's sister Olive claimed that Hubbell had dramatised and embellished the events. After fifteen months, the activity subsided and Esther refused to discuss the phenomena, "afraid they would come back."
