# The Great Amherst Mystery

A famous Canadian poltergeist case centred on **Esther Cox** of Amherst, Nova Scotia (1878–79), directly referenced in the Borley story as background to both [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]]'s understanding of poltergeists and [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s theoretical framework. In the Borley sources, the case is primarily deployed by sceptics to argue that **the most violent period of the Borley haunting was a manufactured hoax modelled directly on the Amherst events** [2–5].

## The Case

In August 1878, eighteen-year-old **Esther Cox** was living in a small wooden house on Princess Street, Amherst, in the home of her sister Olive Teed and her husband Daniel. On 28 August, a young man named **Bob McNeill** attempted to sexually assault Esther in nearby woods, then fled town and was never seen again.

Fifteen days later, strange phenomena began:
- Esther's hair would stand on end
- Her face would turn blood red and her body would swell inexplicably — physical manifestations entirely absent from the Borley case, and cited by defenders as a key difference
- She experienced fits and heard knocking and banging throughout the night, with her attacks reportedly arriving on a strict twenty-eight-day cycle mirroring the menstrual cycle [15, 20]
- Objects would fly around the house and chairs would be stacked by unseen forces
- Above her bed, words were scratched deep into the plaster: *"Esther Cox, you are mine to kill"*

A doctor who was called had the bedclothes violently stripped from the bed by an unseen force in his presence. The activity lasted approximately fifteen months before subsiding as suddenly as it had started.

> **Contested:** Esther Cox's sister Olive claimed that Hubbell had dramatised and embellished the events. After fifteen months, the activity subsided and Esther refused to discuss the phenomena, "afraid they would come back."

## Hubbell's Book and the Commercial Template

**Walter Hubbell**, an actor and theatrical impresario, arrived to observe Esther and arranged a commercial lecture tour. He then published a book, *The Haunted House* (claiming to be "A True Ghost Story"), which became a bestseller selling over 55,000 copies [4, 15]. Esther Cox died poor in 1912; Hubbell's book has never been out of print.

[[harry-price]] noted that the peaks of the phenomena occurred every twenty-eight days, mirroring the menstrual cycle — a detail he used in his theorising about the role of the psychic focus.

The commercial template of Hubbell's book — a sensational first-hand account that sold 55,000 copies — is precisely the model that Price himself attempted to replicate with *The Most Haunted House in England*, and that sceptics argue [[lionel-foyster]] had in mind when he began writing *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*.

## Relevance to Borley

[[marianne-foyster]] reportedly first heard the story of the Amherst Mystery while convalescing at Amherst Hospital in [[sackville-new-brunswick|Sackville]], Nova Scotia [9]. She later told [[iris-owen|Iris Owen]] and Pauline Mitchell that her understanding of what was happening at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] was informed partly by this case. In the afterword to O'Connor's book, Marianne is quoted as saying that some of the phenomena at Borley were inspired by her knowledge of the Amherst case: *"What set out as a bit of fun can surely get one into trouble."*

[[harry-price]] also referenced the Amherst case in his theoretical writings, as one of the best-documented poltergeist outbreaks and a parallel to Borley in terms of the role of a young female focus.

## The Sceptical Argument: A Blueprint for a Hoax

The authors of the SPR *Borley Report* ([[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]]), alongside sceptical author [[robert-wood]], made specific claims that [[lionel-foyster]] and [[marianne-foyster]] used the Great Amherst Mystery as a blueprint for fabricating the phenomena at Borley [2–6].

- **Geographical proximity**: Before arriving at Borley, the Foysters lived in [[sackville-new-brunswick|Sackville, New Brunswick]], situated close to Amherst [4, 7, 8]. The historian Andrew Clarke claims that Marianne stayed in Amherst in 1927, where she heard the story of Esther Cox, which subsequently inspired her to fake the [[wall-writings]] [9]
- **Striking phenomenological similarities**: The SPR authors documented that in both cases manifestations lasted around fourteen to fifteen months; a female resident was the focus of the activity and claimed to see apparitions invisible to others; both women suffered physical injuries; and both locations experienced mysterious fires, thrown objects, upset furniture, pulled bedclothes, and mysterious wall writings [3, 10–14]
- **The "Teed" pseudonym**: When [[lionel-foyster]] wrote his novelised account of the Borley hauntings, *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*, he chose the pseudonym "Teed" — the surname of Esther Cox's brother-in-law — for one of the characters [2, 4, 6]
- **Financial motive**: Facing losses from the Wall Street Crash and retirement due to ill health, [[lionel-foyster]] allegedly orchestrated a copycat haunting to write his own profitable bestseller. During interviews with [[iris-owen|Iris Owen]] and Pauline Mitchell in 1978, [[marianne-foyster]] reportedly confirmed that her husband knew of Hubbell's financial success and conceived the idea of writing a similar book about Borley [7, 18]

## Counter-Arguments: Differences Between the Cases

Defenders of the Borley hauntings counter the SPR's assertions by arguing that the similarities have been overstated and that significant differences exist [15, 20]:

- **Differences in phenomena**: The Amherst haunting featured phenomena completely absent at Borley — Esther Cox would "visibly swell" in the presence of observers, her attacks came on a strict twenty-eight-day cycle, and heavy pounding was heard on the roof
- **Historical and geographic discrepancies**: Critics of the SPR report note that Amherst and Sackville are roughly ten miles apart, not five as the SPR claimed [8]. The Amherst Mystery occurred more than thirty years before [[lionel-foyster]] ever set foot in Canada, and neither he nor [[marianne-foyster]] was ever known to publicly refer to the case [21]
- **The pseudonym explanation**: The use of the name "Teed" is dismissed by defenders as coincidental — it is a fairly common surname in Canada, and Foyster used many other highly unusual pseudonyms (such as Greycastle, Tuke, Lawless, and Footman) that had absolutely no connection to the Amherst case [8]
