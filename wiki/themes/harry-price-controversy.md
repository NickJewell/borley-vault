# The Harry Price Controversy

Was [[harry-price|Harry Price]] a serious investigator or a fraud? This is the meta-question beneath the entire Borley case. The Borley sources depict Price as either a cynical, fraudulent showman who manufactured evidence for financial gain, or a dedicated, albeit flawed, psychical investigator who was the victim of a malicious posthumous witch-hunt [1, 2].

## The Charges

The 1956 SPR report (*The Haunting of Borley Rectory*, [[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], [[trevor-hall|Hall]]) concluded that Price erected a "house of cards" based on lies, and charged him with willful deception and fraud in virtually every aspect of his involvement [4, 5]. Specific accusations:

- Actively faking phenomena (throwing stones, breaking windows)
- Manipulating and editing the evidence to tell a better story
- Planting the bones found during the 1943 cellar excavation
- Misrepresenting the "flying brick" as a poltergeist projectile
- Ignoring null evidence (Henry Lawton's blank August 1933 residency)
- Inviting amateurs to the tenancy investigation specifically to prevent being caught

### The Sutton Accusation (The Central Charge)

*Daily Mail* reporter [[charles-sutton|Charles Sutton]] published a brief anecdote in the *Inky Way Annual* (Christmas 1948) stating that during a July 1929 visit to Borley with Price, he seized Price by the hand and "found his pockets full of bricks and pebbles" [6–9].

O'Connor's reconstruction of the incident (hbrs-ch17): Sutton had been suspicious of Price throughout the evening — Price had thrown a stone at a window pane as they arrived ("swishing noise" just before the glass broke), then stones appeared to roll down stairs ahead of Price. Sutton planned to catch him. While in the Blue Room, he sensed Price's arm swing, heard a stone roll, and in the darkness "grasped Price by the hand" then "plunged his hands into Price's coat pockets: they were full of stones and pebbles." Price asked: "What are you going to do? What are you going to do?" Sutton told Price he would phone the *Daily Mail*. Price: "You are very lucky."

The *Daily Mail* lawyer killed the story: only one witness ([[lucie-kaye|Lucie Kaye]] was present but was Price's employee and said nothing).

**[[lucie-kaye|Lucie Kaye's]] counter-testimony** (interviewed by [[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]] and [[lord-charles-hope|Lord Charles Hope]]): "Well, really, you know, I just don't remember the occasion at all... I vehemently believe Sutton's allegation to be 'a falsehood from beginning to end.'" Lord Charles accepted her manner was convincing. Her 1939 letter to Price mentions Sutton without any hint of a dramatic confrontation ever having occurred [35, 36].

### Other Credible Fraud Evidence

- **The Battersea poltergeist (1928)**: Two Robinson family members said "things always seemed to happen" when Price was present; "there was some power about Mr Price that attracted things"
- **The fake Roman silver ingot (1910)**: Price's career as "Sussex archaeologist" collapsed when a fake he had "found" was identified as a copy of a Tower of London piece. Established a pattern of fabrication
- **The "flying brick" (1944)**: [[cynthia-thompson|Cynthia Ledsham]], photographer David Scherman, and Price were all present; they all knew a workman was throwing demolition bricks in the frame. Price wrote it up as "the first photograph ever taken of a poltergeist projectile in flight" [10–14]
- **[[sidney-glanville]] not invited to 1943 excavation**: Price's most loyal collaborator and nearest neighbour was absent from the excavation that conveniently found bones near the surface. The Report implies the bones were planted [15–17]
- **The "altered" observer report**: Glanville told Price an observer account was impossible given the Rectory's floor plan; Price: "No. It makes a chapter. I will alter it"
- **Faked noises and wall-writings**: [[major-douglas-home|Major Henry Douglas-Home]] suspected Price of faking a ghostly rustling noise by manipulating a roll of cellophane hidden in his pocket or the stove flue [23, 24], and claimed new [[wall-writings]] only appeared when Price was positioned at the rear of the investigating party [25, 26]
- **The [[blue-book-of-instructions|Blue Book]] suggestion**: Sceptics argued that Price's manual for his amateur observers deliberately planted suggestions of what they should expect to see and hear (bells, footsteps, the nun), thereby ensuring his inexperienced volunteers would interpret natural sounds as supernatural [27–30]

## The Defence

[[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] and [[paul-tabori|Paul Tabori]], and more rigorously [[robert-hastings|R.J. Hastings]] in his 1969 counter-report:

1. Price's character flaws (vanity, self-promotion, rivalry) are not the same as systematic fabrication
2. The accusations emerged posthumously (nine months after his death); the full SPR report appeared eight years later — Price could not respond
3. Price openly deposited his archive at the University of London during his lifetime — not the act of a man who thought it incriminating [34]
4. [[lucie-kaye|Lucie Kaye]] knew him for over a decade in close collaboration and maintained his integrity absolutely, calling him a man of "unimpeachable integrity" [35, 36]
5. [[sidney-glanville]], his most trusted associate, never doubted his honesty
6. [[robert-hastings]] found the Report was "a prosecutor's case and no attempt had been made to find arguments in Price's defence"

## The Bias Problem

O'Connor: the Report was shaped by William Salter's personal vendetta against Price (the rivalry dated from 1929) and [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]]'s "near-fanatical hatred of Price." [[kathleen-goldney|Mollie Goldney]] was the most objective author but was guided by Salter. The society also wanted to protect its reputation for having been excluded from Price's tenancy investigation.

## The Most Balanced Assessment

Many contemporaries and modern researchers adopt a nuanced perspective, acknowledging Price's flaws without condemning him as a complete fraud. Researchers like [[dr-vernon-harrison|Dr Vernon Harrison]] and Alan Wesencraft note that Price was not a trained scientist but an enthusiastic entrepreneur and publicist — a "psychic journalist" who viewed his Borley books as "scientific journalism" designed to be readable and thrilling, leading to exaggerations and the suppression of mundane details [57, 58]. The psychical researcher Anita Gregory described Price as "divided against himself" — passionately devoted to psychical research but equally devoted to his own self-aggrandizement [59].

O'Connor's conclusion: Price arrived at a house that already had a genuine if low-level haunting and "helped" the phenomena by throwing objects — "But when the Bull sisters attended the séance that night in the Blue Room, Price was soon compelled by the potency — and the potential — of the story." He may have started as a fraud but became genuinely gripped by the case; his books, whatever their distortions, contain a real ghost story shaped by real experiences.

[[charles-sutton|Charles Sutton]] (Price's adversary) admitted this: "There had clearly been activity there — well before Price had arrived on the scene — that should be properly investigated. Price's tricks occluded anything of real value."

Many researchers (such as [[guy-lyon-playfair|Guy Lyon Playfair]] and [[peter-underwood]]) conclude that while Price was undoubtedly an egotist who may have embellished reports, he genuinely believed Borley was haunted, and the core phenomena existed long before he arrived and persisted long after he left [60–64].

## Clarke's Assessment (slbr-01, slbr-02, slbr-12, slbr-15)

[[andrew-clarke|Andrew Clarke]] offers the most nuanced local assessment: Price "traversed the entire spectrum of emotions and beliefs, from complete scepticism to uncritical belief in the paranormal." Clarke identifies a trajectory: the mischievous conjuring-trick sceptic of 1929–1931 → the increasingly credulous investigator of the Glanville period → the "sombre and worried old man of declining health" who wrote the second book. Glanville and Phythian-Adams's theory melted Price's former scepticism, and he may have been genuinely ashamed of having changed his mind.

Clarke also notes that Price kept his private conclusions (phenomena faked) entirely separate from his public writing — a pattern that, charitably, may reflect journalistic pragmatism (*"the public preferred Bunk to Debunk"*) rather than deliberate fraud.

## The Hastings Counter-Report (1969)

[[robert-hastings|R.J. Hastings]]'s *An Examination of the 'Borley Report'* (SPR Proceedings Vol. 55, 1969) is the systematic scholarly rebuttal, funded by the SPR's own Research Advisory Committee. Hastings worked from the same HPL archive as the SPR authors and commissioned Alan Gauld to re-interview Sutton in 1966 — revealing what HBR had concealed.

**On the Sutton accusation:**
- Sutton told Gauld he did *not see* Price throw any stones; did not take stones out of the pockets (only felt them); volunteered that he might have been "retrospectively influenced in the direction of pebbles by his subsequent discovery of pebbles in Price's pocket"
- Sutton admitted: "a skilful defence counsel would, on these grounds and on grounds of the various lacunae in his memory, demolish his testimony without trouble"
- Price's angina (since 1928; killed him 1948) may explain his silence during the confrontation — cardiac distress rather than guilt [37, 38]. Price and Sutton also remained perfectly friendly for years afterward [39, 40]

**On the medals:**
- The substitution thesis requires Price to have manufactured evidence to support a theory (the French nun legend) he publicly and repeatedly stated he *disbelieved* — "I do not believe in spirits, and I do not believe in the legend" (*The Times*, April 1939; italicized "not" on MHH p.185) [47–49]
- All three medals found in Price's files after death — incriminating evidence left in accessible academic archives
- The supposed "French" description of the Confirmation medal was never written by Price at all — it was fabricated by HBR's authors
- Discrepancies in the medals were attributed to Price's careless writing from memory rather than malice [50, 51]

**On the bone fragments:**
- Price physically could not dig (angina since 1928; painful walking by 1940s) [41, 42]
- Both Henning and [[sidney-glanville]] were present for excavation and subsequent digging; both noticed nothing suspicious
- Electrical wiring found by demolition men post-dated Glanville's last visit — irrelevant to excavation
- Furthermore, Price arranged a Christian burial for the bones — an act of sacrilege a devout churchgoer like Price would be highly unlikely to commit for a hoax [46]
- Wartime restrictions, lack of transport, and the presence of other people made secretly planting bones in heavy clay practically impossible [43–45]

**On Douglas-Home's cellophane theory:**
- Douglas-Home himself conceded in 1953: "I have no idea how the sound was produced" and "I must confess I have no idea how it was done"
- Writing twelve years after the events, from memory, after reading Sutton's *Inky Way Annual* article
- Hastings showed the tour where Douglas-Home supposedly kept Price in his flashlight beam occurred at 5:15 AM in July — which was broad daylight, rendering the flashlight accusation absurd [55, 56]

**On the flying brick:**
- Price never explicitly claimed the flying brick was a paranormal event in his book, merely posing the question as a curiosity [52, 53]
- Photographic experts noted that the workman visible in the photo was facing the wrong way to have thrown the brick to where it appeared [54]

**On Price's evolution re. Marianne:**
- Price was repeatedly *barred* from visiting Borley by the Foysters — HBR hid this
- His change of view was driven by receiving Lady Whitehouse's testimony (1938) and Dom Richard Whitehouse's account (August 1939) — previously missing first-hand independent corroboration
- His last statement (March 1948): Marianne may have been "the instrument through which the scripts appeared — as a secondary personality" — not a claim she was genuine, but that poltergeist agency could operate through her unconsciously

**Dom Richard Whitehouse's own rebuttal** (letter to [[peter-underwood]], August 1956; Hastings Appendix C):
- His MHH chapter XV was drawn from *contemporary notes made in June 1931*, not from Marianne's later statements
- The stiletto landed on his lap while he was *looking at Marianne* who "had not moved hand or foot" — he "shall never go back on what I said"
- HBR made "most significant suppression" of all reference to his notes
- His recovery from nervous breakdown was in 1924–25; by 1931 he was a deacon and drafted his chapter without difficulty

**Hastings's overall finding:** "The allegations made against him in HBR were very grossly exaggerated... it can hardly be denied that the material I have brought forward in Price's defence ought — and I use this word in its strongest possible sense — to have been made public at the time the allegations were made."

**Miss Kaye's assessment** (to [[lord-charles-hope|Lord Charles Hope]], 1949): "I know he told little fibs now and again, but he did not fake phenomena. I feel so very sure."
