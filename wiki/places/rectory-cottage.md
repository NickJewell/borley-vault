# Rectory Cottage

The only surviving structure of the Borley Rectory complex. Located immediately adjacent to the former Rectory on its western side. Predates Henry Bull's 1863 Rectory by at least fifty years — a tithe map of 1800 shows the building (then serving as a coach house and stables) in its present position, alongside the earlier Herringham Rectory.

## Physical Description

A modest brick structure whose façade has evolved over the centuries — probably originally plain stucco, later faced in red brick to match the Bull Rectory's style. Over the years various extensions and modifications have changed its appearance significantly. Today (2009) it is a private residence.

## Phenomena at the Cottage

The Rectory Cottage has its own paranormal history distinct from the main Rectory:

- **Cooper period (1916–1920):** Padding dog sounds almost nightly; crashing sound from kitchen; black running shape in bedroom; nun seen crossing courtyard; phantom coach
- **Turner period (1947–1950):** Heavy furniture-shifting sounds from upstairs; laughter and chattering voices in the garden (James Turner); Cathy Turner's deck-chair footsteps circling her; footsteps heard in car by four witnesses (August 1949); phantom cat (28 July, 28 August 1949); crockery-smashing sound heard by two workmen; heavy footsteps and mutterings heard by [[peter-underwood]] and Laurence Gafford (4–5 March 1950)
- **Bacon period (1951–1972):** Terry Bacon and Mr Williams reported phantom nun sightings; Mr Williams saw "whirling circles of light"; strange smells; panting dog on Nun's Walk; planchette sessions by Len Sewell

## The Face in the Wall

In August 1950, [[james-turner|James Turner]] discovered a carved stone face built into the stable wall at eye level — "clearly defaced though very old," measuring 7½ inches across and 5½ inches high. Ethel Bull remembered it, thinking someone had found it "lying around" and built it into the wall. After the face was removed the Bacon family took charge of it.
