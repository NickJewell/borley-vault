# Rectory Cottage

The only surviving structure of the Borley Rectory complex, also known in later years as **Priory Cottage**. Located immediately adjacent to the former Rectory on its western side. Predates [[henry-bull|Henry Bull]]'s 1863 Rectory by at least fifty years — tithe maps of 1800 and 1841 show the building (then serving as a coach house and stables with servant living quarters above) in its present position, alongside the earlier [[herringham-rectory|Herringham Rectory]].

## Physical Description

A modest brick structure whose façade has evolved over the centuries — originally plain stucco, it was likely faced in red brick to match the Bull Rectory's style. Internally, the ground floor retained wheel ruts in the brickwork of what later became the kitchen and dining room, and the building contained four upper rooms. By the late 1940s it was modified into a six-room cottage with a new rear porch and a bay window facing the road; upstairs doors salvaged from the demolished Rectory were also installed [4, 6, 7]. Later owners added a large garden-side extension, double glazing, and a blockhouse-type dormer on the upper floor [8]. Today it is a private residence.

## Phenomena at the Cottage

The Rectory Cottage has its own paranormal history distinct from the main Rectory.

**Cooper period (1916–1920):** Padding dog sounds almost nightly in an adjoining room; a terrifying crash sounding as though all kitchen crockery had been smashed (nothing disturbed); a black running shape in the bedroom; [[the-nun|nun]] seen crossing courtyard; [[phantom-coach|phantom coach]] witnessed from the rear window of the cottage. (When investigators interviewed [[edward-cooper|Edward Cooper]] in 1954, he corrected [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s published account, which had described the sighting from the *front* window; Cooper stated Price had the story muddled [13, 40].)

**Mitchell/Pearless period (early 1930s):** Mrs Mitchell reportedly saw an apparition of [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] through the landing window. [[francois-darles|Frank Pearless]] (also known as François d'Arles), a subsequent tenant, was intimately involved with [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] [14, 16, 17].

**Arbon period (1936–1938):** Unlike their predecessors and successors, the Arbons experienced no paranormal phenomena during their stay [14, 19].

**Turner period (1947–1950):** Heavy furniture-shifting sounds from upstairs; a tremendous crash of breaking crockery in the kitchen (nothing disturbed); pistol-shot noises; laughter and chattering voices in the garden (James Turner); Cathy Turner's deck-chair footsteps circling her; footsteps heard in a car by four witnesses (August 1949); [[phantom-cat|phantom cat]] (28 July, 28 August 1949); heavy footsteps and mutterings heard by [[peter-underwood]] and Laurence Gafford (4–5 March 1950) [7, 26–30].

**Bacon period (1951–1972):** Terry Bacon and Mr Williams reported phantom nun sightings on three separate occasions; José Bacon saw the lower half of a figure in a long black skirt striding in a masculine fashion past the stairs window [38]; Mr Williams saw "whirling circles of light"; strange smells; a panting dog on [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]]; planchette sessions by Len Sewell [31–38].

**Later years:** A bizarre rumour circulated that during the late 1970s, the building was used as a studio for making pornographic films [39].

## The Face in the Wall

In August 1950, [[james-turner|James Turner]] discovered a carved stone face built into the stable wall at eye level — "clearly defaced though very old," measuring 7½ inches across and 5½ inches high. [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]] remembered it, thinking someone had found it "lying around" and built it into the wall. After the face was removed the Bacon family took charge of it.

## Sceptical Explanations

The cottage features heavily in sceptical attempts to debunk the Rectory hauntings. Investigators (including Major Douglas-Home and the authors of the SPR *Borley Report*) pointed out that the enclosed Rectory courtyard acted as a massive amplifier. The Arbons' mundane footsteps across the yard, or the sounds of Mr Arbon working the loud hand-pump late at night, echoed directly inside the empty Rectory — causing many observers to falsely log "ghostly footsteps," thumps, and even voices inside the main house [41–45]. Similarly, Mr Arbon had a habit of leaving his oil lantern burning in the courtyard while he pumped water; sceptics note that the reflection of this lantern on the Rectory windows likely accounted for several reports of "unexplained lights" in the empty building [41, 46].

The Turners' report of a [[phantom-cat|phantom cat]] squealing from the first floor of the cottage has been rationalised by noting that the building sits only six feet from the road: an acoustic quirk could easily have made the sound of a real stray cat or fox outside seem as though it originated from an upstairs room [30].
