# Liston Rectory (Old Rectory, Liston)

A large Georgian house in the hamlet of Liston, approximately one mile from [[borley-church]]. It served as the rectory for the combined Borley-cum-Liston living from 1936 onwards, per the London Gazette order of 1928 which specified that the incumbent should reside at Liston Rectory rather than the much larger and less habitable [[borley-rectory]]. The building features a driveway, garage, outbuildings, and grounds containing large mature trees. (Note: a former Liston incumbent, the Revd Stewart Travers Fisher, had previously built a "New Rectory" — later named "Highfield" — for his own family; the ecclesiastical scheme nonetheless stipulated that the official parsonage must be the Old Rectory.)

## Occupants

The Rev [[rev-ac-henning|Alfred Clifford Henning]] and his wife Annette, together with their sons, moved in at the end of 1936 after the parishes were formally united. According to Henning's own booklet *Haunted Borley*, the property was chosen because it was "well-maintained and had electric light and generally better facilities" than [[borley-rectory]], which was too large, in poor condition, and lacked electricity [8].

> **Contradiction:** [[claudine-mathias|Claudine Mathias]] explicitly disputed this assessment, stating that Henning's claim of an electricity supply was "not really very accurate" [4]. She clarified that the rectory was not connected to the national grid; instead, erratic power came from an old generator housed in an outbuilding, forcing her family to rely primarily on oil lamps [4]. There was likewise no mains water supply — water had to be laboriously pumped from a well up to tanks in the attic [4].

[[harry-price|Harry Price]] was a frequent guest at Liston Rectory, using the house for meetings and meals during his Borley investigations [10, 11]. Various other observers also stayed there, including Dr and Mrs Martin, Mark Kerr-Pearse, and the Revd John C. Dening [12–15].

After Henning's death in January 1955, the Rev [[rev-lanfranc-mathias|Edward Lanfranc Morgan Mathias]] and [[claudine-mathias|Claudine]] moved in on 28 July 1955 and remained until 1967. Claudine raised three children there and describes it as a busy but happy home. Part of Lanfranc's stipend consisted of unpaid rents from glebe land — a recurring irritation.

## Key Events

**The tenancy agreement (May 1937):** On 19 May 1937, [[harry-price|Harry Price]] met the Revd Henning for lunch at Liston Rectory, where they negotiated and finalised the terms for Price to lease [[borley-rectory]] for his year-long investigation [10].

**The WWII bombing (c. 1941):** During a daylight air raid, as Henning was steering his car up the rectory's driveway, a stick of bombs bracketed the house and grounds [3]. The blast nearly blew Henning out of his car, uprooted several large trees, and damaged the garage and windows, but caused no serious casualties [3].

**Post-excavation debriefing (August 1943):** After unearthing human bone fragments in the [[borley-rectory]] cellars on 17 August 1943, [[harry-price|Harry Price]], his note-taker Ethel English, and the Hennings returned to Liston Rectory for dinner to discuss their findings [11, 19].

**The "White Witch" cure:** While living at the Old Rectory, [[claudine-mathias|Claudine Mathias]] suffered from stubborn warts on her hands [20]. An elderly villager named Mr Ling called at the rectory door, noticed the warts, and within weeks they mysteriously withered and vanished; a local doctor subsequently identified Mr Ling matter-of-factly as a "white witch" [20].

## Significance

The bones found under [[borley-rectory]] in August 1943 were interred by Henning in **Liston churchyard on 29 May 1945** — buried here rather than at Borley partly because of local objections to re-interment at the rectory site.

[[liston-hall|Liston Hall]] (a separate building in Liston, now largely demolished) was used during WWII as a NAAFI station. Dorothy Lane was billeted there in summer 1944 and experienced the bedside apparition of a figure in grey robes. This demonstrates that the [[the-nun|Nun]] phenomenon reportedly extended throughout the Liston-Borley district, not only the rectory grounds.
