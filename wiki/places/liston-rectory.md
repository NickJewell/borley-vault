# Liston Rectory (Old Rectory, Liston)

A large Georgian house in the hamlet of Liston, approximately one mile from [[borley-church]]. It served as the rectory for the combined Borley-cum-Liston living from 1936 onwards, per the London Gazette order of 1928 which specified that the incumbent should reside at Liston Rectory rather than the much larger and less habitable [[borley-rectory]].

## Occupants

The Rev [[rev-ac-henning|Alfred Clifford Henning]] and family moved in at the end of 1936 after the parishes were formally united. Henning chose Liston Rectory over Borley Rectory because the latter was too large, in poor condition, and lacked electricity — Liston had better facilities and electric light (though in practice this came from an old generator in an outbuilding, not the national grid).

After Henning's death (January 1955), the Rev [[rev-lanfranc-mathias|Edward Lanfranc Morgan Mathias]] and [[claudine-mathias|Claudine]] moved in on 28 July 1955 and remained until 1967. Claudine raised three children there and describes it as a busy but happy home. The electricity came from a generator; water was pumped from a well to attic tanks. Part of Lanfranc's stipend consisted of unpaid rents from glebe land — a recurring irritation.

## Significance

Liston Hall (a separate building in Liston, now largely demolished) was used during WWII as a NAAFI station. [[dorothy-lane|Dorothy Lane]] was billeted there in summer 1944 and experienced the bedside apparition of a figure in grey robes. This shows the [[the-nun|Nun]] phenomenon extended throughout the Liston-Borley district, not only the rectory grounds.

The bones found under [[borley-rectory]] in August 1943 were interred by Henning in **Liston churchyard on 29 May 1945** — buried here rather than at Borley partly because of local objections to re-interment at the rectory site.
