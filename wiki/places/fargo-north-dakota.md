# Fargo, North Dakota

A city on the North Dakota–Minnesota border, at the railroad crossing of the Red River, approximately 90 miles east of [[jamestown-north-dakota|Jamestown]] and described as a "much larger place." [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] moved here in **autumn 1958** after [[robert-swanson|Robert Swanson's]] investigation at Jamestown unsettled her, fearing her scandalous past in England would be exposed.

## Marianne at Fargo (1958–1963)

In Fargo, Marianne claimed to have obtained a job as a **"Special Instructor"** at **North Dakota Agricultural College**. Wood was unable to discover from the institution what her exact duties were, and by the time of writing no one there remembered her — the claim was entirely unverifiable. By 1961, she was employed as a case worker for the **Lutheran Welfare Society**, organising the adoption of babies for "unwedded mothers." Despite her turbulent past at Borley, she was reportedly very good at this work and was remembered by colleagues with great affection. A local resident later recalled seeing her "propping up the VFW bar" and looking like she was "having a lot of fun."

To maintain her privacy, Marianne moved house every year. Fargo street directories document her addresses:

- **1959**: 1120 South 13th Street, listed with son Vincent aged 13
- **1960**: 1325 South 8th Street
- **1961**: 1126 4th Avenue South — now listed as the **widow of Robert O'Neil**

## The "Widow" Deception

> **Contradiction:** The 1961 directory entry listing Marianne as the "widow of Robert O'Neil" was entirely false. She had divorced [[robert-oneil|O'Neil]] in 1959, but because he still lived with her and was constantly in debt due to his drinking and refusal to work — "a hopeless case, unable to support himself and disowned by his family in Hokah" — she adopted the title of "widow" to protect them both from local creditors.

## Departure

She left Fargo for good in 1963, moving to [[la-crosse-wisconsin|La Crosse, Wisconsin]], where she remained for the rest of her life. Wood notes that "no one to whom I spoke had the faintest idea that when they knew her she was already in her late fifties" — her consistent habit of appearing significantly younger than her real age persisted into her American years.
