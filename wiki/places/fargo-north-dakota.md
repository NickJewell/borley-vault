# Fargo, North Dakota

A city on the North Dakota–Minnesota border, at the railroad crossing of the Red River, approximately 90 miles east of [[jamestown-north-dakota|Jamestown]]. [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] moved here in **autumn 1958** after [[robert-swanson|Swanson]]'s investigation at Jamestown unsettled her, and she lived here until 1963.

## Marianne at Fargo

In Fargo, Marianne worked as a **"Special Instructor"** at **North Dakota Agricultural College**. Wood was unable to discover from the institution what her exact duties were, and by the time of writing no one there remembered her.

Street directories of Fargo document her addresses:
- **1959**: 1120 South 13th Street, listed with son Vincent aged 13
- **1960**: 1325 South 8th Street
- **1961**: 1126 4th Avenue South — now listed as the **widow of Robert O'Neil**

The "widow" entry is inaccurate: she divorced O'Neil in 1959 but continued to allow him to live with her because "he was such a hopeless case, unable to support himself and disowned by his family in Hokah." Describing herself as a widow protected both of them from creditors.

## Departure

She left Fargo for good in 1963, moving to [[la-crosse-wisconsin|La Crosse, Wisconsin]], where she remained for the rest of her life. Wood notes that "no one to whom I spoke had the faintest idea that when they knew her she was already in her late fifties" — her consistent habit of appearing significantly younger than her real age persisted into her American years.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

Based on the Borley sources, **Fargo, North Dakota** features in the concluding chapters of the Borley Rectory saga as one of the American cities where Marianne Foyster (the former wife of the Borley rector) lived while attempting to escape her past and the pursuit of psychical researchers. 

Here are the concrete details recorded about Fargo in the sources, formatted for a reference article:

### **Physical Description and Location**
*   **Location:** Fargo is located on the North Dakota-Minnesota border, at the point where the railroad crosses the Red River [1].
*   **Size:** It is situated 90 miles east of Jamestown, North Dakota, and is described as a "much larger place" than Jamestown [1, 2].

### **Associated Persons**
*   **Marianne Foyster (living as Marianne O'Neil):** The former occupant of Borley Rectory who relocated to the American Midwest after the Second World War [2, 3]. 
*   **Vincent O'Neil:** Marianne's adopted son, who lived with her in Fargo and was 13 years old in 1959 [4].
*   **Robert O'Neil:** Marianne's American GI husband. Although she officially divorced him in 1959, she allowed him to live with her in Fargo because he was a "hopeless case," disowned by his family and unable to support himself [5].

### **Key Events (1958–1963)**
*   **Relocation (Autumn 1958):** Following a tense 1958 interview in Jamestown with A. Robert Swanson (a private detective hired by psychical researchers), Marianne felt uneasy and feared her scandalous past in England would be exposed [2]. To keep investigators at a distance, she moved to Fargo in the autumn of 1958 [2].
*   **Employment at the Agricultural College (1958):** Upon arriving in Fargo, Marianne claimed to have obtained a job as a "Special Instructor" at the North Dakota Agricultural College [2, 3]. 
*   **Frequent Moves (1959–1961):** To maintain her privacy, Marianne moved house every year [4]. Fargo street directories list her residing at **1120 South 13th Street** in 1959, **1325 South 8th Street** in 1960, and **1126 4th Avenue South** in 1961 [4]. 
*   **Employment as a Social Worker (1961):** By 1961, Marianne was employed as a case worker for the Lutheran Welfare Society [3, 4]. Her duties involved organizing the adoption of babies for "unwedded mothers" [4]. Despite her turbulent past at Borley, she was reportedly very good at this job and was remembered by her colleagues with great affection, with absolutely no hint of scandals or poltergeists [4].
*   **Social Life:** A local resident later recalled seeing Marianne in Fargo "propping up the VFW bar" and looking like she was "having a lot of fun" [5].
*   **Departure (1963):** Marianne moved again in 1962 and 1963, eventually leaving Fargo for good to settle in La Crosse, Wisconsin [6].

### **Contradictions and Discrepancies**
*   **The "Widow" Deception:** In the 1961 Fargo street directory, Marianne is explicitly listed as the "widow of Robert O'Neil" [4, 5]. However, this was entirely false [5]. She had divorced him in 1959, but because he still lived with her and was constantly in debt due to his drinking and refusal to work, she adopted the title of "widow" to provide them both with some protection against local credit agencies [5].
*   **The Agricultural College Position:** While Marianne claimed to have worked as a "Special Instructor" at the North Dakota Agricultural College, a later investigator visiting Fargo noted a complete lack of corroborating evidence [2]. The investigator found that absolutely no one at the institution remembered her, and he was completely unable to determine what her alleged duties there actually were [2].
