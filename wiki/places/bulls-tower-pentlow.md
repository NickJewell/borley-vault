# Bull's Tower / Bull's Folly, Pentlow

A large tower in the rectory garden at Pentlow, built in 1858 by the [[henry-bull|Rev Edward Bull]] (the brother of [[henry-bull|Rev Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] who built [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]]). Edward was the rector of Pentlow, and the tower stands as a memorial to his parents, though local legends attribute other purposes to its construction.

## Description

When viewed from close quarters the tower displays fine brickwork and a crenellated top. From a distance of a mile or more it looks, in the authors' description, "rather like a chimney poking out of the middle of a wood." There is a stairway inside but it is no longer safe to use.

## Local Legends

Two alternative explanations circulate locally for the tower's construction:
1. **The sea theory:** Edward Bull wanted to be able to see the sea from the top of his tower.
2. **The labour-watching theory:** He wanted to watch the farm labourers working in his fields and take note of those not working hard enough. The authors note this is perhaps not entirely improbable, as Edward Bull had a reputation as a tyrant — local tradition holds that when he rode through the village in his trap, those who failed to adopt a sufficiently grovelling posture risked feeling his whip.

## Edward Bull's Character

Edward Bull is described as a somewhat eccentric and domineering figure. On one occasion he was one of the guns in a local shoot; finding nothing else to shoot at, he shot a passing pig. Another version holds that the pig bit the rector first ("there is an element of divine retribution in it," the authors remark).

## Context

The Bull family of Pentlow provided several consecutive rectors of the village: two Johns, then Edward, then Felix. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull (the Borley rector) was Edward's brother; together the two siblings were rectors concurrently in these two small and nearby rural parishes, though Felix outlived Henry by three years.
