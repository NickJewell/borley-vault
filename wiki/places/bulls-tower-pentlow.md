# Bull's Tower / Bull's Folly, Pentlow

A large tower in the rectory garden at Pentlow, built in 1858 by the [[henry-bull|Rev Edward Bull]] (the brother of [[henry-bull|Rev Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] who built [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]]). Edward was the rector of Pentlow, and the tower stands as a memorial to his parents, though local legends attribute other purposes to its construction.

## Description

When viewed from close quarters the tower displays fine brickwork and a crenellated top. From a distance of a mile or more it looks, in the authors' description, "rather like a chimney poking out of the middle of a wood." There is a stairway inside but it is no longer safe to use.

## Local Legends

Two alternative explanations circulate locally for the tower's construction:
1. **The sea theory:** Edward Bull wanted to be able to see the sea from the top of his tower.
2. **The labour-watching theory:** He wanted to watch the farm labourers working in his fields and take note of those not working hard enough. The authors note this is perhaps not entirely improbable, as Edward Bull had a reputation as a tyrant — local tradition holds that when he rode through the village in his trap, those who failed to adopt a sufficiently grovelling posture risked feeling his whip.

## Edward Bull's Character

Edward Bull is described as a somewhat eccentric and domineering figure. On one occasion he was one of the guns in a local shoot; finding nothing else to shoot at, he shot a passing pig. Another version holds that the pig bit the rector first ("there is an element of divine retribution in it," the authors remark).

## Context

The Bull family of Pentlow provided several consecutive rectors of the village: two Johns, then Edward, then Felix. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull (the Borley rector) was Edward's brother; together the two siblings were rectors concurrently in these two small and nearby rural parishes, though Felix outlived Henry by three years.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Bull’s Tower** (also known widely as **Bull’s Folly** or **Pentlow Tower**) is an eccentric and imposing architectural folly situated in the garden of the former Pentlow Rectory, a few miles from Borley [1, 2]. 

Based on the Borley sources, here are the concrete details suitable for a reference article:

### **Physical Description**
*   **Architecture and Style:** The tower is a Tudor-style, octagonal structure constructed of red brick with white dressings and fine brickwork [3-5]. 
*   **Dimensions:** It measures 16 feet in diameter at the base, with walls 3 feet 6 inches thick [4]. The structure tapers to a height of 60 feet, where it contracts in diameter and rises perpendicularly another 30 feet, reaching a total height of 90 feet [4, 6]. (When viewed from a distance of a mile or so, it resembles "a chimney poking out of the middle of a wood" [3]). 
*   **External Features:** The tower is illuminated on the sides by lancet windows and is topped with a machicolated cornice and an embattled (crenellated) parapet [3, 4]. It was originally surmounted by a flagstaff intended for public and special occasions [4]. 
*   **Internal Features:** The interior contains a central spiral staircase [4, 6]. 
*   **Inscriptions:** The stonework bears the carved initials of several Bull relatives [7]. It also features a memorial plaque that reads: *"Erected to the memory of his honoured parents the Rev. John Bull, M.A. and Margaret his wife. On a spot they loved so well by EdW. Bull, M.A. 1859."* [7]. 
*   **Modern Condition:** The tower is currently in a state of severe disrepair [8]. The roof was blown off in a storm, and the internal stairway is now deemed entirely unsafe and inaccessible due to an accumulation of pigeon droppings [3, 8]. 

### **Associated Persons**
*   **The Rev. Edward Bull:** Rector of Pentlow (from 1834 to 1877) and father to Borley's Rev. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull [5, 9]. He commissioned the tower as a memorial to his parents, John and Margaret Bull [2, 5, 7]. 
*   **John Johnson:** The architect from Bury St Edmunds who designed the tower [5, 6]. Impressed by this structure, Edward's son Henry later hired Johnson to design Borley Rectory [5, 8]. 
*   **Mr. Webb:** A builder from Sudbury who was selected to construct the tower [6].

### **Key Events**
*   **February–March 1859 (Construction):** In February 1859, the *Suffolk Free Press* announced the impending construction of the 90-foot memorial tower [6]. On Friday, February 25, 1859 (or shortly before March 3), the Rev. Edward Bull laid the first brick, delivering a speech that referenced his brother's labors in the parish [4]. The tower was scheduled to be completed by June of that year [4]. 
*   **August 1886 (The Poltergeist Visit):** P. Shaw Jeffrey and the Rev. Harry Bull visited Pentlow Rectory, then occupied by Harry's uncle, Felix Bull [10]. Jeffrey specifically noted the presence of the "watch-tower in the garden" during this visit, which occurred because Felix had requested help dealing with a poltergeist in his home [10]. 

### **Contradictions and Legends**
**1. Discrepancies in Height:** 
While contemporary 1859 newspaper accounts and most modern descriptions agree the tower is 90 feet tall [4, 6, 8], the sources contain minor contradictions, with one passage claiming it is 95 feet [2], and another author loosely describing it as "some sixty feet high" [11]. 

**2. The Purpose of the Tower (Facts vs. Folklore):**
The official and documented reason for the tower's existence is that it was built by Edward Bull as a solemn memorial to his parents [6, 7]. However, Edward Bull had a reputation as a local tyrant (nicknamed "Old Bull"), and his eccentricity inspired three distinct local legends regarding the tower's true purpose:
*   **The Sea View Legend:** A widespread rumor claimed the incumbent had a "crazy idea" to build a tower tall enough to see the sea, but was deeply disappointed when it was finished because he could not see the ocean even from the very top [10-12]. 
*   **The Spy Tower Legend:** Others believed Edward Bull built the tower as a high vantage point to watch over his farm laborers in the fields, allowing him to take note of anyone who was shirking their duties [11, 12]. 
*   **The Phallic Joke:** Because of its shape, some locals mockingly sniggered that Reverend Bull's "phallic tower" celebrated his father's fertility much more than it honored his memory [5].
