# Bull's Tower / Bull's Folly, Pentlow

A large tower in the rectory garden at [[pentlow|Pentlow]], built in 1858–1859 by the **Rev. Edward Bull** — the brother of [[henry-bull|Rev Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] who built [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]]. Edward was the rector of Pentlow from 1834 to 1877, and the tower stands as a memorial to his parents, though local legends attribute other purposes to its construction.

## Physical Description

The tower is a Tudor-style **octagonal structure** constructed of red brick with white dressings and fine brickwork. It measures **16 feet in diameter at the base**, with walls 3 feet 6 inches thick. The structure tapers to a height of 60 feet, then contracts in diameter and rises perpendicularly another 30 feet, reaching a **total height of 90 feet** (though one source claims 95 feet, and another loosely describes it as "some sixty feet high"). The sides are illuminated by lancet windows and the top is finished with a machicolated cornice and an embattled (crenellated) parapet. It was originally surmounted by a flagstaff intended for public and special occasions. When viewed from a distance of a mile or more it looks, in the authors' description, "rather like a chimney poking out of the middle of a wood."

The interior contains a central spiral staircase. The stonework bears the carved initials of several Bull relatives and a memorial plaque reading: *"Erected to the memory of his honoured parents the Rev. John Bull, M.A. and Margaret his wife. On a spot they loved so well by EdW. Bull, M.A. 1859."*

**Modern Condition:** The tower is currently in severe disrepair. The roof was blown off in a storm, and the internal stairway is deemed entirely unsafe and inaccessible due to an accumulation of pigeon droppings.

## Construction

In February 1859, the *Suffolk Free Press* announced the impending construction of the 90-foot memorial tower. On Friday 25 February 1859 (or shortly before 3 March), the Rev. Edward Bull laid the first brick, delivering a speech that referenced his brother's labours in the parish. The architect was **John Johnson** of Bury St Edmunds, and the builder was **Mr Webb** of Sudbury. Impressed by this structure, Edward's son Henry later hired the same architect Johnson to design Borley Rectory.

## Edward Bull's Character

Edward Bull is described as a somewhat eccentric and domineering figure — nicknamed "Old Bull" locally — and his reputation as a tyrant inspired the local legends about the tower. On one occasion he was one of the guns in a local shoot; finding nothing else to shoot at, he shot a passing pig. Another version holds that the pig bit the rector first ("there is an element of divine retribution in it," the authors remark). Local tradition holds that when he rode through the village in his trap, those who failed to adopt a sufficiently grovelling posture risked feeling his whip.

## Local Legends

The official and documented purpose of the tower is as a solemn memorial to Edward Bull's parents. However, three alternative explanations circulate locally:

1. **The sea theory:** A widespread rumour held that Edward Bull had a "crazy idea" to build a tower tall enough to see the sea, but was deeply disappointed when finished because he could not see the ocean from the very top.
2. **The spy tower theory:** Others believed he built it as a high vantage point to watch over his farm labourers in the fields, taking note of anyone shirking their duties. The authors note this is perhaps not entirely improbable given his tyrannical reputation.
3. **The phallic joke:** Because of its shape, some locals mockingly sniggered that the "phallic tower" celebrated his father's fertility much more than it honoured his memory.

## Borley Connection

**August 1886:** P. Shaw Jeffrey and the Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] visited Pentlow Rectory, then occupied by Harry's uncle, Felix Bull. Jeffrey specifically noted the presence of the "watch-tower in the garden" during this visit, which occurred because Felix had requested help dealing with a poltergeist in his home.

The Bull family of Pentlow provided several consecutive rectors of the village: two Johns, then Edward, then [[felix-bull|Felix]]. Henry Dawson Ellis Bull (the Borley rector) was Edward's brother; together the two siblings were rectors concurrently in these two small and nearby rural parishes, though Felix outlived Henry by three years.
