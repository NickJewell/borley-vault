# Priory Walk, Sudbury

A street in the market town of Sudbury, Suffolk, recorded on Kate Boreham's death certificate as the place where she died on Easter Day, 1888. Ivan Banks's investigation establishes that this address is highly problematic, and uses the discrepancies to argue that Kate may in fact have died at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] rather than in Sudbury. The specific residence associated with the case is described by Banks as a "secluded and unimpressive house" [1].

## The Death Certificate

Kate Boreham's death certificate states she died in **Priory Walk, Sudbury**, from "acute cerebritis." The attending physician is recorded as **W. Nigel Mason**.

## Banks's Investigation

Banks traced the address through the Suffolk County Records Office at Bury St Edmunds and the electoral register for 1888. His findings:

1. **The Borehams did not own or rent the house.** Walter Boreham, Kate's husband, was a farm labourer from Acton, Suffolk — his wages would not have permitted home ownership in Sudbury. Researchers confirm the house "never belonged to or was rented by anyone in the Boreham family" [2]. The 1881 census shows no Boreham in Priory Walk; the 1888 electoral register shows no Boreham registered there either [6].

2. **The registered owner of the Priory Walk house was Jessie Mitchell** — the only person officially recorded as owning a house there at the time of Kate's death — a name with no established connection to either the Boreham family or the Bulls of Borley [4].

3. **A William Henry Bull** owned a property in Market Hill, Sudbury, according to the same electoral register. Banks notes the coincidence of the "Henry" middle name and the Bull surname, suggesting — though without proof — that this person may have been connected to the Borley Bulls, who owned substantial property in the Sudbury area, and that he provided access to a Sudbury property for the family's discreet use [1, 4].

## The Burial Problem

Kate Boreham is recorded as **buried at [[borley-church|Borley]]**, not at a Sudbury church. Banks finds this anomalous: if she died in Sudbury, why was she transported back to Borley for burial? A parish levy was charged for each parish through which a body passed — an expensive and logistically unusual arrangement for a farm labourer's wife. Richard Lee-van den Daele, who visited Borley, reported that he could not find Kate's grave in Borley churchyard.

## Banks's Theory

Banks proposes two possible explanations:

1. Kate died at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] and was moved to Priory Walk after death (where an inexperienced or compliant doctor issued a death certificate with a false location and false cause of death).
2. Alternatively, the Borley burial register entry is also false — Kate's body was never in Borley churchyard at all.

The theory posits that Kate Boreham had become pregnant by the Rev. [[henry-bull|Henry D. E. Bull]] and was fatally poisoned by lead acetate used as an abortifacient [2, 5]. According to this reconstructed scenario, a dying Kate was placed in the Bull family's hunting carriage and driven by the coachman, Joseph Tyler, away from the Rectory after dark [1, 5]. She was allegedly deposited at the house in Priory Walk, where she died shortly afterward, prompting the family to find an inexperienced doctor to certify her death as "acute cerebritis" to avoid a local scandal [1]. Banks further suggests this supposed event is the factual origin of the famous legend of a [[phantom-coach|phantom coach]] speeding away from the Rectory at night [5].

In either case, the Priory Walk address functions as a **cover address** in Banks's theory, used to conceal that Kate's death occurred in the Bull household.

> **Contradiction:** Some researchers carry the theory further, arguing that no one actually died at Priory Walk at all [7]. This version holds that Kate Boreham died inside Borley Rectory itself, that her body was buried secretly in the cellars (accounting for the skull fragments found there by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] in 1943), and that the Priory Walk location on her death certificate was entirely falsified by the Bull family to conceal her true fate [7].

> **Contested:** None of Banks's theories about the death certificate can be proven from available records. Professor Bernard Knight's forensic analysis established that cerebritis as a cause of death is "meaningless as such and was probably speculative," but this does not confirm the alternative narrative.
