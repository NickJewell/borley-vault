# Borley Church

The twelfth-century parish church of Borley, standing directly across the road from the main entrance to the former [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] site. Dedication unknown (possibly St Andrew). Built of flint rubble; small, consisting of chancel, nave, south porch, and west tower.

## Historical Significance

- Contains the major Waldegrave monument: effigies of Sir Edward Waldegrave (d. 1561) and his wife Frances (d. 1599) — one of the finest sixteenth-century sculptures in the area
- Also contains a tablet for their daughter Magdala (d. 1598)
- Churchyard contains the graves of [[henry-bull|Rev. H. D. E. Bull]], [[harry-bull|Rev. Harry Bull]], and Mrs H. D. E. Bull
- In May 1947, the pre-Reformation stone altar was discovered upside-down in the central aisle; restored to its proper position

## As a Paranormal Location

Chapter 6 documents the church as an independent locus of phenomena, distinct from the Rectory:

**Footsteps** — the most frequently reported phenomenon; heard on the path, in the porch, and inside the nave by dozens of independent witnesses from the 1920s to the 1970s.

**Organ music** — heard when the church was locked and empty by multiple independent groups: John May (midnight, October 1947); Mrs Walrond and [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Henning]] (October 1947, on the path); Mrs A. G. Wilson and her sister-in-law (summer 1949). *One incident later explained by two local boys playing the organ as a prank — showing investigators were alert to normal explanations.*

**Strange smells** — incense without source; one dramatic account (Miss Dudley, February 1949) of an overwhelming smell in a precise "bank" of air on the path, experienced by three of four visitors simultaneously while the fourth smelled nothing.

**Paranormal bell-ringing** — on Low Sunday 1949, the church bell rang when the Rector was at Liston and the church was locked; both Pearsons heard and verified it.

**Apparitions** — the nun-like figure has been reported in the churchyard as well as at the Rectory site: Rev. Kipling (August 1949) saw a veiled girl for a "split second" near the porch; Terry Bacon saw the Nun twice in the churchyard; G. Croom-Hollingsworth's 1970 sighting began near the lower end of the Nun's Walk and the figure passed toward the church.

**Knocks** — three loud, distinct blows near the Waldegrave monument during a catechism class (20 March 1949), heard by all the children present.

## The Waldegrave Theory

The Waldegraves — a prominent local Catholic family — are repeatedly connected to the haunting. One theory: a Waldegrave murdered [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] at Borley. Sir Edward Waldegrave's daughters are "contenders" for the identity of the ghost-nun. The 1955–57 planchette séances named "Leslie Waldegrave" as the murderer.

## Phantom Organ Music

Borley Church is associated with repeated incidents of unexplained organ music heard when the building was empty and locked:

- **1947**: Norah Walrond and [[rev-ac-henning|Alfred Henning]] heard organ music from halfway up the path; on entering, complete silence and empty church
- **1947**: A "Mr Hardy junior" heard singing and chanting from within the locked church
- **c.1955**: Mrs Maud Stearn, approaching to play the organ for a service, heard the organ already playing; on reaching the porch, silence; building deserted
- **September 1947**: John May heard footsteps approach the locked church, a key in a lock, door open and close, then "soft notes and chords of organ music" — atonal, "not in any sequence... a jumble of atonal chords" — lasting from 12.18 a.m. Several Borley villagers had previously heard the organ playing when the church was locked at night

## The Church Crypt (Discovered 1988)

Members of the East Anglian Group for Psychic Research Studies found a concealed entrance in the churchyard on 6 July 1988. Removing stones from what appeared to be a dummy grave with a flagstone cover, they descended about 30 feet via broken brick steps, passing a skeleton in soil. They found a large arched chamber extending the length of the church; a small altar covered with a purple cloth bearing a crucifix; four or more lead coffins on stone supports; brickwork at the far end that appeared less ancient than other parts; and a barred wooden door with a keyhole, marked "Anno Domini 1931 H.F.B." The chamber's entrance has since been sealed by the authorities.

No explanation for the curious entrance construction has been established. It may be connected to the Reformation and the Catholic [[waldegrave-family|Waldegraves]].

## Croom-Hollingsworth Recordings

Denny Densham recorded apparently paranormal sounds inside Borley Church in 1972–74 when tape recorders were left running in the locked building: footsteps on bare wood, knocks, bolts sliding, doors creaking, and a deep human sigh. These were featured in the BBC's *The Ghost Hunters* and released on cassette as *The Ghost of Borley Church*. Peter Underwood, during a tour of the church for the broadcast, located objects that "could have made it possible for every noise in the recording to have a natural explanation."

## Investigation Summary

Many investigators have noted a cold spot near the [[waldegrave-monument]]. Sound recordings have been made by multiple teams. Direct investigations include the Cambridge Commission (1939–44, one recorded temperature drop), Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth's team (1970–80), the Enfield Parapsychical Research Group (1974–c.1979), the Revd John Dening (1948, 1958 séances with mediums), and Professor John Taylor of King's College (who noted unexplained sensations of cold and unease but no detectable psychic force with magnetometer equipment).

## The Crypt Discovery (1988, brfa-ch10)

On 6 July 1988, five members of a local psychical research group discovered the entrance to a previously unknown crypt beneath the church — the most significant architectural finding in the entire Borley investigation. See [[borley-church-crypt]] for full details.

Key facts: entrance hidden under a blank, uninscribed tombstone (a false grave) surrounded by iron fence, barely two yards from the altar window; stairs descend 25–30 feet; at the foot, a huge chamber with arched ceiling; four or more lead Waldegrave coffins with green patina; a small altar with purple cloth and crucifix; the entrance door is heavily barred with the initials "H F B" and date "1921" (Rev Harry Foyster Bull).

The clairvoyant who visited in 1986 had reported feeling that "there should have been a door" near the font — confirmed by the group as historically accurate.

## Organ Music — Documented Occurrences

Multiple witnesses have heard what they describe as properly constructed music from the locked, empty church:
- [[rev-ac-henning]] and Mrs Walrond, **15 October 1947**: both heard "proper music" as they approached; rushed in to find empty church and closed organ
- E. W. Jephcott, **16 September 1947**: companion heard the organ playing; church was empty when they entered
- [[maud-stearn]], **1955**: heard organ playing on approach; music ceased on entry; church empty, organ locked
- [[thomas-musk]] and three friends, **late autumn 1987**: heard music from locked church for 5–10 minutes at 4am; described as "properly constructed music like an old gramophone record"

Babbs and Mathias note that tape-recording equipment has twice been found **tangled and destroyed** when left in the church unattended: once by the [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth|Harlow group]], once by Denny Densham's BBC team (~10 years apart). They attribute this to possible electromagnetic effects of [[ley-lines-at-borley|ley lines]] intersecting at Borley.

## Sunday School Children Locked In (brfa-ch05)

Children waiting for Sunday school heard distinct footsteps on the path approaching the porch, then the key being turned in the lock — but no one was there. Kathleen Finch (then about 13) called out "We are here, Miss Byford" — total silence. No departing footsteps were heard after the key turned. Henning investigated and concluded the incident was paranormal.
