# Borley Church

The twelfth-century parish church of Borley, standing directly across the road from the main entrance to the former [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] site. Dedication unknown, though [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. A.C. Henning]] speculated it may have been dedicated to St Andrew [6, 2]. Built of flint rubble; small, consisting of chancel, nave, south porch, and west tower. Many investigators believe paranormal activity shifted to the church after the Rectory was destroyed [1, 2].

## Architecture and Historical Record

The earliest parts of the church — nave and chancel — were built in the twelfth century from flint rubble [3, 4]. Later fourteenth- and fifteenth-century alterations to the windows and fabric gave it a late Gothic (Perpendicular) appearance [5, 6]. Around 1500, a three-stage embattled western tower adorned with gargoyles and a distinctive red-brick south porch were added [3, 5, 6, 7]. The church's main pathway to the door is bordered by an avenue of clipped topiary yew trees [8, 9, 10].

The church has no known dedication. Its earliest surviving parish registers date from 1652 for baptisms, 1656 for burials, and 1709 for marriages [15, 6, 9].

## Historical Significance

- Contains the major [[waldegrave-monument|Waldegrave monument]]: a massive 14-foot-high memorial holding effigies of [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Sir Edward Waldegrave]] (d. 1561) and his wife Frances (d. 1599) — one of the finest sixteenth-century sculptures in the area [11, 12]
- Also contains a tablet for their daughter Magdala (d. 1598)
- The church houses an old-fashioned organ that required a second person to pump the air bellows [13, 14]
- Churchyard contains the graves of [[henry-bull|Rev. H. D. E. Bull]], [[harry-bull|Rev. Harry Bull]], and Mrs H. D. E. Bull [9]
- In August 1943, [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Henning]] used a block and tackle to lift a one-ton stone slab in the central aisle, hoping to find an entrance to the fabled church crypt [16, 17]. They found only sand beneath, but discovered the stone was the original pre-Reformation altar laid upside-down [17, 18]. In June 1947, [[rev-ac-henning|Henning]] officially restored this stone altar onto four Portland stone pillars in the chancel [19, 20].

## As a Paranormal Location

Chapter 6 of *Ghosts of Borley* documents the church as an independent locus of phenomena, distinct from the Rectory.

**Footsteps** — the most frequently reported phenomenon; heard on the path, in the porch, and inside the nave by dozens of independent witnesses from the 1920s to the 1970s. In August 1947, author [[james-turner|James Turner]] sat in the porch after midnight and heard heavy, limping footsteps accompanied by the rustle of a skirt approach up the path, though no one was visible [29, 30]. Similar invisible footsteps were reported by investigator John Durrant in 1946 and by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and the Hennings in June 1947 [31, 32].

**Organ music** — heard when the church was locked and empty by multiple independent groups (see Phantom Organ Music section below).

**Strange smells** — incense without source; one dramatic account (Miss Dudley, February 1949) of an overwhelming smell in a precise "bank" of air on the path, experienced by three of four visitors simultaneously while the fourth smelled nothing.

**Paranormal bell-ringing** — on Low Sunday 1949, the church bell rang when the Rector was at [[liston-rectory|Liston]] and the church was locked; both Pearsons heard and verified it.

**Apparitions** — [[the-nun|the nun-like figure]] has been reported in the churchyard as well as at the Rectory site: Rev. Kipling (August 1949) saw a veiled girl for a "split second" near the porch; Terry Bacon saw the Nun twice in the churchyard; [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth|G. Croom-Hollingsworth's]] 1970 sighting began near the lower end of the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] and the figure passed toward the church.

**Knocks** — three loud, distinct blows near the [[waldegrave-monument|Waldegrave monument]] during a catechism class (20 March 1949), heard by all the children present.

**Displacement of objects** — during the 1940s, objects within the locked church were repeatedly found moved. A sanctuary lamp on the altar had its wick tampered with, and protective covers placed over it by the churchwarden were mysteriously thrown to the floor overnight [33, 34, 35].

## The Waldegrave Theory

The [[waldegrave-family|Waldegraves]] — a prominent local Catholic family and Lords of the Manor of Borley for approximately three hundred years [36, 37, 38] — are repeatedly connected to the haunting. One theory: a Waldegrave murdered [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] at Borley. [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Sir Edward Waldegrave's]] daughters are "contenders" for the identity of the ghost-nun. The 1955–57 planchette séances named "Leslie Waldegrave" as the murderer. See [[waldegrave-family]] and [[waldegrave-monument]].

## Phantom Organ Music

Borley Church is associated with repeated incidents of unexplained organ music heard when the building was empty and locked. Proponents highlight that the organ required a second person to manually pump the bellows, making a quick hoax exit extremely difficult [14]. Skeptics have attempted to explain the sounds as air pressure slowly releasing from the bellows, or minor earth tremors shifting the instrument [49]; proponents dismiss these as "straw-clutching," noting witnesses specifically reported coherent, structured music — like Handel's *Largo* or church voluntaries — rather than discordant noise [49, 50].

Documented occurrences:

- **September 1947**: [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Henning]] and Mrs Walrond heard "proper music" as they approached on October 15; rushed in to find empty church and closed organ [28, 14]
- **September 1947**: John May heard footsteps approach the locked church, a key in a lock, door open and close, then "soft notes and chords of organ music" — atonal, "not in any sequence... a jumble of atonal chords" — lasting from 12.18 a.m.
- **September 1947**: E. W. Jephcott's companion heard the organ playing; church was empty when they entered
- **1947**: A "Mr Hardy junior" heard singing and chanting from within the locked church
- **c.1955**: [[maud-stearn|Mrs Maud Stearn]], approaching to play the organ for a service, heard the organ already playing; on reaching the porch, silence; building deserted [24, 25, 26, 27]
- **Late autumn 1987**: [[thomas-musk|Thomas Musk]] and three friends heard music from the locked church for 5–10 minutes at 4am; described as "properly constructed music like an old gramophone record"

Babbs and Mathias note that tape-recording equipment has twice been found **tangled and destroyed** when left in the church unattended: once by the [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth|Harlow group]], once by Denny Densham's BBC team (~10 years apart). They attribute this to possible electromagnetic effects of [[ley-lines-at-borley|ley lines]] intersecting at Borley.

## Sunday School Children Locked In (brfa-ch05)

Children waiting for Sunday school heard distinct footsteps on the path approaching the porch, then the key being turned in the lock — but no one was there. Kathleen Finch (then about 13) called out "We are here, Miss Byford" — total silence. No departing footsteps were heard after the key turned. [[rev-ac-henning|Henning]] investigated and concluded the incident was paranormal.

## Associated Persons

- **[[waldegrave-family|The Waldegrave Family]]:** Lords of the Manor of Borley from the sixteenth century; held the church patronage for three hundred years. The church contains their ornate family monuments [12].
- **[[rev-ac-henning|The Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning]]:** Rector of Borley-cum-Liston from 1936 to 1955. He documented many of the phenomena associated with the church, published the booklet *Haunted Borley*, and collaborated closely with Price's investigations [39, 40, 41].
- **[[harry-bull|The Rev. Harry Foyster Bull]]:** Rector 1892–1927. His initials and the date 1921 were found carved into the barred door of the crypt, suggesting he secretly sealed it during his lifetime [42, 23]. [[ernest-ambrose|Ernest Ambrose]], organist at Borley Church for sixteen years prior to the First World War, corroborated early stories from the Bull era — including Harry Bull tracking mysterious tapping sounds around the church walls [43, 44].

## The Church Crypt (Discovered 1988)

Members of the East Anglian Group for Psychic Research Studies found a concealed entrance in the churchyard on 6 July 1988 [21, 22]. On 6 July 1988, five members of a local psychical research group discovered the entrance to a previously unknown crypt — the most significant architectural finding in the entire Borley investigation. See [[borley-church-crypt]] for full details.

The entrance was hidden under a blank, uninscribed tombstone (a false grave) surrounded by iron fencing, barely two yards from the altar window. Removing stones from what appeared to be a dummy grave with a flagstone cover, they descended about 25–30 feet via broken brick steps, passing a skeleton in soil. They found a large arched chamber extending the length of the church; a small altar covered with a purple cloth bearing a crucifix; four or more lead Waldegrave coffins on stone supports with green patina; brickwork at the far end that appeared less ancient than other parts; and a barred wooden door with a keyhole, marked "Anno Domini 1921 H.F.B." (Rev. Harry Foyster Bull) [21, 23]. The chamber's entrance has since been sealed by the authorities.

No explanation for the curious entrance construction has been established. It may be connected to the Reformation and the Catholic [[waldegrave-family|Waldegraves]].

The clairvoyant who visited in 1986 had reported feeling that "there should have been a door" near the font — confirmed by the group as historically accurate.

## Croom-Hollingsworth Recordings

[[denny-densham|Denny Densham]] recorded apparently paranormal sounds inside Borley Church in 1972–74 when tape recorders were left running in the locked building: footsteps on bare wood, knocks, bolts sliding, doors creaking, and a deep human sigh. These were featured in the BBC's *The Ghost Hunters* and released on cassette as *The Ghost of Borley Church*. [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]], during a tour of the church for the broadcast, located objects that "could have made it possible for every noise in the recording to have a natural explanation."

## Investigation Summary

Many investigators have noted a cold spot near the [[waldegrave-monument]]. Sound recordings have been made by multiple teams. Direct investigations include the [[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]] (1939–44, one recorded temperature drop), [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth|Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth's]] team (1970–80), the Enfield Parapsychical Research Group (1974–c.1979), the Revd John Dening (1948, 1958 séances with mediums), and Professor John Taylor of King's College (who noted unexplained sensations of cold and unease but no detectable psychic force with magnetometer equipment).

## Contradictions and Source Perspectives

**The moving coffins and [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]]:** [[harry-price|Harry Price]] published a claim that Miss Ethel Bull (daughter of Rev. Henry Bull) told him the heavy Waldegrave coffins in the church crypt had been paranormally displaced, similar to the famous Barbados vault [45, 46]. However, SPR investigators reported that Ethel Bull firmly denied making this statement, claiming she knew nothing of any crypt at Borley [47].

> **Contradiction:** Skeptics used Ethel Bull's denial to accuse Price of fabricating evidence; however, later investigator [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth|Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth]] stated he possessed proof that Ethel Bull *did* know about the crypt and had personally been down into it [47, 48].

**The monastic legend:** Sensational early press reports and Price's initial articles claimed the church and Rectory sat on the site of a thirteenth-century monastery [51, 52, 53]. This was definitively debunked in 1938 when the Essex Archaeological Society confirmed no ecclesiastical building other than the parish church had ever existed at Borley [54, 55, 56]. The confusion likely arose because the Manor of Borley was granted to the Benedictine monks of Christ Church, Canterbury, in 1362, but they administered it as an absentee landlord rather than building a monastic house there [57, 58, 59].
