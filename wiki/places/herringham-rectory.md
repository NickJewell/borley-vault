# Herringham Rectory

The residence of the Rectors of Borley in the first half of the nineteenth century, subsequently demolished to make way for the famous [[borley-rectory|Bull Rectory]].

## History

The Herringham Rectory was built in the early 1800s for the **Revd William Herringham**, Rector of Borley from 1807 to 1819. He was succeeded by his son **John Philip Herringham** until 1862. When [[henry-bull|Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] was ordained and became the new Borley parish priest, he decided to replace the existing building with one more suited to his large family's needs.

No plans exist of the Rectory itself. What is known about its former location is drawn from the Borley tithe map of 1841: it was a two-storey rectangular building orientated in the same fashion as Henry Bull's house, with the main entrance porch and driveway facing north-east toward the roadway, while the principal living rooms were south-west facing and overlooked the garden.

The [[rectory-cottage]] was used by the Herringhams while at Borley, and later adapted for use by Henry Bull.

## Relationship to the Bull Rectory

After the Herringham Rectory was pulled down, the Bull Rectory was built further to the east. The parts of Henry Bull's house constructed on the footprint of the former building were the kitchen quarters, the later 1875 extension, and the courtyard. The foundations of the Herringham Rectory were not uncovered during digging work in the 1950s (see [[bones-excavation|Excavations]]); traces of an earlier building dating from around 1550–1600 were discovered at that time, which is known as the "post-mediaeval rectory."

## In Later Investigations

John Philip Herringham's late decision to reside at [[borley-place|Borley Place]] rather than the Herringham Rectory has never been fully explained. [[ivan-banks]] suggested — without supporting evidence — that the Herringham Rectory may have experienced paranormal activity, pointing to an unsubstantiated 1843 sighting of a "phantom nun" (see Chronology). The BRC notes this claim is not corroborated.

A study of the locations of the various rectory buildings at Borley is included in [[andrew-clarke]]'s *The Bones of Borley*.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**The Herringham Rectory** was the immediate predecessor to the famous Victorian building known as "the most haunted house in England." It stood on the Borley Rectory site in the first half of the nineteenth century and was the subject of early, undocumented ghost stories. 

### **Physical Description and Location**
*   **Architecture and Style:** The Herringham Rectory was a two-storey rectangular building in the fashionable Georgian style [1, 2]. It was likely constructed of pale "Suffolk Galt" brick with a slate roof [2]. 
*   **Layout and Orientation:** Its main entrance porch and driveway faced north-east toward the road, utilizing the exact same driveway entrance later used by the Bull family [1, 2]. Its principal living rooms faced south-west, taking full advantage of the views down the long rectory garden [1, 2]. 
*   **Features:** It featured an ornamental pond to the east [3, 4]. The Rectory Cottage (which survives today as a private residence) served as its coach-house and stables [1, 2, 5]. 
*   **Location Relative to the Bull Rectory:** It was a large building, roughly the same size as the nearby Borley Place, but not as massive as the Victorian rectory that replaced it [2, 6]. The Herringham Rectory stood further west on the site; when the Rev. Henry Bull built his new rectory, he constructed it partially over the Herringham footprint, specifically placing his kitchen quarters, 1875 extension, and enclosed courtyard over the older site [3, 6, 7]. Consequently, modern excavations of the Borley cellars in the 1950s missed the Herringham foundations completely, as they lay buried beneath the rubble of the kitchen wing [8]. 

### **Associated Persons**
*   **The Rev. William Herringham:** Rector of Borley from 1807 to 1819 [9]. It is generally believed that he had the house built during his incumbency [10]. 
*   **The Rev. John Philip Herringham:** William's son, who succeeded him as Rector of Borley from 1819 to 1862 [9, 11]. 

### **Key Events**
*   **Construction (c. 1807–1819):** The Rectory was built during the incumbency of either William or John Herringham on a site containing traces of a much older post-medieval building dating to 1550–1600 [7, 8, 12]. 
*   **Abandonment:** For reasons that remain heavily debated, the Rev. John Herringham abandoned his rectory in his later years. The building was locked up and stood completely disused for a long time while the Rector took up residence across the road at Borley Place until his death in 1862 [13, 14].
*   **Demolition (1862–1863):** When the Rev. Henry D. E. Bull took over the living in 1862, he decided to completely demolish the Herringham Rectory to make way for his new, larger home [11, 14]. He did this because the Herringham Rectory, while elegant, could not easily be expanded to provide the massive, formal drawing rooms his highly sociable family required for hosting dances and large parties [6]. 

### **Contradictions, Discrepancies, and Hauntings**
*   **The Reason for Abandonment:** The sources debate why the Rev. John Herringham abandoned the house. Some suggest it fell into a bad state of repair making it uninhabitable, or that the financial burden of the Window Tax drove him out [14]. However, this theory is directly contradicted by *White’s Gazetteer* of 1848, which officially described the Herringham Rectory just twelve years before its demolition as "a good modern residence" [13, 14]. 
*   **The "Haunted" Theory:** Because the building was abandoned while seemingly in good condition, researchers like Ivan Banks have speculated that John Herringham actually moved out because the house had become plagued by paranormal activity [14]. There are vague, undocumented claims that the Rev. William Herringham had "experiences" there, that ghosts of puritans were seen, and a flimsy claim that the phantom nun was witnessed at Borley as early as 1843 [14, 15]. However, there is no solid historical evidence to prove the Herringham Rectory was haunted [14].
*   **The 1841 Fire Discrepancy:** Ghost book writer Wesley Downes published a theory that the Herringham Rectory was actually destroyed by a fire in 1841 [12, 16]. This claim is unconfirmed and is widely contradicted by tithe maps and historical records proving the house stood intact until the Rev. Bull deliberately demolished it in the early 1860s [6, 12-14].
