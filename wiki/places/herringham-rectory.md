# Herringham Rectory

The residence of the Rectors of Borley in the first half of the nineteenth century, subsequently demolished to make way for the famous [[borley-rectory|Bull Rectory]].

## Physical Description and Location

The Herringham Rectory was a **two-storey rectangular building** in the fashionable Georgian style, likely constructed of pale "Suffolk Galt" brick with a slate roof. It was roughly the same size as the nearby [[borley-place|Borley Place]], though not as massive as the Victorian rectory that replaced it.

Its main entrance porch and driveway faced north-east toward the road, using the exact same driveway entrance later used by the Bull family; its principal living rooms faced south-west, taking full advantage of the views down the long rectory garden. It featured an **ornamental pond to the east**. The [[rectory-cottage|Rectory Cottage]] (which survives today as a private residence) served as its coach-house and stables.

No plans exist of the Rectory itself. What is known about its former location is drawn from the **Borley tithe map of 1841**: a two-storey rectangular building oriented in the same fashion as Henry Bull's house, with the main entrance porch facing north-east toward the roadway.

## History

The Herringham Rectory was built during the incumbency of the **Revd William Herringham**, Rector of Borley from 1807 to 1819. He was succeeded by his son **John Philip Herringham** from 1819 until 1862. When [[henry-bull|Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] was ordained and became the new Borley parish priest, he decided to completely demolish the existing building — because the Herringham Rectory, while elegant, could not easily be expanded to provide the massive, formal drawing rooms his highly sociable family required for hosting dances and large parties.

When Henry Bull built his new rectory, he constructed it partially over the Herringham footprint, specifically placing his kitchen quarters, 1875 extension, and enclosed courtyard over the older site. Consequently, modern excavations of the Borley cellars in the 1950s missed the Herringham foundations completely, as they lay buried beneath the rubble of the kitchen wing. The construction also revealed traces of an **earlier building dating from around 1550–1600** — known as the "post-mediaeval rectory."

## The Abandonment Controversy

For reasons heavily debated, the Rev. John Herringham abandoned his rectory in his later years, locking it up and residing across the road at [[borley-place|Borley Place]] until his death in 1862.

> **Contradiction:** Some suggest the building fell into disrepair or that the financial burden of the Window Tax drove him out. However, *White's Gazetteer* of 1848 officially described the Herringham Rectory just twelve years before its demolition as "a good modern residence" [13, 14] — directly contradicting the disrepair theory.

A third possibility, advanced by [[ivan-banks|Ivan Banks]], is that John Herringham moved out because the house had become plagued by paranormal activity — pointing to vague, undocumented claims that the Rev. William Herringham had "experiences" there, that ghosts of puritans were seen, and a flimsy claim that the [[the-nun|phantom nun]] was witnessed at Borley as early as 1843. However, there is no solid historical evidence to substantiate this.

> **Contradiction:** Ghost writer Wesley Downes published a theory that the Herringham Rectory was destroyed by a **fire in 1841**. This claim is unconfirmed and is widely contradicted by tithe maps and historical records proving the house stood intact until the Rev. Bull deliberately demolished it in the early 1860s [6, 12–14].

## In Later Investigations

[[ivan-banks|Ivan Banks]] suggested — without supporting evidence — that the Herringham Rectory may have experienced paranormal activity, pointing to the unsubstantiated 1843 sighting. The BRC notes this claim is not corroborated.

A study of the locations of the various rectory buildings at Borley is included in [[andrew-clarke|Andrew Clarke's]] *The Bones of Borley*.
