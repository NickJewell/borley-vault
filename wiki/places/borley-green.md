# Borley Green

The westernmost section of Borley parish, accessible by proceeding through the village from the church and rectory site. It consists of farmhouses, thatched cottages, and some more modern houses and bungalows. From Borley Green the road descends in two steep hills toward the lane leading to Sudbury in one direction and various small villages in the other.

## Who Lived There

Several key figures in the Borley story were associated with Borley Green:

- **[[alfred-finch|Alfred]] and [[margaret-finch|Margaret Finch]]** — their semi-detached cottage at Borley Green was home for most of their lives. One of the adjoining cottages was occupied during the early 1930s by [[marion-pitt|Miss Marion Pitt]], the village schoolmistress, who became friendly with [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]].
- **[[herbert-mayes|Herbert Mayes]]** — chauffeur-gardener to the [[rev-ac-henning|Rev Henning]] during the Henning incumbency; lived at Borley Green and cycled to and from work at Liston Rectory each day. On one evening while cycling up Hall Lane from Borley Green, he heard the sound of horses trotting toward him (the [[phantom-coach|phantom coach]] phenomenon), pulled his bicycle aside, and found nothing visible passing close by him.

## The Telephone Box

During the fire of 27 February 1939, [[captain-gregson|Capt Gregson]] sent his sons to the nearest telephone to call the fire brigade. According to [[alan-gregson|Alan Gregson's]] 1985 letters, this was at Borley Green — indicating that the only public telephone available was at the far end of the parish from the rectory.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Borley Green** is a distinct, small hamlet forming the westernmost third of the scattered rural parish of Borley. Situated about half a mile west of the church and the former Rectory site, it is reached by proceeding along Hall Lane, past a few modern bungalows and a half-mile stretch of open fields [1-3]. 

### **Physical Description**
*   **Architecture and Layout:** The hamlet comprises a mixture of farmhouses, more modern dwellings, and ancient thatched cottages, some of which date back over 400 to nearly 700 years [3-5]. Leaving Borley Green, two steep hills drop down to a lane leading toward Sudbury and other nearby villages [3].
*   **Amenities:** Borley Green traditionally lacked major commercial development but historically served as a modest hub for the locals. It once possessed a post office (run at one time by resident Mrs. Margaret Finch), the village pump (which was originally the only water supply for the village), and a beer shop or off-licence, functioning as a part-time parlour where drinks were served before its eventual closure [3, 6, 7]. 

### **Associated Persons**
*   **Alfred and Margaret Finch:** Lived in a semi-detached cottage "near the Green" during the 1920s and 1930s. Margaret Finch had previously been a servant at the Rectory during the Rev. Harry Bull's incumbency [8, 9]. 
*   **Miss Marion Pitt:** The village schoolmistress lived in the cottage adjoining the Finches. She was notably one of the few villagers to become close friends with Marianne Foyster, and the two women were often heard shrieking with laughter together at the cottage [9, 10].
*   **Herbert Mayes:** The gardener and chauffeur to the Rev. Alfred Henning resided at Borley Green [11, 12].
*   **Karen Waddy:** A resident of Red House Cottage on Borley Green during the 1980s [13].

### **Key Events and Paranormal Claims**
*   **The Phantom Hooves (March 1939):** Shortly after the Rectory burned down, Herbert Mayes was cycling home to Borley Green at night when he heard the terrifying, invisible sound of stampeding horses approaching him from the direction of Borley Green, heading toward the Rectory [14].
*   **The Witch Sighting (1941–1945):** During World War II, American airmen stationed in the area reported seeing the apparition of a witch specifically on Borley Green [15]. 
*   **The Tunnel Discovery (September 1957):** Workmen employed by the local rural council were digging a trench from the direction of Borley Green toward Rodbridge Corner to lay water mains when their excavator broke through brickwork, uncovering the legendary Borley subterranean tunnel [16, 17]. 
*   **The Haunted Cottage (1980s):** Karen Waddy reported a distinct "presence" in a small upstairs room of Red House Cottage on the Green. She noted that the room remained inexplicably cold despite being situated directly above a warm bathroom, and her pet cat adamantly refused to enter the room [13].

### **Contradictions and Source Perspectives (Borley Hysteria)**
The area of Borley Green features prominently in skeptical arguments regarding the psychological phenomenon of **"Borley Hysteria"**—a period characterized by what critics call "extravagant theory, unrestrained assumption, and extreme credulity" [18, 19]. 

The prime example of this occurred on April 27, 1947, when a visitor named Mr. S. L. Croft lost a pencil during a trip to the village. After unsuccessfully searching the ruined Rectory site, the churchyard, and a wood at Borley Green, Croft formally wrote to investigator Harry Price with a precise description of the lost pencil "in case it is involved in any phenomena" at a later date [20, 21]. 

The authors of the highly skeptical *Borley Report* (Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) utilized the Borley Green wood search to illustrate how the immense media publicity surrounding the village caused suggestible visitors to attribute supernatural significance to entirely mundane occurrences [20, 21]. Similarly, claims such as the American airmen's sighting of a witch on the Green remain isolated, uncorroborated anecdotes that skeptics view as extensions of local folklore rather than genuine paranormal events [15].
