# Borley Green

The westernmost section of Borley parish, accessible by proceeding through the village from the church and rectory site. It consists of farmhouses, thatched cottages — some dating back over 400 to nearly 700 years — and some more modern houses and bungalows. From Borley Green the road descends in two steep hills toward the lane leading to Sudbury in one direction and various small villages in the other.

## What Was There

Borley Green once possessed a **post office** (run at one time by resident [[margaret-finch|Margaret Finch]]), the **village pump** (originally the only water supply for the village), and a **beer shop or off-licence** functioning as a part-time parlour before its eventual closure. The hamlet traditionally lacked major commercial development but served as a modest hub for locals.

## Who Lived There

Several key figures in the Borley story were associated with Borley Green:

- **[[alfred-finch|Alfred]] and [[margaret-finch|Margaret Finch]]** — their semi-detached cottage at Borley Green was home for most of their lives. Margaret had previously been a servant at the Rectory during Rev. Harry Bull's incumbency. One of the adjoining cottages was occupied during the early 1930s by [[marion-pitt|Miss Marion Pitt]], the village schoolmistress, who became closely friendly with [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]]; the two women were often heard shrieking with laughter together at the cottage.
- **[[herbert-mayes|Herbert Mayes]]** — chauffeur-gardener to the [[rev-ac-henning|Rev Henning]] during the Henning incumbency; lived at Borley Green and cycled to and from work at Liston Rectory each day.
- **Karen Waddy** — a resident of Red House Cottage on Borley Green during the 1980s.

## The Telephone Box

During the fire of 27 February 1939, [[captain-gregson|Capt Gregson]] sent his sons to the nearest telephone to call the fire brigade. According to [[alan-gregson|Alan Gregson's]] 1985 letters, this was at Borley Green — indicating that the only public telephone available was at the far end of the parish from the rectory.

## Paranormal Claims

**Herbert Mayes and the Phantom Hooves (March 1939):** Shortly after the Rectory burned down, Mayes was cycling home to Borley Green at night when he heard the terrifying, invisible sound of stampeding horses approaching him from the direction of Borley Green, heading toward the Rectory (the [[phantom-coach|phantom coach]] phenomenon), pulled his bicycle aside, and found nothing visible passing close by him (single witness).

**The Witch Sighting (1941–1945):** During World War II, American airmen stationed in the area reportedly saw the apparition of a witch specifically on Borley Green (single witness; isolated anecdote).

**The Tunnel Discovery (September 1957):** Workmen employed by the local rural council were digging a trench from the direction of Borley Green toward Rodbridge Corner to lay water mains when their excavator broke through brickwork, uncovering the legendary Borley subterranean tunnel.

**The Haunted Cottage (1980s):** Karen Waddy reported a distinct "presence" in a small upstairs room of Red House Cottage on the Green. She noted the room remained inexplicably cold despite being situated directly above a warm bathroom, and her pet cat adamantly refused to enter the room (single witness).

## "Borley Hysteria" and Skeptical Perspectives

The area of Borley Green features in skeptical arguments regarding what the *Borley Report* authors termed **"Borley Hysteria"** — a period characterised by "extravagant theory, unrestrained assumption, and extreme credulity" [18, 19].

The prime example cited by [[eric-dingwall|Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Hall]] occurred on 27 April 1947, when a visitor named S.L. Croft lost a pencil during a trip to the village. After unsuccessfully searching the ruined Rectory site, the churchyard, and a wood at Borley Green, Croft formally wrote to [[harry-price|Harry Price]] with a precise description of the lost pencil "in case it is involved in any phenomena" at a later date [20, 21]. The SPR authors used this to illustrate how immense media publicity had caused suggestible visitors to attribute supernatural significance to entirely mundane occurrences.
