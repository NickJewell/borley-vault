# Dairy Cottage, Rendlesham

A two-storey red-brick building about half a mile up a narrow lane off the Rendlesham-to-Campsea-Ashe road, Suffolk. The most significant of the post-Borley locations in the [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] story: it is where the Fisher–Foyster household lived from June 1941 to 1945, and where [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] died on 18 April 1945.

## Physical Description

The cottage was originally used to house workers from the Rendlesham Hall estate. At the time of Marianne's residence it was divided internally into two. Robert Wood visited it decades later and noted it had "hardly changed since 1945." It lies about half a mile from the road, deeply secluded. The nearby **Bentwaters airbase** (American) did not yet exist in Marianne's day; the setting was nothing but Tunstall Forest and scattered cottages.

Wood: "A visit to Dairy Cottage will convince the reader that Marianne was trying to hide herself."

## Occupants and Events

The household at Dairy Cottage during 1941–1945 included:
- [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] (as "Mrs Fisher")
- [[henry-fisher|Henry Francis Fisher]] (as apparent husband; frequently away)
- [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] (as the invalid "father", bedridden and confined to one room)
- [[john-knights|John Fisher]] (adopted son; sent to boarding school, St Joseph's RC in Ipswich, returning at weekends)
- [[astrid-fisher|Astrid Fisher]] (adopted daughter; mostly at Braintree convent)
- Two evacuees: David and Stewart Downing (see [[sergeant-downing]])
- [[robert-oneil|Robert O'Neil]] (GI, frequent visitor from Parham airfield)

Adelaide Foyster had "lived away from home for some time" by this point.

## Foyster's Death

[[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] died here on **18 April 1945**. His death certificate was signed by [[kjt-keer|K.J.T. Keer MRCS]], Wickham Market, with causes of death given as "exhaustion, bedsores, and rheumatoid arthritis" — causes Wood and several consulting doctors considered insufficient. Marianne was present at the death. See [[death-of-lionel-foyster]] for full analysis.

John Fisher's memoir: "Rev. Foyster was certainly bedridden and stayed in one room all the time at Rendlesham. His arms and legs were drawn up with arthritis. I used to like to spend some time with Rev. Foyster and can remember writing letters for him and feeding him with an invalid cup."

Foyster was buried at [[campsey-ashe|Campsea Ashe]] churchyard, where his stone reads: *"Lionel Algernon Foyster, Priest. 1878–1945. The Lord is my Shepherd."*

## The GI Connection

The nearby **barracks at Rendlesham Park** housed American servicemen, and Robert O'Neil was billeted at Parham (Framlingham) with the **390th Heavy Bombardment Group**. He came to Dairy Cottage ostensibly to help when Foyster was ill; the relationship with Marianne developed from there. John Fisher confirms O'Neil was a frequent visitor, liberal with sweets for the children and beer for Marianne (he installed a barrel in the larder).

## Aftermath

In late September 1945 Marianne moved to 1 Deben Avenue, Martlesham. She had already married O'Neil (11 August 1945) and was planning her departure to America. The household at Dairy Cottage — including its extraordinary secret of the bedridden "father-in-law" who was actually the legal husband — ended quietly. Neighbours in the 1950s remembered "Mr and Mrs Fisher" and that "Mrs Fisher's old father Mr Foyster was bedridden and rarely seen."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Dairy Cottage** (often referred to as Dairy Cottages) was located in Rendlesham, Suffolk, and serves as the final setting for the bizarre, tragic climax of the Foyster family’s story following their departure from Borley Rectory. 

### **Physical Description and Location**
*   **Location:** The property is situated in an extremely isolated area of Suffolk, located about half a mile up a narrow lane off the Rendlesham-to-Campsey Ashe road [1]. During the 1940s, it was surrounded by Tunstall Forest with no other cottages within half a mile, though the Bentwaters American airbase was located somewhat nearby [1]. 
*   **Architecture:** It is a two-storey, red-brick building, divided internally into two dwellings, originally built to house estate workers for Rendlesham Hall [1]. 
*   **Amenities:** Skeptical investigator Trevor Hall and author Robert Wood described it as the "most remote and inhospitable" of all the houses Marianne Foyster inhabited [1]. It reportedly had very primitive facilities, lacking running water and possessing only a single outside lavatory [2]. 

### **Associated Persons**
During the early 1940s, Dairy Cottage housed an extraordinarily complex and deceptive domestic arrangement:
*   **Marianne Foyster (living as Mrs. Fisher):** The former wife of the Borley Rector, who used the cottage to hide her bigamous lifestyle [1].
*   **Henry Francis Fisher:** Marianne’s bigamous husband, who paid for their homes but was often away working in a munitions factory [2, 3].
*   **The Rev. Lionel Foyster:** The former Borley Rector. Crippled by arthritis and completely bedridden, he was passed off to neighbors and the children as Marianne’s elderly father or godfather [2, 4]. 
*   **The Children:** Marianne's adopted son, John Fisher; her adopted daughter, Astrid (who was mostly away at a convent but occasionally visited); and Adelaide Foyster (the child adopted during the Borley years, vaguely remembered by John) [3]. 
*   **Evacuees:** Two young brothers evacuated from Bromley, Kent, named David and Stewart Dowding [2, 3]. 
*   **Male Callers:** Sergeant Dowding (the evacuees' father, who wished to marry Marianne) and **Private Robert Vincent O’Neil**, an American GI stationed at Parham who became Marianne's lover and eventually her third husband [3, 5].

### **Key Events**
*   **Relocation (June 1941):** Marianne moved her household from Snape to Dairy Cottage. Author Robert Wood suggests this specific location was chosen due to her natural restlessness and a desperate need for concealment to hide her bigamy [1].
*   **Domestic Abuse:** While Henry Fisher was away, Marianne frequently entertained soldiers and callers [3, 6]. Her adopted son John recorded that she treated the children with sadistic cruelty, beating them and punishing them by holding their heads in buckets of cold water [6]. John found his only refuge in the room of the bedridden Rev. Foyster, where he would read to him, write his letters, and feed him with an invalid cup [3, 4].
*   **Death of the Rector (18 April 1945):** The Rev. Lionel Foyster died at Dairy Cottage [7, 8]. He was subsequently buried in the nearby churchyard at Campsey Ashe [7, 9].
*   **The Midnight Flit (c. 1945/1946):** After obtaining probate for Foyster's will, Marianne vanished abruptly from Dairy Cottage. She took the children, all the furniture, and Henry Fisher’s car. When Fisher arrived at the cottage, he found it completely empty and never saw his family again [8, 10]. 

### **Contradictions and Source Perspectives**
*   **Discrepancies in the Physical Description:** The grim, primitive picture of Dairy Cottage painted by investigators sharply contrasts with the memories Marianne passed down to her American son, Vincent O'Neil Jr. She repeatedly described Dairy Cottage to him as a "beautiful place" that she loved, telling him it was an idyllic home filled with flowers, chickens, a cow, an arbor, and a fishpond [11]. 
*   **The Cause of Foyster's Death:** A major point of suspicion raised by author Robert Wood (in *The Widow of Borley*) concerns Lionel Foyster's death certificate. The certificate, signed by local physician Dr. K.J.T. Keer, listed the causes of death as "exhaustion, bedsores, and rheumatoid arthritis" [8]. Wood consulted several doctors who all agreed these causes were medically insufficient (e.g., bedsores may cause fatal septicaemia, but are not a cause of death themselves) [8]. Wood heavily insinuated that Foyster may have died from deliberate neglect or foul play, and speculated that Marianne might have blackmailed the doctor into signing a questionable certificate to avoid an inquest [8]. 
*   **Identity Deception:** Local history records show how completely Marianne managed to deceive the community at Rendlesham. Former neighbors interviewed in the 1950s had no idea that the "Mr. Fisher" they occasionally saw was part of a bigamous marriage, nor did they know the bedridden old man in the house was actually Marianne's legal husband; they entirely believed her story that she was "Mrs. Fisher" and the invalid was her retired clergyman father [1, 12].
