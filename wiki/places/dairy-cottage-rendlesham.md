# Dairy Cottage, Rendlesham

A two-storey red-brick building about half a mile up a narrow lane off the Rendlesham-to-Campsea-Ashe road, Suffolk. The most significant of the post-Borley locations in the [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] story: it is where the Fisher–Foyster household lived from June 1941 to 1945, and where [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] died.

## Physical Description

The cottage was originally built to house workers from the Rendlesham Hall estate. At the time of Marianne's residence it was divided internally into two dwellings. It lay about half a mile from the road, deeply secluded. The nearby **Bentwaters airbase** (American) did not yet exist in Marianne's day; the setting was nothing but Tunstall Forest and scattered cottages. Robert Wood visited it decades later and noted it had "hardly changed since 1945."

Investigators [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] and Wood described it as the "most remote and inhospitable" of all the houses Marianne Foyster inhabited, with very primitive facilities — reportedly lacking running water and possessing only a single outside lavatory.

> **Contradiction:** This grim picture sharply contrasts with the memories Marianne passed to her American son, [[vincent-oneil|Vincent O'Neil Jr.]] She repeatedly described Dairy Cottage as a "beautiful place" she loved — an idyllic home filled with flowers, chickens, a cow, an arbor, and a fishpond.

Wood: "A visit to Dairy Cottage will convince the reader that Marianne was trying to hide herself."

## Occupants and Events

The household at Dairy Cottage during 1941–1945 included an extraordinarily complex and deceptive domestic arrangement:

- [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] (living as "Mrs Fisher")
- [[henry-fisher|Henry Francis Fisher]] (as apparent husband; often away working in a munitions factory)
- [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] (crippled by arthritis and completely bedridden, passed off to neighbours and the children as Marianne's elderly father or godfather)
- [[john-knights|John Fisher]] (adopted son; sent to boarding school, St Joseph's RC in Ipswich, returning at weekends)
- [[astrid-fisher|Astrid Fisher]] (adopted daughter; mostly at Braintree convent, occasionally visiting)
- Two evacuees from Bromley, Kent: David and Stewart Downing (see [[sergeant-downing]])
- [[robert-oneil|Robert O'Neil]] (GI, frequent visitor from Parham airfield; became Marianne's lover and eventually her third husband)

Adelaide Foyster had "lived away from home for some time" by this point. John Fisher's memoir records that Marianne treated the children with sadistic cruelty, and he found his only refuge in the room of the bedridden Rev. Foyster, whom he would read to, write letters for, and feed with an invalid cup.

## Foyster's Death

[[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] died at Dairy Cottage. His death certificate was signed by [[kjt-keer|K.J.T. Keer MRCS]], Wickham Market, with causes of death given as "exhaustion, bedsores, and rheumatoid arthritis."

> **Contradiction:** Wood consulted several doctors who all agreed these causes were medically insufficient — bedsores may cause fatal septicaemia but are not themselves a cause of death. Wood heavily insinuated that Foyster may have died from deliberate neglect or foul play, and speculated that Marianne might have blackmailed the doctor into signing a questionable certificate to avoid an inquest. See [[death-of-lionel-foyster]] for full analysis.

> **Contradiction:** Sources contradict each other on the exact date of Foyster's death. One records it as **18 April 1945**; another places it eight days later on **26 April 1945**.

Foyster was buried at [[campsey-ashe|Campsea Ashe]] churchyard, where his stone reads: *"Lionel Algernon Foyster, Priest. 1878–1945. The Lord is my Shepherd."*

## The GI Connection

The nearby **barracks at Rendlesham Park** housed American servicemen, and Robert O'Neil was billeted at Parham (Framlingham) with the **390th Heavy Bombardment Group**. He came to Dairy Cottage ostensibly to help when Foyster was ill; the relationship with Marianne developed from there. John Fisher confirms O'Neil was a frequent visitor, liberal with sweets for the children and beer for Marianne (he installed a barrel in the larder).

## Identity Deception

Local history records show how completely Marianne managed to deceive the community at Rendlesham. Former neighbours interviewed in the 1950s had no idea "Mr Fisher" was part of a bigamous marriage, nor did they know the bedridden old man was actually Marianne's legal husband; they entirely believed her story that she was "Mrs Fisher" and the invalid was her retired clergyman father.

## Aftermath

In late September 1945 Marianne moved to 1 Deben Avenue, Martlesham. She had already married O'Neil (11 August 1945) and was planning her departure to America. When Henry Fisher arrived at the cottage after Marianne's disappearance, he found it completely empty and never saw his family again.
