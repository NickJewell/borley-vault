# Amherst, Nova Scotia

Amherst is a small town in Nova Scotia, Canada, approximately five miles from [[sackville-new-brunswick|Sackville]], where [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]] was rector from 1928 to 1930, immediately before he and [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] moved to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

## The Great Amherst Mystery

Amherst is famous as the site of the [[great-amherst-mystery|Great Amherst Mystery]] poltergeist case of 1878–79, centred on [[esther-cox|Esther Cox]], then living with her sister [[daniel-and-olive-teed|Olive and Daniel Teed]] at Princess Street. The phenomena — violent poltergeist activity lasting fourteen months, wall-writings addressed to Esther, small fires, and claims of injury — were commercially exploited by [[walter-hubbell|Walter Hubbell]] in a bestselling book that sold over 55,000 copies.

[[dr-walter-prince|Dr Walter F. Prince]] subjected Hubbell's account to rigorous critical analysis in 1919 and demonstrated extensive embellishment compared with original testimony.

## Relevance to Borley

The SPR's 1956 investigators drew eighteen explicit parallels between the Amherst phenomena and those of the Foyster incumbency at Borley, and argued that it was "not unreasonable to postulate" that the Foysters would have known Hubbell's book, given:

1. Lionel Foyster was rector of Sackville, five miles from Amherst, for two years immediately before moving to Borley
2. The book had sold tens of thousands of copies in multiple editions
3. Foyster used the pseudonym "Teed" (the unusual surname of Esther Cox's hosts) in his manuscript *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*

The proximity of Amherst to Sackville is central to the SPR's suggestion that Marianne Foyster may have used the Amherst case as a "textbook" when producing the Borley phenomena.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

Based on the provided Borley Rectory sources, the events in Amherst, Nova Scotia—commonly referred to as the "Great Amherst Mystery"—are frequently cited due to their striking similarities to the phenomena later reported by the Rev. Lionel Foyster and his wife Marianne at Borley. Foyster’s previous parish of Sackville was located just across the provincial border from Amherst [1, 2].

Here are the concrete details recorded about Amherst in the sources, formatted for a reference article:

### **Physical Description of the Location**
*   **The Town:** In the late 1870s, Amherst, Nova Scotia, was a small town with a population of approximately 3,500 people [3]. 
*   **The House:** The center of the disturbances was Number 6, Princess Street. It is described as a neat, bright, and clean two-story wooden house painted yellow, with bright geraniums in the window [3, 4]. 
*   **Layout:** The ground floor consisted of an entrance hall and four rooms, while the first floor contained four bedrooms [4].

### **Persons Associated with the Case**
*   **The Teed/Cox Household:** The house belonged to Daniel Teed, a God-fearing 35-year-old foreman/shoemaker at the Amherst Boot and Shoe Factory, and his wife, Olive [4, 5]. They shared the home with their two small sons, Daniel's brother John, Olive's brother William, and Olive's two unmarried sisters, Jean (21) and Esther Cox (18 or 19). The entire household was composed of devout, active members of the Wesleyan Methodist Church [4, 5].
*   **Esther Cox:** The primary focus of the phenomena, described variously as a "simple-hearted village girl" and "dark and sullen" [5, 6].
*   **Bob McNeill:** A young shoe factory worker courting Esther [5].
*   **Walter Hubbell:** An actor and investigator who documented the case in his highly successful book, *The Haunted House: A True Ghost Story... The Great Amherst Mystery* (1879), which sold over 55,000 copies and never went out of print [7, 8].
*   **Dr. Walter F. Prince:** A psychical researcher who later published a highly critical analysis of the case in the *Proceedings of the American Society for Psychical Research* (1919) [9].

### **Key Events (September 1878 – July 1879)**
The phenomena, which lasted between 10 and 14 months, were classified as a rare combination of apparitions and exceedingly violent objective poltergeist activity [4, 10]. 
*   **The Inciting Incident:** The outbreak was allegedly triggered by a severe emotional disturbance. On August 28, 1878, Bob McNeill took Esther for a carriage ride into the woods, where he reportedly threatened her with a pistol and attempted to sexually assault her. McNeill fled town the next day and was never seen again [5, 11].
*   **Physical and Poltergeist Activity:** The phenomena peaked in cycles every 28 days (matching Esther's menstrual cycle) [8]. Esther suffered sudden attacks of ill health, and on one occasion "visibly swelled" in the presence of five people [3, 12]. Heavy pounding was heard from the roof, loud raps and taps occurred, and furniture was repeatedly upset [3, 6, 13]. 
*   **Object Movement and Apparitions:** Household objects were transported or thrown violently [13]. Much of the activity centered on Esther's bed; bedclothes were inexplicably repositioned, mattresses moved, and occupants were tipped out onto the floor [6, 14]. Esther claimed to see apparitions that were invisible to anyone else, and she was physically injured by the phenomena, though never seriously [10, 15].
*   **Fires and Wall-Writings:** Small outbreaks of fire occurred in the house but were always extinguished before causing significant damage [14]. Sensational, threatening messages addressed directly to Esther were scrawled on the walls, including the phrase: "Esther Cox, you are mine to kill!" [14, 16].
*   **Conclusion of the Events:** The activity stopped entirely when Esther was absent from the house for several weeks [12]. Ultimately, Esther was tried, convicted, and sentenced to four months in prison as a thief and an incendiary [10]. The supernatural manifestations purportedly ceased abruptly following the "incantations and conjurations of an Indian doctor" [10]. Esther retired to a quiet married life, dying in poverty in 1912, and refused to speak of the events out of fear they would return [1, 8].

### **Contradictions and Discrepancies Between Accounts**
*   **Authenticity and Embellishment:** While Walter Hubbell promoted his bestselling book as a "True Ghost Story," Esther's sister, Olive Teed, publicly claimed that Hubbell had heavily dramatised and embellished the events [8]. This was later scientifically supported by Dr. Walter F. Prince, whose 1919 study demonstrated that Hubbell's published account was greatly exaggerated when compared to an earlier, shorter diary of the events. Dr. Prince concluded the mystery was a case of trickery carried out by Esther while she was in a state of psychological dissociation [9, 17]. 
*   **Geographic Discrepancies in the Borley Literature:** In using the Amherst case to explain the later phenomena at Borley, critics (such as the authors of the *Borley Report*) frequently noted that Amherst was just "five miles" from Sackville, where Lionel Foyster lived [2]. However, later commentators pointed out that Amherst and Sackville are in different provinces (Nova Scotia and New Brunswick, respectively) and that the distance between them is "nearer ten rather than five miles 'as the crow flies'" [18].
