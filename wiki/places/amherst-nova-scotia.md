# Amherst, Nova Scotia

Amherst is a small town in Nova Scotia, Canada — population approximately 3,500 in the late 1870s — approximately five miles (or nearer ten, as the crow flies) across the provincial border from [[sackville-new-brunswick|Sackville, New Brunswick]], where [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]] was rector from 1928 to 1930, immediately before he and [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] moved to [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]].

## The Great Amherst Mystery

Amherst is famous as the site of the [[great-amherst-mystery|Great Amherst Mystery]] poltergeist case of 1878–79, centred on [[esther-cox|Esther Cox]], then living with her sister [[daniel-and-olive-teed|Olive and Daniel Teed]] at Number 6, Princess Street — a neat, bright, clean two-storey wooden house painted yellow, with bright geraniums in the window. The ground floor consisted of an entrance hall and four rooms; the first floor contained four bedrooms.

### The Household

The house belonged to Daniel Teed, a 35-year-old foreman at the Amherst Boot and Shoe Factory, and his wife Olive [4, 5]. They shared the home with their two small sons, Daniel's brother John, Olive's brother William, and Olive's two unmarried sisters — Jean (21) and [[esther-cox|Esther Cox]] (18 or 19). The entire household were devout, active members of the Wesleyan Methodist Church [4, 5].

### The Phenomena (September 1878 – July 1879)

The outbreak was allegedly triggered by a severe emotional disturbance: on August 28, 1878, a young shoe factory worker named Bob McNeill took Esther for a carriage ride into the woods, where he reportedly threatened her with a pistol and attempted to sexually assault her. McNeill fled town the next day and was never seen again [5, 11].

The phenomena lasted between ten and fourteen months and were classified as a rare combination of apparitions and exceedingly violent objective [[poltergeist-activity|poltergeist activity]] [4, 10]. They peaked in cycles every 28 days, reportedly matching Esther's menstrual cycle [8]. Esther suffered sudden attacks of ill health and on one occasion "visibly swelled" in the presence of five people [3, 12]. Heavy pounding was heard from the roof; loud raps and taps occurred; furniture was repeatedly upset [3, 6, 13]. Household objects were transported or thrown violently [13], and much of the activity centred on Esther's bed — bedclothes were inexplicably repositioned, mattresses moved, and occupants tipped onto the floor [6, 14].

Small outbreaks of fire occurred throughout but were always extinguished before causing significant damage [14]. Sensational, threatening messages addressed directly to Esther were scrawled on the walls, including the phrase: *"Esther Cox, you are mine to kill!"* [14, 16]. Esther claimed to see apparitions invisible to anyone else and was physically injured by the phenomena, though never seriously [10, 15].

The activity stopped entirely when Esther was absent from the house for several weeks [12]. Ultimately, Esther was tried, convicted, and sentenced to four months in prison as a thief and an incendiary [10]. The supernatural manifestations purportedly ceased following "the incantations and conjurations of an Indian doctor" [10]. Esther retired to a quiet married life, dying in poverty in 1912, refusing to speak of the events out of fear they would return [1, 8].

The case was commercially exploited by actor and investigator [[walter-hubbell|Walter Hubbell]] in a bestselling book — *The Haunted House: A True Ghost Story... The Great Amherst Mystery* (1879) — which sold over 55,000 copies and never went out of print [7, 8].

### Authenticity and Embellishment

> **Contradiction:** While Hubbell promoted his book as a "True Ghost Story," Esther's sister Olive Teed publicly claimed he had heavily dramatised and embellished the events [8]. This was later supported by [[walter-prince|Dr Walter F. Prince]], whose 1919 study in the *Proceedings of the American Society for Psychical Research* demonstrated that Hubbell's published account was greatly exaggerated when compared to an earlier, shorter diary. Prince concluded the mystery was a case of trickery carried out by Esther while she was in a state of psychological dissociation [9, 17].

## Relevance to Borley

The SPR's 1956 investigators drew eighteen explicit parallels between the Amherst phenomena and those of the Foyster incumbency at [[borley-rectory|Borley]], and argued that it was "not unreasonable to postulate" that the Foysters would have known Hubbell's book, given:

1. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] was rector of [[sackville-new-brunswick|Sackville]] for two years immediately before moving to Borley
2. The book had sold tens of thousands of copies in multiple editions
3. Foyster used the pseudonym "Teed" (the unusual surname of [[esther-cox|Esther Cox's]] hosts) in his manuscript *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*

The proximity of Amherst to Sackville is central to the SPR's suggestion that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] may have used the Amherst case as a "textbook" when producing the [[foyster-phenomena|Borley phenomena]].

> **Contradiction:** Critics frequently cited Amherst as being just "five miles" from Sackville. Later commentators pointed out that Amherst and Sackville are in different provinces (Nova Scotia and New Brunswick, respectively) and that the distance is "nearer ten rather than five miles 'as the crow flies'" [18].
