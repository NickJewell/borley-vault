# Borley Place

A substantial historical building immediately next to [[borley-church]], on the opposite side of the road from the former [[borley-rectory]] site. Babbs and Mathias consider it "a much more likely candidate for the original manor house" of Borley than Borley Mill, given its proximity to the church — a common arrangement in which lords of the manor lived close to their place of worship.

## Tunnel Connection

The **1957 Tudor tunnel** discovered under Hall Lane ran north–south under the road. Its **northern end terminated in the cellar area of Borley Place**, where there is an alcove at an appropriate point that could have been the entrance. The east wall of the Borley Place cellars shows signs of later alteration — an inner wall has been built that does not extend to the ceiling — consistent with a connection that was subsequently blocked.

The southern end of the tunnel led to the farmyard across the road, which Babbs and Mathias propose was the site of a former house (shown on a 1772 map).

The tunnel's dimensions (Tudor bricks, 32" wide, 28" arched height) made it too elaborate for drainage but too small for comfortable passage — consistent with emergency or secret use.

## Crypt Connection

The western wall of the [[borley-church-crypt|church crypt]] (discovered 1988) shows signs of later brickwork alteration similar to those seen in the Borley Place cellars. Babbs and Mathias propose the crypt, Borley Place, and the former house across the road were all connected underground — forming a secret network used for Catholic Mass during the Reformation.

## The Farrance Tunnel Discovery

A labourer named Farrance, working in the front garden of Borley Place during [[harry-bull|Rev Harry Bull's]] incumbency (early 20th century), broke into another section of tunnel while repairing a wall. He attempted to explore it but was driven back by foul air. The 1957 contractors encountered no such problem when they broke into the same tunnel system.

## Payne Family

In 1936, Borley Place was occupied by Mr and Mrs Basil Payne — "a well-known local landowner, his family continuing to be farmers in the Borley area." The Hennings initially stayed with the Paynes before moving to Liston Rectory. A later dispute about a right-of-way caused the Paynes to cease attending Borley church for the remainder of Henning's incumbency.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Physical Description and Location**
**Borley Place** is an historic building situated at the top of Hall Lane, practically adjoining Borley Church and lying directly across the road from the former site of Borley Rectory [1, 2]. Described as a picturesque, delightful, and comfortable country house [3], the property presents a seventeenth-century façade, though its core structure may date back as far as the thirteenth or fourteenth century [1]. Other sources describe it as a fine sixteenth-century house [2]. 

A notable external architectural feature is a "false" (dummy) first-storey window that overlooks the churchyard [2, 4]. Beneath the house are extensive, very old cellars; the east wall of these cellars (adjacent to the church) shows signs of later alterations, including an inner wall that does not quite reach the cellar ceiling [5]. In 2007, Borley Place was put up for sale for just under one million pounds [6].

**Associated Persons**
Throughout its history, Borley Place has served as a farmhouse, a manor house (often referred to as Borley Manor), and the official or temporary residence for several Borley rectors and local figures [1, 7]:
*   **The Rev. John P. Herringham:** Resided at Borley Place in his later years prior to his death in 1862 [8, 9].
*   **The Rev. Henry D. E. Bull:** Lived at Borley Place upon his induction to the living in 1862 while the old Herringham rectory was demolished and his massive new Borley Rectory was being constructed across the road [2, 10].
*   **The Rev. Harry Foyster Bull & Ivy Brackenbury:** Harry Bull and his new wife made Borley Place their home upon their marriage in 1911, residing there until 1920 when they finally moved into the main Rectory [2, 10].
*   **The Payne Family & The Biggs:** The house, along with the Borley estate and Borley Hall, was owned by the Payne family [10, 11]. It was leased for a time to a farmer, William Bigg, during the Smith and Foyster incumbencies [11, 12]. Later in the 1930s, Mr. and Mrs. Basil Payne lived there [13, 14].
*   **The Rev. Alfred C. Henning:** After finding the main Rectory too vast and impractical in 1936, the Henning family was given temporary accommodation at Borley Place for nine months before permanently moving to Liston Rectory [10].

**Key Events**
Borley Place was never reputed to be haunted; Harry Price explicitly noted that no "manifestations" were ever experienced inside the house [3]. However, it played a peripheral role in the Borley Rectory saga:
*   **An Observation Point:** Because it directly faced the Rectory, Borley Place served as a vantage point for witnesses. In 1937, Mr. and Mrs. Basil Payne claimed to have witnessed unexplained lights in the Rectory windows from the house [14]. Harry Price and his colleague Ellic Howe were entertained for tea at Borley Place by the Paynes on June 2, 1937, right before beginning their year-long tenancy investigation at the Rectory [3, 14]. At the time of the fire that destroyed Borley Rectory in February 1939, the Rev. Henning and his wife were staying at Borley Place [15]. 
*   **The Tunnel Discoveries:** Borley Place is heavily linked to the local legends of subterranean tunnels. During Harry Bull's time, a local labourer named Farrance broke through brickwork while repairing a well in the grounds of Borley Place and discovered a high, dome-shaped tunnel [16, 17]. Later, in September 1957, workmen digging a trench in Hall Lane outside the property broke into a well-constructed Tudor brick tunnel [5, 18]. Investigators Len Sewell and L.G. Rayner examined it and concluded its path passed into the front garden and terminated in a specific alcove within the cellars of Borley Place [18, 19].

**Contradictions and Proposed Explanations**
*   **Historical Status:** While often referred to in historical records as a farmhouse or manor house [1], the 1841 tithe map actually designates Borley Place as being a Rectory itself [20]. This clarifies why multiple clergymen, such as Herringham and the Bulls, utilized it as their official residence. 
*   **Architectural Age:** The sources contain minor discrepancies regarding its exact age, describing it variously as a sixteenth-century house [2], an "ancient" house [21], or a building with a seventeenth-century facade but thirteenth/fourteenth-century bones [1].
*   **The Purpose of the Tunnels:** The function of the tunnels connected to Borley Place is highly disputed. Some investigators theorized the tunnels were a secret means of escape built during the Reformation for the devoutly Catholic Waldegrave family, linking the cellars of Borley Place to the church crypt or to an older building on the Rectory site [5, 19]. Harry Price recorded rumours that the tunnel entrance in the farmyard of Borley Place might be a branch of the legendary 7-mile tunnel leading to Bures [17, 21]. Conversely, skeptics and a report from the *Suffolk Free Press* in 1866 suggest that these "tunnels" were simply large, old drainage culverts or sewers [18].
