# Borley Place

A substantial historical building immediately next to [[borley-church]], on the opposite side of the road from the former [[borley-rectory]] site. Babbs and Mathias consider it "a much more likely candidate for the original manor house" of Borley than Borley Mill, given its proximity to the church — a common arrangement in which lords of the manor lived close to their place of worship.

## Physical Description

Borley Place is described as a picturesque and comfortable country house, situated at the top of Hall Lane practically adjoining Borley Church and lying directly across the road from the former Rectory site. The property presents a seventeenth-century façade, though its core structure may date back as far as the thirteenth or fourteenth century; some sources describe it as a fine sixteenth-century house [2].

A notable external feature is a **"false" (dummy) first-storey window** that overlooks the churchyard. Beneath the house are extensive, very old cellars; the east wall of these cellars (adjacent to the church) shows signs of later alteration — an inner wall has been built that does not extend to the cellar ceiling. In 2007, Borley Place was put up for sale for just under one million pounds.

> **Contradiction:** The 1841 tithe map actually designates Borley Place as being a *rectory*, clarifying why multiple clergymen — including the Herringhams and the Bulls — utilized it as their official residence, rather than merely as a farmhouse or manor house as it is described in many historical records.

> **Contradiction:** Sources offer minor discrepancies on the building's age, variously describing it as a sixteenth-century house, an "ancient" house, or a building with a seventeenth-century facade but thirteenth/fourteenth-century structural bones.

## Associated Persons

Throughout its history, Borley Place has served as a farmhouse, manor house, and official or temporary residence for several Borley rectors:

- **The Rev. John P. [[john-herringham|Herringham]]:** Resided at Borley Place in his later years prior to his death in 1862.
- **The Rev. [[henry-bull|Henry D. E. Bull]]:** Lived at Borley Place upon his induction in 1862 while the old Herringham rectory was demolished and his new Borley Rectory was being constructed across the road.
- **The Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Foyster Bull]] and [[ivy-brackenbury|Ivy Brackenbury]]:** Made Borley Place their home upon their marriage in 1911, residing there until 1920 when they finally moved into the main Rectory.
- **The Payne Family and William Bigg:** The house and the Borley estate were owned by the Payne family. It was leased for a time to a farmer, William Bigg, during the Smith and Foyster incumbencies. In 1936, Mr and Mrs Basil Payne lived there — "a well-known local landowner, his family continuing to be farmers in the Borley area."
- **The Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|Alfred C. Henning]]:** After finding the main Rectory too vast and impractical in 1936, the Henning family was given temporary accommodation at Borley Place for nine months before permanently moving to Liston Rectory.

## Key Events

Borley Place was never reputed to be haunted; [[harry-price|Harry Price]] explicitly noted that no "manifestations" were ever experienced inside the house. However, it played a peripheral role in the saga:

- **June 2, 1937:** Price and [[ellic-howe|Ellic Howe]] were entertained for tea at Borley Place by the Paynes right before beginning their year-long tenancy investigation. Mr and Mrs Basil Payne claimed to have witnessed unexplained lights in the Rectory windows from the house.
- **February 1939:** At the time of the [[fire-1939|fire]] that destroyed Borley Rectory, the Rev. Henning and his wife were staying at Borley Place.

## Tunnel Connection

The **1957 Tudor tunnel** discovered under Hall Lane ran north–south under the road. Its **northern end terminated in the cellar area of Borley Place**, where there is an alcove at an appropriate point that could have been the entrance. The east wall of the Borley Place cellars shows signs of later alteration — an inner wall has been built that does not extend to the ceiling — consistent with a connection that was subsequently blocked. Investigators Len Sewell and L.G. Rayner examined the tunnel and concluded its path passed into the front garden and terminated in a specific alcove within the cellars.

The southern end of the tunnel led to the farmyard across the road, which Babbs and Mathias propose was the site of a former house (shown on a 1772 map). The tunnel's dimensions — Tudor bricks, 32" wide, 28" arched height — made it too elaborate for drainage but too small for comfortable passage, consistent with emergency or secret use.

During [[harry-bull|Rev Harry Bull's]] incumbency, a local labourer named Farrance broke into an earlier section of tunnel while repairing a wall in the grounds of Borley Place, discovering a high, dome-shaped tunnel. He attempted to explore it but was driven back by foul air. The 1957 contractors encountered no such problem when they broke into the same tunnel system.

## Crypt Connection

The western wall of the [[borley-church-crypt|church crypt]] (discovered 1988) shows signs of later brickwork alteration similar to those seen in the Borley Place cellars. Babbs and Mathias propose the crypt, Borley Place, and the former house across the road were all connected underground — forming a secret network used for **Catholic Mass during the Reformation**, when the devoutly Catholic [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]] could not legally hold services. This theory is disputed: skeptics and a report from the *Suffolk Free Press* in 1866 suggest these "tunnels" were simply large, old drainage culverts or sewers. Harry Price recorded rumours that the tunnel entrance in the farmyard might be a branch of the legendary 7-mile tunnel leading to Bures — largely dismissed as folklore.
