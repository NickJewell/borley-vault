# Jamestown, North Dakota

An isolated prairie town in North Dakota, described as "a long way from anywhere else," with a population of approximately 7,000. The nearest towns of any size are Fargo, 90 miles to the east, and the state capital Bismarck, 100 miles to the west. The town experiences severe, freezing winter weather with deep, hard-settled snow. [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] worked here as a reporter for the **Jamestown Sun** newspaper, and it is the town where [[robert-swanson|A. Robert Swanson]] — the American private investigator hired by [[eileen-garrett|Eileen Garrett]]'s Parapsychological Foundation — found her in **February 1958**.

## Marianne at Jamestown

Marianne moved to Jamestown after the collapse of the [[hokah-minnesota|Hokah]] household. Desperate to make ends meet, she traveled 500 miles to Jamestown and secured a job as a reporter and assistant editor for the *Jamestown Sun* after an interview with the paper's editor, Mr. Hansen. Her salary was $45 a week. O'Neil later joined her and worked briefly as a plumber, though his career was "blighted by his general dislike of work and his heavy drinking." Marianne ended up supporting not only the child, but also her hopeless husband, on her meagre salary.

Specific locations in the town include the **Gladstone Hotel** (the largest hotel in Jamestown, where Marianne was later interviewed) and a residence called **"Four Acres"** where the family had to struggle to keep wood-stove fires burning during the cold weather.

In Jamestown Marianne was well-liked and apparently competent at her work. Wood spoke to several people who knew her: "there was no hint of scandal. No one remembered the drunken husband at all." Locals did not remember O'Neil well; he "kept his head down."

> **Contradiction:** While carefully concealing her true past at Borley, Marianne engaged in what Wood calls "Munchausening" — entertaining the local ladies' club with dramatic first-hand accounts of hair's-breadth escapes during the London Blitz. In fact, during the Blitz she was living safely in rural Suffolk or, as Wood puts it, "having trances in the lounge of the [[grosvenor-hotel-victoria|Grosvenor Hotel]]." She also falsely maintained to Jamestown acquaintances that she was the daughter of a doctor from Maine.

## The Swanson Interviews

In **February 1958** Swanson arrived at Marianne's door. Swanson was a tough American private investigator hired by the Parapsychology Foundation of New York — prompted by psychical researcher [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] in England — to track down and interview Marianne. The interviews were conducted at the **Gladstone Hotel, Jamestown**, and tape-recorded. It is from these transcripts — extensively quoted throughout Wood's book — that much of the direct evidence about Marianne's activities in England comes.

> "At first, she refused to discuss anything with Swanson, but she later relented and, at the Gladstone Hotel, Jamestown, gave the interviews (tape-recorded by Swanson) which have been extensively quoted throughout this book."

The discovery that [[ian-greenwood|Ian Shaw]] had warned her about Swanson in advance did not prevent her being caught off-guard. Her early responses were evasive but she gradually gave ground when Swanson produced documentary evidence she could not deny. During these sessions, Marianne dismissed the vast majority of the Borley phenomena as either natural occurrences or children's pranks, though she admitted that certain rooms felt "unhappy" and that inexplicable temperature drops occurred.

During one interview, Marianne was asked about books being knocked down at [[borley-rectory|Borley]]. She mentioned a very recent incident at her Jamestown home where a pile of books had suddenly fallen with "quite a clatter" — though she pragmatically attributed this to a tilted bookshelf rather than anything paranormal.

## After Jamestown

The arrival of the private investigator deeply unsettled Marianne, making her fear that her history — and her falsified official records — would be exposed in such a small town. She moved to [[fargo-north-dakota|Fargo]] in autumn 1958, then on to [[la-crosse-wisconsin|La Crosse]] in 1963.
