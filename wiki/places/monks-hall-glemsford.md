# Monks Hall, Glemsford

A historic house in Glemsford, the parish two miles across the Stour valley from [[borley-rectory]], with a long-established local legend of a former monastery, a ghost monk, and a secret tunnel to the church. [[andrew-clarke]] identifies Monks Hall as the probable direct source from which the Bull family borrowed Borley's own tunnel and monk ghost legends.

## Description

Clarke describes the building in essay 17 ("Tunnels!"):

> *Monks Hall is a lovely house, and a beautiful setting, overlooking the Stour valley opposite Borley. The house itself is a far more suitable setting for ghost stories than Borley Rectory as it is undeniably old.*

The house is described in Rev Kenneth Glass's *A Short History of Glemsford* (1967) as an "undeniably old" property likely erected in the 16th century upon a more ancient site, featuring ancient roofs and chimneys that give it "an air of enchantment and mystery" [1, 2]:

> *MONKS HALL — It is probable that the present fascinating building was erected in the 16th Century upon an ancient site. There are many stories of this house most suggesting that it was at one time a monastery, and it is said that a secret tunnel linking it with the Church still exists. Several ghost stories are told of this area and from them it appears that a ghostly figure of a monk walks the paths near his ancient haunts.*

## The Tunnel Legend

A prominent legend held that a secret tunnel linked Monks Hall with the local church, used during the Reformation to enable monks to worship in the church undetected [1]. The Glemsford tunnel legend was actively maintained in living local memory. Clarke notes that "the church tower was used as a lookout post during the war and it is said that the tunnel's entrance was used as a shelter in case of air attack." A stone slab outside the West Door was said to mark the tunnel entrance, and local people claimed the tunnel could still partly be traced across the field between hall and church.

The legend was taught in the local school as part of local history lessons [1, 3] — making it widely known to the [[henry-bull|Bull family]] who lived and preached throughout the Stour Valley parishes. The tunnel was "almost certainly fictional": there was never a monastery at Monks Hall, and no evidence of a physical tunnel has been found [1].

## Source of the Borley Legends

Clarke's argument is that the Bull sisters' story of a monastery at Borley connected by a tunnel to a convent at Bures was lifted wholesale from the Glemsford legend:

> *This story seems to have been lifted by the Bull girls wholesale from the next parish in the same way that the coach with its headless coachmen came from the neighbouring Acton.*

The Glemsford legend was far stronger than anything at Borley: it had specific physical locations, living witnesses who claimed to have seen the tunnel entrance, schoolroom teaching, and decades of accumulated story. The Bull family visited Glemsford regularly as it was "the next parish" across the Stour. Critically, while the tunnel and monk legends were widely known and believed by Glemsford residents, similar legends regarding [[borley-rectory]] could not be found among Borley locals themselves [1].

## Comparison with Other Borrowed Legends

Clarke identifies three main borrowed elements in the [[borley-legend-origins|Borley legend]]:
1. **Headless coachmen** — from the Acton, Suffolk legend (attested in *Bury and Norwich Post*, March 1852)
2. **Monastery/tunnel** — from the Monks Hall, Glemsford legend
3. **Nun's Walk** — the name borrowed from the driveway at Great Yeldham rectory, where the Bulls preached

See also: [[borley-legend-origins]], [[tunnels]], [[borley-legends]]

> **Note:** The Glemsford skeletons discovered near Glemsford bridge in 1851 (a male and young female, buried east-west) also contributed to the monastery-and-forbidden-romance narrative. Clarke suggests their discovery — and the local press reporting of it — was the immediate catalyst for the monastery legend being associated with a "monk and nun" romantic tragedy.
