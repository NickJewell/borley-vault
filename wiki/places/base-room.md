# Base Room

Name given by [[harry-price]] to the former library at [[borley-rectory]] that was used as his centre of operations during the [[price-tenancy]] of 1937–1938. The term appears throughout his Borley books and his observers' documentation.

## Location

The Base Room was located on the ground floor at the south-east corner of the Rectory — Room 2 on the [[floor-plans|Glanville floor plans]]. The French doors of the library opened onto the glass-roofed verandah, providing easy access to the grounds and a vantage point for keeping the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] under observation. The room was located directly off the main stairwell — giving quick access to the upper floors — and the haunted [[blue-room]] was immediately above.

## Setup and Equipment

Price equipped the Base Room with:
- A folding camp bed with pillow, mattress and blankets
- A brass paraffin table lamp and spirit kettle with supplies
- Candles and matches
- Cutlery, plates, cups and a teapot (from Woolworths)
- Supplies of tea, sugar and condensed milk
- A table and two wooden benches already present
- Twenty or so books unrelated to ghost hunting
- Later, an oil stove for the colder months

[[sidney-glanville]]'s scaled floor plans were pinned up over the mantelpiece to aid observers.

## Iconic Photograph

One of the most iconic photographs of the Borley investigation was taken by Sidney Glanville in November 1937 and published in *MHH* in 1940 — it shows his son [[roger-glanville]] resting on the camp bed in the Base Room. The original print in the [[borley-dossier|Borley Dossier]] at the University of London includes more of the room than the cropped version usually published.

## Phenomena Reported

During the year-long investigation, several incidents of apparent phenomena were reported from the Base Room or in close proximity:
- Movement of a coal bag (50 lb) in the passageway outside (21 September 1937)
- Shuffling footsteps heard outside the Base Room door (25 October 1937)
- A door-locking incident (26 October 1937 — lock appeared to turn by itself with audible click)
- Footsteps heard inside the Base Room itself (28 October 1937)
- The [[swinging-blind-incident|swinging blind incident]] (20 November 1937)

The [[strange-coat-incident|strange coat]] found in the Rectory was also connected to a table-turning experiment in the Base Room when [[mark-kerr-pearse]] and the Hennings heard extraordinary footsteps coming down the kitchen passage.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**The Base Room**, originally the library or study of Borley Rectory, served as the central operational headquarters for Harry Price and his team of independent observers during his year-long tenancy investigation from May 1937 to May 1938 [1-3]. 

### **Physical Description and Location**
*   **Location:** The Base Room was situated on the ground floor of the Rectory, directly off the main hallway and opposite the main staircase [4, 5]. It was positioned immediately beneath the notoriously haunted "Blue Room" [2, 6]. 
*   **Access:** The room featured French doors that opened outward onto a glass-roofed verandah, providing observers with an excellent vantage point and direct access to the upper lawn, the large summer-house, and the "Nun's Walk" [2, 5, 7].
*   **Furnishings:** When Price and his associate Ellic Howe established the Base Room on June 2, 1937, the Rectory was completely unfurnished. They outfitted the room like a camper's base, supplying it with a folding metal camp bed, mattress, blankets, a brass paraffin table lamp, a spirit kettle, basic crockery, and provisions like tea and condensed milk [8-10]. They also utilized a large wooden table and some school benches found in the garage [11]. For the colder months, the observers relied on the room's open fireplace for warmth [8]. 
*   **Decor:** To assist the observers in locating phenomena, copies of scaled floor plans drawn by Sidney Glanville were pinned to the wall above the mantelpiece [12]. Price also provided about twenty "readable books"—none of which were on psychical research—to help the observers pass the time while off duty [8, 10].

### **Associated Persons**
The room was historically utilized as a study by the successive Borley Rectors, including the Rev. Henry Bull, Rev. Harry Bull, Rev. Guy Smith, and Rev. Lionel Foyster [2, 4, 5]. During the 1937–1938 investigation, it was occupied by Price, Ellic Howe, and various **official observers** including Sidney Glanville, Roger Glanville, Mark Kerr-Pearse, Alan Cuthbert, Dr. H. F. Bellamy, Capt. H. G. Harrison, and Rupert Haig [2, 13-15]. 

### **Key Events and Phenomena**
Due to its role as the primary waiting area for investigators, numerous instances of alleged paranormal activity were reported within or immediately outside the Base Room:
*   **June 2, 1937:** While Price and Howe were resting in the newly established Base Room, they experienced a sequence of short, sharp taps from the passage outside, followed fifteen minutes later by two loud "thumps" and the sound of an upstairs door slamming [16-18].
*   **June 28, 1937:** Observer Mark Kerr-Pearse placed a screw inside a chalk control ring on the Base Room mantelpiece. Upon returning five minutes later, he found the screw had moved completely outside the chalk circle, despite being alone in the building [19, 20]. 
*   **September 21, 1937:** Kerr-Pearse and his cousin Rupert Haig noted that a **50-pound bag of coal had moved 18 inches** along the passageway outside the Base Room door, its original placement verified by a floor stain [13, 19, 21].
*   **September 22, 1937:** While resting in the Base Room, Rupert Haig was awakened by an icy draught playing over his face. He noted this was highly unusual as all doors and windows were closed, a fire had been burning in the grate for twelve hours, and the room thermometer read a comfortable 60°F [22, 23].
*   **October 24, 1937:** Sidney Glanville, Roger Glanville, and Mark Kerr-Pearse were sitting in the dark on the landing above when they distinctly heard **"heavy muffled footsteps"** and the sounds of a body moving across the hall directly below them. Suspecting it was their colleague Alan Cuthbert, they rushed down immediately but found Cuthbert sound asleep in the Base Room [24-26].
*   **October 26, 1937:** While finishing his supper in the Base Room, Kerr-Pearse heard a distinct click. When he went to leave the room seconds later, he found **he had been locked inside.** Remarkably, the key was on the *inside* of the lock with him, meaning whatever turned the key must have been inside the room [13, 24, 27, 28]. 
*   **November 20, 1937 (The Swinging Blind Incident):** Sidney Glanville, Roger Glanville, Capt. Harrison, and Dr. Bellamy observed the Base Room window blind moving with a regular "palpitating action." [13, 15, 29]. 
*   **March 12, 1938:** Observers M. Savage and Bridgewater heard an extremely heavy, sharp knock emanating from the top of the Base Room bookcase, loud enough to make them jump [30, 31].

### **Contradictions and Source Perspectives**
The events associated with the Base Room drew heavy criticism and sparked significant contradictions between skeptical reviewers and defenders of the investigation:
*   **The Cellophane Accusation:** Major Henry Douglas-Home claimed that while sitting in the Blue Room with Price, they heard a rustling noise coming from the Base Room below. Douglas-Home subsequently accused Price of fraudulently creating this "ghostly" noise by dropping a crumpled piece of cellophane paper down the hot stove flue, alleging he later found a jagged roll of cellophane in Price's suitcase [32-34]. However, researcher Robert J. Hastings fiercely defended Price, noting the physical impossibility of Price secretly shoving cellophane into a stove while sitting tightly pressed against Douglas-Home in the dark, and pointing out that cellophane naturally shrinks and burns silently when heated. Hastings also noted a perfectly innocent explanation for the cellophane in Price's bag: it was used to wrap items in his ghost-hunting kit [35-37]. (Another observer present, S.J. de Lotbinière, simply attributed the Base Room rustling to mice [33, 38]). 
*   **The Swinging Blind Incident:** The highly critical authors of the *Borley Report* (Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) dismissed the moving blind in the Base Room as the result of a simple, natural draught. They used this incident to castigate the observers, claiming they made "little real attempt" to scientifically investigate the cause [39-41]. However, Hastings explicitly pointed out a contradiction in the *Borley Report's* methodology: the SPR authors had deliberately suppressed and omitted the fact that Glanville's team *did* test for a draught by blowing a cloud of cigarette smoke between the blind and the window. The smoke did not disperse, proving to the observers that no air current was present [29, 41, 42].
