# Base Room

Name given by [[harry-price]] to the former library at [[borley-rectory]] that was used as his centre of operations during the [[price-tenancy]] of 1937–1938. The term appears throughout his Borley books and his observers' documentation.

## Location

The Base Room was located on the ground floor at the south-east corner of the Rectory — Room 2 on the [[floor-plans|Glanville floor plans]], directly off the main hallway and opposite the main staircase. The French doors of the library opened onto the glass-roofed verandah, providing easy access to the grounds and a vantage point for keeping the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] under observation. The room was located directly off the main stairwell — giving quick access to the upper floors — and the haunted [[blue-room]] was immediately above.

The room had historically served as a study for successive Borley rectors, including the Rev. [[henry-bull|Henry Bull]], Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]], Rev. [[rev-eric-smith|Guy Smith]], and Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]].

## Setup and Equipment

When Price and his associate [[ellic-howe|Ellic Howe]] established the Base Room on 2 June 1937, the Rectory was completely unfurnished. They equipped the Base Room with:

- A folding metal camp bed with pillow, mattress and blankets
- A brass paraffin table lamp and spirit kettle with supplies
- Candles and matches
- Cutlery, plates, cups and a teapot (from Woolworths)
- Supplies of tea, sugar and condensed milk
- A large table and some school benches found in the garage
- Twenty or so books unrelated to ghost hunting, to help observers pass the time
- Later, an oil stove for the colder months; for earlier months the open fireplace served

[[sidney-glanville]]'s scaled floor plans were pinned up over the mantelpiece to aid observers.

## Iconic Photograph

One of the most iconic photographs of the Borley investigation was taken by Sidney Glanville in November 1937 and published in *MHH* in 1940 — it shows his son [[roger-glanville]] resting on the camp bed in the Base Room. The original print in the [[borley-dossier|Borley Dossier]] at the University of London includes more of the room than the cropped version usually published.

## Phenomena Reported

During the year-long investigation, numerous incidents of apparent phenomena were reported from the Base Room or in close proximity (multiple witnesses):

- **2 June 1937:** Price and Howe, resting in the newly established Base Room, heard short sharp taps from the passage outside, followed fifteen minutes later by two loud thumps and the sound of an upstairs door slamming.
- **28 June 1937:** Observer [[mark-kerr-pearse|Mark Kerr-Pearse]] placed a screw inside a chalk control ring on the mantelpiece. Upon returning five minutes later, the screw had moved completely outside the chalk circle, despite his being alone in the building.
- **21 September 1937:** Kerr-Pearse and his cousin [[rupert-haig|Rupert Haig]] found that a **50-pound bag of coal had moved 18 inches** along the passageway outside the Base Room door, its original placement verified by a floor stain.
- **22 September 1937:** While resting in the Base Room, Haig was awakened by an icy draught playing over his face — unusual given all doors and windows were closed, a fire had burned for twelve hours, and the room thermometer read 60°F.
- **24–25 October 1937:** [[sidney-glanville]], [[roger-glanville]], and Kerr-Pearse heard heavy muffled footsteps and the sounds of a body moving across the hall directly below them; rushing down they found colleague [[alan-cuthbert|Alan Cuthbert]] sound asleep in the Base Room.
- **26 October 1937:** While finishing supper in the Base Room, Kerr-Pearse heard a distinct click and found **he had been locked inside**. Remarkably, the key was on the *inside* of the lock with him, meaning whatever turned the key must have been inside the room.
- **20 November 1937 (The [[swinging-blind-incident|Swinging Blind Incident]]):** Sidney Glanville, Roger Glanville, [[capt-hg-harrison|Capt. Harrison]], and [[dr-hf-bellamy|Dr Bellamy]] observed the Base Room window blind moving with a regular "palpitating action."
- **12 March 1938:** Observers M. Savage and Bridgewater heard an extremely heavy, sharp knock from the top of the Base Room bookcase, loud enough to make them jump.

The [[swinging-blind-incident|strange coat]] found in the Rectory was also connected to a table-turning experiment in the Base Room when Kerr-Pearse and the Hennings heard extraordinary footsteps coming down the kitchen passage.

## Controversies

The events associated with the Base Room drew sustained criticism from the authors of the *Borley Report* ([[eric-dingwall|Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Hall]]):

**The Cellophane Accusation:** [[major-douglas-home|Major Henry Douglas-Home]] claimed that while sitting in the [[blue-room]] with Price, they heard a rustling noise from the Base Room below. He subsequently accused Price of fraudulently creating this sound by dropping crumpled cellophane paper down the hot stove flue, alleging he later found a jagged roll of cellophane in Price's suitcase. However, [[robert-hastings|Robert Hastings]] fiercely defended Price, noting the physical impossibility of Price secretly handling cellophane while pressed tightly against Douglas-Home in the dark, and pointing out that cellophane shrinks and burns silently when heated. Hastings also noted a perfectly innocent explanation: cellophane was used to wrap items in Price's ghost-hunting kit. (Another observer present, [[sj-de-lotbiniere|S.J. de Lotbinière]], simply attributed the rustling to mice.)

**The Swinging Blind Incident:** The *Borley Report* authors dismissed the moving blind as the result of a natural draught, using the incident to castigate the observers for making "little real attempt" to investigate scientifically.

> **Contradiction:** Hastings pointed out that the SPR authors had deliberately suppressed the fact that Glanville's team *did* test for a draught by blowing a cloud of cigarette smoke between the blind and the window. The smoke did not disperse, proving to the observers that no air current was present [29, 41, 42].
