# Langenhoe

An overlooked but important case of haunting with many parallels to the events at Borley, albeit in reverse. Situated in bleak marshland near the sea, approximately 25 miles south-east of Borley and close to Colchester, Essex. The place name derives from the Anglo-Saxon for "long hill."

The village featured the Church of St Mary the Virgin and the adjacent **Langenhoe Hall**, with the rectory situated a quarter of a mile away. The rectory was sold off as a private residence in 1937, forcing the incumbent to live in the nearby village of West Mersea. The church suffered extensive damage during a freak earthquake in 1884 and received only minimal repairs; it was abandoned in the late 1950s after being found structurally unsound and was finally demolished in 1962, though the Hall and the rectory building still survive.

## Historical Connection to Borley

Langenhoe and Borley are historically linked through the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]]. In 1583, **Nicholas Waldegrave**, Lord of the Manor of Borley and son of [[sir-edward-waldegrave]], acquired the manor of Langenhoe when he married **Catherine Browne**. Because the Waldegraves served as patrons of both livings, they appointed rectors at Langenhoe as well as Borley; during the seventeenth century their chosen clergymen — including William Playne, Thomas Muriell, and Robert Morton — officiated at both churches.

The livings of Langenhoe and Borley were formally combined in 1661, again in 1680, and yet again in 1758.

## The Haunting

The church's earlier paranormal reputation rested on a **1908 sighting**: two sisters reported seeing a woman dressed in "old fashion nun-like garb" who disappeared into the north wall of the church. Local legends also spoke of a "Lady in Black" walking the church at night.

In early 1937, the **Revd Ernest Merryweather** was inducted as rector. Over a twenty-year period he experienced a range of psychic activity that increased in frequency and intensity, including:

- The great west door being violently slammed shut on still, windless days
- Objects disappearing only to be found elsewhere
- Footsteps within the church and locks on his valise refusing to open
- Voices and singing emanating from inside the empty church
- Mrs. Baines (his housekeeper), her seventeen-year-old daughter Irene, and the congregation witnessing thuds against the outside of the vestry door during Sunday services, accompanied by feelings of being watched

Merryweather's diary — covering twenty years of activity and later presented to [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] — is the primary source for the twentieth-century phenomena. Underwood also received from Merryweather a credence bell from the church that had reportedly rung repeatedly without human agency.

### Apparitions

Specific apparition experiences recorded by Merryweather include:

- **21 August 1949**: a woman in late medieval or Tudor dress crossed the west end of the church and vanished through a wall
- **8 July 1951**: a repeat apparition in the same location
- While practising at the organ: the figure of a woman in a modern cream dress
- **September 1947**: while being shown around Langenhoe Hall by the owner, Mrs. Cutting (who stated the room gave her "the creeps"), Merryweather turned to follow her out and experienced walking directly into the form of a "naked invisible woman"

Following the Hall incident, Merryweather consulted the Bishop of Colchester, who proved sympathetic — the Bishop had previously experienced a paranormal event at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] during the Foyster incumbency, when "a stone was thrown at him, narrowly missing his head before smashing into the fireplace."

> **Contradiction:** The intense paranormal activity appears to have been entirely isolated to Merryweather's tenure. His immediate predecessors — the Revd Thomas Whitton (1915–1933) and the Revd Spokes (1933–1937) — reportedly experienced no paranormal activity whatsoever during their incumbencies.

## Comparison with Borley

The BRC notes the structural reversal: at Langenhoe, the church and Hall were adjacent and the haunting concentrated there; the rectory played little part. At Borley, the Hall was a private home a mile distant and the Rectory was the locus of phenomena. The Langenhoe case thus inverts the Borley pattern while sharing its core elements — a [[the-nun|nun-like figure]], [[poltergeist-activity|poltergeist incidents]], a "Lady in Black" tradition, and phenomena concentrated around a specific ecclesiastical site historically connected to the Waldegraves.

## Investigations

[[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] took a keen interest and in September 1949, with [[john-dening|John Cranmer Dening]], spent an all-night vigil in Langenhoe church during a dramatic thunderstorm — with no positive results. A further vigil the following year likewise produced nothing. [[rev-ac-henning|Alfred Henning]], [[len-sewell|Len Sewell]], and [[steuart-kiernander|Steuart Kiernander]] also took interest. On 2 June 1958, Dening, Sewell, Kiernander, and a medium named Mrs. May Lampard held a séance inside the haunted church. Dening eventually wrote a full-length account of the haunting: *The Restless Spirits of Langenhoe* (2000).

## Current Status

Today all is quiet at Langenhoe with no reports of paranormal phenomena, and the strange events of sixty years ago seem all but forgotten in this lonely village on the marshes.
