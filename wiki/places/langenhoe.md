# Langenhoe

An overlooked but important case of haunting that has many parallels with the events that occurred at Borley, albeit reversed. Situated in bleak marshland some 25 miles south-east of Borley near Colchester, Essex.

## The Haunting

The church of St Mary the Virgin was abandoned in the late 1950s after being found structurally unsound. It had suffered extensive damage in 1884 during a freak earthquake and received only minimal repairs. It was finally demolished in 1962.

In early 1937, the **Revd Ernest Merryweather** was inducted as rector. Over a twenty-year period he experienced a range of psychic activity that increased in frequency and intensity, including:

- The great west door being violently slammed shut on a still, windless day
- Objects disappearing only to be found elsewhere
- Footsteps within the church and locks on the rector's valise refusing to open
- Voices and singing from inside the empty church
- **Apparitions**: On 21 August 1949 he saw a woman in late medieval or Tudor dress cross the west end of the church and vanish through a wall; a repeat on 8 July 1951; and while practicing at the organ, seeing the figure of a woman in a modern cream dress
- In September 1947: walking into the form of a "naked invisible woman" in Langenhoe Hall

## Historical Connection to Borley

Merryweather discovered that Langenhoe and Borley were historically connected: in 1583, **Nicholas Waldegrave**, Lord of the Manor of Borley and son of [[sir-edward-waldegrave]], acquired the manor of Langenhoe when he married **Catherine Browne**. The [[waldegrave-family|Waldegraves]], as patrons of both livings, appointed rectors at Langenhoe as well as Borley; during the seventeenth century their chosen clergymen officiated at both churches.

The livings of Langenhoe and Borley were combined in 1661, again in 1680, and yet again in 1758.

## Comparison with Borley

The BRC notes the reversal: at Langenhoe the church and Hall were adjacent and the haunting concentrated there; the rectory played little part. At Borley, the Hall was a private home a mile distant and the Rectory was the locus. Phenomena at Langenhoe also included poltergeist incidents, a "Lady in Black" tradition, and a 1908 sighting of a "woman dressed in old fashion nun-like garb."

## Investigations

[[peter-underwood]] took a keen interest and in September 1949, with [[john-dening]], spent an all-night vigil in Langenhoe church (dramatic thunderstorm; no positive results). A further vigil the following year also produced nothing. [[rev-ac-henning|Alfred Henning]], [[len-sewell]] and [[steuart-kiernander]] also took interest. Dening eventually wrote a full-length account of the haunting: *The Restless Spirits of Langenhoe* (2000).

Merryweather's diary (presented to Underwood), covering twenty years of activity, is the primary primary source. The Bishop of Colchester — who had previously experienced paranormal phenomena at Borley during the [[foyster-incumbency]] when "a stone was thrown at him, narrowly missing his head before smashing into the fireplace" — lent a sympathetic ear to Merryweather's account.

## Current Status

Today all is quiet at Langenhoe with no reports of paranormal phenomena, and the strange events of sixty years ago seem all but forgotten in this lonely village on the marshes.
