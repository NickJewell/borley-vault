# Chilton Lodge

A solid, ivy-clad Georgian building that stood in Cornard Road, Great Cornard, near [[sudbury-dominican-friary|Sudbury]], Suffolk, near to the Chilton Brick Works. Purchased by the [[harry-bull|Bull family]] in 1920.

## Physical Description

Chilton Lodge was a solid, ivy-clad Georgian building with a sombre interior atmosphere created by thick furnishings, leather-seated armchairs, and heavy furniture. The rooms were filled with bric-a-brac, pictures, and family portraits, including oil paintings of the Rev. H.D.E. Bull (sporting mutton-chop whiskers) and the Rev. Harry Bull. The china, glass, and furniture filling Chilton Lodge were the exact same items visible in old photographs of the interior of Borley Rectory — the Bull siblings had deliberately sought to make the new home as much like their former much-loved Borley Rectory as possible.

## Bull Family Home

When the [[harry-bull|Bull sisters]] left [[borley-rectory]] in 1920 — the year [[harry-bull]] married [[ivy-brackenbury]] and moved into the Rectory with his wife — Chilton Lodge became the family's new base. The primary occupants were the Misses [[ethel-bull|Ethel]], Constance, and [[freda-bull|Freda Bull]], along with their brothers Alfred and Gerald Bull. [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]] died 29 April 1961 aged 93; Alfred died 17 March 1956.

## Investigators' Visits

Chilton Lodge served as the principal location where researchers could interview the surviving Bull sisters — the only direct witnesses to the pre-Price period of the haunting, who had lived at [[borley-rectory]] from birth. It hosted an extraordinary range of Borley personalities: [[harry-price|Harry Price]], [[lucie-kaye|Lucie Kaye]], [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]], [[lord-charles-hope|Lord Charles Hope]], [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]], [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen Goldney]], W.H. Salter, Dr Peter Hilton-Rowe, the Rev. John Cranmer Dening, Air Cdre R. Carter Jonas, Flt Lt Caunter, [[robert-hastings|Bob Hastings]], [[sj-de-lotbiniere|S.J. de Lotbinière]], and others. Former Borley residents including the Rev. Guy and [[mabel-smith|Mabel Smith]], the Rev. A.C. and Annette Henning, and James and Catherine Turner were also entertained there.

**Key dates:**
- **13 June 1929:** Price and [[lucie-kaye|Lucie Kaye]] visited to interview Ethel, Constance, and Freda Bull during Price's very first Borley investigation, gathering the foundational testimonies of the phantom nun and early legends.
- **July 1929:** [[lord-charles-hope|Lord Charles Hope]] visited to gather his own evidence regarding the hauntings.
- **October 1931:** W.H. Salter of the [[spr|SPR]] visited and reportedly tried to persuade the Bull family to drop Price, telling them he was "not the ideal person to carry out confidential investigations." Ethel Bull recalled the visit with bitterness.
- **29 March 1939:** Price and Glanville interviewed Freda, Ethel, Constance, and Walter Bull to gather final testimonies for *The Most Haunted House in England*.
- **1951 onwards:** [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] made approximately nine or ten visits, always finding the house "full of heavy furniture and bric-a-brac, portraits and pictures, typically Victorian." He collected Borley relics including a watercolour sketch of Borley Church and an original blue-and-white plate from the Rectory.

## Controversial Testimony

While there are no contradictions regarding the physical building, Chilton Lodge was the epicentre for some of the most disputed testimony in the Borley saga:

**The Smear Campaign:** During interviews at Chilton Lodge — most notably with [[sidney-glanville|Glanville]] and [[lord-charles-hope|Lord Charles Hope]] — the Bull sisters, particularly Ethel, frequently shared malicious gossip about their sister-in-law [[ivy-brackenbury|Ivy Brackenbury]]. They wrongly suggested she was a "gold digger" and circulated baseless rumours that Rev. Harry Bull had died from poisoning. Sceptical researchers point out that séance "data" gathered at Borley — which accused "Ivy" or "Henry" of murder — was heavily contaminated by investigators unconsciously feeding these fireside tales into the planchette sessions.

**Contradictions in Sighting Accounts:** The testimony the Bull sisters gave at Chilton Lodge was entirely uncorroborated and often collected decades after the fact. There was intense disagreement even within the household: while the sisters vehemently defended the ghosts, their brother Gerald Bull told investigators that he had never seen anything paranormal at Borley in his life and considered the ghost stories to be pure "feminine imagination" on the part of his sisters.

## Demolition

In the late 1960s, Chilton Lodge was demolished by a local builder and replaced by modern housing. Like Borley Rectory itself, no sign of the historic building remains today, although its name survives in two modern estate roads — **Chilton Lodge Road** and **Chilton Court**.
