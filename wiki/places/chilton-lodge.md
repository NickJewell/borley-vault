# Chilton Lodge

A solid Georgian building that stood in Cornard Road, Great Cornard, [[sudbury|Sudbury]], Suffolk, near to the Chilton Brick Works. Purchased in 1920 by the [[bull-family]].

## Bull Family Home

When the [[bull-family|Bull sisters]] left [[borley-rectory]] in 1920 — the year [[harry-bull]] married [[ivy-brackenbury]] and moved into the Rectory with his wife — Chilton Lodge became the family's new base. [[ethel-bull]] (who died 29 April 1961 aged 93), Constance Bull, and [[walter-bull|Alfred Bull]] (who died 17 March 1956) were the principal occupants in its later years.

## Peter Underwood's Visits

[[peter-underwood]] visited Chilton Lodge on approximately nine or ten occasions, sometimes alone and sometimes accompanied — on different occasions by Tom Brown, [[peter-hilton-rowe|Dr Peter Hilton-Rowe]], [[john-dening|the Revd John C. Dening]] and other friends. He always found the house "full of heavy furniture and bric-a-brac, portraits and pictures, typically Victorian," with the surviving Bulls appearing to have done their best to make the place as much like their former much-loved home at Borley as possible.

At the time of his first visit in 1951, the occupants were Constance, Ethel and Alfred Bull.

## Significance

Chilton Lodge was the principal location where researchers could interview the surviving Bull sisters — the only direct witnesses to the pre-Price period of the haunting, who had lived at [[borley-rectory]] from birth. Visits there by [[sidney-glanville]], [[lord-charles-hope]], Underwood, [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth]] and others provided important first-hand testimonies about the Bull period.

## Demolition

In the late 1960s Chilton Lodge was demolished and new properties were constructed by a local builder. Like Borley Rectory itself, no sign of the historic building remains today, although its name survives in two modern estate roads — **Chilton Lodge Road** and **Chilton Court**.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Chilton Lodge** was a significant location in the periphery of the Borley Rectory story, serving as the post-Rectory home for the surviving members of the Bull family and a primary interviewing ground for almost every major psychical investigator involved in the case. 

Based on the Borley sources, here are the concrete details suitable for a reference article:

### **Physical Description and Location**
*   **Location:** Chilton Lodge was situated on Cornard Road in Great Cornard, a suburb of Sudbury, Suffolk, located near the Chilton Brick Works [1, 2]. 
*   **Architecture:** It was a solid, ivy-clad Georgian building [1, 3]. 
*   **Interior and Decor:** The interior was heavily Victorian, described as having a "sombre atmosphere" created by thick furnishings, leather-seated armchairs, and heavy furniture [4, 5]. The rooms were filled with bric-a-brac, pictures, and family portraits, including oil paintings of the Rev. H.D.E. Bull (sporting mutton-chop whiskers) and the Rev. Harry Bull [5, 6]. 
*   **The Borley Connection:** The Bull siblings deliberately sought to make Chilton Lodge as much like their former, much-loved home of Borley Rectory as possible [4, 7]. Visitors noted that the china, glass, and furniture filling Chilton Lodge were the exact same items visible in old photographs of the interior of Borley Rectory [8]. 

### **Associated Persons**
*   **The Bull Siblings:** Following the marriage of the Rev. Harry Bull to Ivy Brackenbury, his unmarried siblings were forced to vacate Borley Rectory [2]. They purchased Chilton Lodge in 1920 [1, 9]. The primary occupants were the Misses Ethel, Constance, and Freda Bull, along with their brothers Alfred and Gerald Bull [4, 10]. 
*   **Psychical Investigators & Visitors:** Because the Bull siblings lived there for decades, Chilton Lodge hosted an incredible "Who's Who" of Borley personalities. Visitors included Harry Price, Lucie Kaye, Sidney Glanville, Peter Underwood, Lord Charles Hope, W.H. Salter, Kathleen Goldney, Dr. Peter Hilton-Rowe, the Rev. John Cranmer Dening, Air Cdre R. Carter Jonas, Flt Lt Caunter, Bob Hastings, Seymour J. de Lotbinière, and Brigadier C.A.L. Brownlow [7, 8, 11-16]. Former Borley residents like the Rev. Guy and Mabel Smith, the Rev. A.C. and Annette Henning, and James and Catherine Turner were also entertained there [12, 17, 18]. 

### **Key Events**
*   **June 13, 1929:** During his very first investigation of Borley Rectory, Harry Price and his secretary Lucie Kaye visited Chilton Lodge to interview Ethel, Constance, and Freda Bull, gathering the foundational testimonies of the "phantom nun" and other early Borley legends [12, 19].
*   **July 1929:** Lord Charles Hope visited the sisters at Chilton Lodge to gather his own evidence regarding the hauntings [13, 20].
*   **October 1931:** W.H. Salter (an official from the Society for Psychical Research) visited Chilton Lodge. Ethel Bull recalled that he used the visit to try to persuade the Bull family to drop Harry Price and let the SPR take over the investigation, telling them Price was "not the ideal person to carry out confidential investigations" [14]. 
*   **March 29, 1939:** Harry Price and Sidney Glanville interviewed Freda, Ethel, Constance, and Walter Bull at the Lodge to gather final testimonies for Price's forthcoming book, *The Most Haunted House in England* [12, 21].
*   **1951 and Beyond:** Investigator Peter Underwood began a series of visits (totaling nine or ten) to Chilton Lodge, taking tea with the elderly Ethel and Alfred Bull and obtaining several Borley relics, including a watercolor sketch of Borley Church and an original blue and white plate from the Rectory [7, 22]. 
*   **Late 1960s (Demolition):** Following the deaths of the remaining Bull siblings, Chilton Lodge was bulldozed by a local builder and replaced by modern housing. Today, no trace of the historic building remains, but its name survives in the modern estate roads built over it: Chilton Lodge Road and Chilton Court [23, 24]. 

### **Contradictions and Source Perspectives**
While there are no contradictions regarding the physical building, Chilton Lodge was the epicenter for some of the most contradictory and controversial *testimony* in the Borley saga:
*   **The Smear Campaign:** During interviews at Chilton Lodge (most notably with Sidney Glanville and Lord Charles Hope), the Bull sisters—particularly Ethel—frequently shared malicious gossip and slander about their sister-in-law, Ivy Brackenbury [25, 26]. They wrongly suggested she was a "gold digger" and even circulated baseless rumors that their brother, the Rev. Harry Bull, had died from poisoning [25, 26]. Sceptical researchers point out that the seance "data" gathered at Borley Rectory (which accused "Ivy" or "Henry" of murder) was heavily contaminated by the investigators listening to these vindictive fireside tales at Chilton Lodge and unconsciously feeding them into the planchette [27, 28].
*   **Discrepancies in Sighting Accounts:** Sceptics like Lord Charles Hope and the authors of the *Borley Report* noted that the testimony the Bull sisters gave at Chilton Lodge was entirely uncorroborated and often collected decades after the fact [29]. Furthermore, there was intense disagreement even among the siblings living at the Lodge; while the sisters vehemently defended the ghosts, their brother Gerald Bull told investigators that he had never seen anything paranormal at Borley in his life and considered the ghost stories to be pure "feminine imagination" on the part of his sisters [30, 31].
