# Polstead Rectory

A former rectory about ten miles from [[borley-rectory|Borley]], over the county boundary in Suffolk, with a lengthy history of alleged paranormal disturbances. The building is approximately 400 years old, set within four acres of garden and paddock, and is now a private residence. Babbs and Mathias discuss it in detail as the closest parallel case to the Borley haunting, and note that the Church Commissioners handled it very differently from Borley — acting quickly and decisively to sell the property in 1979.

## History of the Haunting

Nobody knows precisely when the disturbances at Polstead began. The earliest documented response was an exorcism carried out by the **Rev John Whitmore**, rector from 1795 to 1840, who performed a service of exorcism "at the beginning of the 19th century." Whitmore remained in the rectory for 45 years, suggesting the phenomena were either not too severe or that he was prepared to tolerate them.

## 20th-Century Reports

- **Mrs Mary Neads** (wife of the rector from 1963 to 1976) described "unusual but not unpleasant spiritual activity," including the sounds of footsteps when no one was about. She and her husband reportedly "got used to the phenomena" [1, 2].
- The wife of the preceding rector, **Rev Paul Biddlecombe**, told *The Church Times* in 1978 that she felt pushed out of bed by an unseen force, and that her young daughter would wake in distress because of screaming noises from the hearth and flue of her bedroom [1].

## The Foster Incident (1979)

The last clergy family to live at Polstead Rectory were the **Rev Hayden Foster**, his wife Margo, and their son Gerard. They lasted precisely five days and nights. The first four days were quiet, but on the night before the new rector's induction ceremony, the couple moved to a smaller bedroom to accommodate guests. At about 3 a.m., Mrs Foster was woken by screaming. She then observed that the room had changed in appearance — newly-decorated, it now appeared to have dirty, peeling wallpaper. Most disturbingly, she experienced a terrifying sensation of being strangled and suffocated [2].

The following morning the Fosters packed their bags, moved in with friends, and refused to return.

## The Church Commissioners' Response

Unlike the Borley case, which was allowed to drag on for decades without resolution, the Church Commissioners acted promptly in 1979. They put Polstead Rectory on the market and instructed the estate agents to mention the haunting reputation explicitly in the marketing brochure — a candour that contrasts sharply with the protracted institutional silence around Borley. The property was then sold as a private residence [4].

> **Contradiction:** A slight discrepancy exists among church officials regarding the reasons for the sale. The Archdeacon, the Ven Jeremy Walsh, claimed the house was being sold primarily due to its size and high maintenance costs, though he admitted the recent phenomena had "hastened the decision." He commented: "As Christians believe that there is a spiritual dimension to our lives, then they should not be surprised if they find that spiritual forces break through occasionally." Meanwhile, the then-bishop, the Rt Rev Leslie Browne, said he was unaware of the rectory's haunted reputation and would not have moved the Fosters into it had he been informed [2].

## The Red Barn Murder

Polstead is also the site of the famous **Red Barn murder** (1827), in which Maria Marten was killed by William Corder and her body buried in a barn — discovered reportedly after her stepmother dreamed of the location. The sources explicitly clarify that this murder has no connection to the alleged rectory haunting: the Rev Whitmore conducted his exorcism of the house long before the 1827 murder took place [3, 5].

## Significance

The Polstead case came to a head 40 years after the Borley fire. Babbs and Mathias note that the similarities between the two cases — a rural rectory with a long history of disturbances, paranormal footsteps as a common feature, and eventual disposal by the church — confirm that [[borley-rectory|Borley]] is not unique in broad outline. However, the Polstead case received far less publicity and was handled far more efficiently.
