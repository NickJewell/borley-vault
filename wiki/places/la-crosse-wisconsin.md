# La Crosse, Wisconsin

A substantial city on the Wisconsin side of the Mississippi River, approximately 100 miles south-east of Minneapolis. [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] settled here in **1963** after leaving [[fargo-north-dakota|Fargo]] and remained for the rest of her documented life. She is also mentioned as residing here by the time Wood was writing (mid-1980s).

## Marianne at La Crosse

In La Crosse, Marianne worked in **social welfare** and had a highly successful career. Wood records:

> "The project of which she was a co-founder received state (i.e. from the State of Wisconsin) recognition and funding, and she has become a highly respected pillar of the community. She was still working in the mid-Eighties!"

This represents the most dramatic discontinuity in Marianne's life: after four decades of imposture, bigamy, abandonment, and possible murder in England, she built a genuinely productive and respected life in La Crosse. Wood attributes this partly to the lesson taught by Hall's investigation ("she must have learned that 'what starts off as a bit of harmless fun' can have terrible consequences"), partly to age and the absence of the desperate financial pressures that had driven her English escapades, and partly to the severing of her bond with [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]]:

> "There is, therefore, an obvious discontinuity between her behaviour in England and her behaviour in the United States... Marianne's transformation when she was at last free of him and in another country."

## End of the Borley Story

The introduction to Wood's book places Marianne at La Crosse as of his writing (presumably c. 1988–1992). It was from [[jamestown-north-dakota|Jamestown]] that [[robert-swanson|Swanson]] had found her in 1958; La Crosse represents her successful evasion of further scrutiny. Other sources — notably [[vincent-oneil|Vincent O'Neil]]'s account and [[hbrs-ch20|O'Connor ch. 20]] — confirm that Marianne died at La Crosse in **1992**, aged 93.

> **Cross-reference:** Robert O'Neil had been placed in a children's home in La Crosse briefly during the Hokah breakdown period — the city appears in the earliest American days as a nearby reference point before becoming Marianne's permanent home.

## Marianne's Last Letter

Writing to Robert Swanson in 1958, from what was then still Jamestown, Marianne said:

> "All that I ask is just to be left in peace to live out the rest of my life in quiet decency and in the serenity of the American way of life. I have friends, I have my child, my work, here in America, life is good..."

She got her wish, largely, at La Crosse.
