# La Crosse, Wisconsin

A substantial city on the western Wisconsin side of the Mississippi River, approximately 100 miles south-east of Minneapolis and not far from [[hokah-minnesota|Hokah, Minnesota]], where the O'Neil family farm was situated. [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] settled here in **1963** after leaving [[fargo-north-dakota|Fargo]] and remained for the rest of her documented life. She died at La Crosse in **1992**, aged 93.

The city appears in the Borley story even before Marianne's permanent settlement there: during the [[hokah-minnesota|Hokah]] breakdown, baby Vincent was placed temporarily in the La Crosse Home for Children, and the city served as the nearest reference point for the earliest American years.

## Marianne at La Crosse

After arriving in America in August 1946, Marianne and her son travelled over a thousand miles by train to the region, initially staying at the O'Neil grandmother's farm in nearby Hokah before beginning the restless movement through the American heartlands that would eventually bring her back to La Crosse permanently.

Following the collapse of her marriage to the heavy-drinking O'Neil, Marianne's son Vinny was placed in care at the La Crosse Home for Children — where Marianne herself was also employed for a time. Although O'Neil promised to pay $35 a month for the boy's board, he paid nothing and provided no clothes. After securing a regular income in North Dakota, Marianne worked with the La Crosse probation department to regain custody of Vinny and moved him into a modest apartment with her.

Returning permanently in 1963, Marianne worked in **social welfare**, dealing with families. By 1966 she was working as coordinator for the La Crosse Committee on Aging. Wood records:

> "The project of which she was a co-founder received state (i.e. from the State of Wisconsin) recognition and funding, and she has become a highly respected pillar of the community. She was still working in the mid-Eighties!"

## End of the Borley Story

This represents the most dramatic discontinuity in Marianne's life: after four decades of imposture, bigamy, abandonment, and possible murder in England, she built a genuinely productive and respected life in La Crosse. Wood attributes this partly to the lesson taught by Hall's investigation ("she must have learned that 'what starts off as a bit of harmless fun' can have terrible consequences"), partly to age and the absence of the desperate financial pressures that had driven her English escapades, and partly to the severing of her bond with [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]]:

> "There is, therefore, an obvious discontinuity between her behaviour in England and her behaviour in the United States... Marianne's transformation when she was at last free of him and in another country."

In 1973 Wallace Tower — the biological brother of Adelaide Foyster — travelled to La Crosse to ask Marianne about his sister's whereabouts. Marianne initially denied having any knowledge of Adelaide, but eventually relented, giving him two photographs and Adelaide's last known English address.

In 1978 psychical researchers [[iris-owen|Iris Owen]] and Pauline Mitchell tracked Marianne down and she agreed to meet them at the **Stoddard Hotel** in La Crosse. Though she was seventy-nine at the time, she arrived well turned out and "very youthful-looking," agreeing to discuss her life and the Borley hauntings on the condition that nothing would be published until after her death.

## Marianne's Last Letter

Writing to [[robert-swanson|Robert Swanson]] in 1958, from what was then still [[jamestown-north-dakota|Jamestown]], Marianne said:

> "All that I ask is just to be left in peace to live out the rest of my life in quiet decency and in the serenity of the American way of life. I have friends, I have my child, my work, here in America, life is good..."

She got her wish, largely, at La Crosse.
