# Hokah, Minnesota

A small town in the Mississippi bluff country of Minnesota, situated on **Highway 44** not far from [[la-crosse-wisconsin|La Crosse, Wisconsin]] (which lies just across the Mississippi River) and approximately 100 miles south-east of Minneapolis. It was the first American home of [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] after her arrival in August 1946 as one of "Eisenhower's Sweethearts" — the British GI brides. To the west lies an endless succession of small communities and pleasant, wooded bluff country that eventually gives way to the rolling prairies stretching toward the Rocky Mountains. The region's climate is severe by British standards, with very hot summers and intensely cold winters accompanied by frequent blizzards that can cut off small communities for days.

Robert Wood, who knew the area personally, writes: "Hokah today is much the same as it was when Marianne first saw it, and the town could have sprung from the pages of Sinclair Lewis's classic tale of small-town America, *Main Street*."

## The O'Neil Farm

The O'Neil family farm lay outside Hokah. [[esther-oneil|Esther O'Neil]] (Robert's mother) and her own mother lived there. Wood describes it as having "never made more than the barest living." The farm represented the sharp gap between the America of wartime propaganda and the actual life awaiting GI brides. Upon arriving, Marianne reportedly discovered that her new husband was "a work-shy drunkard and completely unreliable."

## Events at Hokah

Marianne and O'Neil soon moved from the farm into a small wooden house in Hokah itself. The couple quickly defaulted on bills owing to local stores and gained a reputation as bad credit risks. Marianne found work at the local newspaper, the **Hokah Chief**.

The household collapsed when O'Neil left Hokah with [[beverly-st-jacques|Beverly St Jacques]]. Baby Vincent ("Robert Vincent O'Neil") was placed temporarily in a children's home in [[la-crosse-wisconsin|La Crosse]]. A car accident on Highway 44 in which [[sauer-hokah|Sauer]] (nephew of the Hokah barber) was driving and O'Neil was injured gave rise to Marianne's pointless false letter to [[mr-and-mrs-fenton|Mrs Fenton]] claiming she had been in the crash.

## Significance

Hokah represents Marianne's disillusionment and adaptation. In England she had always been able to flee to the next town, the next lodgings, the next man. In Hokah she faced the reality of a work-shy, unreliable husband and a community far smaller and more watchful than anything she had known. Her time there marked the beginning of a restless movement through the American heartlands — she later relocated to [[jamestown-north-dakota|Jamestown]] and Fargo in North Dakota, and eventually settled permanently in [[la-crosse-wisconsin|La Crosse, Wisconsin]]. Her resourcefulness — finding work, managing finances, building a local reputation — begins to emerge in this period as the dominant feature of her American life.
