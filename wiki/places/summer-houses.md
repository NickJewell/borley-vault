# Summer-Houses

Two timber structures erected in the grounds of [[borley-rectory]] during the time of the [[bull-family]] that became associated with the haunting. Both were still standing after the [[fire-1939|fire of February 1939]] but were eventually pulled down.

## Large Summer-House

An octagonal wooden structure with a pitched roof, built around a central supporting post to which a table was attached [1, 2]. Positioned on the edge of the upper lawn — shaded by a large cedar of Lebanon — it directly faced the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] and appears on the 1933 Ordnance Survey map of Borley. Nearly half of its upper portion on the garden side was left unboarded, providing a wide, unobstructed view across the grounds [1, 2]. Continuous wooden benches ran around the interior periphery.

The common belief is that [[henry-bull]] built it specifically to comfortably watch for the [[the-nun|phantom nun]], and photographs in the [[borley-dossier|Harry Price Collection]] at the University of London, taken from inside the summer-house, show what an excellent vantage point it commanded. [[peter-underwood]] recorded that [[harry-bull]] spent "hours and hours sitting in the large summer-house 'communing with the spirits,' hoping to see the nun and converse with her."

> **Contradiction:** [[harry-price]] and local lore maintained that [[henry-bull]] erected the large summer-house for the "express purpose" of watching for the ghost nun [2, 4, 24]. However, a more mundane explanation is that it was built simply for use by the Bulls during their frequent summer croquet and tennis parties — documented in [[dodie-bull]]'s 1885 diary [24, 25]. Furthermore, investigators [[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], and [[trevor-hall]] noted that the specific claim that Henry Bull used the *smaller* remote summer-house in the early hours to "commune with the spirits" was explicitly denied, both in conversation and in writing, by his surviving son and daughter [27].

During the [[blue-room-seance|Price tenancy]], [[harry-price]] instructed observers in his Blue Book to spend at least one half-hour before and after dusk watching the Nun's Walk from the large summer-house [12, 13]. Observer Mark Kerr-Pearse frequently slept there on a camp bed during his vigils [14]. The only reported sighting of the nun during Price's entire on-site investigation occurred while a group of observers were in the summer-house doing exactly that (see Chronology, 18 February 1938).

The [[cambridge-commission]], under the leadership of Dr. Andrew J.B. Robertson, subsequently used the large summer-house as a base and sleeping quarters during their wartime visits to the ruined Rectory [15, 16].

One of the last photographs of the large summer-house dates from 5 January 1944, showing it in an advanced state of disrepair. One of the bungalows subsequently built on the Rectory garden now occupies the site.

## Small "Gothic" Summer-House

A smaller, more decorative structure — known as the "pseudo-Gothic" summer-house — situated at the extreme south-east of the garden, some 200 yards from the main house [3, 6–9]. Hidden among trees and brambles in a copse near a small stream, it was described as open at the sides with a roof resembling the lid of an old-fashioned tea caddy [9, 10]. It was evidently a haven of tranquillity for [[harry-bull]], who often spent much time there.

This smaller structure features little in the Rectory haunting. It was apparently vandalised and demolished in the summer of 1943 by military authorities — or soldiers — stationed nearby, who also inexplicably excavated a large earthwork directly on the site of the structure [6, 10, 21]. It is illustrated in *MHH* (plate 2).

## Key Events

- **28 July 1900 — The Most Famous Sighting:** Three of the Bull sisters stopped by the large summer-house and watched a dark, bowed figure glide along the garden path, while a fourth sister approached it before it vanished [17, 18].
- **12 June 1929 — Dusk Vigil:** [[harry-price]] and *Daily Mirror* reporter V.C. Wall stood at the entrance of the large summer-house at dusk. Wall claimed to see a dark shadow moving among the trees on the Nun's Walk; Price was unable to confirm the sighting [19, 20].
- **22–23 July 1944 — Summer-House Séance:** Five members of the Cambridge Commission slept in the large summer-house. At 3:15 a.m. they heard faint knockings and, using a tapping code, established communication with an "entity" that claimed to be a nun who had died around 1250 [16, 22].
- **19 December 1945 — Disappearing Brick:** Visiting students camping in the summer-house placed a two-inch brick in the centre of the fixed table inside a chalk circle. The brick fell to the ground untouched and then vanished, later found on the opposite side of the table — a movement that required it to make a curvilinear flight around the table's central support post [23].

## Significance

The large summer-house is one of the architectural features most strongly associated with the Borley legend — representing the Bull family's active, decades-long watch for the phantom nun, which [[dodie-bull]] later embedded in the family's oral tradition. Its placement on the edge of the lawn looking out over the Nun's Walk is deliberate and suggests that the Nun's Walk had an established reputation long before [[harry-price]] arrived.
