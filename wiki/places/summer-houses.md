# Summer-Houses

Two timber structures erected in the grounds of [[borley-rectory]] during the time of the [[bull-family]] that became associated with the haunting. Both were still standing after the fire of February 1939 but were eventually pulled down.

## Large Summer-House

An octagonal wooden building with a pitched roof, built around a central supporting post. Positioned near the house overlooking the main lawn, it appears on the 1933 Ordnance Survey map of Borley. This location, with its unrestricted view across to the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]], supports the common belief that Henry Bull built it specifically to comfortably watch for the [[the-nun|phantom nun]].

The garden side was left open, and photographs in the [[borley-dossier|Harry Price Collection]] at the University of London, taken from inside the summer-house, show what an excellent vantage point it commanded. [[john-osborne-harley]] confirmed to [[peter-underwood]] that [[harry-bull]] spent "hours and hours sitting in the large summer-house 'communing with the spirits,' hoping to see the nun and converse with her."

A more mundane explanation is that it was erected simply for use by the Bulls during summer tennis parties — they held frequent garden parties, as documented in [[dodie-bull]]'s 1885 diary.

During the [[price-tenancy]], [[harry-price]] instructed observers in the [[blue-book-of-instructions|Blue Book]] to spend at least one half-hour before and after dusk watching the Nun's Walk from the large summer-house. The only reported sighting of the nun during Price's entire on-site investigation occurred while a group of observers were in the summer-house doing exactly that (see Chronology, 18 February 1938).

One of the last photographs of the large summer-house dates from 5 January 1944, showing it in an advanced state of disrepair. One of the bungalows subsequently built on the [[rectory-grounds|Rectory garden]] now occupies the site.

## Small "Gothic" Summer-House

A smaller, more decorative structure — known as the "pseudo-gothic" summer-house — situated amongst the trees at the east end of the Rectory grounds. It was evidently a haven of tranquillity for [[harry-bull]], who often spent much time there.

This smaller structure features little in the Rectory haunting and was apparently vandalised and demolished by a party of visiting soldiers in 1943 (see Chronology, 1 August 1943). It is illustrated in *MHH* (plate 2).

## Significance

The large summer-house is one of the architectural features most strongly associated with the Borley legend — representing the Bull family's active, decades-long watch for the phantom nun, which [[dodie-bull]] later embedded in the family's oral tradition. Its placement on the edge of the lawn looking out over the Nun's Walk is deliberate and suggests that the Nun's Walk had an established reputation long before [[harry-price]] arrived.
