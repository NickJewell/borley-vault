# Bush Boake Allen / Stafford Allen Factory

A large essence and flavourings factory in the Stour valley just north of [[borley-rectory]], identified by [[andrew-clarke]] as the mundane source of the lavender, perfume, and cooking smells that were reported throughout the Foyster period — and by subsequent visitors — as paranormal phenomena.

## History and location

The factory was established in 1899 as "Stafford Allen" on the site of an old watermill in the Stour valley, approximately a short walk to the north of Borley Rectory, just over the brow of the hill. Clarke describes it in essay 8 ("Borley Rectory and the Smell of Fear"):

> *Bush, Boake and Allen were there, a short walk to the north, just over the brow of the hill, in full production when the Foysters were there, and still there now. At the time, it was the largest manufacturer of essences and flavourings in Britain.*
> — *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, essay 8

The factory milled herbs and spices — many grown on site — including belladonna, digitalis, lavender, liquorice and peppermint, as well as imported ginger, pepper, gums and spices. It produced food flavouring essences (butter, almond, honey-cream and madeira) and naturally-based insecticides such as pyrethrum and derris.

> *Fields of lavender grew in the fields for miles around, along with exotic crops. Even saffron was grown.*

## The smell phenomena

From the earliest days of the [[foyster-phenomena]], both Lionel and Marianne Foyster noted strange smells drifting into their bedroom window at night — particularly lavender and unidentifiable cooking smells — which Lionel attributed to paranormal causes and classified as good omens. The smells were reported by a range of subsequent visitors and became a recognized part of the Borley legend.

Clarke's explanation is unambiguous:

> *Borley Rectory only got the smell when the wind blew from the north. This only happens occasionally. When it does, the weather is cold and changeable.*

The night shift at the factory cleaned out the vats at night, releasing concentrated smells. Long Melford residents remembered "the exotic scents that came to the village when the wind was in the right direction." Clarke had personal experience of the same phenomenon from the firm's Witham factory in the 1960s.

## Response from Foysters and investigators

Harry Price described the smells as "paranormally made manifest." The SPR Report authors attributed the cooking smells to "late-night cooking in the rectory cottage" but did not identify the factory. Clarke regards both explanations as inadequate compared to the obvious local source.

Marianne herself remembered the smells but could not explain them: "I don't know what it was; I never did discover what it was, but it was there and it was very pleasant, and a lot of people beside me had noticed that."

## Significance

The Bush Boake Allen factory is Clarke's most concrete single analytical contribution in the Sidelights: a specific real-world source that explains a class of reported phenomena without invoking either the supernatural or deliberate fraud. The factory remained in operation and was still locally known for its smells at the time Clarke was writing.

> **Note:** The factory appears in no other major Borley source. Its identification as the smell source is Clarke's original finding.

## Research Notes

**Location and Physical Description**
The **Bush, Boake and Allen factory** (originally known as **Stafford Allen**) was a large chemical and manufacturing facility located on the outskirts of Long Melford, a short walk north of Borley, just over the brow of the hill [1, 2]. The factory nestled in the Stour valley and was built upon the site of an old watermill [2, 3]. It was surrounded for miles by fields where exotic crops were grown specifically for the factory's use, including lavender, liquorice, saffron, belladonna, digitalis, and peppermint [1-3]. 

**History and Operations**
Established in 1899, the Stafford Allen factory grew to become the **largest manufacturer of natural pharmaceuticals, essences, and flavourings in Britain** [1, 2]. The factory processed both locally grown herbs and imported spices such as ginger, pepper, and various gums [1, 2]. Its diverse production lines created perfumes, condiments, food flavouring essences (including butter, almond, honey-cream, and madeira), and naturally-based insecticides like pyrethrum and derris [1]. A routine part of the factory's operations involved the night-shift cleaning out the large vats in the evening [2, 3]. The factory operated for over a century before **finally closing in 2004** [4].

**Association with Borley Rectory Phenomena**
The factory is heavily cited in the Borley sources to provide a **perfectly mundane explanation for the allegedly paranormal odours** reported at Borley Rectory [2]. 
*   **The Scent Phenomena:** During the incumbencies of the Rev. Guy Smith and the Rev. Lionel Foyster, the residents frequently reported sudden, enchanting smells of lavender, roses, and eau de cologne floating through open windows late at night [2]. Because they could find no immediate source in the house or garden, they assumed these smells were of supernatural origin [2]. 
*   **The Natural Explanation:** When the Stafford Allen night-shift cleaned their vats, thick clouds of exotic scents—particularly lavender—would drift up the hill and settle over Borley whenever the wind blew from the north [2, 3]. While long-time local residents and villagers in Long Melford were completely accustomed to these industrial smells, strangers to the area like the Smiths and Foysters were entirely unaware of the factory's existence or its operations, leading them to misidentify a local chemical byproduct as a ghost [2].
