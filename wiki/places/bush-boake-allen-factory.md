# Bush Boake Allen / Stafford Allen Factory

A large essence and flavourings factory in the Stour valley just north of [[borley-rectory]], identified by [[andrew-clarke]] as the mundane source of the lavender, perfume, and cooking smells reported throughout the [[foyster-phenomena|Foyster period]] — and by subsequent visitors — as paranormal phenomena.

## History and Location

The factory was established in **1899** as "Stafford Allen" on the site of an old watermill in the Stour valley, approximately a short walk to the north of Borley Rectory, just over the brow of the hill. It grew to become the **largest manufacturer of natural pharmaceuticals, essences, and flavourings in Britain**. Clarke describes it in essay 8 of *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*:

> *Bush, Boake and Allen were there, a short walk to the north, just over the brow of the hill, in full production when the Foysters were there, and still there now. At the time, it was the largest manufacturer of essences and flavourings in Britain.*

The factory milled herbs and spices — many grown on site — including belladonna, digitalis, lavender, liquorice and peppermint, as well as imported ginger, pepper, gums and spices. It produced food flavouring essences (butter, almond, honey-cream and madeira) and naturally-based insecticides such as pyrethrum and derris. Fields of lavender and saffron grew in the surrounding area for miles around.

A routine part of the factory's operations involved the **night-shift cleaning out the large vats**, releasing concentrated smells. The factory operated for over a century before finally closing in **2004**.

## The Smell Phenomena

From the earliest days of the Foyster incumbency, both [[lionel-foyster|Lionel]] and [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] noted strange smells drifting into their bedroom window at night — particularly lavender and unidentifiable cooking smells — which Lionel attributed to paranormal causes and classified as good omens. The smells were reported by a range of subsequent visitors and became a recognised part of the Borley legend.

Clarke's explanation is unambiguous:

> *Borley Rectory only got the smell when the wind blew from the north. This only happens occasionally. When it does, the weather is cold and changeable.*

When the Stafford Allen night-shift cleaned their vats, thick clouds of exotic scents — particularly lavender — drifted up the hill and settled over Borley. Long Melford residents were completely accustomed to these industrial smells, but strangers to the area like the Smiths and Foysters were entirely unaware of the factory's existence. Clarke had personal experience of the same phenomenon from the firm's Witham factory in the 1960s.

## Response from Investigators

[[harry-price|Harry Price]] described the smells as "paranormally made manifest." The *Borley Report* authors attributed the cooking smells to "late-night cooking in the rectory cottage" but did not identify the factory. Clarke regards both explanations as inadequate compared to the obvious local source.

[[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] herself remembered the smells but could not explain them: "I don't know what it was; I never did discover what it was, but it was there and it was very pleasant, and a lot of people beside me had noticed that."

## Significance

The Bush Boake Allen factory is Clarke's most concrete single analytical contribution in the *Sidelights*: a specific real-world source that explains a class of reported phenomena without invoking either the supernatural or deliberate fraud.

> **Note:** The factory appears in no other major Borley source. Its identification as the smell source is Clarke's original finding.
