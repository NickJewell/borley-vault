# The Whin, Snape

A large detached house in the village of **Snape**, Suffolk, a few miles from Aldeburgh. The Fisher–Foyster household moved here from [[hill-farm-house-chillesford|Chillesford]] in **May 1940** and remained until **June 1941**, when they moved to [[dairy-cottage-rendlesham|Dairy Cottage, Rendlesham]].

## Character

Wood describes Snape in the pre-festival era as "as remote as Chillesford." The village predates Benjamin Britten's annual music festival by decades; in 1940 it was a quiet Suffolk backwater. The landlady confirmed to Trevor Hall in the 1950s that it had seemed odd that "Mrs Foyster appeared to have no friends, and that no one called at the house."

## Significant Anomaly: the Reversion to "Foyster"

The most notable feature of the Snape period is that Marianne reverted to the title **Mrs Foyster** rather than Mrs Fisher. [[henry-fisher|Henry Fisher]] was absent — apparently hospitalised in Saxmundham with duodenal trouble. His sister believed there had been a breach; Fisher may have suffered a breakdown when his suspicions about Marianne crystallised.

Wood: "The most likely explanation of Marianne's reversion to her true married name at Snape is, therefore, that there had been trouble with Fisher, whose doubts could no longer be contained."

Harry Price's first Borley book, *The Most Haunted House in England*, was published in August 1940 — several months after the move to Snape. Wood notes it is possible that Fisher read it and realised the truth about his "father-in-law." The timing cannot be confirmed as the direct cause of Fisher's absence, but Wood finds it suggestive.

## Context

[[price-harry|Harry Price]]'s publication in 1940 made Borley nationally famous and briefly gave Marianne's past a large public profile. Neighbours at Snape never knew her as anything other than the quiet, friendless Mrs Foyster.
