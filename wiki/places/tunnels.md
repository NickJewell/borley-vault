# Tunnels

There always seem to have been rumours of secret tunnels or passages at Borley. Multiple discoveries over the years have confirmed that some form of underground structure exists.

## Farrance Discovery (Bull Period)

During the time that [[harry-bull]] was rector, a local workman named **Farrance** was employed as a general labourer. When repairing a well in the grounds of [[borley-place]] next to the church, he uncovered a section of brickwork a few feet below the surface. After breaking away part of the old two-inch bricks, Farrance revealed a high and dome-shaped tunnel, which he apparently entered and followed for an unstated distance before turning back. [[harry-price]] included a short account in *EBR* (where Farrance was wrongly called "Tarrance").

## 1950 Underground Tests

In 1950, [[peter-underwood]] carried out tests with Laurence C. Gafford of Stevenage that convinced them hollows of some sort — and probably a tunnel — existed under the road outside the main entrance to the Borley site. Further exploration was impracticable at that time.

## 1957 Discovery

On 12 September 1957, the long-sought tunnel between the vanished Rectory and [[borley-place]] was finally discovered. Water-main contractors — workmen employed by Biggs Wall, contractors to Halstead Rural District Council — digging a trench from the direction of Borley Green toward Rodbridge Corner cutting water mains cut through brickwork with their excavator.

[[len-sewell]] was on site and made an immediate and thorough examination. He found:
- A flat brick floor covered with hard earth
- Upright walls about a foot high and a domed or arched roof
- **Tudor-style bricks** two inches thick (consistent with a Tudor date)
- The whole construction strongly built and in excellent condition
- Actual width: 32 inches; height: 28 inches
- Top: 12 inches below road surface (including 3 inches of metalling)
- The northern (Borley Place) end blocked by a brick wall
- The southern (Rectory side) end blocked by an accumulation of earth

Sewell concluded that the tunnel's line would have taken it into the cellars of Borley Place.

## Purpose

The reason for the existence of this tunnel has not been definitively established. It does not appear to be an old culvert or land drain. Possible explanations include:

- **Catholic refuge**: The tunnel could conceivably have been constructed by the Catholic [[waldegrave-family|Waldegraves]] during the time of Henry VIII, when priests' hiding holes and escape routes were common in the homes of wealthy Catholic families. The Tudor brick size is consistent with this date.
- **Drainage**: [[granville-squiers]] suggested well-constructed sewers were sometimes used as tunnels and hiding places in later years.
- **Herringham era**: The tunnel may have served the earlier [[herringham-rectory|Herringham Rectory]] as a drainage pipe for flooding cellars — a view supported by [[andrew-clarke]]'s analysis that the tunnel running between Borley Place and the old Rectory site originated as the Herringham foul-water drain.

## Significance

The 1957 tunnel discovery is significant because it confirms that a real underground structure exists at Borley, lending some material basis to the longstanding tradition of tunnels, secret passages and hidden chambers associated with the site. The Tudor-brick construction is consistent with the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave]] period and the Catholic persecution context that forms part of the historical background to the haunting.

The discovery came after [[philip-paul]]'s excavations of 1954–57 had specifically sought (and failed to find) the tunnel — making the accidental discovery by water-main contractors all the more ironic.
