# Tunnels

There always seem to have been rumours of secret tunnels or passages at Borley. Multiple discoveries over the years have confirmed that some form of underground structure exists.

## The Borley–Bures Legend

The most famous tunnel legend claimed an underground passage stretched 7 to 8 miles from a Borley monastery to a nunnery in [[bures-en-bray|Bures]], allegedly used by the doomed monk and nun whose affair forms the heart of the [[borley-legends|Borley ghost story]] [25–27]. Investigators have entirely debunked this: the engineering feat would have required passage beneath the River Stour and through waterlogged ground, and historians proved there was never a monastery at Borley nor a nunnery at Bures [26, 28, 29]. It is highly probable that the young Bull sisters simply "lifted" this tunnel legend from the neighbouring parish of [[monks-hall-glemsford|Glemsford]], which had identical local lore [29, 30].

## Farrance Discovery (Bull Period)

During the time that [[harry-bull]] was rector, a local workman named **Farrance** (wrongly recorded as "Tarrance" in Harry Price's *EBR*) was employed as a general labourer [9–11]. When repairing a well in the grounds of [[borley-place]] next to the church, he broke through old two-inch brickwork a few feet below the surface and revealed a high and dome-shaped tunnel. Farrance apparently entered it and followed it for an unstated distance before retreating — the foul air extinguished his candle and caused his eyes to smart [9–11, 20]. He then sealed the entrance.

## 1947 Well Exploration

Following theories that the tunnel connected to the deep 80-foot well in the Rectory courtyard, author [[james-turner]] descended into the well to investigate a visible depression in its side. He confirmed the brickwork was intact and that no tunnel led off from the well [14, 21, 22].

## 1950 Underground Tests

In 1950, [[peter-underwood]] carried out acoustic tests with Laurence C. Gafford of Stevenage that convinced them hollows of some sort — and probably a tunnel — existed under the road outside the main entrance to the Borley site [12, 14, 15]. Further exploration was impracticable at that time.

## 1957 Discovery

On 12 September 1957, workmen employed by Biggs Wall (contractors to Halstead Rural District Council) were digging a trench to lay water mains in Hall Lane when their mechanical digger accidentally cut through the brickwork of the tunnel [1, 13, 23]. The long-sought passage had been specifically searched for — and not found — by [[philip-paul]]'s excavations of 1954–57, making the accidental discovery all the more ironic.

[[len-sewell]] and a reporter from the *East Anglian Daily Times* were immediately notified and conducted a thorough exploration [3, 8, 12, 13]. Sewell found:

- A flat brick floor covered with hard earth
- Upright walls about a foot high and a domed or arched roof
- **Tudor-style bricks** two inches thick (consistent with a Tudor date), found to be in excellent condition [1–4]
- Actual width: **32 inches**; height: **28 inches**
- Top: **12 inches below road surface** (including 3 inches of metalling)
- The tunnel ran roughly north-south under Hall Lane at approximately right angles to the road [2, 5]
- The northern (Borley Place) end blocked by a modern brick wall; its direction indicated it originally linked to an alcove in [[borley-place]]'s cellars [2, 3, 6]
- The southern (Rectory side) end blocked by an accumulation of hard clay [3, 7, 8]
- Curiously, the tunnel featured a **deliberate dip of several inches** under the grass verge, appearing to follow the natural contour of the land rather than suffering from subsidence [7, 8]

Sewell concluded that the tunnel's line would have taken it into the cellars of Borley Place.

## 1987 Flooding

Following a heavy storm in the summer of 1987, the cellars of Borley Place flooded, prompting some investigators to argue this proved the "tunnel" was actually a blocked storm-water culvert rather than a secret passageway [24].

## Purpose

The reason for the existence of this tunnel has not been definitively established. It does not appear to be an old culvert or land drain, though that interpretation remains contested. Possible explanations include:

- **Catholic refuge**: The tunnel could conceivably have been constructed by the Catholic [[waldegrave-family|Waldegraves]] during the time of Henry VIII, when priests' hiding holes and escape routes were common in the homes of wealthy Catholic families. The Tudor brick size is consistent with this date, and Sir [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Edward Waldegrave]] is the only local figure with both the wealth and motivation to build such a structure for religious concealment [17–19, 31, 32].
- **Drainage**: [[granville-squiers]] suggested well-constructed sewers were sometimes used as tunnels and hiding places in later years [14, 16].
- **Herringham era**: The tunnel may have served the earlier [[herringham-rectory|Herringham Rectory]] as a drainage pipe for flooding cellars — a view supported by Andrew Clarke's analysis that the tunnel running between Borley Place and the old Rectory site originated as the Herringham foul-water drain [33, 34]. The 1987 flooding of Borley Place cellars is cited in support of this interpretation [24].

> **Contradiction:** The actual purpose of the 1957 tunnel remains debated. Some investigators argue that the deliberate dip in the tunnel's construction and its high-quality Tudor brickwork rule out a simple land drain [7, 8, 31], theorising it as a secret escape route built during the Reformation. Skeptics and historical architects point out that identical "tunnels" across East Anglia almost always turn out to be medieval or Tudor conduits for water and sewage — a theory seemingly confirmed by the 1987 flooding [24, 33, 34].

## Disconnection from the Rectory

While legends tightly weave the tunnel into the hauntings of [[borley-rectory]], the actual excavated tunnel ran between [[borley-place]] and the farmyard. It entirely missed both the Victorian Rectory site and the church, severely undermining any direct link between the physical tunnel and the Rectory's specific ghosts [35, 36].

## Significance

The 1957 tunnel discovery is significant because it confirms that a real underground structure exists at Borley, lending some material basis to the longstanding tradition of secret passages and hidden chambers associated with the site. The Tudor-brick construction is consistent with the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave]] period and the Catholic persecution context that forms part of the historical background to the haunting.
