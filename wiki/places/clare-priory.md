# Clare Priory, Suffolk

An Augustinian (Austin Friars) priory in Clare, Suffolk, approximately four miles beyond Borley. Dissolved on November 29, 1538, and re-established in 1953. Banks identifies Clare Priory as the origin of [[stephen-luskin|Rev. Stephen Luskin]], who became Rector of Borley in 1565 — creating a direct link between the Austin Friars and the Borley site.

## History and Physical Description

Clare Priory was founded c. 1248 by Richard de Clare, Earl of Clare, who had been in France in 1248 and returned the following year bringing members of the Augustinian or Austin order to live under a Royal writ of protection at Clare in Suffolk.

In its original form, the Priory consisted of an extensive collection of buildings. For most of its perimeter, the estate was **bounded by the course of the River Stour**; on the third side the monks created an **artificial ditch with flood gates**, effectively turning the priory into a small island. The 1291 Ecclesiastical Taxation of Pope Nicholas IV noted that Sudbury Priory held property or possessions in Clare.

## Dissolution and Luskin

One year before the final dissolution, on **December 20, 1537**, a monk named **Stephen Luskin** obtained a dispensation from Archbishop Thomas Cranmer to continue wearing his monk's habit beneath the robes of a secular priest — at a cost of £4. Banks interprets this as evidence that Luskin hoped for a reversal of the Dissolution.

On **November 29, 1538**, the monks were ultimately forced to surrender the property to the King's agent, **Richard Ingworth**, and were expelled.

Twenty-seven years later, Luskin appeared as Rector of Borley in 1565 — the living in the gift of the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegraves]]. Banks speculates on what Luskin had been doing in the interim, and whether his Augustinian background and crypto-Catholic sympathies made him useful to the Waldegrave family during the dangerous years of Elizabeth I's reign.

In **1953**, after an absence of over four hundred years, the Austin Friars returned to Clare and re-established their order on the site.

## The Psychometrist's Impression

Banks notes a curious connection via the psychometrist [[josef-kraus-marion|Fred Marion]], who in 1937 attempted to obtain psychic impressions from a piece of wood found at Borley Rectory. Marion described "a feeling of some sort of collection of buildings, perhaps on an island" — which Banks suggests could relate to Clare Priory, whose estate was surrounded by water on most sides.

He acknowledges the speculation: "The idea could be a possibility relevant to the psychic side of the Borley story at that time, because at that period between the wars, the Clare order was not then in existence at Clare."

## The Clare–Lairre Connection

Banks explores whether "Lairre" (the supposed nun's name from the planchette sessions) might be a corruption of "Clare" — the similarity between the two names being phonetically close enough to suggest a possible place-name confusion. The Augustinian order at Clare had historical connections with France (the order came from France originally), and "repeatedly France and matters French reappear in connection with stories attached to the site on which Borley Rectory was built."

The order at Clare was also the destination of **[[fred-cartwright|Fred Cartwright]]** the day he saw the ghost nun outside the Rectory gates in 1927.

## The Tunnel Legend

A persistent local rumour claims that a subterranean tunnel ran from Clare and met another tunnel from Bures somewhere underneath the grounds of Borley Rectory. This is largely dismissed as folklore, as digging such a tunnel under the River Stour would have been an immense engineering feat.
