# Borley Rectory

The central location of the entire Borley case. Famously dubbed "the most haunted house in England," it was a large Victorian parsonage serving as the focal point for a complex and highly controversial series of alleged paranormal phenomena. Built 1863; gutted by fire 27 February 1939; demolished 1944.

## Location

Two-and-a-half miles from [[long-melford|Sudbury, Suffolk]], and the same distance from Long Melford; on the north bank of the River Stour, on the Essex side of the Essex/Suffolk border. The name Borley derives from the Anglo-Saxon *Barlea* — "boar's clearing" or "boar's pasture" (Domesday Book, 1086). A parish of 121 people (1931 census). Nearest stations: Sudbury and Long Melford (c.60 miles from London on the former LNER).

## Historical Background

The site has layers of occupation beneath the 1863 building. [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard Whitehouse]] traced a reference in Dugdale's *Monasticon Anglicanum*: in 1362, Edward III gave Borley Manor to the Prior and Community of Christ Church, Canterbury — definite evidence of a Benedictine connection. A Henry VIII-period entry gives a valuation of £32 14s. 4d. A fourteenth-century monastery on or near the site is therefore probable.

The [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]] held the manor for approximately three hundred years and are buried in [[borley-church|Borley Church]]. The previous Rectory (before 1863) was at [[borley-place|Borley Place]], on the opposite side of the road.

> **Contradiction:** [[harry-price|Price]] often repeated the legend that the Rectory sat on a thirteenth-century monastery, which historians and the Essex Archaeological Society completely debunked [80, 81]. The Manor of Borley was granted to Benedictine monks in 1362, but they administered it as an absentee landlord rather than building a monastic house there. The specific monk-and-nun legend has no historical support whatever.

## Physical Description

Built by [[henry-bull|Rev. Henry D. E. Bull]] in 1862–1863 on the site of the former [[herringham-rectory|Herringham Rectory]], incorporating ancient two-inch Tudor bricks from a much older pre-18th-century structure [1–5]. Designed by architect John Johnson and built by Thomas Farrow for £3,000, it was a massive, unpicturesque red-brick building in the Victorian-Gothic style, featuring a slated roof, a square stair tower, and a distinctive entrance porch topped with a turret and ironwork pinnacle [2, 6–8]. Originally L-shaped; a wing was added 1875–6 to accommodate Henry Bull's fourteen children, almost completely enclosing an uncovered bricked courtyard [9–11]. By the 1890s: "an irregular, sprawling conglomeration." The house contained 23 to 30 rooms [4, 9, 12, 13].

**Ground floor:** Ten rooms plus cupboards, storehouses, and cellars; dining-room, drawing-room, library (used by [[harry-price|Price]] as a base room), sewing-room, large hall; kitchen, larder, dairy, pantry, scullery. Two bay windows overlooking the lawn; French windows from the library to the lawn. Two entrance doors from the carriage drive; service entrances from the courtyard. No gas, electric light, or mains water; water was pumped by hand from an 80-foot courtyard well to an attic tank [4, 12, 13, 16]. Impossible to heat adequately.

**The bricked-up window:** The dining room window facing the carriage drive was permanently blocked up by [[henry-bull|Henry Bull]]. Price heard from three independent sources that this was because [[the-nun|the Nun]] "habitually peered into the room from the drive." He systematically dismissed the alternative "privacy" explanation: the drive is too wide to see through from the road; a hedge and belt of trees form an impenetrable screen; a blind would have sufficed for any normal person.

> **Contradiction:** Skeptics counter that the window was bricked up simply for privacy from the adjacent road, and that the nun-peering story was a retrospective legend [82, 83].

**The marble mantelpieces:** Dining-room and drawing-room mantelpieces are Italian marble, exhibited at the Great Exhibition (1851); the dining-room mantelpiece is adorned with "quaint marble busts of two monks." The monks' head fireplace was not built to perpetuate any legend — it was merely purchased from the 1851 Exhibition [8, 19].

**Domestic quarters:** Servants' windows heavily barred — not against burglars but to prevent maids leaving at night [2, 15].

**First floor:** Fourteen rooms including the [[blue-room|Blue Room]], bathroom, large lavatory, and a chapel (with altar rails and imitation stained-glass windows). Attics above with water cistern. Three staircases connecting floors.

**The cellars:** Staircase from kitchen passage; extend under the passage, main hall, library, and corner of dining-room. Damp and slimy walls. A shallow well extends into the cellar portion — covered with rotting boards, "discovered" by an observer who put his foot through. [[sidney-glanville|Glanville's]] excavation of November 1937 found foundations of a much older building underneath (two-inch bricks, not the 1863 type), facing roughly south.

**The cold spot:** On the first-floor landing outside the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] is the documented "cold spot" where multiple observers felt cold and shivered. [[harry-price|Price]] established that this spot sits directly above the ancient foundation discovered in the cellar — the correlation was noted by Price himself.

**The well:** 71-foot water depth, 80 feet total. At 61.9 feet below ground level, [[sidney-glanville|Glanville's]] flare and sinker were sucked into a cavity on the house side — suggesting a passage or hole leading off the well shaft. This connects to the tunnel evidence discussed in [[tunnels|gotb-ch05]].

**The bell system:** Old-fashioned bell-levers with wires anchored to the rafters; twenty bells hung in the kitchen passage — the system underlying all reports of simultaneous [[bell-ringing|bell-ringing]]. The rafter inscription found on Price's first visit (June 12, 1929) reads: "Bells hung by S. Cracknell and Mercur, 1863."

**The [[base-room|Base Room]]:** The library/study on the ground floor, used by [[harry-price|Price]] as the operations centre for the 1937–38 observer programme. Its French windows give direct access to the lawn and [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]]; it sits below the [[blue-room|Blue Room]]. Price equipped it with a camp bed, paraffin lamp, and supplies. All observer reports were written here.

**Garden:** Large lawn (about 2 acres — the full property was nearly four acres, later reduced), shielded from the road by tall elms and copper-beeches and cedars of Lebanon [8, 20, 21]. The [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]] skirted the south side. The two summer-houses (see [[nuns-walk]]): the octagonal pavilion built by [[henry-bull|Henry Bull]] specifically to watch for the Nun (c.100 yards from house, two sides entirely open for wide-angle view); and a smaller Gothic summer-house in the copse at the garden's end, also built by Henry Bull. Both were in regular use by [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]]. A stream runs through the end of the garden (crossed by a small bridge); the stream empties into a pond in which "someone once drowned himself." The cats' cemetery: overgrown, at the end of the Nun's Walk, near the stream — wooden headboards for "Gem," "Rollo," and "Sandy." During the observer programme, [[sidney-glanville|Glanville]] found the grave of "Gem" had been extensively disturbed; excavation found large non-feline bones.

**The tunnels:** Remains of an underground tunnel are visible in the Rectory farmyard. Portions have been discovered in a line between Borley and Bures (7 miles), where remains of a nunnery have been found. [[harry-price|Price]] was told a second tunnel from Clare meets the Bures tunnel in the Rectory grounds. The road outside the Rectory sounds hollow under heavy vehicles ([[edward-cooper|Cooper]] confirmed this). The tunnel system connects to the Borley-Bures nunnery story that underlies the [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] theory.

**A plague burial ground:** A portion of the garden is the burial place of victims of the Great Plague (1654–5); skulls are occasionally turned up by the spade and reinterred in the churchyard.

**Outbuildings:** Stable building unattached from the main house — survived the fire; later converted to a cottage. See [[rectory-cottage]].

## The Skull in the Library

Early in the [[rev-eric-smith|Smith]] incumbency (before Price's arrival), [[mabel-smith|Mrs Smith]] found in a library cupboard "a parcel neatly wrapped and tied with string." Inside: "the skull of, apparently, a young woman. It was in perfect condition, and the teeth were also perfect." [[rev-eric-smith|Eric Smith]] and the sexton buried it in the churchyard in the presence of the churchwarden and the widow of the previous rector.

> **Note:** This skull predates the [[marie-lairre|human bones found in 1943]] by fifteen years and is apparently separate from them. Its provenance — a young woman, in perfect condition, wrapped and stored in the library — is entirely unexplained. Neither the SPR's 1956 report nor Underwood and Tabori mention it.

## The Legend

Local legend held that a monastery existed on the site and a monk fell in love with a nun from Bures; both were executed. Two versions: (1) the groom strangled the nun, both grooms were caught and beheaded; (2) all three were caught by monks, the grooms beheaded, the nun buried alive in the monastery walls. **No historical evidence supports this specific narrative,** though the Benedictine connection to the site is documented (see above). The Essex Archaeological Society confirmed in 1938 that no ecclesiastical building other than the parish church had ever existed at Borley [54, 55, 56].

## Paranormal Activity

Over seventy years of reported phenomena, spanning all incumbencies: see [[the-nun]], [[phantom-coach]], [[bell-ringing]], [[mysterious-footsteps]], [[headless-man]], [[poltergeist-activity]], [[wall-writings]], [[fire-1939]], [[phantom-cat]].

Key landmark events:

- **1885–1886:** Undergraduate [[p-shaw-jeffrey|P. Shaw Jeffrey]] reportedly witnessed the first objective poltergeist activity, including falling stones and a displaced French dictionary [44–46].
- **28 July 1900:** The foundational sighting of the [[the-nun|Phantom Nun]] gliding across the lawn by four of the Bull sisters in evening twilight [47–49].
- **10–12 June 1929:** Following the Smiths' appeal for help, the *Daily Mirror* published sensational articles by V. C. Wall. [[harry-price|Price]] arrived on June 12, triggering a sudden, unprecedented outbreak of violent phenomena, including thrown stones, crashing glass, and the "appearance" of historical medallions [50, 53–56]. *Daily Mail* reporter [[charles-sutton|Charles Sutton]] later claimed he caught Price with pockets full of pebbles during a stone-throwing incident — a claim Price disputed (multiple witnesses; disputed) [54, 88, 89].
- **October 1930 – January 1932 (The Foyster Era):** The most violent phase — over 2,000 reported events [32, 57]. [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] was purportedly thrown from her bed, struck by invisible assailants, and targeted by messages scrawled on the walls pleading for "light, mass, and prayers" [58–62]. See [[foyster-phenomena]].
- **May 1937 – May 1938:** Price leased the empty Rectory. Forty-eight observers logged sporadic phenomena, including sudden temperature drops, moving objects, footsteps, locked doors, and a "luminous patch" on the ceiling of the Blue Room [34, 58, 63–65]. During planchette séances by the [[sidney-glanville|Glanville]] family, an entity named "[[sunex-amures|Sunex Amures]]" threatened to burn the Rectory down [66–68].
- **27–28 February 1939:** Almost exactly a year after the planchette prophecy, the Rectory was severely gutted by fire beginning at midnight [66, 69–71]. Witnesses claimed to see strange figures walking amid the flames [41, 72]. See [[fire-1939]].
- **August 1943:** Acting on séance clues, [[harry-price|Price]] excavated the ruined cellars and discovered human bone fragments (including a partial skull) which he attributed to a murdered French nun, "[[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]" [73–75]. See [[bones-excavation]].
- **1944:** The ruined shell was completely demolished by a local contractor, its bricks used as hardcore for nearby military airfields [76–79].

## Three Rectories on the Same Site (slbr-21)

[[andrew-clarke|Andrew Clarke's]] cartographic analysis demonstrates that the "Haunted Rectory" of [[henry-bull|Henry Bull]] was the third building on the site:

1. **Pre-1600 building** (~1550–1600): Three-bay oak-framed house. Cellars found during 1950s excavations. Positioned under the kitchen wing of later buildings.
2. **Herringham Rectory** (c.1800, demolished 1862): Large elegant building in pale "Suffolk Gault" brick; faced south down a long garden with ornamental pond. Demolished by Henry Bull to build a grander house with larger entertaining rooms for the local social season. Its **foul-water drainage system** ran under the kitchen wing — probably the "tunnel" found by Rev Smith and Ian Shaw.
3. **Bull Rectory** (1863–fire 1939, demolished 1944): Built partially over the Herringham foundations, particularly in the kitchen wing. Drive, stables, and coachhouse in the same positions across all three buildings.

The 1954–57 excavations (Rayner/Sewell with a mechanical digger) dug in the pre-1600 cellar area and missed the Herringham foundations entirely, which lay under the kitchen-wing rubble.

## The Cellar Trapdoor

An **external cellar trapdoor** in the courtyard gave unsecured access from outside to the cellars and thence into the house. Noted by Price's observers Colonel Westland (11 July 1937) and Ryberg (8 December 1937). [[andrew-clarke|Clarke]] identifies this as [[francois-darles|Frank Pearless]]'s primary access route for engineering poltergeist effects during the Foyster period.

## Domestic Scale (slbr-06)

In its heyday under [[henry-bull|Henry Bull]]: approximately 16 family members and guests, plus 4+ resident domestic staff, plus day-workers (5 garden staff, 2+ stable staff, tradesmen). The green-baize door divided family from servants' quarters. By the Foyster period (1930), all this was gone — one maid-of-all-work when available, one odd-jobber.

## Post-Fire Period (1939–1945)

After the fire of February 27–28, 1939, the shell of the Rectory remained standing — the upper storey and roof destroyed; ground floor rooms damaged but explorable. By 1943, gales had brought down all brick gables. The cellars were open where flooring had collapsed from above.

**[[cambridge-commission|Cambridge Commission]]** (1939–1944): 58 persons, 25 visits. Despite the ruins, phenomena persisted — footsteps, knockings, stone-throwing, horse-stamping sounds on the road, cold-spot temperature measurements, and a light in the upper rooms (Visit 22, April 30, 1944).

**Military use**: Soldiers attempting to billet in the Rectory during wartime manoeuvres were driven out by bell-ringing; an officer slept there and was awakened several times in the night by all the bells ringing.

**The 1943 excavation** (August 17–18 and 30): Led by [[harry-price|Price]], [[rev-ac-henning|Rev. Henning]], and a labourer named Jackson. The [[bones-excavation|cellar floor]] was dug at the site of the former well-tank. Found at 3 feet depth: human jaw-bone and skull fragment (young woman); gold pendant; Miraculous Medal copper plaque. Round well (6 feet deep) yielded a Sheffield-plate cream-jug (~1863). See [[bones-excavation]] and [[marie-lairre]].

**The "last phenomenon"** (April 5, 1944): *Life* magazine photographer [[david-scherman|David Scherman]] photographed the ruins; a brick was seen by Price, Scherman, and Miss Ledsham to shoot c.4 feet upward from the kitchen passage area. Scherman's photograph captures the brick in mid-air. Price calls it "the first photograph ever taken of a Poltergeist projectile in flight."

## Final Demolition (1944–45)

The ruins were sold to a local man and demolished for materials. By June 1945, "not one brick stands upon another." The Italian marble mantelpieces — including the monks' heads mantelpiece — were carefully removed, then found smashed next day; attributed to souvenir hunters.

The large yard-bell (rung paranormally many times) was presented to [[harry-price|Price]] by [[captain-gregson|Gregson]] and now hangs in Price's Pulborough garden.

Bricks used for hayrick foundations and American airfield runways throughout the district. Eric Calcraft (photographer, Bulmer) used oak beams for a garage. The Gooch family bought part of the orchard/garden for £300 and planned to build a home there, over the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]]. The Rectory [[rectory-cottage|cottage]] (four rooms over the stables) survived and was expanded to a real cottage.

**Final price**: Ruins and grounds sold for approximately £560 (the building had been insured for £3,500 when built, and offered for £500 freehold before the fire).

## Controversies and Competing Interpretations

The Borley corpus reveals deep divisions between believers in the supernatural and skeptical researchers — particularly the SPR authors [[eric-dingwall|Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Hall]], whose 1956 *Borley Report* [[the-borley-report|heavily accused]] [[harry-price|Harry Price]] of artificially manufacturing the haunting for profit and publicity [85, 86].

**On Price's conduct:** Skeptics noted that violent poltergeist activity only began when Price arrived in 1929 [87]. Skeptics also strongly suspected Price fraudulently planted the "[[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]" bones in the cellar in 1943 to provide a sensational climax for his sequel book, *The End of Borley Rectory* [90, 91]. R. J. Hastings's 1969 rebuttal (*An Examination of the 'Borley Report'*) challenged each of these fraud allegations systematically, concluding the SPR authors had overstated their case.

**On [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]]:** Skeptics concluded that Marianne faked the spectacular 1930s phenomena (including the [[wall-writings|wall writings]]) out of boredom, frustration with her older, ailing husband, or to draw attention to herself and her lover, [[francois-darles|François d'Arles]] [92–95]. Marianne herself gave highly contradictory statements in later life, sometimes admitting to playing tricks, while at other times insisting genuine, inexplicable phenomena occurred [88, 96, 97].

**On the fire:** While proponents pointed to the [[fire-1939|1939 fire]] as a spectacular fulfilment of the "[[sunex-amures|Sunex Amures]]" planchette prophecy, insurance investigators Sir [[william-crocker|William Crocker]] and Col. [[alan-cuthbert|Cuthbert Buckle]] bluntly accused [[captain-gregson|Capt. Gregson]] of committing arson to collect the £10,000 insurance policy on the dilapidated £500 property [98–101].
