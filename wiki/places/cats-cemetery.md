# Cats' Cemetery

A small burial ground situated at the far south of the [[borley-rectory|Rectory]] garden, on the edge of the main lawn by the stream. It was here that the deceased pets of the [[bull-family]] were laid to rest during their long incumbency, with headboards inscribed with the names of cats including "Jem," "Rollo," "Sandy" and others, placed in an orderly circle.

## During the Price Tenancy

The cats' cemetery was still a significant garden feature when [[harry-price]] took his tenancy, marked on [[sidney-glanville]]'s map of the Rectory grounds reproduced in *EBR* (see [[floor-plans]]).

On 14 August 1937, [[sidney-glanville]] noticed while patrolling the grounds that the cemetery had been extensively disturbed. A portion of ground had been dug over and the graves defiled; headboards had been removed and nearby bushes and grass had been trampled. Glanville and his son [[roger-glanville]] re-dug the whole area and discovered many bones — some apparently from horses and oxen, plus a few they couldn't identify. They took some remains home intending to show them to Price, but this appears not to have happened and they were presumably re-buried in Glanville's garden at Fittleworth, West Sussex.

Despite enquiries in Borley village, neither Price nor his observers could find an explanation for who disturbed the graves or why.

## Séance Connection

Price noted in *EBR* that [[seance-data|planchette sittings]] carried out by [[helen-glanville]] and her brother obtained the communication that "under the flooring… the heap of bones will be found from the garden and the pond…" — the pond or stream being located by the cats' cemetery. This led Price to suggest the unidentified bones could in fact have been human, perhaps part of the skeleton of the nun. He also pointed to a local tradition that part of the Rectory grounds was used as a burial pit for victims of the 1664–65 Great Plague — if such a pit existed, all the human remains found at Borley could have originated there.

## Current State

Today the cats' cemetery lies buried and forgotten beneath the lawns of the bungalows that now stand on the former Rectory garden.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

The **Cats' Cemetery** was a small pet burial ground located within the extensive gardens of Borley Rectory. It became a point of significant mystery and investigation during the late 1930s due to an unexplained disturbance at the site.

**Physical Description and Location**
*   **Location:** The cemetery was situated far down the Rectory garden, on the edge of the main lawn by a small stream (or pond) at the extreme end of the "Nun's Walk" [1-3].
*   **Layout:** The graves were arranged in an orderly circle, each marked with a small wooden headboard painted with the name of the deceased pet [1, 2]. Recorded names on the surviving markers included **"Gem" (or "Jem"), "Rollo", and "Sandy"** [1, 2].
*   **Condition:** By the time Harry Price and his observers rented the Rectory in 1937, the cemetery had been long unused and was heavily overgrown, hidden by a dense thicket of brambles [2, 4]. 
*   **Modern Status:** The cemetery was eventually destroyed, plowed over, and today lies buried and forgotten beneath the lawns of the modern bungalows built over the former Rectory gardens [5, 6].

**Associated Persons**
*   **The Rev. Harry Foyster Bull:** The cemetery was primarily associated with the eccentric Rev. Harry Bull, who was devoted to cats. He kept between 20 and 34 cats at the Rectory at one time, treating them like a menagerie and knowing each of them by name [7, 8].
*   **Sidney H. Glanville and Roger Glanville:** Harry Price's chief official observers, who mapped the site and later excavated the cemetery [1, 2]. 

**Key Events**
*   **The Vandalism (14–15 August 1937):** While patrolling the grounds during an observational vigil, Sidney Glanville discovered that the cemetery had been inexplicably disturbed by an unknown nocturnal intruder [2, 9]. An area roughly five feet across (described as "the size of a small room") had been heavily dug up and the fresh earth scattered [2, 10]. The wooden headboard belonging to the cat "Gem" had been uprooted and flung into the nearby bushes [2, 11]. 
*   **The Excavation (Late August 1937):** Baffled by why anyone would fight through dense brambles to dig up a pet cemetery, Sidney Glanville and his son Roger returned with spades and re-dug the disturbed area. They unearthed a large quantity of sizable bones [11, 12]. 

**Contradictions and Source Perspectives**
*   **The Nature of the Bones:** Glanville and his son noted that the bones they dug up were much too large to belong to cats (*Felis domestica*) [12]. **Glanville concluded they were simply the remains of farm animals like horses or oxen, humorously remarking later that they must have belonged to the local butcher** [11]. The bones were taken back to Glanville's home in West Sussex with the intention of showing them to Harry Price, but they were never officially preserved or examined and were likely just re-buried in Glanville's own garden [9]. 
*   **Harry Price's Paranormal Speculations:** Rather than accepting the bones as mundane farm animal refuse, Harry Price theorized the digging was connected to the haunted history of Borley. He speculated that the unknown intruder had either buried something sinister to hide a crime or dug up human remains [12]. Price suggested that some of the bones Glanville could not identify might have been human, pointing out a local tradition that the garden had been used as a plague pit for victims of the 1654–1655 Great Plague [13-15]. 
*   **The Planchette Connections:** Price explicitly linked the cats' cemetery to later planchette seance messages received by the Glanville family. During these seances, an entity claimed that a "heap of bones" belonging to the murdered French nun, "Marie Lairre," had originally been buried in the garden by the pond (the exact location of the cats' cemetery) [5, 14]. The entity claimed these bones had subsequently been removed from the garden and re-buried under the Rectory cellar floor, where Price did indeed discover human bone fragments during his 1943 excavations [14, 16].
