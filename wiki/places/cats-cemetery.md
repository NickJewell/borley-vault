# Cats' Cemetery

A small burial ground situated at the far south of the [[borley-rectory|Rectory]] garden, on the edge of the main lawn by the stream at the extreme end of the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]]. It was here that the deceased pets of the [[harry-bull|Bull family]] were laid to rest during their long incumbency. The cemetery was primarily associated with the eccentric Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]], who was devoted to cats and kept between 20 and 34 of them at the Rectory at one time, treating them like a menagerie and knowing each by name. The graves were arranged in an orderly circle, each marked with a small wooden headboard painted with the name of the deceased pet; recorded names on surviving markers included **"Gem" (or "Jem"), "Rollo,"** and **"Sandy."**

## During the Price Tenancy

By the time [[harry-price|Harry Price]] took his tenancy in 1937, the cemetery had been long unused and was heavily overgrown, hidden by a dense thicket of brambles. It was still marked on [[sidney-glanville]]'s map of the Rectory grounds (see [[floor-plans]]).

**14–15 August 1937 (The Vandalism):** While patrolling the grounds during an observational vigil, [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] discovered that the cemetery had been inexplicably disturbed by an unknown nocturnal intruder. An area roughly five feet across — "the size of a small room" — had been heavily dug up and the fresh earth scattered. The wooden headboard belonging to the cat "Gem" had been uprooted and flung into the nearby bushes. Despite enquiries in Borley village, neither Price nor his observers could find an explanation for who disturbed the graves or why.

**Late August 1937 (The Excavation):** Baffled by why anyone would fight through dense brambles to dig up a pet cemetery, [[sidney-glanville]] and his son [[roger-glanville]] returned with spades and re-dug the disturbed area. They unearthed a large quantity of sizeable bones — some apparently from horses and oxen, plus a few they could not identify. They took some remains home intending to show them to Price, but this appears not to have happened; the bones were never officially preserved or examined and were presumably re-buried in Glanville's garden at Fittleworth, West Sussex.

## Contradictions: The Nature of the Bones

> **Contradiction:** Glanville concluded the bones were simply farm animal refuse — "they must have belonged to the local butcher" — noting they were much too large to belong to cats. Harry Price theorized the digging was connected to the haunted history of Borley, speculating the unknown intruder had either buried something sinister or dug up human remains, and suggesting some unidentified bones might be human and pointing to a local tradition that the garden had been used as a plague pit for victims of the 1664–65 Great Plague [13–15].

## Séance Connection

Price explicitly linked the cats' cemetery to later planchette séance messages received by [[helen-glanville|Helen Glanville's]] family. An entity claimed that a "heap of bones" belonging to [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] had originally been buried in the garden by the pond — the exact location of the cats' cemetery — and had subsequently been removed and re-buried under the Rectory cellar floor. Price did indeed discover human bone fragments there during his 1943 excavations (see [[bones-excavation]]). He also pointed to the planchette communication "under the flooring… the heap of bones will be found from the garden and the pond…" as connecting the two sites.

## Current State

Today the cats' cemetery lies buried and forgotten beneath the lawns of the bungalows that now stand on the former Rectory garden.
