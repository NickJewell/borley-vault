# St Alban's Church, Holborn

An Anglo-Catholic church in Holborn, London, completed in 1863 and described at the time as "one of the ecclesiastical curiosities of London" [2]. It is associated with the Victorian Anglo-Catholic controversy and is the church where [[harry-bull|the Rev Harry Foyster Bull]] was married on 12 September 1911, a choice that caused a family feud and has been mischaracterised by at least one Borley author.

## The Anglo-Catholic Controversy

St Alban's became notorious in the mid-Victorian period under its incumbent **Father Alexander Heriot Mackonochie** (1863–1882), who practised a highly ceremonial, Roman Catholic-influenced style of worship — incense, lighted candles at the altar, priests facing east, frequent genuflection, and a confessional. Although this style was highly popular and succeeded in "packing them in," the **Church Association** took legal action against Mackonochie multiple times on the grounds that he was acting unlawfully, eventually forcing his resignation in 1882 [1]. Despite this, the church authorities allowed the Anglo-Catholic tradition to continue at St Alban's, and the church became famous for that tradition.

## Harry Bull's Wedding

On 12 September 1911, the 48-year-old [[harry-bull|Rev Harry Bull]] married 29-year-old widow **Ivy Brackenbury** at St Alban's [5, 6]. Prior to the ceremony, Ivy had taken up residence next door at **St Ursula's Women's Hostel** [2].

The wedding caused a family feud. Harry's siblings disapproved of his marriage to Ivy Brackenbury, who appeared to have come "out of the blue." The sources record three competing explanations for why the ceremony was held in London rather than at [[borley-rectory|Borley]]:

- **The "family feud" theory:** Robert Wood, author of *The Widow of Borley*, suggested that Harry's maiden sisters bitterly opposed the marriage, prompting Harry to quietly marry in what Wood dismissed as an "unfashionable London church" [4, 6].
- **The "parochial law" explanation:** This is contradicted by researchers who point out that the law at the time dictated a marriage must take place in the parish where the bride resided; because Ivy was living at the St Ursula's Women's Hostel next door, the wedding legally had to take place at St Alban's [2].
- **The "Anglo-Catholic" explanation:** Other sources reject the characterisation of the church as "unfashionable" and suggest that [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] likely had Anglo-Catholic leanings himself, actively choosing St Alban's because he wished to be married in a church associated with that highly ceremonial tradition [3].

> **Contradiction:** The claim that Harry's sisters universally boycotted the wedding due to a bitter feud is directly contradicted by records showing that his brother Alfred and his sister [[dodie-bull|Dodie]] both attended the ceremony and acted as official witnesses [2]. At the time of the wedding, relations between Harry's siblings and his new wife were reportedly "perfectly civil" [2].

Babbs and Mathias challenge Wood's dismissal of the venue and argue that as an ordained priest with probable Anglo-Catholic sympathies, [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] may positively have wanted to be married in a church of that tradition, rather than in his own simple country church.

## Destruction in WWII

The St Alban's church known to both Mackonochie and [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] was destroyed almost totally during the air raids of 1941 and had to be rebuilt after the war [3].
