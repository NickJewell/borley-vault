# St Alban's Church, Holborn

An Anglo-Catholic church in Holborn, London, built in 1863 and associated with the Victorian Anglo-Catholic controversy. It is the church where [[harry-bull|the Rev Harry Foyster Bull]] was married on 12 September 1911, a choice that caused a family feud and has been mischaracterised by at least one Borley author.

## The Anglo-Catholic Controversy

St Alban's became notorious in the mid-Victorian period under its incumbent [[father-machonochie|Father Alexander Heriot Mackonochie]] (1863–1882), who practised a highly ceremonial, Roman Catholic-influenced style of worship — incense, lighted candles at the altar, priests facing east, frequent genuflection, and a confessional. The **Church Association** took legal action against him multiple times on the grounds that he was acting unlawfully.

Despite his forced resignation in 1882, the church authorities allowed the Anglo-Catholic tradition to continue at St Alban's, and the church became associated with this style of service.

## Harry Bull's Wedding

Robert Wood, author of *The Widow of Borley*, described St Alban's as "unfashionable" and implied that Harry Bull chose it to avoid the awkwardness of holding the ceremony at Borley where disapproving relatives would be present. Babbs and Mathias challenge this dismissal. They argue that as an ordained priest with probable Anglo-Catholic sympathies, Harry Bull may positively have wanted to be married in a church of that tradition, rather than in his own simple country church.

The wedding on 12 September 1911 caused a family feud. Harry's siblings disapproved of his marriage to [[ivy-brackenbury|Ivy Brackenbury]], a young widow and nurse who appeared to have come "out of the blue," and the ceremony far from home may have served multiple purposes simultaneously.

## Destruction in WWII

The St Alban's church known to both Mackonochie and Harry Bull was destroyed almost totally during the air raids of 1941 and had to be rebuilt after the war.
