# The Crypt of Borley Church

An underground chamber beneath [[borley-church]], whose existence was long rumoured and whose entrance was only discovered in 1988. The discovery is the most significant architectural finding in the entire history of the Borley investigation.

## The Rumour and Earlier Searches

[[ethel-bull]] had reportedly told [[harry-price]] that there was a crypt under the church and that the Waldegrave coffins in it had been found disturbed on several occasions. When [[eric-dingwall|Dingwall]] and [[trevor-hall|Hall]] interviewed Ethel in 1953, she appeared to deny all knowledge of it. Babbs and Mathias suggest she may have become cautious after feeling that Price had over-used her earlier statement, and that Dingwall and Hall were "kept short of certain information" by their hosts — noting that by that time the villagers of Borley were angry about constant tourist influxes and Ethel had reportedly become the butt of local jokes for her ghost stories.

**August 1943:** [[harry-price|Price]] and [[rev-ac-henning|Henning]] attempted to lift the stone under the altar — guided by local masons, they used a block and tackle to raise a one-ton, five-foot-long slab in the central aisle. Instead of a crypt, it proved to be the original pre-Reformation stone altar laid face-down on a bed of sand. The stonemason remarked that an entry might be in the churchyard.

**26 May 1947 (Whit Monday):** [[rev-ac-henning|Henning]], [[james-turner|James Turner]] and John Durrant removed some wooden flooring in the sanctuary and discovered a **black marble tombstone** of the Rev Humphrey Burrough (rector 1722–1757; former headmaster at Sudbury grammar school; taught Thomas Gainsborough). Breaking into his vault, they found three skeletons in three feet of water; the vault was sealed again.

**1986:** A local clairvoyant visiting the church felt that "there should have been a door" near the font — and was told the entrance to the crypt had been at that point historically.

## The 1988 Discovery — 6 July 1988

Five members of the **East Anglian Group for Psychic Research Studies**, led by **Paul Moss-Kemp**, were examining the churchyard when they noticed a large, plain tombstone barely **two yards from the altar window**, surrounded by a low iron fence approximately four feet high. The stone had **no inscription whatsoever** — not worn away, simply blank.

One member scaled the fence and **tapped the stone sharply**. It produced a **hollow sound**. The stone was lifted. Beneath it: **a flight of stairs**.

### The Staircase

- Descended approximately **25–30 feet** into the earth
- Sides lined with bricks, but walls in poor condition — large sections of brickwork fallen down, revealing bare earth behind
- Steps littered with rubble
- On the right side going down: **a human skeleton embedded in the earth**
- Near the foot: a memorial stone to the Herringham family (who provided two Borley rectors before the Bulls)

### The Chamber and Door

At the foot of the staircase:
- A small chamber with floor littered with brick rubble
- Opposite the stairs: a **large ancient wooden door**, heavily barred both vertically and horizontally
- Etched into the woodwork: **"H F B" and the date "1921"** — the initials and a date in the incumbency of the Rev Harry Foyster Bull, who evidently had the door barred in that year

### What Was Visible Through the Keyhole

With torches, the group could see:
- A **huge underground chamber**, at least the length of the church; possibly further — arched and grooved ceiling
- Near the door on the right: a **small altar or table covered with a purple cloth**, with a crucifix standing on it
- To the left: **four or more lead coffins** encrusted with green patina, resting on stone supports
- On the floor: a large bundle of what appeared to be papers
- At the far (western) end: **brickwork showing signs of later alteration** — newer work replacing or built over the ancient wall

Following the modern explorations, authorities reportedly filled in the stairway at least partially and sealed the entrance, forbidding further access.

## Connection to the Tunnel Network

The western wall of the crypt showed later alterations. The east wall of [[borley-place|Borley Place]]'s cellars (adjacent to the church) shows similar signs, with an inner wall that does not reach the ceiling. The 1957 tunnel discovery (Tudor brickwork under Hall Lane) terminated at the north end in the Borley Place cellar area. Babbs and Mathias propose a secret underground network connecting:
- The crypt
- Borley Place (large old house next to the church)
- A now-demolished house that stood across the road

The purpose: **secret Catholic Mass** during the Reformation, when the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]] (lords of the manor for ~300 years, ardent Catholics) could not legally hold Mass. Sir [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Edward Waldegrave]] was imprisoned in the Tower of London for permitting Mass at Borley. The underground route allowed worship in the crypt while maintaining deniability.

## Rev Harry Bull's Barring of the Door (1921)

The door was heavily barred in 1921, by which time [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] was 58 years old and rector. The initials "H.F.B." and date "1921" on the door confirm he knew about the crypt, had access to it, and deliberately sealed it. Why he did so in that specific year is unknown. Ethel Bull's apparent knowledge of the crypt suggests it was not a secret within the family.

## The Moving Coffins Controversy

A major point of contention revolves around paranormal activity allegedly occurring within the crypt. In his 1940 book, Price claimed that Ethel Bull told him the heavy Waldegrave coffins had been found displaced and moved from their positions on several occasions — mimicking the famous "haunted vault" phenomenon of Barbados [17, 19–21].

> **Contradiction:** On 4 April 1953, Dingwall and Hall interviewed Ethel Bull, who they stated outright denied ever making this statement to Price, claiming she had absolutely no knowledge of a crypt at Borley — a contradiction used by skeptics to accuse Price of fabricating evidence [17, 18].

In 1972, investigator [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth|Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth]] countered this, claiming he possessed evidence proving that Ethel Bull did know about the crypt and had personally descended into the vault herself [18, 22]. Furthermore, [[wesley-downes|Wesley H. Downes]] published notes supporting the original legend, stating that prior to the entrance being sealed, coffins had repeatedly been found poised at odd angles after being straightened up [16, 23].

## Current State

The iron fence is still in position but very rusty; the tombstone has been removed; the stairway has been filled in at least partially; the area inside the fence is roughly level, covered in ivy and weeds. Three large iron bars were found lying in the undergrowth by one of the authors.

## Significance

The 1988 discovery does not directly solve any of the Borley mysteries. But it raises new questions:
- Does the hidden crypt (with its secret entrance and barred door) reflect a history of secret Catholic worship that might explain the Roman Catholic flavour of the [[marie-lairre]] legend and the [[wall-writings]]?
- Could a nun have prayed in the crypt, or been imprisoned or killed there?
- Why was the entry disguised as a false grave?
