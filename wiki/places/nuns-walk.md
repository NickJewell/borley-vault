# Nun's Walk

A long path skirting the far side of the lawn at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]], entirely visible from the octagonal summer-house. Named during the incumbency of [[henry-bull|Rev. H. D. E. Bull]] after the apparition of [[the-nun]] repeatedly reported along it.

## Physical Description

The Walk ran east to west along the south side of the main lawn, parallel with the property's boundary wall, for approximately 150 feet (46 m), beginning about 60 feet (18 m) away from the Rectory building itself [1]. It was originally bordered by a low hedge on each side [3] and was completely visible from the large octagonal summer-house on the opposite side of the lawn, as well as partly visible from the windows of the Rectory's principal living-rooms and bedrooms [2].

The path was farthest from the principal living-rooms. The nun is reportedly seen walking or gliding along it, typically at dusk.

**Two routes** are reported by different witnesses:
1. Leaving the path at the Rectory end (nearest the drawing-room), walking the full length of the lawn, and disappearing among trees overhanging the stream at the far end
2. Stepping over the low boundary wall on the far side of the lawn, traversing half the Walk's length, turning left across the lawn at right angles, and entering the clump of old trees near the octagonal summer-house

**The octagonal summer-house**: Built by [[henry-bull]] specifically to watch for the Nun [8, 9]. Two of its three visible sides are entirely open; a third is open for more than half its height — providing "a very wide angle of view." To its centre pole a table is attached; seating runs round the periphery. Both [[henry-bull]] and his son [[harry-bull]] spent considerable time here. [[harry-price|Price]] and [[vc-wall]] used it for their first June 1929 vigil. [[mark-kerr-pearse]] occasionally moved the camp bed out of the Base Room and slept in it. During the 1937–38 investigation, Price's *Blue Book of Instructions* explicitly ordered observers to take up position in the summer-house for a half-hour before and after dusk specifically to watch the Nun's Walk [10, 11].

**The smaller summer-house**: In the copse at the garden's far end, nearly hidden by overgrowth, also built by [[henry-bull]] for "communing with the spirits" in the early hours.

**The stream**: Runs through the end of the garden (crossed by a small bridge); empties into a pond. In one version of the nun's route, the figure disappears into the stream.

**The cats' cemetery**: At the Walk's end, near the stream — wooden headboards for "Gem," "Rollo," and "Sandy." During the 1937 observer programme, [[sidney-glanville]] found the grave of "Gem" had been extensively disturbed — dug up and refilled. Excavation turned up large bones that "certainly never formed part of the anatomy of a cat." Who disturbed it and why is unknown.

**Later status**: Following the demolition of the Rectory, the vast garden was sold off in building plots [6]. The Nun's Walk was subsequently split up, built over, and absorbed into the private gardens of the modern bungalows that now occupy the site [6, 7].

## The Origin of the Name

> **Contradiction:** There is confusion regarding when the path actually received its famous moniker. [[ethel-bull|Miss Ethel Bull]] gave contradictory statements, claiming on one occasion that the name "Nun's Walk" originated from her brother [[harry-bull|Harry]], but stating on another occasion that both her parents used the term and it had been called that for as long as she could remember [36].

## Key Sightings on the Walk

| Date | Witness(es) | Account |
|------|-------------|---------|
| 1885–86 | [[p-shaw-jeffrey|P. Shaw Jeffrey]] | Saw the Nun "several times" (testimony from 1942; self-declared unreliable by 1950) |
| July 28, 1900 | Four Bull sisters ([[ethel-bull|Ethel]], [[freda-bull|Freda]], [[mabel-bull|Mabel]], [[dodie-bull|Elsie/Dodie]]) | Dark female figure gliding with bowed head toward the stream; four simultaneous witnesses; figure turned when approached, showed "expression of intense grief," vanished |
| Late 1900 | [[ethel-bull]] + Rectory cook | Same figure leaning over garden gate |
| June 12, 1929 | [[vc-wall]] (+ Price thinks he saw something) | Shadowy figure moving along Walk in the dusk; Wall ran across lawn; "something certainly moved along the path" |
| February 18, 1938 | Mrs Lloyd Williams (BBC) | "Round, dark object" moving from tree near Rectory to central fir-tree, then vanished |
| March 26, 1939 | Rosemary Williams and C. Graham Browne | Observed from the Nun's Walk the apparition of a young woman in blue or buff clothing appearing in the burnt-out [[blue-room]] window [25–28] |
| June–July 1943 | Lt G.B. Nawrocki and Polish military officers | Black shadow moving slowly between the trees on the Nun's Walk on two separate visits [29–31] |
| June 20, 1970 | Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth (and later Roy Potter) | Grey-clad figure reportedly gliding about a foot above ground level in a modern bungalow's back garden on the former Walk site; passed through a modern fence; visible for approximately twelve minutes [34, 35, 7] |

> **Contradiction:** A major point of contention exists regarding [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s reporting of the famous July 1900 sighting. Price published that the sisters saw the nun in **"sunlight"** and that the phantom had an **"expression of intense grief on her face"** [37–39]. This was directly contradicted by the Bull sisters and investigator Lord Charles Hope, who noted the sighting actually occurred in **"late twilight"** around 9:00 p.m., well after sunset [40–42]. [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]] explicitly told SPR investigators that the light was too poor to see the figure's face, making an expression of "intense grief" impossible to verify [43, 44]. (Price's defenders noted he claimed it was *Elsie* Bull who got close enough to see the face, not Ethel [45].)

[[ethel-bull]] noted that Price's published account was not entirely accurate on the 1900 sighting: the light was poor; the distance about a hundred yards; no features distinguishable; no white colouring.

## Associated Persons

- **[[james-turner|James Turner]]**, the author and poet who purchased the Rectory site in 1947, physically cleared the overgrown Nun's Walk of brambles and weeds [14]. While working there in the evenings during May–June 1947, he and his wife Catherine repeatedly heard the distinct sounds of a happy, chattering garden party emanating from the Rectory site — voices that invariably faded away as soon as he stopped working to listen [14, 32, 33].
- **[[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]]** and [[mark-kerr-pearse]] were among the prominent observers who kept watch at the summer-house during the official investigation [12].
