# Salmonhurst, New Brunswick

Small farming community of approximately 150 people, mostly Danish immigrants, situated in the "wild country" near the border between New Brunswick and Maine [1, 2]; the Danish-speaking settlement where [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]] served as missionary from approximately 1910, and where he and [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Shaw]] married on 28 August 1922.

## The Settlement

Despite its small size, the village had a school, a shop, a post office, and a hotel [2]. The church, **St Ansgar's**, flew the Danish flag and held services in Danish, with men and women strictly segregated in the two aisles as was Danish custom [2]. The settlement's rectory was not overly large, but featured beautiful hardwood floors throughout. Lionel had made a little chapel in one of the rooms. Initially the house had no heating, but while the Foysters were on vacation in England in 1924, the locals kindly installed a new furnace under the house [2, 3].

## Lionel Foyster's Ministry

Lionel arrived in the area in September 1910, sailing to Bay du Vin in New Brunswick, serving as Missioner of Drummond [1]. He served several congregations across a large area in extreme weather conditions, with bouts of ill health leading to periods of absence. He wrote to the Shaw family in Ireland describing "blizzards coming up out of nowhere" and "summer wild raspberries, wild strawberries, and fishing for trout and salmon."

He characterised his Canadian parishioners honestly: many were illiterate, some areas unable to appoint magistrates for want of educated inhabitants. The fishermen and lumbermen had, he found, "little interest in God."

## Marianne's Arrival and Marriage

When [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] accepted Lionel's marriage proposal in 1922, she arrived at Salmonhurst in August after a five-day voyage from Liverpool to Quebec. She was immediately charmed by the local Danes, who were "happy, hospitable, hardworking, and full of life." She judged the rectory manageable after the hardships of her Irish upbringing.

They married at St Ansgar's on 28 August 1922 in a ceremony conducted by **Dr Allan Smithers** — who then caused a scene at the reception by objecting that Lionel, as Marianne's childhood baptiser, could not also be her husband. Lionel was 44 at the time of the marriage; Marianne was 23 [5, 10, 11].

Marianne's son Ian Shaw from a previous relationship eventually joined them in Canada [6].

## Community Life and Departure

During the winter of 1924–1925, Lionel, an enthusiastic producer of pastoral plays, successfully put on a children's operetta at Salmonhurst, with everyone in the community pitching in to make costumes [12, 13].

Due in part to a mild heart attack suffered during a swimming holiday at Prince Edward Island and the onset of severe rheumatoid arthritis, Lionel realised he could no longer face the harsh Canadian winters. He resigned from the Salmonhurst/Drummond parish in early 1927 to take the living at [[sackville-new-brunswick|Sackville]] [13–15].

## Contradictions and Discrepancies

> **Contradiction:** Ian Shaw later claimed that his mother had witnessed [[poltergeist-activity|poltergeist activity]] at Salmonhurst Rectory [16]. However, [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] explicitly contradicted this, stating that Salmonhurst "wasn't haunted" [7]. She explained that a previous rector (Clarence Minen) had fought so violently with his wife that he once took an axe to a door she had locked herself behind [7]. When a young Ian asked about the scrapings and split wood on the doors, Lionel thought the truth was inappropriate for a child and jokingly blamed "spirits" — a joke Marianne said was later taken out of context [7].

> **Contradiction:** Ian Shaw alleged that Marianne's behaviour caused major scandals in Salmonhurst, claiming she had affairs with both Father du Parc (a Roman Catholic priest) and a clergyman named Hall, which deeply displeased the Bishop of Fredericton [9, 17]. These claims remain uncorroborated by diocesan records or contemporary newspapers, though author Robert Wood notes they are "plausible" given her later documented activities in England [9, 18].

> **Contradiction:** Marianne corrected investigator Robert Swanson when he suggested that a "Reverend Hallard" had been consulted about hauntings prior to the Foysters' tenancy at Salmonhurst; she clarified that Hallard actually *succeeded* Lionel there, and that the preceding rector was the Rev. Britton [8].
