# The Blue Room

The principal bedroom at [[borley-rectory]] on the first floor, identified as the most phenomenally active single room in the house. Designated Room No. 6 on Price's floor plan. It overlooks the lawn and [[nuns-walk]]. Three generations of Bull rectors died in it.

## Physical Description

Large first-floor bedroom with two doors — one opening onto the main landing, one connecting to the adjoining dressing-room (Room 5). A window faces the lawn. The "cold spot" on the landing outside the Blue Room door is documented as a physically anomalous temperature zone (see below). A dressing-table with a large mahogany mirror was typically present.

## History of Deaths

[[henry-bull]] (builder-Rector), his wife, and [[harry-bull]] all died in the Blue Room. Harry Bull died there on June 9, 1927; the phenomena intensified notably around the anniversary of his death (especially June 8 in subsequent years).

## Key Events

**June 12–13, 1929 — The Blue Room Séance** ([[mhh-ch08]]): At 1 a.m., [[harry-price]], [[vc-wall]], the Smiths, and Misses Ethel and Freda Bull gathered. Rapping began at the window, localised to the mahogany mirror. Entity claiming to be Harry Bull confirmed its footsteps, said it wanted to attract attention, said a medium could help. At c.2 a.m., a cake of soap "jumped" from its washstand across the room in full lamplight — all witnesses saw it.

**1931 — Foyster period**: [[marianne-foyster]] given a black eye by an invisible blow on the landing outside the Blue Room door (February 26, 1931). Both doors locked spontaneously; opened by prayer and the Curé d'Ars relic. [[dom-richard-whitehouse]] was in Room 7 (adjacent) when the brass stiletto levitated and Mrs. Foyster was thrown from her bed.

**June 2, 1937 — The "strange coat"**: When [[harry-price]] and Ellic Howe established the Base Room, they found in the Blue Room a "long, dirty, mouldy, torn, rather moth-eaten dark blue serge lady's coat" hanging behind the door. It had not been there a fortnight earlier. No owner was ever identified. During the tenancy it disappeared for one week; then reappeared. Price and Rev. Henning both confirm it was not present on their May 19, 1937 inspection; Henning's man [[herbert-mayes]] confirmed it was not there June 1. It appeared between June 1 and June 2, 1937.

**Observer period (1937–38)**: The Blue Room was a primary focus of observer activity per the Blue Book instructions. Phenomena recorded there include: pencil marks appearing under observation; scrabbling and shuffling sounds; heavy thuds; the pendulatory blind (four witnesses watched it palpitate without any detectable draught); the luminous rectangular patch on the ceiling (S. G. Welles, February 1938); Bellamy's contact-breaker bell ringing while observers were all in the Blue Room. The "cold spot" outside the Blue Room door was experienced by at least three independent observers who did not know each other: Squadron-Leader Horniman's friend, Miss Reid, and Rupert Haig (each affected suddenly at the identical spot on the landing).

**May 9, 1938 — The golden apport**: Price and Geoffrey Motion found a 22-carat gold wedding ring hallmarked Birmingham 1864 (year after the Rectory was built) in the doorway between the Blue Room and dressing-room, during the last night of Price's tenancy, after six thorough searches of the room during the same day.

**February–March 1939 — Fire and post-fire**: During the 1939 fire, two figures were seen at the upper windows — one apparently a girl, near the Blue Room. On March 26, 1939, Rosemary Williams (signed statement) and C. Graham Browne saw a small female figure in blue at the burned-out Blue Room window aperture for several seconds — she "walked through the wall." Price confirmed the floor had entirely burned away, making it physically impossible for anyone to stand there.

## The Cold Spot

On the landing outside the Blue Room — directly above the ancient pre-1863 foundations discovered in the cellar by Glanville — multiple independent observers experienced sudden, unexplained intense cold at the *identical spot*:
- Squadron-Leader Horniman's unnamed friend (September 1937): "came over desperately cold"
- Miss Reid (August 1937): sudden terror, "pins and needles all over," icy hands; recovered on leaving the spot
- Rupert Haig (September 1937): woke at night with sudden cold "all over," hair standing on end; wrote from the ship afterwards confirming the experience

None of these three knew each other; none was told about the others' experiences.

## Significance

The Blue Room is the focal point of the haunting across all periods and occupancies. The concentration of Bull family deaths, the 1929 séance, the Foyster-period violence, the observer-period phenomena, and the post-fire apparition all converge on the same room. The "cold spot" on its landing — directly above ancient foundations — may indicate a geophysical dimension.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**The Blue Room** was the master bedroom at Borley Rectory and earned the reputation of being the most intensely haunted room in the building. It was the focal point of many dramatic paranormal claims, serving as a primary observation post for investigators and the site of several deeply contested events. 

Based on the Borley sources, here are the concrete details suitable for a reference article:

### **Physical Description and Location**
*   **Location:** The Blue Room was situated on the first floor (Room No. 6 on Sidney Glanville's floor plans), directly above the ground-floor Library (which later became Harry Price's "Base Room") [1, 2]. 
*   **Layout & Features:** Its main window faced south-east, overlooking the large lawn and the glass-roofed veranda [1, 3, 4]. A door in the left-hand wall opened into a small adjoining dressing room (Room No. 5), while another door led out to the main landing and the notoriously cold area known as the "cold spot" [1, 5]. 
*   **Furnishings:** During the Smith incumbency, it was simply furnished with beds, chairs, a washstand with a water ewer by the door, and a dressing table in front of the window holding a large, solid mahogany mirror with a wooden back [5, 6]. 
*   **The Decor Discrepancy:** The origin of the room's name is debated. Harry Price noted the room was decorated with gentian blue wallpaper [5]. However, Marianne Foyster later claimed she could not understand why it was called the Blue Room, as the walls during her stay were "pale gentian-pink" [1, 7]. 

### **Associated Persons**
*   **The Bull Family:** The Blue Room was heavily associated with the Rectory's builders. Both the **Revd Henry D. E. Bull** (in 1892) and his son, the **Revd Harry Bull** (in 1927), died in this room [1, 8, 9]. 
*   **Occupants:** It served as the primary bedroom for both the **Revd Guy and Mabel Smith** and the **Revd Lionel and Marianne Foyster** [6, 10].
*   **Investigators:** During the 1937–1938 Price tenancy, the *Blue Book of Instructions* explicitly required all official observers to spend at least a portion of their day and night (in complete darkness) keeping watch in the Blue Room [11, 12]. 

### **Key Events**
*   **The Screaming Girl Legend (Pre-1928):** A local legend claimed that shortly after the Rectory was built, a girl in a white or blue dress was attacked in the Blue Room. She allegedly clung screaming to the outside window sill before losing her grip and falling to her death through the glass veranda roof [13-15]. 
*   **The First Seance (June 12–13, 1929):** Harry Price, his secretary Lucie Kaye, reporter Vernon Wall, the Smiths, and two Bull sisters held a late-night seance in the room. Unexplained taps answered questions from the wooden back of the mahogany mirror [6, 16]. Later that night, a new cake of toilet soap reportedly jumped out of its dish and struck a water jug on the floor with enough force to dent it [17, 18]. 
*   **Poltergeist Activity (1930–1935):** During the Foysters' stay, the room was subjected to intense alleged poltergeist activity. Marianne Foyster claimed she was thrown out of bed by an invisible force, and the room was frequently found in extreme disorder with furniture overturned [19-22]. The Foysters also reported an enchanting smell of lavender and cologne manifesting in the room [10]. 
*   **The Tumbler Incident (December 14, 1931):** Investigator Edwin Whitehouse claimed that while standing in the Blue Room with the Foysters, a fragile glass tumbler dropped to the floor, circled around him, and came to rest without breaking [3, 23]. 
*   **The Strange Coat (June 2, 1937):** While setting up their equipment, Harry Price and Ellic Howe discovered a dirty, moth-eaten blue serge lady's coat hanging behind the Blue Room door. Price noted it had not been there during an inspection a fortnight earlier [24-26].
*   **The Luminous Patch (February 16, 1938):** Oxford undergraduate S. G. Welles, sitting in the darkened Blue Room, watched a clearly outlined, luminous rectangular patch of light (about a foot by five inches) move slowly across the ceiling for about a minute. Extensive experiments with torches, candles, and car headlights failed to replicate the shadowless phenomenon [27-30].
*   **Post-Fire Apparition (March 26, 1939):** A month after the Rectory was gutted by fire, Rosemary Williams and Charles Browne saw the figure of a young woman in blue or buff standing in the burnt-out aperture of the Blue Room window, remaining visible for several seconds [31-34]. 
*   **The Polish Officers (July 28–29, 1943):** Polish Army officers conducting a vigil in the ruins "rebuilt" the floor of the Blue Room by laying debris and planks across the burnt joists. From this makeshift platform, they reported stones being thrown and witnessing shadows [35-37].

### **Contradictions and Source Discrepancies**
*   **The Soap Incident Discrepancy:** Accounts of the jumping soap during the 1929 seance are highly contradictory. Harry Price and Vernon Wall claimed the *entire party* was in the room and witnessed it [18, 38]. However, Lucie Kaye later wrote that only she and Price were in the room when it happened [39]. Ethel Bull insisted *no one* was in the room at the exact moment the soap jumped [40, 41]. Furthermore, Mrs. Smith claimed the room was entirely dark and the Smiths only entered afterward to be told about the event [42, 43].
*   **Edwin Whitehouse and the Tumbler:** In 1940, Edwin Whitehouse wrote to Price correcting his published account of the tumbler incident. He stated he had actually *arrived just after it had taken place* and thus did not witness it [44, 45]. Decades later, in 1956, Whitehouse recanted this correction, claiming he had simply mixed up his incident numbers in the 1940 letter and insisted he *did* see the tumbler fall and circle around him [46, 47].
*   **Architectural Impossibility of the Screaming Girl:** Skeptical researchers completely dismissed the legend of the girl falling from the Blue Room window. Photographs of the south-east elevation prove that the veranda roof pitched directly underneath the Blue Room window sill, leaving absolutely no physical space for anyone to dangle from it as the legend described [14].
*   **Post-Fire Apparition Logistics:** Skeptics heavily questioned the sighting by Rosemary Williams, pointing out that because the 1939 fire had completely consumed the first floor, there was absolutely nothing for a physical human to stand on at the Blue Room window aperture, suggesting the sighting was an optical illusion caused by moonlight [48-50]. Price used this exact physical impossibility to argue the figure *must* have been a ghost [49].
