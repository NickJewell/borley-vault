# The Blue Room

The principal bedroom at [[borley-rectory]] on the first floor, identified as the most phenomenally active single room in the house. Designated Room No. 6 on [[sidney-glanville|Glanville's]] floor plans [1, 2]. It overlooks the lawn and [[nuns-walk]]. Three generations of Bull rectors died in it, and it served as a primary observation post for investigators across several decades.

## Physical Description

Large first-floor bedroom situated directly above the ground-floor Library (which later became [[harry-price|Harry Price's]] "[[base-room|Base Room]]") [1, 2]. Its main window faced south-east, overlooking the large lawn and the glass-roofed veranda [1, 3, 4]. A door in the left-hand wall opened into a small adjoining dressing-room (Room No. 5), while another door led out to the main landing and the notoriously cold area known as the "cold spot" [1, 5]. During the Smith incumbency it was furnished with beds, chairs, a washstand with a water ewer by the door, and a dressing-table in front of the window holding a large, solid mahogany mirror with a wooden back [5, 6].

**The naming discrepancy:** [[harry-price|Price]] noted the room was decorated with gentian blue wallpaper [5]. However, [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] later claimed she could not understand why it was called the Blue Room, as the walls during her stay were "pale gentian-pink" [1, 7].

## History of Deaths

[[henry-bull|Rev. Henry D. E. Bull]] (in 1892) and his son [[harry-bull|Rev. Harry Bull]] (on June 9, 1927) both died in the Blue Room [1, 8, 9]. The phenomena intensified notably around the anniversary of Harry Bull's death, especially June 8 in subsequent years. It served as the primary bedroom for both the [[rev-eric-smith|Rev. Guy and Mabel Smith]] and [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel]] and [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] [6, 10].

## The Cold Spot

On the landing outside the Blue Room door — directly above the ancient pre-1863 foundations discovered in the cellar by [[sidney-glanville|Glanville]] — multiple independent observers experienced sudden, unexplained intense cold at the *identical spot* (multiple witnesses):

- Squadron-Leader Horniman's unnamed friend (September 1937): "came over desperately cold"
- Miss Reid (August 1937): sudden terror, "pins and needles all over," icy hands; recovered on leaving the spot
- Rupert Haig (September 1937): woke at night with sudden cold "all over," hair standing on end; wrote from the ship afterwards confirming the experience

None of these three knew each other; none was told about the others' experiences. [[harry-price|Price]] himself noted the correlation with the ancient foundations directly below.

## Key Events

**The Screaming Girl Legend (Pre-1928):** Local legend claimed that shortly after the Rectory was built, a girl in a white or blue dress was attacked in the Blue Room. She allegedly clung screaming to the outside window sill before losing her grip and falling to her death through the glass veranda roof [13–15].

> **Contradiction:** Skeptical researchers completely dismissed this legend. Photographs of the south-east elevation prove that the veranda roof pitched directly underneath the Blue Room window sill, leaving absolutely no physical space for anyone to dangle from it as described [14].

**June 12–13, 1929 — The Blue Room Séance** (mhh-ch08): At 1 a.m., [[harry-price|Price]], his secretary [[lucie-kaye|Lucie Kaye]], reporter Vernon Wall, the Smiths, and Misses [[ethel-bull|Ethel]] and [[freda-bull|Freda Bull]] gathered. Rapping began at the window, localised to the mahogany mirror. Entity claiming to be [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] confirmed its footsteps, said it wanted to attract attention, said a medium could help. At c.2 a.m., a new cake of toilet soap reportedly jumped from its washstand across the room in full lamplight and struck a water jug on the floor with enough force to dent it [17, 18].

> **Contradiction:** Accounts of the jumping soap are highly contradictory. [[harry-price|Price]] and Vernon Wall claimed the *entire party* was in the room and witnessed it [18, 38]. [[lucie-kaye|Lucie Kaye]] later wrote that only she and Price were in the room when it happened [39]. [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]] insisted *no one* was in the room at the exact moment the soap jumped [40, 41]. Mrs Smith claimed the room was entirely dark and the Smiths only entered afterwards to be told about the event [42, 43].

**1931 — Foyster period**: [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] was given a black eye by an invisible blow on the landing outside the Blue Room door (February 26, 1931). Both doors locked spontaneously; opened by prayer and the Curé d'Ars relic. [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard Whitehouse]] was in Room 7 (adjacent) when the brass stiletto levitated and Mrs Foyster was thrown from her bed [19–22]. The Foysters also reported an enchanting smell of lavender and cologne manifesting in the room [10].

**The Tumbler Incident (December 14, 1931):** Investigator [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] claimed that while standing in the Blue Room with the Foysters, a fragile glass tumbler dropped to the floor, circled around him, and came to rest without breaking [3, 23].

> **Contradiction:** In 1940, Whitehouse wrote to Price correcting his published account, stating he had actually *arrived just after* the incident and thus did not witness it [44, 45]. Decades later in 1956, Whitehouse recanted this correction, claiming he had mixed up his incident numbers in the 1940 letter and insisted he *did* see the tumbler [46, 47].

**June 2, 1937 — The "strange coat"**: When [[harry-price|Price]] and [[ellic-howe|Ellic Howe]] established the Base Room, they found in the Blue Room a "long, dirty, mouldy, torn, rather moth-eaten dark blue serge lady's coat" hanging behind the door [24–26]. It had not been there a fortnight earlier; Price and Rev. Henning both confirm it was not present on their May 19, 1937 inspection; Henning's man Herbert Mayes confirmed it was not there June 1. It appeared between June 1 and June 2, 1937. During the tenancy it disappeared for one week; then reappeared. No owner was ever identified.

**Observer period (1937–38)**: The Blue Room was a primary focus of observer activity per the [[blue-book-of-instructions|Blue Book of Instructions]] [11, 12]. Phenomena recorded there include: pencil marks appearing under observation; scrabbling and shuffling sounds; heavy thuds; the pendulatory blind (four witnesses watched it palpitate without any detectable draught); a luminous rectangular patch on the ceiling; and [[bell-ringing|Bellamy's contact-breaker bell ringing]] while observers were all in the Blue Room.

**The Luminous Patch (February 16, 1938):** Oxford undergraduate S. G. Welles, sitting in the darkened Blue Room, watched a clearly outlined, luminous rectangular patch of light (about a foot by five inches) move slowly across the ceiling for about a minute. Extensive experiments with torches, candles, and car headlights failed to replicate the shadowless phenomenon [27–30] (single witness).

**May 9, 1938 — The golden apport**: [[harry-price|Price]] and Geoffrey Motion found a 22-carat gold wedding ring hallmarked Birmingham 1864 (the year after the Rectory was built) in the doorway between the Blue Room and dressing-room, during the last night of Price's tenancy, after six thorough searches of the room during the same day.

**February–March 1939 — Fire and post-fire**: During the [[fire-1939|1939 fire]], two figures were seen at the upper windows — one apparently a girl, near the Blue Room. On March 26, 1939, Rosemary Williams (signed statement) and C. Graham Browne saw a small female figure in blue at the burned-out Blue Room window aperture for several seconds — she "walked through the wall" [31–34]. [[harry-price|Price]] confirmed the floor had entirely burned away, making it physically impossible for anyone to stand there, and used this fact to argue the figure *must* have been a ghost [49].

> **Contradiction:** Skeptics heavily questioned the sighting, pointing out that because the 1939 fire had completely consumed the first floor, there was absolutely nothing for a physical human to stand on at the window aperture, suggesting the sighting was an optical illusion caused by moonlight [48–50].

**The Polish Officers (July 28–29, 1943):** Polish Army officers conducting a vigil in the ruins "rebuilt" the floor of the [[blue-room|Blue Room]] by laying debris and planks across the burnt joists. From this makeshift platform, they reported stones being thrown and witnessing shadows [35–37].

## Significance

The Blue Room is the focal point of the haunting across all periods and occupancies. The concentration of Bull family deaths, the 1929 séance, the Foyster-period violence, the observer-period phenomena, and the post-fire apparition all converge on the same room. The "cold spot" on its landing — directly above ancient foundations — may indicate a geophysical dimension to the activity.
