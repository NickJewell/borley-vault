# Arthur Hall, Sudbury

A late Georgian-period property located just outside the centre of Sudbury, on the road to [[long-melford|Long Melford]] [1]. Originally called Holgate House, it was renamed Arthur Hall in the early nineteenth century. The façade remains largely unchanged, though the interior was renovated beyond recognition in 1980 when two Italian brothers purchased the empty property, renamed it Sudbury Hall, and converted it into a restaurant [2].

## Associated People

During the peak of the Borley Rectory hauntings in the early 1930s, Arthur Hall was the home of [[sir-george-whitehouse|Sir George]] and [[lady-whitehouse|Lady Florence Whitehouse]] [1]. Sir George was a churchwarden at Borley and a friend of the Bull and Foyster families [4–6]. Their nephew, [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] (who later became a Benedictine monk known as Dom Richard Whitehouse), frequently stayed at the property [1, 7]. The house is also closely associated with [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel]] and [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]], the occupants of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] from 1930 to 1935 [1].

## Borley Connection

The Whitehouses were friends of [[lionel-foyster|Lionel]] and [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] during the 1930–35 incumbency. When the phenomena became too much for the Foysters in the summer of 1931, [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] persuaded Lady Florence Whitehouse (Sir George had died in March 1931) to offer the Foysters the sanctuary of Arthur Hall. Following severe disturbances in May and June 1931, Lady Whitehouse insisted the Foysters leave the Rectory and they were taken to Arthur Hall for several days [8, 9]. Marianne stayed for most of July, while [[lionel-foyster|Lionel]] only returned to the Rectory to carry out his parish duties. For most of June and July 1931 the Rectory was unoccupied at night.

On 6 June 1931, [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] arrived at Arthur Hall for a holiday to recuperate from a nervous breakdown [6]. This visit catalysed his heavy involvement in the Borley case, as he soon began witnessing extraordinary phenomena at the nearby Rectory [6].

[[harry-price|Harry Price]] and [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] visited Arthur Hall on 29 March 1939 to interview Sir George and Lady Whitehouse about their experiences [10, 11]. Years later, investigators from the Society for Psychical Research also visited the property to interview Lady Florence Whitehouse [12, 13].

## Contradictions and Source Perspectives

**The SPR visit date:** There is a minor contradiction in the sources regarding exactly when the SPR investigators ([[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]], [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]], and [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen Goldney]]) interviewed Lady Whitehouse at Arthur Hall. One record dates the visit to **3 November 1949** [12], while a passage from the SPR authors' own report states they called on Lady Whitehouse there on **3 November 1951** [13].

**[[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] and [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]]:** Skeptical author Robert Wood (*The Widow of Borley*) suggested, completely without evidence, that Marianne Foyster might have attempted to seduce Edwin Whitehouse while the families were closely associated through Arthur Hall [14]. Other commentators reject this, noting that Lady Whitehouse was a shrewd, sensible woman who would not have been easily fooled by trickery or such schemes [14].

**Edwin's reliability as a witness:** The SPR authors utilised Edwin's 1931 stay at Arthur Hall to cast doubt on his testimony. They argued that because he was recovering from a nervous breakdown at the Hall, his mental state prejudiced his accounts of the Borley phenomena, suggesting he was highly strung and easily duped by [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] [15, 16]. Edwin fiercely repudiated these attempts to undermine his evidence, defending both his own observations and his belief in the paranormal nature of the events [16, 17].

## Later History

The Whitehouse family eventually left Arthur Hall at an unknown date. By the late 1970s the building was in poor condition after lying empty. In 1980 it was purchased by two Italian brothers, renamed Sudbury Hall, and converted to a restaurant with much subsequent alteration. The façade of the building remains largely unchanged, but much of the interior has been heavily renovated.

On the early hours of 25 January 2008, the building was badly damaged by fire — "scenes reminiscent of the destruction of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in February 1939." According to the *Suffolk Free Press*, at the height of the blaze 100 firemen fought to bring the fire under control [3].
