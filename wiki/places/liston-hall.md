# Liston Hall

A hall near the village of Liston (adjacent to Borley) that was used during the Second World War as a NAAFI station, with Nissen huts erected in the grounds to accommodate service personnel. By the 1990s most of the original Hall was no longer in existence.

## WWII Use and the 1944 Apparition

During the early summer of 1944 — shortly after the D-Day invasion of Europe that began on 6 June — [[dorothy-lane|Dorothy Lane (née Tabbart)]], a twenty-year-old woman serving with the NAAFI, was billeted at Liston Hall and slept in one of the Nissen huts with three other young women.

In the early hours of one morning in that period, at approximately 5 o'clock, Dorothy woke suddenly to find a **shadowy figure dressed in long grey robes** standing at her bedside. Despite her intense fear she sensed no malevolence from the figure, and reached out her hand to touch it — but felt nothing. The blackout screens were in position, preventing any detail of the face being seen. The figure was of average height. After standing still for about ten seconds, it did not vanish suddenly; instead, it faded away gradually over the same period.

Dorothy screamed and woke the other three women in the hut, who believed her account and noted she was clearly in a state of shock. She remained shaken for several days. At the time she had a feeling that the apparition was some sort of premonition, though nothing untoward subsequently happened to her.

## Distance from Borley and Later Significance

Liston Hall lies in the hamlet of Liston, approximately a mile from [[borley-rectory|the Borley rectory site]]. Dorothy Lane only learned of the connection to the Borley haunting years later when a radio broadcast informed her that the human remains found under the rectory cellars had been interred in **Liston churchyard** in May 1945 — almost a year after her experience. This naturally led her to wonder whether she had seen the [[the-nun|phantom nun]].

The Liston Hall sighting is one of a series of apparition reports in the general area around Borley — not confined to the rectory grounds or church — supporting the view that the haunting extended beyond the specific buildings to the broader locality.
