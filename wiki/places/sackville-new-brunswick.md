# Sackville, New Brunswick

Small Canadian town on the Isthmus of Chignecto, in the county of Westmorland, New Brunswick; the Foysters' final posting in Canada before coming to England, where [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]]'s deteriorating health eventually forced him to seek treatment at St Luke's Hospital in London. The town lies approximately five to ten miles from Amherst, Nova Scotia [3, 4].

## The Town

Sackville had a population of just over 2,000 and was a much more civilised and comfortable posting for the Foysters than [[salmonhurst-new-brunswick|Salmonhurst]]. It featured wooden sidewalks and sat on the edge of the **Sackville marshes**. The town's defining institution was the **Methodist Mount Allison University**, the first university in the British Empire from which a woman had graduated [1]. The church where the Rev. Foyster served was a Gothic Revival design clad in local New Brunswick timber, on the edge of the marshes, approached by those wooden sidewalks. As at Salmonhurst, Lionel made over a room in the Rectory as a chapel for private prayer [1].

## The Foysters at Sackville

Lionel resigned from [[salmonhurst-new-brunswick|Salmonhurst]] in January 1927 following his mild heart attack during a swimming holiday at Prince Edward Island. According to the registers of the Bishop of Fredericton, he was instituted into the Rectory of **St Paul's, Sackville** on February 13, 1927 [2]. [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] initially found the town "quiet, quaint and 'prim in an old world way'" and missed the freedoms of Salmonhurst [1]. Lionel's liberal High Church ministry did not endear him to the tight-knit parish.

While at Sackville, Marianne:
- Enrolled at **Mount Allison Ladies College** and took university courses
- Was diagnosed with polycystic ovaries and had surgery at **Amherst Hospital** across the Nova Scotia border
- First heard the story of the **Great Amherst Mystery** (Esther Cox) while convalescing there — Esther Cox had previously visited her married sister (Mrs Teed) in Sackville [3]
- Took on care of the infant **Barbara Tower** (later [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide Foyster]]) when her parents died; the child had been born in West Sackville on March 20, 1928, and was formally baptised by Lionel at Sackville on March 3, 1929 [6, 7]

Marianne's son **Ian Shaw** from a previous relationship also lived with them during this period [5].

## The Amherst Connection and Sceptical Implications

Because Sackville was so close to Amherst, sceptical psychical researchers later theorised that the Foysters must have learned of the "Great Amherst Mystery" while living there — noting striking similarities between the Amherst phenomena and the subsequent [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] outbreak. They suspected [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] even used Walter Hubbell's book on the Amherst case as a "textbook" for the Borley hauntings, and pointed out that Foyster used the name "Teed" (the surname of Esther Cox's sister in Sackville) as a pseudonym in his own Borley manuscript [3, 11, 12].

Ian Shaw later claimed — though this is uncorroborated — that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] had witnessed [[poltergeist-activity|poltergeist activity]] at the Sackville Rectory itself [11].

## The Wall Street Crash and Departure

Lionel requested a leave of absence due to failing health (chronic rheumatoid arthritis) and financial difficulties following the October 1929 Wall Street Crash. He resigned from the parish with effect from September 15, 1929 [5, 8–10], before going to London for treatment at St Luke's Hospital for Clergymen and leaving Marianne in Sackville. His finances deteriorated further. By 1930, having received the invitation from the Bulls to take the Borley living, the Foysters decided to leave Canada.

## Contradictions and Discrepancies

> **Contradiction:** Several Borley sources exhibit geographic confusion regarding the town's location. While some accurately record it as being in New Brunswick (specifically Westmorland County) [2, 6, 12], others incorrectly state that Foyster became the rector of "Sackville, Nova Scotia" [3, 8].

> **Contradiction:** A discrepancy exists regarding the exact years of Foyster's tenure. While Bishop Richardson's official diocesan registers confirm his institution in 1927 and his resignation in 1929 [2, 10], Crockford's *Clerical Directory* (1931) reportedly listed his time at Sackville as 1928 to 1930 [3].
