# Pentlow

A remote, old-style Essex village three to four miles northwest of Borley [1, 2]. Its name derives from the Anglo-Saxon *Paente* (an old name for the River Stour) and *Low* (a hill), translating roughly as "the hill by the Paente river" [1].

## The Bull Family Dynasty

The Bull family were wealthy "squarsons" (squire-parsons) who held the Pentlow living continuously for 171 years [3], with four members serving consecutively: John Bull (1756–1802), his son John (1802–1834), Edward Bull (1834–1877), and [[felix-bull|Felix Bull]] (1877–1927) [3, 10, 11].

Pentlow is the **birthplace of [[henry-bull|Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]]** in 1833, the son of Edward Bull. Rev Edward Bull — described as something of a tyrant ("Old Bull"), who reportedly used his whip on villagers who failed to adopt a "grovelling posture" when he passed in his trap [12] — erected the **Bull Tower** (also called Bull's Folly) in the grounds of his Rectory as a memorial to his parents, John and Margaret.

The choice of the same architect and builder for both Pentlow and [[borley-rectory]] establishes that [[henry-bull|Henry Bull]]'s building of the Rectory was a family project consistent with an established pattern, not an unusual or eccentric enterprise.

## Pentlow Rectory and the Bull Tower

Hidden down a long, curving driveway, Pentlow Rectory is described as a massive, red-brick house with "weird and incongruous" design features, including enormous stained-glass windows that give it an Elizabethan appearance [1, 4–7]. In modern times, the residence is known as **"Pentlow Tower,"** taking its name from the adjacent folly [5].

Standing in the rectory's rambling garden is the **Bull Tower**: a massive 90-foot (or 95-foot, sources vary) octagonal brick structure [1, 5, 8] built in the Tudor style using red brick with white dressings, its base 16 feet in diameter with walls 3.5 feet thick [9]. It originally featured a central spiral staircase, lancet windows, a machicolated cornice, and an embattled parapet [9]. By the 1990s, storm damage had removed its roof, and pigeon droppings made the stairs inaccessible [8].

Edward Bull commissioned the tower in 1858–59, engaging architect John Johnson of Bury St Edmunds and builder Mr Webb of Sudbury [15]. A plaque on the tower states it was erected to the memory of his parents, the Rev John and Margaret Bull [16]. However, local legends claimed he built it to see the sea (which he failed to do) or to watch his farm labourers to ensure they were working hard [12, 17]; it was claimed that up to forty churches could be seen from the top [4, 15].

It is possible that Pentlow Rectory, the Pentlow Folly, and [[borley-rectory]] were all built by "Webb of Sudbury" and designed by architect Frederick Chancellor (or John Johnson — the sources give both names).

> **Contradiction:** There is a discrepancy in the sources regarding the construction of Pentlow Rectory. One source states that the original 17th-century rectory was demolished and completely replaced in **1886** by architect John Johnson [1]. However, another source claims the former rectory "still stands there — **older than Borley** by several years" [8] (Borley was built in 1863). Additionally, [[henry-bull|H.D.E. Bull]] was born in the rectory in 1833, decades before the reported 1886 rebuilding [1].

The village church features a tiny round tower of unusual form [3].

## The Bull Connection Ends

[[henry-bull|Henry Bull]]'s brother [[felix-bull|Felix]] became Rector of Pentlow in 1887. When [[felix-bull|Felix]] died in 1927 — the same year his nephew [[harry-bull]] passed away in the [[blue-room]] at [[borley-rectory]] — the Bull family connection with both Pentlow and Borley came to an end.

## P. Shaw Jeffrey

[[p-shaw-jeffrey|P. Shaw Jeffrey]], an Oxford friend of [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] who stayed at Borley during the 1880s and later testified to seeing the [[the-nun|phantom nun]], described one poltergeist incident involving a French dictionary at Pentlow Rectory — the only documented case of phenomena extending outside Borley to the family's other property.

## Poltergeist Activity

[[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] reported that during his Uncle [[felix-bull|Felix]]'s tenure at Pentlow, a maid exhibited psychokinetic powers. On one occasion, Harry and a friend watched as the maid continued her chores while the fire irons inexplicably flew across the drawing-room floor with a clatter [7]. [[p-shaw-jeffrey|P. Shaw Jeffrey]] also claimed to have witnessed poltergeist activity at Pentlow Rectory [18].

## Historical Note

An earlier Catholic parish priest of Pentlow, John Devon, is recorded in 1561 as staying with the doomed Catholic Lord of the Manor, [[sir-edward-waldegrave]], and bringing holy service items from Pentlow Church [14].
