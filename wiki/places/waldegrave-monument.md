# Waldegrave Monument

A sixteenth-century tomb at the north-east end of the nave of [[borley-church|Borley Church]], last resting place of Sir Edward Waldegrave (died 1561) and his wife Frances (died 1599). One of the finest examples of early classical architecture in rural England, and the focal point of several reported paranormal incidents in the church.

## Physical Description

The monument stands 14 feet high, 9 feet long, and 5 feet wide. An ornate stone canopy supported by six marble Corinthian columns covers two stone effigies: Sir Edward in armour with his head resting on his helmet; Frances in tight-fitting bodice and skirt, a flat cap, a large ruff, with a squirrel at her feet. The Waldegraves' six children are commemorated in carvings on the sides. The canopy top bears the family crest flanked by three shield-bearing cherubs — one left unfinished. A Latin inscription on the black marble edge translates:

> *"Edward died in the year of our Lord 1561, in the forty-fourth year of his age on the first day of September. Francis died in the year of our Lord 1599, seventy years of age, on the Eighteenth day of October. Lo within this tomb lies Edward Waldegrave and with him Francis, formerly the companion of his bed, but now his grave. A family untouched by fortune. Behold, O man, what honours, what descent, what wealth will profit thee, when breath of life shall leave thy frame! Thou seest nothing remain when the structure of man is dissolved. Earth its part reclaims, and so doth heaven."*

The tomb retains traces of its original colouring.

## Paranormal Associations

The monument is consistently associated with reports of cold sensations, unexplained sounds, and unease within Borley Church:

- **Canon Powell (1936)**: A dark figure in long robes moved from the hedge into the roadway near the old Rectory grounds and melted back — Canon Powell was on his bicycle; connected by some to the Waldegrave presence
- **Mr L.W. Butler (April 1974)**: Felt a distinct cold draught "almost as if it was coming through a hole" near the tomb; heard a regular thump-thump sound like a church clock, which then ceased — "like a breeze" but no source found
- **Stephen White (26 March 1976)**: "definite 'cold spot' near the Waldegrave tomb... like a breeze"
- **Philip Dickinson (28–29 July 1984)**: Flesh on arms stood up in goose-pimples beside the tomb despite hot weather outside; shuffling sounds only audible from pews adjacent to the tomb; sounds ceased when a sitter moved away
- **March 1949**: During catechism class, [[rev-ac-henning|Alfred Henning]] spoke the words "Christ is here" — immediately three loud distinct blows were heard close to the monument. All the children heard them; no explanation found

[[rev-ac-henning|Alfred Henning]] himself admitted reservations about the monument: he thought it "too big for a little church and liable to distract attention," and its bulk cast a shadow over the stall from which he sang evensong.

## Historical Significance

Sir Edward Waldegrave was the first of his family to be associated with [[borley-rectory|Borley]]. He died a prisoner in the Tower of London for permitting the saying of a Mass at Borley Hall during the Protestant reign of Elizabeth I. His wife Frances survived him by 38 years. Their family held the lordship of Borley Manor for 300 years (c.1500–1890).

For the role of the Waldegrave family in theories about the phantom nun's identity, see [[marie-lairre]] and [[phantom-nun]].
