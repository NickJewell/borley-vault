# Waldegrave Monument

A sixteenth-century tomb at the north-east end of the nave of [[borley-church|Borley Church]], last resting place of Sir Edward Waldegrave (died 1561) and his wife Frances (died 1599). One of the finest examples of early classical architecture in rural England, and the focal point of several reported paranormal incidents in the church.

## Physical Description

The monument stands 14 feet high, 9 feet long, and 5 feet wide, primarily constructed from "clunch" (indurated clay or hard chalk) and retaining traces of its original colouring [2, 4]. An ornate stone canopy supported by six marble Corinthian columns — noted as an early example of classical architecture in England [1, 3] — covers two stone effigies: Sir Edward depicted in plate armour with a ruff and his head resting on his helmet; Frances in a tight-fitting bodice and skirt, a flat cap, a large ruff, originally with a squirrel (the emblem of thrift) at her feet [1–3]. The stone squirrel was broken off and stolen at an unrecorded later date [26]. The Waldegraves' six children — three sons and three daughters — are commemorated in kneeling figures with respective coats of arms on the panelled sides [1–3]. The canopy top bears the family crest flanked by three shield-bearing cherubs — one left unfinished [1–3]. A Latin inscription on the black marble edge translates:

> *"Edward died in the year of our Lord 1561, in the forty-fourth year of his age on the first day of September. Francis died in the year of our Lord 1599, seventy years of age, on the Eighteenth day of October. Lo within this tomb lies Edward Waldegrave and with him Francis, formerly the companion of his bed, but now his grave. A family untouched by fortune. Behold, O man, what honours, what descent, what wealth will profit thee, when breath of life shall leave thy frame! Thou seest nothing remain when the structure of man is dissolved. Earth its part reclaims, and so doth heaven."*

Historically, during the September harvest thanksgiving festival, the columns of the tomb were decorated with entwined wild hops [14].

## Historical Significance

Sir [[sir-edward-waldegrave|Edward Waldegrave]] was the first of his family to be closely associated with [[borley-rectory|Borley]]. He was a prominent politician and Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster who died a prisoner in the Tower of London in 1561, having permitted the saying of a Mass at Borley Hall during the Protestant reign of Elizabeth I [1, 3, 5–7]. His wife Frances survived him by 38 years. Their family held the lordship of Borley Manor for approximately 300 years (c.1500–1890).

> **Contradiction:** There is historical ambiguity regarding whether the tomb actually contains Sir Edward's body. While the monument commemorates him, he died a prisoner in the Tower of London, and many believe his body was interred in the Tower chapel. The claim that his body was returned to Borley for burial remains a local tradition rather than a documented fact [21–24].

For the role of the [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave family]] in theories about the phantom nun's identity, see [[marie-lairre]] and [[the-nun]].

## Paranormal Associations

The monument is consistently associated with reports of cold sensations, unexplained sounds, and unease within Borley Church. Numerous psychical researchers and visitors have reported phenomena at the tomb, including L.W. Butler, Philip Dickinson, Donald W.L. Newland, Ronald Russell, and comedian Michael Bentine [9–13].

[[rev-ac-henning|Alfred Henning]] himself admitted reservations about the monument: he thought it "too big for a little church and liable to distract attention," and its bulk cast a shadow over the stall from which he sang evensong, leaving him in semi-darkness [8].

Reported incidents include:

- **March 1949 — Catechism Knocks:** During a Sunday morning catechism class on confirmation, [[rev-ac-henning]] spoke the words "Christ is here." Immediately, three loud and distinct blows were heard coming directly from the monument. The sounds were heard by the children, Mr. Henning, and Mrs. Henning; no explanation was ever found [4, 15, 16].
- **April 1947:** Donald W.L. Newland heard a distinct noise "like earth falling" from the monument, accompanied by an unnatural chill [13].
- **April 1974 — L.W. Butler:** Felt a distinct cold draught "almost as if it was coming through a hole" near the tomb; heard a regular thump-thump sound like a church clock, which then ceased. No source was found for either sensation [9].
- **March 1976 — Stephen White:** Reported a definite "cold spot" near the Waldegrave tomb, "like a breeze" [18].
- **1970s — Ronald Russell:** Located a centre of disturbance near the tomb, describing it as "tangible, like a swirling column of energy" that crackled like static electricity when a hand was passed through it [11].
- **July–August 1984 — Philip Dickinson:** Flesh on arms stood up in goose-pimples beside the tomb despite hot weather outside; shuffling sounds were only audible from pews adjacent to the monument and ceased when a sitter moved away [19, 20]. Investigators taped a microphone to a parapet via a broken adjacent window and overnight recorded a very loud bang, noises resembling falling tin-tacks, a grandfather clock tick-tock, and a 15–20 second "whispered prayer" in a human voice.
- **Michael Bentine:** Reported feeling a "chilling, uncanny aura" about the tomb; psychic Cheryl Carter felt "hostile emanations" so strongly she had to leave the building [12].

## Other Notable Occurrences

- **The Parchment Appearance (Late 1950s):** A visitor, Mrs. Phyllis Cowan-Douglas, was looking for a specific family name in the empty church. Finding nothing, she turned to leave, only to discover an old piece of parchment lying on top of the Waldegrave tomb with very old-fashioned writing bearing the exact name she had been seeking [17].
- **Canon Powell (1936):** A dark figure in long robes reportedly moved from the hedge into the roadway near the old Rectory grounds and melted back; Canon Powell was on his bicycle. Some connect this sighting to the Waldegrave presence.

## An Unnamed Daughter and the Nun Theory

According to one theory, an unnamed daughter depicted on the tomb might be [[arabella-waldegrave]], a primary contender for the identity of the famous [[the-nun|phantom nun of Borley]]. This relies on speculation connecting the effigies to the later ghost legends and is not corroborated by documentary evidence [25, 26].
