# Campsey Ashe

Village two miles south of Wickham Market in Suffolk, many miles to the northeast of Borley. The [[lionel-foyster|Revd Lionel Foyster]] was laid to rest here after passing away at [[dairy-cottage-rendlesham|Dairy Cottages, Rendlesham]].

## Lionel Foyster's Grave

The burial plot is at St John the Baptist Church. While both sources agree the marker is a plain stone, there is a minor discrepancy in its recorded inscription:

> **Contradiction:** One source states it bears the inscription *"Lionel Algernon Foyster, Priest 1878–1945. The Lord is my Shepherd"* [2]. Another source claims it reads only *"Lionel Foyster, Priest, 1878–1945"* [3].

> **Contradiction:** Accounts also contradict each other on the exact date of Foyster's death. One text records it as **18 April 1945**, while another places it eight days later on **26 April 1945** [2, 3].

## Post-War Investigation

In the 1990s, ghost writer Wesley H. Downes and Paul Moss-Kemp went to St John the Baptist Church on the anniversary of Foyster's death and held a vigil in the churchyard. According to their account (given to [[tony-broughall|Tony Broughall]]), around midnight they reportedly saw mist or smoke rise from Foyster's grave, move along horizontally, then rise vertically and drift across the churchyard, where it passed through the door of the closed and locked church. When Broughall asked what they did: "We ran for our lives!" (single witness account)

## Paranormal Tradition

Despite leaving Borley Rectory years earlier, Foyster's association with the supernatural allegedly followed him to his grave. According to local lore, his ghost is now thought to walk the Campsey Ashe churchyard, appearing as a figure wearing clerical skirts and afflicted with a noticeable limp caused by his chronic rheumatism (single witness; unverified).

## Significance

Campsey Ashe is the final resting place of one of the most important figures in the Borley story — the rector who lived through the "fifteen months" and documented the most intense phase of the haunting. The village is remote, consistent with the isolated outposts that defined Foyster's ministry from Borley through to his final years at Rendlesham. His grave is the most accessible physical memorial to the Borley incumbency.
