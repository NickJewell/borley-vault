# Hill Farm House, Chillesford

A large red-brick farmhouse with high roofs in the village of Chillesford, Suffolk, near Tunstall Forest and not far from Orford. The Fisher–Foyster household moved here from Ipswich in **June 1938**, renting for £40 per year, and stayed until May 1940.

## Description

Robert Wood notes that Hill Farm "stands well back from the road" and has "a striking similarity to Borley Rectory as the latter appears in old photographs" — both are sprawling, high-roofed, red-brick Victorian structures surrounded by trees. The parallel may be coincidental, but Wood finds it worth remarking.

Chillesford at the time was a remote village. Wood: "Even today, when the population of Suffolk has greatly increased and the county has undergone several post-war booms, Chillesford can still be described as remote and isolated."

## The Household

The occupants were:
- [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] (as "Mrs Fisher")
- [[henry-fisher|Henry Francis Fisher]] (frequently absent on business)
- [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] (as the invalid "father", bedridden, rarely seen by neighbours)
- [[john-knights|John Fisher]] and [[astrid-fisher|Astrid Fisher]] (adopted children)
- [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide Foyster]] (though she was increasingly away)

Wood notes that "the children were badly neglected" according to former neighbours and John Fisher himself: "Marianne frequently beat them, and devised punishments such as sticking their heads into buckets of cold water for what seem to have been dangerously long periods of time."

## Why Chillesford?

Marianne later claimed that Foyster found the Ipswich bungalow unsatisfactory (traffic, small rooms, neighbours able to see him in the garden) and wanted to live in the country. Wood finds these reasons "plausible" but suspects concealment was also a motive: [[sydney-glanville|Sydney Glanville]] had proposed visiting Foyster in Ipswich, ghost hunters were circulating, and the household's secret was precarious. Moving to a remote village provided additional protection.
