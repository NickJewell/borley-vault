# Hill Farm House, Chillesford

A large red-brick farmhouse with high roofs in the village of Chillesford, Suffolk, near Tunstall Forest and not far from Orford. The property stands well back from the road and is noted for its striking visual similarity to old photographs of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] — both are sprawling, high-roofed, red-brick Victorian structures surrounded by trees. The Fisher–Foyster household moved here from Ipswich in **June 1938**, renting for £40 per year, and stayed until May 1940.

## Description

[[robert-wood|Robert Wood]] observes that Hill Farm "stands well back from the road" and has "a striking similarity to Borley Rectory as the latter appears in old photographs." The parallel may be coincidental, but Wood finds it worth remarking.

Chillesford at the time was a remote and isolated village, situated north of Woodbridge on the margins of Tunstall Forest and not far from Orford. Wood notes: "Even today, when the population of Suffolk has greatly increased and the county has undergone several post-war booms, Chillesford can still be described as remote and isolated."

## The Household

The occupants were:
- [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] (living as "Mrs Fisher")
- [[henry-fisher|Henry Francis Fisher]] (frequently absent on business; reportedly away much of the time according to neighbours)
- [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] (posing as Marianne's elderly invalid father; bedridden, rarely seen by neighbours; the children referred to him as "Fafa")
- [[john-knights|John Fisher]] and [[astrid-fisher|Astrid Fisher]] (adopted children; John believed Marianne was his mother, though the children were instructed to call her "Morny")
- [[adelaide-foyster|Adelaide Foyster]] (though she was increasingly away)

Wood notes that "the children were badly neglected" according to former neighbours and John Fisher himself: "Marianne frequently beat them, and devised punishments such as sticking their heads into buckets of cold water for what seem to have been dangerously long periods of time."

> **Contradiction:** Marianne always maintained that she adopted the children out of a deep love for them, having despaired of having her own. This claim is directly contradicted by the consistent testimonies of her Chillesford neighbours and her adopted son John, who described severe neglect and physical cruelty.

## Why Chillesford?

Marianne later claimed that Foyster found the Ipswich bungalow unsatisfactory — the traffic was irritating, the rooms too small for his wheelchair, the garden lacked privacy, and he simply wanted to live in the country. Wood finds these reasons "plausible" but notes a significant counter-argument: [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]] had recently proposed visiting Foyster in Ipswich, ghost hunters were circulating, and Foyster was a well-known figure there. Moving to such an isolated village, Wood suggests, was primarily motivated by the need to conceal their highly unorthodox living arrangements from investigators.

## Departure

The family remained at Hill Farm House until **22 May 1940**, when they moved to [[the-whin-snape|The Whin]], a remote detached house in the village of Snape.
