# Bures-en-Bray, Normandy

A small town in the diocese of Rouen, Normandy, with a documented Benedictine monastery (Priory of Saint Etienne). Ivan Banks proposes it as a more historically plausible origin for the "Bures" referenced in the Borley planchette sessions than the commonly assumed Bures St. Mary in Suffolk.

## Location

Bures-en-Bray lies in the Seine-Maritime département of Normandy, near the River Béthune and roughly in the area between Dieppe and Neufchâtel-en-Bray. Its full historic name is sometimes given as "Bures Londiniers." It should not be confused with Bures St. Mary / Bures Hamlet on the Essex/Suffolk border, which sits astride the River Stour approximately six miles south of Borley.

## The Priory of Saint Etienne

Dom L. H. Cottineau's *Répertoire Topo Bibliographique des Abbayes et Prieurés* (published Macon) records an entry for Bures-en-Bray noting the existence of the **Benedictine Priory of Saint Etienne**. Banks discovered this entry when consulting the directory in the Paleography Department at Middlesex Library, London University.

*The End of Borley Rectory* contains a footnote acknowledging the existence of "Bures Londiniers" but states there was no trace of a nunnery there. Banks notes that while there is no evidence of a *nunnery* specifically, the priory's documented existence means there was *a monastery* — and if that monastery incorporated female quarters, a nunnery might have existed without separate documentation.

## Relevance to the Planchette Sessions

The Borley planchette sessions of 1937 consistently produced references to "Bures" as the origin or location of the nun Marie Lairre. The traditional assumption has been that this referred to Bures St. Mary in Suffolk — reinforcing the legend of the "Novice of Bures."

Banks challenges this assumption on two grounds:

1. **There is no evidence of a nunnery at Bures St. Mary.** No documentary record has surfaced of any convent in or near the Suffolk Bures.

2. **The planchette also produced "Le Havre" and French-language references** in the same sessions. If the nun came from a French convent, embarkation from Le Havre or Dieppe — both of which are close to Bures-en-Bray — would be geographically consistent. Dieppe is notably close to Bures-en-Bray in Normandy.

> "In the diocese of Rouen in Normandy, there is another Bures, its full name being Bures en Bray… There is an entry for Bures en Bray, recording the existence of the Benedictine Priory of Saint Etienne."

## The "HAIV" Clue

During Helen Glanville's Streatham planchette session (October 28, 1937), the entity spelled the name of its nunnery as **"H-A-I-V."** Banks finds nothing matching this in or near Bures St. Mary in Suffolk (the nearest is Haverhill, miles away and with no monastery). However, near Bures-en-Bray in Normandy there is a place called **La Haye** — phonetically close to "HAIV" as rendered through a planchette by a French-speaking entity.

## Significance

Banks does not claim to have definitively identified Bures-en-Bray as the origin of Marie Lairre. He presents it as a historically plausible alternative that deserves investigation — and notes that the archives at Bures-en-Bray and the regional archives at Rouen would be the next logical step for any researcher wishing to pursue the question.

> **Contested:** No researcher has yet confirmed or denied the existence of English-connected nuns at the Priory of Saint Etienne, Bures-en-Bray.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

Based on the Borley sources, **Bures-en-Bray** (also referred to in some texts as **Bures Londiniers**) is a location in France that emerged as a potential solution to one of the most perplexing mysteries of the Borley Rectory hauntings: the true origin of the "Phantom Nun" who allegedly came from a convent in "Bures."

Here are the concrete details recorded in the sources, structured for a reference article:

### **Physical Description and Location**
*   **Location:** Bures-en-Bray is located in the diocese of Rouen in Normandy, France [1]. 
*   **Geography:** It is situated not far from the port town of Dieppe [2]. In the immediate hinterland of Bures-en-Bray is a place called La Haye [3]. 

### **Key Events and Features**
*   **The Priory of Saint Etienne:** According to the French monastic directory _Repertoire Topo Bibliographique Des Abbayes et Prieures_ by Dom L.H. Cottineau, Bures-en-Bray was the site of the Benedictine Priory of Saint Etienne [1]. 
*   **The Origin of the Borley Nun:** During the 1937 planchette séances at Borley, an entity identifying herself as a French nun named "Marie Lairre" claimed she came from a nunnery at Bures [4]. Because the entity also appeared to reference a French point of origin ("Havre" or "Haiv"), later historical researchers proposed that she originally came from Bures-en-Bray in Normandy, rather than the English village of Bures [2]. Researchers noted that a journey from Bures-en-Bray to England could easily be made via the nearby port of Dieppe, or from "La Haye" (which investigators had previously assumed meant Le Havre) [2, 3].

### **Associated Persons**
*   **"Marie Lairre":** The alleged 17th-century French novice whose tragic story of murder at Borley is tied to the monastic sites of Bures [2, 4].
*   **Dom L.H. Cottineau OSB:** The compiler of the monastic directory that proved the existence of a religious house at Bures-en-Bray [1].
*   **Harry Price:** The primary investigator of Borley Rectory, who acknowledged the existence of Bures Londiniers in his second book, _The End of Borley Rectory_ [5]. 

### **Contradictions and Discrepancies**
*   **Geographical Confusion (Suffolk vs. Normandy):** For years, the Borley legend and investigators assumed that the "Bures" mentioned in the ghostly legends and séances referred to Bures St. Mary, a village six miles south of Borley on the Essex/Suffolk border [6]. However, no historical evidence was ever found to prove that a convent or nunnery existed at Bures in Suffolk [1, 6]. The discovery of Bures-en-Bray shifted the geographical focus, providing a location that actually matched the "French" and "Bures" clues given in the planchette scripts [1, 2].
*   **The Nunnery vs. Monastery Debate:** In a footnote in _The End of Borley Rectory_, Harry Price acknowledged Bures-en-Bray (calling it Bures Londiniers) but dismissed it by stating there was no trace of a *nunnery* ever existing in the town [5]. However, later researchers using Dom Cottineau’s directory proved that Bures-en-Bray *did* possess a Benedictine monastery [1, 5]. Proponents of the nun theory argue that if this monastery had incorporated a convent or nunnery within its estate, it would perfectly explain why no standalone nunnery was recorded in Price's sources [5].
