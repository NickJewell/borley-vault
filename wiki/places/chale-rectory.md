# Chale Rectory (Isle of Wight)

A rectory on the Isle of Wight where, from 1940, the Reverend [[rev-c-sinclair|C. Sinclair]] and his wife experienced phenomena strikingly similar to those reported at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]]. The Sinclairs' account was published in a letter to the *Church Times* in October 1945, the same issue in which [[mabel-smith|Mrs Mabel Smith]] denied that Borley had been haunted.

## Phenomena

Former maids at Chale Rectory claimed that a carriage and pair had been driven into the yard at midnight on many occasions — heard but not seen, a close parallel to the Borley [[phantom-coach|phantom coach]]. The Sinclairs themselves reported:

- Jingling as of harness; creaks, clangings, grinding and jarring sounds; a "coconutty" horses-hooves noise
- Preserves and objects knocked off shelves
- Stealthy footsteps on landings
- "Cold spots"
- Mysterious vigils by the family cat and dog outside closed doors of empty rooms
- Pencil-scribbling (established as due to a sub-tenant's child)

Two visitors then arrived and placed "little heaps of powder here and there with ancillary mutterings," returning three times at three-day intervals. After their final visit, all the curious noises ceased.

## Significance

The Sinclairs' account is cited in *Ghosts of Borley* as one of many parallel cases — churches and rectories across Britain reporting carriage sounds, footsteps, cold spots, and poltergeist-like activity. Underwood and Tabori note the irony that the Chale Rectory letter appeared in the same *Church Times* issue as Mrs Smith's denial: the parallel phenomena might have suggested to Smith that Borley's disturbances had normal explanations — though the authors pointedly questioned whether rats could account for "creaks, clangings, grinding and jarring sounds."

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

Based on the Borley Rectory sources, **Chale Rectory**, located on the Isle of Wight, serves as a notable comparative case of a "haunted" parsonage where spectacular phenomena were ultimately attributed to completely mundane causes. Its story played a direct role in shaping the later skeptical testimony of Borley's own Mrs. Mabel Smith. 

Here are the specific details recorded about Chale Rectory in the sources:

### **Physical Description and Location**
The sources provide very few architectural details about Chale Rectory other than noting it is located on the Isle of Wight [1]. Interior and exterior features mentioned in passing include a yard, landings, shelves for preserves, and empty rooms [1]. 

### **Associated Persons**
*   **The Reverend C. Sinclair:** The Rector of Chale who took up the living in 1940 [1]. 
*   **Mrs. Sinclair:** The Rector's wife [1].
*   **Former Maids:** Servants from the "palmier days of the living" who perpetuated the initial ghost stories [1].
*   **A Sub-tenant's Child:** The individual proven to be responsible for the mysterious pencil scribblings [1].
*   **Two "Exterminators":** Two unnamed individuals who laid powder to rid the house of pests [1]. 

### **Key Events and "Paranormal" Claims (1940)**
When the Sinclairs arrived at Chale Rectory in 1940, they were warned by former maids that strange things occurred there, including the sound of an invisible carriage and pair driving into the yard at midnight [1]. 

Eager for manifestations, the Sinclairs were not disappointed and reported a barrage of phenomena strikingly similar to the Borley hauntings:
*   **Auditory Phenomena:** They heard the jingling of harness, creaks, clangings, grinding and jarring sounds, and "stealthy footsteps" on the landings [1, 2]. They also heard a "rather stagey horses-hooves noise of a somewhat 'coconutty' quality" [1].
*   **Physical/Poltergeist Activity:** Preserves and other objects were inexplicably knocked off shelves [1, 2]. Pencil-scribblings were discovered on the walls [1, 2]. 
*   **Atmospheric/Animal Reactions:** They experienced sudden "cold spots," and the family cat and dog were observed keeping mysterious "vigils" outside the closed doors of empty rooms [1].

**The Resolution:**
The haunting was entirely debunked. The pencil-scribblings were discovered to be the work of a sub-tenant's child [1]. To address the noises, two people arrived and placed little heaps of poison powder around the Rectory "with ancillary mutterings" [1]. They returned three times at three-day intervals, after which all the curious noises permanently ceased [1]. From then on, the Rev. Sinclair answered any inquiries about his poltergeist with a single word: "rats" [1].

### **Source Perspectives and Connection to Borley**
Following a review of Harry Price’s book *Poltergeist Over England* in the *Church Times* on September 28, 1945, the Rev. Sinclair wrote a letter to the newspaper in the following issue detailing his experiences at Chale Rectory and their mundane resolution [1]. 

*   **Impact on Mabel Smith:** The Borley sources suggest that this letter directly inspired Mrs. Mabel Smith (the former Borley rector's wife). Struck by the uncanny similarities between Chale and Borley (ghostly carriages, stealthy footsteps, wall writings, and falling objects), she subsequently wrote her own letter to the *Church Times* (and later the *Daily Mail*), asserting that she now believed Borley Rectory was also haunted only by "rats and local superstition" [2]. 
*   **Skepticism of the "Rat" Theory:** While the rat explanation satisfied Sinclair and Mabel Smith, the authors of *The Ghosts of Borley* viewed the Chale conclusion with a touch of irony. They questioned whether the Borley/Chale phenomena could truly be explained away so easily, noting that it is "an open question whether rats can produce 'creaks, clangings, grinding and jarring sounds' unless they possess remarkable onomatopoeic talents" [3].
