# Chale Rectory (Isle of Wight)

A rectory on the Isle of Wight where, from 1940, the Reverend [[rev-c-sinclair|C. Sinclair]] and his wife experienced phenomena strikingly similar to those reported at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]]. The Sinclairs' account was published in a letter to the *Church Times* in October 1945, the same issue in which [[mabel-smith|Mrs Mabel Smith]] denied that Borley had been haunted.

## Phenomena

Former maids at Chale Rectory claimed that a carriage and pair had been driven into the yard at midnight on many occasions — heard but not seen, a close parallel to the Borley [[phantom-coach|phantom coach]]. When the Sinclairs arrived in 1940 they were warned of these stories, and they proved not disappointed. They reported:

- Jingling as of harness; creaks, clangings, grinding and jarring sounds; a "rather stagey horses-hooves noise of a somewhat 'coconutty' quality"
- Preserves and objects knocked off shelves
- Stealthy footsteps on landings
- "Cold spots"
- Mysterious vigils by the family cat and dog outside closed doors of empty rooms
- Pencil-scribbling on the walls (subsequently established as due to a sub-tenant's child)

## Resolution

The haunting was entirely debunked. The pencil-scribblings were the work of a sub-tenant's child. Two unnamed individuals arrived and placed "little heaps of powder here and there with ancillary mutterings," returning three times at three-day intervals. After their final visit, all the curious noises permanently ceased. From then on, the Rev. Sinclair answered any inquiries about his poltergeist with a single word: "rats."

## Connection to Mabel Smith and the Borley Debate

Following a review of Harry Price's book *Poltergeist Over England* in the *Church Times* on 28 September 1945, the Rev. Sinclair wrote a letter detailing his experiences and their mundane resolution. The Borley sources suggest this letter directly inspired [[mabel-smith|Mrs Mabel Smith]]. Struck by the uncanny similarities between Chale and Borley — ghostly carriages, stealthy footsteps, wall writings, and falling objects — she subsequently wrote her own letter to the *Church Times* (and later the *Daily Mail*), asserting that she now believed Borley Rectory was also haunted only by "rats and local superstition."

The irony of the *Church Times* issue placement was noted by Underwood and Tabori: Sinclair's letter and Smith's denial appeared in the same issue, suggesting to the authors that the parallel phenomena might be interpreted as validating the mundane explanation for Borley — though they pointedly questioned whether rats could account for "creaks, clangings, grinding and jarring sounds unless they possess remarkable onomatopoeic talents."

## Significance

The Sinclairs' account is cited in *Ghosts of Borley* as one of many parallel cases — churches and rectories across Britain reporting carriage sounds, footsteps, cold spots, and poltergeist-like activity. The Chale case serves as a recurring reference point in the debate over whether the Borley disturbances had natural explanations.
