# Long Melford

A large village in Suffolk, located approximately two to two-and-a-half miles north-east of Borley across the River Stour valley [1–3]. The name derives from the length of its main street and possibly from "the mill by the ford," or alternatively from an old stocking purse known as a "long melford" [1]. The village is noted for its fine fifteenth-century wool merchants' houses [1] and two prominent Elizabethan mansions: Melford Hall (seat of Sir William Hyde Parker) and Kentwell Hall [3, 4]. It is dominated by Holy Trinity Church, which is of cathedral proportions, as well as a smaller church, St Catherine's, built as a memorial by a former rector [5, 6].

Historically the town prospered from travellers and the wool trade [7]; it was served by the London and North-Eastern Railway (the Stour Valley line) [3, 8] and featured maltings, an ironworks, a coconut-matting factory [5, 9], and the Stafford Allen chemical and pharmaceutical factory [10].

## The Bull Inn

The most significant building for the Borley story is the **Bull Inn** (or Bull Hotel), an impressive timbered inn at the heart of the village [5]. Dating from 1450, it was likely built as the home of a wealthy clothier or woolman before becoming an inn around 1570 [11]. Its later brick façade was stripped away in 1935 to restore the original timber exterior [11]. Inside the lounge, a carved figure of a "woodwose" or "wild man" sits on a timber post, originally intended to ward off evil spirits [11].

In July 1648, a man named Richard Evered was attacked and slain in the entrance hall of the Bull Inn by Roger Grene, who was subsequently convicted of wilful murder [11].

[[harry-price|Harry Price]] and colleagues frequently stayed at the Bull Inn whilst investigating [[borley-rectory]], advising his roster of official observers that excellent meals and beds could be procured there [20, 21]. The inn served as the principal base of operations during the early visits to Borley from 1929 onward.

## Role in the Borley Story

**The Smiths' retreat (1929):** Finding [[borley-rectory]] unlivable due to a lack of amenities, freezing conditions, and the nuisance of ghost-hunting sightseers, the Rev [[rev-eric-smith|G. Eric Smith]] and his wife abandoned the Rectory on 14 July 1929 and moved into rented lodgings in Long Melford, running the Borley parish from there until April 1930 [12–14].

**[[charles-sutton|Charles Sutton]]'s alleged exposure (1929):** On 25 July 1929, *Daily Mail* journalist [[charles-sutton|Charles Sutton]] claimed he caught [[harry-price|Harry Price]] throwing stones down the stairs at [[borley-rectory]]. They immediately retreated to the Bull Inn in Long Melford, where Sutton reportedly attempted to telephone his editor to publish the exposé, but the story was killed over libel fears. According to Sutton, a shaken Price sat silently in the lounge of the inn [15–17].

**WWII requisition:** During [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s 1943 excavations at the ruined Rectory, he was unable to use the Bull Hotel as his usual base because the inn and the town's lodgings were entirely filled with military officers and their wives, forcing Price to stay at [[liston-rectory]] instead [18, 19].

When [[lionel-foyster]] was seriously incapacitated in the 1930s, he spent several months in a nursing home at Long Melford.

The 1885 "Melford riot" saw the town's rector, Rev Charles John Martyn, attempt to exert a calming influence on dangerous civil disorder [6].

## Associated Persons

- **[[harry-price|Harry Price]] and his observers** used the Bull Inn as a comfortable headquarters throughout the investigation years [20, 21].
- **Ernest Ambrose**, the long-serving organist of [[borley-church]] (1878–1972), lived his entire life in Long Melford and authored *Melford Memories* [7].
- **[[len-sewell|Len Sewell]]**, who excavated the Borley site in the 1950s, lived in Long Melford and conducted numerous planchette séances at his house there during the 1950s [22, 23]. His photographic associate, Leonard Rayner, was also a Long Melford resident.
- **Miss Mary E. Braithwaite and Sir John Braithwaite**, fierce critics of [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]], lived at Brook House directly opposite the Bull Inn [9, 24].
- **Dr Margaret Abernethy**, a senior partner in a Long Melford medical group, reportedly made a famous daylight sighting of the phantom nun in 1949 [25].

## Sceptical Theories Linked to Long Melford

Long Melford features in several rational explanations proposed for the Borley phenomena:

**Phantom smells vs the Stafford Allen factory:** The Foysters and other observers frequently reported "paranormal" smells at the Rectory, particularly lavender and eau de cologne [10, 26]. The Stafford Allen factory, which opened on the outskirts of Long Melford in 1899, was reportedly Britain's largest manufacturer of natural flavourings and pharmaceuticals [10, 27]. They milled lavender, peppermint, spices, and exotic ingredients. According to local knowledge, whenever the wind blew from the north, the scent from the factory's cleaning vats drifted directly up the hill to [[borley-rectory]], providing a natural explanation for the mysterious perfumes [10, 27].

**Ghost lights vs the railway:** Sceptics including [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] theorised that mysterious lights seen shining in the windows of the empty [[borley-rectory]] were merely reflections of trains passing on the Sudbury to Long Melford railway line [28, 29]. This was contested by others who noted the Rectory was surrounded by dense trees and that the old gas-lit carriages on the Stour Valley line were nowhere near bright enough to cast reflections half a mile away [8, 30].

**The Black Magic theory:** In the 1950s, a Mrs Guy Taylor proposed to investigator [[trevor-hall|Trevor Hall]] that the hauntings were the result of an active coven practising black magic and [[satanism-at-borley|Satanism]] at Long Melford and Borley [31].
