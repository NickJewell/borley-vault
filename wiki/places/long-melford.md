# Long Melford

Village in Suffolk, two miles north-east of Borley. The name derives from the length of its main street and possibly "the mill by the ford." Contains many fine fifteenth-century wool merchants' houses.

## Role in the Borley Story

[[harry-price]] and colleagues frequently stayed at the **Bull Inn** in Long Melford whilst investigating [[borley-rectory]]. The Bull Inn was the principal base of operations during the early visits to Borley from 1929 onward.

The [[smith-incumbency|Revd Guy Smith]] and his wife ran the Borley parish from Long Melford after leaving the Rectory in July 1929 — they moved to an address there for the remaining months of their incumbency (until April 1930) as the haunting and its publicity had become too much for them to tolerate at the Rectory itself.

When [[lionel-foyster]] was seriously incapacitated in the 1930s, he spent several months in a nursing home at Long Melford.

[[len-sewell]], who excavated the Borley site in the 1950s, lived in Long Melford; [[leonard-rayner]], his photographic associate, was also a Long Melford resident.

## Historical Context

During the fourteenth century, a Dominican friary was founded in nearby Sudbury (1248). Long Melford itself was one of the great centres of the East Anglian wool trade. The church of the Holy Trinity in Long Melford is one of the finest examples of perpendicular Gothic architecture in England and dates from the same medieval period that shapes the Borley story's historical background.
