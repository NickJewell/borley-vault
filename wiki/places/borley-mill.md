# Borley Mill

The largest historical building in the eastern section of Borley parish, situated near the A134 road and the river Stour. Some local opinion holds that it was the original manor house of the area, though [[borley-place|Borley Place]] — adjacent to the church — is considered by Babbs and Mathias to be the more likely candidate given its proximity to the medieval church.

## Location and Description

The eastern end of Borley parish is near the main A134 road linking Colchester to Bury St Edmunds, and borders the River Stour (which marks the Essex-Suffolk county boundary). The Mill is the most prominent building in this part of the parish, surrounded by modern bungalows, semi-detached houses, and the old Victorian school (now partly a private residence, with the schoolroom used for village meetings).

## Significance

Borley Mill lies at the lower (eastern) end of Hall Lane, which rises up the gentle hill toward the church, Borley Place, and the former rectory site. The presence of a mill in this location reflects the agricultural and water-powered character of the Stour valley area. Its nomination as a possible original manor house reflects the uncertainty about the pre-medieval settlement pattern of the parish.

**Physical Description and Location**
*   **Location:** Borley Mill is situated in the easternmost section of the scattered Borley parish, located very close to the A134 road (which links Colchester to Bury St Edmunds) and sitting on the River Stour [1]. 
*   **Architecture and Historical Significance:** It is described as the largest and most historical building in that part of the parish, with some speculating that the mill might have been the original manor house of Borley [1]. 
*   **The 1308 Extent Record:** The mill is documented in the "Extent of the Manor of Borley" from the year 1308. At that time, it was an operating water-mill with a mill dam and a race from which eels were caught [2]. 

**Key Events**
*   **1308 Valuation:** In the 14th-century Extent of the Manor, the water-mill was valued at 60 shillings per year on lease, with the fishpond and the catch of eels contributing an additional 5 shillings yearly in value [2]. 
*   **July 1932 (The Anonymous Letter Incident):** The mill served as a landmark in a famous anonymous letter sent to Harry Price. The writer claimed that in July 1932, after witnessing a mysterious old-fashioned coach while walking the Borley high road, she and her brother walked on until they were "opposite to Borley Mill," where they noted a policeman standing at the door of the Police Station [3]. 
*   **1947 (Cessation of Operations):** The building functioned as a working mill until 1947. During the exceptionally harsh winter of that year, the mill's wheel shaft split, bringing its operational life to an end [4]. 

**Associated Persons**
*   **Rupert Brown:** A modern resident of Borley Mill who created a signed, limited-edition print of Borley Church (restricted to 100 copies) to raise funds for the redecoration of the Village Hall [5].
