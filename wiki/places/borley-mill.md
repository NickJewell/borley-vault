# Borley Mill

The largest historical building in the eastern section of Borley parish, situated near the A134 road and the River Stour (which marks the Essex–Suffolk county boundary). Some local opinion holds that it was the original manor house of the area, though [[borley-place|Borley Place]] — adjacent to the church — is considered by Babbs and Mathias to be the more likely candidate given its proximity to the medieval church.

## Location and Description

The eastern end of Borley parish is near the main A134 road linking Colchester to Bury St Edmunds. The Mill is the most prominent building in this part of the parish, surrounded by modern bungalows, semi-detached houses, and the old Victorian school (now partly a private residence, with the schoolroom used for village meetings). The Mill lies at the lower (eastern) end of Hall Lane, which rises up the gentle hill toward the church, Borley Place, and the former rectory site.

## Historical Record

The mill is documented in the **"Extent of the Manor of Borley"** from the year 1308, when it was an operating water-mill with a mill dam and a race from which eels were caught. In that 14th-century valuation, the water-mill was assessed at **60 shillings per year** on lease, with the fishpond and the catch of eels contributing an additional **5 shillings yearly** in value.

The building functioned as a working mill until **1947**, when the exceptionally harsh winter of that year caused the mill's wheel shaft to split, bringing its operational life to an end.

## The Anonymous Letter Incident (July 1932)

The mill served as a landmark in a famous anonymous letter sent to [[harry-price|Harry Price]]. The writer claimed that in July 1932, after witnessing a mysterious old-fashioned coach while walking the Borley high road, she and her brother walked on until they were "opposite to Borley Mill," where they noted a policeman standing at the door of the Police Station.

## Associated Persons

**Rupert Brown**, a modern resident of Borley Mill, created a signed limited-edition print of Borley Church (restricted to 100 copies) to raise funds for the redecoration of the Village Hall.

## Significance

The presence of a mill in this location reflects the agricultural and water-powered character of the Stour valley area. Its nomination as a possible original manor house reflects the uncertainty about the pre-medieval settlement pattern of the parish. The sources contain no contradictions or conflicting accounts specifically regarding the Mill's history or description.
