# St. Bartholomew's Priory, Long Melford

A small Benedictine priory cell located near present-day **St. Bartholomew's Lane** on the main road between Sudbury and Long Melford, approximately one mile from Borley's parish boundary. Ivan Banks identifies this priory as the most probable historical source of the persistent legend about a monastery at Borley.

## Foundation and History

- **Founded:** 1115 by **Wulfric, Master of the Mint to King Henry I**
- **Order:** Benedictine
- **Location:** Near the Sudbury–Long Melford road, close to the River Stour
- **Suppressed:** 1538 (Henry VIII's Dissolution of the Monasteries)
- **Survived:** Only the ruins of the priory church remain; the Prior's lodging was destroyed in 1779

The priory appears in William Dugdale's *Monasticon Anglicanum* and in Roy Midmer's gazetteer *English Mediaeval Monasteries — 1066 to 1540* (1979), confirming its documented existence.

## Borley Connection

According to local historian [[georgina-dawson|Georgina Dawson]], this priory had **free warren** (access rights to woodland) within Borley's parish boundaries. Banks regards this as the key link: if Benedictine monks from the priory regularly entered Borley land for hunting or timber rights, their presence would have been known to local residents. Over centuries, "Benedictine monks in Borley" could easily have crystallised into the legend of "a monastery at Borley."

The priory also held land in Acton (Suffolk), Chelveton, Clare, Kedington, Melford, Thorpe, and Wratting — but not Borley itself in the documented tax records (Ecclesiastical Taxation of Pope Nicholas IV, 1291). Banks notes that absence from the list does not prove absence from the land: informal woodland rights may not have appeared in formal taxation returns.

## The Plague Connection

Banks develops a speculative but coherent theory connecting St. Bartholomew's Priory to the supposed plague pit on the Borley Rectory site. The plague of 1348–49 devastated monastic communities across East Anglia. The proximity of the priory to Borley, combined with the priory's likely use as a waystation for travellers, could have meant that plague victims — including monks — were buried at the isolated knoll above the Stour Valley that later became the Rectory site, rather than in the priory's own graveyard.

> "By far the worst of these [plagues] was that of the autumn and winter of 1348–9… What better place than upon an isolated and at that time possibly unoccupied spot on a knoll a good mile from the Priory itself across the river."

## Significance

The identification of St. Bartholomew's Priory resolves the "monastery at Borley" controversy without requiring a monastery on the Rectory site itself. The prior narrative (that monks held the Manor of Borley 1362–1364) was a misreading of the Christ Church Canterbury ownership. The actual monastic presence was this smaller priory on the opposite side of the parish, with documented land rights in Borley.

Banks summarises: "It becomes apparent that here indeed, at last, lies the most probable answer to the persistent stories about a Borley Monastery."
