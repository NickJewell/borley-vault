# The Haunting of Borley Rectory — Afterword

**Source:** Sean O'Connor, *The Haunting of Borley Rectory* (c. 2022)
**Slug:** `hbrs-aft`

## Summary

The author's personal framing and final assessment. O'Connor (who ran BBC Radio's *The Archers*) first encountered Borley via the *Hamlyn Book of Ghosts* (Daniel Farson) at Christmas 1978, aged 10. He met Farson himself in 1990 at a Plymouth theatre. In Spring 2022 he visited Borley village; met Michael Stebbing (stepson of Patrick Payne) who opened the church.

### State of Borley in 2022

- The church costs £50,000/year; 20 parishioners at a service held once every two months.
- CCTV installed to discourage ghost hunters. Rector Gill Morgan serves 14 parishes across 80 square miles.
- The Bull family graves have all been vandalised — stone crosses "knocked over or stolen by ghost hunters."
- Vince O'Neil's website has been taken down at the request of locals.
- Colonel Doerry (former occupant of Rectory Cottage) chased ghost hunters with a gun.

### O'Connor's Assessment

The author offers a four-tier analysis of the legend's origins:

1. **Structural causes**: clay subsidence, hard cement cracking, tightly fitted floorboards creaking in temperature changes; deathwatch beetle tapping; unusual acoustics in the enclosed courtyard.
2. **Cultural origins**: anti-Catholic Gothic fiction of the 19th century (convent memoirs, doomed nuns); local ghost legends; Dodie Bull's imagination and her brother Harry's encouragement.
3. **The Smiths' role**: Mabel Smith and Mary Pearson created stories to scare newcomers; Eric Smith deliberately inflated the haunting to try to force a move or depreciate the Rectory's value.
4. **The Foysters' role**: Marianne used the established reputation as a smokescreen for her affair with D'Arles. Some phenomena were Lionel's fictional testing; some were Edwin Whitehouse's (the wall-writings). The Marks Tey Circle séance coincided exactly with Edwin leaving — not coincidental.

Price's role: arrived at a house that already had a genuine if minor haunting and "helped" the phenomena by throwing stones — then built a bestselling book around it, manipulating evidence throughout.

O'Connor's verdict: "Several of the witnesses were adamant that there was something strange about the house and that at least some of the phenomena were real... The Rectory seemed to infect successive residents and investigators like a contagious disease." Marianne herself told Swanson: "there are other things inhabiting the house beside human beings."

"The haunting of Borley Rectory may be interpreted as a curious parable... demonstrating the commanding power of the story over the facts in uncertain times, the real ghosts perhaps manifestations of deeply held prejudices of a small, isolated and insular community towards anything — anyone — that it regards as 'other': strangers, foreigners, autonomous women or believers in an old religion long dismissed as superstition."
