# The Most Haunted House in England — Ch. 4: The Story of the Rectory

*Harry Price (1940)*

A meticulous account of the Rectory's historical background, physical layout, and grounds — compiled from Price's own inspections and [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville's]] architectural plans.

## Historical Background

**Name:** Borley derives from the Anglo-Saxon *Barlea* ("boar's clearing" or "boar's pasture"), recorded in Domesday Book (1086).

**Benedictine connection:** [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard Whitehouse]] traced a reference in Dugdale's *Monasticon Anglicanum*: in 1362, Edward III gave Borley Manor to the Prior and Community of Christ Church, Canterbury. A Henry VIII-period entry gives a valuation of £32 14s. 4d. Price concludes: "definite evidence that Borley Manor became the property of Benedictine monks in the fourteenth century."

Simon of Sudbury — Archbishop of Canterbury, Chancellor of England, beheaded by the Peasants' Revolt on 14 June 1381 — is associated with the area; a suggestion is made that he haunts Borley and the neighbourhood.

**Waldegrave family:** Held the manor and were patrons of [[borley-church|Borley Church]] for approximately three hundred years. Sir Edward Waldegrave (d. 1 September 1561) is buried at the east end of the nave with his wife Frances (survived him 38 years); their six children represented on the tomb sides; daughter Magdala d. 1598 commemorated on the north chancel wall. Sir Edward had been imprisoned under Edward VI, knighted by Mary I; later MP for Essex and Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster; died in the Tower. The Waldegrave family seat is now Chewton Priory, Bath.

## The Bull Incumbencies

- **[[henry-bull|Henry Bull]]**: born 1833; d. 2 May 1892; rector 1862–92; built the Rectory in 1863 on foundations of earlier dwellings; added a wing 1875–6 to accommodate a growing family (14 children, 12 surviving)
- **[[harry-bull|Harry Bull]]**: born 1862; d. 9 June 1927; rector 1892–1927; buried in the churchyard

## The Bricked-Up Dining Room Window

The window in the dining room facing the carriage drive was permanently blocked up by Henry Bull. From three independent sources Price heard that the window was bricked up because the Nun "habitually peered into the room from the drive." He systematically demolishes the alternative "privacy" explanation:

- The carriage drive is wide enough that it is "a sheer impossibility to see through the window from the road"
- A belt of trees and hedge form an "impenetrable screen" between road and drive
- A simple curtain or blind would have sufficed for any normal observer

> "Whatever the reason, a drastic remedy was decided upon, and the window of the principal living-room was strongly and permanently bricked up, as it remains to-day, completely spoiling this side of the house."

See [[the-nun]] — this is among the most striking pieces of evidence for the Nun as a witnessed recurring presence.

## Physical Description

- Originally L-shaped (1863); wing added 1875–6 making it nearly rectangular, enclosing an uncovered bricked courtyard
- Two storeys; two bay windows overlooking the lawn; French windows from library to lawn
- Ground floor: 10 rooms plus cupboards, storehouses, cellars; first floor: 14 rooms including chapel, Blue Room, bathroom
- No gas, electric light, or mains water (water pumped by hand-wheel into cistern); no central heating
- Marble mantelpieces in dining-room and drawing-room: Italian origin, exhibited at the Great Exhibition (1851); dining-room mantelpiece adorned with "quaint marble busts of two monks"
- Domestic servants' windows heavily barred — to keep maids in at night, not to exclude burglars

## The Cellars and the Cold Spot

A portion of the cellar floor had subsided. On **1 November 1937**, Glanville and Mark Kerr-Pearse excavated it:

- Found foundations of a much older building — two-inch bricks, not the 1863 type
- A cellar wall running diagonally under the library is composed of these older bricks
- The older structure's foundations faced roughly south

Directly above this subsidence — on the first-floor landing outside the [[blue-room]] — is the location of the documented "cold spot," where multiple observers felt cold and shivered. Price explicitly connects the two findings: the cold spot sits over the ancient foundations.

## The Well and the Tunnel

- 71-foot water depth; 80 feet total
- At **61.9 feet** below ground, Glanville's flare and sinker were "sucked or blown into a cavity on the house side of the well"
- Price: "This suggests that there must be a passage or hole of some sort leading out of the well side... Perhaps it leads into one of the subterranean passages"
- Connects directly to the tunnel evidence discussed in [[gotb-ch05]]

## The Bell System

Old-fashioned bell-levers with wires anchored to the rafters; 20 bells hung in the kitchen passage — the system underlying all reports of simultaneous bell-ringing throughout the case. See [[bell-ringing]].
