# Source: *Ghosts That Will Not Die* — Ch. 1: The Most Haunted House In England

**Author:** Vincent O'Neil

A narrative overview of the entire Borley story from a 1990s retrospective perspective, drawing on all published sources. Introduces the cast of characters and key events from the Rectory's construction through to post-fire phenomena, with emphasis on Marianne's 1978 testimony.

## Key Content

### Henning's research into Borley's history

A.C. Henning (rector 1936) researched the Domesday Book: Borley Manor listed 1086; first rector of record Peter de Cacheporc, installed April 28, 1236; church built in the twelfth century; tower and brick porch added c.1500.

Henry Bull's wife is named here as **Caroline Sarah Foyster** — establishing the family connection. The rectory plot was almost four acres; a cottage adjoined with stable below and servants quarters above. The dining room fireplace included effigies of monks heads, apparently "installed by Bull to perpetuate a legend in which he may have believed."

### Henry Bull's death

Henry Bull died May 7, 1892 — **reportedly of syphilis** (detail not in prior sources). His nickname "Carlos" later became significant.

### Harry Bull's marriage

Harry Bull married Ivy Johnson September 12, 1911. Ivy brought her daughter Constance (c.10) from a prior marriage to Harold Brackenberry. Harry apparently treated his wife cruelly, possibly using the excuse that he had unknowingly married a divorced woman. In later years the marriage was so unhappy that "Constance and her mother would hide the cream from the house cow so Harry should not have his share."

Harry died June 9, 1927 — said to have "communications with spirits" and claimed he would throw moth balls after his death.

### The Smiths

On October 2, 1928, Rev. Guy Eric Smith and wife moved to Borley and were **not told it was "haunted."** Smith heard the words "Don't Carlos, don't" — a woman's voice from the archway.

Very early in the Smith incumbency, Mrs. Smith found **a skull of a young woman wrapped in paper**; Smith buried it in the churchyard.

Twelve clergy reportedly refused the Borley position before the Smiths accepted (1927–28).

### Price's first visit (June 12, 1929)

Price's ghost-hunting kit listed in detail: soft felt overshoes, measuring tape, room-sealing gear, camera, movie camera, thermometer, finger-print kit. This equipment was pictured in the 1939 *Encyclopedia Britannica Year Book* under Price's article on "Psychical Research."

Blue Room séance June 12–13: with Price, two Bull sisters, Mr. and Mrs. Smith, Wall, and Lucie Kaye. Harry Bull appeared and tapped that "he was unhappy, and that there was money trouble. When he told the group that he had been killed, the Smiths stopped the séance immediately."

**Mary Pearson** identified as the maid who saw the nun — later identified as having "played at least one prank at the Rectory when she put her apron over her head and pretended to be the nun." She later married Fred Tatum (from Baines's unpublished research). Pearson witnessed keys jumping from locks; saw the phantom coach on two occasions and a headless man on another.

**Charles Sutton** of the *Daily Mail* visited July 25 with Price. Claimed Price threw stones at him, but could not convince his editor to print the accusation.

### Marianne's 1978 testimony (Owen/Mitchell)

O'Neil quotes the key Owen/Mitchell (1978) finding: Marianne stated "flatly that by far the majority [of events] were completely invented by Lionel, as part of [a book he was writing]. However, she stated that from time to time odd things would happen of a poltergeist nature which would puzzle her..."

Marianne confirmed: "the events of the bottle, tumbler, and stiletto happened as described."

Owen/Mitchell conclusion: "We feel that what Marianne has told us makes sense. It is logical, and... everything she has told us fits in with what is already known about the Borley hauntings during the Foyster incumbency. It has the ring of truth."

### Price's view (1946)

Price told Dingwall in 1946: "if you cut out the Foysters, the Bulls, the Smiths, etc., something still remains."

In a 1938 letter to the BBC, Price admitted "the Foysters play a very small part — so far as we are concerned — in the Borley story."
