# New Horizons Foundation: Borley Rectory Report (1986)

**Full title:** *A Report by New Horizons Foundation of some Investigations made into this alleged Haunting*
**Authors:** New Horizons Foundation (principally Iris M. Owen and Pauline Mitchell)
**Date:** October 1986 (report prepared; published after Marianne Foyster's death in 1992)
**Background:** The Owen/Mitchell 1978 conversation with Marianne Foyster, funded by the Perrot Warwick Fellowship of Trinity College, Cambridge. Published in the Journal of the Society for Psychical Research. The public report was withheld during Marianne's lifetime per her explicit request; this version was published after her death.

This is the published version of the Owen/Mitchell 1978 report cited in many other sources, most significantly by Vincent O'Neil in *Ghosts That Will Not Die* and throughout the Borley literature after 1980. Prior to this publication it was known only through quotations and paraphrases.

---

## Introduction

**New Horizons Foundation context:**
- Iris Owen was associated with Trevor Hall during his 1950s SPR investigation of the Foyster period; Hall's suggestion of poltergeist literature review led the Foundation to its major work on the Philip Experiment (the creation of a fictional ghost through collective belief)
- Owen reports knowing the Borley story from her 1930s reading, when Lionel Foyster was still at the Rectory
- In fall 1978 they had an opportunity to talk with Marianne Foyster in Canada and record her account
- They had promised not to publish until after Marianne's death; Marianne died in 1992 aged about 86

**Their characterization of the Borley case:**
The authors position Borley as a "benchmark" poltergeist case that had become overlaid with so many competing claims and investigations that it was almost impossible to disentangle truth from fiction. They note that both committed believers and committed skeptics had become equally unhelpful. Their approach: go directly to Marianne.

**Key connection to Hall and Owen's own history:**
"As a result of that investigation by Mr. Hall, who was also under supervision by George Owen in his conducting of telepathy tests for a Fellowship at Trinity College, that we ourselves became especially interested in poltergeist phenomena... The unspoken thought was that we might find that all other cases were as patently fraudulent as Trevor believed that Borley was, but in the event, it turned out the opposite. Borley is unique in its complications and admixture of truth and fraud."

This is a remarkable statement: **Iris Owen, who worked alongside Hall and shared his initial skeptical hypothesis, emerged from her research believing Borley is genuinely unique and contains both truth and fraud** — not simply a total fabrication.

## The Legend of Borley Rectory (Historical Overview)

The Foundation's substantive essay on Borley, covering the full history as they understood it in 1986.

**Bull period (1863–1927):**
The authors characterize the Bull incumbency as typical rural English ghost tradition — phantom coaches, headless horsemen, nun apparitions — stories that "would have completely faded in the normal course of events as the countryside became more fully integrated into the modern age." The phenomena were traditional and culturally expected; no spectacular poltergeist activity was recorded. The nun and coach were consistent with legends that "abound all over England, especially in the more remote rural areas of the Eastern parts of England."

**Smith period (1928–1929):**
A clear shift to active poltergeist phenomena: dragging footsteps, mysterious lights, voices, objects moved and thrown, bells ringing spontaneously. The authors characterize the Smiths as naive newcomers — appalled by the Rectory's condition — who hoped an investigation would produce a verdict of "nothing sinister" to reassure their fearful parishioners. Instead they got Harry Price and a flood of publicity. "The situation was totally out of hand."

**Key quotation on Miss Bull's account:** "What is more disturbing is the discovery of indications that Price apparently overstated Miss Bull's narrative in his books." (Quoting the SPR investigation's own findings about Price's interpretation of the headless man story: the original note said "thought poacher" — Price omitted this.)

**Foyster period (1930–1935):**
The Foundation's framework is notable:
- Edwin Whitehouse is described as a "young man who had suffered severe shell-shock during the First World War and was in something of an emotional and nervous state" whose name "figured prominently in some of the wall writings" and during whose stays "poltergeist phenomena were at their height"
- Marianne is described as attractive, lively, bored by village life, fond of London trips, running a flower shop "in partnership with a certain man, Mr. D'Arles, whose son she was also looking after"
- The Foundation notes Marianne's "baby farming" reputation in the village (private adoption activity)
- They present a three-part framework distinguishing: (1) traditional Bull-period apparitions, (2) Smith-period poltergeist onset, (3) Foyster-period explosion of phenomena inseparably linked to Marianne's presence

**Price's observer period and aftermath:**
Clear and factually accurate summary. Notes that the Glanville planchette sessions built up the Marie Lairre story. The Foundation is notably measured: "Putting on one side the various taps, cracks, thumps, and rustlings reported by the team, we are struck by the paucity and insignificance of most of the phenomena they reported. But there were a number of clear-cut events which those experiencing them attributed to a paranormal cause."

**SPR Report (1956) and its findings:**
The Foundation summarizes the SPR committees' conclusions, but their framing is notably balanced. Their summary of the four committee findings:
1. Bull period: traditional folklore, not extraordinary
2. Smith period: phenomena coincided with Price's involvement; Price may have manipulated or exaggerated
3. Foyster period: substantial evidence Marianne fabricated; her later interviews showed inconsistencies
4. Price's tenancy: Price criticized for sensationalism and lack of rigor; accusations of fraud by multiple witnesses

The Foundation explicitly acknowledges that the SPR report remains "one of the most critical assessments of the Borley Rectory phenomena" while noting that the controversy continues.

**The Foundation's balanced conclusion:**
Harry Price is characterized as "first and foremost a journalist, and his research was secondary to this." They note he founded his own research organization (NLPR / University Council for Psychical Investigation — "though this organization had nothing at all to do with London University"). His involvement "was crucial and responsible for the tremendous notoriety of the whole story."

## Marianne's 1978 Account (as summarized/quoted in the report)

The Foundation explicitly states that Marianne's account forms the basis of their most significant contribution to Borley scholarship. The key findings from the 1978 conversation:

**Marianne's own characterization of events:**
> "She stated flatly that by far the majority [of events] were completely invented by Lionel, as part of [a book he was writing]. However, she stated that from time to time odd things would happen of a poltergeist nature which would puzzle her, and which she did not think Lionel or anyone else was responsible for."

> "She believes Harry Price performed a magic trick when he converted the wine [into ink]."

> "Marianne says that Lionel threw objects many times... He threw things in order to observe [guests'] reactions and to note what they would say. She says the minute these people left the house all such throwing of objects stopped. The phenomena ceased completely when Lionel became confined to a wheelchair. Marianne says she herself was never sure who threw what, because she says, other people would join in, particularly the village children. Foyster's intention was not to frighten and deceive so much as to observe and test people's reactions to the phenomena. Marianne also says he would relate, with great relish, stories of phenomena that were alleged to have happened, and which the family members present knew were not true, in order to observe his visitor's reactions."

> "Marianne herself confirms that the events of the bottle, tumbler, and stiletto happened as described."

> "Marianne says that sometimes there were events and happenings that puzzled them. They did hear footsteps from time to time, when there really did not seem to be any cause. Objects did sometimes appear to move entirely on their own. The wall writings were an example of phenomena that puzzled them. These would initially appear, apparently from nowhere."

**The Foysters' attitude to Borley:** "Far from wishing to leave the rectory, as has been alleged, they were happy there... Marianne says she loved the place, especially the garden and grounds."

**Owen and Mitchell's assessment:**
> "We feel that what Marianne has told us makes sense. It is logical, and, what is more important, everything she has told us fits in with what is already known about the Borley hauntings during the Foyster incumbency. It has the ring of truth."

## Key Analytical Points

**The Foundation's "three-strand" framework:**
Borley must be understood as containing:
1. A genuinely unusual *place* — the pre-Price Bull-period tradition of apparitions, coaches, and strange sounds that existed entirely independently of any investigator
2. A *Marianne layer* — her deliberate staging, her husband's staging, d'Arles's staging, and village children's participation in a collective performance
3. A genuine *residual unexplained layer* — the bottle, tumbler, and stiletto incidents (confirmed by Marianne herself); the wall writings that "initially appeared, apparently from nowhere"; the footsteps for which no cause could be found

This framework — developed by Owen and Mitchell from Marianne's own testimony — is the most sophisticated analytical model of the Foyster period. It is not "all fake" (the SPR position) and not "all genuine" (the Price/Underwood position). It is a complex mixture in which genuine phenomena and deliberate staging were interwoven.

**On Lionel Foyster as active agent:**
The 1978 account presents Foyster as the *primary* organizer of the Borley performance — writing a book and conducting experiments on visitors' reactions — rather than Marianne. This is importantly different from HBR's framework (Marianne as sole perpetrator, Lionel as passive dupe) and from Robert Wood's later framework (Marianne as initiator, Foyster as voyeuristic accomplice).

**On the Foyster manuscript:**
The New Horizons report explicitly connects the phenomena to Foyster's literary ambitions. Their framework is consistent with the Hastings Report's identification of Writing C ("Fifteen Months in a Haunted House") as Foyster's novelized account — a manuscript he hoped to publish.

## Significance

The New Horizons Foundation report is the only published account of a conversation with Marianne Foyster conducted by investigators with significant prior knowledge of the case and its documentation. Unlike Swanson's 1958 detective interview (commissioned by Hall to expose Marianne), the Owen/Mitchell conversation was conducted as a scholarly exercise, with Marianne aware and cooperative. It is therefore the most reliable single account of Marianne's own considered view of what happened at Borley.

Its "three-strand" conclusion — genuine place phenomena, deliberate staging, and genuine inexplicable residue — represents the current scholarly consensus on the Foyster period.

The report was published in 1986, forty-eight years after Foyster left Borley. Marianne died in 1992.
