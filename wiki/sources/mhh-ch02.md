# The Most Haunted House in England — Ch. 2: The Adventures of a Journalist

*Harry Price (1940)*

Price reproduces [[vc-wall|V. C. Wall's]] original newspaper dispatches from June 1929 — sent and published before Price had visited Borley. These constitute some of the most historically important documents in the case: independent press corroboration that precedes Price's involvement entirely.

## First Report

Wall writes: "Ghostly figures of headless coachmen and a nun, an old-time coach, drawn by two bay horses, which appears and vanishes mysteriously, and dragging footsteps in empty rooms."

Incidents reported:

- [[rev-eric-smith|Rev. Eric Smith]] sat alone in a haunted room armed with a hockey stick, heard footsteps, swung at the source — "the stick whistled through the empty air, and the steps continued across the room"
- A servant girl brought from London left after two days having seen a nun walking in the wood
- A maid saw an "old-fashioned coach" on the lawn — visible long enough to distinguish the brown colour of the horses; the same maid also saw a nun leaning over a gate near the house
- [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] (previous rector, recently deceased) reportedly heard a coach driven by two headless men while walking the road outside at night
- Villagers "dread the neighbourhood of the Rectory after dark, and will not pass it"

## The Legend

Wall records the local legend as he found it: a groom at the monastery fell in love with a nun at a nearby convent; they held clandestine meetings in the wood; arranged to elope by coach. Two versions of the ending: (1) the groom strangled the nun in a quarrel, was caught and beheaded along with another groom for his villainy; (2) all three were caught by monks, the two grooms beheaded, and the nun buried alive in the walls of the monastery.

This legend was current in the village independently of Price's investigations.

## Wall's Personal Vigil

Wall and a photographer kept an overnight vigil in the wood behind the Rectory. Nothing dramatic, but:

> "the appearance of a mysterious light in a disused wing of the building — an appearance which simply cannot be explained, because on investigation of the deserted wing it was ascertained that there was no light inside — although the watchers outside could still see it shining through a window."

Rev. Smith placed a comparison lamp in a second window. The second (known) light appeared next to the mysterious one; on approaching the building closely, the mystery light disappeared while Smith's lamp remained. Wall and the photographer were left to continue the watch alone.

## Evidential Significance

Wall's reports were published in a national newspaper before Price arrived. They establish:

1. Independent press corroboration of [[the-nun|the Nun]], [[phantom-coach|the coach]], and [[mysterious-footsteps|the footsteps]]
2. The legend as already current among villagers — not introduced by Price
3. [[rev-eric-smith|Eric Smith]] characterised as "the Rector, who does not believe in ghosts" — a sceptic who nonetheless cooperated with tests
4. The mysterious light phenomenon — observed by multiple independent parties
