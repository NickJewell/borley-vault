# Sidelights — 4: Legless at Borley Rectory

**Source:** *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, Essay 4
**Author:** Andrew Clarke

## Summary

Traces the step-by-step evolution of the "headless/legless man" story from a mundane account of an orchard intruder to a supernatural phantom, documenting how each retelling by [[harry-price]] and [[peter-underwood]] added dramatic elements not present in the original notes.

## Evolution of the Story

**Original (1929 Price notes, from Ethel Bull):**
> Rev. Harry Bull saw a man's legs behind fruit trees, thought he was a poacher, followed with his dog. Man left through the postern gate. Gate thought to be locked.

**Price's 1940 version (*Most Haunted House in England*):**
- Figure emerges from shrubbery into full view, revealed to be **headless**
- Disappears in the vegetable garden (impossible given the layout)

**Price's second book (*End of Borley Rectory*):**
- Now "phantasm" that "passed right through a closed wicket, across the vegetable garden where it disappeared"
- Happens at night (not in the original)

**Underwood's slide lecture (*Borley Postscript*):**
- Inverted to **legless** (not headless): "A legless man in the garden… The legless man disappeared as he watched"

**Attribution to Mary Pearson:**
- Price later attached a similar story to [[mary-pearson]] (the maid). When re-interviewed by Dingwall and Hall, she could not remember this incident.

## Conclusion

The original story describes a poacher obscured by fruit trees who slipped through a gate. Clarke's prosaic explanation: *"a simple story of a trespasser or poacher caught scrumping apples, who slipped through a gate that they thought was locked."* The postern gate opened onto the public road, where travellers regularly passed.

[[ethel-bull]] complained to investigators in 1953 and 1965 that Price had "misrepresented her" and that her statements were "altered to make the story more sensational." She became the butt of village jokes as a result.
