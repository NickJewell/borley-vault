# Ghosts of Borley — Chapter 4: Price at Borley

**File:** `raw/ghosts of borley/chapter-4---price-at-borley.md`

## Summary

Tells Price's story in full: from his first visit (June 1929) through his year's tenancy (May 1937–May 1938), the [[fire-1939|fire of 1939]], and the post-war investigation period. The most Price-focused chapter; deals extensively with the accusations against him.

## Price's First Visit (10 June 1929)

Price and Wall observed from the summer-house; Wall thought he saw a shadow on the Nun's Walk. On returning to the house: a half-brick crashed through the glass-topped verandah; a red glass candlestick hurled down from the Blue Room. Then moth-balls, pebbles, and slate fell from the first floor. Bells rang — Price saw the bell-pulls moving but could not explain how. Library and dining-room keys dropped to the floor simultaneously. Price, a trained conjuror, "immediately examined them" and could find no normal cause.

## Fred Cartwright

A journeyman carpenter who saw a [[the-nun|Sister of Mercy]] at the Rectory drive gate on four occasions in the autumn of 1927 (between Harry Bull's death and Smith's arrival). On the last occasion she vanished as he turned to approach. He heard nothing about the haunting until he told his landlord and others that evening. Critics tried to dismiss him as a pub-story teller; the authors defend him.

## The Sutton Accusation

Charles Sutton (*Daily Mail*) claimed in a 1948 article (nine months after Price's death) that he had caught Price with his pockets full of "bricks and pebbles" on a visit to Borley. The chapter presents a detailed forensic demolition of Sutton's account: his multiple contradictory versions (1948, 1949, 1950, 1966); Miss Kaye's firm denial of any memory of the incident; Price's character (hot-tempered — he would have hit Sutton, not meekly muttered); and the absurdity of a trained conjuror stuffing his pockets with bricks. Mr Hastings' summary: Sutton never saw Price throw stones; he didn't even see the stones.

## The Year's Tenancy (May 1937 – May 1938)

Price advertised in *The Times* for observers: 200+ applications; 40 selected. Prepared a "Blue Book of Instructions." Base Room established in the library. A long list of observers reported auditory phenomena: thuds, footsteps, raps, taps, a "dragging noise," wailing, icy draughts, moving objects (a 50-lb bag of coal), new pencil markings. Critics said the Blue Book planted suggestions; Price said it established methodology.

## The Planchette Sessions and Marie Lairre

Most significant: 27 March 1938 — Helen Glanville obtained a message from "Sunex Amures" predicting the Rectory would burn at 9 pm that night. Nothing happened — but exactly eleven months later (27 February 1939, midnight) the Rectory was gutted. Earlier planchette sessions identified a murdered French nun named [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] as the communicating entity.

## The Fire (27 February 1939)

Captain Gregson (new owner from December 1938) was sorting books when a lamp overturned. The hall was ablaze within minutes. A police constable later questioned Gregson about a "woman in grey and a man in a bowler hat" seen leaving the burning building — no one was inside. A figure of a young girl was seen at an upper window of the blazing Rectory by several witnesses on 26 March 1939.

## The Excavations (1943)

Prompted by Canon Phythian-Adams's analysis of the wall-writings. Human bone fragments found three feet below the cellar floor. Two religious pendants found nearby: a Miraculous Medal (French, first struck 1832) and an older gold pendant, likely French. A dental surgeon identified the bones as probably belonging to a young woman with a chronic abscess — which might account for the Nun's invariably "pale, sad" appearance. Bones buried in Liston churchyard, 29 May 1945, by Rev. Henning.

## Post-Fire Sightings

Continued reports: footsteps from the ruins, mysterious lights, phantom horses' hooves, stone-throwing (reported by Polish officers, undergraduates, journalists). The Cambridge Commission (Dr. Robertson, 1939–1944): 58 people, 25 visits. One-third reported nothing; one-third noted unusual events; one-third described "definitely unusual" events.
