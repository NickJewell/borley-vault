# Borley Postscript Ch.3: The Anonymous Letter

**Form:** Anonymous letter sent to the Editor of the *Suffolk Free Press*, Sudbury, on 10 June 1947, for forwarding to Harry Price
**Author:** Anonymous (a woman, native of Sudbury)
**Slug:** `bps-03`

## Background

Price established that all detailed descriptions and references in the letter were accurate and factual. He intended to analyse it at length in the projected *Borley III* and called it "another Versailles vision." Dr Paul Tabori called it "perhaps the most fantastic story that ever reached Price," who said it had "the ring of truth." Underwood raises the question: was it a hoax designed to test what Price would accept?

## Content

The writer and her brother were walking from Sudbury through fields to Borley on a Friday in July 1932. It was a sunny afternoon. After passing through the village and stopping to admire a cottage garden, they rounded a bend and heard thundering horse hooves.

### The Coach

A "huge coach drawn by four grey and white beautiful horses" drew up at the vicarage door. It was "newly painted in violet, with golden scrolls all around it, almost semi royal." Two servants on the box wore grey-and-white homespun suits with knee breeches, grey silk stockings, black square-toed patent shoes with silver buckles, tricorn hats with white ruche and a coat-of-arms lozenge, and long grey wigs. They opened the door for a young golden-haired lady in her twenties in dove-grey satin with a poke bonnet, and an elderly lady in black satin.

### The Confrontation

The footman remounted and the horses came out of the gate at full gallop toward the witnesses. The brother shouted and pulled the writer. The young lady inside had her head out of the window — seemingly smiling graciously — but as the coach came within inches, the coachman whipped viciously directly at the writer. She ducked; the whip curled and cracked just above her head. The coach was six inches away; she had to press against the railing and keep her dress clear of the wheels. "The smell of decay sickened me." The elderly lady sat turned away, "looking very bored."

### The Disintegration

As the coach should have rounded the bend — in plain sight, with no room to turn into a field and the gate confirmed locked — "the whole thing rose in the air just above the high bank and hedge and disintegrated like a gigantic jigsaw puzzle." Pieces separated: "three legs locked together like the crest of the Isle of Man," "cocked hats whirling in the sunshine," the spokes of the back wheel falling out. The disintegration took just under five minutes and dissolved "not in smoke but in a sun haze." The vicarage gate, which the coach had exited, had its gateposts still standing — "that coach could never have come out of that gateway and left the posts standing."

### The Aftermath

A fortnight later, the brother returned to the spot with a spiritualist friend. No unusual events occurred. The friend suggested they "got caught in the cycle of time" — that the event "actually happened to us, generations ago."

## Assessment

This account is exceptional in several respects:
1. The coach is **fully material**: it had smell, mass (nearly crushing the writer against the railings), sound (thundering hooves) and physical threat (the whip).
2. Two independent witnesses, one a former WWI sniper ("I had some bad times as a sniper in Mespot. But I never felt so bad as I did when that whip curled round you").
3. The disintegration was observed by both witnesses in clear daylight.
4. The detail of violet-painted livery and specific heraldic elements would be verifiable (or falsifiable) if the family were identified.

Paul Tabori: "perhaps the most fantastic story that ever reached Price." Harry Price: it has "the ring of truth."
