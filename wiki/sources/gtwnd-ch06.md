# Source: *Ghosts That Will Not Die* — Ch. 6: Marianne Talks About Borley

**Author:** Vincent O'Neil

Excerpts from Marianne's own unpublished autobiography outline, written at the prompting of Robert Swanson and Eileen Garrett after the New York interviews. The outline — never completed — found its way into Trevor Hall's unpublished exposé *Marianne Foyster of Borley Rectory*. Full text appears in O'Neil's *Fifteen Months in the Most Haunted House in England*.

This is Marianne's own voice, without interrogation pressure. O'Neil considers it the most reliable record of her account.

## Key Content

### The Decision to Come to Borley

They came to Borley because Lionel's arthritis and heart condition made Canadian winters impossible. He had had a heart attack in 1925 on Prince Edward Island. He saw Borley as "an ideal spot for a sick man" — knowing he could not undertake country mission work much longer.

Lionel was a cousin of the Bulls and had stayed at Borley as a young boy. He described Henry and Harry Bull as "conventional churchmen" and planned to run the parish on Canadian democratic lines. He had "a tremendous love of music and theatricals."

### Arriving at Borley

They arrived in August 1930 (before October 16 official date), exploring and planning. On their first night Lionel heard footsteps upstairs and shouted for Adelaide to get back to bed — but Adelaide was in bed. "Must be rats," he decided.

### The Man She Saw

"There was one who used the front hall, but who did not go out the front door. He — an old man with stooped shoulders and an aquiline face — used to walk through the study and up the stairs... He wore a queer kind of long top coat with braid on the lapels." She followed him into the garden, saw him in the summerhouse, and both she and Adelaide saw him go into the churchyard.

She asked Mrs. Parson (= [[mary-pearson|Mary Pearson]]) who the old man was. "She turned a queer kind of greenish white and gasped, 'Mr. Harry. He did wear such a garment.'" Lionel advised her not to tell anyone. This is the apparition the SPR interpreted as Marianne fabricating or hallucinating Harry Bull — but here she describes it as a flesh-and-blood experience.

### Harry Price and His "Cohorts"

"He gave me the creeps. He had pointed ears, a balding head with high forehead, and eyes which were startling. They were not polite eyes."

The visit arrived late; Price brought two women and a man. The women were Mrs. Richards and Mrs. Goldney. Marianne's assessment of Goldney: "the vulgar type, who not only believed in calling a spade a spade, but believed in knocking down women with the same instrument."

**Most significant:** "Price insisted that we should have wine. Lion refused wine... I refused because I did not like the company... Price pulled his wine into ink trick." — Marianne explicitly says the wine-to-ink conversion was a magician's trick by Price.

**The affair:** "Price and Mrs. Goldney were constantly running out together... I sneaked out of the garden door and slipped through the side door. They got a surprise. So did I. They were in the throes of an affair." This is Marianne's most explicit statement on the subject.

### Edwin Whitehouse

"He was, at the time I first made his acquaintance, recovering from a love affair. He was seriously thinking of becoming a monk... Later he became a nuisance. He was forever wanting to analyze his feelings... Sin was his preoccupation."

Lionel "would fly out of the house — anywhere — mostly he took refuge in the church" when Edwin arrived. "I was stuck."

### The Marks Tey Mediums and the Quiet Period

After mediums from Marks Tey held a séance, "The mediums told us we would not be really bothered again. We might have little happenings, but there would never in our time be much trouble. There wasn't."

At least not "compared with what actually happened."

The house then stayed quiet for "over three and one-half years" when they left — confirming roughly 18 months of intense activity (the "Fifteen Months" period), then three and a half years of relative quiet.

### Garden and Animals

"We could never get a cat or dog to stay at Borley. We tried several — the cats died and the dogs ran away." A mysterious creature, about the size of a large mongoose but not a mongoose, lived under the summerhouse: "We called it 'That Owld Thing.'"

Nothing would grow in one section of the garden — a walk lined with box hedges where "nothing flowered. Box would grow and yew, but lilacs no."

### Final Years at Borley

Lionel's health deteriorated. He became "no longer interested in plays and play production." After collapsing in church, the doctor said "no more preaching." Marianne worked as an usherette in a local cinema, sold washing machines, tried running a flower shop. "The house had been quiet enough for over three and one-half years when we left Borley Rectory."

## Significance

This is the clearest account of Marianne's experience in her own words, without the adversarial pressure of the Swanson interviews. Key conclusions:
1. The man she saw (attributed to Harry Bull) was, to her, flesh-and-blood — not a ghostly apparition
2. The wine-to-ink incident was explicitly Price's magician trick
3. Price and Goldney were having an affair — observed firsthand
4. The mediums' visit effectively ended the worst phenomena
5. They were genuinely happy at Borley; left only because of Lionel's health
