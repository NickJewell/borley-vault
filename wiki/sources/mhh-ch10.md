## Summary

Price's account of two important working-class witnesses: [[edward-cooper]] (groom-gardener at the Rectory cottage 1916–1920) and [[fred-cartwright]] (carpenter, four nun sightings in 1927). Both are independent, unbiased witnesses with no prior knowledge of the haunting.

## Key Content

**Edward Cooper — "the padding dog"**: From April 1916, Cooper and his wife in the Rectory cottage were disturbed almost every night for three years by padding sounds from the next room, "as if a large dog or small child had jumped from a height and was padding round the room." They grew used to it. Then one night the padding started as usual, followed immediately by a "terrific crash as if all the china on the kitchen dresser had fallen to the floor simultaneously" — investigation found nothing displaced. The padding was never heard again.

**Black shape in bedroom (1919)**: In early morning light, both Coopers saw "a black shape, in the form of a little man, running round their bedroom" — it vanished before they could act.

**Nun crossings**: Cooper and wife saw the nun-figure crossing the courtyard multiple times from their cottage window. Once they observed her step onto the loose cover of an inspection chamber — the cover would rattle when stepped on — but she produced *no sound*. Cooper confirmed next morning that no one in the neighbourhood had seen a woman matching the description.

**The phantom coach — most detailed account in the record**: On a bright moonlight night, Cooper looked from his window and saw lights approaching across the meadow opposite the church. He realised they were headlamps on a "large, old-fashioned black coach" sweeping rapidly across the hedge and road toward the Rectory. He saw: two horses with glittering harness; two figures in high top hats on the box. He shouted to his wife — she arrived just as it swept into the farmyard and disappeared. Duration: approximately 30 seconds. *No sound whatsoever.* His account matches [[mary-pearson]]'s: same two horses, same black coach.

**Details match**: Coopers left March 1920; no phenomena experienced since.

**[[fred-cartwright]] — four sightings at drive gate (autumn 1927)**: A journeyman carpenter working farms near Borley, walking past the Rectory daily at 6–7 a.m. during the vacant period between Harry Bull's death and the Smiths' arrival. On four separate mornings over two weeks he saw a Sister of Mercy at the drive gate — eyes closed, pale, tired-looking, perfectly normal in appearance. On the fourth sighting he turned back to speak to her; she had vanished. Gate was tightly closed. He walked the whole drive — empty. That evening he told his landlord and others in Sudbury, and heard of the haunting for the first time. He had *never heard of the Borley Rectory nun before his sightings*.

Price conducted the Cooper interview twice (1929 and March 1939 with Rev. Henning): "They do not vary by a hairsbreadth."

## Significance

Cooper's coach account is the most detailed and specific in the record — harness, coachmen, headlamps visible, zero sound. Cartwright is the archetypal "unknown witness": no prior knowledge of the haunting, working-class, reliable, consistent. Both are strong evidential pillars for [[the-nun]] and [[phantom-coach]].
