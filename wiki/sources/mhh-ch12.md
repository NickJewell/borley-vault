## Summary

Price's return to Borley during the Foyster incumbency — his only visits during that period — and his council's formal deliberation about whether [[marianne-foyster]] was the source of the phenomena.

## Key Content

**The party**: Price, Mrs. Henry Richards, Mrs. A. Peel Goldney (trained nurse, National Laboratory council), Miss May Walker, chauffeur James Ballantyne. They arrived October 13, 1931.

**Wine into ink**: Price opened a bottle of Chambertin Burgundy; Mrs. Foyster poured it — it turned to jet-black ink in the glass, though the bottle itself remained normal wine. Simultaneously, the Sauterne Miss Walker was pouring smelled of eau de Cologne. Price tested with household ink mixed with Burgundy: indistinguishable from the transformed wine. Conclusion: ink introduced into the glasses, not the bottles.

**Claret bottle on stairs**: As the party was at the study door, a claret bottle was hurled down the staircase well, hit the iron stove (same as the 1929 candlestick), and smashed. Ballantyne the chauffeur remained in the kitchen reading; reported that as he looked up, a "grisly black hand" moved slowly up and down between the kitchen door jamb and the door, twice, then disappeared. He was entirely unperturbed.

**Mrs. Foyster locked in Blue Room**: Doors locked themselves; Foyster used the relic of the Curé d'Ars and recited a reliquary prayer; Price joined him kneeling in the passage; at "Amen" there was a vigorous click and the door unlocked. Price records this straight.

**Second night — sealed house**: All exterior doors, windows, and children's rooms sealed with screw-eyes, tapes, and seals. All occupants confined to kitchen. A bell rang from Adelaide's room — the wire had been broken off close to the ceiling, making it physically impossible for a 3½-year-old to have pulled it. No seals were disturbed.

**The Price council's conclusion**: Meeting in Price's bedroom at the inn afterwards: *every* phenomenon of the first night occurred when Mrs. Foyster was not within sight or under control. But: every phenomenon had been witnessed by many people previously, had been going on for years. Could they accuse a hospitable hostess of fraud? Price told Foyster their suspicion next morning. Foyster pointed out that every occupant had been similarly accused — which was true.

## Significance

The strongest sceptical moment in Price's own account: his council identified Mrs. Foyster as a possible agent. But the sealed-house bell from Adelaide's room on the second night, and the scale and history of prior phenomena, prevented them from reaching a firm conclusion.
