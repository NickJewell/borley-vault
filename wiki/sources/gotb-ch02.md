# Ghosts of Borley — Chapter 2: The Smiths Come and Go

**File:** `raw/ghosts of borley/chapter-2---the-smiths-come-and-go.md`

## Summary

Covers the brief (nine-month) incumbency of [[rev-eric-smith|Rev. G. Eric Smith]] (October 1928 – July 1929) and its pivotal role in bringing [[harry-price|Harry Price]] to Borley. The Smiths' stay was the turning point that made Borley nationally known.

## How Price Arrived

Smith wrote to the *Daily Mirror* for help "quelling the ghosts." The paper sent reporter V. C. Wall and simultaneously contacted Price. The first press report appeared 10 June 1929; Price visited two days later with his secretary Miss Lucy Kaye. This marked the start of a formal investigation spanning almost twenty years.

## Phenomena During the Smith Incumbency

- "Sibilant whisperings" heard by the Rector under the arch on the landing; once he caught the words "Don't, Carlos, don't!"
- [[bell-ringing]] — as during the Bull period; no human agency found
- Slow, deliberate footsteps in passages and upper rooms
- Two maidservants saw apparitions
- [[the-nun]] seen on the Walk; similar figure seen disappearing at the bottom of the garden
- An upper window lit up inexplicably
- A shadowy shape at one of the drive gates
- A maid saw the [[phantom-coach]] on the lawn before it disappeared into the hedge
- Keys shot out of locks; a vase smashed at the foot of the main stairs

## The First Séance (June 1929)

Held in the [[blue-room]] at 1 am. Participants: Smiths, Misses Bull, Price, Kaye, Wall. A code of raps established contact with an apparent entity claiming to be [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]]. He "accepted responsibility" for the footsteps but denied wishing to alarm anyone. A cake of soap apparently flew across the room — later used to accuse Price of trickery. [[ethel-bull]] flatly denied Price had faked anything.

## The Medals and Apports

Around mid-1929, small objects appeared inside the Rectory: keys, pebbles, and a brass medal bearing the head of St Ignatius with "ROMA" on the reverse. A small gilt confirmation medal and a medal dated AN VIII (1799, Paris) also appeared. Price was accused of planting them from his collection; the authors argue this makes no psychological or logical sense given that Price had no idea in 1929 that a French nun theory would later emerge.

## The Mabel Smith Problem

The chapter's central problem: [[mabel-smith|Mrs Smith's]] statements in 1929 (frightened, puzzled, reporting phenomena as genuine) contra her statements in 1945–49 (denying anything happened; blaming rats, local superstition, and Price's trickery). Her motivations are analysed: possible resentment after Price declined to help publish her novel *Murder at the Parsonage*; a desire to stop "dangerous" publicity; a letter suggesting she believed Price was "suffering in the Beyond" and needed her help. The authors call the discrepancy "clear" but leave motive to the reader.

## Edward Cooper's Evidence

The former gardener/groom (cottage resident 1916–1920): heard padding footsteps (like a large dog) most nights; saw a "black shape" running round the bedroom; saw the phantom coach from his bedroom window one moonlit night.
