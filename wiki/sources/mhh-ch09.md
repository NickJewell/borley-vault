## Summary

Price's composite account of interviews with the surviving Bull sisters at Chilton Lodge, Sudbury — conducted in June 1929, March 1939, and June 1938 by Price and Glanville. Establishes the family's long personal history with the phenomena, including the canonical four-sister nun sighting of 1900.

## Key Content

**July 28, 1900 — the four-sister sighting**: Ethel, Freda, and Mabel Bull returned from a garden party at dusk and saw a figure with bowed head, dressed entirely in black, as a nun, gliding (not walking) along the Nun's Walk toward the stream. They were terrified. Elsie Bull, fetched from the house, said "A ghost? Oh, what nonsense, I'll go and speak to it!" She ran across the lawn; the figure stopped, turned, showed an expression of "intense grief" — Elsie froze and it vanished. Price's assessment: four simultaneous witnesses, objective, solid figure.

**November 1900**: Miss Ethel and the cook saw the nun simultaneously leaning over the garden gate.

**Childhood phenomena in the Rectory**:
- Nightly footsteps at 10:30 p.m. past their bedroom door, always stopping outside the night nursery with exactly three taps — heard at least 100 times over years
- All 20 bells simultaneously (Ethel)
- A face-slapping in the night (a sister)
- Tall dark man seen in upper passage, vanished immediately (Ethel)
- An old man in dark old-fashioned clothes with a tall hat, sitting on the edge of Ethel's bed — seen many times

**[[harry-bull]]'s own experiences**: Headless man walking through the locked postern gate; "Old Amos" the gardener's ghost standing on the lawn pointing up and down; the phantom coach with headless coachman, seen and heard — "a great clatter of hoofs on the roadway" — which passed him, leaving no visible trace.

**[[walter-bull]]'s testimony**: Harry's brother, born at the Rectory, spent much of his life at sea. Heard footsteps following him on the lane outside the Rectory "at least fifty times" — always turned round, saw nothing.

**[[mrs-e-byford]]** (nursemaid 1886, letter received June 11, 1929): Independently confirms footsteps in corridor ("someone in slippers"), heard after fortnight in residence, left after a month because place was "so weird." "My grandparents would never let me pass the building after dark." Confirms the phenomena were "common talk" in the neighbourhood before 1890.

**Coffins displaced**: Ethel reports that some old coffins in the church crypt were found moved from their prescribed positions on several occasions; no explanation found.

## Significance

The most detailed account of Bull-period phenomena from first-hand family testimony. The July 1900 four-sister sighting is the single most-witnessed simultaneous apparition in the record. Mrs. Byford's 1886 nursemaid letter is independent pre-Price corroboration of footstep phenomena.
