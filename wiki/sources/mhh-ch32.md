## Summary

Price's closing argument — summarising the evidence, the witness count, the analytical patterns, and his own verdict that Borley Rectory was genuinely haunted.

## Key Content

**The witnesses**: 100 named witnesses over 70 years, mostly still living and available at time of writing. Price argues: eliminate 50% and the remainder still suffice to "prove up to the hilt that manifestations have occurred at Borley to which no laws known to physical science can be applied."

**Arguments against hallucination**: Kerr-Pearse was physically locked in (a fact, not a perception); the four Misses Bull saw the nun simultaneously; Mrs. Foyster, Dom Richard, and Katie all saw a bottle materialise in mid-air in the kitchen together; Price personally watched a candlestick hurled past his head.

**Arguments against lying**: Would 100 people tell the same or similar lie over 70 years? "I am quite certain they would not."

**Spatial analysis**: Phenomena most concentrated in — ground floor: kitchen passage, kitchen, sewing-room, back stairs, Base Room. First floor: Blue Room, landing "cold spot," Chapel. Wall-writings concentrated in passage between Room 5 and bathroom.

**Temporal patterns**: June worst month; Monday worst day; Sunday best; Holy Week quiet. Phenomena 80% in darkness. These patterns are consistent across multiple independent accounts by people who did not know each other.

**Negative reports**: Only three entirely blank reports received from 48 official observers — a very small fraction.

**Price's verdict**: "My answer to my own question is 'Yes, decidedly!' [The Rectory] is haunted." He closes with a call for science to take psychical research seriously.

## Significance

The formal closing argument of the primary book. The 100-witness count, the spatial and temporal pattern analysis, and Price's public commitment to the "yes" verdict are all significant for assessing his position. See [[harry-price]], [[the-observer-program]], [[harry-price-controversy]].
