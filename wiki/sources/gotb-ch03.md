# Ghosts of Borley — Chapter 3: The Mysteries of Marianne

**File:** `raw/ghosts of borley/chapter-3---the-mysteries-of-marianne.md`

## Summary

The most complex and disputed chapter. Covers the five-year Foyster incumbency (October 1930 – October 1935) — the most intense poltergeist period in Borley's history — and the controversy surrounding [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] as its alleged cause.

## The Foysters

[[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]] — cousin of the late [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]]; Cambridge MA; had been in Canada 1910–1929. A gentle, cultured man widely liked locally. His wife [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] was twenty-one years his junior: "bright, vivacious and intelligent." Their adopted daughter Adelaide was 3½ on arrival.

## Phenomena (1930–1935)

An extraordinary range: voice calling Marianne's name; footsteps; Harry Bull's apparition (wearing his plum-coloured dressing-gown — which Marianne could not have known about); jugs and utensils disappearing and reappearing; strange smells (especially lavender); bells; physical assaults (Marianne given a black eye by an invisible attacker; struck on the head; Adelaide bruised); objects hurled through air; pictures taken from walls; furniture displaced; doors locking themselves; mysterious fires (smouldering skirting in an unused locked room, witnessed by Sir George and Lady Whitehouse). [[wall-writings]] appeared throughout — messages on scraps of paper and on walls, mostly in French, mostly addressing "Marianne," appealing for "light, mass, prayers."

## Price's Visit (October 1931)

The wine-into-ink episode: burgundy poured into a glass turned jet-black; sauterne smelled of eau-de-cologne. Price concluded Marianne was responsible and told Foyster so — causing a near-permanent breach. However, Price later changed his mind after hearing the Whitehouse testimony. He was "not in a position to make his doubts public" for libel reasons.

## The Whitehouse Testimony

Sir George and Lady Whitehouse independently witnessed the smouldering-room fire incident and stone-throwing — events the authors regard as among the strongest evidence against a purely Marianne-centred explanation, since she was present but could not have staged the fire.

## Dom Richard Whitehouse (Edwin Whitehouse)

The most extensively defended witness in the chapter. Witnessed the bottle materialisation (13 November 1931), the stiletto levitating onto his lap, and the tumbler incident. Attacked in the SPR report for: writing from memory eight years later, having had a nervous breakdown in 1924–5 (misattributed by Goldney to 1931), and getting facts from Marianne. Whitehouse's 1956 letter systematically refutes all five SPR charges. The authors find him credible.

## The Wall-Writings

Attributed by most critics to Marianne, consciously or unconsciously. Graphologist: all but one word ("Edwin") written by the same hand. Most messages appear around summer 1931. Heights: 4'6"–4'8" (adult height) for legible messages; 2'3"–2'6" for squiggles (child height). Price's instruction to Glanville to "strengthen" the writings for photography came *in response to* Glanville's own suggestion — not Price's initiative.

## The Amherst Comparison

SPR critics drew parallels between the Foyster case and the "Great Amherst Mystery" (New Brunswick, 1878–79), noting Foyster had lived near Amherst. The authors find the parallels "attractive" but the differences significant, and note that Foyster never referenced the Amherst case in any of his writings.

## The Marianne Accusations

SPR theory: Marianne faked everything to escape the lonely Rectory. After she left (1935), nothing comparable recurred. The authors' verdict: "not proven." They note that 12+ people witnessed visual phenomena during the Foyster period — not just Marianne — and that Foyster himself, who would have been the most deceived, reported phenomena carefully and with evident integrity.

## Marianne's Own Words (letter, 3 July 1956)

> "If you mean did I haunt the place, the answer is No. And there is nothing and no one going to make me say I did."
