# The Enigma of Borley Rectory — Ivan Banks (1994)

**Ivan Banks**, Maidstone, Kent (1994). Acknowledged: 30 files (preface, introduction, acknowledgements, chapters 1–27). Prefix: `enbr`.

A comprehensive pro-Price treatment of the Borley case, notable above all for original historical archival research — archivists in England, France, Belgium, and Normandy; Catholic conventual archives; forensic pathology consultations; State Papers research; parish registers; census returns; and legal documents — that no earlier source had undertaken. Banks acknowledges A.H. Wesencraft, Peter Underwood, Countess Mary Waldegrave, Professor Bernard Knight (Cardiff), Roger Glanville, Richard Lee van den Daele, and contributors to Chapter 24.

---

## Structure

- **Part I (Chs 1–8):** Narrative of the haunting from 1863 through wartime
- **Part II (Chs 9–15):** Biographical studies of each occupant: Henry Bull, Harry Bull, Smiths, Foysters, Harry Price, Gregson, James Turner
- **Part III (Chs 16–27):** Historical investigation: bones/tunnel, monastery controversy, Sir Edward Waldegrave, Marie Lairre, Katie Boreham mystery, screaming girl, full séance transcripts (from Locked Book HPL microfilm), contemporary views, Locked Book provenance

## Key Unique Contributions

### 1. Henry Dawson Bull's Syphilis

Banks obtained Henry Dawson Bull's death certificate and identified cause of death as **Locomotor Ataxia**, which a medical dictionary identifies as "a manifestation of tertiary syphilis." Henry died aged 59 in 1892. This is the documentary basis for longstanding local village gossip that Henry indulged in affairs with village girls and fathered illegitimate children. Banks treats this as proven through the death certificate itself. See [[henry-bull]].

### 2. The Kate Boreham Investigation (Banks's most distinctive contribution)

Banks conducted extensive independent research into the death of [[kate-boreham]], consulting:
- Parish registers (Borley baptisms, marriages, burials)
- Suffolk County Records (1888 electoral register for Priory Walk, Sudbury)
- Death certificate: Kate died in Sudbury, cause "cerebritis," aged 31 (though death certificate shows 38 — discrepancy)
- **Professor Bernard Knight** (Cardiff University Medical School): the stated cause "cerebritis" is medically meaningless as a post-mortem diagnosis without virological evidence; lead poisoning causes encephalopathy including fits, tremors, or death and could be misidentified as cerebritis
- **Dr. David Whittaker** (colleague of Knight): reviewed dental report on Borley skull fragments; molar wear consistent with a person aged around 30 — consistent with Kate's age of 31

Banks's hypothesis: Kate Boreham was a maid at Borley Rectory in the 1880s; she became pregnant by Henry Dawson Bull; was given or took **sugar of lead (lead acetate)** as an abortifacient; died as a result of lead poisoning; was hurriedly transported to a house in Priory Walk, Sudbury, where a compliant doctor certified "cerebritis" as cause of death; and was subsequently buried at Borley (Easter 1888) to cover up the scandal.

Additional points:
- The house in Priory Walk, Sudbury where she allegedly died has no record in the 1888 electoral register as ever belonging to the Borehams
- The only person owning a house in Priory Walk in 1888 was a "Jessie Mitchell" — identity unknown
- A "William Henry Bull" is listed as owning a house in Market Hill, Sudbury — possibly related to the Borley Bulls
- Richard Lee van den Daele visited Borley and could not find Kate Boreham's grave in the churchyard despite the parish register recording her burial there
- Kate Boreham was born Catherine Barber, Ballingdon, Sudbury, 1857; married Walter Boreham at Borley Church February 19, 1876 (conducted by Henry Bull); first child baptized June 4, 1876 — indicating possible premarital conception
- **Joseph Tyler**, the Bull family coachman (1881 census), is identified by Banks as the likely driver of the carriage that transported the dying Kate to Sudbury — which also provides Banks's explanation for the phantom coach (a Victorian cab with two men on the driver's box)

Banks's conclusion: the Katie Boreham episode is at the root of psychic disturbances at Borley, and séance results from October 1937 were attempting to reveal this truth without the sitters fully understanding. The dead baby (Banks suggests disposed of in the well) may account for some wall-writing appeals to "Marianne."

> **Connection:** Banks notices that the voice heard by Guy Smith pleading "No Carlos, Don't" on the Blue Room landing is consistent with a protest against Henry Bull ("Carlos") at the moment of Katie's removal or death.

### 3. Sir Edward Waldegrave as a Ghost Candidate

Banks conducted archival research in the *Calendars of State Papers Domestic* (1547–1580) and identified Sir Edward Waldegrave as likely one of the ghost figures seen at Borley. Key findings:

- Waldegrave was **Lord of the Manor of Borley** under Henry VIII and was responsible for the maintenance of a **Rectory at Borley** — the same site later built upon by Henry Bull
- He was a committed Catholic; imprisoned in the Tower under Edward VI for refusing to suppress Mass in Princess Mary's household; freed when Mary acceded
- Under Elizabeth I: arrested April 19–20, 1561 after a priest, **John Cox alias Devon** (parish priest of Pentlow), was apprehended and confessed to having said Mass at Waldegrave's house at Borley Hall
- A manservant "Goldney" — described in a Thomas Parker letter (November 1560, *Calendars of State Papers*) as "a lying, deceitful and cunning individual" — is Banks's candidate as the betrayer
- **The Earl of Oxford's letter** to Sir William Cecil confirms papers found at Borley Hall containing "diverse presumptions of scheming minds and doings towards the state"
- Waldegrave died in the Tower, September 1, 1561, aged 44; buried under Protestant rites — which Banks sees as a posthumous insult that would generate a ghost
- The séance response **"Tfismong"** (October 23, 1937): Banks identified this as "T\*fis\*mong" = "THE fishmonger" = **Forster the fishmonger** from a Thomas Parker letter in the *Calendars of State Papers* — confirming, Banks argues, Waldegrave's presence at the séance and the betrayal narrative
- Ethel Bull saw a tall man in old-fashioned costume with a tall hat sitting on her bed — Banks notes Elizabeth I-era clothing matches

Banks also notes the irony that the fishmonger's name "Forster" is strikingly close to "Foyster."

### 4. The Monastery Controversy Resolved

Banks concludes there was no Benedictine monastery at Borley but provides the historical context:

- **1248:** Part of Borley district under Benedictine nuns of **Barking Monastery** (a legal dispute with Foxearth Rector over tithes; document in British Museum)
- **1301–1539:** The **Prior and Convent of Christ Church, Canterbury** (Benedictine) held the Manor of Borley; day-to-day running was by a land steward, not resident monks
- The popular "monastery at Borley" story is a misinterpretation of Canterbury's ownership
- **Sudbury Priory (St Bartholomew's)**: Benedictine Priory cell on the opposite side of the River Stour, close to the present main road near St Bartholomew's Lane; founded 1115 by Wulfric, Master of the Mint to Henry I; survived until 1538; had "free warren" or access to woodland in Borley (per Georgina Dawson, confirmed from Dugdale and Roy Midmer's *English Mediaeval Monasteries 1066–1540*)
- Banks suggests the plague of 1348–49 may have killed monks of Sudbury Priory who were then buried on the Borley knoll — the origin of the "plague pit" story
- A Dominican Friary at Sudbury (dedicated to St Saviour, founded c.1248 by Baldwin of Shipling) is also nearby

### 5. Marie Lairre / The Borley Nun — Banks's Research and Conclusion

Banks spent years researching the identity of the nun through conventual archives in England, France, Belgium. His exhaustive investigation covered:

- **Caudebec-en-Caux (Normandy):** Town archives virtually lost in WWII bombing; no trace of St François de Sales convent or a "Lairre" family
- **Bures-en-Bray (Normandy):** Documented in Dom Cottineau's monastic directory as having a **Benedictine Priory of Saint Etienne** — making Bures en Bray a plausible origin for the nun rather than Bures in Suffolk
- **Lyre (Normandy):** Banks considered similarity of "Lairre" to "Lyre" but found no nun connection
- **Lier/Lierre (Belgium):** Carmelite English nuns in Lier; no connection to Borley
- **Joanna Waldegrave:** Banks traced her through Brussels Benedictine archives — she was in fact **Sister Theodosia**, died 1719 not 1667; ruled out as the nun
- **Two Arabellas:** Arabella Fitzjames (King James II's daughter by Arabella Churchill) = Sister Ignatia at Pontoise; Arabella Waldegrave (granddaughter of James II, daughter of Henry Waldegrave and Henrietta Fitzjames) entered Pontoise as a child of 7, then became a Paris nun — Countess Mary Waldegrave confirmed she kept correspondence with mother and brother, so did not "vanish" as supposed
- **Brussels Benedictines:** Many Waldegrave daughters were nuns there; the order eventually settled at East Bergholt, Suffolk (18 miles from Sudbury) after fleeing the French Revolution in 1794
- **The 1929 St Ignatius medal:** Banks notes it was struck in Paris in 1799 — five years after the French Revolution expelled English convents from France — suggesting a possible French Revolution–era connection

**Banks's final conclusion:** The most likely candidate for the ghost nun is **Barbara Waldegrave (Sister Mary Apollonia)**, daughter of Sir Nicholas Waldegrave of Borley, born 1603. She entered the Brussels English Benedictines in 1622, received the habit September 12, 1623, was professed November 24, 1624, and died April 18, 1638, aged only 36. The Brussels chronicle describes her as "happily deceased" — which Banks finds ambiguous. She was one of two Waldegrave daughters born at Borley (the other being Jeronima, who died of consumption at Ghent). Of all the candidates, Banks argues, Barbara is the one born at Borley, who was a nun, who died young, and of whom the circumstances remain unexplained. See [[marie-lairre]].

### 6. The Gregson Fire Investigation

Banks traced both of Captain Gregson's sons through Richard Lee van den Daele:

- **Anthony Hart Gregson** (Canada): "When this didn't pan out, he torched it for the insurance." Also reveals Gregson was a British Union of Fascists area organiser for Maldon district in the 1930s; describes him as essentially a "man of straw"
- **Allan Gregson** (Tasmania): Says fire was accidental — stack of books keeled over onto an oil lamp in the hall where furniture was temporarily stored; immediately sent both boys to phone fire brigade from Borley Green
- The **contradiction** between the two sons is itself revealing: Banks argues that Allan, sent off the premises, had no reason to doubt his father's story; Anthony, with more insider knowledge, states it was deliberate arson for insurance
- Banks notes that storing combustible materials (books, paper) near the main stairwell was "monumentally risky" and that sending **both** boys away left Gregson alone with the building for ~20 minutes before the brigade arrived
- Allan Gregson confirms carriage wheel tracks in freshly fallen snow accessible only to a wheelbarrow — corroborating this well-known phenomenon

Purchase price discrepancy: Banks states £500 (the figure previously quoted to Price); the brfa book cited a different figure. Banks says Gregson insured the building for £10,000 — twenty times the purchase price.

### 7. Henry Bull's Ordination Records

From Durham University archives (Auckland Castle Episcopal and Ordination Registers 1881–89), Banks found Harry Bull's examination assessments:
- "Weak!" / "Only Sufficient!" / "On Isaiah... Fair Matter, but form weak!"
- "Style Weak, but Fair Matter!" (Durham Diocesan Records 1887–94)
- Had to be told to improve before being ordained

Banks also documents Harry Bull's marriage to Ivy Brackenbury (1911): held at St Alban's, Holborn; her previous husband may have been still alive; her daughter by that marriage (a Catholic) had strained relations with Harry; his will left everything to Ivy with nothing to his sisters.

### 8. Full Séance Transcripts from Locked Book

Banks reproduced verbatim the table-tipping and planchette sessions from the Harry Price Library microfilm copy of the Locked Book (October 23–31, 1937), comprising over 560 numbered questions and answers. He analyzed them systematically and proposed a framework of multiple overlapping entities:

| Entity | Evidence |
|--------|----------|
| Henry Dawson Bull ("Carlos") | Direct identification at Q.24–25; Katie/baby references |
| Mrs Caroline Sarah Bull ("Caldibec/Carol Beth") | Pet name of Henry; confirmed called Henry "Carlos"; Katie was her servant |
| Harry Bull | Blue Room; "misfortune / a wife"; poisoned by wife (séance says yes) |
| Sir Edward Waldegrave | "Tfismong" = Forster the fishmonger; Latin Catholic appeals; "mass, chant, light" |
| Kate Boreham ("Katie") | Identified herself; kitchen death; baby; "Light Mass erslre" |
| Miss Brackenbury (Harry's stepdaughter) | Catholic; possible source of Catholic appeals; may have discovered sugar of lead bottle |
| Maria Rolf | Swiss governess (1881 census); born Lausanne; possible "Jane" entity; may have known truth about Kate |
| Marie Lairre | Confirmed strangled; murdered by "Waldegrave"; 1667; May 17; from Havre; novice |

Notable séance analysis:
- "Caldibec" = Banks's reading as "Carol Beth" (short for Caroline Sarah/Elizabeth, not a place-name)
- "Tfismong" = "THE fishmonger" = Forster the fishmonger from Calendars of State Papers (1561 Thomas Parker letter)
- "Fadenoch" = possibly "Father Enoch"; was a monk buried in the garden
- "Oif" (wall writing author) = attempt at "Foister/Foyster"
- "LUNOLOWTUMNALITYC" in German = possibly "Lügner" (liar) / "Lümmel" (ruffian); or related to Maria Rolf's Swiss-German origins

### 9. Harry Price Born in London, Not Shrewsbury

Banks researched Price's birthplace in detail and concluded:
- Price was born in London (Price himself stated this in a newspaper interview)
- His family had a long association with Shropshire (Crudgington, Rowton area)
- T.H. Hall's claim that Price invented a Shrewsbury birthplace is wrong; the error originates from Paul Tabori's biography misinterpreting Price's account of seeing "Sequah" the medicine man in Shrewsbury
- Banks confirmed with the Wellcome Foundation that Sequah did visit Shrewsbury (Shrewsbury Chronicle, November 1, 1889, advertising Sequah's arrival)
- Paper mill at Tibberton (near Crudgington) connected to Edward Ditcher Price (Harry's father)

### 10. Mrs Lucy Alice Turner's Letter

Banks received a letter from Mrs Lucy Turner (wife of James Turner) describing their experiences at Rectory Cottage 1947–50:
- Heard heavy footsteps on bare boards in old driveway in broad daylight
- Grey cat appeared to run under their car but was not found
- Sound of smashing crockery in kitchen
- Crack like a pistol shot above her head while washing up
- Children's laughter and voices in the old orchard — "were they the Bull children?"

### 11. Other New Material

- **Violet Sorfleet** (via Joan Forman's *Haunted East Anglia*): group visited burned Rectory; thick clammy fog dropped on them from nowhere; felt something "unnatural and unpleasant"
- **Mr Hardy** (foreman painter at Rectory during 1937 repainting): saw coil of smoke rising from lawn without cause; his son heard chanting from locked church at night
- **Frank Farrance**: local man who found a tunnel section in Borley Place grounds during Harry Bull's era; candle guttered in stale air, preventing full exploration
- **Patrick Payne**: most recent owner of Borley Hall (until his death in May 1983); much liked in village
- **Nandor Fodor** (psychical researcher): defended Price's integrity in *Tomorrow Magazine* before the 1956 SPR report was published, despite personally disliking Price — Banks regards this as particularly powerful testimony
- **Bull baptismal records**: Banks found 13 baptisms at Borley (not 14 as commonly stated), from the Rev. Christopher Cook's check of parish registers; includes exact dates; Cyril Garwood Bull died April 9, 1887, aged 16 weeks; no "Freda" in the register

## Contemporary Views Contributed (Chapter 24)

- **A.H. Wesencraft** (HP Library curator, letter June 10, 1985): Price was sincere, devoted, liked to talk about his work; "The Borley story cannot be dismissed as a journalistic stunt"; confirmed there was "something very mysterious about the place" per James Turner
- **Guy Lyon Playfair** (SPR/Enfield poltergeist researcher): "Borley was, I think, haunted. I only wish I could be sure." Biased slightly against Price (knew Dingwall); but acknowledges odd things went on
- **Dr Vernon Harrison** (SPR): Price was an entrepreneur not a trained scientist; believed Price presented Borley in good faith; "without his efforts, it is unlikely anyone would have heard of Borley"
- **Andrew MacKenzie** (SPR): Critical of Blue Book pre-suggestion; compares unfavorably with Professor Stratton's reticence at Abbey House Cambridge
- **Richard Lee van den Daele** (Yorkshire researcher): traced both Gregson sons; brother's team recorded chanting in locked church (1984) using professional tape deck

## Author's Conclusion

Banks concludes:
1. **Borley was genuinely haunted** — phenomena across 82 years, too many independent witnesses
2. **Historical causation** is multi-layered: Sir Edward Waldegrave (persecuted Catholic); the plague pit; the Katie Boreham episode; Harry Bull's troubled life
3. **The nun is Barbara Waldegrave** (Sister Apollonia), died 1638 aged 36
4. **Gregson committed arson** — deliberate fire for insurance, using both sons as unwitting accessories
5. **Price presented the case in good faith** — Hastings report vindicates him; Hall's attacks are discredited
6. The continuing disturbances after the fire suggest the "entities" are still awaiting their story to be told

Banks's key regret: Price never investigated the historical background thoroughly, which would have removed the main basis for his critics' attacks.
