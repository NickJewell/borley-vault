# The Haunting of Borley Rectory — Chapter 2: The Thirteenth Man

**Source:** Sean O'Connor, *The Haunting of Borley Rectory* (c. 2022)
**Slug:** `hbrs-ch02`
**Time period:** 1 November 1924 – 12 June 1929

## Summary

The most significant new context on [[rev-eric-smith]] in any source.

### Eric Smith's Background

- Born in Calcutta; father a Freemason; son of the Indian Civil Service clerk class.
- Anglo-Indian: too English for the expatriate community, not Indian enough for the native population; excluded from executive ICS roles.
- Married Mabel Hart, daughter of a railway clerk from Isleworth; Mabel's mother and grandmother born in India.
- In 1920s Britain, a "colour bar" was routinely operated by hotels, restaurants, dance halls. Race riots had taken place in London, Glasgow, Liverpool, Cardiff.

### The Thirteenth Man

Bishop of Chelmsford (Guy Warman) could not fill the Borley vacancy; the living had been refused by **twelve other clergy**. Eric Smith was the thirteenth candidate. The Bishop did not tell Smith this, nor that the position had stood empty since Harry Bull's death.

### Rectory Condition

- 26 rooms, roof in deplorable condition, riddled with deathwatch beetle (makes a tapping sound at night).
- No electricity, no gas, only paraffin lamps and candles.
- Water pumped by hand from a 80-foot well.
- Infested with rats (cellar), newts, lizards and toads; courtyard full of domestic waste.
- The Smiths spent £200 of their savings making it habitable.

### The Skull and the Poison Bottle

On arriving, Mabel found a human skull (young woman, perfect teeth) in a library cupboard; buried in the churchyard. She also discovered a bottle labelled "Poison" in the cellar — the same bottle Harry's sisters believed Ivy had used to poison him.

### The Phenomena Under the Smiths

Muffled footsteps past the bedroom door; doors and windows opening/closing; sibilant whisperings on the landing; tablecloth pulled; house cats staring at something invisible; bricks apparently thrown at the ceiling; mysterious vehicle lights in the drive. Mabel's maid quit after seeing the phantom nun on her bicycle ride.

### Church and Village Politics

- William Bigg (churchwarden) resented losing his authority.
- The village had long resisted a new rector; they had been using the Rectory for courting and play.
- Bishop Warman's disdain for Smith also driven by personal ambitions (moving to Manchester in 1929).
- The Church planned to unite Borley and Liston parishes and sell Borley Rectory — but never told the Smiths.

### The Smiths' Strategy

Mabel had read Price's *Sunday Sentinel* article. Writing to the *Daily Mirror* was both an attempt to investigate and to bolster their case for a transfer to another living.
