# Sidelights — 14: Lawless, the Lodger

**Source:** *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, Essay 14
**Author:** Andrew Clarke

## Summary

A full biography and analysis of [[francois-darles|Frank Pearless]] (self-styled François d'Arles), focusing on his role in the Foyster phenomena and his subsequent life.

## Frank Pearless: Biography

- Real name: Frank Pearless (also used aliases "Black" and "Wade")
- Origins: Cockney; sold flowers from a barrow outside a London cemetery
- **Claimed:** French Secret Service veteran, master florist, based in Arles after WWI
- **Actual:** Small-time confidence man; obtained the name "d'Arles" after living briefly in the French town
- Arrived at Borley c. March 1931, initially bringing his son Douglas (from a liaison with Kate Fernie)
- Same age as [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] (~early 30s)
- In "Fifteen Months in a Haunted House," Lionel renamed him "Lawless" — Clarke: *"a surprisingly apt name"*

## The Affair

Marianne to Swanson (1958): *"I think it was several weeks before we had sexual relations."* The affair began c. May–June 1931 and lasted until spring 1934. The relationship was *"no casual fling"* — nearly three years. Marianne acknowledged it: *"He said that I was a naughty girl."*

The Foysters' marriage remained emotionally intact despite the affair; Lionel's tolerance was "what would nowadays be regarded as a mature and reasonable arrangement."

## Pearless and the Haunting

- Frank confessed to Ian Shaw that he had caused much of the phenomena and advised Marianne to end it.
- The external cellar trapdoor gave him undetected access to the house.
- His alibi during Price's surveillance: arrived at the door to report he had seen phenomena while the Foysters were asleep in bed.
- The spectacular Marks Tey night was a joint production by Marianne and Frank as a staged finale to end the haunting.

## The Black Eye

Lionel recorded Frank appearing at breakfast with a black eye after a third "ghost attack" in the bedroom. Clarke: *"His hands went right through the apparition... but something touched him. A terrific smack in the face"* — plausibly a domestic incident with Marianne, who described their relationship as fighting "like cats and dogs."

## The Flower Shop and Dissolution

- Late 1933: Pearless, Marianne, and Ian Shaw moved to a flower shop in Wimbledon.
- The shop was a financial disaster; Pearless showed "little aptitude and less inclination to work."
- Pearless used his knowledge of Marianne's prior marriage (to Greenwood) and Ian's true parentage to blackmail her: *"he was fiendishly jealous."*
- 1934: Pearless divorced his wife Ada Ewens and married 16-year-old shop assistant Jessie Irene Dorothy Mitchell (August 1934).
- Lionel's cousin Bernard Foyster (solicitor) ejected Pearless from the shop.

## Frank Pearless's End

- Taxi-driver, then motor mechanic
- Immigrated to Australia with third wife (Jessie Pring Steed) in the 1950s
- Returned to London 1966; went straight into hospital; died ~6 weeks later
- **Buried in an unmarked pauper's grave** — family had no money for a funeral

## Significance

Without Frank Pearless, the most dramatic phase of the Borley haunting — the Marks Tey night, the spectacular bell-ringing, the bottle-throwing — likely would not have occurred in the form it did. His role was entirely suppressed in Price's books.
