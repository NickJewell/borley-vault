# The Haunting of Borley Rectory — Chapter 6: Murder at the Parsonage

**Source:** Sean O'Connor, *The Haunting of Borley Rectory* (c. 2022)
**Slug:** `hbrs-ch06`
**Time period:** 13 June 1929 – 14 March 1930

## Summary

The aftermath of Price's first visit: the Smiths decide to leave; Lord Charles Hope's visit; Lucie Kaye interviews the Bulls and Coopers.

### The Smiths' Decision to Leave

The invasion of sightseers made the Rectory uninhabitable. The Smiths left for Westgate Terrace, Long Melford. Eric Smith found his inability to secure another parish humiliating — he commuted from Long Melford for parish duties. The Rectory stood empty again.

### Lord Charles Hope's Visit (5 July 1929)

Price, Lucie and Lord Charles Hope visited the now-empty Rectory. Hope: Old Etonian, second son of the 1st Marquess of Linlithgow; SPR Council Member; champion golfer. During the evening keys flew through the hall; bells rang; pebbles fell; linoleum appeared on the stairs. Hope suspected Mary Pearson (the maid) or possibly Mrs Smith. He was particularly suspicious of Price himself (noting that Price absented himself at crucial moments).

### Ethel Bull and Cooper Interviews

Price and Lucie visited Chilton Lodge to interview the Bull sisters. Ethel: the nun sighting of summer 1900 was the first she recalled; she saw a woman "bent over in a flowing black robe"; didn't see face or rosary. Harry Bull had seen phantom coach; his retriever Juvenal was terrified by legs visible among the fruit trees — "though, Miss Bull admitted, it could have been a poacher."

The Coopers (Rectory Cottage): Mrs Cooper heard large dog sounds overhead in the attic (turned out to be nothing). Cooper followed a nun from the Rectory back door to the road — by the time she reached the road she had disappeared. He saw the phantom coach "with glittering harness and horses, but no sound whatever."

### Mabel Smith's Novel

During the harsh winter of 1928–29, Mabel Smith began writing a murder mystery set "in a large, creepy house in an isolated rural village." The novel started with the discovery of a skull in a cupboard. She titled it **Murder at the Parsonage** — "most lurid," she noted with satisfaction.

### Price's Article

Price wrote about Borley in the *Journal of the American Society for Psychical Research*, claiming "we have not yet had an opportunity of 'laying' the ghosts of Borley Rectory."
