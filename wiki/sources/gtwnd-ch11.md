# Source: *Ghosts That Will Not Die* — Ch. 11: Some Concluding Thoughts

**Author:** Vincent O'Neil

The book's final chapter addresses the psychology of paranormal belief, analyses Marianne's various statements for a consistent core truth, and arrives at the author's personal verdict: "I believe *something* happened at Borley."

## On Marianne's Contradictions

O'Neil explains the apparent contradictions in Marianne's testimony as the result of:
1. **25-year gap**: The first Swanson interview was 25 years after the events
2. **Hostile interviewers**: Mrs. Goldney "possess a steam-roller personality, capable of crushing any witness and any independent opinion around her" (Nandor Fodor, *Tomorrow* magazine, Winter 1956)
3. **Fear of persecution**: Marianne was terrified that Mrs. Goldney had her address and was "coming to the States" — "I live in the shadow of a volcano"
4. **Protective instinct**: Telling interviewers it all never happened was a way to make them go away

## From Her Own Hand (the Autobiography)

O'Neil considers Marianne's unfinished autobiography the most reliable evidence, since it was written "in the privacy of her own room, without someone hounding her."

Key sentence: she wrote that "many strange incidents occurred at Borley, including the ghost lights, the bells, and the *apparitions.*" The word "apparitions" — used voluntarily, not under pressure — contradicts her denials to Swanson.

The sentence "at least not to be compared with what actually happened" implies substantial prior events. The phrase "quiet enough for over three and one-half years" implies 18 months of genuine activity — consistent with the Fifteen Months period.

## Andrew Clarke: The Clinical Psychology Perspective

Essex-based clinical psychologist Andrew Clarke's analysis:

"Lionel was taking artificial digitalin for his heart and he was on a cocktail of painkillers for his rheumatism... Harry Bull was taking painkillers too, I'm sure. He was severely depressed towards the end of his life... The account of his final years indicates clinical depression. He would have had medical treatment for this."

Clarke applies the framework of Evelyn Waugh's *The Ordeal of Gilbert Pinfold* (a near-contemporary account of drug-induced hallucinations) to suggest that both Harry Bull and Lionel Foyster may have experienced genuine drug-induced perceptions they interpreted in terms of the growing Borley legend.

"The Borley legend mutated and developed, and was shared by a number of people."

## The Smith Testimony Re-evaluated

O'Neil notes that Smith's letters to Glanville during the 1937 observer period (in the Locked Book) show the Smiths hoping the ghosts "will show up in plenty" and planning to bring their old objects (ticking mirror, keys, bells, tricky clock) back for their visit: "It sounds very much to me like the Smiths probably suffered from the same paranoia afflicting my mother in later years — too much attention. Tell 'em anything to make 'em go away!"

## Final Verdict

"I believe *something* happened at Borley. While nothing happened to me personally during my two visits to Borley... that does not rule out the possibility something happened to others. For over one hundred years, scores of people have reported several hundred alleged phenomena at Borley. Surely they can't all be wrong?"

Sarah Hapgood in *The World's Great Ghost and Poltergeist Stories*: "At the core I believe the haunting was genuine, but it has grown so many layers that no one wants to take it seriously." About the church she added: "Like most extensive hauntings, some of it is substantial, and some is just wishful thinking."
