# Borley Postscript Ch.1: A Pictorial History of the Borley Haunting

**Author:** [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]]
**Form:** Script of a 100-slide illustrated lecture, presented to the Royal Photographic Society, Ghost Club Society, SPR, and at Cambridge, Bedford, Birmingham, Sheffield, and Essex Hall
**Audience included:** Dr Eric Dingwall, Mrs K.M. Goldney, Dr Letitia Fairfield, Robert J. Hastings, Flight-Lieut R. Carter Jonas, Ellic Howe, Robert Aickman
**Slug:** `bps-01`

## Summary

A chronological overview of the Borley haunting from Underwood's perspective, drawing on sixty years of personal investigation and interviews with virtually every significant witness. Robert Aickman (himself one of Price's observers) called it "the finest appraisal of the Borley haunting" he had ever heard.

## Key Contributions

### The Nun's Day
Old Mrs Bull and H.D.E. Bull already knew July 28 as "the nun's day" before 1900 — a cousin recalls Bull saying at breakfast that he would not be surprised if his wife had seen the ghost nun, "for the previous day had been July 28th, the nun's day." This predates the famous 1900 sighting.

### Harry Bull's Nickname "Carlos"
Underwood: "there are grounds for believing that Harry Bull was known to his intimates as 'Carlos'" — directly explaining the [[rev-eric-smith|Eric Smith]] voice incident ("Don't, Carlos, don't"), which Smith would not have been expected to know.

### The 1957 Tunnel Discovery
In September 1957, contractors cutting a trench for water mains between the church and Rectory site cut through the tunnel. It was in "almost perfect condition" — red two-inch Tudor bricks, blocked by rubble at both ends. Underwood had already confirmed hollow sounds in experiments in March 1950.

### The Borley Bell
The great courtyard bell was given to Harry Price after the fire. When Constance Price left Arun Bank, the Harry Price Estate and the University of London presented the bell to Underwood personally. It hung in his garden for the rest of his life.

### Dr Abernethy
A local female physician, on her way to visit a patient, saw the stooping figure of a sad-looking nun close to the hedge near the Rectory site. She braked, backed the car, found nothing, and got out for a thorough look — all clear.

### Ethel Bull (aged ~94)
Underwood interviewed Ethel Bull at Chilton Lodge. She posed for a photograph with him and her brother Alfred. They were "charming people who lived very much in the present but had no doubts whatever that Borley Rectory was haunted."

### The Locked Book
Underwood possessed a complete copy of "The Locked Book of Private Information" (compiled by Price and Glanville). Trevor Hall later sold the original from the Harry Price Library to an American collector for £1,200.

### Rectory Subsequent Owners (post-Turner)
After the Turners, the Bacons bought the cottage site; after the Bacons, "a man named Martin" who never lived there but made "considerable alterations and improvements including partial solar heating"; the place was then on the market for £87,000.

### Failed Attempts to Re-examine the Bones
Underwood tried for years to have the human remains at Liston disinterred for modern dating analysis, petitioning the relevant MP, the Bishop of the Diocese, the Archdeacon of Colchester, the Central Synod of the Church of England, and the Home Office — all to no avail.
