## Summary

[[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard Whitehouse O.S.B.]]'s personal signed narrative of approximately 30 visits to Borley Rectory between June and December 1931, printed verbatim. Widely regarded as the strongest single piece of first-hand witness testimony in the entire corpus, authored by a future Benedictine monk of impeccable reputation.

## Key Content

**Background**: Richard Whitehouse was Lady Whitehouse's nephew. He had known the Rectory since boyhood and all its occupants since 1912 (Gregson excepted). He was considering the priesthood and had ample time to visit during summer 1931.

**June 8, 1931 — the brass stiletto incident**: Summoned to the Rectory, found Marianne lying in Room 7 (beside Blue Room), hands under bedclothes. She and Richard talked for about ten minutes. Then: "I felt something land lightly on my lap, coming apparently from behind me." He picked it up: a brass stiletto (paper knife), approximately 8–9 inches, weighing nearly 8 oz, normally kept in the study below. Mrs. Foyster was still lying with hands under covers — he had carefully noted this. She told him she had seen it "rise up from the floor behind me and do one or two curious convolutions in the air before settling on my lap." Richard: "felt certain that by no possible means could she have got the object onto my lap without my detecting it." He concludes levitation was supernormal.

**Mrs. Foyster thrown from bed**: Richard left the room briefly; heard a piercing shriek and rushed back to find her face-down on the floor with the mattress on top of her. "The whole thing must have happened in an instant, for I am quick on my feet and I was no distance from the room." She had felt the bed tilt and herself pushed out.

**June 16 — the Novena message**: Richard and Mrs. Foyster made a Novena (nine days' Rosary) for guidance. On the last day, as they knelt on the landing near the Chapel, both simultaneously became "aware of a presence" near them. On checking the wall opposite where they had knelt, Richard found a fresh message in pencil: "Get light mass and prayers. M." — written while they were both on the landing. Neither had seen it there before. He was "sorry afterwards that I did not have the writing photographed."

**November 13 — the bottle materializations**: With Mrs. Foyster and maid Katie in the kitchen, doors and windows closed: a bottle burst under Mrs. Foyster's chair; fifteen minutes later another burst under Dom Richard's chair; then a bottle "poised itself in mid-air within a foot or so of the kitchen ceiling" for one to two seconds before crashing to the floor — all three watched. A fourth bottle rolled in through a door from the staircase without breaking, circling round the floor.

**Bell-ringing directly witnessed**: Richard watched the pull-bells moving with nobody touching them; cut wires and all.

**Conclusion**: "I must confess that I, personally, never had any occasion to suspect any member of the household of producing phenomena artificially or fraudulently." He insists: "I agree with Mr. Price in asserting that this place was once the most haunted house in England."

**Historical note**: Cites Dugdale's *Monasticon Anglicanum* — Edward III gave the Manor of Borley to the Prior and community of Christ Church, Canterbury (Benedictine) in the 36th year of his reign. This is independent documentary evidence for a Benedictine connection.

## Significance

Dom Richard's account is the most detailed single narrative by an independent witness during the Foyster period. As a trained Catholic scholar with no interest in public reputation as a ghost-hunter, his testimony carries unusual weight. The stiletto incident is the most carefully described levitation in the record. See [[dom-richard-whitehouse]], [[marianne-foyster]], [[wall-writings]], [[poltergeist-activity]], [[marie-lairre]].
