# The Haunting of Borley Rectory — Chapter 4: Ancient and Modern

**Source:** Sean O'Connor, *The Haunting of Borley Rectory* (c. 2022)
**Slug:** `hbrs-ch04`
**Time period:** 1066–1862

## Summary

A deep historical survey of Borley from the Domesday Book to the construction of the Rectory. Key contributions:

### The Village

"Barlea" (boar's clearing) appears in the Domesday Book. After the Norman Conquest, gifted to the sister of William the Conqueror. Medieval church first built in 1236 of flint rubble. The "Doom" painting on the west wall was whitewashed out under Edward VI.

### The Waldegraves

- Edward Waldegrave acquired the Manor in 1546 from Henry VIII (who confiscated it from Canterbury monastery).
- A Catholic recusant; imprisoned in the Tower 1551 for permitting Mass in Mary Tudor's household.
- Died in the Tower 1561 after Elizabeth I's accession.
- The Waldegrave tomb in Borley Church: effigies of husband and wife; husband in armour; wife with squirrel at her feet; six Corinthian marble columns. A local legend held that the coffins beneath would move.
- The church is one of only a handful in Essex that was **undedicated** — known simply as "Borley Church."

### Anti-Catholic Context

Essex and Suffolk had fierce anti-Catholic sentiment since the Reformation. The 1862 Ipswich anti-Catholic riots; Gothic "convent memoirs" as popular culture. O'Connor argues this context directly shapes the Borley nun legend: the story of a doomed romantic nun punished for breaking her vows is characteristic of anti-Catholic Gothic fiction of the period.

### Construction of the New Rectory (1862)

- Architect: John Johnson of Bury St Edmunds (also designed St Matthew's vicarage, Camden).
- Builder: Thomas Farrow, estimated at £3,000.
- Built on clay that expands when wet — prone to subsidence. Hard cement (not flexible lime mortar) made cracking likely.
- The carpenters fitted the floorboards tightly with no allowances for movement — meaning the woodwork would **creak and groan** with every temperature change.
- The carpenters scratched their names on the rafters.

### John Whyard's Drowning

On 5 August 1862, a 17-year-old bricklayer's labourer named John Whyard drowned in the River Stour while the builders were bathing after a hot day. Locals murmured it was a bad omen for the new Rectory. His death is documented at the Long Melford inquest.
