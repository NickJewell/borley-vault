# Ghosts of Borley — Chapter 5: Tunnels and Graves

**File:** `raw/ghosts of borley/chapter-5---tunnels-and-graves.md`

## Summary

A detailed account of all excavations at the Borley site from the 1930s to 1957. The least paranormal chapter — primarily archaeological — but significant for what it confirmed and failed to find.

## Six Excavation Episodes

1. **The tunnel encounter (c.1920s):** Mr Farrance, while repairing a well at Borley Place, broke into what appeared to be a "high and vaulted tunnel." Pursued it until forced back by foul air.
2. **The cats' cemetery (1937):** Glanville found the grave of "Gem" disturbed; when dug, turned up large bones (likely a cow skull). Further digging by Henning (1946–47) found nothing.
3. **Price's excavations (August 1943):** Found human bone fragments (skull parts) 3 feet below the cellar floor; two religious pendants. Human remains identified as probably female. Critics: bones were planted. Authors: physical evidence of thick clay patina makes this implausible.
4. **Henning's digging (1946–47):** Looking for lost church plate; nothing found.
5. **James Turner's explorations (1947–50):** Cleared rubble; filled cellars; made sunken rose garden; found nothing new.
6. **The Philip Paul / Sewell excavations (1954–57):** Most systematic. Discovered iron-age quern; pre-Bull era brickwork confirming earlier building on site; and — crucially — the tunnel itself.

## The Tunnel Discovery (12 September 1957)

Workmen laying water mains cut through brickwork and found the tunnel running roughly north-south near Borley Place. Mr Sewell examined it: red two-inch Tudor bricks, 32 inches wide, 28 inches high, top 12 inches below road surface. Strongly built; roof still clean. Southern end blocked by earth; northern end by a modern brick wall. Continued for ~90 feet on both sides of the road. The tunnel was running toward the ancient cellars of Borley Place — where Mr Farrance had found a similar passage decades earlier.

## Borley Church Discoveries

In May 1947, floorboards in the sanctuary were removed to install the pre-Reformation altar (a stone slab that had been upside-down in the central aisle). Under it: the tomb of Rev. Humphrey Burrough (rector 1722–1757), Thomas Gainsborough's uncle. A vault below contained three skeletons in three feet of water (coffin wood long rotted). Sealed and re-floored.

## Key Finding: Evidence of Earlier Buildings

Tithe Map 1841: confirms a "Herringham rectory" on the site, smaller and further west than Bull's building. Map of 1772: shows three buildings. Pre-Bull brickwork (two-inch Tudor bricks) found throughout cellars confirms the site had significant earlier occupation. This undermines the legend's claim of a monastery but establishes that the site had complex pre-Rectory history.
