# Borley Postscript Ch.9: Visits to Chilton Lodge

**Author:** [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]]
**Form:** Account of nine to ten personal visits to the Bull family home at Chilton Lodge, Cornard Road, Sudbury (from 1951 until Ethel Bull's death)
**Slug:** `bps-09`

## Background

Chilton Lodge was a "solid Georgian building" purchased by the surviving Bull children in 1920 when they vacated [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] following Harry Bull's marriage. Ethel, Constance, and Alfred Bull were the principal residents. The house was "bulldozed" in the late 1960s.

## Ethel Bull's First Words to Underwood (1951)

"Oh yes, I saw the nun on at least two occasions." Spoken in "matter of fact tones and as an unemotional statement of fact."

## The 1900 Sighting (Ethel's Version)

Confirmed in first-hand detail: the three sisters (Ethel, Freda, Mabel) saw the nun on 28 July (year not remembered). Ethel's immediate reaction: "Certainly, I was scared stiff!" Ethel saw the nun again less than four months later, with the Rectory cook and a cousin also present at the gate.

**The parents knew:** Ethel was "quite sure" their parents knew about the ghost "but we were never told." This explains why Walter and Gerald Bull were somewhat sceptical — they were rarely at home and the subject was not discussed within the family.

## The 1929 Blue Room Séance (Ethel's Correction)

[[harry-price|Price]] wrote and [[vc-wall]] wrote that the soap "jumped out of its dish" while they were all "seated round the mirror." Ethel told Underwood: "We were none of us in the room when the piece of soap jumped out of its washstand" — and confirmed this in a letter dated 22 September 1954. Underwood notes this correction actually strengthens her 1900 testimony: "having known Ethel I do feel that if there had been any confusion or possibility of malobservation, she would have said so."

## Ethel's Assessment of Mabel Smith

Regarding Mrs Smith's 1945 *Church Times* letter claiming she and her husband never believed the house haunted by "anything else but rats and local superstition": "All nonsense, she used to shriek with fright." Confirmed in writing, 22 September 1954.

## Ethel on Marianne

Bull sisters collectively felt Marianne was "probably mediumistic because psychic phenomena seemed to occur wherever she happened to be." They did not like her. Ethel described her to Sidney Glanville as "mad as a hatter"; Constance called her "a little beast." When Underwood pressed her: "Did I really say that? Well she was very self-centred you know, and eccentric and she did some very disreputable things. She wasn't a happy woman, we felt."

## Ethel on Guy Eric Smith

"Quite charming but an ignoramus in psychic matters." Mrs Smith: "seemed frightened of shadows" and had "some silly idea about writing a best-selling book."

## Notable Visitors to Chilton Lodge

Underwood traces the visitor list as a "Who's Who" of the Borley story:
- **Harry Price and Lucie Kaye** (first visit, June 1929): Price found the sisters "very charming and cultured and quite ready to give any information."
- **Lord Charles Hope** (July 1929): "Whatever criticisms are levelled at the Bull sisters, they can never be accused of changing their story!" — said, Underwood observes, "as though it was a criticism."
- **W.H. Salter** (October 1931): Visited to try to persuade Ethel to sever links with Price. She "made no comment but thought he had a cheek to ask."
- **Flight-Lieutenant Carter Jonas** and Flight-Lieutenant Caunter (March 1938): Found on this visit "the house and grounds at night were as still as a vault."
- **Dingwall and Trevor Hall** (April 1953): "An enjoyable and interesting occasion being entertained to tea by Ethel and Alfred Bull." Underwood never actually met Hall in person; his quote from Conan Doyle's *A Study in Scarlet* applies: "His ignorance was as remarkable as his knowledge."
- **Bob Hastings**: Interviewed surviving Bull family for his 1969 SPR critique.
- **Seymour de Lothiniere** (BBC Director of Outside Broadcasts): Still convinced 35 years after the event that the wall markings he witnessed in July 1937 "could not have been executed by human hands."
- **Brigadier C.A.L. Brownslow**: First visited Borley 1953 (meeting Bacon and Williams families at Priory Cottage — post-Turner occupants); visited Chilton Lodge February 1954. "Completely convinced of the authenticity of the Borley haunting."

## Mollie Goldney at the Ghost Club

At a Ghost Club Annual Dinner, Goldney made a speech praising Underwood's revival of the Club: "I was beginning to feel that this Club would never be what it was in Harry Price's day but now that Peter Underwood has taken us under his wing... it is something Harry would have approved of." But when Underwood once defended Price at an SPR lunch, Goldney shot back: "Oh, I hope you are not a Price worshipper?"

## The Locked Book

Underwood confirms: Trevor Hall sold the original Locked Book to an American collector for **£1,200**. Underwood himself possesses a complete copy.

## The Len Sewell Bequest

[[len-sewell|Len Sewell]] lived in a caravan on the Rectory site for a time and kept Underwood informed of local activities (including the 1957 tunnel discovery). He left Underwood all his Borley photographs and collections of cuttings and correspondence in his will.

## The Carter Jonas Files

Air Commodore Carter Jonas (formerly Flight-Lieutenant), when leaving Cornwall, presented Underwood with his complete files on hauntings, personal Borley records, log book, and correspondence with Harry Price.
