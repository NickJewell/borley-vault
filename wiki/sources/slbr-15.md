# Sidelights — 15: The Well Tank Bothers Me

**Source:** *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, Essay 15
**Author:** Andrew Clarke

## Summary

Demolishes the "well tank" clue that drove the 1943 excavation, demonstrates Price's confusion about the Rectory's plumbing system, and raises the possibility that the human bones were planted or switched.

## The Plumbing System

Clarke spoke with the plumber who maintained Borley Rectory's system in the 1930s:
- The house had a cast-iron pump in the south wing courtyard, pumping from a well into a galvanised steel **"Well Tank" in the attics** — not the cellars.
- This provided pressure for kitchen and bathroom taps.
- The cellars contained a **sump** (shallow well with bricked base for draining cellar floods) and a **soft-water cistern** (for irrigation).

The wall-writing "Marianne get help **Well Tank bother me**" (if that is the correct reading) would refer to something *above the ceiling*, not in the cellars. Price's entire theory of cellar burial was based on a misreading of the domestic plumbing.

## Price's Confusion (1943 Dig)

- Price excavated the sump (which he called the "well tank") and found it to be a 6-foot-deep sump, not a well.
- He then sought the "well tank" — which had been excavated and removed by Kerr-Pearse years earlier.
- Price, confused, dug by guesswork in a new spot — and found the bones.

## The Bones

Jackson, Henning's farm-labourer gardener who did the digging, **immediately identified the jawbone as a pig's**. When Price handed it to the waiting pathologist (Dr Eric Bailey), Bailey declared it human. Jackson maintained his view his entire life. Johnnie Palmer (worker at Borley Place) also observed the dig and was certain what he saw were *"awd Sows Bunz"* (old sow's bones).

Three-year excavation by L. G. Rayner and Leonard Sewell (1954–57), using a mechanical digger, found **no further human bones** despite digging to five feet below the cellar floor.

## Clarke's Theory

Clarke raises the possibility that Price — who was confident enough to have a pathologist standing by — performed a sleight of hand when taking Jackson's pig bone and giving it to Dr Bailey. This would explain:
- The contrast between Price's "lucky" result and all subsequent null excavations
- The peculiar circumstance of having a pathologist pre-positioned
- Jackson's lifelong insistence on a pig bone

Clarke hedges: *"It is not certain… but it seems odd that he was so sure that he would find bones in the cellars."* The village accepted this interpretation: *"This was the theory that found ready acceptance in the village."*

## The Phythian-Adams Connection

Dr W. J. Phythian-Adams (Canon of Carlisle) read Price's first book on Christmas holiday and constructed an elegant theory from the wall-writings that posited a buried nun. This theory directly drove Price's second book and the 1943 dig. Clarke: without Phythian-Adams, *"the second book would probably not have been written."*
