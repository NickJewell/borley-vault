# Source: *Ghosts That Will Not Die* — Ch. 10: Return to England

**Author:** Vincent O'Neil

A personal memoir chapter describing O'Neil's discovery of his identity (September 22, 1994), his preparations to return to England, and his 1997 trip — visiting Borley Church, Lionel's grave, the Harry Price Library, and ultimately meeting his biological family.

## Discovery of Identity (1994)

O'Neil was doing genealogy research via the Internet when Nick Rowland in England found a reference to Borley in connection with O'Neil's mother's name. On September 22, 1994, Rowland wrote: "Please, before you read further, would you sit down with a cup of coffee and take a deep breath."

Until that date, O'Neil had no idea of the Borley connection. His mother (Marianne) died December 18, 1992 without ever telling him. "She never talked about anything supernormal. In the 47 years I knew her, I never heard about or witnessed any psychic phenomena of any kind."

The first book he bought was *The Widow of Borley* by Robert Wood.

## Funding the Trip

An old school district pension Marianne had invested $50 in during 1950 (registered under the name "Ena Howlett O'Neil") had grown to roughly $3,000 by 1994. After the finders' fee, O'Neil received just over $2,000 — "My mother was going to pay for my trip home."

## The 1997 England Trip (September 17–26)

### London (September 17–20)

Meets Borley researcher **Stewart Evans** — Borley collector with the most extensive private collection outside Peter Underwood and the Harry Price Library. Evans had one very rare document: a letter regarding the insurance claim of Capt. Gregson.

Met **Guy Lyon Playfair** (paranormal author) and **Alan Wesencraft** — curator of the Harry Price Library at University of London Library. O'Neil toured the Library: "It was a tremendous pleasure to touch the actual documents that supplied the fuel for the Borley Legend." Notes that additions to the collection continue to arrive.

Visited the Society for Psychical Research; met secretary Eleanor O'Keeffe. Met **Ron Russell**, founding member of the Enfield Parapsychological Research Group, whose tape recordings from Borley Church included "distinct moans of a male voice."

### Borley Visit (September 21)

Attended Sunday services at Borley Church with reporter Joe McNally from *Fortean Times* and author Peter Hough. Only nine parishioners present.

Key observations:
- The Waldegrave tomb: "not as large as I envisioned, but it is still the dominating feature of the church"
- The organ is opposite the tomb; he was able to touch the keys but not play
- The church wardens, Colonel and Mrs. Dorie, were wary — the previous week, vandals had broken a stained glass window, stolen the chalice, and used the altar cross to pry open a door
- "No one experienced anything abnormal at Borley Church"

### Lionel's Grave, Campse Ash

O'Neil went alone to [[lionel-foyster|Lionel's]] grave at Campse Ash, placed his mother's remaining possessions (small treasure box, medals, rosary, scapular, her ashes), and spoke aloud asking for a sign about whether Marianne had killed Lionel. **Nothing happened.** The tape recording showed nothing unusual.

*"After several minutes, I asked God to help me. Nothing."*

### Meeting Biological Family

On September 22 — exactly three years after learning of his Borley connection — O'Neil met **Peter Frederick Long** (his cousin) at 54B Roseview Avenue, Ipswich — the house where O'Neil was born. Long was six years old at the time and remembered the birth.

On September 24, O'Neil met his half-brother **Billy** (and Billy's wife Gwen) at a pub. He learned his biological mother Kate (born 1906, died 1980) had been considered clever at school and played piano. She was cremated in Ipswich. "Katie was never so happy as during those two years" — the two years she was with O'Neil's father. His youngest brother (born 1948, named Philip) was not told about Vincent; O'Neil agreed not to contact him.

### Meeting Peter Underwood

September 26 — Ghost Club Society event in London. Underwood, whose correspondence with Marianne had initiated O'Neil's entire search, arrived and was briefly cordial but had to leave immediately because his wife was ill. "He has since become one of my strongest supporters."

## Significance

This chapter establishes O'Neil's personal biography, confirms Marianne's secrecy about Borley in her American life, and documents the first modern visit to Borley church by a direct family member of the Foyster incumbency. The visit to Lionel's grave is perhaps the most emotionally charged moment in the book.
