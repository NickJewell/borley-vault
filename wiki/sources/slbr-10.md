# Sidelights — 10: Edwin and the High Water Mark

**Source:** *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, Essay 10
**Author:** Andrew Clarke

## Summary

A detailed account of [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]]'s involvement with the Foysters, reinterpreting the "high water mark" poltergeist events of June 8–9, 1931, and tracing the intensifying relationship between Edwin, [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]], and [[lionel-foyster|Lionel]].

## Edwin Whitehouse: Full Biography

- Midshipman at the Battle of Jutland (1916); invalided out of the Royal Navy 1917 with what we would now recognise as PTSD.
- Worked in Istanbul at Whittall and Co. Ltd.; returned to England mid-1920s for treatment at a Harrow nursing home for **anxiety neurosis**.
- Drifted into teaching; became intensely interested in spiritualism and Anglo-Catholicism.
- Nephew of churchwarden Sir George and [[lady-whitehouse|Lady Whitehouse]] (Arthur Hall, Sudbury), who took him under their wing.
- First visited Borley on 6 June 1931, introduced by Lady Whitehouse.
- Eventually re-admitted to mental hospital after involvement with the Foysters.

## The High Water Mark (June 8, 1931)

Clarke's analysis of the famous "turfed out of bed" sequence:
- Marianne was supposedly ill in the "sickroom" (Room 7, the Schoolroom — a flimsy "jerry" bed, not the Foysters' main bedroom).
- Edwin and Marianne were left alone while Lionel went to church.
- The stiletto levitation: Edwin saw Marianne "start" and felt the object land on his lap. Marianne later denied all memory of it. Clarke notes: *"Edwin's phrase 'when I suddenly saw her start' could be significant."*
- The bed collapses three times: Clarke identifies this as Marianne leaping quickly out of bed (causing the unstable "jerry" bed to tip) — caught out each time when Lionel returned.

## The Edwin–Foyster Relationship

Edwin escalated the "high-church" flavour of the haunting: introduced relics of the Curé d'Ars, scapulae, Catholic medals, novenas, incense. *"The Figis, having mastered the art of writing, picked up the mood by writing 'Light Mass Prayer' on the wall."*

The relationship between Edwin and Lionel became close — possibly intimate: Owen/Mitchell (1979) noted they "played a lot of chess together" and Eileen Garrett's letter to Trevor Hall reported Marianne *"intimated that Whitehouse and Foyster were exceedingly intimate, which suggested a spot of homosexuality."* In "Fifteen Months," Edwin was transformed into "Miss Edith Greycastle."

Lady Whitehouse eventually banned Edwin from visiting the rectory, fearing the effect on his mental state. She called Marianne "man mad" and felt *"Borley Rectory did Edwin no good at all."*

## Significance

Edwin's intense engagement with the haunting — and Marianne's exploitation of his credulous sympathy — directly drove the phenomena to their peak. Without Edwin, the wall-writings (which developed under his encouragement) and the spectacular manifestations of June 1931 would likely not have occurred.
