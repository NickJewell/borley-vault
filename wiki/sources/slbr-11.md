# Sidelights — 11: Bullsheet! The Bulls at Borley Rectory

**Source:** *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, Essay 11
**Author:** Andrew Clarke

## Summary

Drawing on 15 years of local newspaper research by the Foxearth Historical Society and Clarke's extensive local knowledge, this essay demolishes the idea that any genuine haunting was documented at Borley before 1929. It traces the borrowed origins of each legend element.

## The Foxearth Historical Society Research

Clarke and the Foxearth Historical Society searched all local papers from 1780 onwards for any reference to Borley. Result: **absolutely no mention of any haunting whatsoever** before 1929. Farm accidents, poaching, and arson — but no ghosts.

## The Dodie Bull Diary

A handwritten diary by [[dodie-bull|Caroline "Dodie" Bull]] (the eldest daughter) was discovered. It chronicles daily family life in detail — weather, farm animals, family events — with **no mention of any haunting**. The only relevant entry is a "chair-walking" séance experiment with her sisters and a Swiss governess. Clarke: this "confirms the statement that the Bull sisters made late in life that no physical manifestations had occurred in the Bull era."

## The Borrowed Legends

- **Headless coachman:** A virtually identical legend existed at Acton (adjacent parish), documented in the *Bury and Norwich Post* March 1852 — a century before Borley.
- **Nun/monk tunnel legend:** Borrowed wholesale from Monks Hall, Glemsford (across the river), where tunnel stories were taught in local schools.
- **Skeleton stories ("Katie Boreham"):** Derived from gossip about Rev John Foster (Foxearth vicar, rape case 1871) and from two skeletons found near Glemsford bridge in 1850.
- **"Nun's Walk":** The actual Nun's Walk is in Great Yeldham, where the Bulls occasionally preached. A road near the haunted rectory there.

## Harry Bull's Position

Harry Bull spent **9 years away from the rectory** (at Borley Place across the road) after his marriage in 1911, returning only in 1920. For much of his incumbency he served other parishes. He believed personally in hauntings and told the stories to various people, but the village choir, his wife, and his brothers heard nothing. Canon Lambert (family friend) said the Bull sisters recounted hauntings "not in a serious vein" — *"as if it were an extended family joke... related with pride, but with a slight air of mischief."*

## Ethel Bull's Complaint

In 1953, Ethel told the SPR that Price's habit of "gingering up" her testimony annoyed her: single apparitions seen at twilight were presented as seen "many times"; the 1900 Nun sighting described as having "an expression of intense grief on her face" was actually seen in poor light with no face visible.

## Significance

The pre-1929 haunting legend was largely a family construction — a ghost story embellished over decades by a few of the Bull sisters from borrowed regional legends. The inheritance dispute following Harry Bull's death in 1927 gave the sisters a motive to publicise and dramatise the haunting.
