# Sidelights — 21: Where Was Borley Rectory?

**Source:** *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, Essay 21
**Author:** Andrew Clarke

## Summary

A cartographic and archaeological study overlaying three historical maps to establish the locations of three successive rectory buildings on the same site, demonstrating that the 1950s excavations missed the Herringham Rectory entirely.

## The Three Rectories

Clarke overlays:
1. **Chapman and André Map (1773)** — shows two buildings on the site
2. **1841 Tithe Map** — labels "Rectory House/Garden" clearly; shows the Herringham rectory facing south down a long garden with ornamental pond and stream
3. **1931 Ordnance Survey** — the Bull rectory as demolished/destroyed

### Pre-1600 Building (oldest)
- A typical three-bay oak-framed building
- Archaeological evidence suggests c.1550–1600
- Its cellars were found during 1950s excavations
- Positioned under the kitchen wing of later buildings

### Herringham Rectory (c.1800–demolished 1862)
- Large, handsome rectangular building in pale "Suffolk Gault" brick
- Faced south down the rectory garden (taking advantage of the long view)
- Demolished by [[henry-bull|Henry Bull]] to build the "Haunted" Rectory
- Henry Bull's motive: needed larger entertaining rooms for local "season" entertaining
- The **Herringham rectory's foul-water drainage system** ran under the kitchen wing — probably the "tunnel" found by Rev Smith and Ian Shaw
- The 1950s excavations missed this entirely (under kitchen-wing rubble)

### Bull Rectory (1863–fire 1939, demolished 1944)
- Built partially over the Herringham foundations, particularly in the kitchen wing
- Drive, stables, and coachhouse in same position across all three buildings

## The Excavations

The 1954–57 Rayner/Sewell systematic excavation dug in the wrong area — focused on the pre-1600 cellars, missing the Herringham foundations entirely. Had they dug in the kitchen-wing/courtyard area they might have found structural remains of the Herringham building.

Clarke's overlay maps show the three buildings' footprints superimposed. His conclusion: *"The closeness of the glebe suggests that it was [a rectory]. Rectories were not always near the church but they usually kept near their glebe."*

## Site History

Borley shows evidence of occupation from Roman times. The "plague burial ground" mentioned by Price (skulls occasionally turned up by the spade) was real — victims of the 1654–55 epidemic buried in the garden, accounting for bones found during various diggings that may have no connection to Marie Lairre.
