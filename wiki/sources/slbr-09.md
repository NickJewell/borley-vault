# Sidelights — 9: Borley Bellsheet

**Source:** *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, Essay 9
**Author:** Andrew Clarke

## Summary

A mechanical analysis of the Borley Rectory bell system, demonstrating that virtually all documented bell-ringing has prosaic explanations: rats/mice in the attics triggering unfixed wires, deliberate string-pulling from outside, and the cellar-access route used by [[francois-darles|Frank Pearless]].

## Technical Description

The bells were an old mechanical system: small handbells on springs, each connected by a bronze wire running across the attic space to a bell board in the kitchen passage. The wires ran **unfettered across the attics** (not in tubes), making them vulnerable to vermin. The system included pull-chords in each room; the Smiths had disconnected the pulls in some bedrooms, but the wires were all left intact.

Key point: **bells were never fully disconnected.** Price's claim that "some of the wires were broken or had been cut" meant only that pull-cords in some rooms were detached — the bells themselves remained connected.

## The String in the Ivy

Ian Shaw (Marianne's son) discovered a piece of string hidden in the ivy near the kitchen-passage door that led into the courtyard. When he pulled it, all the house bells rang. Marianne was reported to have said "Oh, shut up" — later explaining the string as merely a broken wire. Clarke identifies this as [[francois-darles|Frank Pearless]]'s installation for producing the spectacular mass bell-ringing (as heard by Guy L'Estrange during the Marks Tey Circle visit).

## The Adelaide Bell Incident (Price's Account)

During Price's surveillance visit (Oct 1931), a bell connected to Adelaide's room rang while Marianne was in full view. Price declared Adelaide the suspect. Clarke notes: *"In his excitement, Price would seem to have forgotten that, once the bell was swinging, the bell wire at the other end would have swung too, by simple physics."* Any interference anywhere in the wire chain — from the cellar, from the passage, from outside — could produce the effect.

## Marks Tey Circle Bell-Ringing (January 1932)

The spectacular clanging of all thirty bells during the Marks Tey visit is attributed by Clarke to Pearless pulling the string in the ivy from outside the house — a plausible action from the courtyard where he had easy access. After this night, bell-ringing effectively ceased.

## Conclusion

No bell-ringing incident at Borley requires a supernatural explanation. Mechanisms: rats on attic bell-wires (Mrs Smith's own explanation), deliberate pulling from the pantry or bedroom passage, and the string in the ivy operated from outside.
