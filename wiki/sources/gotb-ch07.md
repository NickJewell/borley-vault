# Ghosts of Borley — Chapter 7: The Persistent Ghosts

**File:** `raw/ghosts of borley/chapter-7---the-persistent-ghosts.md`

## Summary

Documents continued paranormal reports from the Rectory site, cottage, and surroundings after the 1939 fire — right up to 1972. The authors' argument: the phenomena did not depend on the building.

## The Cottage

Active as far back as Edward Cooper (1916–1920): padding footsteps nightly, a "black shape" in the bedroom. The cottage became the centre of continued activity after the Rectory burned.

## Post-Fire Phenomena

- **Phantom horses** — sounds of hooves heard by Herbert Mayes (16 March 1939, just weeks after the fire); by five Cambridge Commission members (1953); by other visitors.
- **The Nun** — continued sightings: Dr Abernethy (July 1949) saw a "stooping nun" by the hedge, backed her car up, nothing there; Terry Bacon (1951+) saw her three times; Mr Williams (August 1953) saw her in broad daylight on the Nun's Walk from a shed window; Dr Peter Hilton-Rowe (July 1956) saw a black-habited figure running past the gate; the Kelman party (July 1966, three independent witnesses) saw a grey-blue figure near the shed at 5 am.
- **[[phantom-cat]]** — the Turners (1947–50) experienced a persistent phantom cat; described by multiple witnesses including Ronald Blythe (writer, former librarian) who saw it in the bathroom. Consistent with the earlier "padding dog" heard by the Coopers.
- **Footsteps** — three times heard from an empty upper room by Peter Underwood and Laurence Gafford during an overnight vigil (March 1950); Turners confirmed they had slept soundly throughout.
- **Smells** — lavender (Turners; also Foysters before them); cooking smells at 4 am (Williams, Bacon families); incense (Mrs Gooch).
- **James Turner's voices** — for over a fortnight while clearing the Nun's Walk, Turner and his wife heard "happy, laughing voices" from the direction of the Rectory site that ceased whenever he stopped work and fled when he pursued them.

## The Croom-Hollingsworth Sighting (20 June 1970)

The most detailed post-Rectory Nun sighting. Observed for 12 minutes by Croom-Hollingsworth with radio contact to two companions. The figure (grey, with a shawl over her head) passed through a fence twice, was independently seen by Mr Potter at ~20 feet. Described in close detail: "face of sadness... eyes tightly shut... a mole on her left cheek... she looked at least sixty." An expert subsequently verified the described habit as consistent with monastic dress.

## The Phantom Cat

A recurring motif across three decades and multiple occupants. Wentworth Day (1939): a gigantic black cat shot between his legs into the ruins, never came out. A journalist (1940): identical experience. Turners (1947–50): a scraggy grey-white cat chased their own cat Fred; seen gliding past the kitchen doorway; a scratching at the bedclothes in a locked room; a dip in the bedclothes; Ronald Blythe saw it in the bathroom. The final appearance: a piercing cat-squeal from inside the cottage when all cats were accounted for outside (March 1950).

## The Authors' Conclusion

After interviewing "practically every witness," the authors find "an overwhelming belief" among unbiased observers that Borley was a genuine centre of paranormal events. They stop short of a metaphysical explanation, ending with a plea for the "open mind" — neither Spiritualist nor anti-Spiritualist but willing to see "forces and events... open to different, non-materialistic interpretations."
