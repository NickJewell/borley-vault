## Summary

Price's first-person account of his first full day at Borley Rectory — described as "sixteen hours of thrills." Contains the most vivid primary-source description of the June 12, 1929 events, confirming and greatly elaborating on the GOTB accounts.

## Key Content

**Systematic morning survey**: Price and his secretary spent hours measuring every room with tape and yard-stick, sounding walls for cavities, exploring rafters and cellars. They sealed every door and window they finished with. Found inscription on bell-rafter: "Bells hung by S. Cracknell and Mercur, 1863" — definitively dating the Rectory. Found frogs, toads, newts and lizards in the cellars; scores of old wine bottles. Did NOT find the rotten well cover (discovered later by observers).

**[[mary-pearson]] interview** (Smith's maid, from Price's notes, June 12, 1929): Saw a coach on the lawn twice — first going down the garden (12:30 p.m., three weeks earlier), second from the road (two days later). "Like a big cab" with two brown horses, no coachman, disappeared through trees. Also saw a headless man behind a tree; she chased it into the garden and it disappeared.

**V.C. Wall's nun sighting**: During the evening vigil in the octagonal summer-house, Wall suddenly gripped Price's arm: "There she is!" He ran across the lawn. Price reports: "Against the darker background of the trees I fancied I could discern a shadowy figure, blacker than the background." He is careful not to claim certainty. Wall was certain: "Something certainly moved along the path on the other side of the lawn."

**The poltergeist outbreak**: As Price and Wall returned from the garden, a thick glass pane from the veranda roof smashed at their feet — a brick-like fragment found as missile. Back inside, a glass candlestick was hurled down the main stairs past their heads, hit the iron stove, disintegrated. They both *saw it in flight*. Mothball followed. Then pebbles, a piece of slate, more pebbles — all tumbling down the stairs.

**Bell-ringing**: That evening the bells rang spontaneously, with wires visibly moving and pulls swinging.

**Keys falling**: Two door keys from library and drawing-room fell simultaneously onto the hall floor while Price was watching. He found no mechanism that could account for it. Notes: during the Foyster period, every key in the house disappeared.

## Significance

Price's most important single evening of first-hand witness. The candlestick incident — seen by both himself and Wall in flight — is one of the strongest pieces of personal evidence he cites throughout the book.
