# The Borley Rectory Companion (2009)

**Authors:** Paul Adams, Eddie Brazil, Peter Underwood  
**Published:** The History Press, 2009  
**Prefix:** `brc`

The most comprehensive reference work on the Borley case ever published. A three-part encyclopaedic treatment covering the entire history of the haunting from the 1860s through to 2008. All three authors have studied the case for decades: Peter Underwood first visited Borley in 1947 and interviewed nearly every person connected with the haunting; Eddie Brazil became interested through *The Most Haunted House in England* found while "playing truant in a south London library in 1968"; Paul Adams discovered the case in the mid-1970s.

## Structure

### Introduction (`brc-intro`)

Explains the genesis of the Companion and its three-part structure. Notes Robert Vajna's visit from Perth, Australia as emblematic of Borley's global reach.

### Part I: The Haunting of Borley Rectory (`brc-i`)

A 17-section narrative overview of the entire case — the shortest way into the story. Covers the Bull family, the legends, the phantom nun and coach, the Smith incumbency, Price's first visit, the Foyster period, the Price tenancy, the fire, the excavations, the SPR Report, and "Later Borley" sightings through the 1980s. Each section ends with cross-references to the Part II dictionary.

Key narrative additions beyond earlier sources:
- Detailed Croom-Hollingsworth 1970 nun sightings (12 minutes, brick thrown through figure)
- Samuel Seals 1942 phantom coach lights
- James Turner's 1948 churchyard lame-footstep experience
- Cathy Turner's deck-chair footstep incident (summer 1948)
- Montague Ellman's charred-wood poltergeist (1946–1955)
- John May's 1947 churchyard account (organ music; evil entity entering church)
- Dr Margaret Abernethy's 1949 roadside sighting (face visible, age ~40)
- Rev Stanley Kipling's 1949 veiled figure in churchyard
- Dr Peter Hilton-Rowe's 1947 exposure-meter malfunction at Rectory gate
- Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth's 1970 12-minute sighting and Roy Potter's brick test
- Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth's second 1972 sighting
- Mrs Hilary Wright's 1971 sighting on Lower Road (standing above road level)
- Denny Densham's 1974 Borley Church recordings

### Part II: Encyclopaedic Dictionary (`brc-ii-ab` through `brc-ii-wz`)

Fourteen alphabetical files covering every person, place, phenomenon, document, organisation, and incident connected with the case. The most detailed biographical and analytical reference on Borley in existence.

**Coverage highlights:**
- Full biography of Harry Price including his commercial-traveller day job, NLPR history, and duality as researcher/publicist
- Full biography of Trevor Hall — false Cambridge degree, Locked Book theft, Marianne Foyster manuscript, career trajectory
- Complete Foyster phenomena analysis including Owen/Mitchell conclusion and Marianne's late-life account
- Detailed wall-writing analysis — seven numbered Foyster messages with heights, dates, and translations
- Full "well tank" analysis proving the well tank never existed and settling the bones controversy
- Complete bones/excavation account with both plant-theory scenarios systematically refuted
- Sidney Glanville's Locked Book — full history of its theft by Trevor Hall
- Comprehensive seance data register (Price tenancy + post-Price planchette sessions)
- Waldegrave family history (1500–1890) including Arabella Waldegrave spy theory and Ivan Banks's Barbara Waldegrave theory
- Full text of the Blue Book of Instructions and Haunted House Declaration Form
- Australian connection: Captain Gregson's death in Tasmania 1957; his sons' subsequent careers
- Croom-Hollingsworth's phantom-nun sightings assessed; contradictions in his accounts documented
- Polish Officers' 1943 visits to ruins — SPR dismissal challenged
- Cambridge Commission — 58 students, 25 visits, 1939–44; temperature readings near cold spot
- Tunnel under the road (Tudor bricks, 32in wide, discovered 1957 by road workers)

### Part III: A Borley Rectory Chronology (`brc-iii`)

The most extensive date-order record of the Borley haunting ever compiled, covering 1066 (Domesday) through to 2008. Cites published and unpublished sources for each entry. Divided into 12 historical chapters.

### Appendix 1: Harry Price's Blue Book of Instructions (`brc-app1`)

Full text of the 20-point observer instruction manual — 8 pages printed privately. Includes guidance on apparitions, raps, perfumes, lights, apports, disappearances, thermal variations, and exterior phenomena. Ends with the "Important Note" urging observers to check for natural causes (rats, small boys, death-watch beetle, etc.).

### Appendix 2: Haunted House Declaration Form (`brc-app2`)

Full text of the signed agreement observers were required to sign before visiting Borley during Price's tenancy. Prohibited press contacts and publicity.

## Key New Perspectives This Source Brings

1. **The well tank never existed.** Kerr-Pearse's August 1937 report and sketch plan prove he examined the round cellar sump only — there was no rectangular trough in the passage. Price was genuinely confused when writing from memory ten years later. This eliminates the main "planted bones" scenario.

2. **Trevor Hall's dishonesty.** The Companion documents Hall's false claim to a Cambridge degree, his extraction of the Locked Book from the dying Glanville, his sale of it to an American dealer for £1,000, and the University of London's formal objection.

3. **The Hastings Report's importance.** Robert Hastings' 1969 *Examination of the "Borley Report"* systematically demolished the SPR's case. Mollie Goldney told Peter Underwood she had no doubt genuine paranormal activity took place at Borley, despite co-authoring the report that denied it.

4. **Croom-Hollingsworth.** The 1970–1972 investigations were significant but Croom-Hollingsworth's accounts contain contradictions (sighting duration changed multiple times; facial description conflicts with earlier sightings). His unpublished Borley book was destroyed by his widow after his death (Harlow, May 2, 1987).

5. **The Companion itself as a corrective.** Written by three authors who between them span six decades of firsthand engagement with the case — including personal interviews with virtually everyone still alive in the 1950s–1970s. It corrects numerous errors in the Borley Report and later sceptical works while maintaining a rigorously evidence-based tone.
