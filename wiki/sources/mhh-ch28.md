## Summary

[[captain-gregson]]'s acquisition of Borley Rectory (which he renamed Borley Priory) and the phenomena he experienced before the fire in February 1939.

## Key Content

**How Gregson came to Price's attention**: After Price's November 1938 BBC broadcast on haunted houses (not naming Borley by name), Gregson wrote in the next day identifying himself as the new owner. He had bought the Rectory without knowing Price; the broadcast was the means of introduction.

**Gregson's character**: A retired Royal Engineers captain. Price describes him as approaching the haunting with curiosity and without nervousness. He moved to the Rectory cottage with his two sons Alan (18) and Andrew (14) in early December 1938, while gradually furnishing the main house.

**Pre-fire phenomena**:
- **The cellar well hatch**: Gregson placed a heavy cover over the open well to protect his sons. Next morning: "the cover had been thrown off, and lay some distance away on the floor." Locked house, no one had entered.
- **The glass of water**: Alan placed a nearly-full glass of water on the hall table. Next morning: found "in four or five pieces, quite dry, and not a trace of moisture either on the table (which was a polished mahogany one) nor on the floor beneath." Place definitively locked.
- **The disappearing dog**: Gregson took his black cocker spaniel into the courtyard at night and heard footsteps over the trapdoor. The dog "shrieked, and tore away, still shrieking, and we have not seen or heard of him since." Gregson searched; no one there.
- **Footsteps in kitchen wing**: In broad daylight, footsteps in the kitchen wing so distinct Gregson called to his son — both sons were in the cottage; doors had not been unlocked.
- **Sightseers**: Motor-cars lined the road with curious visitors; one man claiming to feel "malevolent influence" in the library (Base Room) "began to collapse."

## Significance

Gregson's pre-fire phenomena follow the exact same pattern as those of all previous occupants — footsteps, displaced objects, the well cover. His dog's terrified reaction echoes the reactions of animals throughout the Borley record. His account is independent of all previous occupants.
