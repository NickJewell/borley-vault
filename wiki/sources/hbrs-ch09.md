# The Haunting of Borley Rectory — Chapter 9: They Settle in the House

**Source:** Sean O'Connor, *The Haunting of Borley Rectory* (c. 2022)
**Slug:** `hbrs-ch09`
**Time period:** 26 July – 31 December 1930

## Summary

The Foysters' arrival, settlement at Borley, and first encounters with phenomena.

### Village Reception

- At Great Cornard tea with the Bulls: Marianne charmed but found them Dickensian eccentrics, the haunting "a lot of tall stories."
- At Great Cornard lodgings: the landlady shocked by Marianne parading in a dressing gown; by her lipstick; by the Foysters singing popular songs; by their walking for pleasure; by Marianne winning at tennis against men.
- The village gentry called them "too friendly with the wrong kind of people – the lower classes" and "perverts, socialists and entirely un-English."

### Moving into the Rectory (16 October 1930)

- Marianne inspected the Rectory beforehand: "coming from darkness into light" compared to Canadian rectories.
- They moved in on 16 October.
- On their first night: chess, cards, a fire in the library — and footsteps overhead when Adelaide was in bed. Lionel: "must be rats."

### Physical Changes and Village Relations

- The Foysters redecorated with Canadian paint and new furniture; turned the old bathroom in the tower into a chapel.
- Lady Whitehouse disapproved of the chapel; Sir George accepted it as long as there were no chairs.
- Lionel and Marianne stripped and waxed the floors. Sir George Whitehouse (churchwarden) insisted on a parish meeting to approve changes; forbade them to drive nails in walls or keep a goat.
- Florence Whitehouse thought Lionel honest but "trifle bigoted"; thought Marianne didn't treat him well; suspected her.

### First Phenomena

- Unaccounted footsteps; Adelaide hearing "Daddy" coming when Lionel was a mile away; Marianne hearing her name called in Lionel's voice.
- Mysterious smells of perfume and cooking at night — explained by O'Connor as the pharmaceutical factory of Stafford Allen in Long Melford, which processed lavender, peppermint, ginger and spices; the night-shift cleaned the vats and the north wind carried the scents up the hill.
- Crockery and kitchen utensils disappearing and reappearing; a bag of lavender materialising; Marianne's watch bracelet vanishing.
- The old man in a plum-coloured smoking jacket: Marianne saw him twice. Mrs Pearson identified the figure as [[harry-bull]].

### The Rectory's Context

The R101 airship crash (4 October 1930; crashed at Beauvais Ridge, killing 46) is interwoven with the Foysters' arrival as a symbol of the anxious times.
