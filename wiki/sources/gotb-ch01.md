# Ghosts of Borley — Chapter 1: Borley and the Bulls

**File:** `borley/ghosts of borley/chapter-1---borley-and-the-bulls.md`

## Summary

Chapter 1 covers the physical history of [[borley-rectory]] and the two Bull incumbencies (1862–1927): [[henry-bull|Rev. Henry D. E. Bull]] and his son [[harry-bull|Rev. Harry Foyster Bull]]. It establishes the earliest reported paranormal phenomena and introduces the local legend that became the backbone of the Borley story.

## The Physical Rectory

Built 1863; L-shaped, expanded over time to an "irregular, sprawling conglomeration." Key features:
- [[nuns-walk]] — long path skirting the south lawn; named after the nun sightings
- [[blue-room]] — one of the principal bedrooms; where Harry Bull died
- Summer-house (octagonal pavilion) — where Harry Bull would watch for the Nun
- Nine rooms + large hall on ground floor; fourteen rooms on first floor
- Cellars with evidence of an earlier building (confirmed by 1955 excavation)
- No gas, electricity, or mains water

## The Legend

A monastery and nunnery legend: a lay brother fell in love with a novice, they eloped, were caught; he was hanged, she was bricked up alive. **No historical evidence** for a monastery at Borley or a nunnery at Bures. No record of nuns being walled up in Britain. The authors treat this as romantic folklore that shaped expectations, not documented history.

A separate thread: the Bulls may be descended from the Boleyns (Anne Boleyn's family). Also without firm genealogical evidence.

## Henry Bull Incumbency (1862–1892)

- Built the Rectory; fathered at least fourteen children
- Named the Nun's Walk (he and his wife knew the legend)
- Phenomena reported: bells, footsteps, slapping of a Bull daughter, the tall-hatted man seen by Ethel Bull

## Harry Bull Incumbency (1892–1927)

- Openly interested in psychical phenomena; would watch for the Nun nightly
- Saw [[the-nun]], [[phantom-coach]], headless coachman, [[headless-man|headless man]] in garden, and little old man (resembling a former gardener)
- 1900: Four Bull sisters witness [[the-nun]] on the Nun's Walk; one of the strongest collective sightings
- Discussed experiences freely with many visitors (Rev. Carpenter, Rev. Stote, Mr. Harley, etc.)
- Made a prediction: if dissatisfied with his successor, he'd manifest by "throwing moth-balls" — moth-balls were later reportedly thrown inside the Rectory
- Mrs Newman (cook, 1924–1927): Pear Room door repeatedly opened despite being locked; stopping the interference by removing the key

## Evidential Notes

- P. Shaw Jeffrey: claimed to see the Nun in 1885–86 but by 1950 (age 88) would not swear to the evidence
- Walter Bull ("utterly unbelieving"): heard mysterious footsteps on at least 50 occasions — *outside*, not inside
- Gerald Bull: never saw anything abnormal; dismissed the Nun as "figment of feminine imagination"
- Division within the Bull family itself: some believers, some complete sceptics
- Ethel Bull maintained consistent testimony for over fifty years
