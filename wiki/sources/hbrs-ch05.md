# The Haunting of Borley Rectory — Chapter 5: A Clergyman's Son, a Clergyman's Daughter

**Source:** Sean O'Connor, *The Haunting of Borley Rectory* (c. 2022)
**Slug:** `hbrs-ch05`
**Time period:** 1863–1922

## Summary

The most detailed account of the Bull family life at the Rectory and the origins of the haunting legend.

### Henry Dawson Bull's Family

- Harry (Henry Foyster Bull, b. March 1863 at Pentlow; first child).
- **Dodie** (Caroline Sarah Bull, b. 17 April 1864) — the key figure in this chapter.
- Eventually 13 children (12 survived infancy): Freda, Alfred, Basil, Ethel, Mabel, Gerald, Constance, Hubert, Emily, Elsie, Kathleen.
- Henry's first wife, Caroline (née Foyster), died 1914. The family was devastated by WWI: Basil killed in Belgium 1 August 1917. Dodie served as a Red Cross nurse at Vichy for four years.

### Dodie Bull — Originator of the Legend

**Dodie's diary (1885)** is the key source. O'Connor argues Dodie was the primary architect of the haunting legend:
- She had a "strong imagination" and wrote "rather good short stories."
- Diary: "I long to do something wicked even for the sake of change. I have an intensely wicked heart I fear."
- She and Harry "encouraged the notion of the Rectory being haunted by the tragic nun and it soon became part of family lore."
- She experimented with chair-moving ("I made a chair walk all over the room, and answer questions").
- She engineered pranks to impress a visiting Oxford friend, Percy Shaw Jeffrey, competing with her sister Freda for his affections.

### Percy Shaw Jeffrey

Harry's Oxford friend who visited in 1885 and 1886. Interested in folklore and witchcraft. Reported: stones falling, boots found on wardrobe, ghostly coach sounds at night. O'Connor suggests his French dictionary "disappearing and reappearing" was a prank between two sisters competing for his affections.

### Earliest Female Servant Experiences

- Young Ethel saw a man in dark clothes by her bed; she and Mabel then heard three raps on their door every night for over 100 times.
- Martha Seaber Wilson (later **Mrs Ernest Byford**) joined as under-nursemaid around 1885–86. Heard slippered footsteps in the corridor; left after a month. Married Ernest Byford in 1891. Her letter to the *Daily Mirror* in 1929 confirmed early haunting history.

### Harry Bull's Syphilis

Henry Dawson Bull died in the Blue Room on 2 May 1892, aged 59. Cause recorded as locomotor ataxia — O'Connor identifies this as the symptom of syphilis (blindness, loss of motor skills, progressive neurological deterioration). "Syphilis was the much-feared, unspoken corruption at the heart of Victorian life."

### Harry Bull and Ivy

Harry Bull married Ivy Brackenbury on 12 September 1911 (at St Alban's, London — she was living at St Ursula's Women's Hostel; Ivy was a nurse who had cared for Harry at Walnut Tree Hospital, Sudbury). Ivy was likely a bigamist (previously married to Harold Brackenbury; divorce would have been expensive and rare).

Harry's sisters were expelled from the Rectory by Ivy in 1920; their hatred festered into a "bitter and abiding hatred."

### John Osborne Harley

In 1922, John Meighen Osborne Harley (15, from Cavendish) stayed at the Rectory for Latin tuition. He and Harry Bull would spend long nights ghost-watching in the summer houses. The large courtyard bell rang at 2 a.m.; Harry rushed out claiming "it's the nun, she does sometimes." Harry promised: when he died, if dissatisfied with his successor, he would "turn into a poltergeist and pelt 'em with mothballs."

### Lucie Kaye's Background

O'Connor provides the fullest account: Lucy Kay (later Lucie Kaye); 24 in 1926; father a German who settled in England and worked in insurance; mother English (taught English in Germany). Attended private schools, then a finishing school in Folkestone. Trained at RADA for four terms alongside John Gielgud, Charles Laughton, Edith Evans. Left RADA in spring 1925 to work as a model. Met Price at the Laboratory opening on 26 January 1926; immediately hired as secretary.
