# Source: *Ghosts That Will Not Die* — Ch. 8: Understanding the Poltergeist

**Author:** Vincent O'Neil

An analytical chapter exploring poltergeist phenomena as a framework for understanding Borley, drawing on Iris Owen's New Horizons Foundation research and the Philip Experiment (1972). The chapter attempts to place Borley within a broader parapsychological context without affirming or denying supernatural causation.

## Owen/Mitchell on Poltergeist Reality

The Owen/Mitchell report (quoted verbatim in part) notes significant progress in poltergeist understanding since the 1950s SPR report on Borley:

*"During the last 20 years, we have come to understand the poltergeist phenomenon much more than we did previously... with modern recording equipment the sounds have been recorded, the strange lights... have been photographed, and even objects have been photographed in flight... Poltergeist phenomena are much more commonly experienced than most people realize."*

Owen/Mitchell believe Borley likely contained some genuine poltergeist phenomena: "We are inclined to believe [there were some] genuine phenomena."

## The Philip Experiment

In 1972, Iris Owen and seven colleagues created a fully fictional character named "Philip" — a cavalier of the 1600s, complete with invented backstory — and then attempted to communicate with him. Over several years, "Philip" communicated through rapping, moving tables, and switching lights on and off. He even performed on live television.

**Key finding for Borley:** The Philip experiment demonstrates that:
- Communicating "spirits" will claim to be whoever participants expect or wish
- The content of planchette/séance messages comes from subconscious minds of participants
- "In this situation what the mind can create, it can also, when it wants to, destroy"
- "A poltergeist outbreak... is usually the result of *one person's* thoughts"

Applied to Borley: The Blue Room séance in which "Harry Bull" communicated is consistent with Philip-type communication — the group expected and wished for Harry Bull to appear. The planchette sessions identifying "Marie Lairre" can be understood the same way.

## Owen on the Blue Book

Price's Blue Book of observer instructions, "well-meaning though it may have been, probably had the effect of stimulating imagination and producing exactly the apparitions and stories that were expected."

## Adelaide as Possible Focus

Owen/Mitchell raise the possibility that Adelaide Foyster — "a very emotionally confused little girl" separated from her original family, taken to a strange country amid talk of ghosts — might have been a poltergeist focus. Adelaide herself told O'Neil (in a 1994 letter): "I only have vague memories of Borley but didn't know until I read books that it was haunted." She spent "most of my childhood in boarding schools."

## Conclusion

O'Neil summarises: "if a group of people can use their mental abilities to conjure up an imaginary spirit; and if a telepathic person like a Uri Geller can make objects move or bend using only his mind; then why couldn't people tuned to the paranormal create or cause so-called 'poltergeist' phenomena in a large, drafty rectory?"

Iris Owen's final statement: "Psychic researchers have been trying to *prove scientifically* for a hundred years that there is such a thing as life after death... To my mind *no such proof exists*. Religious teaching tells us there is life after death and it is a matter of faith — not scientific proof."
