# Borley Rectory: The Final Analysis

**Authors:** Edward Babbs (EB) and Claudine Mathias (CM)
**Introduction by:** Alan Wesencraft, former curator of the Harry Price Library, University of London

## Unique Position of the Authors

This is the only Borley book written with direct domestic access to the parish. CM is the widow of the Rev Edward Lanfranc Morgan Mathias MA, who was inducted as the last rector of Borley and Liston on 28 July 1955 (the famous sighting anniversary) and served until 1967. She and her husband lived at Liston Rectory and knew many of the witnesses and local families personally. EB is a former SPR member who has studied the case for approximately 40 years and attended Goldney's 1975 SPR lecture in person.

Their declared approach: maximum use of local interviews with people still alive who remembered the events; avoid the tendency of other researchers to air theories rather than gather testimony. They do not pay witnesses for statements.

## Key New Material

### 1. The 1988 Crypt Discovery (`brfa-ch10`)

The most significant architectural find in the entire Borley saga. On 6 July 1988, five members of a local psychical research group discovered the entrance to the crypt of [[borley-church]].

- A plain, uninscribed tombstone surrounded by a low iron fence, barely two yards from the altar window, produced a **hollow sound** when tapped
- Lifting the stone revealed a **flight of stairs descending ~25–30 feet** into the earth, brick-lined but deteriorating; a human skeleton was embedded in the earth on the right side
- At the foot: a small chamber with a massive, heavily barred ancient wooden door, barred both vertically and horizontally
- Etched into the door: **"HFB 1921"** — the initials of the Rev Harry Foyster Bull, who barred the door in 1921
- Through the large keyhole: a **huge underground chamber**, at least the length of the church, with arched and grooved ceiling; near the door, a small altar/table covered with a purple cloth and a crucifix; to the left, **four or more lead coffins** with green patina resting on stone supports; far end showed later brickwork suggesting alteration
- The crypt appears to connect to both Borley Place cellars (which show corresponding signs of later alteration) and the 1957 Tudor tunnel, suggesting a network of underground passages possibly used for secret Catholic Mass during the Reformation

### 2. The 1957 Tudor Tunnel Discovery (`brfa-ch07`)

On 12 September 1957, workmen digging for mains water in Hall Lane broke into an ancient tunnel:
- Tudor bricks, 32" wide, 12" wall height, 28" central arched-roof height; deliberately dipped under the road
- Ran at right angles to Hall Lane; northern end terminated in the cellar area of Borley Place; southern end emerged in a farmyard
- Far too large and elaborate for a drain or culvert; no foul-air problem
- CM's hypothesis: a secret passage between Borley Place and the house formerly across the road, possibly for Reformation-era secret Catholic Mass

### 3. Alan Gregson Letters (`brfa-ch11`)

Two letters from Australia from Alan Gregson, son of Capt William Hart Gregson (the final owner), written in 1985. Previously unpublished details:
- Purchase price was £350, not £500 as reported elsewhere
- The family lived in the flat above the coach-house, never slept or ate in the main rectory
- **The fire was accidental:** a stack of books fell onto an oil lamp while the captain was rummaging for a book; the kerosene ignited and spread; father sent the boys to Borley Green telephone to call Sudbury fire brigade
- **Carriage tracks in snow** — iron-shod wheel tracks ran from the front of the rectory, down the lawn past the cedar tree, and appeared to "lift-off at the shrubbery"; no physical access for anything bigger than a wheelbarrow led to that part of the grounds
- **Both dogs** (Peter the spaniel-retriever cross, then a second dog) panicked in the courtyard and ran away, never seen again
- Gregson wrote to Price in January 1940 saying he and his sons wanted to return to live at Borley

### 4. Previously Undocumented Witnesses (`brfa-ch08`, `brfa-ch09`, `brfa-ch10`)

Many first-hand accounts not previously published:

- **[[dr-margaret-abernethy]]** — local GP (Long Melford practice); solid nun sighting 26 July 1949 in Hall Lane; later saw a rosary appear and vanish on the road at the same spot; appeared in ITV programme 31 October 1955
- **[[canon-leslie-pennell]] and [[ursula-pennell]]** — December 1965, hooded figure in dark grey robes walked immediately in front of their Dormobile at night near the Borley/Liston road junction; no impact despite figure being feet from bonnet; son David in back seat did not see it
- **[[marie-poore]]** — early 1970s, she and husband saw old-fashioned horse-drawn coach with small lamps crossing a large sloping field toward them near Rodbridge Corner; no tracks next morning; later told the old pre-enclosure road ran straight on exactly that line (confirmed by 1777 map)
- **[[hilary-wright]]** — autumn 1971, at 2am from moving car, figure in long grey robes in Hall Lane appearing to stand on "high ground which is no longer there"; experienced "bad vibes"
- **[[dorothy-lane]]** — summer 1944, NAAFI stationed at Liston Hall; shadowy figure in long grey robes at her bedside in Nissen hut at 5am; figure stood still ~10 seconds then faded gradually rather than vanishing
- **[[thomas-musk]]** — late autumn 1987, with three friends camping at Borley; awakened at 4am by footsteps on frosty ground circling tent for ~1 minute; half-hour later, music from locked church for 5–10 minutes, "properly constructed music... like an old gramophone record"
- **[[russell-herbert-bem]]** — summer 1941, as a teenager entered rectory grounds and saw three large cats arching and hissing; walked toward them; they vanished instantly (did not run); felt intense cold and awful smell of rotting vegetation
- **[[dr-alec-ritchie]]** — Foyster period (1930–35): medicine bottle rose from bedside table and returned without breaking while Marianne stayed in bed; on another visit felt pushed and forced down the main staircase by an unseen presence; car engine cut out in Hall Lane between church and rectory (no problems elsewhere)
- **[[jose-simmonds]]** (née Bacon, lived at Rectory Cottage 1951–1972) — late 1950s: saw lower half of figure in long black skirt/priest's robes stride past the staircase window; early 1960s: deep rhythmic breathing from an empty corner of the living room in darkness
- **[[canon-edward-powell]]** (vicar of Belchamp St Paul; rural dean) — early 1960s, cycling past Borley church in daylight, a shadowy robed figure moved from his left onto the road and "melted back into the hedge"; thorough search found no one; he declared Borley a haunted place
- **[[maud-stearn]]** — 1955, driving to play the organ at Borley church, heard the organ playing as she approached; music stopped on her entry; church was empty and organ locked; she moved away from Lindsey shortly afterwards
- **[[jose-simmonds]]**, **[[mrs-gooch]]** — additional cottage and garden sightings during the Bacon family residency (1951–1972)
- **[[godfrey-thurston-hopkins]]** — *Picture Post* photographer, January 1955: photo of the rectory gate showed a shadowy figure not visible in the viewfinder; photographic department confirmed negative was genuine and unaltered

### 5. Claudine Mathias's Personal Testimony (`brfa-ch07`, `brfa-ch08`)

CM's direct observations as a resident of the area 1955–1967:
- Met Ethel and Alfred Bull for the induction luncheon at the Four Swans Hotel, Sudbury; Alfred began to ask about "the Borley gh...?" but Ethel stopped him — Ethel never claimed the haunting was a hoax
- **Ethel Bull's last years:** Alfred Bull died 17 March 1956; Ethel Bull died 29 April 1961 aged 93
- Knew Lady Whitehouse personally; found her "very sensible and shrewd and would not have been fooled easily"
- CM's husband, Rev Lanfranc Mathias, told not to prioritize paranormal investigation by Bishop Faulkner Allison; said he felt "another presence" in Borley church on occasions when alone; said "It would be best if the children did not hear about this" when Maud Stearn described her organ experience
- CM is confident the Mrs Smith letter to *The Church Times* (1945) may have been written by an imposter (placed Borley in Sussex; signed in dead husband's name; misspelled it)

### 6. Alfred and Margaret Finch — Key Local Witnesses (`brfa-ch03`)

Alfred Finch (born Borley) and his wife Margaret (née Finch; served as a resident maid at Borley Rectory in the 1920s) provided detailed, firsthand knowledge of all the families:
- Margaret described the rectory in the Bulls' time as "a lovely house, kept in excellent condition," contrasting sharply with its later reputation
- Harry Bull: "slightly strange man who seemed to live in his own dream world"; had separate bedroom from Ivy; walked to church with his last surviving cat; Ivy Bull came downstairs backwards to check stair-dusting
- Alfred Finch confirmed the Coopers (Edward and Mrs Cooper) as "sensible and rather religious" people
- Alfred died; Margaret died 4 June 2002 aged 97 while the book was in preparation

### 7. Documents Examined (`brfa-ch11`)

Original documents examined by the authors:
- **1871 census for Borley Rectory** — full household listing (Rev H D E Bull aged 38; wife Caroline Sarah aged 34; 8 named children; 6 servants including the Sharp family in the cottage)
- **Will of Rev Henry Dawson Ellis Bull** (dated 22 March 1877) — gross estate only £3,132 (far below expected wealth, suggesting trusts kept money out of probate); codicil 1887 reduced Mrs Bull's rights; executor was George Alfred Foyster of Hastings (Lionel Foyster's father)
- **Will of Rev Harry Bull** (dated 31 January 1912) — £6,643 gross / £5,983 net; executor included Bernard Brereton Foyster (Lionel's brother); Ivy Bull did not remarry; died 1955 in nursing home, Hastings
- **London Gazette, 23 November 1928** — Order in Council uniting benefices of Liston and Borley (implemented 1936)
- **Suffolk and Essex Free Press, 13 June 1929** — editorial "The Borley Ghost": clear evidence the haunting was already common local knowledge before Price arrived
- **1935 electoral roll for Borley** — 91 electors; the Foysters listed at the Rectory; Herbert Mayes on Hall Lane; Marion Pitt near the Green
- **Stanley Moger's surveyor's report, 26 May 1938** — recommended reserve of £450; explicitly mentions the haunting as "a considerable detriment to selling"; predicts demolition for materials if not sold
- **Alan Gregson letters, 1985** (from Australia) — fire was accidental; fire centred on the main staircase; family stored furniture in ground-floor rooms of rectory; neither son witnessed apparitions in the fire

### 8. Dodie Bull Diary (`brfa-app`)

The diary (1 Jan – 28 Oct 1885) discovered by Croom-Hollingsworth in a Sudbury second-hand shop:
- Primarily social record: balls, tennis parties, suitors, lessons
- One paranormal reference: an amateur séance with sister Freda and a guest; Dodie as medium tried to move chairs; "I seem to have a lot of electricity in me"
- Ends before the famous 28 July 1900 sighting

### 9. The Ley Lines Hypothesis (`brfa-ch12`)

Based on Alfred Watkins's theories (rediscovered 1921), the authors identify four ley lines passing through Borley:
1. Roughly west–east: Sturmer, Wixoe, Stoke-by-Clare, **Borley**, Hadleigh, Chattisham
2. NW–SE: Cavendish, Pentlow, Foxearth, **Borley**, Great Cornard, Honey Tye, Nayland, Boxted Cross, Ardleigh, Little Bentley
3. SW–NE: Great Saling, Sible Hedingham, Gestingthorpe, **Borley**, Rodbridge, Thorpe Morieux
4. SW–NE: Belchamp Walter, **Borley**, Rodbridge, Lavenham, Preston St Mary, Stowmarket

The intersection of multiple ley lines at Borley may explain the repeated reports of electrical and mechanical equipment malfunctioning at the site (cars, tape recorders, cameras, light meters, the self-playing organ). The authors distinguish this from the apparitional phenomena, which they attribute to psychic echoes of historical tragedy.

## Review of *The Widow of Borley* (Robert Wood, 1992)

A substantial portion of chapters 2–6 is a critical engagement with Wood's book. Key points of disagreement:
- Wood's description of Ethel Bull as a "cantankerous old spinster" and "malicious and vindictive" contradicts CM's direct knowledge of her as honest and kind
- Wood's suggestion that Foyster was a pervert and paedophile who enjoyed Marianne's affairs has no evidential basis; authors prefer "declining health and mental faculties" explanation
- The 1900 sighting is dismissed by Wood as a matter of observation vs. interpretation; authors note this is inapplicable — either they saw a nun or all four sisters were lying
- Wood wrongly states Lionel Foyster failed to take the Hastings living due to scandal; authors show it was entirely normal curate practice
- Wood says Gregson was an arsonist; Alan Gregson's letter confirms accidental fire
- The *Church Times* 1945 letter attributed to Mrs Smith is doubted by authors as a possible forgery

## Conclusions (`brfa-ch12`)

The authors endorse a **fourth hypothesis** (beyond Goldney's three of: genuine/Price fraud/others fraud): a **mixture** of genuine phenomena and some fraudulent additions. When EB proposed this at Goldney's 1975 SPR lecture, it was "accepted reluctantly by her and enthusiastically by most of the audience," including Robertson of the Cambridge Commission.

Their final verdict: "We believe, to put it at its very lowest, that at least some of the phenomena were (or are) genuine, although there has been exaggeration and at certain points trickery as well."

They note the phenomena appear to be diminishing over time and may have "run out of steam."

## Marianne's Marriages — Legal Clarification (`brfa-app`)

The authors consulted lawyers and established:
- The 1914 marriage to Harold Gifford Greenwood was **legal** (age of consent for females was 12 before the 1929 Age of Marriage Act raised it to 16)
- No divorce or presumption-of-death order was ever obtained for Greenwood's disappearance
- Therefore marriages to Foyster, and all subsequent ones, were bigamous
