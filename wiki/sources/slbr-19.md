# Sidelights — 19: The Icy Hand

**Source:** *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, Essay 19
**Author:** Andrew Clarke

## Summary

A brief essay about the social life of the Borley area and an extraordinary local anecdote involving a member of the church choir.

## Context: Late-Night Pub Returns

Borley had no pub; the nearest was at Rodbridge Corner or Long Melford. After closing time, there would be a trail of cyclists and walkers returning past [[borley-rectory|the Rectory site]] via the lane. Local men routinely pulled pranks on ghost-hunters passing the Rectory — leaping from bushes with jackets over their heads as "the Nun." (One man dressed in a full nun's costume; the prank went badly wrong when an American reporter shot at him — cf. slbr-18.)

Mrs Smith's famous phantom-coach sighting on the driveway was almost certainly cyclists from the pub returning in close formation, some of them heading up the drive to see home colleagues who worked at the Rectory.

## The Icy Hand

A long-standing member of the Borley church choir told Clarke that in the 1930s he had stepped into the hedge below the postern gate to relieve himself. At the critical moment, his exposed anatomy was suddenly gripped by an icy hand. He gave a shout of terror; the unseen assailant let go; he leapt back into the road. Clarke laughed; the old man turned white: *"It was the Nun!"* he whispered.

Clarke records this as "the strangest ghost story I've ever heard" — without offering a prosaic explanation.
