## Summary

The full text of Price's printed instruction manual for official observers — the "Blue Book" — the first time in the history of psychical research that formal printed instructions were produced for investigating a specific haunted house.

## Key Instructions

**Operational**:
- Search house thoroughly on arrival; close and fasten all doors and windows; seal if thought necessary
- Patrol at approximately hourly intervals
- Occasionally extinguish all lights and wait in complete darkness
- Keep detailed notes of all sounds and occurrences, with exact times
- Watch the summer-house for half an hour before and after dusk, watching the Nun's Walk
- Establish a Base Room for equipment

**Phenomenon-specific protocols**:
- **Bell-ringing**: identify which bell; note if pull is moving; record duration and time
- **Object movement**: check chalk rings; draw plan showing flight direction; note force; restore to ring
- **Footsteps**: judge direction, duration, type (heavy/soft/pattering/shuffling)
- **Apparitions**: *do not approach*; observe carefully — method of appearance, clothing, solidity, whether carrying anything; if it speaks, ask name, age, sex, origin, cause of visit; "if figure speaks, do not approach"; ask it to return at a definite time
- **Raps**: try to communicate using alphabet; ask yes/no questions
- **Thermal variations**: read thermographs regularly; note anomalous cold or warmth
- **Apports**: note time of arrival if possible; preserve them

**Caution**: "Very important that the greatest effort should be made to ascertain whether such manifestations are due to normal causes, such as rats, small boys, villagers, the wind, wood shrinking, the Death Watch beetle, farm animals nosing the doors, trees brushing against windows, birds in chimney-stack or between double walls, etc."

## Significance

The Blue Book's existence — printed, formal, specific to one house — was unprecedented in psychical research. Its careful protocols (particularly the instruction not to approach apparitions) reflect Price's concern for controlled observation. The Monotype Corporation asked to preserve a copy.
