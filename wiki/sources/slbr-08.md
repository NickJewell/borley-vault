# Sidelights — 8: Borley Rectory and the Smell of Fear

**Source:** *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, Essay 8
**Author:** Andrew Clarke

## Summary

Identifies the industrial source of the "paranormal" lavender and cooking smells reported by the Foysters and subsequent visitors to the Borley site.

## The Smells

[[lionel-foyster]] recorded in his "Diary of Occurrences": *"A wonderfully delicate perfume [Mrs. Bigg called it lavender] would come into the house and especially into our bedroom and on the bed. An odd smell of cooking would often come through our bedroom window between 11 and 12 p.m."* Later, in "Fifteen Months," pleasant smells were interpreted as heralding "good spirits" and a quiet night.

The Foysters' private joke — *"Oh, they're at it again... Lionel would suggest frog's legs, and grasshoppers"* — was later recorded deadpan by [[lionel-foyster]] as a supernatural phenomenon.

## The Explanation: Bush, Boake and Allen

Clarke identifies the source as **Bush, Boake and Allen** (established 1899 as "Stafford Allen"), a large essence-and-flavouring manufacturer located just over the hill to the north of Borley in the Stour Valley. At the time of the Foyster incumbency it was **the largest manufacturer of essences and flavourings in Britain**, producing:
- Lavender (grown in surrounding fields for miles around)
- Pepper, ginger, gums, saffron
- Food flavouring essences (butter, almond, honey-cream, madeira)
- Natural pharmaceuticals (belladonna, digitalis, liquorice, peppermint)

The factory cleaned its vats at night, releasing strong smells. The smell reached Borley **only when the wind blew from the north** (an occasional occurrence in East Anglia, associated with cold changeable weather). Clarke: *"Residents of Long Melford remember well the exotic scents that came to the village when the wind was in the right direction."* Clarke himself lived near the firm's Witham factory in the 1960s and experienced these smells directly.

## Significance

The smells have been cited in every book on Borley as one of the more inexplicable phenomena. The prosaic explanation was known to local people but not to Price, Glanville, or subsequent investigators. The correlation with northerly winds also matches Foyster's observation that the smells heralded quiet periods — cold northerly weather may have been associated with reduced poltergeist activity for other reasons.
