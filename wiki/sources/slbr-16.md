# Sidelights — 16: Things That Go Creak in the Night

**Source:** *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, Essay 16
**Author:** Andrew Clarke

## Summary

Andrew Clarke — who has lived within 3 miles of Borley for 18 years and in various large East Anglian houses for 50 years — catalogues the natural causes of the sounds and disturbances attributed to the haunting.

## Natural Sources of "Phenomena"

**Structural noises:**
- Victorian houses built to tight tolerances with little allowance for wood movement; thermal changes cause boards to creak, skirting boards to snap, and panels to tap.
- Iron truss rods and brackets expand/contract with temperature, transferring stresses to joints.
- [[borley-rectory]] was built over a pre-existing structure on clay — different parts could rise and fall at different rates, producing sudden cracking sounds "that sound as if the house is falling down around you."
- A resident of the Rectory Cottage rushed downstairs after a crack, found nothing amiss — should have looked for new cracks in the walls.

**Draughts and air pressure:**
- The architect positioned a north-facing dining-room window exposed to full north-east winds (soon bricked up).
- Henry Bull's rear wings created a funnel for prevailing westerlies.
- Draughts could rattle doors, shake windows, move curtains, cause dust-devils visible at twilight.
- Keys blown from locks: Clarke has heard of this from others in old East Anglian houses.

**Vermin:**
- Rats in roofspaces: *"I've heard them racing along in the roof space above a passageway sounding like the ghost of a whole choir of nuns."*
- Borley was directly adjacent to farm buildings — guaranteed ongoing rat problems.
- Phosphorescent rat poison used by the Bulls **glowed in the dark**, frightening passers-by.

**Wildlife:** Screech owls; bats; hedgehogs; starlings nesting under roof tiles.

**Insects:** Death-watch beetle (sepulchral tapping in rotten wood).

**The Well Pump:** The hand-pump in the courtyard that filled the attic well tank was operated regularly — including at night — producing footstep and groan sounds.

## Clarke's Self-Experience

Clarke recounts a personal experience of being "followed" by a sound in a dark attic corridor — which turned out to be his own cat in the roof space above. He also describes a friend waking to feel something on his chest in a wartime billet — a large rat.

## Significance

Price himself, in his Blue Book for observers, listed natural causes that should be ruled out: *"rats, small boys, the villagers, the wind, wood shrinking, the Death Watch Beetle, farm animals nosing the doors etc."* The irony, Clarke notes, is that this checklist was rarely applied by Price or his observers.
