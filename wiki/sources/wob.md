# The Widow of Borley — Robert Wood

**Author:** Robert Wood
**Publication:** c. late 1980s/early 1990s (precise date not established; written when Marianne was still alive in La Crosse, Wisconsin — she died 1992)
**Thesis:** A skeptical biography of Marianne Foyster presenting the Borley haunting as entirely fraudulent; a product of Marianne's pathological personality and Lionel Foyster's complicity, compounded by Harry Price's sensationalism and the credulity of psychical researchers

Wood is unusual among Borley authors in having grown up a few miles from Borley (at Acton, Suffolk) and in knowing the local folklore tradition independently of the haunting literature. He is the only author to have documented the insurance company's repudiation of Gregson's fire claim with the specific language used.

**Critical note:** This is the most aggressively skeptical of all major Borley books. Wood dismisses virtually all phenomena as explainable by fraud, delusion, or misinterpretation. He does not engage seriously with the pre-Foyster testimonies from the Bull period or the extensive post-fire sightings. His framework — Foyster as "sexual pervert," Marianne as "pathological liar" — is polemically stated and should be read as one analytical perspective among several. The New Horizons Foundation and the Hastings Report each provide important correctives.

---

## Introduction

Wood's thesis stated explicitly at the outset:
> "I believe, however, that there is compelling evidence to suggest that Marianne's claims were wholly fraudulent and that the entire Borley haunting, including the cult that persists to this day, is based on nothing more than unscrupulous sensation-seeking on the one hand and credulity on the other."

**Key framing:**
- Psychical researchers confuse an experience with its interpretation; they also don't understand burden of proof
- "Borley cultists" display a "peculiar mixture of snobbery and naivety" — accepting Foyster's testimony partly because he was a Cambridge graduate and country parson
- The insurance company that covered Borley Rectory under Gregson's ownership had the fire investigated professionally. The insurance company's lawyer William Crocker and loss adjuster Col. Cuthbert Buckle concluded: *"We repudiated his impudent claim for 'accidental loss by fire' — £7,356 — pleading bluntly that he had fired the place himself."* This is documented; Wood's is the only major source to state the insurance company's finding in this direct form.
- Wood grew up at Acton, Suffolk, a few miles from Borley; knew local folklore; heard identical death-coach stories about Acton Place (miser Robert Jennens's ruined home) — demonstrating that the Borley legends were standard regional folklore

## Chapter 1: Borley Rectory and Its Ghosts

**The Bulls as an ecclesiastical dynasty:**
The Bull family descended from John Bull, Cambridge (Christ's 1752), Rector of Pentlow 1756–1802. His son John succeeded him 1802–1834. His grandson Edward Bull (St John's Cambridge, admitted 1831) was Rector of Pentlow 1834–1877 and built "Bull's Tower" (1858, octagonal crenellated structure 60 feet high) as a memorial. Edward had two clergymen sons: **Henry Dawson Ellis Bull** (Wadham College Oxford) who took Borley, and **Felix Edward Pepys Bull** who took Pentlow.

The Rev. H.D.E. Bull's Rectory (built 1863): "As ugly as the bad taste of 1863 could make it" (insurance lawyer William Crocker). Floor plans show the 1875–76 wing nearly enclosed a courtyard, "leaving only a narrow gap between the end of the new wing and the scullery and bedroom of the old one. This is said to have given rise to curious acoustic effects which have been attributed to supernatural causes."

**Harry Foyster Bull:**
Educated Exeter College Oxford; remained bachelor until 48 (married widow Ivy Brackenbury of Sudbury sweetshop 1911). His sisters bitterly opposed the marriage; ran a "campaign of slander and vilification." After Harry's death (June 9, 1927, "Blue Room"), sisters spread rumors about murder and suppressed will. The campaign of slander about Ivy Brackenbury directly informed what Marianne would later fabricate about Harry Bull's ghost carrying something to communicate "about his will."

**Phantom coach tradition:**
Wood explicitly compares the Borley death-coach to identical legends from his own childhood village of Acton (death-coach appeared at Acton Place, former home of notorious miser Robert Jennens). Concludes: "We must conclude either that these sightings of death-coaches are not to be taken at face value, or that the lanes of the Suffolk-Essex border are congested with phantom horse-drawn conveyances."

**The Smiths:**
Wood's unique theory: **Mrs. Smith wanted material for her supernatural thriller** (*Murder at the Parsonage*) and approached the *Daily Mirror* deliberately. "The theory that the Smiths wanted to attract publicity explains their strange and ambiguous attitude to Borley in later years." They cooperated with Price but backed off sworn statements for hard-headed SPR investigators. Mrs. Smith's subsequent antipathy to Price: Price published the bestseller about Borley (MHH), hijacking her own literary project.

## Chapter 6: Marianne's Early Life

**Birth and family:**
- Born **26 January 1899** at 5, Guy Wood Cottages, Romiley, near Stockport, Cheshire
- Given names on birth certificate: **Mary Anne Emily Rebecca** (not "Marianne" — a later romantic invention)
- Father: William Steele Shaw, failed schoolmaster, failed publican, private tutor of shorthand; family in near poverty
- Mother: Annie Elizabeth Shaw née Woodyatt
- Elder brother: Geoffrey, born 1897
- Family moved to Lymm near Warrington c. 1901; Lionel Foyster was curate of Oughtrington (about a mile from Lymm) at this time

**The Foyster family background:**
An equally deep ecclesiastical dynasty — multiple generations of Cambridge-educated clergymen in Hastings (St Clement's and All Saints'). Lionel Algernon (born January 7, 1878, Hastings): fourth son of Rev. George Alfred Foyster; educated Bilton Grange and Haileybury, Pembroke College Cambridge (degree "by special examination," Easter 1900), Wells Theological College, curate at Heptonstall Yorkshire 1903–05.

**Why Foyster did not take the family living at All Saints' Hastings** when his father retired 1904: Wood concludes there was "some scandal or family difficulty involving Lionel Algernon" — possibly a sexual indiscretion — which led him to serve a second curacy at Oughtrington under his brother-in-law Rev. Edmund Peel Wethered. Then to New Brunswick, Canada in 1910 as Rector of Hardwicke with Bay du Vin (remote missionary territory).

**Foyster baptized Marianne** on 19 June 1906 at Oughtrington — she was seven years old. This sacramental role was later invoked by Foyster as theological justification: he claimed the baptismal relationship made him her "Spiritual Father," which precluded Holy Matrimony and therefore permitted Marianne to have sexual relations with other men.

**Marianne's first marriage (bigamous):**
- Met Harold Giffard Greenwood (21) at Stranraer while on holiday in Scotland, 1914
- Civil ceremony registered at Stranraer; married again at St Anne's Cathedral, Belfast, 12 November 1914
- Marianne's stated age on certificate: 17 (she was actually 15); father's occupation given as "schoolmaster" (he no longer was)
- Son **Ian Geoffrey William** born 19 April 1915
- Six weeks after birth, Greenwood "ran off, never to be seen again"
- No divorce; only a legal separation document drawn up by a solicitor
- Marianne reverted to maiden name "Shaw"; son raised by grandparents; Ian later used the name Ian Geoffrey William Shaw throughout his life
- **This marriage was bigamous when Marianne later married Foyster in 1922**

**Marianne's marriage to Foyster:**
- Foyster wrote from Salmonhurst, New Brunswick, in 1922, proposing marriage
- Married at Salmonhurst, 22 August 1922; Lionel 44, Marianne 23
- Certificate states Marianne "a spinster of no occupation" — no mention of previous marriage or child

**Wood's thesis on the Foyster-Marianne relationship:**
Foyster baptized Marianne at age 7 and developed an obsessive fascination with her that Marianne has described as her husband "wanting her around like a doll if nothing else." Wood suggests Foyster may have had "a tendency towards voyeurism and paedophilia" and that their relationship developed in an atmosphere of "sexually charged fantasy which caused Foyster, in his religious frame of mind, great guilt and anguish." This explains his exile to Canadian backwoods and his willingness to tolerate and accommodate Marianne's subsequent infidelities.

## Chapter 8: Fifteen Months in a Haunted House (Analysis)

**D'Arles:**
Wood establishes that François d'Arles (real name: **Frank Pearless**, "a former London street vendor who sold flowers outside Stoke Newington cemetery") was Marianne's sexual partner — "a lover whom Marianne had succeeded in installing at the Rectory." He rented the cottage but ate and often slept in the Rectory itself. Foyster *knew his real name was Pearless* from an early stage (this is explicit in Foyster's own writing) — demonstrating that the "credulous dupe" narrative doesn't hold up.

**D'Arles characterizations:** Witnesses described him as "huge sexually," "Rasputin," "a thoroughly evil man," "an extraordinary person." In *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*, Foyster gave d'Arles the pseudonym **"Mr Lawless"** — a significant choice. D'Arles later attempted to blackmail the Foysters by threatening to expose Foyster's domestic arrangements to the Bishop of Chelmsford.

**Ian Shaw's account (Marianne's abandoned son):**
Ian (later Ian Geoffrey William Shaw) lived briefly at Borley in early 1933. His testimony:
- D'Arles told him Marianne was "a sexual maniac"
- D'Arles told him the poltergeist effects had been faked by Marianne and himself
- D'Arles had advised Marianne that things were getting out of hand, which was why they exploited the Spiritualist group's visit in January 1932 to "exorcise" the ghosts
- Village boy **Willy Palmer** was Marianne's confederate — rigged a collapsing shelf in the pantry that deposited pots and pans on the floor at the tug of a string

**Wood's interpretation of the phenomena:**
- Most phenomena rely entirely on Marianne's uncorroborated testimony or Edwin Whitehouse's (shell-shocked, emotionally unstable)
- Objects always came "from behind" the person reporting — Foyster's position is always one of wondering observer, never initiating witness
- The tricks were childish: pins on chairs, books moved, milk jugs emptied — "attention-seeking behaviour familiar to some degree to any parents"
- Marianne's name written on scraps of paper "in shaky childish hand" is consistent with Adelaide being too young to write — Marianne wrote them herself
- Foyster himself strewed ashes from the hall stove over the floor and blamed "The Things" (Marianne's account)
- The powder bowl and tin travelling trunk that "appeared": Marianne had simply bought them and introduced them as "apports" to circumvent Foyster's parsimony

**Wood's thesis on Foyster's complicity:**
He was not a passive dupe but a **willing accomplice**. His motive: to write a bestselling supernatural thriller. He tolerated d'Arles because the arrangement provided financial and (vicariously) sexual satisfaction. He tolerated Marianne's other lovers on the same basis. He publicly defended Marianne against Price's accusations because exposing the fraud would destroy his literary project. He was "sin-obsessed" and guilty-feeling but derived vicarious sexual pleasure from his wife's activities.

> "So it is that the fog of poltergeists and ghosts dissolves, and we see the cruel and malicious tricks played upon one another by the members of a household who lived in an atmosphere of mutual suspicion, sexual jealousy and obsessive love."

**The bigamous Fisher marriage (February 23, 1935):**
Marianne contracted a bigamous marriage with **Henry Francis Fisher** (mentally unstable commercial traveller) at St Pancras Roman Catholic Church, Ipswich. Fisher's father later provided money for a house to be built in Ipswich. After the Foysters left Borley (October 1935), Fisher believed Foyster was Marianne's elderly, invalid *father*. All three lived in the same house in Ipswich.

## Chapter 14: Epilogue — Main Street USA

**Marianne's American life:**
- Arrived New York 1946 as Marianne O'Neil; husband Robert O'Neil was work-shy and alcoholic
- First home: O'Neil family farm near Hokah, Minnesota (Mississippi bluff country, Highway 44)
- Brief work for *Hokah Chief* newspaper; then incompetent one-room schoolteacher at prairie school near Bangor, Wisconsin (claimed to be graduate of University of London)
- Adopted American identity: daughter of a successful doctor from Maine named Fisher (borrowing from her last bigamous husband's name) with wartime service in Europe
- O'Neil left with Beverly St Jacques; (Robert) Vincent placed briefly in La Crosse children's home; Marianne then worked as reporter for *Jamestown Sun* (Jamestown, North Dakota)
- O'Neil returned; continued drinking, refused to work; Marianne supported household on $45/week
- A.R. Swanson (private investigator hired by Hall/Parapsychological Foundation) arrived Jamestown February 1958; Marianne gave the taped Swanson interviews in a state of shock

**Swanson's impressions (1958):**
- Marianne "has no mean opinion of herself"; identifies with Rita Hayworth
- "As soon as he got her to break down, she did reveal actual fear and much agitation lest she should be deported to England" (she had entered US under false pretences — baby was not her natural child with O'Neil)
- Swanson had "the distinct impression that Marianne did in fact murder Foyster" — Eileen Garrett reported Marianne said when asked point-blank: "it had often been in her mind, but that he was too cunning for anything like that to happen to him"

**Eileen Garrett's impression (New York, May 1958):**
> "Marianne laughs easily and has a quite beguiling personality once she is off her guard... lying is like a game... She was amusing, gay all through dinner... I can readily see that she is willing to enter into any form of mischief. I asked her point blank whether she had killed Foyster. She answered equally candidly that it had often been in her mind, but that he was too cunning for anything like that to happen to him... Again and again she remarked that he lay in bed and wrote all kinds of imaginative stories which were to be presented about Borley."

**Later American life:**
- Moved to Fargo, North Dakota autumn 1958 after investigation disturbed her in Jamestown; then La Crosse, Wisconsin 1963 where she remained
- Career at Lutheran Welfare Service: co-founded project organizing adoption of babies of "unwedded mothers" — received State of Wisconsin recognition and funding; "highly respected pillar of the community"
- "No one to whom I spoke had the faintest idea that when they knew her she was already in her late fifties"
- Divorced O'Neil 1959 but allowed him to continue living with her (hopeless case)
- Marianne's 1958 letter to Swanson: *"I hoped that I had heard the last of that other life, in another country, in another world. What more can I add? After all, it is twenty years ago and I am not the person I was."*

**Wood's final assessment:**
Wood sees a "discontinuity" between Marianne's English and American lives — she appears to have genuinely reformed in America, becoming a productive and respected social worker. His explanation: Hall's investigation frightened her; she was getting older; her false age (reduced by 14 years on her GI marriage certificate) obliged her to maintain the fiction for the rest of her life.

> "Perhaps the real villain of the piece is that deeply religious and saintly cleric, Lionel Algernon Foyster, whose response to his wife's character was to assist her to sink into the depths with men such as d'Arles, and who used her sexual abnormalities for vicarious gratification."

## New Information Contributed by This Source

1. **Gregson insurance repudiation:** Insurance company lawyer William Crocker and loss adjuster Col. Cuthbert Buckle stated in professional judgment: "We repudiated his impudent claim for 'accidental loss by fire' — £7,356 — pleading bluntly that he had fired the place himself." (No other major source states this as documented fact.)
2. **Marianne's birth details:** Full name Mary Anne Emily Rebecca Shaw, born 26 January 1899, Romiley Cheshire; "Marianne" was a romantic invention
3. **First marriage:** Harold Giffard Greenwood (not previously named in other sources); civil ceremony Stranraer 1914; religious ceremony Belfast November 1914; son Ian born April 1915; Greenwood abandoned her six weeks later; no divorce obtained
4. **Foyster's baptism of Marianne:** June 19, 1906, at Oughtrington; used later as "Spiritual Father" theological justification
5. **"Mr Lawless" pseudonym:** Foyster's pseudonym for d'Arles in *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*
6. **Willy Palmer:** Village boy identified as Marianne's confederate in faking effects; rigged the collapsing shelf
7. **Swanson/Garrett on murder:** First publication of Garrett's interview notes in which Marianne admitted killing Foyster had "often been in her mind"
8. **Bull Tower detail:** Edward Bull built the 60-foot octagonal crenellated tower at Pentlow in 1858 as parental memorial — Wood knew this from childhood
9. **Mrs. Smith's literary motive for contacting Daily Mirror:** Wood's unique theory that she wanted material for *Murder at the Parsonage* and sought publicity deliberately

## Critical Assessment

Wood's book is the most thorough attempt to understand Marianne as a person rather than as a pawn in the paranormal debate. His research on her early life, her American career, and the Greenwood marriage adds genuinely new documentary material. His characterization of Foyster as complicit is consistent with Marianne's 1978 account to Owen and Mitchell (in which Foyster is the primary organizer of the haunting performance, not Marianne).

However:
- Wood completely ignores the pre-Foyster Bull-period testimony (multiple independent witnesses over decades)
- He gives no serious treatment to the post-fire sightings (1939–1970s) from wholly independent witnesses
- His dismissal of Dom Richard Whitehouse's testimony is inadequate given Whitehouse's direct rebuttal of every charge (Appendix C of Hastings Report)
- His sexual psychology is polemically stated and not fully supported
- The "three genuine unexplained residua" acknowledged by Marianne herself (bottle, tumbler, stiletto) are not addressed
