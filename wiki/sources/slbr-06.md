# Sidelights — 6: Borley Rectory and the Green-Baize Door

**Source:** *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, Essay 6
**Author:** Andrew Clarke

## Summary

A detailed reconstruction of domestic life at [[borley-rectory]] in its heyday under the Bulls, and the contrast with the dilapidated house that the Foysters inherited. Provides essential context for understanding the house's layout, staffing, and social dynamics.

## Domestic Life Under the Bulls

At full capacity, the Rectory housed approximately **16 family members, friends, and governesses**, plus **4+ resident domestic staff** and additional day-workers:
- Two lads cleaning shoes and tack
- Two stables lads plus head gardener with assistant
- Five full-time garden helpers
- Three servants managing ~20 fireplaces
- A cook supervising scullery maids and cooking for up to 40 people

The **green-baize door** divided family from servants' quarters. Junior staff took orders from senior staff (cook, housekeeper), not from Mrs Bull directly. The children were not permitted in the domestic wing unsupervised.

The governess system educated the Bull girls at home — "all educated at home by governesses." The schoolroom (later "Schoolroom No. 7") was theirs.

## The Decline

By the time [[lionel-foyster]] and [[marianne-foyster]] arrived (1930), all this had gone:
- One maid-of-all-work when they could find one
- An odd-jobber for mowing and basic maintenance
- The larder, scullery, laundry all deserted
- The kitchen fitted with a modern cooker but no staff to run it
- The garden wild and overgrown
- The butler's pantry bare

Clarke notes: *"The house that had once been the perfect design proved incapable of being adapted to modern times."*

## Significance

The sheer number of people passing through the house — servants, garden staff, tradesmen, visitors — in the Bull era makes it extremely difficult to attribute any unexplained sound or movement to the supernatural. The house was never silent or empty. This context is largely absent from Price's books.
