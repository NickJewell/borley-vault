# An Examination of the 'Borley Report' — R.J. Hastings (1969)

**Full title:** *An Examination of the 'Borley Report'*
**Author:** R.J. Hastings (Tan-y-coed, Llandre, Cardiganshire)
**Published:** SPR Proceedings (1969), with support of a Research Advisory Committee grant
**Purpose:** A systematic rebuttal of the SPR's 1956 *Haunting of Borley Rectory* (HBR), charge by charge
**Research access:** All Harry Price Library (HPL) papers at University of London; relevant SPR files; correspondence with surviving witnesses; Alan Gauld's 1966 interview with Sutton; Underwood's Dom Richard Whitehouse files

This is the most important defense of Harry Price ever published. Working from the same primary archive as the SPR authors, Hastings demonstrates that every major fraud allegation against Price either dissolves under examination or cannot be sustained on the evidence adduced. The Hastings Report has never been substantively refuted.

---

## Introduction

The SPR Report (HBR) was described by *The Economist*: "the late Harry Price emerges unmistakably as a rogue, a falsifier, and a manufacturer of evidence." Hastings notes two initial reasons for skepticism: (1) Price deposited his files at the University of London during his lifetime and told readers he had done so — a fraudster would not do this; (2) HBR is entirely a prosecutor's case with no systematic attempt to find defenses.

Hastings worked with SPR Research Advisory Committee support, had access to all HPL materials, and also relied on Peter Underwood's unique archives and assistance.

---

## Chapter 1: The Sutton Affair — The Allegations

**The charge:** Charles Sutton (Daily Mail journalist) claimed that during a July 1929 visit to Borley, he seized Price's wrists, found his pockets "full of bricks and pebbles," and concluded Price had been fabricating phenomena.

**Hastings's findings:**
- Sutton did *not* see Price throw stones — he says so himself
- Except in one instance, Sutton did not see any stones at all — experiences were purely auditory or tactile
- The half-brick crash down the stairs has an innocent explanation: removal men routinely used bricks to prop doors; Smiths had recently moved out
- The "ventriloquial act": Price did not produce it — sounds came from "a corner of the room away from him"; later discovered that voices from the adjacent cottage could be strongly heard throughout the Rectory
- **Miss Kaye's letter (1949):** "I HAVE NO MEMORY WHATEVER OF A STONE, BRICK, OR PEBBLE EPISODE THAT EVENING" — she was present throughout; her 1939 letter to Price refers casually to Sutton as an old acquaintance, impossible if she had witnessed a dramatic exposure
- **S.H. Glanville's assessment:** "He was the last man I would suspect of cheating. The story that has been given of this incident seems to me to be quite out of character with the man I knew fairly intimately for about thirteen years"
- **Price's continued behavior:** He recommended Sutton to another researcher as a useful contact; invited him to Rudi Schneider sittings; cordial correspondence through 1932
- **Miss Beenham (first secretary):** Astonished at allegations; described relations between Sutton and Price after the alleged incident as of "apparent friendliness"
- **Lord Charles Hope's 1949 notes:** Written from memory 20 years later, after lunching with Sutton specifically to discuss the incident — not independent evidence; contain demonstrable errors
- Price suffered from angina pectoris (first attack 1928; died of it 1948). A heart attack at the moment of Sutton's seizure would perfectly explain Price's silence and "numbed" appearance in the hotel

> **Hastings's verdict:** The case against Price is "not proven." The most plausible explanation is a general misunderstanding compounded by Price's heart condition, with Sutton retrospectively constructing a fraud narrative from ambiguous auditory experiences.

## Chapter 2: The Sutton Affair — Missing Links

**Alan Gauld's 1966 interview with Sutton** (commissioned by Hastings) reveals what HBR concealed:

- **Sutton did not see Price throw stones** — he says this explicitly to Gauld
- **Sutton did not see the stones in Price's pocket** — he felt them; he is "not completely certain" he looked for any of the thrown pebbles; volunteered that he "might have been retrospectively influenced in the direction of pebbles by his subsequent discovery of pebbles in Price's pocket"
- The "half-brick" that crashed down the stairs is the only object Sutton ever actually *saw*
- Sutton told Gauld he would have accepted an innocent explanation for the stones had Price offered one — but Price (possibly in cardiac distress) said nothing
- **Gauld's assessment:** "A skillful defence counsel would, on these grounds and on grounds of the various lacunae in his memory, demolish his testimony without trouble"
- Sutton's own characterization: the relationship with Price was one of mutual interest in publicity stories; both knew phenomena were often fraudulent; the Borley "exposure" may have been a minor event quickly forgotten by Price amid a cardiac episode

> **Sutton's signed statement (Appendix A)** and **Gauld's interview notes (Appendix B)** are reproduced in full. The two accounts diverge on multiple crucial details.

## Chapter 3: The Testimony of Mrs. Smith

**The charge:** Mrs. G.E. Smith's 1945 letter to the *Church Times* and 1949 letter to the *Daily Mail* state "neither my husband nor myself believed the house haunted by anything else but rats and local superstition."

**Hastings's findings:**
- **Mr. Smith's private letters to Lord Charles Hope (1929)** explicitly assume Borley *is* haunted and say it was "no place for my wife to reside in" — the SPR Report quotes the second letter but omits the words implying haunting
- Mr. Smith described hearing "a vase flung down" (one of two separate incidents — Glanville's Locked Book confirms two); hockey-stick test; displaced articles; bell ringing; medallion appearance
- **Glanville's Locked Book (1937 interview with Smiths):** They spoke freely about paranormal phenomena; Mrs. Smith showed Whitehouse her "animated dressing table" at Sheringham
- **Mrs. Smith's 1940 letter to Price:** Called MHH a "wonderful book" and praised his "clever brain"; could not remember burning it — contradicts her 1954 claim
- Mrs. Smith "cannot remember" every incident that conflicts with her later disbelief — a systematized forgetting consistent with repressive psychology
- She has invented memories: the "ink transformation" story she told SPR investigators is contradicted by Price-Smith correspondence showing the ink episode only happened during the Foyster period

> **Verdict:** "Once this is recognized, even as a possibility, her uncorroborated testimony must obviously be valueless for the purpose of showing that Borley was not in some sense haunted." Mrs. Smith is sincere but her memory is repressively organized to excise anything that implies paranormality.

## Chapter 4: The Curious Matter of the Medals

**The charge:** HBR alleges Price "substituted" French medals for an English St Ignatius medal, to manufacture a link to the "French nun" Planchette story.

**Hastings's fatal objections:**
1. No evidence Price ever collected foreign medals (only Sussex coins, collection stolen 1923)
2. All three medals were found in Price's files *after his death* — a fraudster would have removed the "wrong" medal
3. Price never told readers that the Confirmation medal was French — making the supposed substitution pointless as evidence for the French nun story
4. **Price explicitly disbelieved in the Borley legends and in Planchette**: "According to the Borley legends, in which I do not believe" (MHH p.185, "not" in italics); "I do not believe in spirits, and I do not believe in the legend" (*The Times*, April 29, 1939); "I do not believe in séance information unless confirmation is forthcoming from other sources"
5. The authors' claimed description "French Roman Catholic Confirmation medal" never appears in Price's writing — they fabricated a description attributed to Price
6. Price would have been manufacturing evidence to support a theory he publicly and repeatedly declared he disbelieved — a logically absurd form of fraud

**Alternative explanation:** Harry Bull married a Catholic widow whose daughter's childhood medals were hidden in corners of the Rectory to keep them safe from her step-father, who is recorded as hostile to her.

> **Verdict:** The substitution charge collapses entirely. Price's "only undisputable mistake" was confusing the description of one medal with another — trivial carelessness, not fraud.

## Chapter 5: The Excavated Bone Fragments

**The charge:** HBR implies Price "planted" the 1943 cellar bone fragments, analogous to the medal substitution.

**Hastings's demolition:**
- Price had angina since 1928; in later years walking was painful — he physically could not dig
- If planting occurred it must have been between January 1941 (Phythian-Adams letter) and August 1943 (excavation) — but who?
- **Rev. Henning** was present throughout excavations and assisted with digging; noticed nothing suspicious; his widow wrote: "I don't think villagers would have lent themselves to such fraud; it would probably have leaked out. I don't think a stranger could have negotiated that bewilderment of ruin"
- **Glanville** visited with his son days later for independent digging; drew the cellar plans; noticed nothing suspicious
- The "suspicious electrical wiring" found by demolition men must have been installed *after* Glanville's last visit (itself post-excavation) — probably for convenience lighting for paying visitors under Gregson
- Price never expected a second Borley book to be commercially successful — publisher correspondence shows the first was financially disappointing; Longmans had been advising a sequel book regardless of excavation results
- HBR's claimed "dilemma" forcing Price to plant bones relies on multiple false premises, including misquoting the date of Phythian-Adams's letter "After all if nothing is done..." (written *after* the excavations, not before them)

> **Verdict:** "The charge of planting the bone fragments is not supported by anything more substantial than a series of surmises, some of which are demonstrably false."

## Chapter 6: The Suspicions of Major Henry Douglas-Home

**The charge:** Major Henry Douglas-Home (1937 official observer) alleged Price produced wall markings when at the rear of touring parties, and faked "ghostly noises" with cellophane paper.

**Hastings's analysis:**

*Wall markings:*
- Douglas-Home wrote his account in 1949 — 12 years after the events
- De Lotbinière's *contemporary* BBC report (published in MHH) records five tours on July 21, 1937: new markings appeared in tours 1–4; tour 5 (5:15am — daylight!) yielded "nothing to report"
- Douglas-Home's "all our other tours" and "never found another mark" refer to exactly one tour — and it was in daylight
- Wall markings throughout the investigation appeared when Price was nowhere near Borley

*Cellophane noise:*
- Douglas-Home wrote 12 years after the event that the noise resembled cellophane on a hot stove
- When the SPR authors pressed him to explain the mechanics (how could Price have heated cellophane silently in the Blue Room?), Douglas-Home replied: "I have no idea how the sound was produced" and "I must confess I have no idea how it was done"
- Cellophane in Price's suitcase has an innocent explanation: it was standard packaging for items in his ghost-hunter's kit
- A conjurer intending to fake noises would bring a small prepared piece, not a whole roll

> **Verdict:** "There is no case to go to a jury." Douglas-Home's account is a twelve-year-old memory, internally incoherent, and doesn't meet the standard for evidence of fraud.

## Chapter 7: Price's Alleged Duplicity Concerning the Foyster Phenomena

**The charge:** HBR alleges Price dishonestly changed his stated view on Marianne Foyster's credibility between 1931 (skeptical) and 1940 (MHH: sympathetic).

**Hastings traces five stages:**

1. **1931 — Disbelief:** Price wrote to Fraser-Harris: "We think that the rector's wife is responsible for the trouble." Clear and explicit.
2. **1931–35 — Continued disbelief but desire to return:** Letter to Smith (1932): "I should like to go down again but the Foysters will not permit it." Letter to Feilding (1935): "I certainly want to go down again, and am waiting for Mr. Foyster to move out... Five years ago the place was literally alive with something." [HBR omits the italicized words]
3. **1937–38 — Ambivalence:** Price's correspondence with BBC's Glover shows tentative openness but continued primary skepticism about Marianne
4. **1939 — Passage to belief:** Price received Lady Whitehouse's testimony (1938) and Dom Richard Whitehouse's detailed account (August 1939). These provided what had been lacking: first-hand testimony from witnesses of standing who saw phenomena independently of Marianne
5. **1939–48 — Sustained partial belief:** Price never claimed all Foyster phenomena were genuine; his last known statement (March 1948): "Mrs. Foyster MAY have been the instrument through which the scripts appeared on the walls — as a secondary personality. If so, this would NOT invalidate the veridical content of the messages."

**HBR had withheld evidence** that Price was repeatedly barred from revisiting Borley by the Foysters, suggesting to readers that his lack of visits proved indifference.

> **Verdict:** "A comparison between Price's expressed views and the material available to him at the time the views were expressed does not give any ground for thinking that his partial change of view concerning Mrs. Foyster was other than completely honest."

## Chapter 8: The Flying Brick

**The charge:** HBR quotes Cynthia Thompson (née Ledsham) claiming the "flying brick" (photographed April 5, 1944) was a demolition workman, and that Price "flagrantly misrepresented" events.

**Hastings's analysis:**
- Price never claimed the brick was paranormal — he presented it as a curious coincidence and called it "the last phenomenon" in deliberate quotation marks
- Thompson's account *contradicts* her own earlier account on multiple points:
  - She originally said "one workman"; later testimony says "workmen" (plural)
  - She originally said bricks came "out" laterally from behind a wall; Goldney's interview notes record bricks being thrown "up" — physically incompatible with the same workman position
  - If a workman was throwing bricks horizontally outward from behind a wall, he could not have been seen by the party walking toward the cellar as Thompson claims
- The photograph shows a brick in the air at the Kitchen Passage cellar — consistent with Price's account of a brick rising from the ground
- Thompson was never asked to indicate on a floor plan where the workman stood
- Thompson was never asked whether she believed paranormal phenomena were possible — a relevant check on unconscious bias

> **Verdict:** The charge of "flagrant misrepresentation" is not established. Price's reporting is not demonstrably false and he never claimed paranormality.

## Chapter 9: Minor Allegations

A systematic rebuttal of further charges, including:

**Exaggeration of Misses Bull:** HBR compared rough pencil notes with Price's published account. Hastings identifies the *typewritten* notes (the source Price actually used) which agree closely with MHH. Glanville's 1938 Locked Book interview with Misses Bull is even more detailed than Price's account — both recording multiple paranormal experiences, not just one.

**Foyster's writings:** HBR's complaint that Price mislabeled Writing D as a "diary" is trivial; Foyster received MHH and made no correction about this description.

**L'Estrange testimony:** HBR retracted its original allegations (Journal SPR Dec. 1959); L'Estrange wrote a "straightforward, if uncritical, account" of genuine experiences.

**Photographic evidence of wall markings (the squiggles):**
- Glanville and Harrison photographed markings, chalked rings around them, and on return found new marks that had not been there when photographed — a logically airtight demonstration of paranormal appearance
- Price's reporting error (claiming a second photograph was taken) does not affect the substance; Glanville confirmed the phenomenon in correspondence
- C.E.M. Joad wrote a carefully documented report of a marking that appeared during his own observation — omitted from HBR

**Rosemary Williams (post-fire Blue Room apparition):** Price returned her letter without purchasing her story; she independently offered to sell — he refused. The editing of her report actually retained the strongest elements.

## Chapter 10: Price's Aims and Evidence of Character

**Price's stated aim:** To produce a Borley book "comparable with *An Adventure*" — readable, supported by deposited documents, written neither too scientifically nor too popularly. Miss Kaye's 1949 assessment: *"I know he told little fibs now and again, but he did not fake phenomena. I feel so very sure."*

**Key points:**
- Price was simultaneously publicity man and research worker — roles with different standards; critics must separate these
- His worst fault was carelessness as a writer, not dishonesty; he was writing from memory at times, often without a secretary, while seriously ill
- His archive was his ultimate defense — a fraudster would not have deposited incriminating documents and advertised their existence
- As a churchman he was described by the Rector of Pulborough as having "complete integrity" — consistent with Glanville's and Kaye's assessments
- HBR set a damaging precedent for posthumous attacks on psychical researchers — the wave of subsequent attacks on Crookes, Myers, Soal et al. followed from it

> **Overall verdict:** "The allegations made against him in HBR were very grossly exaggerated... whether or not the reader agrees with me on this, it can hardly be denied that the material I have brought forward in Price's defense ought — and I use this word in its strongest possible sense — to have been made public at the time the allegations were made."

## Appendices

**Appendix A — Charles Sutton's signed statement to the SPR (1950):** Full verbatim text. Sutton describes arriving at dusk, Price's brief disappearance (raising his suspicions), auditory phenomena in ground-floor rooms, "ventriloquial" sounds upstairs, the half-brick crash, his seizure of Price's wrists and his finding of stones in pockets. States his newspaper editor refused to publish the story because "there were two of them and only one of you."

**Appendix B — Alan Gauld's interview notes (June 6, 1966) and subsequent correspondence:** Sutton clarifies he did not see Price throw stones; did not take stones out of pockets (only felt them); might have been "retrospectively influenced in the direction of pebbles"; would have accepted an innocent explanation had Price offered one. Sutton admits: "a skillful defence counsel would ... demolish his testimony without trouble."

**Appendix C — Dom Richard Whitehouse's letter to Peter Underwood (August 6, 1956):** Whitehouse's own rebuttal of HBR's criticisms of his chapter XV in MHH. Key assertions:
- His account was drawn from *contemporary notes* made in June 1931, not from Marianne's later statements
- The stiletto landed on his lap while he was *looking at Marianne* who "had not moved hand or foot" — he never went back on this
- He explicitly distinguishes phenomena he witnessed (stiletto, bottles, tumbler) from those he merely reported on Marianne's account
- HBR made "most significant" suppression of all reference to his notes; criticized his mental health while misplacing the dates by seven years
- Noted that Mrs. Smith had similarly claimed not to remember meeting *Glanville* — pattern of "amnesia and dichotomy"
- His graphologist (Lewis T. Ackermann) found all Borley wall writings "executed by the same personality" except the word "Edwin"

## Significance

The Hastings Report is the definitive scholarly rebuttal of the case against Harry Price. It works from exactly the same archive as HBR's authors, has the advantage of Gauld's 1966 Sutton interview (unavailable to the SPR team), and demonstrates point-by-point that:

1. The Sutton accusation rests on twenty-year-old memory of ambiguous events, with the key witness himself uncertain about every crucial detail
2. The medal substitution charge is logically incoherent given Price's publicly stated disbelief in the legend he supposedly fabricated evidence for
3. The bone planting charge has no plausible mechanism and was dismissed by everyone who was actually present
4. Douglas-Home's cellophane theory is mechanically impossible and was abandoned by Douglas-Home himself
5. Price's change of view on Marianne was honest, gradual, and driven by new evidence (Whitehouse testimony) received years after his initial skepticism
6. The "flying brick" was never claimed to be paranormal
7. Price's primary weakness was carelessness as a popular writer — not systematic fraud
