# The Most Haunted House in England — Preface

*Harry Price (1940)*

Price's prefatory note establishes his role as "merely the narrator of the story" who has "edited the remarkable records" that constitute the book. He explicitly disclaims responsibility for incidents outside his own personal experience, while affirming that the evidence "is mostly in documentary form" and wherever possible he has reproduced "verbatim the oral and written testimony which, in nearly every case, is first-hand."

## Key Acknowledgements

- **The Bull family** — permission to print experiences; the Misses Bull; Walter Bull
- **Successive rectors** — Rev. G. Eric Smith, Rev. L. A. Foyster, Rev. A. C. Henning and their wives; Foyster specifically credited for permission to reproduce extracts from his Diary
- **Lady Whitehouse and [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard Whitehouse]], O.S.B.** — credited with contributing "two of the most interesting and convincing chapters in this work"
- **Chief observers** — [[sidney-glanville|Mr. Sidney H. Glanville]], Mr. Mark Kerr-Pearse, and Lieut.-Colonel F. C. Westland — for plans, photographs of wall-writings, etc.; "strangers to me before my investigation began"
- **C. E. M. Joad** — philosopher; permitted quotation of his experiences as originally published in *Harper's Magazine*
- **[[captain-gregson|Captain W. H. Gregson]]** — for recounting his strange experiences before and after the fire

## Evidential Stance

Price's framing is significant: he positions the book as a documentary collection rather than advocacy. He accepts that "a few of these records were negative" (i.e., some official observers found nothing). His disclaimer about incidents outside his personal experience is important context for later controversy — he is explicitly signalling that the Foyster-period material (which he did not witness directly) rests on others' testimony, which he presents as verbatim as possible.
