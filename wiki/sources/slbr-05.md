# Sidelights — 5: Borley Rectory and the Travelling Scissorman

**Source:** *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, Essay 5
**Author:** Andrew Clarke

## Summary

Andrew Clarke — an East Anglian resident who personally knew itinerant knife-sharpeners working in the area into the 1970s — argues that the 1900 "Nun" sightings are better explained by itinerant workers in black clothing than by a supernatural apparition.

## Key Arguments

**The Physical Description:**
- Ethel Bull (SPR interview): *"She could only see a woman bent over in a flowing black robe such as nuns wear. She could not see the face, not whether she wore anything white, nor whether she carried a rosary, or wore a crucifix or medal."*
- The sighting was in twilight (9pm, July — approximately one hour after sunset; 10pm in modern BST).

**The Setting:**
- The Borley road was a well-used route between Long Melford and East Anglia, with wide verges used by travellers.
- The "Nun's Walk" actually runs along the edge of a medieval driftway — a well-trodden shortcut for those travelling from Rodbridge or the Watermill toward Bulmer.
- Women hoeing beet in summer wore large black scarves over their heads: *"making a team of hoers look for all the world like nuns at a distance."*

**Itinerant Workers:**
- Clarke knew knife-sharpeners in East Anglia who dressed entirely in black.
- A "gang of 12 vagabonds" was apprehended at Borley as early as 1815 (*Bury Post*).
- The adjacent meadowland would have been attractive to itinerants seeking shelter.

**The Nun's Walk Name:**
- The actual "Nun's Walk" is a road in nearby Great Yeldham, near the church hall and known locally as "haunted." The Bulls visited Great Yeldham church occasionally — the name and associations likely migrated.

## Assessment

A plausible mundane explanation for the foundational 1900 sighting. Does not explain all subsequent sightings across different time periods and conditions.
