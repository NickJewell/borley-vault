# Source: *Ghosts That Will Not Die* — Ch. 9: Satan at Borley Rectory?

**Author:** Vincent O'Neil

An exploration of a darker theory about Borley — that Satanic or occult forces, rather than (or in addition to) conventional haunting, may have been responsible for some phenomena. Draws on Trevor Hall's unpublished papers, letters from a Mrs. Guy Taylor, Reverend Henning's own investigation, and Marianne's personal belief in "Tantrabobous" (her code-word for Satan).

## Marianne's Belief in Satan

Marianne Foyster "would not speak his name, but referred to him with her own code word of 'Tantrabobous' with hushed respect." She believed Satan's influence caused her second husband Robert O'Neil to become an alcoholic. She crossed herself when language in the home became violent or abusive. She wore a religious scapular throughout her life.

She "made it very clear in later interviews... that many of the phenomena at the rectory did not have any explanation. She never elaborated about the dark side for fear that Tantrabobous would overhear and react."

## Hall's Unpublished Research: Mrs. Guy Taylor

Trevor Hall — despite being the most determined debunker of Borley — uncovered a darker side he never published. Mrs. Guy Taylor of London held several séances devoted to Borley in 1947.

**January 26, 1957 letter to Hall:** "I am unalterably convinced that something happened in that countryside in the past that caused it to be disturbed, at times, to this day... it was begun by [a priest and two others] who round about 1480 raised something they couldn't control."

**February 22, 1957:** "My... theory was that a local group practiced some form of black magic at [Long] Melford and Borley... When we asked if murder or some such crime had occurred at Borley before the 17th century, we got 'Death — Violent,' and that it was due to Satanism."

Taylor alleged that the perpetrators of various crimes at Borley (including Marianne allegedly trying to poison Lionel, the maid's illegitimate child, Harry Price manufacturing evidence) were possessed.

A medium who went into trance at Borley "woke up in a terrible state, declaring that she had seen the devil and that the whole place was reeking in Black Magic. After that, no medium for miles around would go into trance at Borley."

## Reverend Henning's Investigation (1959)

Henning confirmed that the possibility of Black Mass at Borley "had been suggested to me... The Black Mass (it was contended) had taken place within living memory. I find it difficult to believe... There seems, however, to be some foundation for the suggestion of witchcraft."

Henning was told of a warlock who "could stop a wagon and horses with his eye." He was told witch trials had been "notorious" in Essex.

A letter from the Essex Record Office: "The question of Black Magic has also been mentioned... while I have no proof of this, it would not — to be quite honest — surprise me to learn that it had been practiced... I feel that an atmosphere created as a result of evil practices is as strong as that created by good ones."

## Additional Evidence

**Colin Wilson** in *The Supernatural* called Borley "the kind of place that *would* be chosen for a monastery, and that probably held some pagan site of worship long before that."

**Captain Gregson's hoof marks:** During an April 15, 1939 BBC broadcast, Gregson described finding strange footprints in the snow that "were not human, nor were they animal." These have since been referred to by some as "the Devil's hoof marks."

**Scrapfaggot Green, Essex (1944):** A US bulldozer widening a road overturned a 2-ton stone marking a witch's grave from the 17th century. Immediately after: the tenor bell in the church tower tolled in the early morning, the clock lost an hour, chickens drowned in water butts, sheep got through unbroken hedges. On Friday October 13, 1944 at midnight, the villagers restored the stone — and the manifestations ceased.

**1985–1990:** Black masses said in Borley church.

## Hall's Conclusion

Despite not publishing the Taylor correspondence, Hall wrote privately: "The number of reported cases of haunting and poltergeist activity in the Borley district... is a little surprising." He also wrote that he had "often thought it might be of great interest to live in the Stour valley for a year, making a thorough investigation of all these stories... It is possible, too, that a year would be a sufficient period to ascertain whether any spiritual danger exists at Borley."
