# The Haunting of Borley Rectory: A Critical Survey of the Evidence

**Authors:** Dr E. J. Dingwall, Mrs K. M. Goldney, Mr Trevor H. Hall  
**Published by:** Society for Psychical Research  
**Date:** 1956 (written 1955; Council note signed 17 June 1955 by President G. W. Lambert)  
**Raw files:** `raw/spr-haunting-br/` — 11 files (note, preface, Chapters I–VIII, Diary of Events)  
**Prefix:** `spr`

This is the definitive skeptical document on the Borley case: a full-length critical survey commissioned by the SPR Council, based on Price's own Borley files (acquired from the University of London after his death), interviews with surviving witnesses, and access to unpublished correspondence. It is the most exhaustively researched prosecution of the Borley legend and constitutes the primary anti-paranormal case.

---

## Provenance and Method

The three investigators had long personal acquaintance with Price: [[eric-dingwall]] had known him for nearly 30 years; [[kathleen-goldney]] for over 20. They had access to Price's complete Borley file at London University — papers that were not available during his lifetime. The investigation was triggered by [[mabel-smith]]'s 1945 letter to the *Church Times* repudiating the haunting, and by the continuing post-war publicity. Primary research included:

- Reading Price's unpublished correspondence and original field notes
- Comparing original observer reports with Price's published versions in MHH and EBR
- Interviewing over 30 surviving witnesses (the Misses Bull, Glanville, Canon Lawton, Mrs Smith, Edwin Whitehouse, Major Douglas-Home, Lord Charles Hope, Charles Sutton, and others)
- Obtaining signed statements from key accusers including Charles Sutton, Lord Charles Hope, Major Douglas-Home, and Cynthia Thompson (Ledsham)

## Part I: The Bull Incumbencies (Chapter III)

The SPR's verdict on the Bull period is that there is **no credible paranormal evidence**:

- [[ethel-bull]]'s various "apparitions" as described by Price are traceable to a single experience of "a tall dark man seen once" and "something on the edge of the bed" — multiplied into multiple separate events by Price's exaggeration in MHH
- The 1900 four-sister sighting was at "late twilight, about 9 p.m." (confirmed by Lord Charles Hope's contemporary notes), not "sunlight" as Price implied; similar conditions to the 1929 false nun-sighting of the Smiths' maid
- [[harry-bull]]'s visions (nun, Old Amos, headless coachman, spectral coach) are attributed to possible narcolepsy — "always asleep in one or other of the arbours" per Shaw Jeffrey — creating susceptibility to hypnagogic hallucinations
- The haunting was purely local gossip until the 1929 *Daily Mirror* articles; no mentions in any newspaper before then
- Gerald Bull (never cited by Price) told Canon Lawton privately that he was "entirely sceptical, held the view that the nun was a product of feminine imagination"; Walter Bull similarly denied any haunting
- Alfred Bull, Ethel Bull, and Milly Bull all told SPR investigators (1950, 1953) that no objective poltergeist phenomena occurred at the rectory until the Smith incumbency
- Fred Cartwright's 1927 nun sightings dismissed as pub-tale told "over a pint of ale" at the White Horse in 1930 — after two years of nationwide publicity

> **Key SPR verdict:** The Bull period demonstrates only the power of suggestion in a household dominated by Harry Bull's spiritualistic beliefs. "Until 1929 Borley was no different from the many English country houses which possess a local ghost story."

## Part II: The Smith Incumbency and Harry Price (Chapter IV)

This chapter contains the most damaging accusations against Price.

### The Sutton Accusation
[[charles-sutton]] of the *Daily Mail* publicly accused Price (after his death, as libel laws prevented earlier publication) of fraud in the *Inky Way Annual* c.1949. On the occasion of a joint visit to Borley in late July 1929, Sutton searched Price and found his pockets full of bricks and pebbles. When Sutton tried to phone his story to the *Daily Mail*, it was killed by the lawyers ("two of them and only one of you"). His signed statement to the SPR confirms the accusation in full detail.

**Corroboration:** Lord Charles Hope's *contemporary* hand-written notes (July 1929) record that Price was the rear of the party when the pebble-throwing occurred; that Miss Kaye told Hope that Sutton had accused Price "to his face and made him turn out his pockets in which there were several similar stones"; and that "H.P. appeared 'displeased.'" Hope wrote to Price in 1932 asking not to be mentioned in connection with the Borley lecture, "please say I was not impressed and thought the phenomena were produced by normal means."

[[major-douglas-home]], visiting in 1937, independently noted that pencil marks on walls appeared only when Price was at the rear of the party; on all subsequent tours Douglas-Home positioned himself behind Price — "we never found another mark." He found what appeared to be a long roll of cellophane paper with a jagged edge in Price's suitcase, consistent with the "rustling" noises attributed to the rectory.

### Mrs Smith's Testimony
[[mabel-smith]]'s signed statement (1949) to the SPR investigators states:

- All strange phenomena at Borley occurred only when Price was present; nothing odd happened when Miss Kaye visited alone (Miss Kaye confirmed this, explaining it as Price "attracting poltergeists")
- The same pattern appeared at the Battersea Poltergeist case 16 months earlier (1928): objects "appeared" or moved only when Price visited
- Small pebbles flew past Mrs Smith "suddenly" when Price was in the room; "such a phenomenon had never happened before, nor anything like it"
- A guest saw the water in her glass turn to ink while Price sat beside her; the same trick was later performed during the Foyster incumbency — Mrs Smith observed that Price was "the only person present on both of the only occasions when the trick was performed" and was "an enthusiastic numismatist" and conjuror with one of the world's largest conjuring libraries
- Mary Pearson admitted throwing sugar herself after watching Price throw coins; "after he had gone she told me openly 'It was that man threw that coin — so I threw some sugar'"
- Mrs Smith saw a red pencil "in the vicinity of Mr Price" the day after a red cross appeared on their bedroom door

### The Medal Substitution
The SPR documents a probable deliberate substitution of the 1929 "apport" medal:

- **What actually appeared (July 1929):** A single brass medal with a monk's head — a St Ignatius Loyola medal, minted in Rome c.1700. This is confirmed by Lord Charles Hope's contemporary notes, by Lucie Kaye's 1951 letter ("the only medal I remember appearing in the early days"), by Price's own 1929 article in the *Journal of the American S.P.R.*, and by the fact that the St Ignatius medal was found in Price's Borley papers after his death — never mentioned in either MHH or EBR
- **What Price claimed in MHH (1940):** Two quite different medals — (1) a Roman Catholic confirmation medallion and (2) a French Revolutionary badge dated "AN VIII" (1799)
- **The SPR's explanation:** In 1929 Price was still promoting the "monastery and monk" legend (fitting the St Ignatius monk's head). After the 1937 planchette sessions produced the "French Roman Catholic nun" story, the monk-head medal became inconvenient; the monastery legend was discredited in 1938; so in writing MHH in 1940 Price substituted two different medals supporting the new theory. The original medal joined the monastery legend in oblivion.

> **The SPR draws a parallel between this incident and the 1943 excavation:** "an attempt to 'prove' legends and theories by the production of evidence seemingly in existence beforehand but which one is bound to suspect may in fact have been introduced at a later date."

## Part III: The Foyster Incumbency (Chapter V)

### Price's Private Views vs. His Published Claims
Price privately believed Marianne Foyster was responsible for the Foyster phenomena throughout, as documented by four letters spanning nearly the entire Borley investigation:

- October 1931 (to Fraser-Harris): "we are convinced that the many phenomena we saw were fraudulent... We think that the rector's wife is responsible"
- August 1935 (to Feilding): "we were convinced that the Rector's wife... was just fooling us"; "helped to carry Mrs Foyster up to bed"
- March 1938 (to Glover): directed attention to *Confessions of a Ghost Hunter* first edition where he implied the same — later deleted "in case the Foysters took objection"
- October 1946 (to Dingwall): "If you cut out the Foysters, the Bulls, the Smiths, etc., something still remains"

Yet in EBR (1946) he entirely suppressed his suspicions and proclaimed the Foyster period the most convincing evidence for the haunting.

### The Three Foyster Documents
The SPR established the provenance of [[lionel-foyster]]'s three accounts, demolishing Price's claim that he reprinted "verbatim" extracts from "the Rev. L. A. Foyster's Diary":

1. **Diary of Occurrences** (March–July 1931) — written from memory, months after early events; sent to Price October 1931
2. **Fifteen Months in a Haunted House** — narrative for publication; completed after May 1934; Price did not obtain it until 1946
3. **Summary of Experiences** (January–February 1938) — written specifically at Price's request for MHH; what Price published as "The Rev. L. A. Foyster's Diary"

The SPR demonstrates that each successive account becomes *more* dramatic: incidents vague in the Diary become precise and paranormal-sounding in the Summary; one person hearing the "Marianne dear" voice becomes three people "on the first day" in EBR.

### The Amherst Parallel
The SPR draws 19 explicit parallels between the Foyster period and the **Great Amherst Mystery** (Esther Cox, Nova Scotia, 1878–79): same duration (~15 months), same phenomena types, same pattern of cessation during the focus-person's absence, wall-writings addressed to the young woman, small fires, the involvement of a medium figure, abrupt cessation after an "exorcism." **The Foysters had lived in Sackville, Nova Scotia (five miles from Amherst) immediately before coming to Borley (1928–30).** Moreover, Foyster's pseudonym for one character in *Fifteen Months* is "Mr Teed" — the name of Esther Cox's sister and brother-in-law in whose house the Amherst manifestations occurred.

### Analysis of 103 Foyster Diary Incidents
| Class | Description | Count |
|-------|-------------|-------|
| (1) | Occurred while Foyster was absent; only Marianne's word for it | 24 |
| (2) | Natural cause but for Marianne's denial | 11 |
| (3) | Occurred with Marianne alone; only the result witnessed by Foyster | 25 |
| (4) | Both Foysters present but position ambiguous | 30 |
| (5) | Marianne only "experienced" (apparitions etc.) | 9 |
| (6) | Trivial; natural cause | 3 |
| (7) | Not dependent on Marianne; one paint-pot incident (d'Arles) | 1 |

**Result: 99 of 103 incidents depend entirely on Marianne's sincerity.**

### Edwin Whitehouse's Testimony Weakened
The SPR conducts the most detailed critique of [[dom-richard-whitehouse]]'s evidence in the literature:

- His published account of "What I Saw at Borley Rectory" was written 8 years after the events, in 1939
- He suffered a nervous breakdown before his 1931 visits and a second severe breakdown between his 1931 experiences and his 1939 write-up
- In his letter of 6 November 1940 correcting mistakes in MHH, he admitted he "did not *witness* No. 5" (the tumbler circling round his feet) — "arriving as I did just after it had taken place." The published version implies direct eyewitness observation
- The "circling tumbler," the "stiletto convolutions," and the "hovering bottle" are likely all Mrs Foyster's verbal descriptions, not Whitehouse's direct observation — he consistently accepted her testimony without question
- His account of the "materializing bottle" (Price: "first it was of mushroom shape, then its form changed to a bottle") does not match Whitehouse's own original report; the "mushroom shape" was invented by Price

### Motives for the Foyster Phenomena
The SPR advances multiple converging motives for Marianne:

1. She wanted to leave Borley Rectory (cold, inconvenient, isolated); Lionel was determined to stay
2. She was 31, physically attractive; Lionel was 52–57, crippled with arthritis — she needed social stimulation
3. She was receiving attention from Edwin Whitehouse, the Whitehouses, various spiritualists
4. The phenomena ceased almost exactly when (a) she had been repeatedly accused to her face and (b) she had secured the London flower-shop with d'Arles as an alternative life
5. She had leanings towards Catholicism; the Amherst "textbook" was available

### The Wall-Writings
The SPR finds the wall-writings "almost certainly" produced normally by Marianne:

- Miss Braithwaite (August 1931, pre-Price): identified the handwriting as "undoubtedly Mrs Foyster's"; her brother Sir John Braithwaite concurred after examining the originals
- The "i" in "Marianne" wall-messages is dotted with a downward dash — identical to Marianne's normal signature
- The most carelessly written message ("Get light mass prayers") appeared on the one occasion when Whitehouse left Mrs Foyster alone on the landing for "a couple of minutes"
- Glanville was asked by Price to "strengthen carefully" the faint pencil marks before photographing — unintentionally (or intentionally) fixing the "Well-Tank-Bottom-Me" reading into permanence
- The wall-writing phenomenon did not recur at any address where Marianne lived after Borley

## Part IV: The Price Tenancy (Chapter VI)

### The Blue Book as Suggestion Machine
The SPR shows that Price's "Blue Book" of instructions effectively told observers exactly what to experience. Instruction No. 10 directed observers to "take up position in Summerhouse" from half-hour before to half-hour after dusk "to watch the 'Nun's Walk' on far side of lawn." The result:

- Mrs Lloyd Williams's "nun sighting" (February 1938): she saw "a round dark object" in deep twilight from the summer-house after the Blue Book told her to watch for a black draped figure. Her own companion C. Gordon Glover dismissed it as imagination. Price wrote to Glanville: "the wife of a B.B.C. official saw the 'Nun' at Borley" and listed her among 14 people who had seen the nun. Glover's critical commentary was omitted

### Price's Fraudulent Practices during the Tenancy
[[major-douglas-home]]'s testimony details:

- Pencil marks on walls appeared only on tours when Price was at the rear; ceased immediately when Douglas-Home positioned himself behind Price
- The "rustling" in the Blue Room (attributed to entities): Douglas-Home observed it sounded exactly like cellophane paper; on the way home he "managed to peep into Price's suitcase and there was a long roll of cellophane paper with a jagged edge"

### What the Observer Reports Actually Show
When original reports are compared with Price's published versions:

- [[mark-kerr-pearse]]'s report: "I noticed a rose tree which was repeatedly blown backwards and forwards against the wall causing knocks which 'might provide an excellent ghost for the imaginative'" — omitted by Price
- Glanville and Harrison: "faint 'scrabbling' sounds outside which we attributed to the activities of mice" — Price printed "faint 'scrabbling' sounds outside," omitting the mice
- Four separate observer reports mention mice explicitly; Price stated in MHH that "on no occasion have I seen or heard the slightest indication of these rodents" and that "never once has any observer... mentioned rats"
- The book-circuit electric bell: a cat had entered the house the previous evening; Price omitted the cat from his account
- The mysterious "coat": Price confused Room 6 and Room 7; Henning's gardener Mayes had removed the previous coat for a jumble sale — there were simply two ordinary old coats in separate rooms, one of which had been overlooked

### The Courtyard Acoustic Effect
[[major-douglas-home]] (confirmed by Canon Lawton): the Arbons' cottage was only 18 feet from the rectory; their footsteps "sounded as if they were in the corridor of the house"; words spoken outside the pantry were "strongly heard in the Base and other rooms"; the hand-pump in the courtyard at night produced "thumps, groans — galore." This single fact explains a large proportion of the unexplained sounds reported by observers.

### Glanville Partially Excepted
The SPR makes a notable partial concession regarding [[sidney-glanville]]: "we are of the opinion that during the observational periods of himself, his son and a small group of friends... a number of inexplicable incidents, mainly of an auditory nature, occurred under good conditions." Glanville and his son remained convinced that some of the louder sounds "could not be reasonably accounted for" by acoustic effects. The SPR cannot fully explain these incidents but notes that experiments are now impossible with the rectory demolished.

## Part V: Later Borley (Chapter VII)

### The 1943 Excavation — Suspicion of Planting
The SPR regards the discovery of the bone fragments as suspicious:

- Price said the "well tank" (a shallow rectangular concrete trough) had been in the cellar passage in 1929, but "remembered" having confused it with the round courtyard well when writing MHH in 1940
- He then claimed the tank had been "bricked up" between 1939 and 1943 "by some person unknown" — despite the rectory being uninhabited ruins
- He omitted Glanville from the excavation entirely, despite Glanville being his chief observer, near neighbour, and friend — Glanville was "surprised and disappointed" not to be invited
- The bones were only 3 feet down, making nonsense of Phythian-Adams's theory of a body "dropped into a deep well" in 1667
- The SPR notes that electrical wiring and switches were found in the cellar during demolition (1944–45) — neither Glanville nor anyone else had seen these during the investigation period
- The "trompée" reading by Phythian-Adams is disputed by professional graphologist Ackermann, who produced an entirely different reading and stated that the Canon "did not understand the mechanics of handwriting"
- The "Well-Tank-Bottom-Me" reading had been suggested by [[mark-kerr-pearse]] in July 1937 (unpublished by Price), prompting him to search the well tank — finding nothing. Price suppressed this and later presented Phythian-Adams's January 1941 "discovery" of the same reading as a revelation that "led us eventually to excavate"

### The "Flying Brick" (April 1944) — Confirmed as Demolition Work
Price claimed a brick "levitated" during a visit with Life magazine photographer David Scherman and researcher [[cynthia-thompson]] (then Cynthia Ledsham). Price spent £1000 in a bet that it could not be proved faked. However, [[cynthia-thompson]]'s signed statement to the SPR (1950) explains it fully:

> "What he failed to mention is that there was a brawny workman still at work behind the wall. All three of us saw him as we passed the house... There is no doubt at all that the flying bricks, several of which came out at regular intervals, were propelled by this workman as part of his demolition work."

Thompson stated she had *joked* "Look, poltergeists" as the bricks flew; Price "with a large smile" picked one up, said "Yes, indeed; look — no strings, no wires attached," and she "took this as so obviously a joke" that she was "subsequently astounded" to see it presented as a paranormal phenomenon in EBR.

### The Polish Officers — A Probable Hoax
The SPR regards Lt Nawrocki's account (EBR, pp. 68–71) as "so open to suspicion that it is difficult to resist the impression that it was a hoax of some kind":

- [[gb-nawrocki]] and his party experienced virtually the complete range of traditional Borley phenomena (stone-throwing, shadows on Nun's Walk, Harry Bull apparition, whisperings) during two nights in June and July 1943
- Yet the Cambridge Commission visits of 22 June and 16–17 July 1943 — immediately before and between the Polish visits — "produced no results of any kind at all"
- Stone-throwing had previously occurred only when Marianne or Price was present; whisperings had not been reported during the entire observer period; the Harry Bull apparition was said by Price to be "subjective"
- The party allegedly "rebuilt the floor of the Blue Room in an hour" to hold a watch — yet Price had written in 1939 that the Blue Room was entirely inaccessible: "No human being could have climbed up to that window"
- Robert Fordyce Aickman, visiting on 24 and 31 July 1943, was told that substantial fees were being charged for overnight stays; "someone unknown" told him the Polish officers had paid £5

### The Cambridge Commission — Honest but Limited
The SPR treats [[ajb-robertson]] more sympathetically: he was honest (he included the account of a deliberate hoax by a group of jokers who successfully fooled the investigators on 22 July 1944) and appropriately cautious in his conclusions. However:

- P. H. Lord, the undergraduate who played the "apparition" on 22 July 1944, was also present at the "Light in the Bedroom" incident (30 April 1944) that Robertson regarded as one of the more difficult to explain
- The auditory phenomena are largely explicable by the same acoustic and structural factors as the 1937–38 period: disintegrating masonry, birds, pump, cottage sounds
- Robertson himself conceded: "it is probably not difficult to be mistaken with such fleeting impressions, especially when observed in a supposedly haunted ruin"

## Part VI: Conclusions (Chapter VIII)

The SPR's overall verdict:

**On Price:** "The question as to whether Price presented a deliberately distorted account of the Borley affair in his books is not, we think, now in doubt." As for positive trickery: "the curious matter of the medals, the written testimony of Lord Charles Hope, Major the Hon. H. Douglas-Home and Mr Charles Sutton and the odd circumstances surrounding the excavation of the bone fragments, combine to produce a disquieting picture."

**On the Borley case as a whole:** Built on four pillars —
1. The spiritualistic beliefs and authority of Harry Bull in a small agricultural community
2. Price's propaganda machine (Blue Book suggestion, selective publication, exaggeration, probable trickery)
3. Marianne Foyster's probable production of the Foyster phenomena
4. A barrage of suggestion converting normal acoustic and visual experiences into ghost sightings

**On the legal opinions of Jelf and Richardson:** Accepted Price's evidence on the same terms as a legal brief; did not appreciate that "the fallibility of memory, later experiences and beliefs, and the opinions of others" had coloured all the testimony; not entitled to regard the accounts as equivalent to cross-examined witnesses

**On Glanville:** The SPR's only substantive concession. Glanville is found to be of "great intelligence and integrity." His unexplained auditory phenomena "constitute one of the most puzzling features of the whole case." The courtyard acoustic effect may explain them, but Glanville and his son remained convinced some louder sounds could not be so explained. "As the rectory is not now in existence, experiments are not possible and we must be content to leave the matter at that."

## Significance and Reception

- The report effectively ended Price's posthumous reputation as a serious psychical researcher
- Triggered Robert Hastings's 1969 counter-report (see [[robert-hastings]]) and an ongoing debate still unresolved
- [[kathleen-goldney]] privately told Peter Underwood that she believed "genuine paranormal activity" had occurred at Borley — an extraordinary qualification from one of the three authors
- [[eric-dingwall]] maintained a complex relationship with the case; private doubts are recorded
- The [[borley-rectory-companion]] (2009) treats the report as an important source while declining to endorse its more extreme conclusions
- Sean O'Connor's *Haunting of Borley Rectory* (2022) treats the report as broadly correct but notes the authors had institutional motivations (Price's death had embarrassed the SPR; [[william-salter]] who instigated it had a personal antipathy to Price)
