## Summary

The account of artist Mrs. Margaret E. Wilson's experience in the Rectory garden on August 22, 1938 — an anomalous creature that naturalists could not identify.

## Key Content

**Mrs. Wilson's background**: Painter and exhibitor, well known for studies of insects and still life. Not interested in psychical research. Visited solely to paint the Rectory as "a documentary contribution to its history." Received permission from Rev. Henning after Price's tenancy expired. Price describes her as "particularly unemotional and a very hard-headed businesswoman."

**The experience (c.3 p.m., August 22, 1938)**: Wilson was painting on the lawn when she heard "a sound such as of approaching wings or rushing wind." Looking up, she saw "the queerest object with impelling eyes advancing towards me at about eye level. It seemed to be coming out of a mist. It was accompanied by a wasp on its left." She jumped up, struck at it with her mahlstick, "seemed to hit the big object into the grass." She searched but could not find it.

**Description**: Approximately 3 inches long; body entirely black, composed of articulated sections enabling it to bend and expand; eyes "large and the colour of bloomy black grapes"; flattish overall. Wilson painted it from memory immediately on returning home; a friend redrew it in black and white.

**Expert verdict**: Several naturalists and entomologists examined the sketch and declared it was "like nothing on earth."

**Context**: Price includes this as one more "impossible" thing connected with the Rectory — he does not claim it is paranormal but notes Wilson's professional familiarity with insects makes her testimony unusual.

## Significance

A minor but notable incident in the Borley record. The creature's description does not match any known British insect species. As Wilson had no interest in ghosts and no prior exposure to Borley tradition, the report carries a degree of independence not present in reports by involved investigators. Included by Price as part of the broad catalogue of anomalous events.
