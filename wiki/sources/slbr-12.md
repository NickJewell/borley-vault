# Sidelights — 12: Price's Second Visit

**Source:** *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, Essay 12
**Author:** Andrew Clarke

## Summary

A detailed reconstruction of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s October 1931 visit to the Foysters with "the council" (Mollie Goldney, Mrs Richards, and secretary). Price arrived convinced the phenomena were faked by [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]], but his account in *The Most Haunted House in England* suppressed this conclusion.

## Price's Private Conclusion

In a letter to Dr D. F. Frazer-Harris (15 October 1931), Price wrote:
> *"we were convinced that the many phenomena that we saw were fraudulent because we took steps to control various persons and rooms, [and] the manifestations ceased. We think that the rector's wife is responsible for the trouble."*

To the rector: *"of course, no word of this will appear in our report."*

## Prior Investigations (All Suppressed in Price's Books)

- **Professor Cook (Cambridge):** Investigated while phenomena were active; found "nothing in it" after "very thorough investigation." Not mentioned in Price's books.
- **The SPR (October 9, 1931):** W. H. Salter led a team; concluded "the trouble must be due to human agency." Salter advised against allowing Price's re-involvement. When Marianne reported this to Price, he replied pompously that "Salter wasn't supposed to have anything to do with these investigations."

## The Visit Itself

- [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] noted Price's "pointed ears, a balding head with high forehead, and eyes that were startling. They were not polite eyes."
- The "wine to ink" trick: Price produced burgundy that turned to black ink. Marianne and Lionel declined wine. Mollie Goldney "immediately roared with laughter, recognising a trivial conjurers' trick." Price later admitted: *"I agree that Mrs Foyster's wine trick was rather crude"* — but credited it to Marianne, despite this being the same trick Price had performed during the Smith period (remembered by Mrs Smith and warned Marianne about beforehand).
- Marianne's account of finding Price and Goldney in the butler's pantry in a compromising position — Clarke notes this as likely "poetic rather than literal truth."

## The Bell Incident

When Marianne "fainted" and retired upstairs, bottles and pebbles rained down the stairs and bells rang. Clarke: [[francois-darles|Frank Pearless]] (in the cottage, with a key to the servants' door and access via the external cellar trapdoor) was the likeliest agent. Pearless later vouched for Marianne's innocence when he appeared at the door.

## Lord Charles Hope's Third Visit

Clarke identifies a third, brief Price visit (with Lord Charles Hope) in which they inspected the wall-writings. Major Douglas-Home's note to Hope records: *"H.P. makes no mention at all of a pencilled Adelaide in similar writing on the kitchen wall... The writing is at the same height as the Marianne messages!!"* The housekeeper (Miss Dytor) reported *"Adelaide was a terror for scribbling — she used to write on every wall — I spent hours washing them out."* Price brushed aside the observation.

## Significance

Price privately knew the phenomena were faked by Marianne from October 1931 onward but did not change his public stance until Sidney Glanville's investigations and Dr Phythian-Adams's theory persuaded him otherwise in the late 1930s. His published books concealed his earlier conclusions entirely.
