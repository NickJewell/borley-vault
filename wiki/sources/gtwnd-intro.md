# Source: *Ghosts That Will Not Die* — Introduction

**Author:** Vincent O'Neil ("The Son of Borley"), web-published at borleyrectory.com

The introduction establishes the author's identity as Marianne Foyster's adopted son and frames the book's purpose: to report, without vouching for, the accumulation of post-rectory paranormal accounts sent to him once his identity became known.

## Key Content

O'Neil makes clear he "can vouch for none of the stories" but judges them worth making public to replace rumour with documented record. He notes that even though the Rectory was demolished in 1944, reports of strange happenings at the church and former site continue to arrive.

### Visitor accounts cited

- **Tyrone Hopes, 1976**: Mother showered with gravel on a sunny day while strolling the lane — no one else visible. "Very unnerving that whatever it was could strike in the middle of a hot summer day."
- **Monica Brown, October 1990**: With a tour group inside the church; describes "the coldest I have ever been in my life," a wave of cold rolling from the direction of the altar.
- **Andrew Collier, c.1976**: Camera refused to work while photographing the former Rectory site, worked normally when turned away; eventually jammed, then worked again upon leaving Borley.
- **Miles Wilson, undated**: Tape recorder in the church captured the sound of a door opening, footsteps down the aisle, and rustling — despite no one else being present.
- **Anonymous visitor, June 1995**: He and fiancée sat outside hearing "very clear sounds of a church choir in full song — at 2:00 a.m."

### The denial question

The local grave-digger told Rob Hardy in 1990 that the residents of the new house on the Rectory site had "never heard any unusual noise, never felt a cold spot, and never saw an ectoplasmic nun." O'Neil raises the question of whether Borley natives deny the hauntings out of genuine experience or to deter tourists — a question left open.

## Significance

Establishes that the Borley phenomenon had, by the 1990s, outlasted the Rectory itself by over half a century, and that the author's personal connection (as Marianne's son) gave him unusual access to both witnesses and documents.
