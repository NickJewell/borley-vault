## Summary

The departure of the Smiths and the transition to the Foyster period, covering July 1929 to October 1930. Includes Price's visits to the empty Rectory and reports from the Smiths during this interval.

## Key Content

**Smith's departure**: The Smiths left for Long Melford in July 1929 — not frightened by the ghosts, but by the poor sanitation and general unmanageability of the house. Price describes it as like trying to make the Roman catacombs comfortable.

**"Phenomenon at request"**: Price notes that on multiple occasions during visits, when someone said "I wish they would do something," a bell rang within minutes. This happened at least three times.

**The empty Rectory phenomena**: After the Smiths left, Price and party visited the locked, empty Rectory:
- Bell rang from a cut wire in the dining-room (wires had been cut by Smith to stop ringing — but the bell still rang)
- Letters from Mrs. Smith report: table hurled from fireplace to washstand in Blue Room; all bells ringing simultaneously with no intact wires at back of house; a window "unlocked from within" while place was locked; half a fireplace deposited on the main staircase; lumps of stone on stairs; "horrible sounds" at full moon; the window of Room 7 seen lit from outside by villagers

**Key observation**: "The phenomena are more frequent, more varied, more convincing, more interesting, and stronger when the Rectory is occupied than when the place is unfurnished with no family in residence." Price states this is the single most striking pattern of the entire investigation.

**No rats**: Price notes he never once saw or heard rats or mice in the Rectory during the entire ten-year investigation, and no observer ever mentioned them.

## Significance

Establishes the "occupied vs empty" phenomenon differential, which becomes a key analytical point in Price's final argument. The empty-Rectory phenomena during 1929-30 show continuity with no occupants to produce them. See [[rev-eric-smith]], [[borley-rectory]], [[bell-ringing]].
