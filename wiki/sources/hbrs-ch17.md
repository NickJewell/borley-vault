# The Haunting of Borley Rectory — Chapter 17: Search for Truth

**Source:** Sean O'Connor, *The Haunting of Borley Rectory* (c. 2022)
**Slug:** `hbrs-ch17`
**Time period:** 30 March 1948 – 21 August 1952

## Summary

The posthumous campaign against Price's reputation.

### Sutton's Accusation Published

In the *Inky Way Annual* (journalist charity publication) for Christmas 1948, Charles Sutton briefly recalled: while at Borley with Price, he seized Price by the hand and "found his pockets full of bricks and pebbles." His story was killed by the *Daily Mail* lawyer (only one witness vs. Price and Lucie).

The incident: on 25 July 1929, Price primed Sutton with ghost legends on the journey to Borley. In the Blue Room, Price pointed out a window pane that had supposedly shattered the week before — then another window broke while he was speaking (Sutton heard a "swishing noise"). Later, Sutton sensed Price's arm swinging; a stone rolled down the stairs. He "grasped Price by the hand" and plunged hands into Price's pockets — full of stones. Price: "What are you going to do? What are you going to do?" Lucie was present throughout and silent.

Sutton: "I consider Price one of the biggest crooks going, and I know no other crook who has got away with it for so long unmasked."

### Lucie Kaye / Mrs Meeker

Now married; when interviewed by Mollie and Lord Charles about Sutton's accusation: "Well, really, you know, I just don't remember the occasion at all." Insisted Price was "absolutely and completely honest." O'Connor notes she wrote vigorously in his defence but Lord Charles "agreed that there was nothing in her manner that should make them question the truth of her testimony" — meaning she was convincing.

### Mrs Smith's Campaign

In the *Daily Mail* (May 1949) and in the *Church Times* (1945): Mabel Smith publicly stated the Rectory was "haunted by nothing else but rats and local superstition." William Salter wrote to the *Mail* distancing the SPR from Price.

Mollie and Dingwall visited Mabel in Sheringham (July 1949). She was consistent: no ghosts; phenomena were rats, deathwatch beetle, locals playing pranks, and Price's own tricks. She was "appalled" when friends sent her *The Most Haunted House in England*: "My first impression was utter astonishment at the clever mixture of legend, truth, phantasy."

But O'Connor notes Mabel's testimony was also contradictory: she had written to Price congratulating him on the book; she claimed not to have read it. And she had earlier described the Rectory as having an "evil atmosphere."

### Mary Pearson's Counter-Testimony

Mary (née Pearson), still in Sudbury with Fred Tatum and ten children. Convinced the Rectory was haunted: "forces at work" there. She avoided the Rectory and had passed it only three times in twenty years.
