# Borley Postscript Ch.8: Personal Recollections of Some Borley Witnesses

**Compiler:** [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]]
**Form:** First-person testimonies gathered over decades of direct interviews
**Slug:** `bps-08`

## Section 1: Ernest Ambrose (organist)

[[ernest-ambrose|Ernest Ambrose]] was organist at [[borley-church|Borley Church]] for 16 years before WWI. Underwood met him in 1969, aged 91, in the company of his wife and Dr Peter Hilton-Rowe.

**Harry Bull's character:** "rather flamboyant figure" who talked about boxing, cats or the Borley ghosts. Harry Bull was fond of boxing: he once knocked out two street thugs in the East End who had attacked him assuming a parson was easy prey.

**The housemaid:** A young housemaid Ambrose was attracted to saw "a person dressed like a nun or nurse standing by the lower garden gate" in semi-darkness, approached it, and it vanished — "she fainted on the spot and left the rector's employment shortly afterwards."

**Church knocking:** Harry Bull and a friend (just after a service Ambrose had left) heard "distinct and loud knocking sounds from outside, near the porch of the church. As they puzzled as to what it could be, the knocking sound moved and slowly travelled the whole way round the church. They could find nothing to account for the sound and never heard it again."

**The choir boy and the nun at the porch:** Ambrose recalled Harry Bull telling him a choir boy had seen "a nun or sister of mercy seemingly about to knock on the door" and so went away to avoid interrupting. Next day, the rector said no one had called — "Oh yes, it could well have been the ghost nun; she was very active last night."

## Section 2: J. Osborne Harley (Latin student, 1922)

[[john-osborne-harley|J. Osborne Harley]] of Cavendish stayed at Borley Rectory in 1922 (aged 15) for Latin tuition under [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]]. Interviewed by Underwood at his home in Cavendish in September 1952.

**Ghost-watching sessions:** Long hours in the large summer-house or the Gothic summer-house in the rose garden. The rector obtained "considerable satisfaction from his 'attunement with the spirits,'" seeing nothing as disappointing; each session was a ritual.

**Felt watched:** Inside the Rectory, Harley always felt he "was being watched... not by anybody or anything hostile but rather by somebody or some thing who had a more than cursory interest in his movements." This sensation was unique to Borley.

**The bell rings twice:** At about 2 a.m., the large courtyard bell rang violently. Harry Bull burst into Harley's bedroom, trembling and muttering: "Did you hear it?... I hate this sort of thing being talked about. Promise me you won't tell anybody." When the boy promised, Bull calmed. Then it rang again, louder and more violent than before: "I've never known it ring twice in quick succession like that before." Bull recovered: "No, it's the nun, she does sometimes."

**The mothball promise (Harley's version):** Harry Bull told Harley that if after his death he was "dissatisfied with his successor, he would not use footsteps or bell-ringing but throw mothballs." Years later, Harley read in a newspaper that in June 1929, investigators arriving at the deserted Rectory were met "by a shower of mothballs." Comment: "Well, dear old Harry Bull always was a man of his word."

**Quintessential description:** "He really was a puckish, lovable man." And: "He could hail a spectre as easily as I can hail a friend."

## Section 3: Guy P.J. L'Estrange (Justice of the Peace)

[[guy-lestrange|Guy L'Estrange]] — town councillor, county councillor, Justice of the Peace, and medium (Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle). Interviewed by Underwood, his wife, and Steuart Kiernander at L'Estrange's home in Bungay, Suffolk, 23 August 1969.

Visited Borley Rectory in **January 1932** during the Foyster incumbency. Full account:

**Tall clerical figure by porch:** Before entering, saw "a tall figure, like a man in ecclesiastical attire, near the porch" — it vanished when approached and did not reappear.

**Crockery and bottles:** Loud crash in the hall — floor littered with broken crockery. Then "an appalling series of crashing sounds": bottles "being hurled about in all directions, although no human person was to be seen." All stood and watched bottles appear in mid-air and hurl themselves to the floor. "One large wine bottle missed my left ear by about an inch." The shed of empty bottles outside was locked with the window bolted.

**The thirty bells:** As the three descended the stairs, all thirty bells in the kitchen passage began clanging wildly. L'Estrange said: "If some invisible person is present and can hear these words, please stop the ringing for a moment." Instantly, every bell became silent.

**Footsteps through the wall:** In the drawing-room, footsteps entered, crossed the room behind him, and "went through the wall at the far end." Discussion with Lionel confirmed there had once been a doorway there.

**The luminous form:** In the "haunted bedroom" in the small hours, L'Estrange saw "a vague and slightly luminous form" beside his bed. As he watched, it became "larger and larger and more dense until it seemed to represent the shape of a human being in long robes." He attempted to approach but felt "considerable willpower and persistence" required to stay in place — "an attempt was being made to push him away." The form abruptly faded; the icy chill disappeared; he sank back exhausted.

**Marianne at 1 a.m.:** Saw Marianne Foyster "glide rather than walk" past the Blue Room door in a diaphanous night-dress. She replied to his question in a trance-like voice: "To — make — some — tea." Lionel, alerted, was "very annoyed."

**SPR retraction:** The SPR Report (1956) contained "quite untrue and libellous" paragraphs about L'Estrange. The three authors had to publish a retraction. Captain V.M. Deane separately confirmed the bottle materialization: "he had cross-examined the witnesses for hours on end, and there was no shadow of doubt."

**Aftermath:** A week after the visit, Foyster wrote to thank L'Estrange, saying there had been "no more disturbances and quite a different feeling throughout the house." Three years later: "the house is now perfectly normal." But things did not stay that way.

## Section 4: Rev. Clive Luget (Rector of Middleton)

[[clive-luget|Rev. Clive Luget]], Rector of Middleton (one mile south of Sudbury). Visited Borley Rectory many times during the 1930s–40s at the invitation of Lionel Foyster. Interviewed by Underwood and Tom Brown at Luget's home in 1948.

**Ghost nun personally:** Luget personally saw the [[the-nun|ghost nun]] on his visits.

**Pencilled "Marianne" appearing:** About to leave a room he had examined minutely, Luget heard a sound behind him, turned, and found "a sheet of paper in the middle of the room" with "a pencilled 'Marianne' in letters about three inches high." The room had been empty and minutely checked.

**Blue Room pencil:** In the Blue Room with Marianne and Lionel, they all heard "a scribbling sound from the room behind them." Turned to see a pencil drop to the floor and a message asking for prayers on the wall in 2–3 inch letters. All three dropped to their knees and prayed. Nothing further happened that visit.

**Harry Bull's dressing-gown:** In an upstairs corridor with Marianne, they both saw "the figure of a man in a plum-coloured dressing-gown come out of a room ahead of them and then... completely disappeared." Later, Mrs. Payne of Borley Place confirmed Harry Bull "almost lived in" a plum-coloured dressing-gown.

**Stone-throwing:** Pelted with pebbles coming down the main stairs from above; "immediate investigation failed to reveal any possible explanation." The child was in the kitchen.

**Failed exorcisms:** Luget and a brother clergyman used holy water from the Well of Our Lady of Walsingham and performed a mild exorcism; no effect. They then performed an elaborate exorcism rite plus creosote fumigation; no effect. The next day, four priests together sprinkled holy water and prayed every room — no effect.

**Marianne (Luget's perspective):** Delightful, cheerful, excellent hostess; protective of children. Not frightened by odd happenings but "interested and sought for an explanation." Showed anxiety only at signs of fire or very loud noises. "Flirtatious side" that helped with visitors but was "exaggerated and not understood by some villagers."

## Section 5: James and Catherine Turner

[[james-turner|James and Cathy Turner]] (April 1947–June 1950). Previously published accounts were in tension with what they told Underwood privately; this section draws on James's unpublished manuscripts and diaries and Cathy's gifts to Underwood before her death.

**What Turner said publicly:** In *Sometimes into England* (1970), he wrote "I never saw anything of a ghostly nature" and attributed the voices to "the noise of aeroplanes warming up."

**What Turner said privately:**
- **2 November 1947:** Heard "something or somebody with a lame leg and a swishing skirt" pass along the church path at midnight toward the porch — recorded at the time and used in a feature film *Spotlight on Spooks* (1951).
- **Whit Monday, 26 May 1947:** All heard footsteps and muffled talking from inside the locked church; sounds ceased the instant the door began to open.
- **27 May 1947:** A very loud noise "like heavy furniture being shifted" from empty upper rooms of the cottage. Doors all closed; nothing moved.
- **Voices (May–June 1947):** While clearing brambles, Turner heard "happy and laughing" voices from the direction of the Rectory site. Cathy also heard them. "Always, as soon as I stopped work and laid down the sickle, the voices, happy and laughing, fled before me."
- **September 1948:** Loud crash of crockery heard by both; nothing disturbed; two workmen in the yard came to the door to ask what had happened.
- **Summer 1949:** A spectral cat chased their own cat Fred under the car; Fred emerged from the other side and shot up the walnut tree; the pursuing cat was nowhere to be found.
- **March 1950:** Underwood and Laurence Gafford, staying in the lounge below the Turners' bedroom, heard heavy footsteps and muffled voices from above three times during the night (1:15, ~2:30, ~4:15 a.m.). Each time sounds lasted ~20 seconds. Turners confirmed they had slept soundly throughout.
- **Privately confirmed:** Both James and Cathy told Underwood they had "both glimpsed a nun-like figure near the Nun's Walk one summer evening and seen a tall male clerical figure in the churchyard." They also claimed contact with an entity "claiming to be the Rev. Harry Bull." Harry Price wrote to James saying he had "accumulated a lot more evidence" and wanted to include the Turners' experiences in *Borley III*.
