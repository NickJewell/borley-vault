## Summary

Price's critical assessment of the Borley legends — the bricked-up nun, the phantom coach — alongside the first systematic catalogue of all recorded phenomena at the Rectory. Price separates the unreliable legend from the well-evidenced phenomena.

## Key Content

**The nun legend debunked**: The story of a novice from Bures nunnery who eloped with a Borley lay brother and was subsequently bricked up alive is historically implausible. A *Times* correspondence (prompted by Price's April 1939 Ghost Club lecture) established: (a) nuns being bricked up alive is "mythical" according to Father Herbert Thurston S.J. after careful research; (b) coaches did not exist in England until the mid-16th century, ruling out the medieval elopement narrative. Price is explicit: "it was not 'my legend' but one that had been current for fifty years."

**The coach legend**: Similarly, a "black coach" in a 13th- or 14th-century monastic context is anachronistic. However Price insists this does not explain away the *sightings* of a coach — many witnesses have reported one, and it presumably corresponds to a vehicle that actually existed at a later date.

**The nun herself persists**: Despite demolishing the legend, Price emphasises the nun has been seen by many people simultaneously, vanished before witnesses, and appeared to a man (later [[fred-cartwright]]) who knew nothing of the haunting.

**The screaming girl legend**: A young woman allegedly fell from the Blue Room window to her death. Not substantiated. However, a figure of a white/blue-clad young woman was seen leaning from the aperture of the burnt-out Blue Room window by two people simultaneously shortly after the 1939 fire — which Price connects loosely.

**The first comprehensive phenomenon list**: Price catalogues all phenomena recorded at the Rectory up to 1940: poltergeist manifestations; telekinesis; appearance/disappearance of objects; voices; footsteps; spontaneous combustion; wall-writings; paranormal bell-ringing; thermal variations; "touchings"; choir music; strange lights; rumbling coach sounds; galloping horses; odours; fright in animals; smoke column; strange footprints in snow; intelligent raps; fulfilled Planchette prediction.

## Significance

Establishes Price's methodological stance: sceptical of legend, open to empirical evidence. The debunking of the bricked-up nun legend is Price's own position, not just sceptics'. The phenomenon catalogue serves as a master checklist against which all subsequent reports are measured. See [[the-nun]], [[phantom-coach]], [[poltergeist-activity]].
