## Summary

Price's survey of the Rectory grounds, written from his own first-hand investigation. Establishes the precise physical layout of the garden and its associated legends, providing the spatial context for all subsequent Nun sightings.

## Key Content

**The Nun's Walk itself** is a long path skirting the lawn on its far side from the main living-rooms. It runs for the full length of the lawn; the nun is reported to glide along it, typically at dusk, and allegedly always on July 28. Two routes are recorded: one running the full length to the stream, the other involving a turn across the lawn to a clump of trees near the octagonal summer-house.

**The two summer-houses**: The large octagonal summer-house was built by [[henry-bull]] *specifically to watch for the nun* in comfort — two of its three sides are entirely open, giving a wide angle of view across the Walk. A smaller summer-house in the copse at the garden's end was also built by Henry Bull, used for "communing with the spirits" in the early hours. Both were in regular use by [[harry-bull]].

**The stream and cats' cemetery**: A small stream runs through the garden's end, into which the nun reportedly disappears. A small bridge crosses it. Near the stream sits an overgrown cats' cemetery with headboards for "Gem," "Rollo," and "Sandy."

**The tunnel**: Remains of an underground tunnel are visible in the Rectory farmyard. Portions have been found in a direct line between Borley and Bures (7 miles), where the remains of a nunnery have been found. A second tunnel from Clare reportedly meets the Bures tunnel in the Rectory grounds. The road outside the Rectory sounds hollow under heavy vehicles, suggesting a tunnel beneath it.

**Plague burials**: A portion of the garden is a burial ground for victims of the Great Plague (1654–5). Skulls occasionally turned up by spade are reinterred in the churchyard.

## Significance

Establishes the physical setting for the [[the-nun]] sightings and the tunnel/nunnery backstory underpinning the [[marie-lairre]] theory. The summer-house detail — built deliberately for nun-watching — demonstrates how seriously Henry Bull took the phenomenon.
