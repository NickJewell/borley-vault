# The End of Borley Rectory (Harry Price, 1946)

Harry Price's sequel to *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940). Written April 1946 from the Reform Club, Pall Mall. Covers 1939–1945: new witnesses, the Cambridge Commission investigation, Canon Phythian-Adams's decipherment of the wall-writings, the August 1943 cellar excavation and discovery of human remains, the pendants, the reburial of the supposed [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]] at Liston Churchyard on May 29, 1945, and the final demolition of the [[borley-rectory|Rectory]] in 1944–45.

---

## Chapter Overview

### Preface (`ebr-pref`)
Price acknowledges that the fire (February 27, 1939) did not end the story. Two former rectors have died: the Rev. G. Eric Smith (August 3, 1940) and the Rev. Lionel Foyster (April 18, 1945). [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] contributed two chapters. Sir Ernest Jelf and Sir Albion Richardson provided the first legal opinions in psychical research history.

### Ch. 1 — The Story of 'The Most Haunted House in England' (`ebr-ch01`)
A complete synopsis of the Borley case 1863–1939 for new readers. Contains [[harry-price|Price]]'s first-hand account of June 12–13, 1929 — the candlestick flight, the moth-balls, keys falling, and the Blue Room séance. Confirms [[edward-cooper|Cooper]]'s coach sighting and [[fred-cartwright|Cartwright]]'s four nun sightings (1927). Records the fire of February 27–28, 1939 — [[captain-gregson|Gregson]]'s account of the stacked books falling onto the lamp; the constable's report of a "lady and gentleman in cloaks" leaving during the fire; and [[rosemary-williams|Rosemary Williams]] seeing a woman at the Blue Room window one month after the fire. Price notes that even while ruined, phenomena continued — H.F. Russell was thrown to the ground in November 1941.

### Ch. 2 — Priests vs Poltergeists (`ebr-ch02`)
Detailed account of the Foyster period 1930–35 from Lionel Foyster's diary and *Fifteen Months in a Haunted House*. Estimate of "at least 2,000 Poltergeist phenomena" October 1930 – October 1935. Documents the multiple exorcism attempts (holy water from Walsingham, incense, creosote, four clergymen in one day) — all failed. Records the extraordinary bottle phenomena of November 13, 1931 (bottles materialising in mid-air before [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard Whitehouse]], Marianne, and the maid). Records the Curé d'Ars relic unlocking Marianne's bedroom door on October 13, 1931.

### Ch. 3 — An Exciting Night (`ebr-ch03`)
[[guy-lestrange|G.P.J. L'Estrange]]'s first-person account of January 23–24, 1932, previously published in the *Norwich Mercury* (December 24, 1942). L'Estrange: arrived in early darkness; saw a tall robed figure at the porch that vanished; experienced bottles materialising in mid-air and crashing; stopped the bells by addressing them directly; saw a luminous form in his bedroom that he faced down until it faded. Foyster's diary confirms: "Great demonstrations! Bottles dashed down back stairs." Captain Deane (Marks Tey Circle) vouches for shower of bottles and stones seen collectively by 3–5 percipients. After this night, Foyster wrote "the house is now perfectly normal" — quiet for approximately two years.

### Ch. 4 — Another 'Cloud of Witnesses' (`ebr-ch04`)
New witnesses post-fire:
- **H.F. Russell** (managing director): thrown to ground near stables entrance November 12, 1941, in broad daylight, with his two RAF sons as witnesses; attributed to a forceful invisible "ejection."
- **Lt. G.B. Nawrocki** (Polish Army Medical Corps doctor): two nights at the ruins — June 28–29 and July 28–29, 1943 — with minute-by-minute protocols. Stones thrown in kitchen passage and Blue Room; door opened and closed when no one on ground floor; shadow on Nun's Walk vanishing between second and third trees; shadow of a man silhouetted on chapel wall (second night).
- **Harry Marshall and J.H. Russell** (Trinity College Oxford undergraduates): January 3–6, 1944. Marshall took 50 photographs (some reproduced in this volume). Russell heard what he believed was Marshall's voice — Marshall had not spoken (location: near the Nun's Walk stream). Their tool bag moved to behind the summer-house. The last photographs recorded the rapid demolition.
- **Robert Fordyce Aickman** and friends: multiple visits July 1943 — crash heard from cupboard; on July 31 a night of thunderstorm; floor rectangle had been lifted. Aickman reports the pseudo-Gothic summer-house had been demolished by military authorities, who excavated on its site.
- **Mrs Savage** (local resident): confirmed lights and "queer sounds" to American Weekly reporter; complained about ARP thinking her responsible for black-out violations. Reported army tried to use Rectory but were driven out by "Poltergeister."
- **Rev. Henning's letters**: sanctuary lamp wick moved in locked church; officer awakened by all bells ringing (during wartime manoeuvres); Mrs Martin felt cold all over approaching the Rectory (September 1943); a man working at a nearby aerodrome saw the nun c.1942 — "she did look miserable."
- **Mrs Newman**: cook for the Bull family 1924–27. Nightly unlocking of the door to the "pear room" despite key inside; could not prevent it; finally removed the key. Also saw her clothes being moved on their pegs behind a curtain in the night, silhouetted against the window.

### Ch. 5 — The Enchanted 'Tea Garden' (`ebr-ch05`)
**Gilbert Hayes** (film comedian, appeared in c.350 films): September 1939 answered an advertisement in *Dalton's Weekly* for a property near Sudbury. It was listed as "Borley Priory" — Hayes had never heard of the case. Walking the grounds, he heard and felt phantom footsteps following him for the entire tour, assuming it was his wife. When he turned, no one was there. Upstairs in the cottage, bills he had stuffed in a paper hold-all were moved 2 feet to a shelf behind his back. Both he and his wife independently heard footsteps they thought were the other person. A postman at Sudbury station told them the house was "the most haunted in all England." Hayes: "I don't know Harry Price; he doesn't know me; but our experiences are facts." Rev. Daniel Booth (September 1945): similar experience in the garden thicket, hearing footsteps behind him that proved not to be his son. [[edward-cooper|Cooper]] and [[walter-bull|Gerald Bull]] both confirm hearing footsteps in the road approaching the Rectory on ~50 occasions. Arthur Medcraft (July 1943): footsteps in the road, door slam heard when no wind.

### Ch. 6 — A Century of Evidence (`ebr-ch06`)
Price establishes evidence of haunting spanning approximately a century before 1943:
- **~1878–79** (Charles Boyden, writing from Alberta): as a boy of nine in Borley village heard the ghost story "over fifty years ago" and "experienced a little of it myself." Confirms tradition existed before 1880.
- **1885–86** (P. Shaw Jeffrey, Cape Town letter March 5, 1942): Oxford friend of Harry Bull; stayed with the Bulls at Borley. Saw the nun "several times." Heard "the coach go clattering by" so often he "slept through the noise." Found his French dictionary had fallen to his locked bedroom floor with a loud bump in the night — locked door, closed window. At nearby **Felix Bull's rectory**: witnessed a tooth-glass fly from behind a maid's back and circle round; fender and fire-irons moved across the room; the maid later became a London medium. Jeffrey confirms 17 children in the Bull family; Henry Bull had Trinity Audit Ale in the cellar (former Trinity fellow); he was a "hedge parson" who shot rabbits from the drawing-room floor on Sundays.
- **1886** (Mrs E. Byford): nursemaid at Borley; heard slippered footsteps approach her locked room in the night; left after a fortnight; her grandparents would not let her pass the house after dark. "Common talk that the Rectory was haunted."
- **1929 onward** (multiple new confirmations): Rev. Walter Stote confirms Harry Bull told him personally about seeing the nun; Mrs Gowan's copy of Harry Carpenter's letter confirms Bull saw the headless coachman with a dog that reacted.

### Ch. 7 — Some Readers' Queries Answered (`ebr-ch07`)
Price answers correspondence about *The Most Haunted House in England*. Reproduces full account of how the Glanville family obtained the name "Lairre" through Planchette. Discusses: why no Mass was said (Anglican property; Requiem Masses later said at Arundel and Oxford); why excavation was delayed; the Bures nunnery (Mrs Brand confirms a house at Bures was "used as a private house" having formerly been a nunnery, 1910–21); Spirit photographs (Price is sceptical — the "nun" in Plate I is probably pareidolia; the "Prince of Wales's feathers" markings on walls may be a fleur-de-lis or the Waldegrave crest). Skull found by Mrs Smith in 1929: medical opinion said young woman; reverently buried by Smith; may be unrelated to the cellar remains.

### Ch. 8 — The Locked Book (`ebr-ch08`)
Full publication for the first time of the Planchette and table-tipping séance records from the [[locked-book|Locked Book]] (360 pages, Bramah-locked, deposited at University of London January 1940). Key séances:
- **October 23–25, 1937 (Borley)**: Table-tipping with Glanvilles and Kerr-Pearse. Entities request "Mass, light, chant." Séance of October 24 at kitchen: asks for Mass "in the kitchen." Séance of October 25 Base Room: "Fadenoch" (Father Enoch?) named as a monk buried in the garden. "Caldibec" (identified later as Marie Lairre's pet name) communicates as wife of a previous occupant.
- **Sunex Amures (March 27, 1938)**: Full text of the fire threat: "mean to burn the Rectory to-night at nine o'clock… under the ruins you will find bone of murdered… Under the ruins mean you to have proof." Price acknowledges he did not take this seriously until after the fire, and did not connect the "bone under ruins" injunction until the excavation.
- **Helen Glanville's solo séance (October 28, 1937 Streatham)**: "Well… well in the cellar… well that has been filled in." "Lairre." Confirmed: May 17, 1667. Date of death. 19 years old. "Water" — last thing she remembered wanting. Nunnery at "Haiv" (Havre).
- **October 31, 1937 (Streatham)**: Full group séance. "Mary Lairre." Strangled by "Waldeggrave." French. Havre. Buried "at the end of the wall." "Katie Boreham" named (and confirmed in register as "Kate Boreham of Sudbury died Easter Day, 1888, aged 31").

### Ch. 9 — The Cambridge Commission (`ebr-ch09`)
Report by A.J.B. Robertson (St John's College, Chemistry Honours). Commission active 1939–1944, 25 visits, 58 persons (mostly undergraduates). All were sceptical on arrival.

Key findings:
- **Auditory phenomena** well established: footsteps, knockings, rumblings, horse-stamping on road, whistling (4 whistles, Visit 19), clock ticking (Visit 6 — heard simultaneously by observer's mother in Ipswich). Most footsteps occurred when observers were static in the sewing-room, separated from sound by one wall.
- **Luminous patches** on walls observed (Visits 2 and 4) — confirmed by another Oxford observer (Welles, 1938) who saw a luminous ceiling patch. Cannot be reproduced normally.
- **Cold spot temperature drop**: Visit 14, June 19, 1943 — 11°F drop at the cold spot using enclosed vs. exposed thermometers. Investigators calculated mathematically whether removal of heat at 0.006 cal/cm³/sec could explain the reading; answer: possibly. Cold spot exactly above cellar subsidence.
- **Rappings responding to challenges**: Visits 3, 4, 10 — knocks occurred 5–10 minutes after requests. Visit 25: raps answered a 3-knock code (one knock for yes) for ~45 minutes, giving intelligent answers (nun died c.1250, wanted a minister not Presbyterian, was glad the buildings were destroyed).
- Visit 25 also featured the failed "joker" visit where a hoax apparition was staged — yet after the jokers left, genuine raps began and answered questions for ~45 minutes.
- Robertson's conclusion: "There appears to be something at the Rectory which cannot be at all easily explained away." Phenomena weakening over time.

### Ch. 10 — Suggested Causation (`ebr-ch10`)
[[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]]'s long essay (letter dated January 8, 1941; expanded). His analysis:
1. The "apports" (French medallion, Revolutionary badge, strange coat) are indicators, not literal objects — they point to: French, young, Roman Catholic woman.
2. The coach is incompatible with a pre-Reformation date, but consistent with 17th–18th century.
3. Planchette-derived "Mary Lairre" story is coherent: a young French novice from Havre; lodged briefly at a Bures establishment; taken by a young Waldegrave; murdered at Borley.
4. Key wall-writing discovery: **"trompée"** — the French word for "deceived" — is clearly legible in Plate IX. This is the best-written word in all the messages, by someone using their own language. Canon Phythian-Adams was the first to read it.
5. **"Well-Tank-Bottom-Me"**: his reading of the principal Marianne message, previously rendered as gibberish by Price. The "tank" referred to the shallow stone trough at the foot of the cellar stairs — where the remains were eventually found.
6. The "touchwood apport" psychometrized by "Marion" (Josef Kraus) produced impressions of the Waldegrave family and a relic brought from a small island (possibly Glastonbury). The "tall dark man" who died "a lonely man" matches Sir Edward Waldegrave, who died in the Tower in 1561.
7. Causal reconstruction: young Waldegrave meets French novice on travels; brings her to the Borley Rectory site (probably the chaplain's house, with cellar); murders her there; fills in the well to conceal the remains.
8. Poltergeists: Phythian-Adams views them as "distressed human entities" not external forces; exorcism failed because what was needed was not casting out an evil spirit but "the quieting of a miserable mind."

### Ch. 11 — Deciphering the Marianne Appeals (`ebr-ch11`)
Price collects interpretations of the wall-writings from correspondents. Rev. Heaton: writing done in darkness or without looking (letters in one continuous line). Prof. Stevenson: "R.B." could be "Roman Breviary"; the "odd marks" may be an upside-down word "light"; the markings appear only on the most frequented side of the house (most dangerous to a normal writer). Miss Doyle: confirmed Phythian-Adams's "Well-Tank-Bottom-Me" reading independently. Canon Stote-Blandy: the "odd marks" may read "Finis" followed by "Mass by self." Edward Smith: proposes "Sibil" as a signature and "Mas by Boy" as a postscript. Mr Beal: the bottom line may be Devanagari (Sanskrit) characters — unconfirmed. Price's analysis of the lead pencil: microscopically confirmed as graphite/plumbago; both hard and soft pencils used.

### Ch. 12 — The Waldegraves (`ebr-ch12`)
Historical background on the Waldegrave family:
- **Sir Edward Waldegrave (c.1517–1561)**: M.P. for Essex, Chancellor of Duchy of Lancaster; imprisoned in the Tower for "permitting the saying of a Mass at Borley"; died there September 1, 1561. The ornate 14-foot altar tomb in Borley Church (Sir Edward, his wife Frances, 3 sons, 3 daughters) is photographed for this volume.
- Waldegrave family held the Manor of Borley for c.300 years until Philip Waldegrave (died 1720–21); then passed to James, Lord Waldegrave.
- Borley Hall (on the Stour) was the actual manor seat; Borley Place (opposite the Rectory) was the manor house. No Waldegrave manor was where the Rectory stood — it was always a rectory site.
- **Arabella Waldegrave** (1687–?) theory (Mrs [[georgina-dawson|Georgina Dawson]]): daughter of Henry, first Baron Waldegrave, and Henrietta FitzJames (illegitimate daughter of James II). Went to France in 1688 as a one-year-old. At age seven sent to the Benedictine convent at Pontoise (an English house). She was "naughty" and left. According to Foley's Records, went to Paris and became a nun. Then vanishes completely from all family records — extraordinary given her royal blood. Mrs Dawson's theory: she became a spy and was murdered at Borley.

### Ch. 13 — Clues and 'Indicators' (`ebr-ch13`)
Price compiles all the evidence systematically: 
- Indicators that the entity was French: 'trompée'; French medallions; French dictionary fell in Jeffrey's locked room; wall messages show imperfect English construction
- Indicators of Roman Catholic faith: mass/prayers appeals; quiet on Sundays and Holy Days; incense smell; Dom Richard's special appeal
- Indicators that entity was a woman: appeals to Marianne and Helen Glanville; strange coat apport; two gold wedding-rings; sewing-room as focus; Mrs Mansbridge's belt lifted
- Planchette clues in chronological order (October 1937 – March 1938): consistently directing to the "cellar," "well," "bones," "under the floor," "by a wall"
- **"Henry Waldegrave confesses"** (séance November 20–21, 1937): "What did you do to the nun? Killed. How? Hands. Where? Crypt." The crypt = cellar.
- "Joseph Glanvill" entity (December 14, 1937): "Look under the brick floor in the cellar. Well. Borley." Writes single letters "P-e-n."
- "Henry" (December 19, 1937): "The heap of bones will be found from the garden and the pond... From whose body are the bones? Lairre." Instructs: "Go to Borley and look under the cellar floor for the cup and the missal and you will have proof."
- Note: Price did not receive or read most of these scripts until January 20, 1945 — 17 months after the excavation. Their confirmation of excavation findings thus has heightened value.

### Ch. 14 — Truth at the Bottom of a Well? (`ebr-ch14`)
The August 1943 excavation:

**Cellar description**: Three connected compartments below library, dining-room, hall, and kitchen passage. Partly conforms to foundations of an older building (ancient two-inch bricks). Cold spot on first-floor landing was exactly over a cellar subsidence. Four wells: (1) main courtyard well, 80 feet deep, tunnel discovered at 61.9 feet; (2) small round bricked well in cellar extension; (3) soft-water well; (4) the "well-tank" — a shallow stone/cement oblong trough, approximately 2'6" × 2' × 9", at the foot of the cellar stairs, seen by Price in 1929, covered with a rotten wooden hatch.

**The well-tank mystery**: When the excavation party arrived on August 17, 1943, the well-tank had completely disappeared — filled in and bricked over so perfectly that no mark showed. Probably done by someone after the fire, or after Gregson's occupation. Neither Gregson nor Henning could explain it.

**Round well excavation** (Day 1 morning): 6 feet deep; contained oyster-shells, potsherds, ancient two-inch bricks, broken glass, a brass preserving-pan, a broken brass candlestick, part of a small rusty iron coffer — and at 5'6", a silver-quality cream-jug (Sheffield plate?), 4½" high, no hallmarks, c.80 years old (c.1863). Probably lost when the Rectory was built.

**Below the cellar floor** (Day 1 afternoon): Phythian-Adams's "Well-Tank-Bottom-Me" directed excavation to the floor where the well-tank had been. Breaking through the hard semi-glazed bricks and into "blue vinney" (blue clay marl), at depth of 3 feet:
- **1:45 p.m.**: jaw-bone found — Dr Bailey immediately identified it as human; *left mandible with five teeth in situ*.
- **1:50 p.m.**: part of a skull — *parietal and temporal bone, left side*.

**Dental report** (Dr Eric Bailey, M.R.C.S., L.R.C.P.): jaw is that of a woman, probably young. Confirmed in writing same evening.

**Extended dental report** (dental surgeon, May 23, 1945, after X-ray): Jaw almost certainly a woman; age at death c.30, "more probably under than over." Diet contained "a good amount of roughage and probably some finely divided grit" (consistent with 1667 convent food). A large deep-seated bone abscess around the first molar, in close association with the mandibular nerve — could cause "anything from a persistent mild ache to raging toothache, persistent severe neuralgia, pain in and anterior to the ear, pain above the eye or down the side of the nose." This explains why all witnesses described the nun as looking sad, pale, in pain, as if she had been crying.

**Day 2** (August 18): Found the two pendants — a thin worn gold pendant with "Pax" and a figure, probably of the Virgin; and a copper plaque later identified as a "Miraculous Medal" (see Ch. 15).

**Further digging** (August 30): No further finds. Marl replaced.

**Diviner**: Mrs Collett, called in previously to find the missing church plate, had her hazel twig snap "exactly over the area where the human remains and other objects were subsequently found." Price did not know of this until after the excavation.

### Ch. 15 — The Miraculous Medal (`ebr-ch15`)
The copper pendant is identified as a *Miraculous Medal*, issued after the apparitions of Catherine Labouré (1830) to the Virgin Mary in the Chapel of the Rue du Bac, Paris. The Virgin instructed the striking of a medal with the legend "O Mary, conceived without sin, pray for us who have recourse to Thee." Catherine Labouré (Zoë Labouré, 1806–1876) was beatified in 1933; her body was found incorrupt. Price's specimen: obverse shows the Virgin crushing the serpent; reverse shows "M" with two hearts and 12 stars. Pronounced c.60 years old (c.1883) by Messrs Maurice Vanpoulle. The small gold pendant is also French, unknown in England, much older, worn round the neck.

Price's theory: the pendants were lost by the Italian or French workman "Mercur" who hung the bells in 1863 (inscription on a rafter: "Bells hung by S. Cracknell and Mercur, 1863"). "Mercur" may be a French corruption of the noble name Mercœur (Philippe-Emmanuel de Lorraine, Duc de Mercœur). The pendants have no direct connexion to Marie Lairre — they are pointers or indicators only.

### Ch. 16 — New Light on the Borley Haunt (`ebr-ch16`)
Phythian-Adams's second essay, written after learning of the excavation results.

**Scene of the murder**: Remains found under cellar floor → a cellar existed at this site in the 17th century → there was a building here (the chaplain's house / rectory) before Henry Bull built his Rectory in 1863. The Waldegraves had no manor house on this site — their residences were Borley Hall and Borley Place. Borley Rectory was always a rectory. But from 1662, when Rev. William Playne moved to Langenhoo, the rectory was effectively empty or absentee-occupied for perhaps 50 years. The crime could have been committed in an empty rectory, with no one knowing, and the killer could ensure it remained vacant.

**Arabella Waldegrave dismissed**: Arabella (born 1687) was educated at the English Benedictine house at Pontoise — an English house transplanted to France. She spoke English. The entity at Borley writes broken English and in French ('trompée'). Arabella cannot be the Borley nun.

**Return to Marie Lairre**: The date 1667 falls perfectly — 5 years after the rector left for Langenhoo. The rectory was empty. A "Borley Waldegrave" (descendant of Nicholas, died 1621) — not the senior Chewton branch — is the most probable perpetrator.

### Ch. 17 — Strange Occurrences in a London Studio (`ebr-ch17`)
September 13, 1943: Price took the Borley relics (skull fragment, pendants, other objects) to A.C. Cooper Ltd., photographers to the Royal Family, for photography. During the afternoon:
- The skull fragment slipped from both men's hands and broke into four pieces.
- Two oil paintings fell from their easels for no reason — unprecedented in Cooper's 25-year career.
- Cooper ruined two batches of films by forgetting to set the lens stop — also unprecedented.
- A dark-room clock that had not worked for 10 years began ticking; ran for 20 minutes; stopped permanently.

**Ian Aitchison's psychometric impressions** (July 1944, at Glanville's home):
- French Revolution medal: "old man bent" looking at "a large building with castellated wall... the last thing he ever saw."
- Nail-file "apport": "I was pushed in… I was murdered." (drowned)
- Miraculous Medal: "a young girl... something tight round my waist — very tight."
- Confirmation medal: "troubled, frightened feeling."
- Gold pendant: "I want to get rid of it!" ... "a girl, arrogantly defiant... lying on boards and someone is on top of me... I pull it off and throw it away. Very trying time!"
- Jaw-bone: "Florence. Happiness!... it wasn't strangulation, but she was right when she said strangulation: it was rape. Waldegrave did it." She fought; broke her vow; tore off the medal from shame; "she thinks she is French and seems to remember Queen Anne" (aged 2 in 1667 — consistent).
- Gold wedding-ring: dropped it immediately, shouted "Murder!" refused to touch it again.

### Ch. 19 — The End of the Rectory; the End of the Nun? (`ebr-ch19`)
By June 1945 the Rectory was fully demolished — "not one brick stands upon another, and the site is as clean as a swept floor." The ornate Italian marble mantelpieces were found smashed next day after careful removal (attributed to souvenir hunters). The great yard-bell hangs in Price's Pulborough garden. The site was sold for £560 to a local man; the Gooch family bought part of the orchard/garden for £300 and planned to build a home there. The Rectory cottage (four rooms over the stables) survives.

**The last photograph** (April 5, 1944): David Scherman of *Life* magazine photographed the ruins. As he pressed the shutter, a brick shot up about 4 feet into the air in the kitchen passage area — seen by Price, Scherman, and Miss Cynthia Ledsham. Scherman's photograph (10" × 8" enlargement) shows the rectangular brick clearly suspended in mid-air. Price calls it "the first photograph ever taken of a Poltergeist projectile in flight." During the demolition of trees in the grounds: "Three axes broke; one man received a shoulder injury; two trees fell into the road instead of the grounds, the ropes breaking."

**Requiem Mass for Marie Lairre**: October 29–30, 1943, Rev. Father John Wright, S. Philip Neri, Arundel. Another Mass at Oxford. Phenomena began to weaken thereafter.

**Burial at Liston Churchyard** (May 29, 1945, 5:42 p.m. BDST): The skull fragment, mandible, gold pendant, Miraculous Medal, and other objects placed in a dovetailed cedar-wood casket with a brass identification plate and Rev. Henning's visiting card. Rev. Henning recited prayers from *The Missal* (including Gelasian Sacramentary prayer). The sexton filled the grave. Present: Rev. and Mrs Henning, Eric Calcraft (photographer), sexton, and Price.

Price: "I believe that both queries [will the nun phenomena cease? is her spirit at rest?] can be answered in the affirmative."

### Ch. 20 — Borley and Ballechin (`ebr-ch20`)
Detailed comparison with Ballechin House, Perthshire (investigated 1897). Thirteen parallel correspondences including: modern house on older site; isolated location; strong Roman Catholic associations; investigation by a priest led to a psychical researcher; researcher rented the house; nun as central figure; poltergeist phenomena identical in type; nun seen weeping in grounds only (never inside); dog reaction; "bed phenomena"; apparition of former owner who said he would "return"; exorcism failures. Price concludes Borley "outstandingly the best-evidenced" of the two.

### Ch. 21 — 'Miracles' and their Mechanics (`ebr-ch21`)
Theories for the causation of the phenomena:
- **Percy Pigott**: the coach, nun, and similar apparitions are "psychic cinema" — nature records intense emotional scenes in the ether, replaying them under favourable conditions (like a photographic negative). Poltergeist phenomena require a different explanation — an "elemental force" charged by the victim's distress.
- **A.J.B. Robertson** (physical view): Poltergeists may draw energy from (1) adolescent children as mediums; (2) cooling of air (thermodynamic anomaly at Cold Spot); (3) Poltergeist objects typically lose *potential* energy in fall — energy possibly transferred to entity.
- **Canon Phythian-Adams**: All Poltergeist phenomena are of human origin; exorcism fails when what is needed is not casting out an evil spirit but "the quieting of a miserable mind."
- **Rev. Maitland**: The house was built by a psychic (Henry Bull) who "saturated" it with psychic power over 65 years; living occupants unconsciously draw on that power to create manifestations.
- **Mr Harrison** (contra-Maitland): The apparitions are of persons formerly at the place, often outside the knowledge of living witnesses — cannot be projections of current occupants' minds.
- **Dr Joad**: The "psychic factor" persists after death and may combine temporarily with matter to produce physical phenomena.
- **Captain Deane**: phenomena consistent with a "demented female"; the Marks Tey Circle "laid" the ghost for two years by treating it as such, suggesting the assumption was correct.
- **Sidney Paris** (Catholic view): nun is a disembodied soul requesting Requiem Mass; poltergeists may be souls from the original monastery.

### Ch. 22 — Only One Conclusion (`ebr-ch22`)
**Sir Ernest Jelf** (Senior Master of the Supreme Court, King's Remembrancer 1937–43): legal article in the *Law Times* (August 9, 1941). Verdict: the proofs "do present a very strong case indeed — stronger than most of us could ever have believed possible." More than a hundred witnesses of position and intellectual attainment; many brought in from outside as independent persons. The dogs' reaction especially difficult to explain by suggestion. "What would a jury say? They would probably disagree."

**Sir Albion Richardson K.C., C.B.E.** (Recorder of Nottingham, former M.P.): "The evidence which he has collected is as conclusive as human testimony can ever be… to the point of moral certainty."

**Price's conclusion**: The Borley phenomena occurred; were of paranormal origin; have been scientifically proved. "Fraud, malobservation, exaggeration, natural causes, and trickery — conscious, unconscious, or subconscious — could not have accounted for the phenomena."

**The Four Nun Claimants**:
1. **Marie Lairre** — Planchette name, French novice, strangled 1667 by Waldegrave; supported by wall-writings, pendants, and excavation evidence
2. **Arabella Waldegrave** — Mrs Dawson's theory; dismissed by Phythian-Adams (educated by English nuns, could not have written broken English)
3. **Evangeline Westcott** — name given by Mr Warren (medium) at Marks Tey Circle séance April 26, 1932; not corroborated in any records; Captain Deane "pigeon-holes" it
4. **The summer-house nun** — entity communicating at Visit 25 (Cambridge Commission, July 22–23, 1944); 1250 date claimed, but no name given; probably Marie Lairre herself

**Final note**: Progress in academic recognition of psychical research 1925–1945 (Price's National Laboratory; University of London library; Trinity Cambridge Perrott Bequest; New College Oxford Blennerhasset Trust; German Government offer; D.Sc., M.D., D.Phil. degrees awarded for psychical research theses). Price: "I do believe that in fifty years' time we shall know a great deal more about these matters."
