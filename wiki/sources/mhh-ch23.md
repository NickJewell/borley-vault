## Summary

Price's final day at Borley Rectory as tenant (May 9, 1938) and the discovery of a gold wedding ring in the Blue Room after multiple thorough searches — one of the most puzzling single incidents in the record.

## Key Content

**The final day**: Price and Geoffrey Motion made repeated complete searches of the house throughout the day — morning, afternoon, early evening, and midnight. Their searches were so thorough that they found a solitary wooden matchstick on the Blue Room floor, ringed in its own chalk rectangle. After a dinner break and a return at midnight, Price had the paraffin lamp and Motion had a pocket torch.

**The gold ring**: As they entered the Blue Room at midnight, both men simultaneously saw a glint in the opening between the Blue Room and dressing-room. Motion picked it up: a lady's 22-carat gold wedding ring. Hallmarked Birmingham, 1864 — the year after the Rectory was built. Neither man could have failed to find it in six earlier searches, including one where they found a single matchstick.

**Price's inquiries**: He asked all observers whether they had lost a wedding ring (no); made local inquiries (nothing); noted that women visitors to the Rectory had been few and it was unlikely any would wear an antique ring. Found no owner.

**Connection to Foyster diary**: Price had not yet read the Foyster diary extracts when he found the ring. Reading them later, he found an entry for March 10, 1931: "a wedding ring was discovered in the bathroom" — it disappeared the following morning. Could it be the same ring? He concludes there is no way to know.

## Significance

The gold ring appeared in a room that had been searched six times in one day, the last search finding a matchstick. Price presents it as a classic "apport" phenomenon. Its hallmark date (1864 = year after the Rectory was built) makes a mundane explanation involving a visitor seem improbable. The possible connection to the Foyster 1931 ring discovery adds another layer. See [[blue-room]], [[poltergeist-activity]].
