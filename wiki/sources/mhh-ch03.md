# The Most Haunted House in England — Ch. 3: The Rector's Story

*Harry Price (1940)*

Price arrives at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] on Wednesday, 12 June 1929, with his secretary [[lucie-kaye|Miss Lucie Kaye]]. He describes his investigation kit, then gives a full account of the Smith-period phenomena as reported to him directly by the Smiths.

## The Ghost-Hunter's Kit

Price lists his standard equipment in extraordinary detail — establishing his empirical, instrumented approach:

- Soft felt overshoes for silent movement through the house
- Steel measuring tape; screw-eyes, post-office seals, cord and surgical tape for sealing doors, windows, and cupboards
- Tools, wire, nails; electric bells, dry batteries and switches for hidden contact circuits
- 9×12cm reflex camera, film-packs, flash-bulbs; portable telephone for cross-building communication
- Notebook, drawing instruments, sketching block for plans
- Bandages, iodine, and a flask of brandy
- Bowl of mercury for detecting tremors and for making silent mercury switches
- Cinematograph camera with remote electrical control
- Sensitive transmitting thermograph for recording temperature variations
- Graphite and brush for fingerprints
- For long stays with electricity: infra-red lamps and cine films sensitive to infra-red

This list is directly relevant to the [[harry-price-controversy|Harry Price controversy]]: it is evidence of a systematic, sceptic-friendly methodology, the opposite of the credulous showman portrait in the 1956 SPR report.

## Arrival and First Impressions

Price found the Smiths "very intelligent, delightful, and much-travelled people who were, like myself, utterly sceptical as regards 'spirits.'" They knew nothing of psychical research; the subject did not particularly interest them. The living had been offered to and refused by twelve clergy before Smith accepted it.

What ultimately drove the Smiths from the Rectory: "the lack of amenities there and not the 'ghosts.'"

## The Smith-Period Phenomena

All related to Price directly by the Smiths:

**Auditory:**
- Sibilant whisperings heard by Smith on the landing, following him, ceasing abruptly at a second archway "as though a wireless set had been switched off"
- A woman's voice from the arch: *"Don't, Carlos, don't!"* — rising crescendo, dying to a mutter. "Carlos" said to be Henry Bull's nickname; denied by the Bull family
- Mysterious bell-ringing
- Footsteps patrolling passages and upper rooms (more frequent in winter than summer)

**Visual:**
- Mrs Smith: schoolroom window lit up on two separate occasions; maid not present; room dark on investigation. Second occurrence witnessed by the entire choir after their evening practice
- Figure leaning over a drive gate — dark, shadowy; seen by Mrs Smith and multiple others; always disappeared suddenly
- Maid saw the coach on the lawn
- London maid saw a figure dressed in black at the gate; left after two days

**Physical (poltergeist):**
- Keys "shooting" from their locks — found two or three feet away; happened periodically; **Price personally witnessed this phenomenon**
- Vase from bedroom mantelpiece found smashed at the foot of the main stairs; Smiths were in the garden and no one was in the house

**The skull:**
Early in the Smith incumbency, before Price's arrival, Mrs Smith found in a library cupboard a parcel neatly wrapped and tied with string. Inside: "the skull of, apparently, a young woman. It was in perfect condition, and the teeth were also perfect." Eric Smith and the sexton buried it in the churchyard in the presence of the churchwarden and the widow of the previous rector.

> **Note:** This skull is distinct from the human bones found during excavations in 1943 (see [[marie-lairre]]). Its provenance — a young woman, in perfect condition, stored in a library cupboard — is unexplained. No mention of it appears in the SPR's 1956 report or in Underwood and Tabori's *Ghosts of Borley*.
