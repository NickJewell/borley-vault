# Sidelights on Borley Rectory — Introduction

**Source:** *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, Essay 1 (Introduction)
**Author:** Anonymous (East Anglian local historian, resident near Borley)
**Format:** Web-published essay collection; self-contained chapters

## Overview

The author frames this collection as thematic essays, not a comprehensive encyclopedia. Each essay explores "a particular thought or theme," allowing participants to "speak in their own voices" rather than forcing the evidence to fit a predetermined conclusion. The author regards prior Borley writing as marked by agendas and projection: "The mass of evidence of the haunting is like a Rorschach inkblot test, on which the viewer projects his own inner life."

## Key Claims

The author argues that the Borley affair is valuable primarily as social history — a window into interwar Britain — rather than as evidence for the paranormal. The principal figures are sketched as historical types: [[marianne-foyster]] as "an extraordinarily modern woman; sensual, intelligent and feisty"; [[harry-price]] as having "mesmeric manner and compelling journalistic style"; [[lionel-foyster]] as "the loveable, dignified, but ineffectual English Gent"; [[dodie-bull]] as "Ethel Bull weaving a fantastic web of fable"; [[francois-darles|Frank Pearless]] as "the sinister sexual predator."

## Methodological Notes

- The author makes extensive use of primary documents in the Harry Price Library (HPL).
- Much of the Swanson interview material was made available by [[vincent-oneil|Vince O'Neil]].
- The author regrets lost evidence: Mabel Smith's "Murder at the Parsonage" manuscript; parts of the Swanson tapes that were accidentally destroyed; Lionel Foyster's family letters.
- The author argues against obsessive focus on whether Harry Price was a fraud, calling him "too complex a person to have a simple label such as 'fraud' stuck on him."

## Source Assessment

A sympathetic, thoughtful skeptic with deep local knowledge and access to primary documents. More charitable to all parties than the SPR 1956 report. Does not argue for paranormal explanations but does not dismiss all testimony.
