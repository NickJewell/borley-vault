# Sidelights — 17: Tunnels!

**Source:** *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*, Essay 17
**Author:** Andrew Clarke

## Summary

Debunks the tunnel legends at Borley, identifying the "1957 tunnel" as a storm-water culvert between Borley Place and the farm (entirely unconnected to the Rectory), tracing the Borley–Bures tunnel legend to Monks Hall, Glemsford, and cataloguing Essex tunnel legends generally.

## The 1957 "Tunnel"

In September 1957, workmen found a brick-arched tunnel under Hall Road between Borley Place and the farm. Reported breathlessly in local press and two Borley books. Clarke: **it entirely misses Borley Rectory and the church.** It is a Tudor-brick storm-water culvert, running north–south between Borley Place cellars and the farm. Its summer 1987 flooding confirmed its drainage function.

## The Rectory Grounds Tunnel

A different, genuine underground structure existed in the Rectory grounds itself:
- Rev Smith "stumbled on a caved-in portion" and had it filled in.
- In 1933, Ian Shaw discovered a tunnel running "under the road leading into the field by the churchyard" — and traced its entrance to the cellar. Marianne forbade him to enter it; it subsequently caved in.
- Clarke's assessment: this was almost certainly the **foul-water drainage system of the earlier Herringham Rectory**, running under the kitchen wing and courtyard.

## The Tunnel-to-Bures Legend

The legend of a tunnel connecting Borley to Bures (7 miles away) is traced to **Monks Hall, Glemsford** (across the Stour Valley from Borley), where similar tunnel legends were actively taught in local schools:
> *"a ghostly figure of a monk walks the paths near his ancient haunts... a secret tunnel linking [Monks Hall] with the Church still exists."*

Clarke: *"This story seems to have been lifted by the Bull girls wholesale from the next parish."*

## General Context

Clarke catalogues numerous Essex tunnel legends (Coggeshall Abbey to Colchester, Birchanger to Thremhall Priory, etc.) and notes that genuine tunnels, when found, invariably turn out to be storm drains, foul drains, cellar connectors for barrel-rolling, or ice-house accesses. Clarke's own house has a beautiful brick culvert that served as an outdoor privy — *"a work of art"* built for entirely mundane purposes.
