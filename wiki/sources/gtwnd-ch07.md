# Source: *Ghosts That Will Not Die* — Ch. 7: Marianne Reveals More Borley Secrets

**Author:** Vincent O'Neil

The most analytically important chapter of the book. Reproduces large sections of the Owen/Mitchell 1979 report *Marianne's Story — The Alleged Haunting of Borley Rectory*, published in the *Journal of the British Society for Psychical Research* (September 1979). Also includes Iris Owen's personal account of how the interviews were arranged and her assessment of Marianne's character.

## How the Interviews Were Arranged

**Pauline Mitchell**, a book reviewer in Hamilton, Canada, tracked down Marianne in La Crosse, Wisconsin, in 1977 after reading a Borley book. She enlisted **Iris Owen** (parapsychology researcher, New Horizons Foundation, Toronto) to help in January 1977.

"Marianne was shocked that she had been traced." Conditions: nothing published until after her death. The report was written in 1986 and circulated privately. Marianne died December 18, 1992.

Iris Owen's assessment of Marianne: "I completely believe that her version of what happened is the correct one." She described Marianne as "a delightful person — witty, charming... clever — but someone to whom life had dealt a hard hand."

Owen speculated: "I believe Harry Price cheated him [Lionel] out of his manuscript — the original one — which he was actually writing in the hope of producing a bestseller which would solve their financial problems."

## The Owen/Mitchell Report: Key Findings

### Marianne's Background

Born in Antrim, Ireland. Went to Canada in 1922 to marry Lionel. The family spent time in Salmonhurst NB and Sackville NS before returning to England.

Ian (Marianne's son by an earlier relationship, "passed off as her brother"): Owen notes he "told Trevor Hall he had visited Borley at times, and on occasion had been outside and pulled the bells 'for a lark' and with Marianne's and Lionel's full knowledge."

### The Dominant Factor: Lionel's Dying Sentence

The overriding context was not the haunting but **Lionel's terminal prognosis**. He had returned from Canada knowing his illness would worsen progressively — he died 15 years after returning, following 10 years of wheelchair/bed confinement. Marianne was 21 years younger than Lionel and 46 at his death.

"Lionel Foyster was well aware that Walter Hubbell had made a great deal of money from the book he had written about the Amherst poltergeist, and he conceived the idea of doing the same thing by writing a book about Borley. This would solve his financial problems."

**The three Foyster manuscripts were the bones of a fictional best-seller.** Everyone in the household knew of this. Marianne believes Price also knew the accounts were fictional but allowed himself to believe them genuine because it suited his purpose.

### The Three-Strand Theory

Owen/Mitchell identify three strands in the Borley phenomena during the Foyster period:
1. **Genuine poltergeist events** — footsteps when there was no cause; objects moving on their own
2. **Deliberate trickery** — Lionel throwing objects to observe guests' reactions; children's pranks; the fake ghost for d'Arles
3. **Misrepresentation** — events exaggerated or invented for the fictional manuscript

### The Phenomena Cease

The poltergeist phenomena finally ceased after Marianne pleaded that the house be "cleaned" by religious service. This coincided with:
- Lionel becoming permanently confined to his wheelchair (no longer able to throw things undetected)
- Edwin Whitehouse deciding to become a Benedictine Monk and leaving the village

Owen/Mitchell raise the question: "Were the phenomena Edwin's genuine poltergeist activity, or was he Lionel's confederate?"

### The Wall Writings

Marianne denies initiating the wall writings; attributes them to Edwin Whitehouse, "either deliberately or unconsciously." But admits she, Lionel, and Ian all "answered" the writings by writing underneath them.

"The wall writings were an example of phenomena that puzzled them. These would initially appear, apparently from nowhere." She "was mad when they first appeared as the wall had been recently redecorated."

### The Bottle, Tumbler, and Stiletto

"Marianne herself confirms that the events of the bottle, tumbler and stiletto happened as described in the SPR report." She cannot explain them and attributes them to Edwin Whitehouse as "the focus of such genuine poltergeist activity as occurred."

### Their Happiness at Borley

Far from being driven away by fear, "they were happy there." Marianne loved the garden and grounds. They left solely because Lionel's health made it impossible for him to continue his ministry. "Anyone who had lived under the circumstances of their early days in Canada would regard living at Borley as a luxurious way of life."
