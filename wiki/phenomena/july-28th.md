# July 28th

Traditionally the day each year on which the [[the-nun|phantom nun]] is said to return to haunt Borley. Its origin lies in the famous sighting by four of the Bull sisters on 28 July 1900.

## The 1900 Sighting

[[ethel-bull]] and three of her sisters — Freda, Mabel and Elsie — were returning from an afternoon garden party in Sudbury and entered the [[rectory-grounds|Rectory grounds]] by the eastern gate at the end of the garden. As they proceeded up through the greenery they saw a woman in the garb of a nun standing on the lawn, apparently telling her beads with head bowed.

Initially the sisters thought the figure was their mother, Caroline Bull, who was then in mourning. One girl ran to fetch Elsie. According to notes taken by [[lucie-kaye]] at the time of her first visit to Borley in June 1929, Elsie said "A ghost! What nonsense, I'll go and speak to it" and ran into the garden, putting one foot on the lawn — before suddenly growing terrified. She turned and ran back to her sisters standing beside the large [[summer-houses|summer-house]]. The figure had apparently turned its face toward her and almost immediately afterwards vanished.

## Pre-1900 Tradition

[[henry-bull]], who died in 1892, was allegedly well aware of the date before the 1900 sighting. The tradition of the July 28th anniversary may have predated the formal sighting — possibly originating in the earlier encounters reported by Henry and Harry Bull during the building's first decades.

## Price Tenancy Watch

[[harry-price]] and others visited the Rectory on the July 28th anniversary to watch for the figure. During the tenancy, 28 July 1937 was one of the planned observation days — on that occasion [[cem-joad|Dr C.E.M. Joad]] visited and witnessed the soap-throwing incident in the [[blue-room]].

## Post-Demolition Significance

Following the demolition of the Rectory, 28 July continued to attract attention. The [[polish-officers]] visited on 28 July 1943 and reported phenomena. Through to the present day, newspapers have reported as many as forty people camping out in Borley churchyard during the night on or around July 28th.

[[ronald-russell|Ronald R. Russell]] of the [[enfield-group|Enfield Parapsychical Research Group]] felt that the power concentrated in Borley Church was boosted by the presence of observers and reached "full flood" in August — close to this date. The [[enfield-group]] experienced the most intense activity in the month of August.

## Significance

July 28th is one of several elements of the Borley case — along with the [[the-nun|phantom nun]] herself — that has passed into genuine local folklore. The date's association with the most famous daylight sighting by the four Bull sisters (whose combined testimony spanned four independent viewpoints) has given it authority that later sceptical analysis has not entirely dissolved.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

In the Borley Rectory sources, **July 28th** is traditionally renowned as the specific "anniversary" date on which the famous phantom nun is said to walk the Rectory grounds [1-3]. The date has subsequently acted as a magnet for generations of psychic researchers, sightseers, and thrill-seekers [2, 4]. 

A thorough review of the Borley sources reveals the following recorded instances, witness descriptions, and proposed explanations associated with July 28th across the decades:

### **1. July 28, 1900: The Foundational Sighting of the Nun**
*   **The Incident & Witnesses:** The most famous event associated with this date occurred when four daughters of the Rev. Henry D. E. Bull—Ethel, Freda, Mabel, and Elsie (some sources name the fourth sister as Caroline or "Dodie")—collectively saw an apparition while returning from a garden party [5-9].
*   **Witness Description:** The sisters reported seeing a woman dressed in the black flowing garb of a nun, with a bowed head and her hands clasped as if telling her beads [5, 8, 9]. She appeared to be gliding along a path that became known as the "Nun's Walk" [5, 8]. When one of the sisters bravely approached her, the figure turned its face toward them, revealing an expression of "intense grief," and vanished [9, 10]. 
*   **Source Perspectives & Explanations:**
    *   **Believers:** Harry Price championed this incident as one of the best proofs of the haunting, frequently claiming it was a simultaneous, collective sighting that occurred in "sunlight" or broad daylight [3, 11-13]. The Rev. H.D.E. Bull was reportedly unphased by the sighting, claiming he already knew July 28th was the "nun's day" [14].
    *   **Skeptics:** The Society for Psychical Research (SPR) authors (Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) and later researchers like Robert Wood severely criticized Price's account. They pointed out that sunset on July 28, 1900, was at 7:53 p.m., and the sighting happened in "late twilight" around 9:00 p.m. [15]. Consequently, skeptics argue that in the fading light, the sisters merely misidentified a local villager or a poacher using the Rectory garden as a shortcut, who naturally hurried away to avoid the rector's family [16]. 

### **2. July 28, 1929: A Quiet Anniversary**
*   **The Incident & Witnesses:** During the intense early phase of Harry Price's involvement, a group of his friends, including the Hon. Richard Bethell and Lord Charles Hope, visited the Rectory to watch for the nun [17, 18].
*   **Description & Perspective:** Price (who was absent due to illness) recorded that absolutely "nothing of importance happened" and the place seemed particularly peaceful [17-20].

### **3. July 28, 1937: The Price Tenancy**
*   **The Incident & Witnesses:** During Price's year-long observer tenancy, a group including Dr. C. E. M. Joad, Mr. A. C. Elliot Smith (a Harrow schoolmaster), the Rev. Alfred Henning, and Mark Kerr-Pearse waited in the summer-house until 10:40 p.m. hoping to see the nun [21, 22]. 
*   **Description:** The nun failed to appear [22, 23]. However, other phenomena were recorded: Dr. Joad was struck on the cheek by a piece of soap, and he witnessed the spontaneous appearance of new pencil markings on the wall [21, 24, 25]. 

### **4. July 28–29, 1943: The Polish Officers**
*   **The Incident & Witnesses:** While the Rectory was a burnt-out ruin, two Polish Army Medical Corps officers, Lt. G. B. Nawrocki and Lt. Kujawa, accompanied by two English boys, held a night vigil [26-28].
*   **Description:** They reported throwing stones, thumping noises, and visual phenomena. Lt. Nawrocki reported seeing a black shadow moving between the trees of the Nun's Walk, which vanished after five to ten seconds [29]. He also saw a black shape silhouetted on the wall of the ruined chapel [29].
*   **Source Perspectives & Explanations:**
    *   **Believers:** Price eagerly published their report, using the anniversary date to validate the continuity of the ghost nun and the general haunting [3, 30]. 
    *   **Skeptics:** The SPR authors heavily suspected the account was an "elaborate hoax" [31, 32]. They specifically noted the Polish officers claimed to have watched for the nun from the Blue Room window, an act that was physically impossible since the floor had been entirely consumed by the 1939 fire, leaving them nothing to stand on [33]. (Price’s defenders counter that the officers had "improvised" a temporary floor with debris and planks across the remaining joists) [28, 34].

### **5. July 28, 1947: The Water Bottle**
*   **The Incident & Witnesses:** The Rev. Alfred Henning and his son Stephen were arranging flower displays inside Borley Church [35]. 
*   **Description:** During their visit, a water bottle moved apparently by itself, demonstrating the alleged shift of phenomena from the ruined Rectory across the road to the Church [35]. 

### **6. July 28, 1949: The Scratching Phantom Cat**
*   **The Incident & Witnesses:** Catherine (Cathy) Turner, wife of author James Turner, who was living in the surviving Rectory Cottage [36, 37].
*   **Description:** She was awakened in the night by the distinct feeling of something scratching at the bedclothes [36, 37]. An immediate search by the Turners with a candle revealed no sign of any cat or animal in the sealed room [38]. This became linked to subsequent visual sightings of a strange, scraggy "phantom cat" at the property in August and October of that same year [36, 39, 40].

### **7. July 28, 1975: The "Old Amos" Photograph**
*   **The Incident & Witnesses:** A visitor named Ray Armes took a photograph of the former Rectory grounds near the Cottage [41, 42]. 
*   **Description & Explanation:** When developed, some observers claimed to see the figure of a "wizened old man" (often associated with the phantom of "Old Amos," a historical groundskeeper) outlined in the foliage above a gate [41, 43]. However, close examination by researchers proved the "apparition" was merely pareidolia—a mundane optical illusion caused by the unusual effects of sunlight striking the ordinary leaves and foliage [41]. 

### **8. July 28–30, 1984: Modern Church Activity**
*   **The Incident & Witnesses:** Philip Dickinson and Sean Lee (and later a multi-directional microphone setup) conducted vigils in Borley Church [44-46]. 
*   **Description:** On July 28, they experienced significant temperature drops and cold chills near the Waldegrave tomb, accompanied by surreptitious scraping, bumping, and banging noises [44, 45]. They also reported hearing "padding" footsteps walking quietly up the central aisle of the empty church [44]. The observers noted that some sounds could be attributed to the natural contraction of ancient building timbers, but remained certain that others were inexplicable [47].
