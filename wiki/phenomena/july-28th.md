# July 28th

Traditionally the day each year on which the [[the-nun|phantom nun]] is said to return to haunt Borley. Its origin lies in the famous sighting by four of the Bull sisters on 28 July 1900. The date has subsequently acted as a magnet for generations of psychical researchers, sightseers, and thrill-seekers [2, 4].

## 1900 — The Foundational Sighting

[[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]] and three of her sisters — Freda, Mabel, and Elsie (some sources name the fourth sister as Caroline or "Dodie" [5]) — were returning from an afternoon garden party in Sudbury and entered the Rectory grounds by the eastern gate. As they proceeded up through the greenery they saw a woman in the garb of a nun standing on the lawn, apparently telling her beads with head bowed.

Initially the sisters thought the figure was their mother, Caroline Bull, who was then in mourning. One girl ran to fetch Elsie. According to notes taken by [[lucie-kaye]] at the time of her first visit to Borley in June 1929, Elsie said "A ghost! What nonsense, I'll go and speak to it" and ran into the garden, putting one foot on the lawn — before suddenly growing terrified. She turned and ran back to her sisters standing beside the large [[summer-houses|summer-house]]. The figure appeared to glide along a path that became known as the "[[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]]" [5, 8], had its face turned toward her, revealed an expression of "intense grief" [9, 10], and almost immediately afterwards vanished.

[[harry-price]] championed this incident as one of the best proofs of the haunting, frequently claiming it was a simultaneous, collective sighting that occurred in "sunlight" or broad daylight [3, 11–13]. The Rev. H. D. E. Bull was reportedly unphased by the sighting, claiming he already knew July 28th was the "nun's day" [14].

> **Contradiction:** The SPR authors (Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) and later researchers pointed out that sunset on July 28, 1900, was at 7:53 p.m., and the sighting happened in "late twilight" around 9:00 p.m. [15]. In the fading light, they argue, the sisters merely misidentified a local villager or a poacher using the Rectory garden as a shortcut, who naturally hurried away to avoid the rector's family [16].

## Pre-1900 Tradition

[[henry-bull|Henry Bull]], who died in 1892, was allegedly well aware of the date before the 1900 sighting. The tradition of the July 28th anniversary may have predated the formal sighting — possibly originating in the earlier encounters reported by Henry and Harry Bull during the building's first decades.

## 1929 — A Quiet Anniversary

During the intense early phase of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s involvement, a group of his friends including the Hon. Richard Bethell and [[lord-charles-hope|Lord Charles Hope]] visited the Rectory to watch for the nun [17, 18]. Price (who was absent due to illness) recorded that absolutely "nothing of importance happened" and the place seemed particularly peaceful [17–20].

## 1937 — The Price Tenancy Watch

During Price's year-long observer tenancy, 28 July 1937 was one of the planned observation days. A group including [[cem-joad|Dr. C. E. M. Joad]], [[ac-elliot-smith|A. C. Elliot Smith]] (a Harrow schoolmaster), the Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|Alfred Henning]], and [[mark-kerr-pearse|Mark Kerr-Pearse]] waited in the summer-house until 10:40 p.m. The nun failed to appear [22, 23]. However, Dr. Joad was struck on the cheek by a piece of soap, and witnessed the spontaneous appearance of new pencil markings on the wall [21, 24, 25].

## 1943 — The Polish Officers

While the Rectory was a burnt-out ruin, two Polish Army Medical Corps officers, Lt. G. B. Nawrocki and Lt. Kujawa, accompanied by two English boys, held a night vigil on 28–29 July 1943 [26–28]. They reported throwing stones, thumping noises, and visual phenomena. Lt. Nawrocki reported seeing a black shadow moving between the trees of the [[nuns-walk|Nun's Walk]], which vanished after five to ten seconds. He also saw a black shape silhouetted on the wall of the ruined chapel [29].

Price eagerly published their report, using the anniversary date to validate the continuity of the ghost nun [3, 30]. The SPR authors heavily suspected the account was an "elaborate hoax" [31, 32], specifically noting the Polish officers claimed to have watched for the nun from the Blue Room window — an act physically impossible since the floor had been entirely consumed by the 1939 fire, leaving nothing to stand on [33]. (Price's defenders counter that the officers had "improvised" a temporary floor with debris and planks across the remaining joists [28, 34].)

## 1947 — The Water Bottle

The Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|Alfred Henning]] and his son Stephen were arranging flower displays inside Borley Church on 28 July 1947. During their visit, a water bottle moved apparently by itself, demonstrating the alleged shift of phenomena from the ruined Rectory across the road to the Church [35].

## 1949 — The Scratching Phantom Cat

Catherine Turner, wife of author [[james-turner|James Turner]], was awakened in the night of 28 July 1949 by the distinct feeling of something scratching at the bedclothes. An immediate search by the Turners with a candle revealed no sign of any cat or animal in the sealed room [36–38]. This became linked to subsequent visual sightings of a strange, scraggy "[[phantom-cat|phantom cat]]" at the property in August and October of that same year [36, 39, 40].

## 1975 — The "Old Amos" Photograph (disputed)

A visitor named Ray Armes took a photograph of the former Rectory grounds near the Cottage on 28 July 1975 [41, 42]. When developed, some observers claimed to see the figure of a "wizened old man" outlined in the foliage above a gate [41, 43]. Close examination by researchers proved the "apparition" was merely pareidolia — a mundane optical illusion caused by sunlight striking ordinary leaves and foliage [41].

## 1984 — Modern Church Activity

Philip Dickinson and Sean Lee conducted vigils in Borley Church on 28–30 July 1984, later setting up a multi-directional microphone arrangement [44–46]. On July 28, they experienced significant temperature drops and cold chills near the Waldegrave tomb, accompanied by surreptitious scraping, bumping, and banging noises [44, 45]. They also reported hearing "padding" footsteps walking quietly up the central aisle of the empty church [44]. The observers noted that some sounds could be attributed to the natural contraction of ancient building timbers, but remained certain that others were inexplicable [47].

## Post-Demolition Significance

Following the demolition of the Rectory, 28 July continued to attract attention. Through to recent years, newspapers have reported as many as forty people camping out in Borley churchyard during the night on or around July 28th.

[[ronald-russell|Ronald R. Russell]] of an Enfield parapsychical research group felt that the power concentrated in Borley Church was boosted by the presence of observers and reached "full flood" in August — close to this date.

## Significance

July 28th is one of several elements of the Borley case — along with the [[the-nun|phantom nun]] herself — that has passed into genuine local folklore. The date's association with the most famous daylight sighting by the four Bull sisters (whose combined testimony spanned four independent viewpoints) has given it authority that later sceptical analysis has not entirely dissolved.
