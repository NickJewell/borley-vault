# Bottles

Several instances of spectacular poltergeist activity alleged to have taken place during the [[foyster-incumbency]] involved the throwing and breaking of glass wine bottles inside [[borley-rectory]]. The bottle phenomena forms one of the major linchpins of [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]]'s testimony of paranormal events he witnessed in 1931, and was championed by [[harry-price]] in both his Borley books. The period is widely considered the most violent phase of the haunting [1–3].

## Background

The [[henry-bull|Bull family]] maintained a well-stocked wine cellar, with several brick wine bins built into one of the basement rooms. After [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]]'s death in 1927, empty bottles remained scattered in the cellars throughout the subsequent incumbencies. When [[mabel-smith|Mrs. Mabel Smith]] moved into the Rectory in 1928, she discovered a locked cellar door which, when opened, revealed a room "piled high with empty wine bottles" [38, 39]. This vast, pre-existing supply of bottles in the cellar provided the perfect, mundane source for whoever — or whatever — was hurling them upstairs [37, 38].

> **Contradiction:** Decades later, [[marianne-foyster]] claimed: "There was no storage of bottles, and the mystery always to us was where the bottles came from because they were old bottles... we never did discover where the bottles came from" [36]. The Borley sources heavily contradict this claim.

## October 1931 — Price's Visit

During [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s visit of 13–14 October 1931, an empty claret bottle was suddenly hurled down the main staircase well, smashing to pieces on an iron stove in the hall [4–6]. Price and his colleagues noted that the bottle was thrown just after [[kathleen-goldney|Mrs. Goldney]] and the Rector had helped a supposedly unwell [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] up to her bedroom. Because Marianne was uncontrolled in the upper part of the house, **Price concluded she was fraudulently throwing the bottles herself** and openly accused her, causing a severe rift between him and the Foysters [8–10].

## 13 November 1931 — The Whitehouse Bottles

The most spectacular bottle incidents took place a month after Price's visit. [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] was at the Rectory at the request of [[lionel-foyster]] (who was away in London on church business), with Marianne and the maid Katie in the kitchen. In the course of the evening, four separate bottle incidents occurred (multiple witnesses) [11]:

1. **First bottle**: A glass wine bottle suddenly exploded into fragments on the stone floor under Marianne's chair [12, 13]
2. **Second bottle**: Fifteen minutes later, the same thing happened under Whitehouse's chair as he was about to sit at the table [12, 13]
3. **Third bottle** (most spectacular): "Before our very eyes, a bottle poised itself in mid-air within a foot or so of the kitchen ceiling. It remained here for a second or two and then fell with a crash on the floor before us. I repeat that all three of us witnessed this incident." All three — Whitehouse, Marianne, and the maid Katie — simultaneously saw the bottle appear suspended in the air [13, 14].
4. **Fourth bottle**: Another bottle was heard to roll down the back stairs and came through the partly open doorway, circled on the floor without breaking [13, 15]

Whitehouse was absolutely convinced of the paranormal origin of these events, insisting that the bottle hovering in mid-air could not have been manufactured by Mrs. Foyster [17, 18].

## Harry Price's Embellishment

When Price described the mid-air bottle in *EBR*, he added an elaboration not in Whitehouse's original account: "They all saw another bottle materialise in the air above them. First it was of mushroom shape, then its form changed to that of a bottle." [[trevor-hall]] in the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] seized on this "mushroom-to-bottle" transformation as evidence that Price had embellished the account to reinforce the paranormality of the incident [19–21]. Whitehouse told [[peter-underwood]] directly: "Price was wrong to embroider what I said about the bottle poising in mid-air with his own statement about changing from mushroom shape to that of a bottle! He invented that!"

## January 1932 — The Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle

When the [[marks-tey-spiritualist-circle|Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle]] arrived to "cleanse" the house, they were greeted by a barrage of bottles dashing down the back stairs and scattering glass across the kitchen passage [25, 26]. Observers claimed that two or three dozen bottles were thrown, and some "appeared to materialize in mid-air and crash to the floor" [25, 27, 28]. The spiritualists and believers pointed to the sheer volume of bottles and the presence of multiple witnesses in good lamplight as irrefutable proof of powerful poltergeist activity [32, 33]. Capt. V. M. Deane cross-examined the witnesses and declared there was "not the slightest shadow of doubt" about the authenticity of the materializations.

Skeptics noted the physical layout of the house: the back stairs off the kitchen passage were close to the cellar door. An accomplice could easily stand out of sight and heave bottles up the stairwell, one in each arm, causing them to smash together in mid-air to create the illusion of materialization [34, 35].

## Assessment

Whitehouse was adamant to the end of his life that the events occurred as he originally described, stating "I SHALL NEVER GO BACK ON WHAT I SAID ... re stiletto, bottles and tumbler incidents." His notes, made at the time, were used when preparing his testimony for Price in 1939 — eight years after the events. Lionel Foyster's *Summary of Experiences* also confirms that "a rather serious demonstration" took place on 13 November 1931 witnessed by Whitehouse and the maid.

The skeptical SPR investigators (Hall, Dingwall, and Goldney) proposed that Whitehouse was either lying, exaggerating a trivial incident, or that Marianne Foyster and an accomplice — such as her lodger [[francois-darles|François d'Arles]] — faked the entire episode through sleight of hand [22]. Marianne herself, decades later, claimed she was distracted frying pancakes, that the room was chaotic because of a thunderstorm, and that Whitehouse was in a "highly emotional state." She insisted she only saw the bottles crash and **never saw them materialize or start their flight** [23, 24].

Three possible non-paranormal explanations discussed by the BRC: (1) Whitehouse lied or exaggerated; (2) fakery by Marianne or the maid (the in-air bottle would require exceptional sleight of hand); (3) illness affecting Whitehouse's recollection eight years later. The BRC finds none entirely convincing and treats the bottle incidents as among the strongest pieces of testimony for genuine phenomena at Borley.
