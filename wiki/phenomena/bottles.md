# Bottles

Several instances of spectacular poltergeist activity alleged to have taken place during the [[foyster-incumbency]] involved the throwing and breaking of glass wine bottles inside [[borley-rectory]]. The bottle phenomena forms one of the major linchpins of [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]]'s testimony of paranormal events he witnessed in 1931, and was championed by [[harry-price]] in both his Borley books.

## Background

The [[bull-family]] maintained a well-stocked wine cellar, with several brick wine bins built into one of the basement rooms. After [[harry-bull]]'s death in 1927, empty bottles remained scattered in the cellars throughout the subsequent incumbencies.

## October 1931 — Price's Visit

During [[harry-price]]'s visit of 13–14 October 1931, a wine bottle was hurled down the main staircase and shattered on the hall floor. Price came to the conclusion at the time that this was fakery by [[marianne-foyster]] herself — she had been upstairs and uncontrolled.

## 13 November 1931 — The Whitehouse Bottles

The most spectacular bottle incidents took place a month after Price's visit. [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] was at the Rectory at the request of [[lionel-foyster]] (who was away in London on church business), with Marianne and the maid Katie in the kitchen. In the course of the evening, four separate bottle incidents occurred:

1. **First bottle**: A glass wine bottle suddenly exploded into fragments on the stone floor under Marianne's chair
2. **Second bottle**: Fifteen minutes later, the same thing happened under Whitehouse's chair as he was about to sit at the table
3. **Third bottle** (most spectacular): "Before our very eyes, a bottle poised itself in mid-air within a foot or so of the kitchen ceiling. It remained here for a second or two and then fell with a crash on the floor before us. I repeat that all three of us witnessed this incident." All three — Whitehouse, Marianne, and the maid Katie — simultaneously saw the bottle appear suspended in the air.
4. **Fourth bottle**: Another bottle was heard to roll down the back stairs and came through the partly open doorway, circled on the floor without breaking

## Harry Price's Embellishment

When Price described the mid-air bottle in *EBR*, he added an elaboration not in Whitehouse's original account: "They all saw another bottle materialise in the air above them. First it was of mushroom shape, then its form changed to that of a bottle." [[trevor-hall]] in the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] seized on this "mushroom-to-bottle" transformation as evidence that Price had embellished the account to reinforce the paranormality of the incident. Whitehouse told [[peter-underwood]] directly: "Price was wrong to embroider what I said about the bottle poising in mid-air with his own statement about changing from mushroom shape to that of a bottle! He invented that!"

## Assessment

Whitehouse was adamant to the end of his life that the events occurred as he originally described, stating "I SHALL NEVER GO BACK ON WHAT I SAID ... re stiletto, bottles and tumbler incidents." His notes, made at the time, were used when preparing his testimony for Price in 1939 — eight years after the events. Lionel Foyster's *Summary of Experiences* also confirms that "a rather serious demonstration" took place on 13 November 1931 witnessed by Whitehouse and the maid.

Three possible non-paranormal explanations discussed by the BRC: (1) Whitehouse lied or exaggerated; (2) fakery by Marianne or the maid (the in-air bottle would require exceptional sleight of hand); (3) illness affecting Whitehouse's recollection eight years later. The BRC finds none entirely convincing and treats the bottle incidents as among the strongest pieces of testimony for genuine phenomena at Borley.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**The throwing, smashing, and alleged materialisation of glass bottles** is one of the most spectacular and highly debated phenomena associated with Borley Rectory. These incidents took place predominantly during the incumbency of the Rev. Lionel Foyster and his wife, Marianne (1930–1935), a period widely considered the most violent phase of the haunting [1-3].

The sources record three major instances of bottle phenomena, witnessed by different individuals and provoking sharply divided explanations:

### **1. October 13–14, 1931 (Harry Price's Visit)**
*   **The Incident:** During a visit by ghost hunter Harry Price, an empty claret bottle was suddenly **hurled down the main staircase well**, smashing to pieces on an iron stove in the hall [4-6].
*   **Witnesses:** Harry Price, Kathleen Goldney, Mrs. Henry Richards, Miss May Walker, and the Rev. Lionel Foyster [6, 7].
*   **Source Perspectives and Explanations:** Price and his colleagues noted that the bottle was thrown just after Mrs. Goldney and the Rector had helped a supposedly unwell Marianne Foyster up to her bedroom [4, 6]. Because Marianne was uncontrolled in the upper part of the house, **Price concluded she was fraudulently throwing the bottles herself** and openly accused her, causing a severe rift between him and the Foysters [8-10].

### **2. November 13, 1931 (The Kitchen Incidents)**
*   **The Incidents:** While the Rev. Foyster was away in London, four distinct bottle phenomena occurred in the Rectory kitchen [11]. First, a wine bottle allegedly "appeared" and **exploded into fragments directly beneath Marianne Foyster's chair** [12, 13]. Fifteen minutes later, a second bottle **exploded beneath Edwin Whitehouse's chair** [12, 13]. The most dramatic event followed: a bottle suddenly **"poised itself in mid-air within a foot or so of the kitchen ceiling"** before crashing to the floor [13, 14]. Finally, another bottle was heard rolling down the back stairs and circled the kitchen floor without breaking [13, 15].
*   **Witnesses:** Edwin Whitehouse (later Dom Richard Whitehouse), Marianne Foyster, and the maid, Katie [11, 16].
*   **Source Perspectives and Explanations:**
    *   **Edwin Whitehouse** was absolutely convinced of the paranormal origin of these events, insisting that the bottle hovering in mid-air could not have been manufactured by Mrs. Foyster [17, 18]. 
    *   **Harry Price** used this testimony in his books but **embellished Whitehouse's account**, falsely adding that the bottle evolved from a "mushroom shape" in mid-air before materializing [19-21]. 
    *   **Skeptical SPR Investigators** (Trevor Hall, Eric Dingwall, Kathleen Goldney) seized upon Price's embellishment to discredit the account [20]. They proposed that Whitehouse was either lying, exaggerating a trivial incident, or that **Marianne Foyster and an accomplice (such as her lodger/lover François d'Arles) faked the entire episode** through sleight of hand [22]. 
    *   **Marianne Foyster's Later Admissions:** Decades later, Marianne downplayed the event. She claimed she was distracted frying pancakes, the room was chaotic because of a thunderstorm, and Edwin Whitehouse was in a "highly emotional state." She insisted she only saw the bottles crash and **never saw them materialize or start their flight** [23, 24].

### **3. January 23–24, 1932 (The Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle Visit)**
*   **The Incident:** A group of spiritualists arrived to "cleanse" the house. They were greeted by a barrage of bottles dashing down the back stairs and scattering glass across the kitchen passage [25, 26]. Observers claimed that **two or three dozen bottles were thrown**, and some **"appeared to materialize in mid-air and crash to the floor"** [25, 27, 28].
*   **Witnesses:** Guy L'Estrange (a local medium and Justice of the Peace), C. Warren, H.H. Frost, the Rev. Lionel Foyster, and Marianne Foyster [29-31].
*   **Source Perspectives and Explanations:** 
    *   **The Spiritualists and Believers** pointed to the sheer volume of bottles and the presence of multiple witnesses in good lamplight as irrefutable proof of powerful poltergeist activity [32, 33]. Capt. V.M. Deane cross-examined the witnesses and declared there was "not the slightest shadow of doubt" about the authenticity of the materializations [32, 33].
    *   **Skeptics** noted the physical layout of the house. The back stairs off the kitchen passage were close to the cellar door. An accomplice could easily stand out of sight and **heave bottles up the stairwell, one in each arm, causing them to smash together in mid-air** to create the illusion of materialization [34, 35]. 

### **The Origin of the Bottles**
A central debate regarding these phenomena was where the ammunition came from. 
*   **Marianne Foyster** later claimed: "There was no storage of bottles, and the mystery always to us was where the bottles came from because they were old bottles... we never did discover where the bottles came from" [36]. 
*   **Contradictory Evidence:** The Borley sources heavily contradict Marianne's claim. The previous inhabitants, the Bull family, maintained a well-stocked wine cellar with brick wine bins [37]. The predecessor to the Foysters, Mrs. Mabel Smith, explicitly noted that upon moving into the Rectory in 1928, she discovered a locked cellar door which, when opened, revealed a room **"piled high with empty wine bottles"** [38, 39]. This vast, pre-existing supply of bottles in the cellar provided the perfect, mundane source for whoever—or whatever—was hurling them upstairs [37, 38].
