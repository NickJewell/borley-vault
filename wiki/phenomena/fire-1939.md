# The Fire of 27 February 1939

The destruction of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] by fire at midnight on February 27, 1939. One of the most striking events in the case: it had been apparently predicted — in writing, in advance, preserved in [[harry-price|Price's]] files — eleven months before it happened.

## The Planchette Prediction (27 March 1938)

[[helen-glanville]] and her brother Roger held a Planchette session at home in Streatham. An entity calling itself "Sunex Amures" wrote (in the Planchette's hand):

> "Sunex Amures and one of his men MEAN TO BURN THE RECTORY (o-night at 9 o'clock end of the haunting go to the rectory and you will be able to see us enter into our own and under the ruins you will find bone of murdered wardens under the ruins..."

When asked "In which room will the fire start?" — "**Over the hall.** Yes yes you must go if you want proof."

The script was sent to Price's office at the time and carefully preserved. The Glanvilles did not travel to Borley that night — they did not take the threat seriously.

Eleven months later, on February 27, 1939 at midnight: Borley Rectory burned. The fire started in the main hall; the first part of the building to burn was immediately over the hall. The timing ("o-night at 9 o'clock") was not exact (fire was at midnight), but the location ("over the hall") was precisely correct.

Price: "Not only did 'Sunex Amures' say that the Rectory would be burnt, but he said that it would start 'over the hall.' That is exactly where it did start."

## The Fire Itself

[[captain-gregson|Captain Gregson]] was sorting books in the main hall by oil lamp when the pile "fell over onto a lamp and upsetting it." Gregson noted the books were "stacked quite carefully, and in ordinary commonsense they should have remained in their stack without falling over at all." He rushed to the cottage to rouse his sons. The Sudbury Fire Brigade arrived at c.12:15 a.m. to find the roof over the hall already collapsed. After an hour and fifteen minutes, the fire was mastered — but the Rectory was "practically ruined."

Prior fire outbreaks at the Rectory: [[lionel-foyster]]'s diary records skirting boards spontaneously on fire in 1931 (twice; both witnessed by [[dom-richard-whitehouse]] and/or [[lady-whitehouse]]). The building had a pattern of spontaneous fire phenomena preceding its final destruction.

## Apparitions During the Fire

- A police constable asked Gregson "who were the lady and gentleman who had just preceded me in passing over the courtyard." Gregson stated no woman was on the premises; he was entirely alone.
- Two "cloaked figures" were seen at the upper windows by several villagers.
- Two witnesses saw "figures moving amongst the flames, near the Blue Room window" — one apparently a girl, the other formless.

## Post-Fire — Blue Room Window Apparition (26 March 1939)

Rosemary Williams and C. Graham Browne visited the ruins a month later with a party, on a moonlit night. Miss Williams saw "a small woman in the upstairs room (the Blue Room)... a woman clothed in blue. She remained for several seconds and then turned towards the wall and, as it seemed, walked through it." Browne saw a flash as it disappeared. The floor of the Blue Room had burned away — Price himself confirmed on his March visit that it was physically impossible to stand at the Blue Room window aperture. Signed report from Miss Williams.

## Aftermath

- Fire confirmed to have destroyed entire first floor and attics
- Blue box found by Colonel Westland in June 1937 was still in its chalk ring in its cupboard when the Westlands visited the "Psychic Fête" in June 1939 — and they took it home as a souvenir
- 1943: Price excavated the ruins and found human remains associated with [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]
- 1944: Shell of the Rectory demolished

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**The Fire of 1939** occurred on the night of **February 27–28, 1939**, destroying Borley Rectory and reducing "the most haunted house in England" to a gutted, roofless shell [1-5]. The event is one of the most highly debated episodes in the Borley saga, featuring dramatic claims of ghosts walking amidst the flames, a seemingly fulfilled paranormal prophecy, and strong evidence of deliberate insurance fraud.

### **Recorded Instances and the Outbreak**
At the time of the fire, the Rectory's final owner, **Capt. William Hart Gregson**, and his two sons, Allan and Anthony, were living in the adjacent stable cottage while using the main Rectory for storage [6-8].
*   Around midnight, a fire broke out in the main hall of the Rectory [2, 9, 10].
*   According to Gregson, he rushed into the courtyard to get water from the pump, but quickly realized the blaze was out of control [10, 11]. He sent his two sons to the nearest public telephone at Borley Green (half a mile away) to summon help [8, 12].
*   Meanwhile, neighbors Mr. and Mrs. Pearson spotted the fire from their bedroom across the road and alerted Basil Payne at Borley Place, who telephoned the Sudbury Fire Brigade at approximately 12:15 a.m. [10].
*   The fire brigade arrived within 15 minutes, but the fire had spread rapidly up the main staircase. The roof soon collapsed, and despite pumping water from a nearby pond, the building was completely gutted by the time the brigade left around 8:15 a.m. [2, 13-15].

### **Witness Descriptions of Apparitions in the Flames**
Despite the inferno, several witnesses reported seeing figures in and around the burning building:
*   **The Cloaked Couple:** At about 4:00 a.m., a local police constable asked Captain Gregson to identify a **"cloaked lady and gentleman"** (also described as a woman in grey and a man in a bowler hat) he had just seen cross the courtyard in front of Gregson [16-19]. Gregson insisted he was entirely alone on the premises [17, 18].
*   **The Figures at the Window:** Villagers watching the blaze claimed to see two figures at the upper windows of the burning Rectory. One appeared to be a young girl, while the other was a "formless figure" [16-19]. Another account reported a young woman seen hanging by her fingertips from the sill of the Blue Room [20].
*   Capt. Gregson's son, Allan Gregson, later stated that he personally saw none of these figures and questioned how much "subjective thinking" prompted the villagers' reports [21, 22]. 

### **Proposed Explanations for the Fire**

**1. The Accidental Theory (Captain Gregson's Accounts)**
Gregson maintained that the fire was a pure accident, though his explanations varied slightly. 
*   He claimed he was in the hall sorting through a stack of books to dry them out. In one version, he pulled a copy of *Chambers's Encyclopaedia* from the pile, causing the books to topple over and knock down an oil lamp, spilling burning kerosene [8, 10, 23]. 
*   In a 1947 BBC radio broadcast, he omitted the books entirely, claiming the oil lamp suddenly "fell over automatically it seemed" [11, 23].

**2. The Paranormal Theory (The "Sunex Amures" Prophecy)**
Believers in the haunting point to a planchette séance held exactly eleven months prior, on **March 27, 1938**, operated by Helen and Roger Glanville in Streatham [1, 9, 24].
*   An entity calling itself **"Sunex Amures"** communicated a threat: **"mean to burn the Rectory to-night at 9 o'clock... In which room will the fire start? Over the hall"** [24-27].
*   The entity also promised that the fire would reveal the "bone of murdered" victims under the ruins, proving the haunting [24, 26].
*   While the date and time were wrong by eleven months, the fire *did* start in the hall, and human bone fragments were subsequently excavated from the cellars by Harry Price in 1943 [25, 28, 29]. Some researchers theorize the fire was orchestrated by spirits using Gregson as an instrument to reveal the hidden bones [30].

**3. The Arson and Insurance Fraud Theory (Skeptics and Investigators)**
The most grounded explanation is that the fire was deliberately set by Gregson for financial gain. 
*   Gregson had purchased the Rectory for a mere £500 (raising a £600 mortgage to do so), but had immediately **insured the property and his belongings for £10,000** [31-34]. 
*   Following the fire, Gregson submitted an insurance claim for £7,356. The insurance company's lawyer, Sir William Charles Crocker, and loss adjuster Col. Cuthbert Buckle carefully sifted the debris. Gregson claimed he lost a £50 coin collection, but they found only a single Victorian farthing. He claimed to have lost expensive, initialed underwear, but the unburned remnants tightly packed in a charred drawer proved to be darned and worn-out rags [35-37]. 
*   The insurers repudiated the claim, **bluntly accusing Gregson of arson**, though they eventually settled out of court for £750 to avoid the legal costs of fighting a "man of straw" [31, 38, 39].
*   Decades later, **Anthony Gregson** (one of the Captain's sons) explicitly confirmed the arson theory in a letter to an investigator, stating that his father bought the Rectory as a real estate venture to capitalize on its haunted reputation, and **"when this didn't pan out, he torched it for the insurance"** [34, 40-42]. 
*   While his other son, Allan, defended his father and insisted it was an accident [8, 43, 44], critics note that Gregson’s decision to store highly flammable books and papers in the main stairwell, combined with his decision to send *both* of his sons half a mile away to make a phone call instead of having one help fight the fire, provided him the perfect opportunity to ensure the house burned beyond repair [34, 45-47].
