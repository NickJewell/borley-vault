# The Fire of 27 February 1939

The destruction of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] by fire at midnight on February 27, 1939. One of the most striking events in the case: it had been apparently predicted — in writing, in advance, preserved in [[harry-price|Price's]] files — eleven months before it happened. The event is also one of the most highly debated episodes in the Borley saga, featuring dramatic claims of ghosts walking amidst the flames and strong evidence of deliberate insurance fraud.

## The Planchette Prediction (27 March 1938)

[[helen-glanville]] and her brother [[roger-glanville|Roger]] held a Planchette session at home in Streatham. An entity calling itself "[[sunex-amures|Sunex Amures]]" wrote (in the Planchette's hand):

> "Sunex Amures and one of his men MEAN TO BURN THE RECTORY (o-night at 9 o'clock end of the haunting go to the rectory and you will be able to see us enter into our own and under the ruins you will find bone of murdered wardens under the ruins..."

When asked "In which room will the fire start?" — "**Over the hall.** Yes yes you must go if you want proof."

The script was sent to Price's office at the time and carefully preserved. The Glanvilles did not travel to Borley that night — they did not take the threat seriously.

Eleven months later, on February 27, 1939 at midnight: Borley Rectory burned. The fire started in the main hall; the first part of the building to burn was immediately over the hall. The timing ("o-night at 9 o'clock") was not exact (fire was at midnight), but the location ("over the hall") was precisely correct. Some researchers theorize the fire was orchestrated by spirits using Gregson as an instrument to reveal the hidden bones [30].

Price: "Not only did 'Sunex Amures' say that the Rectory would be burnt, but he said that it would start 'over the hall.' That is exactly where it did start."

## The Fire Itself

[[captain-gregson|Captain Gregson]] was sorting books in the main hall by oil lamp when the pile "fell over onto a lamp and upsetting it." Gregson noted the books were "stacked quite carefully, and in ordinary commonsense they should have remained in their stack without falling over at all." He sent his two sons, Allan and Anthony, to the nearest public telephone at Borley Green (half a mile away) to summon help [8, 12]. Meanwhile, neighbours Mr. and Mrs. Pearson spotted the fire from their bedroom across the road and alerted Basil Payne at Borley Place, who telephoned the Sudbury Fire Brigade at approximately 12:15 a.m. [10].

The fire brigade arrived within fifteen minutes, but the fire had spread rapidly up the main staircase. The roof soon collapsed, and despite pumping water from a nearby pond, the building was completely gutted by the time the brigade left around 8:15 a.m. [2, 13–15].

In a 1947 BBC radio broadcast, Gregson omitted the books entirely, claiming the oil lamp suddenly "fell over automatically it seemed" [11, 23].

Prior fire outbreaks at the Rectory: [[lionel-foyster]]'s diary records skirting boards spontaneously on fire in 1931 (twice; both witnessed by [[dom-richard-whitehouse]] and/or [[lady-whitehouse]]). The building had a pattern of spontaneous fire phenomena preceding its final destruction.

## Apparitions During the Fire

- A police constable asked Gregson "who were the lady and gentleman who had just preceded me in passing over the courtyard." Gregson stated no woman was on the premises; he was entirely alone. (The figure was also described as a woman in grey and a man in a bowler hat [16–19].)
- Two "cloaked figures" were seen at the upper windows by several villagers [16–19].
- Two witnesses saw "figures moving amongst the flames, near the Blue Room window" — one apparently a girl, the other formless.
- Another account reported a young woman seen hanging by her fingertips from the sill of the Blue Room [20].

Capt. Gregson's son Allan later stated that he personally saw none of these figures and questioned how much "subjective thinking" prompted the villagers' reports [21, 22].

## Post-Fire — Blue Room Window Apparition (26 March 1939)

Rosemary Williams and C. Graham Browne visited the ruins a month later with a party, on a moonlit night. Miss Williams saw "a small woman in the upstairs room (the Blue Room)... a woman clothed in blue. She remained for several seconds and then turned towards the wall and, as it seemed, walked through it." Browne saw a flash as it disappeared. The floor of the Blue Room had burned away — Price himself confirmed on his March visit that it was physically impossible to stand at the Blue Room window aperture. Signed report from Miss Williams.

## The Arson and Insurance Fraud Theory

The most grounded explanation for the fire is that it was deliberately set by Gregson for financial gain.

Gregson had purchased the Rectory for a mere £500 (raising a £600 mortgage to do so), but had immediately **insured the property and his belongings for £10,000** [31–34]. Following the fire, Gregson submitted an insurance claim for £7,356. The insurance company's lawyer, Sir William Charles Crocker, and loss adjuster carefully sifted the debris. Gregson claimed he lost a £50 coin collection, but they found only a single Victorian farthing. He claimed to have lost expensive, initialled underwear, but the unburned remnants tightly packed in a charred drawer proved to be darned and worn-out rags [35–37]. The insurers repudiated the claim, **bluntly accusing Gregson of arson**, though they eventually settled out of court for £750 to avoid the legal costs of fighting a "man of straw" [31, 38, 39].

Decades later, **Anthony Gregson** explicitly confirmed the arson theory in a letter to an investigator, stating that his father bought the Rectory as a real estate venture to capitalize on its haunted reputation, and **"when this didn't pan out, he torched it for the insurance"** [34, 40–42]. Allan Gregson defended his father and insisted it was an accident [8, 43, 44]. Critics note that Gregson's decision to store highly flammable books and papers in the main stairwell, combined with sending *both* sons half a mile away to make a phone call, provided the perfect opportunity to ensure the house burned beyond repair [34, 45–47].

## Aftermath

- Fire confirmed to have destroyed entire first floor and attics
- Blue box found by Colonel Westland in June 1937 was still in its chalk ring in its cupboard when the Westlands visited the "Psychic Fête" in June 1939 — and they took it home as a souvenir
- 1943: Price excavated the ruins and found human remains associated with [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]
- 1944: Shell of the Rectory demolished
