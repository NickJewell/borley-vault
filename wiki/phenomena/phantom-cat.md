# The Phantom Cat

A recurring and distinctive phenomenon at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] and the cottage — a mysterious feline presence reported by multiple independent witnesses across three decades. The presence of feline ghosts is historically tied to the [[harry-bull|Rev. Harry Bull]], who was known to keep up to thirty-four cats at a time and created a special cats' cemetery in the Rectory grounds for his deceased pets [2–4]. After [[ivy-brackenbury|Ivy Brackenbury]] put her foot down following Bull's 1911 marriage, the number reduced dramatically; eventually he had only one left, which he took to church.

Babbs and Mathias note a 1938 mystery: an intruder dug up the cats' graves one night and no explanation has ever been offered.

## The Black Cat — Wentworth Day (1939–1940)

Four months after the fire, author and journalist [[james-wentworth-day|James Wentworth Day]] visited the burnt-out shell of the Rectory on a moonlit night. He reported that a **"gigantic black cat"** with "harsh bristles and snaky, undulating muscles" suddenly shot between his legs like a bullet, rushed into the ruined house, and never came out [1, 5, 6]. A London journalist subsequently described an identical experience in the same location. Local inquiries revealed no such animal was kept in the area; locals confirmed that multiple people had seen a cat go into the ruins at high speed, always vanishing without trace [1, 6, 7].

## Russell Herbert's Three Vanishing Cats — Summer 1941

A previously unpublished account from [[russell-herbert-bem|Russell Herbert BEM]] (born Sudbury 1922). As a teenager he entered the rectory grounds to answer a call of nature. Walking down the gravel driveway among tall cedars and firs toward the ruined rectory, he suddenly experienced:
- Intense cold and an awful smell of rotting vegetation
- Three large cats arching their backs and hissing at him

He continued walking toward them. They **vanished instantaneously** — not running away, simply ceasing to exist from one step to the next. He fled to his friends, who immediately noticed his distress. He regarded it as one of the most frightening experiences of his life [8].

This account predates the Turners' experiences and is particularly notable because Herbert had no intention of investigating the haunting; his purpose was entirely mundane.

## The Turner Period (1947–1950)

The most sustained and documented series of cat experiences occurred during the tenancy of **James and Catherine (Cathy) Turner** at the Rectory Cottage:

**July 28, 1949 — scratching and the dip in the bedclothes:** Cathy Turner was awakened in the night by the distinct sensation of something scratching at her bedclothes [1, 9–11]. A thorough search with a candle revealed no animal in the sealed room, though they noticed a hollow dip at the foot of the bed as if an animal had just been lying there [1, 9–11].

**August 28, 1949 — the chase:** Both Turners watched a thin, miserable-looking, grey-white cat with a scraggy tail that they didn't recognise chase their cat Fred under the car. The unknown cat followed Fred and then vanished completely; a careful search of the open-ground car park found nothing. Shortly after, they saw the same feline shape glide past an open doorway and vanish [9, 12–14].

**October 1949 — Ronald Blythe's sighting:** The writer and former Colchester librarian (with no prior knowledge of the Turners' cat experiences) visited Borley. In the moonlit bathroom, he saw "a distinct feline shape sliding along against the bath." He turned to look directly — the room was empty. Fred and Holly were both elsewhere. His description of the animal's scrawniness "tallied to a remarkable degree" with the Turners' account of the pursuer [9, 14].

**Undated 1949 — the car headlights:** [[james-turner|James Turner]] reported that while driving home late one night, they saw a lean grey cat dash under their car in the headlights. They got out assuming they had run it over, but there was nothing there [15].

**5–6 March 1950 — the final incident:** A piercing cat-squeal from inside the cottage — clearly from the direction of the landing. Fred was on the sofa; Holly was outside. No cat was found anywhere in the building [16, 17].

After the Turners left in 1950, no further cat incidents at the cottage were reported.

## Later Sightings — 1974

Both [[hilary-wright]] and [[marie-poore]] independently reported seeing a large cat dash into the road at the same spot in the Lower Road/Hall Lane area during the summer of 1974 — at which point it vanished. Neither was able to find the animal [18].

## The "Beast of Borley" (Early 2006)

A large black cat-like animal was recorded from a distance on a hand-held camcorder in broad daylight hunting near Borley village [1].

## Proposed Explanations

**Paranormal perspectives:** Believers strongly associate the phantom cats with the ghosts of the Rev. Harry Bull's many beloved pets, theorising they remained earthbound near the Cats' Cemetery [19]. Psychical researchers who accept the phenomena point to it as evidence that animals, like humans, can survive physical death and manifest as spirits [19, 20]. Some spiritualists view the terror displayed by the Turners' cat Fred as evidence of a genuine paranormal presence, referencing Elliott O'Donnell's theory that animals act as reliable "psychic barometers" when confronted with the supernatural [4, 13].

**Skeptical and mundane explanations:** Skeptics suggest the "Beast of Borley" is merely a large, flesh-and-blood feral cat [1, 7]. The 2006 video footage bolsters the theory that an exotic big cat may have been illegally released and was roaming the Essex/Suffolk countryside [1]. James Turner initially tried to rationalise the phenomena, suggesting his wife imagined the bed-scratching [11]. The loud feline squeal inside the cottage could easily be attributed to a stray cat or a fox outside, with its sound distorted by the building's acoustics [16]. The cat seen in the car headlights might simply have been struck and thrown into a hedge [21]. Skeptics view the proliferation of "phantom cat" stories as an extension of the general hysteria surrounding the Borley site, wherein normal rural wildlife encounters were imbued with sinister, paranormal significance [1].

Babbs and Mathias reference a 1937 Church of England Commission on psychical research and spiritualism, which debated whether animals could survive death and appear as phantoms.

## Significance

The 1941 (Herbert) and 1974 (Wright/Poore) accounts show the phenomenon in the wider grounds and lane area is not tied exclusively to the cottage period.
