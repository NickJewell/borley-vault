# The Phantom Cat

A recurring and distinctive phenomenon at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] and the cottage — a mysterious feline presence reported by multiple independent witnesses across three decades.

## The Black Cat (Wentworth Day, 1939)

Four months after the fire: a gigantic black cat shot between James Wentworth Day's legs and hurtled into the ruins, never coming out. He later met a journalist who described an identical experience in the same location. Locals confirmed that multiple people had seen a cat go into the ruins at high speed, always vanishing without trace.

## The Turner Period (1947–1950)

The most sustained and documented series of cat experiences:

**Scratching and the dip in the bedclothes (28 July 1948):** Mrs Turner awakened by scratching at the bedclothes; lit a candle; no cat in the room (door had been closed all night). Discovered a hollow in the bed surface "as if an animal had recently been lying there."

**The chase (28 August 1948):** Both Turners watched a thin, miserable-looking, grey-white cat they didn't recognise chase their cat Fred under the car. Fred fled up a walnut tree. The unknown cat vanished — a careful search of the open-ground car park found nothing.

**Ronald Blythe (October 1949):** The writer and former Colchester librarian (with no prior knowledge of the Turners' cat experiences) visited Borley. In the moonlit bathroom, he saw "a distinct feline shape sliding along against the bath." Turned to look directly — room was empty. Fred and Holly were both elsewhere. His description of the animal's scrawniness "tallied to a remarkable degree" with the Turners' account of the pursuer.

**The final incident (5 March 1950):** A piercing cat-squeal from inside the cottage — clearly from the direction of the landing. Fred was on the sofa; Holly was outside. No cat was found anywhere in the building.

## Russell Herbert's Three Vanishing Cats — Summer 1941 (brfa-ch09)

A previously unpublished account from [[russell-herbert-bem|Russell Herbert BEM]] (born Sudbury 1922). As a teenager he entered the rectory grounds to answer a call of nature. Walking down the gravel driveway among tall cedars and firs toward the ruined rectory, he suddenly experienced:
- Intense cold and an awful smell of rotting vegetation
- Three large cats arching their backs and hissing at him

He continued walking toward them. They **vanished instantaneously** — not running away, simply ceasing to exist from one step to the next. He fled to his friends, who immediately noticed his distress. He regarded it as one of the most frightening experiences of his life.

This account predates Wentworth Day's sighting (and the Turners' experiences) and is particularly notable because Herbert had no intention of investigating the haunting; his purpose was mundane.

## Later Sightings — 1974 (brfa-ch09)

Both [[hilary-wright]] and [[marie-poore]] independently reported seeing a large cat dash into the road at the same spot in Lower Road/Hall Lane area during the summer of 1974 — at which point it vanished. Neither was able to find the animal.

## The Harry Bull Connection

Rev Harry Bull kept approximately 20–30 cats at a time before his 1911 marriage. After Ivy put her foot down the number reduced dramatically; eventually he had only one left, which he took to church. The cats' cemetery in the garden contains many little graves with individual headboards. The phantom cat phenomenon may be connected to this history. Babbs and Mathias note a 1938 mystery: an intruder dug up the cats' graves one night and no explanation has ever been offered.

## Significance

After the Turners left in 1950, no further cat incidents at the cottage were reported. However the 1941 (Herbert) and 1974 (Wright/Poore) accounts show the phenomenon in the wider grounds and lane area is not tied exclusively to the cottage period. Babbs and Mathias reference a 1937 Church of England Commission on psychical research and spiritualism, which debated whether animals could survive death and appear as phantoms.
