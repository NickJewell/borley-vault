# Sunex Amures

Title of an alleged communicating entity that appeared during a planchette séance at the home of [[sidney-glanville]] in Streatham on 27 March 1938. The entity gave the famous threat to burn down [[borley-rectory]] that night — a threat fulfilled, exactly eleven months later, when the Rectory was gutted by fire on the night of 27/28 February 1939.

## The Séance

[[helen-glanville]] and her brother [[roger-glanville]] were the operators. On asking if there was anyone who wished to speak, the pen answered immediately. Miss Glanville asked "Who are you?" and received the following message:

> "Sunex Amures and one of his men [indistinct] mean to burn the rectory to-night at 9 o'clock end of the haunting go to the rectory and you will be able to see us enter into our own and under the ruins you will find bone of murdered [indistinct] mean you of the haunting of the rectory at Borley [indistinct] the understanding of which gamenl [large full stop written] game tells the story of murder which happened there."

When [[helen-glanville]] asked "In what room will the fire start?" the reply came: "Over the hall. Yes, yes you must go if you want proof." [[harry-price|Price]] acknowledged he did not take the threat very seriously at the time [3, 6].

## Prediction Accuracy

Nothing happened to the Rectory on the night of the séance. However, exactly eleven months later, around midnight on 27/28 February 1939, [[borley-rectory]] was destroyed by fire [1, 3, 7]. Price subsequently championed the message as a spectacularly fulfilled prophecy, arguing three "accurate" aspects:

- The fire did occur (though a year late)
- It started in the main hallway, subsequently destroying the area "over the hall" [5, 9]
- Bones were discovered in the cellar ruins — Price excavated the cellars in August 1943, finding human bone fragments, thereby fulfilling the entity's instruction to search "under the ruins" [6, 8, 9]

> **Contradiction:** Local newspaper reports cited by the SPR stated the fire started among stacked packages on the ground floor of the hall, directly contradicting the entity's claim that it would start *"over"* the hall [15].

Critics also note that a fire in a large, dilapidated Victorian rectory was not an improbable event, and that "over the hall" is a central location in most such buildings.

## Identity of Sunex Amures

Later researchers noting that the name appeared nonsensical proposed that the planchette operators had misinterpreted or received an anagram of the Latin phrase **"Senex Taurus"**, translating as **"Old Man Bull"** — possibly a reference to either [[henry-bull]] or [[harry-bull]] [4, 10, 11]. Under this theory, the spirit of "Old Man Bull," burdened by dark family secrets — possibly including the alleged tragic death of a pregnant maid, [[kate-boreham|Katie Boreham]] — desired to burn the house down to reveal the hidden truth and end the haunting [10–12].

The name also appears alongside other alleged communicators in the Glanvilles' séance transcripts, such as "Dominic Drossy" and "Joseph Glanvil."

## Sceptical and Critical Perspectives

The SPR authors ([[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], and [[trevor-hall]]) criticised [[harry-price|Price]] for suppressing the "Sunex Amures" script until *after* the fire had taken place, using it retrospectively to bolster the Borley legend [15]. [[harry-price|Price]] published the prophecy in *MHH* (October 1940), by which time the fire had already occurred eleven months earlier.

The most significant sceptical counter is the question of arson. The actual cause of the fire is widely attributed to the Rectory's final owner, [[captain-gregson|Capt. William Hart Gregson]], who had insured the dilapidated £500 property for £10,000. The insurance company's lawyer, [[william-crocker|Sir William Crocker]], repudiated Gregson's claim and bluntly accused him of intentionally firing the building for the money [13, 14]. It is considered impossible that Gregson knew about the private planchette session in Streatham — making the fire a mundane act of arson that merely coincided with the séance prophecy [7].

The failed timing also undermines the paranormal case: the prophecy missed the date by exactly eleven months, its most specific and checkable claim [3, 6].

## Significance

The episode stands as one of the most debated episodes in the entire Borley saga — a genuine prediction of a named location that proved partially accurate, set against the strong probability that the fire itself was deliberate arson by its new owner.
