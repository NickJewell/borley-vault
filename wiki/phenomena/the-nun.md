# The Nun

The most frequently reported apparition at [[borley-rectory]] and the centrepiece of its haunting legend. A dark, female figure, typically seen moving slowly along [[nuns-walk]] with bowed head.

## Description

Based on all accounts from Chapter 1:
- Dark female figure; nun-like in posture and movement
- No features distinguishable (witnesses saw from ~100 yards in poor light)
- No white or light colouring reported by reliable witnesses ([[ethel-bull]] corrected this impression in [[harry-price|Price's]] published account)
- Moved with bowed head; glided rather than walked
- Vanished suddenly when approached

The legend ascribes the Nun to a novice from a Bures nunnery who was bricked up alive after a forbidden romance. **No historical evidence supports the existence of that nunnery or that punishment in Britain.**

## The Bricked-Up Window

[[harry-price|Price]] records that [[henry-bull|Henry Bull]] permanently bricked up the dining room window — a drastic alteration that ruined the appearance of that side of the house — because the Nun "habitually peered into the room from the drive." Three independent sources confirmed this to Price. He dismissed the alternative "privacy" explanation systematically.

This is among the most striking pieces of architectural evidence for the Nun as a repeatedly witnessed, recurring presence: it implies the sightings were frequent and consistent enough that a Bull patriarch took permanent structural action.

## Gate Figure (Smith Period)

During the Smith incumbency (1928–29), a dark, shadowy figure was repeatedly seen leaning over one of the drive gates. Multiple witnesses — including [[mabel-smith|Mrs Smith]] and at least one maid — reported it, always describing its strange and sudden disappearance on approach. A London maid saw a figure dressed in black at the gate and left after two days. [[vc-wall|V. C. Wall's]] newspaper report (published before Price arrived) also records the maid seeing the Nun leaning over a gate near the house.

## Sceptical Positions

- Critics suggest the apparitions visible during Harry Bull's incumbency were "illusions or perhaps hallucinations" attributable to "morbid imagination and evident interest in the supernormal."
- Gerald Bull (brother of Harry) dismissed the Nun as "figment of feminine imagination."
- The name "Nun's Walk" may have primed expectation; the legend was widely known among residents.

## The Legend vs the Evidence

[[harry-price|Price]] explicitly debunks the bricked-up nun legend ([[mhh-ch06]]): no historical evidence for nuns being immured in Britain; the coach legend is anachronistic (no coaches in 13th-century England). But he insists this does not explain away the *sightings*. The nun appears as a real, objective figure — seen simultaneously by four people, seen by a carpenter who didn't know the haunting story, seen walking over an inspection cover that would rattle under normal weight without making a sound.

Price's Planchette sessions ([[mhh-ch26]]) connected the apparition to "Mary Lairre" — a French novice, murdered by strangulation in 1667 at the hands of a Waldegrave. Whether this identification is accurate is a separate question from whether the figure was genuinely seen.

## Additional Witnesses from Borley Postscript (bps-01, bps-08)

### The Nun's Day (bps-01)
Underwood confirms that July 28 was already known as "the nun's day" by H.D.E. Bull himself before 1900 — a cousin remembered the old rector remarking at breakfast that he would not be surprised if his wife had seen the ghost, "for the previous day had been July 28th, the nun's day." This predates all recorded sightings.

### Ethel Bull's Second Sighting (bps-09)
Ethel Bull told Underwood she saw the nun again "less than four months" after the famous 1900 sighting — this time with the Rectory cook and a visiting cousin also present, all seeing the figure at the garden gate.

### Housemaid fainted (bps-08)
[[ernest-ambrose|Ernest Ambrose]] related that a young housemaid at the Rectory (c.1900–1910) saw "a person dressed like a nun or nurse standing by the lower garden gate" in semi-darkness. She approached; it vanished. She fainted and left the rector's employment.

### Choir boy at the porch (bps-08)
Harry Bull told Ambrose that a young choir boy, wanting a word one evening, saw "a nun or sister of mercy seemingly about to knock on the door" — and went away to avoid interrupting. The rector confirmed no visitor had called; it was "the ghost nun."

### Dr Abernethy (bps-01)
A local female physician, en route to visit a patient, saw "the figure of a stooping nun close to the hedge" near the Rectory site. Braked; backed car; got out; conducted a thorough search. Nothing.

### The Rev. Clive Luget (bps-08)
[[clive-luget|Luget]], Rector of Middleton, personally saw the ghost nun on at least one of his visits to the Rectory during the Foyster period (1930s).

### Rev. Kipling (bps-01)
A visiting clergyman, previously a sceptic, waiting to officiate at a funeral at Borley Church, saw "the figure of a nun in the churchyard" disappear behind a bush; he immediately approached — nothing behind the bush.

### The Bacon family (bps-01)
During the Bacons' occupancy of the cottage (after the Turners, 1950s), young Terry Bacon twice saw "the figure of a nun in the old Rectory garden" — once it disappeared through a hedge without moving the hedge. Terry's grandfather (also living at the cottage) saw the figure in the garden through a chicken-house window.

### P. Shaw Jeffrey (1885–86, ebr-ch06)
[[p-shaw-jeffrey|P. Shaw Jeffrey]], Oxford contemporary of Harry Bull, stayed at the Rectory in the long vacations of 1885 and 1886. He saw the nun "several times." He also heard the phantom coach so often "that I used to sleep through the noise" — making him the only witness ever to habituate to the phenomenon through sheer repetition. His testimony (Cape Town letter, March 1942) is the earliest direct first-person account.

### Mrs E. Byford (1886, ebr-ch06)
Under-nursemaid at the Rectory in 1886; heard slippered footsteps approach her locked room in the night. Left after a fortnight. "Common talk that the Rectory was haunted." Her grandparents would not let her pass the house after dark.

### Aerodrome worker (c.1942, ebr-ch04)
A man working on a nearby aerodrome who had to pass the Rectory each day saw the nun early one morning, standing at the gate. Told a local foreman painter: "I saw a nun outside that house the other day, and she did look miserable."

### Lt. Nawrocki (June and July 1943, ebr-ch04)
[[gb-nawrocki|Lt. Nawrocki]] (Polish Army) saw a black shadow moving between the trees on the Nun's Walk on both his visits to the ruins — each time vanishing between the second and third tree. On the second visit (July 28, 1943 — the traditional anniversary date), two other observers from a separate group also independently saw a shadow of a man on the chapel wall.

## Post-Fire Sightings

The most striking post-fire sightings — [[captain-gregson|Captain Gregson]] at the time of the fire, Rosemary Williams at the burnt-out Blue Room window — confirm the apparition is not tied to the physical building. The Rosemary Williams sighting is the best-documented: her signed written account specifies the figure was standing at a window whose floor had burned away, making any physical explanation impossible.

## The Nun Claimants / Candidate Identities

Five separate "entities" have claimed or been proposed as the identity of the Borley nun:
1. **[[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]** — French novice, Planchette name; supported by wall-writings, bones, pendants; murdered 1667 by Waldegrave (per séances)
2. **Arabella Waldegrave** (born 1687) — Mrs Dawson's theory; dismissed by Phythian-Adams on linguistic grounds (*trompée* is fluent French, ruling out an English-educated woman)
3. **Evangeline Westcott** — name given at Marks Tey Circle séance, April 1932; not corroborated; "pigeon-holed" by Captain Deane
4. **Summer-house nun** — Cambridge Commission rapping entity, July 1944; no name given; may be Marie Lairre under another description
5. **[[barbara-waldegrave|Barbara Waldegrave (Sister Mary Apollonia)]]** — [[ivan-banks|Banks's]] (1994) conclusion: born at Borley 1603; daughter of Sir Nicholas Waldegrave of Borley; Brussels Benedictine nun; died April 18, 1638 aged ~36; "happily deceased" in chronicles; of all Waldegrave nuns, the one born at Borley whose death circumstances are unexplained

## The Appearance

All witnesses who described the figure agree: she is sad-looking, pale, appears to have been weeping, and has "an expression of intense grief." The dental surgeon's X-ray of the jaw-bone found in the cellar (1943) revealed a deep-seated bone abscess causing severe tooth and jaw pain — consistent with the nun's habitual mournful expression over nearly a century of sightings.

## Post-War and Later Sightings (brfa-ch08, brfa-ch09)

New witnesses documented for the first time in *The Final Analysis*:

- **Dr Margaret Abernethy, 26 July 1949:** Local GP driving up Hall Lane; figure in nun's habit standing by the hedge outside the rectory garden; solid, not wraith-like; face visible; appeared about 40 years old. A later visit to the same spot produced a rosary that appeared on the road and then vanished. See [[dr-margaret-abernethy]]
- **Mrs Gooch's friend, 20 August 1949:** A figure dressed in light-coloured robes seen in the bungalow garden on the rectory site; vanished; search found nothing
- **Dorothy Lane, summer 1944:** Figure in long grey robes at her bedside in a Nissen hut at Liston Hall (one mile from Borley); stood for ~10 seconds then faded gradually. See [[dorothy-lane]]
- **Croom-Hollingsworth group, 19 June 1970:** Three observers; 12-minute observation at close range (15–20 feet); grey cowled robes, elderly appearance, mole on left cheek, eyes shut, sad expression. [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth]] radioed colleagues during the sighting. (Previously in the Companion but now with additional detail from brfa)
- **Hilary Wright, autumn 1971:** Seen from a car at 2am in Hall Lane; long grey robes; apparently standing on "high ground which is no longer there"; "bad vibes" experienced. See [[hilary-wright]]
- **Canon Edward Powell, early 1960s:** Cycling in Hall Lane in daylight; figure moved onto road from left and "melted back into the hedge." See [[canon-edward-powell]]
- **Canon Pennell and wife Ursula, 3 December 1965:** Hooded figure in dark grey robes walked directly in front of their Dormobile on the Borley/Liston road junction; no impact despite proximity. See [[canon-leslie-pennell]]
- **Terry Bacon, Bacon family:** residents at Rectory Cottage 1951–1972; son and grandfather both claimed multiple sightings of the nun (details not recorded in full)

**The age question:** [[dr-margaret-abernethy]] estimated ~40 years old; [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth]] also described an elderly woman. This conflicts with the Marie Lairre planchette account of a 19-year-old novice. Babbs and Mathias suggest the nun may be a different entity — perhaps a nun associated with the Waldegrave household — rather than the murdered Marie Lairre.

## SPR Assessment of Individual Sightings (spr-iii, spr-iv, spr-vi)

The 1956 SPR Report systematically addresses the nun sighting record:

**Bull period (general):** The sightings attributed to Henry and Harry Bull are entirely second-hand (Price's account of what Ethel Bull told him). Ethel Bull confirmed Price "made a number of mistakes" having never given her the chance to approve his account. Her single experience of "a tall dark man" was multiplied by Price into multiple separate apparitions. Gerald Bull told Canon Lawton privately that the nun was "a product of feminine imagination"; Walter Bull denied any haunting during his family's residence

**The 1900 four-sister sighting:** This occurred at "late twilight, about 9 p.m." (confirmed by Lord Charles Hope's 1929 notes and by Ethel Bull herself to KMG in 1950). Price's published account implied "sunlight" — a significant misrepresentation of the light conditions. At 9 p.m. on 28 July, the sun had set at 7:53 p.m. At that level of twilight, misidentification of a dark object or person is not improbable

**V. C. Wall's "nun sighting" (June 1929):** Wall thought he saw a shadowy figure on the Nun's Walk; he ran across to investigate. The same night, the same party "had a terrible shock" and "distinctly saw a white figure flitting about in the gloom" — which turned out to be the Smiths' maid Mary Pearson. Wall published this false alarm in the *Daily Mirror* the following day under the headline "Midnight Apparition That Proved to Be a Maid" — a detail Price omitted in his MHH account, despite claiming to print Wall's article verbatim

**Mrs Lloyd Williams (February 1938):** Debunked in detail — a product of the Blue Book's suggestion to watch from the summer-house at dusk for "the black draped figure." Her companion C. Gordon Glover dismissed the sighting as imaginary in his own report, but Price promoted her as a valid witness. This was "the only alleged apparition during the whole of the observational period"

**The SPR's summary:** "A consistent feature of the early Borley 'apparitions' is the fact that almost without exception they were seen out of doors, where the possibility of the misinterpretation of natural objects seen in poor light is much greater than inside a room." The Rev. G. Eric Smith himself once "saw what looked like a spectre of a white-clad monk in the rectory garden, afterwards discovering that it was the smoke from a bonfire" — an incident Price never published

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**The Phantom Nun** is the most celebrated and frequently reported apparition in the history of the Borley Rectory hauntings, central to its lore and the subject of intense investigation and fierce debate. Descriptions of the entity vary, as do the theories attempting to explain her presence, ranging from romantic medieval tragedies to accusations of psychological suggestion and outright hoaxes.

### **Recorded Instances and Witness Descriptions**

**The Bull Incumbency (Pre-1928)**
*   **c. 1885–1886:** P. Shaw Jeffrey, a friend of the Rev. Harry Bull, claimed to have seen the nun several times during his stays at the Rectory [1-3].
*   **28 July 1900 (The Foundational Sighting):** Four of the Bull sisters (Ethel, Freda, Mabel, and Elsie) collectively saw the nun while returning from a garden party [4, 5]. **They described a figure dressed entirely in flowing black robes, with a bowed head and hands clasped as if telling beads, gliding along the "Nun's Walk"** [4-6]. When Elsie approached, the figure stopped, turned towards her, and vanished [5, 7]. Harry Price later claimed the figure had an expression of "intense grief," though Ethel Bull subsequently told investigators that the light was too poor to see any facial features [5, 8].
*   **November 1900:** Ethel Bull and the Rectory cook saw the nun leaning over a garden gate, a sighting later corroborated by a visiting cousin [7, 9, 10].
*   **1916–1919:** Edward Cooper, the groom-gardener, saw a "Sister of Mercy" leave the back entrance of the Rectory at dusk. He followed her for several yards until she reached the road and vanished instantly [11, 12]. 
*   **Autumn 1927:** Fred Cartwright, a journeyman carpenter, saw a "Sister of Mercy" standing by the Rectory gate on four separate mornings at dawn. On the second and third occasions, her eyes were closed, and she looked pale, ill, and tired. On his fourth encounter, he intended to speak to her, but she vanished before he could reach the gate [13-16].

**The Smith & Foyster Incumbencies and Price Tenancy (1928–1938)**
*   **1928:** A servant girl from London gave notice after just two days, terrified after seeing a nun walking in the wood [17, 18]. The Smiths' subsequent maid, Mary Pearson, also claimed to have seen the nun leaning over a gate and walking on the Nun's Walk [19-21].
*   **12 June 1929:** During Harry Price's first visit, *Daily Mirror* reporter V.C. Wall claimed to see a dark, shadowy figure moving along the Nun's Walk at dusk, though it vanished before he could reach it [22, 23]. 
*   **18 February 1938:** Mrs. Lloyd Williams, one of Price's official observers, was watching from the summer-house at twilight and reported seeing a "round, dark object" resembling a "short, stooping figure" move across the lawn and disappear [24-26]. 

**Post-Fire and Later Sightings (1939 onwards)**
Even after the Rectory burned down in 1939, sightings of the nun continued, often shifting toward the adjacent Borley Church:
*   **26 March 1939:** Rosemary Williams and Charles Browne saw the figure of a woman clothed in blue (or a light buff coat) standing in the burnt-out aperture of the Blue Room window, despite there being no floor left to stand on [27-30].
*   **June/July 1943:** Polish Army officers investigating the ruins reported seeing a black shadow moving between the trees on the Nun's Walk [27, 31, 32].
*   **Early Summer 1944:** Dorothy Lane, stationed at nearby Liston Hall, awoke to find a shadowy figure in long grey robes standing by her bed. It lingered for ten seconds before gradually fading away [33].
*   **26 July 1949:** Dr. Margaret Abernethy saw a solid-looking nun, estimated to be about 40 years old, standing by a hedge. When the doctor reversed her car to offer the woman a lift, the figure had disappeared [34-36]. 
*   **August 1949:** The Rev. Stanley C. Kipling saw a frail-looking veiled girl, aged 18 to 23, wearing a nun's hood in the churchyard. She passed behind a shrub and vanished [37, 38].
*   **20 June 1970:** Investigators Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth and Roy Potter reported watching the nun for 12 minutes. **In a stark departure from earlier descriptions of a young woman, they described an elderly woman in her sixties with a mole on her cheek**, wearing a grey habit and cowl, gliding a foot above the ground and passing straight through a solid fence [39-42].
*   **Autumn 1971:** Hilary Wright saw a nun in long grey robes standing elevated above the road level on Hall Lane [43, 44].

### **Proposed Explanations: Legends and Paranormal Theories**

**1. The 13th-Century Novice of Bures**
The earliest and most popular local legend asserted that the Rectory was built on the site of a 13th-century monastery. According to the tale, a monk eloped with a beautiful novice from a nearby nunnery at Bures in a horse-drawn coach. They were captured; the monk was hanged or beheaded, and the nun was bricked up alive in her convent walls. Her ghost purportedly wandered the Rectory searching for her lover [45-48]. 

**2. Marie Lairre (The 17th-Century French Nun)**
During planchette séances conducted by the Glanville family in 1937, a different narrative emerged. The spirit identified herself as "Marie Lairre," a 19-year-old French novice from Le Havre. She claimed she was induced to leave her convent, brought to England, and strangled at Borley on May 17, 1667, by a member of the powerful Waldegrave family. Her body was allegedly buried in the cellar [49-53]. Canon W.J. Phythian-Adams championed this theory, suggesting the Catholic nature of the wall-writings (appealing for "Light, Mass, Prayers") were her desperate pleas [53, 54]. In 1943, Harry Price excavated the cellars and found bone fragments belonging to a young woman, which he attributed to Marie Lairre and had given a Christian burial in 1945 [55-58].

**3. The Waldegrave Nuns**
Historical researchers later dismissed the "Marie Lairre" story and pointed out that several daughters of the local Waldegrave family had actually become nuns in Europe. Ivan Banks proposed the ghost was **Barbara Apollonia Waldegrave** (born 1603), who became a nun in Brussels and died mysteriously young at age 36 [59-61]. Georgina Dawson proposed the ghost was **Arabella Waldegrave** (born 1687), who entered a French convent, allegedly became a Stuart spy, and was murdered at Borley [62-64].

### **Proposed Explanations: Skeptical and Mundane Perspectives**

**1. Historical Impossibilities**
Skeptics, including Dr. Letitia Fairfield and the Society for Psychical Research (SPR), effectively dismantled the foundational legends. **They proved there was never a monastery at Borley nor a nunnery at Bures.** Furthermore, horse-drawn coaches did not exist in England in the 13th century, and the romanticized gothic punishment of bricking up errant nuns is a complete historical myth [45, 65-68].

**2. Misidentification and Tricks of the Light**
The SPR authors (Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) heavily criticized Harry Price's framing of the famous July 1900 sighting. Price published that the sisters saw the nun in "sunlight" [69, 70]. The SPR pointed out that the event happened around 9:00 p.m., well over an hour after sunset, in "late twilight." **In the fading gloom, skeptics argue the sisters simply misidentified a passing villager, vagabond, or poacher** using the Rectory garden as a shortcut, who hurried away when approached [71-75]. 

**3. Village Pranks and Hoaxes**
Many early sightings of the nun and other figures were attributed to local pranksters. Mrs. Mabel Smith believed village boys played tricks in the dark. It was widely known that Mary Pearson's boyfriend, Fred Tatum, walked around the grounds in the rain with his coat pulled over his head, easily creating the illusion of a cowled or headless figure [76, 77]. Mary Pearson herself was known to put her apron over her head to mimic the nun as a joke [78]. 

**4. Psychological Suggestion and Price's Exaggerations**
Skeptics maintain that Harry Price deliberately exaggerated testimonies to sell books. When Ethel Bull was interviewed by the SPR in 1953, she complained that Price had altered her accounts to make them more sensational, noting she never saw a face on the nun, let alone an expression of "intense grief" [8, 79]. Furthermore, during Price's tenancy, he issued a *Blue Book of Instructions* to his 48 observers, explicitly telling them to watch the "Nun's Walk" at dusk for a "black draped figure." Skeptics argue this heavily primed impressionable observers, like Mrs. Lloyd Williams, causing them to experience subjective hallucinations based on expectancy [25, 80]. Some members of the Bull family themselves, such as Gerald and Walter Bull, entirely rejected the hauntings, calling the nun a product of "feminine imagination" [81, 82].
