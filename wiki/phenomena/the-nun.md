# The Nun

The most frequently reported apparition at [[borley-rectory]] and the centrepiece of its haunting legend. A dark, female figure, typically seen moving slowly along [[nuns-walk]] with bowed head.

## Description

Based on all accounts across the full sighting record:
- Dark female figure; nun-like in posture and movement
- No features distinguishable (witnesses saw from ~100 yards in poor light)
- No white or light colouring reported by reliable witnesses ([[ethel-bull]] corrected this impression in [[harry-price|Price's]] published account) [8, 79]
- Moved with bowed head; glided rather than walked
- Vanished suddenly when approached
- Described by multiple witnesses as sad-looking, pale, appearing to have been weeping, with "an expression of intense grief" — though the SPR notes conditions were generally too dark to distinguish facial features [5, 8]

The legend ascribes the Nun to a novice from a Bures nunnery bricked up alive after a forbidden romance. **No historical evidence supports the existence of that nunnery or that punishment in Britain** [45, 65–68].

## The Bricked-Up Window

[[harry-price|Price]] records that [[henry-bull|Henry Bull]] permanently bricked up the dining room window — a drastic alteration that ruined the appearance of that side of the house — because the Nun "habitually peered into the room from the drive." Three independent sources confirmed this to Price. He dismissed the alternative "privacy" explanation systematically.

This is among the most striking pieces of architectural evidence for the Nun as a repeatedly witnessed, recurring presence: it implies the sightings were frequent and consistent enough that a Bull patriarch took permanent structural action.

## Gate Figure (Smith Period)

During the Smith incumbency (1928–29), a dark, shadowy figure was repeatedly seen leaning over one of the drive gates. Multiple witnesses — including [[mabel-smith|Mrs Smith]] and at least one maid — reported it, always describing its strange and sudden disappearance on approach. A London maid saw a figure dressed in black at the gate and left after two days [17, 18]. [[mary-pearson|Mary Pearson]], the Smiths' subsequent maid, also reported the nun leaning over a gate and walking on the Nun's Walk [19–21].

## Additional Witnesses (Bull Period)

### The Nun's Day (bps-01)
Underwood confirms that July 28 was already known as "the nun's day" by H.D.E. Bull himself before 1900 — a cousin remembered the old rector remarking at breakfast that he would not be surprised if his wife had seen the ghost, "for the previous day had been July 28th, the nun's day." This predates all recorded sightings.

### Ethel Bull's Second Sighting (bps-09)
[[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]] told Underwood she saw the nun again "less than four months" after the famous 1900 sighting — this time with the Rectory cook and a visiting cousin also present, all seeing the figure at the garden gate.

### Housemaid fainted (bps-08)
[[ernest-ambrose|Ernest Ambrose]] related that a young housemaid at the Rectory (c.1900–1910) saw "a person dressed like a nun or nurse standing by the lower garden gate" in semi-darkness. She approached; it vanished. She fainted and left the rector's employment.

### Choir boy at the porch (bps-08)
[[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] told Ambrose that a young choir boy, wanting a word one evening, saw "a nun or sister of mercy seemingly about to knock on the door" — and went away to avoid interrupting. The rector confirmed no visitor had called; it was "the ghost nun."

### P. Shaw Jeffrey (1885–86, ebr-ch06)
[[p-shaw-jeffrey|P. Shaw Jeffrey]], Oxford contemporary of Harry Bull, stayed at the Rectory in the long vacations of 1885 and 1886. He reportedly saw the nun "several times" — the earliest direct first-person account. His testimony (Cape Town letter, March 1942) is given low reliability: it was provided over fifty years after the fact and was subsequently retracted [1–3].

### Mrs E. Byford (1886, ebr-ch06)
Under-nursemaid at the Rectory in 1886; heard slippered footsteps approach her locked room in the night. Left after a fortnight. "Common talk that the Rectory was haunted."

## The 1900 Four-Sister Sighting in Detail

The four Bull sisters — Ethel, Freda, Mabel, and Elsie — collectively saw the nun while returning from a garden party on 28 July 1900 [4, 5]. They described a figure dressed in flowing black robes, with a bowed head and hands clasped as if telling beads, gliding along the Nun's Walk [4–6]. When Elsie approached, the figure turned towards her and vanished [5, 7].

> **Contradiction:** [[harry-price|Price's]] published account implies the sisters saw the nun in "sunlight" [69, 70]. The SPR pointed out the event happened around 9:00 p.m. — confirmed by Lord Charles Hope's 1929 notes and by [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]] herself in her 1950 interview with the SPR — well over an hour after sunset (sun set at 7:53 p.m. on 28 July) [71–75]. At that level of late twilight, misidentification of a dark object or a passing person is not improbable. Price also attributed to the figure an expression of "intense grief" which Ethel Bull denied being able to see given the light conditions [8, 79].

## Key Independent Witnesses

**[[fred-cartwright|Fred Cartwright]] (autumn 1927):** A journeyman carpenter with no prior knowledge of the haunting, saw a "Sister of Mercy" standing by the Rectory gate on four separate mornings at dawn. On the second and third occasions her eyes were closed and she looked pale, ill, and tired. On his fourth encounter he intended to speak to her — she vanished before he could reach the gate [13–16]. (multiple witnesses — self; no prior knowledge)

**[[edward-cooper|Edward Cooper]] (1916–1920):** The Bull family's groom-gardener saw a "Sister of Mercy" leave the back entrance of the Rectory at dusk on multiple occasions. He followed her for several yards until she reached the road and vanished [11, 12]. His account was later confirmed as consistent with other witnesses' descriptions.

**Aerodrome worker (c.1942, ebr-ch04):** A man working on a nearby aerodrome who passed the Rectory daily saw the nun early one morning, standing at the gate. Told a local foreman: "I saw a nun outside that house the other day, and she did look miserable."

**Lt. Nawrocki (June and July 1943, ebr-ch04):** A Polish Army officer saw a black shadow moving between the trees on the Nun's Walk on both his visits to the ruins — each time vanishing between the second and third tree. On the second visit (July 28, 1943 — the traditional anniversary date), two other observers from a separate group also independently saw a shadow of a man on the chapel wall.

## Post-Fire Sightings

The most striking post-fire sightings confirm the apparition is not tied to the physical building.

**Rosemary Williams and C. Graham Browne (26 March 1939):** The best-documented post-fire sighting. Their signed written account specifies the figure was standing at the burnt-out aperture of the Blue Room window, despite there being no floor left to stand on — making any physical explanation impossible [27–30].

**Dorothy Lane (early summer 1944):** Stationed at nearby [[liston-hall|Liston Hall]] (one mile from Borley), she awoke to find a shadowy figure in long grey robes standing by her bed. It lingered for ten seconds before gradually fading away [33]. See [[dorothy-lane]].

**Rev. Stanley C. Kipling (August 1949):** A visiting clergyman, previously a sceptic, waiting to officiate at a funeral at [[borley-church|Borley Church]], saw "the figure of a nun in the churchyard" disappear behind a bush; he immediately approached — nothing behind the bush [37, 38].

**The Bacon family (1951–1972, bps-01):** During the Bacons' occupancy of the cottage, young Terry Bacon twice saw "the figure of a nun in the old Rectory garden" — once it disappeared through a hedge without moving it. Terry's grandfather, also living at the cottage, saw the figure through a chicken-house window.

## Post-War and Later Sightings (brfa-ch08, brfa-ch09)

New witnesses documented for the first time in *The Final Analysis*:

- **[[dr-margaret-abernethy|Dr Margaret Abernethy]], 26 July 1949:** Local GP driving up Hall Lane; figure in nun's habit standing by the hedge outside the rectory garden; solid, not wraith-like; face visible; appeared about 40 years old. A later visit to the same spot produced a rosary that appeared on the road and then vanished [34–36].
- **Mrs Gooch's friend, 20 August 1949:** A figure dressed in light-coloured robes seen in the bungalow garden on the rectory site; vanished; search found nothing.
- **[[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth|Geoffrey Croom-Hollingsworth]] group, 19 June 1970:** Three observers; 12-minute observation at close range (15–20 feet); grey cowled robes, elderly appearance, mole on left cheek, eyes shut, sad expression. Croom-Hollingsworth radioed colleagues during the sighting [39–42].
- **[[hilary-wright|Hilary Wright]], autumn 1971:** Seen from a car at 2am in Hall Lane; long grey robes; apparently standing on "high ground which is no longer there" [43, 44].
- **[[canon-edward-powell|Canon Edward Powell]], early 1960s:** Cycling in Hall Lane in daylight; figure moved onto road from left and "melted back into the hedge."
- **[[canon-leslie-pennell|Canon Pennell]] and wife [[ursula-pennell|Ursula]], 3 December 1965:** Hooded figure in dark grey robes walked directly in front of their Dormobile on the Borley/Liston road junction; no impact despite proximity.

**The age question:** [[dr-margaret-abernethy]] estimated the figure was approximately 40 years old; [[geoffrey-croom-hollingsworth]] described an elderly woman in her sixties. This conflicts with the [[marie-lairre]] planchette account of a 19-year-old novice. Babbs and Mathias suggest the nun may be a different entity — perhaps a nun associated with the Waldegrave household — rather than the murdered Marie Lairre.

## The Dental Evidence

The dental surgeon's X-ray of the jaw-bone found in the cellar during the [[bones-excavation|1943 excavation]] revealed a deep-seated bone abscess causing severe tooth and jaw pain — consistent, if the bones are connected, with the nun's habitual mournful expression over nearly a century of sightings.

## The Nun Claimants / Candidate Identities

Five separate "entities" have claimed or been proposed as the identity of the Borley nun:

1. **[[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre]]** — French novice, Planchette name; supported by [[wall-writings]], bones, pendants; murdered 1667 by a [[waldegrave-family|Waldegrave]] (per séances). Championed by [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] [49–54].
2. **[[arabella-waldegrave|Arabella Waldegrave]]** (born 1687) — proposed by Georgina Dawson; entered a French convent and allegedly became a Stuart spy [62–64]. Dismissed by [[canon-phythian-adams|Phythian-Adams]] on linguistic grounds: *trompée* is fluent French, ruling out an English-educated woman.
3. **Evangeline Westcott** — name given at [[marks-tey-spiritualist-circle|Marks Tey Circle]] séance, April 1932; not corroborated; "pigeon-holed" by Captain Deane.
4. **Summer-house nun** — Cambridge Commission rapping entity, July 1944; no name given; may be Marie Lairre under another description.
5. **[[barbara-waldegrave|Barbara Waldegrave (Sister Mary Apollonia)]]** — [[ivan-banks|Banks's]] (1994) conclusion: born at Borley 1603; daughter of Sir Nicholas Waldegrave of Borley; Brussels Benedictine nun; died April 18, 1638 aged ~36; "happily deceased" in chronicles; of all Waldegrave nuns, the one born at Borley whose death circumstances are unexplained [59–61].

## Origin Legends and Their Historical Impossibilities

The earliest and most popular local legend asserted that the Rectory was built on the site of a 13th-century monastery. A monk allegedly eloped with a novice from a Bures nunnery; they were captured, the monk hanged, and the nun bricked up alive [45–48]. [[harry-price|Price]] explicitly debunks this in [[borley-rectory|MHH]]: **there was never a monastery at Borley nor a nunnery at Bures**, and coaches did not exist in England in the 13th century. Sceptics including [[dr-letitia-fairfield|Dr Letitia Fairfield]] and the SPR effectively dismantled all versions of the foundational legend [45, 65–68]. But Price insists this does not explain away the *sightings* — the nun appears as a real, objective figure seen simultaneously by four people, seen by a carpenter who didn't know the haunting story, and seen walking over an inspection cover that would rattle under normal weight without making a sound.

## SPR Assessment of Individual Sightings (spr-iii, spr-iv, spr-vi)

The 1956 SPR Report systematically addresses the sighting record:

**Bull period (general):** The sightings attributed to [[henry-bull|Henry]] and [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] are entirely second-hand (Price's account of what [[ethel-bull|Ethel Bull]] told him). Ethel Bull confirmed Price "made a number of mistakes" having never given her the chance to approve his account. Her single experience of "a tall dark man" was multiplied by Price into multiple separate apparitions. Gerald Bull told Canon Lawton privately that the nun was "a product of feminine imagination"; Walter Bull denied any haunting during his family's residence [81, 82].

**The 1900 four-sister sighting:** Price's published account implied "sunlight" — a significant misrepresentation. At 9 p.m. on 28 July, the sun had set at 7:53 p.m. At that level of twilight, misidentification of a dark object or person is not improbable. The SPR argues the sisters may simply have misidentified a passing villager, vagabond, or poacher using the Rectory garden as a shortcut, who hurried away when approached [71–75].

**V. C. Wall's "nun sighting" (June 1929):** Wall thought he saw a shadowy figure on the Nun's Walk; he ran across to investigate. The same night, the same party "had a terrible shock" and "distinctly saw a white figure flitting about in the gloom" — which turned out to be the Smiths' maid [[mary-pearson|Mary Pearson]]. Wall published this false alarm in the *Daily Mirror* the following day under the headline "Midnight Apparition That Proved to Be a Maid" — a detail Price omitted in his MHH account, despite claiming to print Wall's article verbatim [22, 23].

**Mrs Lloyd Williams (February 1938):** Debunked in detail by the SPR — a product of the [[blue-book-of-instructions|Blue Book's]] suggestion to watch from the summer-house at dusk for "the black draped figure." Her companion C. Gordon Glover dismissed the sighting as imaginary in his own report, but Price promoted her as a valid witness. This was "the only alleged apparition during the whole of the observational period" [24–26, 80].

**The SPR's summary:** "A consistent feature of the early Borley 'apparitions' is the fact that almost without exception they were seen out of doors, where the possibility of the misinterpretation of natural objects seen in poor light is much greater than inside a room." [[rev-eric-smith|The Rev. G. Eric Smith]] himself once "saw what looked like a spectre of a white-clad monk in the rectory garden, afterwards discovering that it was the smoke from a bonfire" — an incident Price never published.

## Sceptical Positions (Summary)

- Critics suggest the apparitions visible during [[harry-bull|Harry Bull's]] incumbency were "illusions or perhaps hallucinations" attributable to "morbid imagination and evident interest in the supernormal."
- Gerald Bull (brother of Harry) dismissed the Nun as "figment of feminine imagination."
- The name "Nun's Walk" may have primed expectation; the legend was widely known among residents.
- Village boys played tricks in the dark, and [[mary-pearson|Mary Pearson]] is known to have put her apron over her head to mimic the nun as a joke [78].
- During Price's tenancy, the [[blue-book-of-instructions|Blue Book of Instructions]] explicitly told observers to watch the Nun's Walk at dusk for "a black draped figure" — arguably priming impressionable observers toward subjective hallucinations based on expectancy [25, 80].
