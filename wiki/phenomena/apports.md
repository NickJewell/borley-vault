# Apports

Objects materialised or brought into [[borley-rectory]] by apparent paranormal means — as distinct from objects moved or thrown as part of general [[poltergeist-activity|poltergeist activity]]. The BRC provides the most comprehensive systematic survey of Borley apports across all periods. In the Borley sources, "apports" are defined specifically as objects that materialize or are allegedly brought into a building by paranormal means; this distinguishes them from items already in the house that are merely moved [1]. Interpretations are sharply divided between believers who see them as spirit clues and skeptics who allege fraud or natural causes.

## Bull Incumbency

Only one documented case: the **French dictionary incident** from the mid-1880s, reported to [[harry-price]] by [[p-shaw-jeffrey]]. A French dictionary was reportedly lost and subsequently thrown onto Jeffrey's bedroom floor in the night [2–4]. [[canon-phythian-adams]] theorized that the spirit of a murdered French nun, [[marie-lairre]], had stolen the dictionary to learn English so she could later produce the English wall-writings [4, 5]. The skeptical SPR authors dismissed this entirely, attributing the event to a practical joke by the numerous Bull children [6].

## Smith Incumbency

During Price's first visit on 12 June 1929, pebbles and a piece of slate were projected down the main staircase. On a later visit (date disputed — either 27 June or 5–6 July 1929), several items seemingly appeared from nowhere alongside [[lucie-kaye]] and [[lord-charles-hope]]: pebbles, a piece of slate, a shower of door keys, and a brass "St. Ignatius" medallion minted in Rome circa 1700. Price later recorded the appearance of a French Roman Catholic Confirmation medal and a 1799 French Revolution badge. These items collectively constitute the [[borley-medals|Borley Medals]].

> **Contradiction:** Price suggested the medals were apports providing clues to a Catholic presence [10]. However, the SPR investigators ([[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], and [[trevor-hall]]) accused Price of fraud, arguing he originally planted the St. Ignatius medal from his own coin collection to support an early phantom-monk legend, then later suppressed its existence and introduced the two French medals to retroactively prove the French nun theory derived from 1937 séances [11–15]. Additionally, the Smiths' maid, [[mary-pearson]], claimed she saw Price throw a coin and confessed to throwing sugar herself [16, 17].

## Foyster Incumbency

Over 2,000 incidents of phenomena were recorded in fifteen months, of which a smaller subset qualify as true apports:

- **Bag of lavender**: undated, appeared soon after the Foysters arrived
- **Hymn books** (26 February 1931): books unknown to Foyster found over the kitchen range
- **Small tin trunk, china powder box and wedding ring** (10 March 1931): [[marianne-foyster]] found a gold wedding ring in her bathroom; it disappeared the following day [23]
- **Paper slips with "Marianne" written on them**: found lying around the house [19, 22]
- **Crockery egg-cups** (11 August 1931): during a séance held by the Foysters, W. John Braithwaite reportedly saw at least twenty-four old-fashioned coloured crockery egg-cups suddenly appear; twelve were thrown violently across the room, denting a door but falling without breaking [18]
- **Wine bottles** (13 November 1931): the most spectacular episode (multiple witnesses) — see [[bottles|Bottles entry]] for full account
- **Gold pendant and Miraculous Medal** (August 1943 in cellar): appeared during excavations

[[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] was absolutely convinced of the preternatural origin of the bottles [24]. Conversely, skeptics and SPR investigators believed [[marianne-foyster]] was consciously faking these phenomena — perhaps to convince her husband to leave the bleak Rectory, or to gain attention — and argued Whitehouse's testimony was unreliable or exaggerated [25–28].

## Price Tenancy

Several items appeared under circumstances that investigators at the time considered paranormal:

- **Blue ladies' jacket** (2 June 1937): Price and [[ellic-howe]] discovered a dirty, mouldy, moth-eaten dark blue serge lady's coat behind the Blue Room door; Price noted it had not been there during an inspection a fortnight earlier [29–31]. Observers later alleged the coat disappeared completely for a week and then returned [32, 33]. The SPR authors considered it a "trivial occurrence" manufactured into a mystery, suggesting it was a forgotten garment left by a former cleaner, or that Price had confused the doors of Room 6 and Room 7 [34–36]. Howe, however, maintained it appeared while they were inside the building [32].
- **Blue box containing pins** (25–26 June 1937): found by [[colonel-westland]] in cupboard
- **Steel nail file** (shortly after): found by RAF observers in the same cupboard; unknown to the Westlands
- **Three-inch square of rotten wood** ("touchwood apport") (18 July 1937): found by [[mark-kerr-pearse]] in the Sewing Room hearth; lacking soot, it appeared it had not fallen down the chimney [29]. It was later psychometrized by the vaudeville telepathist [[josef-kraus-marion|Josef Kraus ("Marion")]], who claimed it was tied to an old-fashioned building [29, 37].
- **Dry flattened remains of a frog** (5 August 1937): found by Kerr-Pearse in ground floor passage, claiming he had been the only occupant and it had not been there earlier [29]. [[canon-phythian-adams]] argued it was an "indicator" pointing toward a wet place — specifically, the cellar well-tank where human bones were later found [29, 38].
- **Gold 22-carat wedding ring** (19 May 1938, last night of tenancy): found by Price and [[geoffrey-motion]] in the Blue Room doorway; hallmarked Birmingham 1864 [23, 39]. Price speculated it might be the same ring Marianne found in 1931, linking it to the tragic history of the haunting's female victim [23, 39]. He did concede, however, that "the ring found its way into the Blue Room in a perfectly normal manner" [40].

> **Note (multiple witnesses):** Observer J. Burden noted that during high winds, pieces of rag and paper frequently blew down the chimneys, and explicitly warned that these mundane objects "might be mistaken for apports" by overly eager investigators [41].

## Disappearances

Several Borley items later went missing under suspicious circumstances from the University of London collection, including [[borley-medals|Medals B and C]] and the Miraculous Medal. [[ah-wesencraft|Alan Wesencraft]] confirmed their absence after the SPR investigators had borrowed and returned Price's files.

## Analysis

Price believed genuine apports distinguished themselves from poltergeist thrown objects by: (1) appearing from locations where they could not logically have originated; (2) appearing at moments of apparent quiescence. Critics note that most Borley "apports" occurred in conditions of poor control where normal explanations could not be conclusively ruled out. The gold wedding ring of May 1938 is considered by the BRC to be among the most impressive, given the circumstances (multiple searches of the building that day, two witnesses finding it in the last moments before the tenancy ended).
