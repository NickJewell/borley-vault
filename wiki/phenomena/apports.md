# Apports

Objects materialised or brought into [[borley-rectory]] by apparent paranormal means — as distinct from objects moved or thrown as part of general [[poltergeist-activity|poltergeist activity]]. The BRC provides the most comprehensive systematic survey of Borley apports across all periods.

## Bull Incumbency

Only one documented case: the **French dictionary incident** from the mid-1880s, reported to [[harry-price]] by [[p-shaw-jeffrey]]. A French dictionary appeared inexplicably in a room.

## Smith Incumbency

During Price's first visit on 12 June 1929, pebbles and a piece of slate were projected down the main staircase. On a later visit (date disputed — either 27 June or 5–6 July 1929), the main apport phenomenon of the Smith period occurred: the [[borley-medals|Borley Medals]] appeared at the bottom of the staircase along with stones and a collection of door keys.

## Foyster Incumbency

Over 2,000 incidents of phenomena were recorded in fifteen months, of which a smaller subset qualify as true apports:

- **Bag of lavender**: undated, appeared soon after the Foysters arrived
- **Hymn books** (26 February 1931): books unknown to Foyster found over the kitchen range
- **Small tin trunk, china powder box and wedding ring** (10 March 1931): the ring disappeared the following day
- **Paper slips with "Marianne" written on them**: found lying around the house
- **Wine bottles** (13 November 1931): the most spectacular episode; see [[bottles|Bottles entry]] for full account
- **Gold pendant and Miraculous Medal** (August 1943 in cellar): appeared during excavations

## Price Tenancy

Several items appeared under circumstances that investigators at the time considered paranormal:

- **Blue ladies' jacket** (early June 1937): see [[strange-coat-incident]]
- **Blue box containing pins** (25–26 June 1937): found by Colonel Westland in cupboard
- **Steel nail file** (shortly after): found by RAF observers in the same cupboard; unknown to the Westlands
- **Three-inch square of rotten wood** ("touchwood apport") (18 July 1937): found by [[mark-kerr-pearse]] in hearth
- **Dry flattened remains of a frog** (5 August 1937): found by Kerr-Pearse in ground floor passage
- **Gold 22-carat wedding ring** (19 May 1938, last night of tenancy): found by Price and Geoffrey Motion in the Blue Room doorway; hallmarked Birmingham 1864

## Disappearances

Several Borley items later went missing under suspicious circumstances from the University of London collection, including [[borley-medals|Medals B and C]] and the Miraculous Medal. Alan Wesencraft confirmed their absence after the SPR investigators had borrowed and returned Price's files.

## Analysis

Price believed genuine apports distinguished themselves from poltergeist thrown objects by: (1) appearing from locations where they could not logically have originated; (2) appearing at moments of apparent quiescence. Critics note that most Borley "apports" occurred in conditions of poor control where normal explanations could not be conclusively ruled out. The gold wedding ring of May 1938 is considered by the BRC to be among the most impressive, given the circumstances (multiple searches of the building that day, two witnesses finding it in the last moments before the tenancy ended).

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

In the Borley Rectory sources, "apports" are defined specifically as objects that materialize or are brought into a building by allegedly paranormal means, as distinct from items that are already in the house and merely moved as part of poltergeist activity [1]. 

The sources record several highly debated instances of apports across the different incumbencies, with interpretations sharply divided between believers who saw them as spirit clues and skeptics who alleged fraud or natural causes.

### **The Bull Incumbency (1863–1927)**
*   **The French Dictionary (c. 1885):** P. Shaw Jeffrey, a visiting undergraduate, reported that during his stay, a French dictionary was lost and subsequently thrown onto his bedroom floor in the night [2-4]. 
    *   *Proposed Explanations:* Canon W.J. Phythian-Adams (a proponent of the haunting) theorized that the spirit of a murdered French nun, "Marie Lairre," had stolen the dictionary to learn English so she could later produce the English "wall writings" [4, 5]. The skeptical Society for Psychical Research (SPR) authors of the *Borley Report* dismissed this entirely, attributing the event to a practical joke by the numerous Bull children [6].

### **The Smith Incumbency (1928–1929)**
*   **The Borley Medals and Keys (June/July 1929):** During Harry Price's early visits to the Rectory alongside his secretary Lucy Kaye and Lord Charles Hope (dates are disputed between 27 June and 5-6 July 1929), several items seemingly appeared from nowhere. These included pebbles, a piece of slate, a shower of door keys, and a brass "St. Ignatius" medallion minted in Rome circa 1700 [2, 7-9]. In his later books, Price also recorded the appearance of a French Roman Catholic Confirmation medal and a 1799 French Revolution badge [8, 10].
    *   *Proposed Explanations:* Price suggested the medals were apports providing clues to a Catholic presence [10]. However, the SPR investigators (Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) accused Price of blatant fraud. They argued Price originally planted the St. Ignatius medal from his own coin collection to support the early legend of a phantom monk, but later suppressed its existence in his books to introduce the two French medals, thus retroactively "proving" the new French nun theory derived from 1937 séances [11-15]. Additionally, the Smiths' maid, Mary Pearson, claimed she saw Price throw a coin and confessed to throwing sugar herself in competition [16, 17].

### **The Foyster Incumbency (1930–1935)**
*   **The Crockery Egg-Cups (11 August 1931):** During a séance held by the Foysters, W. John Braithwaite reported seeing at least twenty-four "curious old-fashioned coloured crockery egg-cups" suddenly appear. Twelve of them were thrown violently across the room, denting a door but falling to the floor without breaking [18]. 
*   **Materializing Wine Bottles (13 November 1931):** The Rev. Foyster’s wife, Marianne, the maid Katie, and Edwin Whitehouse (later Dom Richard) were in the kitchen when a bottle allegedly materialized. Whitehouse described seeing a bottle poise itself in mid-air near the ceiling, hover for a few seconds, and then crash to the floor [19-21]. Price later published that the bottle evolved from a "mushroom shape" into a bottle, though Whitehouse's original account lacked this detail [20].
*   **The "Marianne" Papers (1931):** Scraps of paper bearing Marianne Foyster’s name appeared lying around the house [19, 22].
*   **The First Wedding Ring (10 March 1931):** Marianne Foyster found a gold wedding ring in her bathroom, which subsequently disappeared the following day [23].
    *   *Proposed Explanations:* Whitehouse was absolutely convinced of the preternatural origin of the bottles [24]. Conversely, skeptics and SPR investigators believed Marianne Foyster was consciously faking these phenomena (perhaps to convince her husband to leave the bleak Rectory or to gain attention), and argued Whitehouse's testimony was unreliable or exaggerated [25-28].

### **The Price Tenancy (1937–1938)**
*   **The Strange Coat (2 June 1937):** While setting up a base room, Harry Price and Ellic Howe discovered a dirty, mouldy, moth-eaten dark blue serge lady's coat hanging behind the Blue Room door. Price noted it had not been there during an inspection a fortnight earlier [29-31]. Observers later alleged the coat disappeared completely for a week and then returned [32, 33].
    *   *Proposed Explanations:* Price called it an apport [30]. The SPR authors considered it a "trivial occurrence" manufactured into a mystery, suggesting it was simply a forgotten garment left by a former cleaner, or that Price had merely confused the doors of Room 6 and Room 7 [34-36]. Ellic Howe, however, maintained it appeared while they were inside the building and could not have been placed there naturally [32].
*   **The Touchwood (18 July 1937):** Official observer Mark Kerr-Pearse discovered a 3-inch square of rotten wood lying in the Sewing Room hearth. Lacking soot, it appeared it had not fallen down the chimney [29]. It was later psychometrized by the vaudeville telepathist Josef Kraus ("Marion"), who claimed it was tied to an old-fashioned building [29, 37].
*   **The Dead Frog (5 August 1937):** Kerr-Pearse found the flattened, dry remains of a frog in a ground floor passage, claiming he had been the only occupant of the house and it had not been there earlier [29]. 
    *   *Proposed Explanations:* Price used the frog to support his theories on the wall writings, while Canon Phythian-Adams argued it was an "indicator" pointing toward a wet place—specifically, the cellar well-tank where human bones were later found [29, 38].
*   **The Golden Apport (19 May 1938):** On the very last day of Price's tenancy, he and Geoffrey Motion made a final midnight inspection and found a 22-carat gold lady's wedding ring (hallmarked Birmingham 1864) lying in the doorway of the Blue Room [23, 39]. 
    *   *Proposed Explanations:* Price speculated it might be the same ring Marianne Foyster found in 1931, linking it to the tragic history of the haunting's female victim [23, 39]. However, Price did concede that "the ring found its way into the Blue Room in a perfectly normal manner" [40].
*   **False Apports (1937):** Observer J. Burden noted that during high winds, pieces of rag and paper frequently blew down the chimneys. He documented this explicitly to warn that these mundane objects "might be mistaken for apports" by overly eager investigators [41].
