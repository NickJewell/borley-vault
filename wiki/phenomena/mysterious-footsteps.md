# Mysterious Footsteps

Unexplained footsteps heard both inside and outside [[borley-rectory]]. One of the most persistently reported and broadly attested phenomena across multiple witnesses, including outright sceptics, spanning from the earliest days of the Victorian household through to the decades following its destruction by fire.

## Bull Incumbency

Mrs E. Byford (nursemaid, 1886): left her employment after just one month, having been awakened in the night by the sound of someone walking down the passage toward her door "suggesting that they were wearing slippers." She checked with the other maids, who denied leaving their rooms, convincing her the house was weirdly haunted [1–3].

[[ethel-bull|Miss Ethel Bull]] recorded that during her childhood, heavy footsteps were heard at approximately 10:30 p.m. every night. The steps would pass her bedroom door, walk along the landing, and stop outside the night nursery. When the footsteps ceased, three distinct taps were heard. She claimed this happened "at least a hundred times" [4].

**Walter Bull** — described as "utterly unbelieving" and dismissive of all indoor phenomena — stated he heard footsteps *following him* down the lane leading to the Rectory "on at least fifty occasions." He would turn around to look but never saw anyone [5, 6]. This is perhaps the most striking testimony: an avowed sceptic, fifty separate incidents, outdoors.

[[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] and a friend, after a service, heard "distinct and loud knocking sounds from outside, near the porch of the church" that "slowly travelled the whole way round the church" — a related auditory phenomenon.

## Smith Incumbency

[[rev-eric-smith|Rev. Guy Eric Smith]] and his wife [[mabel-smith|Mabel]] frequently heard "slow, dragging footsteps across the floor of an unoccupied room" [7, 8]. On one occasion, the Rev. Smith armed himself with a hockey stick and waited for the noises. Hearing the sound of slippered feet on the bare boards, he lashed out at the spot where the sound seemed to originate. The stick whistled through empty air, and the footsteps simply continued across the room [7–10]. This was independently witnessed by journalist [[vc-wall|V. C. Wall]] during his pre-Price visit.

## Foyster Incumbency

On the very day the Foysters moved in (16 October 1930), mysterious footsteps were heard collectively by [[lionel-foyster|Rev. Lionel Foyster]], [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]], their young adopted daughter Adelaide, and a workman [11, 12]. Lionel Foyster recorded in his diary that he would frequently hear footsteps in the hall or upstairs that he assumed belonged to Marianne; upon investigating, he would find she had not left the room she was sitting in downstairs. Conversely, Adelaide would hear footsteps, say "There's daddy," and go to look for him, only to find no one [11, 13].

## Price Tenancy and Official Observers

During Harry Price's year-long rental of the empty Rectory, independent observers recorded numerous auditory phenomena:

- **24/25 October 1937:** S. H. Glanville, [[roger-glanville|Roger Glanville]], and [[mark-kerr-pearse|Mark Kerr-Pearse]] were sitting on the landing in the dark at 2:00 a.m. when they heard "heavy muffled footsteps walk across the hall, just under us." They went downstairs immediately but found nothing except their colleague Alan Cuthbert, sound asleep in the Base Room [14, 15].
- **16 December 1937:** J. Burden sat in the Base Room and heard "shuffling footsteps" coming from the scullery, followed by the "sound of swishing garments as though someone were walking in upstairs hall" [16].
- **16–17 February 1938:** Four Oxford undergraduates heard footsteps at 12:17 a.m. coming from the kitchen, proceeding along the passage to the front hall, and moving toward the drawing room. They thought it was a fellow investigator, but found he was isolated in a distant part of the house [17].

## Post-Fire: Cambridge Commission

Even after the Rectory was gutted by fire in 1939, phenomena continued:

- **31 October 1941:** Investigators A. J. B. Robertson and I. P. Williams heard "three or four very distinct and heavy footsteps, as though a person was descending the pantry stairs" [18].
- **22 September 1942:** Members of the Cambridge Commission heard measured, steady, and heavy footsteps across the ceiling of the room above them, followed by bumps and dragging sounds. The noises moved across the room and stopped abruptly [19].
- **6–7 June 1944:** Three medical students sheltering in the ruined scullery heard steady, slow footsteps from the ceiling above, "as if a tired man were walking about in heavy boots" [20].

## The Cottage and Borley Churchyard

With the Rectory gone, footsteps were frequently reported in the adjacent church and the surviving Rectory Cottage:

- **2 August 1947:** [[james-turner|James Turner]], owner of the Cottage, sat in the church porch after midnight and heard footsteps coming up the path accompanied by a "swishing skirt." He observed that "one footstep was heavier than the other," indicating a pronounced limp. No one was visible in the bright moonlight [21–23].
- **Autumn 1947:** Miss Backhouse, a local headmistress, walked to the church altar and heard limping footsteps follow her. When she turned to leave, the limping footsteps followed her back to the door [24–26].
- **4–5 March 1950:** Investigator [[peter-underwood]] and Laurence Gafford stayed overnight in the Turners' lounge at the Cottage. At 3:15 a.m. and again at 4:15 a.m., they both heard heavy footsteps and muffled voices directly above them. The Turners, sleeping in that exact room, later confirmed they had slept soundly without moving [27, 28].
- **November 1987:** Thomas Musk and three friends camping near the church heard crisp footsteps walking around their tent in the frost at 4:00 a.m. They unfastened the tent flap but found the area completely deserted [29].

Mr J. Harley: "often felt as if he were being watched and followed in the Rectory; not by anyone hostile but by someone who took rather more than a cursory interest in his movements." Published in the *East Anglian Daily Times*, 15 March 1956.

## Proposed Explanations

**Mundane and skeptical explanations:**

The highly skeptical [[spr|SPR]] report emphasised that the enclosed courtyard of the Rectory created bizarre acoustics. Sounds made outside — such as the caretakers Mr. and Mrs. Arbon walking in the yard or operating a noisy hand-pump late at night — echoed loudly inside the empty Rectory, creating the distinct illusion that the footsteps were echoing down interior corridors [30–33]. Later, [[mabel-smith|Mrs. Mabel Smith]] drastically changed her story, completely denying any supernatural belief and emphatically stating that the dragging footsteps her husband chased with a hockey stick were simply caused by "enormous rats" scratching the boards [34, 35]. Other observers noted that "scrabbling" sounds often mistaken for padding feet were mice [36]. Natural decrepitation and shifting timbers of an old, drafty house, along with regular intrusions by local villagers or pranksters, accounted for many internal and external noises [37–39].

**Paranormal perspectives:**

[[harry-price|Harry Price]], [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]], and the official observers strongly believed the footsteps were paranormal. The sounds were often heard collectively by multiple witnesses simultaneously, sometimes in locked, sealed rooms where human entry was impossible, making subjective hallucination highly unlikely [40–42]. The "swishing" sounds were universally attributed by believers to the long robes of the phantom nun or a monk [16, 21, 43]. The "limping" footsteps heard heavily in the churchyard were theorised by some to belong to the Rev. [[henry-bull|Henry Dawson Ellis Bull]] (who suffered a pronounced limp in his later years) or the Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] (who was crippled by severe rheumatism) [44, 45].

## Significance

The footstep reports are notable because they extend to witnesses with no interest in believing (Walter Bull), both inside and outside the building, across multiple decades.
