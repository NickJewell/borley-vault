# The Headless Man

A recurring visual apparition of headless (or partially concealed) figures reported at [[borley-rectory]] by multiple witnesses across several decades. The motif is deeply rooted in the foundational legend of the site, which claims that a monastery groom and a local nun attempted to elope in a horse-drawn carriage; they were subsequently captured, and the groom (or two coachmen) were beheaded for their crimes [1, 2].

## Harry Bull's Encounters (c. 1880s–1920s)

The most famous incident occurred when [[harry-bull|Rev. Harry Bull]] was in the garden with his retriever, Juvenal [3, 4]. According to the original notes taken by [[lucie-kaye|Harry Price's secretary, Lucie Kaye]], in June 1929, Bull's dog was terrified and growled at something hidden by the fruit trees. Bull saw only a **"man's legs"**, assumed it was a poacher, and followed the figure to a locked postern gate, where he watched the legs disappear through it [5].

> **Contradiction:** When [[harry-price]] published his account in *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940), he significantly altered the story, writing that when the figure cleared the bushes, Bull saw that the legs "belonged to a man who was headless" [6, 7]. The SPR authors argue Price deliberately inserted the word "headless" to manufacture a more thrilling, paranormal narrative [16, 17]. Peter Underwood later made a scripting error in a 1970s slide lecture, accidentally transforming Price's headless phantom into a "legless man" who vanished before the Rector's eyes [6, 8].

Bull also claimed to have witnessed the [[phantom-coach|phantom coach]] associated with the monastic legend, telling a friend, the Rev. Harry Carpenter, that he had stepped off the road to let a spectral carriage pass, and that he distinctly saw "one man or two on the box, driving; they had no heads, only hands and the lower part of the body" [9].

The account of the headless garden apparition was told by [[ethel-bull]] to [[harry-price|Harry Price]].

## Mary Pearson's Sighting (1928–1929)

During the incumbency of [[rev-eric-smith|Rev. Guy Eric Smith]], the young Rectory maid, [[mary-pearson|Mary Pearson]], told Price in June 1929 that she had seen the phantom coach on the lawn, and more remarkably, that she had seen a "man, headless, behind a tree." She stated she chased the headless figure into the garden until it disappeared [10].

When SPR investigators interviewed Pearson in 1952, she retracted this story entirely. The SPR researchers concluded that her original account was either a joke or entirely untrue [11, 12]. [[mabel-smith|Mrs. Mabel Smith]] believed the sighting was the result of village gossip combined with a practical joke — Pearson's boyfriend, Fred Tatum, used to walk around the Rectory grounds in the dark with "his coat right over his head," easily creating the illusion of a headless figure [11, 14].

## The Phantom Hitchhiker (November 1950)

Mrs. Jean Clarke, a former WWII spy, claimed that while driving near Borley on a dark, drizzly winter night in November 1950, her dog began to howl. She looked over to find a **headless man wearing an old-fashioned cape-collared coat** sitting in her passenger seat [13]. Assuming he was just a "village yokel" wanting a lift, she bravely asked the headless figure for directions to Sudbury; the apparition gestured ahead, and when she stopped the car, he passed straight through the door and vanished [13]. This account was published in the *Sunday Express* in 1956.

## Headlessness as a Motif and Proposed Explanations

The lack of a head is a recurrent motif in many ghost stories, perhaps introduced in retelling to add to the horror of such an apparition. The [[phantom-coach|phantom coachman]] is also headless, reinforcing the motif.

**The poacher theory:** Modern commentators and local historians suggest that Bull's original assumption was correct. An intruder or poacher stealing apples from the orchard, anxious to avoid recognition by the Rector, would likely slip his jacket over his head [15]. In the twilight, this mundane act of concealment would make a trespasser look exactly like a headless man or a cowled nun [15].

**The "Thought-Form" theory:** Percy Pigott proposed in 1941 that the apparitions were a "mirage" fuelled by the site's tragic past. If a coachman was genuinely beheaded in the original tragedy, he "might very likely think of himself as headless after passing over," and the spirit would then project his own perceived, decapitated self-image into the minds of living observers [18–20].

## Animal Reaction

Bull's dog's behaviour is sometimes cited as evidence for objective phenomena (animals cannot be psychologically primed by legend). This pattern recurs across the Borley record; see also [[phantom-cat]].
