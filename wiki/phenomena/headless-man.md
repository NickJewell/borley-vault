# The Headless Man

Visual apparition reported by [[harry-bull|Rev. Harry Bull]]: a headless male figure in the garden.

## Account

Harry Bull was in the garden with his retriever when the dog "suddenly started to howl and cowered in fright." Turning in the direction of the dog's gaze, Bull saw the *legs* of a figure hidden from view by branches of heavily-laden fruit bushes. As the legs moved clear of the bushes, a headless figure emerged. No explanation was offered.

The account was told by [[ethel-bull]] to [[harry-price|Harry Price]].

## Headlessness as a Motif

The authors note: "The lack of a head is, of course, a recurrent motif in many ghost stories, perhaps to add to the horror of such an apparition." This is a fair caution — it raises the question of whether the feature was genuinely observed or introduced in retelling. The [[phantom-coach|phantom coachman]] is also headless, reinforcing the motif.

## Animal Reaction

The dog's behaviour is sometimes cited as evidence for objective phenomena (animals cannot be psychologically primed by legend). Worth tracking across later chapters.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

The apparition of a **headless man** (often associated with a "headless coachman" or even mistakenly referred to as a "legless man") is a recurring element in the Borley Rectory hauntings. The visual phenomena are deeply rooted in the foundational legend of the site, which claims that a monastery groom and a local nun attempted to elope in a horse-drawn carriage; they were subsequently captured, and the groom (or two coachmen) were beheaded for their crimes [1], [2]. 

A thorough reference profile drawn from the Borley sources reveals the following specific instances, witness descriptions, and evolving explanations regarding the headless figures:

**The Rev. Harry Bull's Encounters (c. 1880s–1920s)**
The Rev. Harry Foyster Bull (Rector of Borley from 1892 to 1927) was the most prominent witness to headless phenomena, though the exact nature of what he saw became heavily distorted over time.
*   **The Figure in the Orchard:** The most famous incident occurred when Harry Bull was in the Rectory garden with his retriever, Juvenal [3], [4]. According to the original notes taken by Harry Price's secretary, Lucie Kaye, in June 1929, Bull's dog was terrified and growled at something hidden by the fruit trees [5]. Bull only saw a **"man's legs"**, assumed it was a poacher, and followed the figure to a locked postern gate, where he watched the legs disappear through the gate [5]. 
*   **The Embellishment and the "Legless Man":** When Harry Price published his account of this event in his 1940 book *The Most Haunted House in England*, he significantly altered the story. Price wrote that when the figure cleared the bushes, Bull saw that the legs **"belonged to a man who was headless"** [6], [7]. To add further confusion to the historical record, investigator Peter Underwood made a scripting error in a 1970s slide lecture, accidentally transforming Price's headless phantom into a **"legless man"** who vanished before the Rector's eyes [6], [8].
*   **The Headless Coachmen:** Bull also claimed to have witnessed the phantom coach associated with the monastic legend. He described to a friend, the Rev. Harry Carpenter, stepping off the road to let a spectral carriage pass, noting that he distinctly saw **"one man or two on the box, driving; they had no heads, only hands and the lower part of the body"** [9]. 

**Mary Pearson and the Headless Figure (1928–1929)**
During the incumbency of the Rev. Guy Eric Smith, the young Rectory maid, Mary Pearson, reported several dramatic encounters.
*   **The Sighting:** In June 1929, Pearson told Harry Price that she had seen the phantom coach on the lawn, but more remarkably, she claimed to have seen a **"man, headless, behind a tree"** [10]. She stated that she chased the headless figure into the garden until it disappeared [10]. 
*   **The Retraction:** When investigators from the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) interviewed Pearson in 1952, she retracted this story entirely, with the SPR researchers concluding that her original account was either a joke or entirely untrue [11], [12]. 

**The Phantom Hitchhiker (November 1950)**
Decades after the Rectory was destroyed, a highly sensational account of a headless man was published in the *Sunday Express* in 1956. 
*   **The Sighting:** Mrs. Jean Clarke, a former WWII spy, claimed that while driving near Borley on a dark, drizzly winter night in November 1950, her dog began to howl [13]. She looked over to find a **headless man wearing an old-fashioned cape-collared coat** sitting in her passenger seat [13]. 
*   **The Interaction:** Assuming he was just a "village yokel" wanting a lift, she bravely asked the headless figure for directions to Sudbury; the apparition gestured ahead, and when she stopped the car, he passed straight through the door and vanished [13]. 

**Proposed Explanations and Source Perspectives**
The Borley sources offer several contrasting explanations for the headless sightings, ranging from mundane misidentifications to complex spiritualist theories:
*   **The "Coat Over the Head" Trick:** The Rev. Guy Smith's wife, Mabel, firmly believed that Mary Pearson's sighting was the result of village gossip combined with a practical joke. She noted that Mary's boyfriend, Fred Tatum, used to walk around the Rectory grounds in the dark and rain with **"his coat right over his head,"** easily creating the illusion of a headless or hooded figure [11], [14]. 
*   **The Poacher Theory:** Modern commentators and local historians suggest that Harry Bull's original assumption was correct. An intruder or poacher stealing apples from the orchard, anxious to avoid recognition by the Rector, would likely slip his jacket over his head [15]. In the twilight, this mundane act of concealment would make a trespasser look exactly like a headless man or a cowled nun [15].
*   **Sensationalism and Fraud:** The skeptical authors of the *Borley Report* point out that Harry Price deliberately omitted the "poacher" detail from his original notes and inserted the word "headless" into his published books specifically to manufacture a more thrilling, paranormal narrative for his readers [16], [17], [12]. 
*   **The "Thought-Form" Theory:** For those who believed the haunting was genuine, investigator Percy Pigott proposed an occult explanation in 1941. He theorized that the apparitions were a "mirage" fueled by the site's tragic past [18]. Pigott suggested that if a coachman was genuinely beheaded in the original tragedy, he **"might very likely think of himself as headless after passing over"** [19], [20]. According to this theory, the spirit simply projected his own perceived, decapitated self-image into the minds of living observers [20].
