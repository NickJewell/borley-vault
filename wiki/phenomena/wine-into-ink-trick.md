# Wine into Ink Trick

Term given to an incident that occurred during the [[foyster-incumbency]] on 13 October 1931 — part of [[harry-price]]'s visit to [[borley-rectory]] accompanied by [[kathleen-goldney]] and Mrs Henry Richards.

## The Incident

The party brought two bottles of wine — a Burgundy and a Sauterne — for supper at the Rectory. In *MHH*, Price described the discovery:

> "I asked for glasses and Mrs Foyster fetched these from the kitchen quarters. I drew the corks of the two bottles, and handed the Burgundy to Mrs Foyster, who poured a quantity into a glass. Consternation! Our beautiful ruby Chambertin had turned to jet-black ink!!"

The second bottle had also been tampered with and reeked of eau de cologne.

## Responsibility Debate

The wine-into-ink trick was almost certainly a conjuror's effect, not a paranormal event. The question is who performed it: [[marianne-foyster]] or [[harry-price]].

### The Marianne Theory

Price was already suspicious of Marianne that evening and came to the conclusion at the time that she was responsible for the disturbances. Her access to the bottles between the time they arrived and the time they were opened, combined with her observed behaviour during the evening (she was upstairs uncontrolled when a wine bottle crashed down the stairs), supports this view. The [[payne-family|Paynes]] and the Hon. Everard Fielding shared this impression.

[[iris-owen]] and Pauline Mitchell in the [[nhf|New Horizons Foundation report]] (1979) included a statement that "Marianne says that they [Marianne and her husband] both recognised this as a conjuring trick of Price's to 'help things along' as she put it."

### The Price Theory

In an interview with the [[spr-investigators|SPR investigators]] in early July 1949, [[mabel-smith]] described a similar incident during Price's dinner visit to the Rectory during the Smith period in 1929: water in a guest's glass turned to ink, and the guest believed Price might have done it. The SPR authors used this to argue Price performed the trick on both occasions.

## Assessment

The BRC points out that this "evidence" is entirely circumstantial. It depends solely on [[mabel-smith]]'s testimony, which has proven conflicting and unreliable throughout, and on Marianne's own claim (to Owen) that she and Lionel saw through Price's trick — which given the inaccuracies and contradictions of her later recollections, cannot be treated as definitive.

[[robert-wood]] noted the wine-into-ink as "a crude conjuror's effect arranged to reinforce to visiting experts the wild claims of haunting." Whether Price or Marianne performed it, it represents one of the less edifying episodes of the Rectory story — exactly the kind of manipulated theatricality that made the Borley case so difficult to evaluate properly.
