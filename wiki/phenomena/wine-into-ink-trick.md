# Wine into Ink Trick

Term given to an incident that occurred during the [[foyster-incumbency]] on 13 October 1931 — part of [[harry-price]]'s visit to [[borley-rectory]] accompanied by [[kathleen-goldney]] and Mrs Henry Richards.

## The Incident

The party brought two bottles of wine — a Burgundy and a Sauterne — for supper at the Rectory. In *MHH*, Price described the discovery:

> "I asked for glasses and Mrs Foyster fetched these from the kitchen quarters. I drew the corks of the two bottles, and handed the Burgundy to Mrs Foyster, who poured a quantity into a glass. Consternation! Our beautiful ruby Chambertin had turned to jet-black ink!!"

The second bottle had also been tampered with and reeked of eau de cologne.

## Responsibility Debate

The wine-into-ink trick was almost certainly a conjuror's effect, not a paranormal event. The question is who performed it: [[marianne-foyster]] or [[harry-price]].

### The Marianne Theory

Price was already suspicious of Marianne that evening and came to the conclusion at the time that she was responsible for the disturbances. Her access to the bottles between the time they arrived and the time they were opened, combined with her observed behaviour during the evening (she was upstairs uncontrolled when a wine bottle crashed down the stairs), supports this view. The [[payne-family|Paynes]] and the Hon. Everard Fielding shared this impression.

[[iris-owen]] and Pauline Mitchell in the [[nhf|New Horizons Foundation report]] (1979) included a statement that "Marianne says that they [Marianne and her husband] both recognised this as a conjuring trick of Price's to 'help things along' as she put it."

### The Price Theory

In an interview with the [[spr-investigators|SPR investigators]] in early July 1949, [[mabel-smith]] described a similar incident during Price's dinner visit to the Rectory during the Smith period in 1929: water in a guest's glass turned to ink, and the guest believed Price might have done it. The SPR authors used this to argue Price performed the trick on both occasions.

## Assessment

The BRC points out that this "evidence" is entirely circumstantial. It depends solely on [[mabel-smith]]'s testimony, which has proven conflicting and unreliable throughout, and on Marianne's own claim (to Owen) that she and Lionel saw through Price's trick — which given the inaccuracies and contradictions of her later recollections, cannot be treated as definitive.

[[robert-wood]] noted the wine-into-ink as "a crude conjuror's effect arranged to reinforce to visiting experts the wild claims of haunting." Whether Price or Marianne performed it, it represents one of the less edifying episodes of the Rectory story — exactly the kind of manipulated theatricality that made the Borley case so difficult to evaluate properly.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**The "Wine into Ink" Trick** (also referred to as the "Water into Ink" incident) is one of the most infamous and hotly contested alleged poltergeist phenomena at Borley Rectory. Instead of being accepted as a genuine supernatural event, it was almost universally recognized by the participants as a deliberate conjuring trick; however, the sources fiercely debate *who* was responsible for performing it.

A review of the Borley sources reveals two separate claims regarding this trick, alongside deeply divided perspectives on who the culprit was:

### **1. The Primary Incident: October 13, 1931 (The Foyster Incumbency)**
*   **The Witnesses:** Harry Price, Kathleen Goldney, Mrs. Henry Richards, Miss May Walker, James Ballantyne (a chauffeur), and the Rev. Lionel and Marianne Foyster [1, 2].
*   **The Event:** Price and his council members were visiting the Rectory and sat down to a supper consisting of food prepared by the Foysters and a luncheon basket brought by Mrs. Richards [3, 4]. The basket included two bottles of wine: a Burgundy and a Sauterne [4].
*   **The Description:** According to Harry Price’s account, he drew the corks from the bottles and handed the Burgundy to Marianne Foyster to pour [4]. As she poured it into a glass, the ruby wine turned jet-black like ink [4, 5]. Simultaneously, Miss Walker discovered that the Sauterne she was serving smelled strongly of eau-de-cologne [6, 7]. Upon inspection, the remaining wine inside the bottles was perfectly normal, indicating the trick had occurred inside the glasses [7]. Kathleen Goldney reportedly roared with laughter at what was obviously a crude conjuring trick [8, 9].

### **2. The Alleged Secondary Incident: 1929 (The Smith Incumbency)**
*   **The Witnesses:** The Rev. Guy E. Smith, Mabel Smith, Harry Price, and unnamed dinner guests [10, 11].
*   **The Event:** In a 1949 statement submitted to the Society for Psychical Research (SPR), Mabel Smith claimed that twenty years earlier, during a dinner party at the Rectory, a guest sitting next to Price remarked that the Borley phenomena could be faked by a clever man [10, 11]. Shortly afterward, the water in that guest's glass inexplicably turned into ink [10, 11]. Mrs. Smith stated that Price called it a poltergeist phenomenon, but the guest quietly suspected Price of performing a trick [11]. 

### **Proposed Explanations and Source Perspectives**

**1. Harry Price's Perspective: Fraud by the Foysters**
Harry Price firmly believed the 1931 incident was a crude fraud orchestrated by Marianne Foyster [12, 13]. Price noted that the glasses had been supplied from the kitchen by Mrs. Foyster, giving her the opportunity to tamper with them [3, 4]. Price conducted tests with the household ink and found he could perfectly replicate the effect, concluding that ordinary ink had simply been placed in the glass beforehand [14]. Price, who liked to live in style, was annoyed that his fine wine had been ruined and openly accused the Foysters of cheating, causing a severe rift that resulted in Price leaving Borley on bad terms the next day [3, 12, 15, 16]. Other observers, such as Mrs. E.E. Payne of Borley Place and the Hon. Everard Fielding, agreed with Price that Marianne Foyster was responsible [17].

**2. Marianne Foyster's Perspective: Fraud by Harry Price**
Decades later, in a 1958 interview with private detective Robert Swanson, Marianne flatly denied causing the trick [18]. She stated that Price had insisted they drink wine, but Lionel Foyster refused for health reasons and she refused because she disliked the company [18]. Marianne asserted that she immediately recognized it as a magic trick performed by Price himself to "help things along" [18, 19]. She noted that Price had openly boasted about being an amateur conjurer and a member of the Magician's Club, so she assumed it was just one of his sleight-of-hand tricks and found it more amusing than supernatural [18, 20]. 

**3. The Skeptical / SPR Perspective: Fraud by Harry Price**
The authors of the highly critical *Borley Report* (Eric Dingwall, Kathleen Goldney, and Trevor Hall) seized upon the ink trick to portray Price as a fraud [21]. They noted that turning liquid into ink is a standard magic trick accomplished by secretly dropping a small, easily obtainable pellet into a glass [22]. Given that Price possessed one of the world's largest libraries on conjuring, they argued he was the most likely person to know and execute this trick [22]. The SPR investigators also heavily relied on Mabel Smith's 1949 testimony about the 1929 "water to ink" incident to argue that Price was the only person present on both occasions, thus proving he was a serial trickster artificially manufacturing phenomena at Borley [22, 23].

**4. Robert J. Hastings' Defense of Price: Flawed Memory**
In his 1969 defense of Harry Price, investigator Robert J. Hastings dismantled the SPR's reliance on Mabel Smith's story [24]. Hastings unearthed an exchange of letters from January 1932 in which Price described the recent 1931 Foyster "wine into ink" incident to the Rev. Guy Smith [25, 26]. Smith replied with jocular surprise, joking about Price's ire at being splashed with ink [26]. Hastings pointed out that if a similar water-into-ink trick had actually happened to the Smiths at their own dinner table in 1929, the Rev. Smith would surely have recognized it and mentioned the similarity in his reply [27]. Hastings concluded that the 1929 incident never happened, and that Mabel Smith had simply conflated her later knowledge of the 1931 Foyster event with her own memories in order to retrospectively smear Price [24, 27, 28].
