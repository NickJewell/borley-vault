# Wine into Ink Trick

Term given to an incident that occurred during the [[foyster-incumbency|Foyster incumbency]] on 13 October 1931 — part of [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s visit to [[borley-rectory]] accompanied by [[kathleen-goldney]] and Mrs Henry Richards.

## The Incident

The party brought two bottles of wine — a Burgundy and a Sauterne — for supper at the Rectory. Witnesses included Harry Price, [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen Goldney]], Mrs. Henry Richards, Miss May Walker, James Ballantyne (a chauffeur), and the Rev. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel]] and [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] [1, 2]. Price described the discovery:

> "I asked for glasses and Mrs Foyster fetched these from the kitchen quarters. I drew the corks of the two bottles, and handed the Burgundy to Mrs Foyster, who poured a quantity into a glass. Consternation! Our beautiful ruby Chambertin had turned to jet-black ink!!"

The second bottle had also been tampered with and reeked of eau de cologne [6, 7]. Upon inspection, the remaining wine inside the bottles was perfectly normal, indicating the trick had occurred inside the glasses [7]. [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen Goldney]] reportedly roared with laughter at what was obviously a crude conjuring trick [8, 9].

The wine-into-ink trick was almost certainly a conjuror's effect, not a paranormal event. The question is who performed it: [[marianne-foyster]] or [[harry-price]].

## The Marianne Theory

Price was already suspicious of Marianne that evening and came to the conclusion that she was responsible for the disturbances. Her access to the bottles between the time they arrived and the time they were opened, combined with her observed behaviour during the evening (she was upstairs uncontrolled when a wine bottle crashed down the stairs), supports this view [12, 13]. The [[payne-family|Paynes]] and the Hon. Everard Fielding shared this impression [17]. Price conducted tests with the household ink and found he could perfectly replicate the effect, concluding that ordinary ink had simply been placed in the glass beforehand [14]. Price was annoyed that his fine wine had been ruined and openly accused the Foysters of cheating, causing a severe rift that resulted in his leaving Borley on bad terms the next day [3, 12, 15, 16].

## The Price Theory

In an interview with the SPR investigators in early July 1949, [[mabel-smith]] described a similar incident during Price's dinner visit to the Rectory during the Smith period in 1929: water in a guest's glass turned to ink, and the guest believed Price might have done it. The SPR authors used this to argue Price performed the trick on both occasions [22, 23].

In a 1958 interview with private detective [[robert-swanson|Robert Swanson]], Marianne flatly denied causing the trick. She stated that Price had insisted they drink wine, but Lionel Foyster refused for health reasons and she refused because she disliked the company [18]. Marianne asserted that she immediately recognized it as a magic trick performed by Price to "help things along," noting that Price had openly boasted about being an amateur conjurer and a member of the Magician's Club [18, 19, 20].

The SPR authors ([[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], and [[trevor-hall]]) argued that turning liquid into ink is a standard magic trick accomplished by secretly dropping a small, easily obtainable pellet into a glass, and that given Price possessed one of the world's largest libraries on conjuring, he was the most likely person to know and execute it [22].

## Assessment — Hastings's Defence

In his 1969 defence of Price, investigator [[robert-hastings|Robert J. Hastings]] dismantled the SPR's reliance on Mabel Smith's story about the alleged 1929 incident [24]. Hastings unearthed an exchange of letters from January 1932 in which Price described the recent 1931 Foyster "wine into ink" incident to the Rev. Guy Smith [25, 26]. Smith replied with jocular surprise, joking about Price's ire at being splashed with ink [26]. Hastings pointed out that if a similar water-into-ink trick had actually happened to the Smiths at their own dinner table in 1929, the Rev. Smith would surely have recognized it and mentioned the similarity in his reply [27]. Hastings concluded that the 1929 incident never happened, and that Mabel Smith had simply conflated her later knowledge of the 1931 Foyster event with her own memories in order to retrospectively smear Price [24, 27, 28].

The BRC points out that this "evidence" is entirely circumstantial. It depends solely on [[mabel-smith|Mrs. Smith's]] testimony, which has proven conflicting and unreliable throughout, and on Marianne's own claim (to [[iris-owen|Owen]]) that she and Lionel saw through Price's trick — which, given the inaccuracies and contradictions of her later recollections, cannot be treated as definitive.

[[robert-wood|Robert Wood]] noted the wine-into-ink as "a crude conjuror's effect arranged to reinforce to visiting experts the wild claims of haunting." Whether Price or Marianne performed it, it represents one of the less edifying episodes of the Rectory story — exactly the kind of manipulated theatricality that made the Borley case so difficult to evaluate properly.
