# Stiletto Incident

Title given to an episode of poltergeist activity alleged to have occurred in the presence of [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] and [[marianne-foyster]] at [[borley-rectory]] on 8 June 1931 — his very first visit to the Rectory, on which he had been summoned by [[lionel-foyster]].

## The Event

Whitehouse arrived to find clothes and other items scattered around the main stairs, and Mrs Foyster apparently ill and confined to bed upstairs. While [[lionel-foyster]] crossed to the church, Whitehouse sat with Marianne, who was lying on her back with her hands under the bedclothes.

In his written account:

> "We had been talking for perhaps ten minutes when I suddenly saw her start. At the same time, I felt something land lightly on my lap, coming apparently from behind me. I took hold of the object. It was a brass stiletto about eight or nine inches in length and weighing, I should imagine, not much less than eight ounces. I was told that it was a paper knife belonging to Mr. Foyster and was always kept in the study below."

Whitehouse was confident that Marianne could not have thrown the stiletto without him noticing, even if she had been concealing it under the bedclothes. He had not seen the paper knife on the floor in the time since entering the room. The incident profoundly affected Whitehouse — he felt he had objective proof that paranormal phenomena were taking place at Borley. This was the event [[robert-hastings]] identified as the critical turning point in Price's "passage to belief" about Foyster-period genuineness.

## SPR Attack

In his assessment in the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]], [[trevor-hall]] made much of the fact that the stiletto incident was technically in two parts: (1) Marianne's apparent reaction and claim that the knife had flown by itself; (2) Whitehouse's statement that the stiletto landed in his lap in a way that was impossible for Marianne to have faked without him seeing.

Hall argued that Whitehouse based his belief in the haunting on Marianne's testimony rather than his own observations. In a letter written after *MHH* was published, Whitehouse mentioned to Price that he "did not witness No. 5" — which Hall interpreted as meaning Whitehouse did not see the flight of the stiletto but only found it in his lap. Whitehouse responded to this charge emphatically in his 1956 statement to [[peter-underwood]]:

> "I SHALL NEVER GO BACK ON WHAT I SAID ... re stiletto, bottles and tumbler incidents."

He emphasised that he had not seen the stiletto in the room before; that Marianne could not have thrown it without him seeing her move; and that "the stiletto incident is actually in two parts — the first being the testimony of Marianne, that the paper knife rose by itself; and the second comprising Edwin Whitehouse's statement that the stiletto landed in his lap in such a way that it was impossible for Marianne to have cheated without being seen."

## The Novena Connection

Whitehouse's June 1931 visits were in the context of a novena — nine days of prayers — that he undertook to address the Rectory disturbances. On 16 June, during the novena's conclusion, the [[wall-writings|Writing No. 7]] "Get Light Mass and Prayers Here" appeared on the wall — witnessed by Whitehouse and Marianne while they were together. The stiletto incident and the wall writing are thus closely connected as part of the same intensive Whitehouse period.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**The Stiletto Incident** is one of the most famous and fiercely debated events of alleged poltergeist activity at Borley Rectory, marking a turning point in the beliefs of key witness Edwin Whitehouse. 

**The Date and Witnesses**
The event took place on the morning of **June 8, 1931** [1-3]. The only two people present in the room during the manifestation were **Marianne Foyster** (the Rector's wife) and **Edwin Whitehouse** (nephew of the local churchwarden, who later became a Benedictine monk known as Dom Richard Whitehouse) [1, 4]. 

**The Incident and Witness Descriptions**
According to the detailed written account provided by Whitehouse:
*   The Rev. Lionel Foyster had left the house to visit the church, leaving Whitehouse to sit with Marianne, who was unwell and confined to a camp bed in an upstairs bedroom [4]. 
*   Whitehouse was sitting on the edge of the bed, noting that **Marianne was lying on her back with her hands firmly tucked under the bedclothes drawn up to her neck** [1, 4, 5]. 
*   After about ten minutes of conversation, Whitehouse suddenly saw Marianne "start" or flinch in alarm [5, 6]. 
*   At that exact moment, **Whitehouse felt a heavy object land lightly in his lap, coming apparently from behind him** [5, 6]. 
*   The object was a **brass stiletto (or paper knife), measuring eight or nine inches in length and weighing nearly eight ounces**, which Whitehouse was told was normally kept downstairs in the Rector's study [5, 6]. 
*   When Whitehouse asked Marianne why she had started, she claimed she had watched the stiletto **rise up from the floor behind him, perform one or two "curious convolutions in the air," and then settle gently onto his lap** [6-8]. 
*   Convinced he had witnessed a genuine supernatural event, Whitehouse asked permission to recite the Rosary and sprinkle the room with holy water, which he did with Marianne joining in the prayers [3, 4, 8].

**Proposed Explanations and Source Perspectives**
The sources reveal deeply divided interpretations of the event:

*   **Edwin Whitehouse (Preternatural/Diabolic Activity):** Whitehouse remained absolutely certain of the event's authenticity for decades, citing it as the moment he "never doubted the objectivity and reality of the paranormal happenings at Borley" [7]. He vehemently rejected later accusations that Marianne had faked it, insisting that he was looking directly at her when the object landed and that her hands never left the covers [8-10]. To his Catholic mind, the phenomenon was preternatural and "diabolic," which prompted his immediate use of holy water [10].
*   **The SPR Investigators (Fraud/Trickery):** The skeptical authors of the *Borley Report* (Trevor Hall, Eric Dingwall, and Kathleen Goldney) dismissed the incident entirely as a magic trick orchestrated by Marianne. They proposed a "simple normal explanation": **Marianne deliberately diverted Whitehouse's attention by pretending to startle, giving her the momentary distraction needed to throw the stiletto herself** [7]. They heavily criticized Whitehouse for credulously accepting Marianne's uncorroborated story about the knife performing mid-air convolutions [7, 11]. Skeptics viewed Whitehouse as an emotionally vulnerable, highly-strung young man who was easily duped by Mrs. Foyster [4, 12, 13].
*   **The Poltergeist Focus Theory:** Investigators Iris Owen and Pauline Mitchell proposed an alternative psychological explanation. They theorized that isolated spontaneous poltergeist phenomena are frequently triggered as a release of suppressed emotion, and suggested that **Edwin Whitehouse himself—rather than Marianne—might have been the unconscious epicenter of the activity**, as he "was certainly the kind of person to become the focus of such events" [14].
*   **Marianne Foyster's Denial:** When interviewed in 1958 by private detective Robert Swanson, **Marianne flatly denied the entire episode**. Upon being read Whitehouse's account, she claimed she had no memory of a stiletto incident, stated that she had never even seen an eight-ounce brass stiletto, and insisted that the only paper knife they owned at the Rectory was made of flat ivory [15, 16].
