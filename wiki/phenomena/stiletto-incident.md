# Stiletto Incident

Title given to an episode of poltergeist activity alleged to have occurred in the presence of [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] and [[marianne-foyster]] at [[borley-rectory]] on 8 June 1931 — his very first visit to the Rectory, on which he had been summoned by [[lionel-foyster]].

## The Event

Whitehouse arrived to find clothes and other items scattered around the main stairs, and Mrs Foyster apparently ill and confined to bed upstairs. While [[lionel-foyster]] crossed to the church, Whitehouse sat with Marianne, who was lying on her back with her hands under the bedclothes.

In his written account:

> "We had been talking for perhaps ten minutes when I suddenly saw her start. At the same time, I felt something land lightly on my lap, coming apparently from behind me. I took hold of the object. It was a brass stiletto about eight or nine inches in length and weighing, I should imagine, not much less than eight ounces. I was told that it was a paper knife belonging to Mr. Foyster and was always kept in the study below."

Whitehouse was confident that Marianne could not have thrown the stiletto without him noticing, even if she had been concealing it under the bedclothes. He had not seen the paper knife on the floor in the time since entering the room. The incident profoundly affected Whitehouse — he felt he had objective proof that paranormal phenomena were taking place at Borley. This was the event [[robert-hastings]] identified as the critical turning point in Price's "passage to belief" about Foyster-period genuineness.

When Whitehouse asked Marianne why she had started, she claimed she had watched the stiletto **rise up from the floor behind him, perform one or two "curious convolutions in the air," and then settle gently onto his lap** [6–8]. Convinced he had witnessed a genuine supernatural event, Whitehouse asked permission to recite the Rosary and sprinkle the room with holy water, which he did with Marianne joining in the prayers [3, 4, 8].

## SPR Attack

In his assessment in the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]], [[trevor-hall]] made much of the fact that the stiletto incident was technically in two parts: (1) Marianne's apparent reaction and claim that the knife had flown by itself; (2) Whitehouse's statement that the stiletto landed in his lap in a way that was impossible for Marianne to have faked without him seeing.

Hall argued that Whitehouse based his belief in the haunting on Marianne's testimony rather than his own observations. In a letter written after *MHH* was published, Whitehouse mentioned to Price that he "did not witness No. 5" — which Hall interpreted as meaning Whitehouse did not see the flight of the stiletto but only found it in his lap. The skeptical authors proposed a "simple normal explanation": Marianne deliberately diverted Whitehouse's attention by pretending to startle, giving her the momentary distraction needed to throw the stiletto herself [7]. They viewed Whitehouse as an emotionally vulnerable, highly-strung young man easily duped by Mrs. Foyster [4, 12, 13].

Whitehouse responded to this charge emphatically in his 1956 statement to [[peter-underwood]]:

> "I SHALL NEVER GO BACK ON WHAT I SAID ... re stiletto, bottles and tumbler incidents."

He emphasised that he had not seen the stiletto in the room before; that Marianne could not have thrown it without him seeing her move; and that "the stiletto incident is actually in two parts — the first being the testimony of Marianne, that the paper knife rose by itself; and the second comprising Edwin Whitehouse's statement that the stiletto landed in his lap in such a way that it was impossible for Marianne to have cheated without being seen." To his Catholic mind, the phenomenon was preternatural and "diabolic," which prompted his immediate use of holy water [10].

## The Poltergeist Focus Theory

Investigators [[iris-owen|Iris Owen]] and Pauline Mitchell proposed an alternative psychological explanation: they theorised that isolated spontaneous poltergeist phenomena are frequently triggered as a release of suppressed emotion, and suggested that **Edwin Whitehouse himself — rather than Marianne — might have been the unconscious epicentre of the activity**, as he "was certainly the kind of person to become the focus of such events" [14].

## Marianne's Denial

When interviewed in 1958 by private detective [[robert-swanson|Robert Swanson]], **Marianne flatly denied the entire episode**. Upon being read Whitehouse's account, she claimed she had no memory of a stiletto incident, stated that she had never even seen an eight-ounce brass stiletto, and insisted that the only paper knife they owned at the Rectory was made of flat ivory [15, 16].

## The Novena Connection

Whitehouse's June 1931 visits were in the context of a novena — nine days of prayers — that he undertook to address the Rectory disturbances. On 16 June, during the novena's conclusion, the [[wall-writings|Writing No. 7]] "Get Light Mass and Prayers Here" appeared on the wall — witnessed by Whitehouse and Marianne while they were together. The stiletto incident and the wall writing are thus closely connected as part of the same intensive Whitehouse period.
