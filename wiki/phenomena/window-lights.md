# Window Lights

Term given to incidents of alleged phenomena at [[borley-rectory]] in which upstairs windows were mysteriously illuminated when the rooms behind them were confirmed empty and unlit. The BRC judges the natural explanations given by the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] as "untenable" in several cases, and considers the likelihood of genuine paranormal activity high.

## Description

Two first-floor rooms were reported as having their windows mysteriously illuminated: Room No. 7 and Room No. 11 (see [[floor-plans]]). During the [[price-tenancy]] this also occurred in the window over the main stairwell. The phenomenon took the form of a concentrated static light, bright enough to be seen "shining through the trees" at a distance and lasting for several minutes.

## Witnesses

- **"Many unnamed villagers"** reported by Price in *MHH*
- [[rev-eric-smith|The Revd Guy Smith]] and his wife [[mabel-smith]]
- Mr and Mrs Basil [[payne-family|Payne]]: Mrs Payne recalled Mabel Smith "calling her over several times" to observe the lights, and taking neighbours to see them
- [[herbert-mayes]]: witnessed the window light on 8 November 1937 during the Price tenancy
- A maid in the service of [[rev-ac-henning|Alfred Henning]] who may have been the last person to see it

## SPR Natural Explanations

The [[spr-investigators|SPR investigators]] proposed several natural explanations:

1. **Railway lights**: The Sudbury to Long Melford line was just over half a mile east. The BRC argues this is implausible: the specific window effects seen could not result from a distant steam locomotive, and Price's observers stationed in the summer-house watching the garden would have seen such regular effects repeatedly
2. **Sudbury house lights**: Two and a half miles away — the BRC finds this "very far-fetched"
3. **Rectory Cottage lantern**: [[lionel-arbon]]'s lantern in the courtyard while using the pump. This could illuminate parts of the courtyard-facing side, but not the windows on the other side of the house seen by the Paynes and Mayes
4. **Internal oil lamps in the bathroom passage**: Requires doors to be left open in specific configurations

## Assessment

The BRC notes that none of the natural explanations would account for the type of light seen "over a course of several years." The specific characteristics — concentrated static light visible at a distance, in confirmed empty rooms, lasting several minutes — remain unexplained. The period of absence during the [[foyster-incumbency]] (when phenomena of other kinds were most intense) is notable; it may have gone unnoticed rather than not occurring.

Conscious fraud (someone inside the building with a light) cannot be entirely ruled out for all incidents, but cannot explain the repeated observations by independent witnesses including the Paynes and Mayes when the building was locked and empty.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**The mysterious illumination of unoccupied rooms**, commonly referred to as the Window Lights phenomenon, is one of the most consistently reported visual anomalies at Borley Rectory. These manifestations were observed by multiple witnesses across different decades, with explanations deeply dividing believers and skeptics. 

### **The Smith Incumbency (1928–1929)**
*   **The Incidents & Witnesses:** The Rev. Guy E. Smith and his wife, Mabel, frequently observed a lighted window in the "schoolroom" (Room 11) or Room 7. On one occasion, Mrs. Smith saw the window illuminated and assumed her maid was inside with a lamp; upon entering the house, she found the maid downstairs and the upstairs room completely dark [1]. On another occasion, she pointed the lighted window out to the church choir, but when the group went upstairs, the room was in darkness [1]. 
*   **June 10–11, 1929:** *Daily Mirror* reporter V.C. Wall and a photographer conducted a night vigil and **"distinctly saw a white figure flitting about in the gloom"** before observing a mysterious light shining through the window of a deserted wing [2, 3]. To test the phenomenon, the Rev. Smith placed a real lamp in an adjacent window for comparison. Observers noted both lights were visible simultaneously, but as they approached the building, the mysterious light vanished while the Rector's lamp continued to burn [4, 5].
*   **Source Perspectives & Explanations:**
    *   **Mrs. Smith's Retraction (1949):** Twenty years later, Mrs. Smith submitted a statement to the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) claiming she had solved the mystery. She stated the lights were **merely reflections in the window glass from passing trains** on the nearby Stour Valley railway line [6, 7].
    *   **Skeptical / SPR Perspective:** The authors of the *Borley Report* (Eric Dingwall, Kathleen Goldney, and Trevor Hall) fully accepted Mrs. Smith’s retraction, suggesting that passing trains, parked cars, or distant lights from the town of Sudbury easily accounted for the reflections, concluding that no paranormal claim could rest on such a normally explained event [8, 9]. 
    *   **Pro-Paranormal Perspective:** Investigators like S.F.C. Kiernander and Ivan Banks heavily criticized the train-reflection theory. They pointed out that the railway line was over half a mile away, the slow-moving trains of that era were dimly lit by gas, and the Rectory was **completely surrounded by tall, thick trees that would have blocked any distant light** [10-12]. 

### **The Price Tenancy & Official Observers (1937–1938)**
During Harry Price’s year-long rental of the empty Rectory, the lights continued to be reported when the building was entirely locked.
*   **November 8, 1937:** Neighbours Mr. and Mrs. Basil Payne reported seeing a bright light shining from the south-facing window of Room 11 [13-15]. On the exact same night, Herbert Mayes (the Rev. A.C. Henning’s chauffeur) reported seeing a bright light in the large window on the main stairs [15-17]. 
*   **November 11, 1937:** The Hennings' maid also reported seeing a light in a Rectory window [16, 18, 19].
*   **Source Perspectives & Explanations:**
    *   **Investigating Natural Causes:** The Rev. Henning immediately checked with the caretaker, Mr. Lionel Arbon, who possessed the only local keys; Arbon confirmed the Rectory was completely locked and unoccupied on those nights [17, 19]. Henning even hired a painter to inspect the window frames to see if luminescent paint was responsible, which it was not [20].
    *   **Skeptical / SPR Perspective:** The SPR authors theorized that the window lights were **reflections from the lantern that Mr. Arbon routinely left burning in the courtyard** while he operated the water pump at night [21, 22]. Mrs. Smith also suggested the effect was caused by oil lamps from the first-floor bathroom shining across the courtyard [23]. However, even the SPR report conceded that the light seen by the Paynes and Mayes **"appears to have been too bright for an explanation of this kind"** [22].

### **Post-Fire Sightings (1939–1944)**
*   **January 1939 (Pre-Fire):** Just weeks before the Rectory burned down, neighbours reported seeing "flame-like lights" in the upper windows [24]. 
*   **April 30 – May 1, 1944 (The Cambridge Commission):** While the burnt-out shell of the Rectory was being actively demolished, observers C.J. Lethbridge and T. Sullivan saw a **"pale, indistinct white light varying slightly in size"** between 3:30 a.m. and 4:00 a.m. [25, 26]. It appeared near the remains of Bedrooms 1 and 2, and then moved to the larder region [26]. The investigators noted this could not have been caused by their own torches, as they were all accounted for [26].
*   **Source Perspectives & Explanations:**
    *   **Skeptical / SPR Perspective:** The SPR noted a highly suspicious detail in the Cambridge Commission's report: one of the observers sleeping inside the ruins during this light phenomenon was P.H. Lord. Lord was an undergraduate who had previously **impersonated the ghost nun as a practical joke** on his fellow investigators, casting serious doubt on the authenticity of any visual phenomena reported while he was present [27].
