# Window Lights

Term given to incidents of alleged phenomena at [[borley-rectory]] in which upstairs windows were mysteriously illuminated when the rooms behind them were confirmed empty and unlit. The BRC judges the natural explanations given by the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] as "untenable" in several cases, and considers the likelihood of genuine paranormal activity high.

## Description

Two first-floor rooms were reported as having their windows mysteriously illuminated: Room No. 7 and Room No. 11. During the Price tenancy this also occurred in the window over the main stairwell. The phenomenon took the form of a concentrated static light, bright enough to be seen "shining through the trees" at a distance and lasting for several minutes.

## Smith Incumbency (1928–1929)

The Rev. [[rev-eric-smith|Guy E. Smith]] and his wife [[mabel-smith|Mabel]] frequently observed a lighted window in the "schoolroom" (Room 11) or Room 7 [1]. On one occasion, Mrs. Smith saw the window illuminated and assumed her maid was inside with a lamp; upon entering the house, she found the maid downstairs and the upstairs room completely dark [1]. On another occasion, she pointed the lighted window out to the church choir, but when the group went upstairs, the room was in darkness [1]. Mrs. Smith often called the [[payne-family|Payne family]] of Borley Place over several times to observe the lights [4].

**June 10–11, 1929:** *Daily Mirror* reporter [[vc-wall|V. C. Wall]] and a photographer conducted a night vigil and "distinctly saw a white figure flitting about in the gloom" before observing a mysterious light shining through the window of a deserted wing [2, 3]. To test the phenomenon, Rev. Smith placed a real lamp in an adjacent window for comparison. Both lights were visible simultaneously, but as they approached the building, the mysterious light vanished while the Rector's lamp continued to burn [4, 5].

**Mrs. Smith's retraction (1949):** Twenty years later, Mrs. Smith submitted a statement to the SPR claiming she had solved the mystery: the lights were merely reflections in the window glass from passing trains on the Stour Valley railway line [6, 7]. The SPR authors fully accepted this retraction, suggesting that passing trains, parked cars, or distant lights from the town of Sudbury easily accounted for the reflections [8, 9].

> **Contradiction:** Investigators [[steuart-kiernander|S. F. C. Kiernander]] and [[ivan-banks]] heavily criticised the train-reflection theory, pointing out that the railway line was over half a mile away, the slow-moving trains of that era were dimly lit by gas, and the Rectory was **completely surrounded by tall, thick trees that would have blocked any distant light** [10–12].

## Price Tenancy and Official Observers (1937–1938)

During [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s year-long rental of the empty Rectory, the lights continued to be reported when the building was entirely locked.

**November 8, 1937:** Neighbours Mr. and Mrs. [[payne-family|Basil Payne]] reported seeing a bright light shining from the south-facing window of Room 11 [13–15]. On the same night, [[herbert-mayes|Herbert Mayes]] (the Rev. A. C. [[rev-ac-henning|Henning]]'s chauffeur) reported seeing a bright light in the large window on the main stairs [15–17].

**November 11, 1937:** The Hennings' maid also reported seeing a light in a Rectory window [16, 18, 19].

The Rev. Henning immediately checked with the caretaker, Mr. [[lionel-arbon|Lionel Arbon]], who confirmed the Rectory was completely locked and unoccupied on those nights [17, 19]. Henning even hired a painter to inspect the window frames to see if luminescent paint was responsible, which it was not [20]. The SPR authors theorised that the window lights were reflections from the lantern that Mr. Arbon routinely left burning in the courtyard while operating the water pump at night [21, 22]. Mrs. Smith also suggested the effect was caused by oil lamps from the first-floor bathroom shining across the courtyard [23]. However, even the SPR report conceded that the light seen by the Paynes and Mayes **"appears to have been too bright for an explanation of this kind"** [22].

## Post-Fire Sightings (1939–1944)

**January 1939 (pre-fire):** Just weeks before the Rectory burned down, neighbours reportedly saw "flame-like lights" in the upper windows [24].

**April 30–May 1, 1944:** While the burnt-out shell of the Rectory was being actively demolished, observers C. J. Lethbridge and T. Sullivan saw a "pale, indistinct white light varying slightly in size" between 3:30 a.m. and 4:00 a.m. [25, 26]. It appeared near the remains of Bedrooms 1 and 2, and then moved to the larder region [26]. The investigators noted this could not have been caused by their own torches [26]. The SPR noted a suspicious detail: one of the observers sleeping in the ruins during this light phenomenon was P. H. Lord, an undergraduate who had previously **impersonated the ghost nun as a practical joke** on his fellow investigators, casting serious doubt on the authenticity of any visual phenomena reported while he was present [27].

## Witnesses

- **"Many unnamed villagers"** reported by Price in *MHH*
- [[rev-eric-smith|The Revd Guy Smith]] and his wife [[mabel-smith]]
- Mr and Mrs [[payne-family|Basil Payne]]: Mrs Payne recalled Mabel Smith "calling her over several times" to observe the lights, and taking neighbours to see them
- [[herbert-mayes|Herbert Mayes]]: witnessed the window light on 8 November 1937 during the Price tenancy
- A maid in the service of [[rev-ac-henning|Alfred Henning]] who may have been the last person to see it

## Assessment

The BRC notes that none of the natural explanations would account for the type of light seen "over a course of several years." The specific characteristics — concentrated static light visible at a distance, in confirmed empty rooms, lasting several minutes — remain unexplained. The period of absence during the [[foyster-incumbency|Foyster incumbency]] (when phenomena of other kinds were most intense) is notable; it may have gone unnoticed rather than not occurring.

Conscious fraud (someone inside the building with a light) cannot be entirely ruled out for all incidents, but cannot explain the repeated observations by independent witnesses including the Paynes and Mayes when the building was locked and empty.
