# Blue Room Séance

Title given to an improvised séance held in the [[blue-room]] of [[borley-rectory]] on the night of 12–13 June 1929, during the first visit by [[harry-price]] and his secretary [[lucie-kaye]]. It was something of an informal affair with no medium; it lasted from 1:00 a.m. for about three hours.

## Sitters

The sitters included [[harry-price]], [[lucie-kaye]], journalist [[vc-wall|Vernon C. Wall]], [[ethel-bull]] and her sister Adelaide (also known as Mabel), together with [[rev-eric-smith|the Revd Guy E. Smith]] and his wife Mabel. The proceedings alternated between darkness and bright lamplight (a Duplex oil lamp).

## The Tapping Mirror

The principal communicating device was the Smiths' large mahogany swing-type dressing-table mirror. After Price's initial invitation for entities to make themselves known, the assembled group heard a series of sharp taps from the wooden back of the mirror. Using the "three taps for yes, one for no, two for doubtful" protocol, plus spelling out words by alphabet, a communicator identified itself as [[harry-bull|the Revd Harry Bull]] — who had died in the same room exactly two years previously.

"Harry Bull" acknowledged that it was his footsteps the Smiths had heard while ostensibly alone in the house. When [[ethel-bull]] asked specifically whether he had been killed and whether money was involved, the entity responded "Yes" on both counts — reflecting the sisters' longstanding private suspicions about [[ivy-brackenbury|Ivy Brackenbury]] and Harry's death. The Smiths became upset at this and requested the séance be brought to a close.

## The Soap Incident

About an hour into the séance, a cake of soap on a washstand at the other end of the room fell from its dish and struck the edge of a water jug on the floor before bouncing across the room. This occurred in bright lamplight, entirely out of reach of anyone present. Price observed that a mark from the jug's edge was clearly visible in the soap, and surmised that it would have had to have been lifted or jumped to fall in the way it did. [[vc-wall]] described it as "the most astonishing event of the night."

## Conflicting Accounts

Several varying accounts describing the Blue Room Séance have been published:
- Vernon Wall in the *Daily Mirror*, the following day
- [[harry-price]] in *MHH*, ten years later
- [[lucie-kaye]] in an account sent to [[peter-underwood]] in 1951

The accounts contain differences in detail. [[ethel-bull]] told Underwood that the soap incident took place when the Blue Room was empty, during a break in the proceedings — contradicting Wall and Price. [[mabel-smith]] acknowledged tapping sounds from the mirror but attributed them to trickery when questioned by the [[spr-investigators|SPR investigators]] in 1949.

## Andrew Clarke's Theory

Local historian [[andrew-clarke]] argued that the ghostly raps were the result of the Duplex lamp being switched on and off during questioning, with fluctuating heat from the lantern causing the wooden back of the mirror to expand and contract. The BRC notes this is plausible if the lamp was on the dressing table itself, but two days later when professional mediums held a second séance in the same room under presumably similar conditions, nothing was heard.

## Significance

The Blue Room Séance is the first documented attempt at formal contact with "entities" at Borley, and the first time [[harry-bull]]'s spirit was claimed to communicate. The soap-throwing incident remains one of the more evidential small-scale poltergeist incidents from the case — occurring in bright light, witnessed by multiple observers, with physical evidence (the soap mark). The tapping mirror would be brought back to the Rectory by the Smiths for a second séance in January 1938.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

In the Borley Rectory sources, the term "Blue Room Séance" almost exclusively refers to a highly publicized and controversial sitting held on the night of **12–13 June 1929** during the incumbency of the Rev. Guy E. Smith. Another notable spiritualist vigil also took place in the Blue Room in **January 1932**. 

Here are the recorded instances, witness descriptions, and proposed explanations for these events:

### **The 1929 Blue Room Séance**
**Date:** 12–13 June 1929 (1:00 a.m. to 4:00 a.m.) [1, 2]
**Witnesses:** Ghost hunter Harry Price, his secretary Lucie Kaye (later Mrs. Meeker), *Daily Mirror* reporter V. C. Wall, the Rev. Guy E. Smith, his wife Mabel Smith, and two sisters of the late rector, Miss Ethel Bull and Miss Freda (or Mabel/Adelaide) Bull [1, 3, 4].

**Recorded Instances and Descriptions:**
*   **The Tapping Mirror:** The participants gathered in the Blue Room, the bedroom where the previous rectors Henry and Harry Bull had both died [4]. According to Price, he formally asked if any entity was present, which was answered by faint taps from the window, followed by loud, sharp raps from the wooden back of a solid mahogany dressing-table mirror [3]. A code was established: three taps for "yes," two for "doubtful," and one for "no" [5, 6]. 
*   **The Communicator:** The entity identified itself as the late Rev. Harry Bull [5]. Through the tapping, the entity claimed that he was unhappy, that there were money troubles, and that he had been murdered [7-9]. 
*   **The Jumping Soap:** The most violent physical phenomenon of the night involved a new cake of toilet soap. Price and Wall reported that the soap suddenly jumped out of its dish on the washstand, flew across the room, and struck a water ewer on the floor with such force that the soap was deeply dented [2, 10, 11]. 
*   **Sparks:** Mrs. Mabel Smith later reported seeing "little lights" or "sparks in the darkness" during the sitting [12].

**Contradictions in Witness Testimony:**
The sources record massive discrepancies among the witnesses regarding almost every detail of the 1929 séance:
*   **Who was present and when:** Price and Wall claimed the entire group sat together for the soap incident and the questioning [2, 11]. However, Lucie Kaye later wrote that only she and Price were initially present, while Mrs. Smith stated she and her husband only entered the room *after* the others told them the soap had already jumped [13, 14]. Ethel Bull also emphatically insisted that the room was entirely empty when the soap incident occurred [14, 15].
*   **Lighting:** Price stated the room was "perfectly illuminated" by a powerful Duplex oil lamp [2, 16]. Lucie Kaye described the light as "fitful and depressing," while Mrs. Smith stated that when she entered, the Blue Room was in total darkness [16, 17].
*   **The Messages:** Lucie Kaye claimed the entity successfully spelled out names and dates regarding Harry Bull's will being misinterpreted [18]. Wall and Price completely contradicted this, stating the entity was a "bad scholar" incapable of spelling and that no answers were received regarding private family affairs [5, 18, 19].
*   **The Conclusion:** Price claimed the séance ended naturally from exhaustion [2, 16], but Mrs. Smith maintained that her husband, frightened and upset by the "indiscreet" questions regarding murder, demanded the séance be stopped immediately [7, 12, 16]. 

**Proposed Explanations (1929):**
*   **Genuine Paranormal Activity:** Price and Wall firmly believed the raps and the flying soap were "perfect and typical" poltergeist manifestations [2]. Believers point out that the mirror itself seemed to act as a paranormal battery, as it reportedly continued to produce tapping sounds months later when the Smiths moved to Long Melford [20, 21]. Ethel Bull believed the séance was genuine because no living person could have known the answers the entity provided [22].
*   **Fraud and Trickery:** Twenty years later, Mrs. Mabel Smith stated her firm belief that "tricks were being played all around us" and heavily suspected that Harry Price faked the phenomena himself to gain publicity [12, 13, 15].
*   **Natural Causes (Thermal Expansion):** Local historian Andrew Clarke proposed that the ghostly raps had a purely mundane origin: the fluctuating heat from the Duplex oil lamp simply caused the wooden back of the mahogany mirror to expand and contract, producing clicking sounds [18, 23]. 
*   **Psychological Factors:** Skeptical writers like Robert Wood likened the séance to a "game-show" reliant on leading questions, arguing the answers merely reflected village gossip and the malicious prejudices of the Bull sisters [20, 24].

### **The 1932 Marks Tey Vigil**
**Date:** 23–24 January 1932 [25, 26]
**Witnesses:** The Rev. Lionel Foyster, Marianne Foyster, medium Guy L'Estrange, and members of the Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle (Mr. Warren, Mr. H.H. Frost, Capt. V.M. Deane) [25, 27-29].

**Recorded Instances and Descriptions:**
During the Foyster incumbency, members of the Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle arrived to "cleanse" the Rectory of its violent poltergeists. 
*   After experiencing flying bottles and bell-ringing, the group used a holy relic to bless the house, which caused the chaotic phenomena to stop [25, 26]. 
*   The group then decided to spend the rest of the night specifically in the Blue Room. Sitting in the moonlight with their backs to the wall, they reported that the atmosphere became "heavy and supernormally cold" [25, 26]. 
*   Around 3:00 a.m., a "black shadow" appeared to develop against the wall. The medium, Guy L'Estrange, went across the room to contact the entity and reported feeling that it was "intensely evil" before the form slowly dissolved [30, 31]. 

**Proposed Explanations (1932):**
*   **Spiritual Cleansing:** The spiritualists and the Rev. Foyster believed the session was a genuine confrontation with evil spirits, noting that the house felt "entirely different" the next morning and that the violent phenomena abruptly ceased for several years [25, 32, 33]. 
*   **The End of a Hoax:** The skeptical authors of the *Borley Report* (Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) proposed a natural explanation: they argued that Marianne Foyster had been faking the phenomena. Because she was increasingly suspected of fraud by visitors, she used the spiritualists' "cleansing" vigil in the Blue Room as a convenient excuse to permanently stop her tricks without admitting guilt [34].
