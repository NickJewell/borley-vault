# Blue Room Séance

Title given to an improvised séance held in the [[blue-room]] of [[borley-rectory]] on the night of 12–13 June 1929, during the first visit by [[harry-price]] and his secretary [[lucie-kaye]]. It was something of an informal affair with no medium; it lasted from 1:00 a.m. for about three hours.

## Sitters

The sitters included [[harry-price]], [[lucie-kaye]], journalist [[vc-wall|Vernon C. Wall]], [[ethel-bull]] and her sister Adelaide (also known as Mabel), together with [[rev-eric-smith|the Revd Guy E. Smith]] and his wife [[mabel-smith|Mabel]]. The proceedings alternated between darkness and bright lamplight (a Duplex oil lamp). They gathered in the [[blue-room|Blue Room]], the bedroom where the previous rectors [[henry-bull|Henry]] and [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] had both died [4].

## The Tapping Mirror

The principal communicating device was the Smiths' large mahogany swing-type dressing-table mirror. After Price's initial invitation for entities to make themselves known, the assembled group heard a series of sharp taps from the wooden back of the mirror. Using the "three taps for yes, one for no, two for doubtful" protocol, plus spelling out words by alphabet, a communicator identified itself as [[harry-bull|the Revd Harry Bull]] — who had died in the same room exactly two years previously.

"Harry Bull" acknowledged that it was his footsteps the Smiths had heard while ostensibly alone in the house. When [[ethel-bull]] asked specifically whether he had been killed and whether money was involved, the entity responded "Yes" on both counts — reflecting the sisters' longstanding private suspicions about [[ivy-brackenbury|Ivy Brackenbury]] and Harry's death. The Smiths became upset at this and requested the séance be brought to a close.

## The Soap Incident

About an hour into the séance, a cake of soap on a washstand at the other end of the room fell from its dish and struck the edge of a water jug on the floor before bouncing across the room (multiple witnesses). This occurred in bright lamplight, entirely out of reach of anyone present. Price observed that a mark from the jug's edge was clearly visible in the soap, and surmised that it would have had to have been lifted or jumped to fall in the way it did. [[vc-wall|V. C. Wall]] described it as "the most astonishing event of the night."

[[mabel-smith|Mrs. Mabel Smith]] later reported seeing "little lights" or "sparks in the darkness" during the sitting [12].

## Contradictions in Witness Testimony

The sources record substantial discrepancies among the witnesses regarding almost every detail of the 1929 séance:

**Who was present and when:** Price and Wall claimed the entire group sat together for the soap incident and the questioning [2, 11]. However, [[lucie-kaye|Lucie Kaye]] later wrote that only she and Price were initially present, while Mrs. Smith stated she and her husband only entered the room *after* the others told them the soap had already jumped [13, 14]. [[ethel-bull]] also emphatically insisted that the room was entirely empty when the soap incident occurred [14, 15].

**Lighting:** Price stated the room was "perfectly illuminated" by a powerful Duplex oil lamp [2, 16]. Lucie Kaye described the light as "fitful and depressing," while Mrs. Smith stated that when she entered, the Blue Room was in total darkness [16, 17].

**The messages:** Lucie Kaye claimed the entity successfully spelled out names and dates regarding Harry Bull's will being misinterpreted [18]. Wall and Price completely contradicted this, stating the entity was a "bad scholar" incapable of spelling and that no answers were received regarding private family affairs [5, 18, 19].

**The conclusion:** Price claimed the séance ended naturally from exhaustion [2, 16], but Mrs. Smith maintained that her husband, frightened and upset by the "indiscreet" questions regarding murder, demanded the séance be stopped immediately [7, 12, 16].

Several varying accounts have been published — Vernon Wall in the *Daily Mirror* the following day, [[harry-price]] in *MHH* ten years later, and [[lucie-kaye]] in an account sent to [[peter-underwood]] in 1951.

## Proposed Explanations

**Genuine paranormal activity:** Price and Wall firmly believed the raps and the flying soap were "perfect and typical" poltergeist manifestations [2]. Believers point out that the mirror itself reportedly continued to produce tapping sounds months later when the Smiths moved to Long Melford [20, 21]. [[ethel-bull]] believed the séance was genuine because no living person could have known the answers the entity provided [22].

**Fraud and trickery:** Twenty years later, Mrs. Mabel Smith stated her firm belief that "tricks were being played all around us" and heavily suspected that Harry Price faked the phenomena himself to gain publicity [12, 13, 15].

**Natural causes (thermal expansion):** [[andrew-clarke|Andrew Clarke]] proposed that the ghostly raps had a purely mundane origin: the fluctuating heat from the Duplex oil lamp simply caused the wooden back of the mahogany mirror to expand and contract, producing clicking sounds [18, 23]. The BRC notes this is plausible if the lamp was on the dressing table itself, but two days later when professional mediums held a second séance in the same room under presumably similar conditions, nothing was heard.

**Psychological factors:** Skeptical writers likened the séance to a "game-show" reliant on leading questions, arguing the answers merely reflected village gossip and the malicious prejudices of the Bull sisters [20, 24].

## Andrew Clarke's Theory

Local historian [[andrew-clarke]] argued that the ghostly raps were the result of the Duplex lamp being switched on and off during questioning, with fluctuating heat from the lantern causing the wooden back of the mirror to expand and contract. The BRC notes this is plausible if the lamp was on the dressing table itself, but two days later when professional mediums held a second séance in the same room under presumably similar conditions, nothing was heard.

## The 1932 Marks Tey Vigil

During the [[foyster-incumbency|Foyster incumbency]], members of the [[marks-tey-spiritualist-circle|Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle]] — medium Guy L'Estrange, C. Warren, H. H. Frost, Capt. V. M. Deane, and the Rev. [[lionel-foyster]] and [[marianne-foyster]] — arrived on 23–24 January 1932 to "cleanse" the Rectory of its violent poltergeists [25–29].

After experiencing flying bottles and bell-ringing, the group used a holy relic to bless the house, which caused the chaotic phenomena to stop. The group then spent the rest of the night in the [[blue-room|Blue Room]]. Sitting in the moonlight with their backs to the wall, they reported that the atmosphere became "heavy and supernormally cold" [25, 26]. Around 3:00 a.m., a "black shadow" appeared to develop against the wall. L'Estrange went across the room to contact the entity and reported feeling that it was "intensely evil" before the form slowly dissolved [30, 31].

**The spiritualists and the Rev. Foyster** believed the session was a genuine confrontation with evil spirits, noting that the house felt "entirely different" the next morning and that the violent phenomena abruptly ceased for several years [25, 32, 33].

**The skeptical *Borley Report*** (Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) proposed a natural explanation: they argued that [[marianne-foyster]] had been faking the phenomena, and because she was increasingly suspected of fraud by visitors, she used the spiritualists' "cleansing" vigil as a convenient excuse to permanently stop her tricks without admitting guilt [34].

## Significance

The Blue Room Séance is the first documented attempt at formal contact with "entities" at Borley, and the first time [[harry-bull]]'s spirit was claimed to communicate. The soap-throwing incident remains one of the more evidential small-scale poltergeist incidents from the case — occurring in bright light, witnessed by multiple observers, with physical evidence (the soap mark). The tapping mirror would be brought back to the Rectory by the Smiths for a second séance in January 1938.
