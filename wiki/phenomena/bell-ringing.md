# Bell Ringing

Unexplained ringing of the Rectory's servant bells. Reported during [[henry-bull|Rev. H. D. E. Bull's]] incumbency and continuing through the Foyster period, with mechanical analysis later revealing several entirely natural pathways by which the bells could have been operated.

## Bull Incumbency

[[ethel-bull|Miss Ethel Bull]] reported an occasion when all twenty of the house's servant bells suddenly pealed simultaneously without explanation [1–3]. Around 1922, J. Osborne Harley — a pupil of the Rev. [[harry-bull|Harry Bull]] — was staying overnight when the large bell in the courtyard rang loudly at 2:00 a.m. When Bull rushed into the room, distressed, the bell rang violently a second time. "I've never known it ring twice in quick succession like that before." The rector's explanation: "No, it's the nun, she does sometimes" [4–6]. [[ernest-ambrose|Ernest Ambrose]] records Harry Bull telling him that one evening after a service, he and a friend heard "distinct and loud knocking sounds from outside, near the porch of the church" that "slowly travelled the whole way round the church."

Skeptical writers generally attribute early stories of the Rectory's phenomena to the overactive imaginations and spiritualist beliefs of the Bull family [7, 8].

## Smith Incumbency

The Revd [[rev-eric-smith|Guy Eric Smith]] and his wife [[mabel-smith|Mabel]] were deeply troubled by incessant bell-ringing [9, 10]. The nuisance was so constant that the Rector cut many bell wires, but the bells continued to ring [9, 11, 12]. During Price's first visit on 12 June 1929 with *Daily Mirror* reporter [[vc-wall|V. C. Wall]] and [[lucie-kaye]], bells rang of their own volition, and the bell pulls in the rooms were seen swinging [9, 13, 14]. On 5 July 1929, [[lord-charles-hope|Lord Charles Hope]] challenged the entities, saying, "If they want to impress us, let them give us a phenomenon now!" — minutes later, a ground-floor bell clanged loudly. The investigators rushed down but found no movement in the spring or clapper to identify which bell had rung [15–17].

Years later, Mrs. Mabel Smith emphatically stated she saw "enormous rats" in the house and was certain they caused the bell-ringing by scratching the boards and running over the wires [18, 19]. Mrs. Smith also suspected the maid, [[mary-pearson|Mary Pearson]], who later confessed to throwing objects and showed Mrs. Smith a spot in the pantry where exposed bell-wires congregated; by standing on a chair, someone could pull the wires and ring the bells across the house [20–23]. Pearson herself discovered this cluster of exposed bell-wires during the Smith period. Lord Charles Hope's contemporary 1929 notes also record that he noticed some bell wires were exposed in the passage and surmised someone could ring a bell by pulling them.

## Foyster Incumbency

[[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] recorded occasional bell-ringing throughout his incumbency, always attributed to "figgis" or "goblins." He was under the mistaken impression that the wires had been disconnected; in fact only the pull-cords in some rooms had been removed — the bell wires ran intact across the attics. The Foysters experienced frequent ringing, noting that the bells often rang as a "warning" if they stayed up past midnight [25–27].

The **October 1931 incident**: During Price's surveillance visit, a bell rang while Marianne was in full view. Price declared Adelaide the suspect. [[francois-darles|Frank Pearless]] subsequently arrived at the door and vouched for Marianne's innocence. Price later noted the wire was broken near the ceiling out of the child's reach, and no adult could have entered without breaking the door's seals [33, 34]. Marianne allegedly prayed to St. Anthony for vindication — and instantly a bell rang furiously [31–34].

The **January 1932 L'Estrange visit** produced the most dramatic ringing. As L'Estrange, [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]], and Lionel descended the stairs, all thirty bells clanged wildly. L'Estrange said aloud: "If some invisible person is present and can hear these words, please stop the ringing for a moment." **Instantly, every bell became silent.** The rector noted that the wires of all but three bells had been cut in a previous attempt to stop the ringing, yet all thirty rang. After this night, bell-ringing effectively ceased [35–39].

Ian Shaw, Marianne's son, later confessed to investigator [[trevor-hall]] that he had found a piece of string hidden in the courtyard ivy; when tugged, it pulled a cluster of exposed wires and rang the house bells [40, 41]. Skeptics and later researchers heavily suspected that Marianne Foyster and her lodger Frank Peerless rigged the string in the ivy to orchestrate the phenomena, specifically to fool investigators like L'Estrange and Price [40, 42, 43]. Marianne later denied this, claiming the wire was just a broken piece hanging in the old knife and boot room [44–46].

The **Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle séance** (23 January 1932) may be the same or a related event — see [[blue-room-seance]] for the group's full account of that night.

## Price Tenancy

The spontaneous ringing of the old servant bells had effectively ceased by the Price period. Observer [[dr-hf-bellamy|Dr H. F. Bellamy]] rigged an experimental electric bell contact-breaker under a pile of books on the dining room mantelpiece on 16–17 October 1937. At 12:50 a.m. the bell rang for about a minute and abruptly stopped; the observers found all the books displaced, though the room was sealed [47–52]. Dr. Bellamy and his team were convinced the displacement was paranormal. The skeptical *Borley Report* authors argued that a stray cat, ejected from the house earlier that evening, might have returned and disturbed the books [48, 53]. [[mark-kerr-pearse|Mark Kerr-Pearse]] also theorized that rats running along the wires caused any residual mechanical bell-ringing [54].

## Post-1939

After the Rectory burned down, an army officer attempting to sleep in the ruined shell reportedly heard all the bells ringing during the night around 1941 [55, 56]. On 24 April 1949, Mr. and Mrs. Pearson heard the church bell ringing at 8:00 a.m. and assumed the Rector was there, but the church was found locked and completely empty [57, 58].

## Clarke's Mechanical Analysis

[[andrew-clarke|Andrew Clarke]] provides a definitive technical explanation for the bell phenomena:

**The system:** Mechanical handbells on springs, connected by bronze wires running unfettered across the attics to a bell board in the kitchen passage. The wires were **never fully disconnected** — only pull-cords in some rooms were removed. Bell wires were vulnerable to rats and mice in the attic (Mrs Smith's own explanation for ringing during her incumbency).

**The string in the ivy:** Ian Shaw discovered a piece of string hidden in the ivy near the kitchen-passage door to the courtyard. When pulled, all the house bells rang. Clarke identifies this as Frank Pearless's installation. Ian: *"The wires were bent and pulled down."*

**Access routes:** The external cellar trapdoor in the courtyard (noted by Price's observers [[colonel-westland|Colonel Westland]] and Ryberg in 1937) gave unsecured access from the yard into the cellars and thence to the bell-wire system. From the pantry, one could also ring bells by reaching the wires (discovered by Mary Pearson during the Smith period).

**Adelaide's bell:** When the Adelaide's-room bell rang during Price's surveillance, Clarke notes the physics: once a bell is swinging, the wire at the other end swings too. Any interference anywhere in the wire chain — from below in the cellar, from the passage, from outside — would produce the effect.

## SPR Analysis

The 1956 SPR Report documents that Price's categorical denial of rats at Borley was demonstrably false. At least four observer reports explicitly mention mice or rats, all suppressed by Price:

- Kerr-Pearse (June 1937): "found many traces of mice in cupboards. Could mice or rats have rung bells?"
- Glover (February 1938, his report printed "verbatim" in MHH): the sentence "A thing we particularly noticed was the large number of mouse marks — droppings and so on — in various parts of the house" was omitted
- Glanville/Harrison (August 1937): sounds attributed to mice — the "mice" was cut from the published version
- Ryberg (December 1937): "mice were heard ravaging in some boxes... gnawed paper was found... mice were seen to scuttle around" — report not quoted at all

[[mabel-smith]] confirmed in her signed SPR statement (1949) that she saw "enormous rats in the place" and was "sure these were responsible for bell-ringing and many noises attributed to the supernatural; they would scratch the boards."

**Conclusion:** No bell-ringing incident at Borley requires a supernatural explanation.
