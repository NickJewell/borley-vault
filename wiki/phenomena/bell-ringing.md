# Bell Ringing

Unexplained ringing of the Rectory's servant bells. Reported during [[henry-bull|Rev. H. D. E. Bull's]] incumbency.

## Key Incident

On one occasion, *all twenty bells* pealed simultaneously without explanation. The event was witnessed and is one of the more striking collective physical phenomena from the early period (bells require mechanical activation and leave no ambiguity about whether something happened — only about its cause).

The Rectory's bells hung in the kitchen passage, controlled by anchor-boards in the attics. The mechanical system means accidental simultaneous ringing of twenty bells is essentially impossible without deliberate action or a structural event.

## Subsequent Reports

[[john-osborne-harley|J. Osborne Harley]] (bps-08) provides the most detailed account of a bell incident during the Bull period: at about 2 a.m., the large courtyard bell rang violently. Harry Bull burst into Harley's bedroom, trembling: "Did you hear it? I hate this sort of thing being talked about." When Harley promised silence, Bull calmed — then it rang again, even louder. "I've never known it ring twice in quick succession like that before." The rector's explanation: "No, it's the nun, she does sometimes."

[[ernest-ambrose|Ernest Ambrose]] (bps-08) records Harry Bull telling him that one evening after a service, he and a friend heard "distinct and loud knocking sounds from outside, near the porch of the church" that "slowly travelled the whole way round the church" — a related auditory phenomenon.

## The Foyster Period

[[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] recorded occasional bell-ringing throughout his incumbency, always attributed to "figgis" or "goblins." He was under the mistaken impression that the wires had been disconnected; in fact only the pull-cords in some rooms had been removed — the bell wires ran intact across the attics.

The **January 1932 L'Estrange visit** (bps-08) produced the most dramatic ringing. As L'Estrange, Marianne, and Lionel descended the stairs, all 30 bells clanged wildly. L'Estrange said aloud: "If some invisible person is present and can hear these words, please stop the ringing for a moment." **Instantly, every bell became silent.** The rector noted that the wires of all but three bells had been cut in a previous attempt to stop the ringing, yet all 30 rang. After this night, bell-ringing effectively ceased.

The **Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle séance** (also January 23, 1932) may be the same or a related event.

During Price's surveillance visit (October 1931), a bell rang while Marianne was in full view. Price declared Adelaide the suspect. [[francois-darles|Frank Pearless]] subsequently arrived at the door and vouched for Marianne's innocence.

## SPR Analysis (spr-iv)

The 1956 SPR Report documents that Price's categorical denial of rats at Borley ("on no occasion have I seen or heard the slightest indication of these rodents... never once has any observer mentioned rats") was demonstrably false. At least four observer reports explicitly mention mice or rats, all suppressed by Price:

- Kerr-Pearse (June 1937): "found many traces of mice in cupboards. Could mice or rats have rung bells?"
- Glover (February 1938, his report printed "verbatim" in MHH): the sentence "A thing we particularly noticed was the large number of mouse marks — droppings and so on — in various parts of the house" was omitted
- Glanville/Harrison (August 1937): sounds attributed to mice — the "mice" was cut from the published version
- Ryberg (December 1937): "mice were heard ravaging in some boxes... gnawed paper was found... mice were seen to scuttle around" — report not quoted at all

[[mabel-smith]] confirmed in her signed SPR statement (1949) that she saw "enormous rats in the place" and was "sure these were responsible for bell-ringing and many noises attributed to the supernatural; they would scratch the boards."

[[mary-pearson|Mary Pearson]] discovered during the Smith period that a cluster of exposed bell-wires in the pantry could be operated by anyone with access to the pantry — ringing all the bells in the kitchen passage.

Lord Charles Hope's contemporary 1929 notes also record that he noticed some bell wires were exposed in the passage and surmised someone could ring a bell by pulling them.

## Clarke's Mechanical Analysis (slbr-09)

Andrew Clarke provides a definitive technical explanation for the bell phenomena:

**The system:** Mechanical handbells on springs, connected by bronze wires running unfettered across the attics to a bell board in the kitchen passage. The wires were **never fully disconnected** — only pull-cords in some rooms were removed. Bell wires were vulnerable to rats and mice in the attic (Mrs Smith's own explanation for ringing during her incumbency).

**The string in the ivy:** Ian Shaw (Marianne's son) discovered a piece of string hidden in the ivy near the kitchen-passage door to the courtyard. When pulled, all the house bells rang. Clarke identifies this as [[francois-darles|Frank Pearless's]] installation. Ian: *"The wires were bent and pulled down."*

**Access routes:** The external cellar trapdoor in the courtyard (noted by Price's observers Colonel Westland and Ryberg in 1937) gave unsecured access from the yard into the cellars and thence to the bell-wire system. From the pantry, one could also ring bells by reaching the wires (discovered by Mary Pearson during the Smith period).

**Adelaide's bell:** When the Adelaide's-room bell rang during Price's surveillance, Clarke notes the physics: once a bell is swinging, the wire at the other end swings too. Any interference anywhere in the wire chain — from below in the cellar, from the passage, from outside — would produce the effect.

**Conclusion:** No bell-ringing incident at Borley requires a supernatural explanation.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

The ringing of bells is one of the most prominent, frequently recorded, and highly debated phenomena associated with Borley Rectory. Occurring across multiple occupancies, the mysterious ringing of the house's mechanical servant bells was attributed to everything from demonic poltergeists to rats and elaborate physical hoaxes. 

Here are the recorded instances, witness descriptions, and proposed explanations from the Borley sources, categorized by the occupancy period:

**The Bull Incumbency (1863–1927)**
*   **Undated (Late 19th/Early 20th Century):** Miss Ethel Bull reported an occasion when she was in the Rectory and all twenty of the house's servant bells suddenly started pealing simultaneously without explanation [1-3]. 
*   **c. 1922:** J. Osborne Harley, a pupil of the Rev. Harry Bull, was staying overnight when the large bell in the courtyard rang loudly at 2:00 a.m. [4, 5]. When Bull rushed into the room, distressed, the bell violently rang a second time [5, 6]. 
*   **Source Perspectives/Explanations:** The Rev. Harry Bull explicitly blamed the ghost of the nun for the courtyard bell, dismissing Harley's suggestion that a mortal was outside [5, 6]. Skeptical writers generally attribute early stories of the Rectory's phenomena to the overactive imaginations and spiritualist beliefs of the Bull family [7, 8]. 

**The Smith Incumbency (1928–1929)**
*   **General Disturbances (1928–1929):** The Rev. Guy Eric Smith and his wife Mabel were deeply troubled by incessant bell-ringing [9, 10]. The nuisance was so constant that the Rector cut many of the bell wires, but the bells continued to ring [9, 11, 12].
*   **June 12, 1929:** During Harry Price's first visit with *Daily Mirror* reporter V.C. Wall and Lucie Kaye, bells rang of their own volition, and the bell pulls in the rooms were seen swinging [9, 13, 14]. 
*   **July 5, 1929:** Lord Charles Hope, Harry Price, and Lucie Kaye held a vigil. Hope challenged the entities, saying, "If they want to impress us, let them give us a phenomenon now!" Minutes later, a ground-floor bell clanged loudly [15-17]. The investigators rushed down but found no movement in the spring or clapper to identify which bell had rung [16, 17]. 
*   **Source Perspectives/Explanations:** 
    *   **Rats and Mice:** Years later, Mrs. Mabel Smith emphatically stated she saw "enormous rats" in the house and was certain they caused the bell-ringing by scratching the boards and running over the wires [18, 19]. 
    *   **Pranks and Sabotage:** Mrs. Smith also suspected the maid, Mary Pearson, who later confessed to throwing objects and showed Mrs. Smith a spot in the pantry where exposed bell-wires congregated; by standing on a chair, someone could pull the wires and ring the bells across the house [20-23]. 
    *   **Skeptical Views:** The Society for Psychical Research (SPR) report suggested that village boys or local pranksters easily manipulated the external wires or exposed internal systems [22, 24].

**The Foyster Incumbency (1930–1935)**
*   **General Incidents:** The Foysters experienced frequent bell-ringing, noting that the bells often rang as a "warning" if they stayed up past midnight [25-27]. 
*   **October 13–14, 1931 (The St. Anthony Incident):** Harry Price, Kathleen Goldney, and others investigated and openly accused Marianne Foyster of faking the phenomena [28-30]. Marianne vehemently denied it and allegedly prayed to St. Anthony for vindication [31, 32]. Instantly, a bell rang furiously [33, 34]. Price traced it to the room of three-and-a-half-year-old Adelaide, who was awake in bed [33, 34]. Price noted the wire was broken near the ceiling out of the child's reach, and no adult could have entered without breaking the door's seals [33, 34]. 
*   **January 23–24, 1932:** The Marks Tey Spiritualist Circle, including medium Guy L'Estrange, arrived. They were greeted by a cacophony of all thirty bells clanging wildly at once [35-38]. L'Estrange commanded the invisible entity to stop, and every bell fell instantly silent, as if "seized and held by a hidden hand" [37, 39]. 
*   **Source Perspectives/Explanations:**
    *   **The String in the Ivy:** Ian Shaw, Marianne's son, later confessed to investigator Trevor Hall that he had found a piece of string hidden in the courtyard ivy [40, 41]. When tugged, it pulled a cluster of exposed wires and rang the house bells [40, 41]. 
    *   **Fraud:** Skeptics and later researchers heavily suspected that Marianne Foyster and her lodger (and lover) Frank Peerless rigged the string in the ivy to orchestrate the phenomena, specifically to fool investigators like L'Estrange and Price [40, 42, 43]. Marianne later denied this, claiming the wire was just a broken piece hanging in the old knife and boot room [44-46]. 

**The Price Tenancy (1937–1938)**
*   **October 16–17, 1937:** The spontaneous ringing of the old servant bells had ceased by this period, but official observer Dr. H. F. Bellamy rigged an experimental electric bell contact-breaker under a pile of books on the dining room mantelpiece [47-50]. At 12:50 a.m., the bell rang for about a minute and abruptly stopped [51, 52]. The observers found all the books displaced, though the room was sealed [51]. 
*   **Source Perspectives/Explanations:** Dr. Bellamy and his team were convinced the displacement was paranormal due to the force required [52]. However, the skeptical authors of the *Borley Report* argued that a stray cat, which had been ejected from the house earlier that evening, might have returned and disturbed the books [48, 53]. Mark Kerr-Pearse also theorized that rats running along the wires caused any residual mechanical bell-ringing [54].

**Later Borley (Post-1939)**
*   **c. 1941:** After the Rectory burned down, an army officer attempting to sleep in the ruined shell reported being awakened several times during the night by all the bells ringing [55, 56].
*   **April 24, 1949:** The phantom ringing seemingly moved to Borley Church. Mr. and Mrs. Pearson heard the church bell ringing at 8:00 a.m. and assumed the Rector was there, but it was later discovered the church was locked and completely empty [57, 58].
