# The Flying Brick (April 5, 1944)

One of the final documented paranormal incidents at Borley — and uniquely, the only Borley phenomenon captured on photograph. Occurred on April 5, 1944, during a visit by [[harry-price|Harry Price]], [[david-scherman|David E. Scherman]] (*Life* magazine photographer), and [[cynthia-ledsham|Miss Cynthia Ledsham]] (*Time and Life* researcher). The trio visited to take photographs for an article on "Haunted England" [2, 3].

## The Incident

Scherman was photographing the rapidly dwindling ruins of [[borley-rectory|the Rectory]] from a distance of approximately one hundred feet. As he pressed the trigger of his miniature camera (American make), all three observers simultaneously saw a brick — or part of a brick — shoot approximately **four feet into the air** in front of what remained of the kitchen passage, just below where the bathroom passage had been [5].

All three saw it. They walked over immediately and examined the bricks in the area. All appeared normal. No wires or strings were attached to any of them. No workmen were visible on that side of the Rectory. According to Price's account, all three laughed and called it "the last phenomenon," joking that the poltergeists were "demonstrating" in honour of their visit [5].

## The Photograph

A few days later, Scherman sent Price proof-sheets of his photographs. Price was unremarkable about the images until Scherman telephoned and asked whether Price had noticed "anything about the photograph of the ruins."

Scherman had **opened his shutter at the precise fraction-of-a-second when the brick was at the apex of its flight**. Enlarged ten-by-eight prints reveal a white rectangular shape against the dark background of the kitchen passage, perfectly stationary (no motion blur) — a poltergeist projectile suspended in mid-air [6–8].

Price reproduces the enlarged section as Plate XXIII, confirming that none of the photographs in the book were retouched.

## Price's Assessment (disputed)

Price published the photograph and an enlargement in *The End of Borley Rectory* (1946), carefully noting the significance of the location — the former kitchen passage where wall-writings and many other manifestations had previously occurred [9, 10]. He carefully refrained from stating definitively that the event was supernatural, writing: **"If, indeed, this was a genuine paranormal phenomenon, then we have the first photograph ever taken of a Poltergeist projectile in flight"** [9, 11]. In a 1946 letter to Dr. [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]], Price offered £1,000 to charity if anyone could prove it was faked [12, 13].

## The Ledsham–Scherman Retraction

Years later, the two *Life* journalists completely contradicted Price's suggestion of paranormality:

**Ledsham's accusation:** In a letter to the [[spr|Society for Psychical Research]] and a subsequent 1950 interview, Ledsham (then Mrs. Thompson) accused Price of "bare-faced hocus pocus" [14, 15]. She claimed that a "brawny workman" was actively engaged in demolition behind a wall, propelling bricks out at regular intervals [14, 15]. She stated she had jokingly yelled "Look, poltergeists!" and that Price had smiled and played along with the joke, making her "astounded" to later see it presented seriously in his book [16, 17].

**Scherman's confirmation:** In 1956, photographer David Scherman confirmed Ledsham's account to investigator Michael Coleman. Scherman admitted he saw a workman throwing bricks and **thought it would be fun to position the camera so the rubble was visible but the workman was hidden** [18]. He assumed Price was "in on the joke" and was delighted by Price's "effrontery" in later passing it off as gospel fact [18, 19].

The skeptical authors of the *Borley Report* ([[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], and [[trevor-hall]]) seized upon Ledsham and Scherman's testimony as a "final accusation of flagrant misrepresentation against Price" [4, 20, 21, 22].

## Hastings's Defence

Investigator [[robert-hastings|Robert J. Hastings]] defended Price in his 1969 examination, pointing out major flaws in the accusations:

**No claim of paranormality:** Hastings noted that Price *never actually claimed* the brick was paranormal; he merely asked "If..." it was, making the SPR's charge of "flagrant misrepresentation" unsubstantiated [13, 23, 24].

**Inconsistencies in Ledsham's story:** Hastings highlighted that Ledsham changed her story. Price's photo showed a single brick rising *up* from the ground, while Ledsham's later statements claimed *several* bricks were being thrown *down or out* onto a pile from behind a wall [25–27]. Hastings argued her memory may have been distorted over time [28].

**Visual evidence in the photograph:** Other researchers analyzed the actual photograph and pointed out that a lone workman *is* clearly visible on the far left side of the image, facing the wrong way. The "floating" brick is on the opposite side of the frame [29]. They argue the brick's trajectory and position do not align with a workman tossing it, suggesting Scherman might simply have been lucky enough to catch a genuine anomaly on film [29, 30].

## Significance

The location adds symbolism: the kitchen passage was one of the most persistently active areas of the Rectory throughout its history, the site of multiple wall-messages and the focus of decades of poltergeist activity. The Rectory was fully demolished within weeks of this photograph being taken.
