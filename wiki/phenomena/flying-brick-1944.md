# The Flying Brick (April 5, 1944)

One of the final documented paranormal incidents at Borley — and uniquely, the only Borley phenomenon captured on photograph. Occurred on April 5, 1944, during a visit by [[harry-price|Harry Price]], [[david-scherman|David E. Scherman]] (*Life* magazine photographer), and [[cynthia-ledsham|Miss Cynthia Ledsham]] (*Time and Life* researcher).

## The Incident

Scherman was photographing the rapidly dwindling ruins of the Rectory from a distance of approximately one hundred feet. As he pressed the trigger of his miniature camera (American make), all three observers simultaneously saw a brick — or part of a brick — shoot approximately **four feet into the air** in front of what remained of the kitchen passage, just below where the bathroom passage had been.

All three saw it. They walked over immediately and examined the bricks in the area. All appeared normal. No wires or strings were attached to any of them. No workmen were visible on that side of the Rectory.

## The Photograph

The observers laughed it off as "the last phenomenon." A few days later, Scherman sent Price proof-sheets of his photographs. Price was unremarkable about the images until Scherman telephoned and asked whether Price had noticed "anything about the photograph of the ruins."

Scherman had **opened his shutter at the precise fraction-of-a-second when the brick was at the apex of its flight**. Enlarged ten-by-eight prints reveal a white rectangular shape against the dark background of the kitchen passage, perfectly stationary (no motion blur) — a Poltergeist projectile suspended in mid-air.

Price reproduces the enlarged section as Plate XXIII, confirming that none of the photographs in the book were retouched.

## Significance

Price calls this "the first photograph ever taken of a Poltergeist projectile in flight." The claim stands: there is no other case in the Borley record — or, Price implies, in the broader psychical research literature — of a paranormal projectile captured photographically at the moment of flight, rather than as a settled or found object.

The location adds symbolism: the kitchen passage was one of the most persistently active areas of the Rectory throughout its history, the site of multiple wall-messages and the focus of decades of poltergeist activity. That the final photographed incident should occur there was, to Price, "interesting."

The Rectory was fully demolished within weeks of this photograph being taken.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**The "Flying Brick"** incident occurred on **5 April 1944**, during the final demolition of Borley Rectory. It centers on a photograph that allegedly captured a brick suspended in mid-air, which became a major focal point in the debate over Harry Price's honesty and his methodology.

A thorough review of the sources reveals the following recorded instances, witness descriptions, and proposed explanations:

**The Incident and Witness Descriptions**
*   **The Witnesses:** The event was witnessed by ghost hunter **Harry Price**, **David E. Scherman** (a photographer for the American magazine *Life* and an accredited US Army war correspondent), and **Cynthia Ledsham** (a researcher and archivist for *Time and Life Ltd.*) [1-3]. 
*   **The Event:** The trio visited Borley to take photographs for an article on "Haunted England" [2, 3]. Because the building was largely demolished, Scherman stood about 100 feet away to frame the remaining ruins, with Price and Ledsham by his side [4, 5]. Just as Scherman snapped the shutter, a brick (or part of a brick) suddenly shot up about four feet into the air in front of the remains of the kitchen passage [5].
*   **Initial Reaction:** According to Price's account, all three saw the brick, laughed, and called it "the last phenomenon," joking that the poltergeists were "demonstrating" in honor of their visit [5].
*   **The Photograph:** When Scherman developed the film, the fast shutter speed had frozen the brick perfectly in mid-air as a white dot against the dark background of the passage, showing no signs of movement blur and no strings or wires attached [6-8]. 

**Proposed Explanations and Source Perspectives**

**1. Harry Price's Perspective (Paranormal Possibility)**
Price published the photograph and an enlargement of it in his 1946 book, *The End of Borley Rectory* (*EBR*) [4, 7]. 
*   He noted the significance of the location, stating it was the former kitchen passage where wall writings (such as "Marianne-Light-Mass-Prayers") and many other manifestations had previously occurred [9, 10].
*   Price carefully refrained from stating definitively that the event was supernatural, instead writing: **"If, indeed, this was a genuine paranormal phenomenon, then we have the first photograph ever taken of a Poltergeist projectile in flight"** [9, 11].
*   Defending his account in a 1946 letter to Dr. Eric Dingwall, Price offered £1,000 to charity if anyone could prove it was faked. He insisted there was "no trickery," adding that they were scores of yards away, saw no workmen on that side of the ruins, and hypothesized that the brick might have been thrown from a long distance and bounced [12, 13].

**2. Cynthia Ledsham and David Scherman (The "Hoax" / Joke Theory)**
Years later, the two *Life* journalists completely contradicted Price's suggestion of paranormality, insisting it was a mundane event that Price deliberately misrepresented.
*   **Ledsham's Accusation:** In a letter to the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) and a subsequent 1950 interview, Ledsham (then Mrs. Thompson) accused Price of **"bare-faced hocus pocus"** [14, 15]. She claimed that a **"brawny workman"** was actively engaged in demolition behind a wall, propelling bricks out at regular intervals [14, 15]. She stated she had jokingly yelled "Look, poltergeists!" and that Price had smiled and played along with the joke, making her "astounded" to later see it presented seriously in his book [16, 17].
*   **Scherman's Confirmation:** In 1956, photographer David Scherman confirmed Ledsham's account to investigator Michael Coleman. Scherman admitted he saw a workman throwing bricks and **thought it would be fun to position the camera so the rubble was visible but the workman was hidden** [18]. He assumed Price was "in on the joke" and was delighted by Price's "effrontery" and "adventurous spirit" in later passing it off as gospel fact [18, 19].

**3. The SPR Investigators (Accusation of Fraud)**
The highly sceptical authors of the *Borley Report* (Eric Dingwall, Kathleen Goldney, and Trevor Hall) seized upon Ledsham and Scherman's testimony as a **"final accusation of flagrant misrepresentation against Price"** [4, 20]. They argued that Price deliberately suppressed the presence of the brick-throwing workman to deceive his readers and artificially bolster the Borley legend [14, 21, 22]. 

**4. Robert J. Hastings and Pro-Price Researchers (Defense of Price)**
Investigator Robert J. Hastings defended Price in his 1969 examination of the SPR report, pointing out major flaws in the accusations:
*   **No Claim of Paranormality:** Hastings noted that Price *never actually claimed* the brick was paranormal; he merely asked "If..." it was, making the SPR's charge of "flagrant misrepresentation" unsubstantiated [13, 23, 24]. 
*   **Inconsistencies in Ledsham's Story:** Hastings highlighted that Ledsham changed her story. Price's photo showed a single brick rising *up* from the ground, while Ledsham's later statements claimed *several* bricks were being thrown *down or out* onto a pile from behind a wall [25-27]. Hastings argued her memory may have been distorted over time [28].
*   **Visual Evidence in the Photograph:** Other researchers (such as the author of *Enigma of Borley*) analyzed the actual photograph and pointed out that a lone workman *is* clearly visible on the far left side of the image, facing the wrong way. The "floating" brick is on the opposite side of the frame [29]. They argue the brick's trajectory and position do not align with a workman tossing it, suggesting Scherman might have simply been lucky enough to catch a genuine anomaly on film, and that Price had reported it accurately [29, 30].
