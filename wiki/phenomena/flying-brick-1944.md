# The Flying Brick (April 5, 1944)

One of the final documented paranormal incidents at Borley — and uniquely, the only Borley phenomenon captured on photograph. Occurred on April 5, 1944, during a visit by [[harry-price|Harry Price]], [[david-scherman|David E. Scherman]] (*Life* magazine photographer), and [[cynthia-ledsham|Miss Cynthia Ledsham]] (*Time and Life* researcher).

## The Incident

Scherman was photographing the rapidly dwindling ruins of the Rectory from a distance of approximately one hundred feet. As he pressed the trigger of his miniature camera (American make), all three observers simultaneously saw a brick — or part of a brick — shoot approximately **four feet into the air** in front of what remained of the kitchen passage, just below where the bathroom passage had been.

All three saw it. They walked over immediately and examined the bricks in the area. All appeared normal. No wires or strings were attached to any of them. No workmen were visible on that side of the Rectory.

## The Photograph

The observers laughed it off as "the last phenomenon." A few days later, Scherman sent Price proof-sheets of his photographs. Price was unremarkable about the images until Scherman telephoned and asked whether Price had noticed "anything about the photograph of the ruins."

Scherman had **opened his shutter at the precise fraction-of-a-second when the brick was at the apex of its flight**. Enlarged ten-by-eight prints reveal a white rectangular shape against the dark background of the kitchen passage, perfectly stationary (no motion blur) — a Poltergeist projectile suspended in mid-air.

Price reproduces the enlarged section as Plate XXIII, confirming that none of the photographs in the book were retouched.

## Significance

Price calls this "the first photograph ever taken of a Poltergeist projectile in flight." The claim stands: there is no other case in the Borley record — or, Price implies, in the broader psychical research literature — of a paranormal projectile captured photographically at the moment of flight, rather than as a settled or found object.

The location adds symbolism: the kitchen passage was one of the most persistently active areas of the Rectory throughout its history, the site of multiple wall-messages and the focus of decades of poltergeist activity. That the final photographed incident should occur there was, to Price, "interesting."

The Rectory was fully demolished within weeks of this photograph being taken.
