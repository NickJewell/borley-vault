# The Phantom Light in the Window

A mysterious light seen in the windows of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] by many witnesses over a prolonged period — most commonly in Room 7 (the room in the attic/tower area in which several Bull family members died) — frequently when the house was known to be empty and locked. Distinct from the window-apparitions seen during and after the 1939 fire.

## Nature

The light appears as an illumination in an upstairs room window visible from the road or garden, but investigation from inside the room reveals no light source present. The anomaly: the light is visible *outside* the room while investigators are *inside* it confirming nothing is there.

## Smith Incumbency (1928–1929)

The Rev. [[rev-eric-smith|Guy E. Smith]] and his wife [[mabel-smith|Mabel]] frequently noticed a mysterious light in the windows of unoccupied rooms [1–3]. Mrs. Smith often pointed out this illuminated window to neighbours, including the [[payne-family|Payne family]] of Borley Place [1, 4]. On one occasion, Mrs. Smith saw the window illuminated and assumed her maid was inside with a lamp; upon entering the house, she found the maid downstairs and the upstairs room completely dark [1]. On another occasion, she pointed the lighted window out to the church choir, but when the group went upstairs, the room was in darkness [1].

**June 10–11, 1929 (The *Daily Mirror* Vigil):** Reporter [[vc-wall|V. C. Wall]] and his photographer held a night vigil in the Rectory grounds [5, 6]. They distinctly saw a mysterious light shining through a window in a disused, deserted wing of the building [5, 7]. To test the phenomenon, Rev. Smith volunteered to go inside and place a real lamp in an adjacent window for comparison. When he did, both lights were visible side-by-side; however, as the watchers approached the building, the mysterious light vanished, while the Rector's lamp continued to burn [7–9].

**Mrs. Smith's retraction (1949):** Twenty years after leaving Borley, Mrs. Smith submitted a statement to the [[spr|Society for Psychical Research]] claiming she had ultimately discovered the cause of the light: it was merely a reflection in the window glass from passing trains on the Stour Valley railway line [10, 11].

> **Contradiction:** The SPR authors supported Mrs. Smith's mundane explanation, suggesting passing trains, parked cars, or distant lights from Sudbury could easily reflect off the Rectory's windows [11–13]. However, investigators [[steuart-kiernander|S. F. C. Kiernander]] and [[ivan-banks]] heavily criticised the train-reflection theory, pointing out that the railway line was half a mile away, the slow-moving trains of that era were dimly lit by gas, and the Rectory was completely surrounded by tall, thick trees that would have easily blocked any distant light [12, 14, 15].

During the empty Rectory period (1929–30), Mrs. Smith's letters to Price report that "the villagers still see the 'light' in the Rectory" even while it was locked and unoccupied. Between 1935–37 (the period when the Rectory was empty and for sale), villagers occasionally saw the strange light in Room 7.

## Price Tenancy and Official Observers (1937–1938)

During [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s year-long rental of the empty Rectory, the lights continued to appear even when the building was entirely locked and unoccupied.

**November 8, 1937:** Neighbours Mr. and Mrs. [[payne-family|Basil Payne]] reported seeing a bright light shining from the south-facing window of Room 11 [16, 17]. On the same night, [[herbert-mayes|Herbert Mayes]] (the Rev. A. C. [[rev-ac-henning|Henning]]'s chauffeur) saw a bright light in the large window on the main stairs [15–17]. Three days later, the Hennings' maid also reported a light in a Rectory window [16, 18, 19].

The Rev. Henning immediately checked with the caretaker, Mr. Arbon, who confirmed the Rectory was completely locked and unoccupied on those nights [18, 19]. Henning even hired a painter to inspect the window frames to see if luminescent paint was responsible, which it was not [20]. The SPR authors suggested the light might have been a reflection from Mr. Arbon's courtyard lantern, but admitted that the light seen by the Paynes and Mayes was "too bright for an explanation of this kind" [21].

[[mark-kerr-pearse]] and [[rev-ac-henning|Henning]] were among those who noted the phenomenon during the Price tenancy. Appendix C records [[herbert-mayes]], Mrs. Henning, and multiple unnamed villagers seeing the light. Several of Price's official observers noted it during vigils.

**February 16–17, 1938 — The Blue Room Ceiling (S. G. Welles's luminous phenomenon):** Oxford undergraduate S. G. Welles, alone in the darkened [[blue-room|Blue Room]], observed a luminous rectangular patch (approximately 12" × 5") on the ceiling, moving slowly back and forth for about a minute [22–24]. Subsequent experiments by Welles and three colleagues with all types of external light sources — torches, candles, matches, and car headlights — completely failed to reproduce it [26, 27]. He wrote: "It was clearly outlined... leisurely yet precise." This is the only in-room luminous phenomenon in the record. They noted that all artificial lights created defined shadows, whereas the luminous patch on the ceiling had produced none [26].

## Post-Fire Sightings (1939–1944)

Even after the Rectory was destroyed by fire on 27 February 1939, illuminated anomalies continued in the ruins.

**October 31, 1941:** Dr. A. J. B. Robertson of the "Cambridge Commission" observed a square luminous patch on the ground floor wall between the sewing room and the corridor at 12:45 a.m. [28, 29]. It lasted for about a second, and Robertson explicitly noted it could not have been caused by moonlight [29, 30].

**April 30–May 1, 1944:** While the Rectory was being actively demolished, observers C. J. Lethbridge and T. Sullivan saw a "pale, indistinct white light varying slightly in size" between 3:30 a.m. and 4:00 a.m. [31, 32]. It appeared near the remains of Bedrooms 1 and 2, and then moved to the larder region [32]. The investigators noted this could not have been caused by their own torches, as they were all accounted for [32]. The SPR noted a suspicious detail: one of the observers sleeping in the ruins during this light phenomenon was P. H. Lord, an undergraduate who had previously impersonated the ghost nun as a practical joke on his fellow investigators [27].

## Later Borley (Post-Demolition)

Once the Rectory was entirely erased from the landscape, the phenomena seemingly moved to the trees and surrounding grounds.

**August 20, 1949:** Members of the Ealing Society for the Investigation of Psychic Phenomena saw a strong, white light emanating from a branch at the top of the trees near the village road [33, 34]. It lasted for two or three seconds, and they confirmed there were no trains or traffic passing at the time [34].

**April 8, 1961:** An investigating party including St. John Saunders and Capt. Ben Martindale watched vivid blue pinpoints of light dancing among the trees on the former Rectory site at 4:00 a.m. [35, 36]. Saunders insisted that street lighting or natural reflections could not explain the lights, which hovered ten to fifteen feet in the air [36].

## Who Has Seen It

Appendix D lists approximately twenty named witnesses including: Rev. and Mrs. Smith and choir boys; [[payne-family|Basil Payne]], Mrs. Payne (from Borley Place opposite); [[vc-wall|V. C. Wall]]; [[mark-kerr-pearse]]; Rev. and Mrs. [[rev-ac-henning|Henning]]; [[dr-hf-bellamy|Dr. Bellamy]]; Bailey and Wintour; J. Burden; Drinkwater's party; [[herbert-mayes|Herbert Mayes]]; Henning's maid; "many unnamed villagers."

## Significance

The phantom window-light is the most repeatedly reported *visual* phenomenon by external observers — seen from outside the building across multiple decades, including during periods of verified vacancy. The specific room (Room 7 / the tower room) is consistently associated with Bull-family deaths and with heavy footstep phenomena. The light's visibility while investigators inside find no source makes normal explanation difficult. The BRC judges the natural explanations given by the *Borley Report* as "untenable" in several cases.
