# The Phantom Light in the Window

A mysterious light seen in the windows of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] by many witnesses over a prolonged period — most commonly in Room 7 (the room in the attic/tower area in which several Bull family members died) — frequently when the house was known to be empty and locked. Distinct from the window-apparitions seen during and after the 1939 fire.

## Nature

The light appears as a illumination in an upstairs room window visible from the road or garden, but investigation from inside the room reveals no light source present. The anomaly: the light is visible *outside* the room while investigators are *inside* it confirming nothing is there.

## Sighting Record

**June 11, 1929 — V. C. Wall**: On the evening before [[harry-price|Price's]] first visit, Wall and others saw a strange light in the window of Room 7 (a room that was unfurnished and unused). Immediate investigation showed no light source in the room — yet the light was still visible from the garden while investigators were inside confirming its absence. "A number of the Bull family died in this room."

**During the Smith incumbency**: The Smiths and neighbouring choir boys saw a strange light in an upper window.

**Empty Rectory period (1929–30)**: Mrs. Smith's letters to Price report that "the villagers still see the 'light' in the Rectory" even while it was locked and unoccupied.

**1935–37 (between Foysters and Price)**: Villagers occasionally saw the strange light in Room 7 window during the period when the Rectory was empty and for sale.

**During Price's tenancy**: [[mark-kerr-pearse|Kerr-Pearse]] and [[rev-ac-henning|Henning]] among those who noted the phenomenon. Appendix C (mhh-app-c) records Herbert Mayes, Mrs. Henning, and multiple unnamed villagers seeing the light. Several of Price's official observers noted it during vigils.

## Who Has Seen It

Appendix D lists approximately 20 named witnesses including: Rev. and Mrs. Smith and choir boys; Basil Payne, Mrs. Payne (from Borley Place opposite); V. C. Wall; [[mark-kerr-pearse]]; Rev. and Mrs. [[rev-ac-henning|Henning]]; Dr. Bellamy; Bailey and Wintour; J. Burden; Drinkwater's party; [[herbert-mayes|Herbert Mayes]]; Henning's maid; "many unnamed villagers."

## S. G. Welles's Luminous Phenomenon (February 1938)

Distinct but related: Oxford undergraduate S. G. Welles, alone in the darkened [[blue-room|Blue Room]], observed a luminous rectangular patch (approximately 12" × 5") on the ceiling, moving slowly back and forth for about a minute. Subsequent experiments with all types of external light sources failed to reproduce it. He wrote: "It was clearly outlined... leisurely yet precise." This is the only in-room luminous phenomenon in the record.

## Significance

The phantom window-light is the most repeatedly reported *visual* phenomenon by external observers — seen from outside the building across multiple decades, including during periods of verified vacancy. The specific room (Room 7 / the tower room) is consistently associated with Bull-family deaths and with heavy footstep phenomena. The light's visibility while investigators inside find no source makes normal explanation difficult.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

The "Phantom Light" (often referred to in the sources as the "mysterious light" or "window lights") is one of the most consistently reported visual phenomena at Borley Rectory. Witnessed by rectors, local villagers, journalists, and independent investigators, these unexplained illuminations were reported in both occupied and deserted rooms, persisting even after the Rectory was gutted by fire.

A comprehensive review of the Borley sources reveals the following recorded instances, witness descriptions, and proposed explanations:

### **The Smith Incumbency (1928–1929)**
*   **The Incidents:** The Rev. Guy E. Smith and his wife Mabel frequently noticed a mysterious light in the windows of unoccupied rooms [1-3]. Mrs. Smith often pointed out this illuminated window to neighbours, including the Payne family of Borley Place [1, 4]. 
*   **June 10–11, 1929 (The *Daily Mirror* Vigil):** Reporter V.C. Wall and his photographer held a night vigil in the Rectory grounds [5, 6]. They distinctly saw a mysterious light shining through a window in a disused, deserted wing of the building [5, 7]. To test the phenomenon, the Rev. Smith volunteered to go inside and place a real lamp in an adjacent window for comparison [7, 8]. When he did, both lights were visible side-by-side; however, as the watchers approached the building, the mysterious light vanished, while the Rector's lamp continued to burn [8, 9]. 
*   **Source Perspectives & Explanations:**
    *   **Mabel Smith's Retraction (1949):** Twenty years after leaving Borley, Mrs. Smith submitted a statement to the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) claiming she had ultimately discovered the cause of the light: it was merely a reflection in the window glass from passing trains [10, 11]. 
    *   **The SPR Sceptics:** The authors of the *Borley Report* (Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) supported Mrs. Smith's mundane explanation, suggesting that passing or parked cars, or distant lights from the town of Sudbury, could easily reflect off the Rectory's windows [11-13].
    *   **The Counter-Argument:** Proponents of the haunting, including investigator S.F.C. Kiernander and author Ivan Banks, heavily criticized the train-reflection theory [14, 15]. They pointed out that the Stour Valley railway line was half a mile away, the trains of that era were dimly lit by gas, and the Rectory was completely surrounded by tall, thick trees that would have easily blocked any distant light [12, 14, 15].

### **The Price Tenancy & Official Observers (1937–1938)**
During Harry Price's year-long rental of the empty Rectory, the lights continued to appear even when the building was entirely locked and unoccupied.
*   **November 8 & 11, 1937:** Mr. and Mrs. Basil Payne saw a bright light shining from the south-facing window of Room 11 [16, 17]. On the same night, Herbert Mayes (the Rev. A.C. Henning’s chauffeur) saw a bright light in the large window on the main stairs [17, 18]. Three days later, the Hennings' maid also reported a light in a Rectory window [18]. 
    *   **Explanations:** The Rev. Henning immediately checked with the caretaker, Mr. Arbon, who confirmed that the Rectory was completely locked and unoccupied on those nights [18, 19]. Henning even asked a painter to inspect the window to see if luminescent paint was responsible, which it was not [20]. The SPR authors suggested the light might have been a reflection from Mr. Arbon's courtyard lantern, but admitted that the light seen by the Paynes and Mayes was "too bright for an explanation of this kind" [21].
*   **February 16–17, 1938 (The Blue Room Ceiling):** Oxford undergraduate S.G. Welles was on duty in the darkened Blue Room when he saw a clearly outlined "luminous patch of light" shaped like a lambent flame on the ceiling [22-24]. It moved slowly back and forth for about a minute [25]. 
    *   **Explanations:** Welles and his three colleagues conducted extensive experiments by shining torches, candles, matches, and car headlights into the room from outside to recreate the effect [26, 27]. They completely failed, noting that all artificial lights created defined shadows, whereas the luminous patch on the ceiling had produced none [26].

### **Post-Fire Sightings (1939–1944)**
Even after the Rectory was destroyed by fire on February 27, 1939, illuminated anomalies continued in the ruins. 
*   **October 31, 1941:** Dr. A.J.B. Robertson of the "Cambridge Commission" observed a square luminous patch on the ground floor wall between the sewing room and the corridor at 12:45 a.m. [28, 29]. It lasted for about a second, and Robertson explicitly noted it could not have been caused by moonlight [29, 30]. 
*   **April 30–May 1, 1944:** While the Rectory was being actively demolished, observers C.J. Lethbridge and T. Sullivan saw a pale, indistinct white light varying slightly in size [31, 32]. It appeared near the remains of Bedrooms 1 and 2, and then moved to the larder region [32]. The investigators noted this could not have been caused by their own torches, as they were accounted for [32].

### **Later Borley (Post-Demolition)**
Once the Rectory was entirely erased from the landscape, the phenomena seemingly moved to the trees and surrounding grounds.
*   **August 20, 1949:** Members of the Ealing Society for the Investigation of Psychic Phenomena saw a strong, white light emanating from a branch at the top of the trees near the village road [33, 34]. It lasted for two or three seconds, and they confirmed there were no trains or traffic passing at the time [34].
*   **April 8, 1961:** An investigating party including St. John Saunders and Capt. Ben Martindale watched vivid blue pinpoints of light dancing among the trees on the former Rectory site at 4:00 a.m. [35, 36]. Saunders insisted that street lighting or natural reflections could not explain the lights, which hovered ten to fifteen feet in the air [36].
