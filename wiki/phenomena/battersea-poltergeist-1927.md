# The Battersea Poltergeist (Eland Road, 1927–28)

A poltergeist case at **Eland Road, Battersea**, investigated by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] in early 1928, approximately eighteen months before the Borley investigation began. The case prefigures Borley in several important ways and is directly cited in the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] as evidence of Price's modus operandi. The backyard of the house bordered a psychiatric home for veterans suffering from shell shock from the Great War [1].

## The House and Family

**Henry Robinson**, an eighty-six-year-old invalid, had lived at a modest terrace on Eland Road for twenty-five years with his son Fred and three daughters — Lilla, Kate, and Eleanor (a widow with a thirteen-year-old son, Peter). The strange occurrences began on 29 November 1927: lumps of coal, soda and pennies fell on a glazed lean-to at the rear of the house. Small pieces of coal and pennies continued to break the glass.

Thinking someone was throwing things over the garden wall from the adjacent psychiatric home, the Robinsons called the police. The phenomena continued even when a constable was present — hitting his helmet — but the source could not be found.

Just before Christmas, heavy bangs disturbed the house; Mr Robinson's bedroom window was smashed; chests of drawers crashed to the floor. Slips of white paper began to appear on the stairs with messages apparently made by a pin — some mundane, some threatening. One signed "Tom Blood: I am having a bad time here. I cannot rest. I was born during the reign of William the Conqueror." Other similar messages were signed "Jessie Blood" [4].

## Price's Investigation

**Eve Brackenbury**, secretary to [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] at the SPR, first investigated and concluded that the thirteen-year-old Peter was responsible. Price visited in January 1928 with a journalist from the *London Evening News*, and then again on subsequent occasions. Each time, phenomena seemed to occur while he was there.

During Price's January 1928 visit, a gas lighter — strictly kept in the scullery and seen there moments before — was inexplicably thrown into the passage (or discovered on the kitchen dresser, depending on the account) [2, 5]. During a subsequent visit by Price and two reporters, the group was asked to wait in the front sitting room, where two small metal figures sat on the mantelpiece. When the group was taken to the kitchen, a heavy thud was heard behind the reporters. Price personally picked up a shoe to reveal that one of the metal figures from the front room was inside it [5].

Two SPR members became convinced the Robinson family was responsible. Fred Robinson was taken to hospital for mental health assessment; in his absence, phenomena continued. The sisters then fled the house after the sightseers attracted by publicity became frightening — a direct precursor of what would happen at Borley the following year.

## Proposed Explanations

**Peter as hoaxer (SPR):** [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] and Eve Brackenbury concluded definitively that the phenomena were deliberately faked by thirteen-year-old Peter [3, 6]. When this conclusion was presented to the family, they subjected the SPR to "a certain amount of abuse" [3].

**Price as medium (Robinson family):** Because spectacular things seemed to happen only when Price was in the room, Lilla Robinson and Mrs. Perkins explicitly asked Brackenbury whether Price possessed a "power" that attracted things to him. Brackenbury told them this was "not probable" [5].

**Genuine phenomena (Price):** Price rejected the SPR's theory and considered the Robinson family "entirely trustworthy" [2]. He eventually featured the case in two books: *Confessions of a Ghost-Hunter* (1936) and *Poltergeist Over England* (1945). Skeptics note that Price used the incident of the teleporting gas lighter to secure an "excellent stop-press paragraph for the evening papers," suggesting a motive rooted in his desire for publicity [2].

## Parallels with Borley

The Battersea case established several patterns that would repeat at [[borley-rectory|Borley]]:

1. **A family in distress** in a house that had acquired a reputation
2. **Press involvement** — Price brought a journalist on each visit
3. **Phenomena occurring around Price** — the family noted that "things always seemed to happen" when he was around
4. **Sightseers** overwhelming the investigation
5. **Price's private verdict differing from his published account** — in a letter to Arnold Lunn, Price stated privately that the family "wanted to force their invalid father out of the house so that they could sell it," but said publicly he could only "write nice things about people"

> **Contested:** The [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] cited the Robinson family's complaint that "things always seemed to happen" when Price was present at Eland Road as evidence of a consistent pattern of his manufacturing phenomena.
