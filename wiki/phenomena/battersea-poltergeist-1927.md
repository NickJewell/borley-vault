# The Battersea Poltergeist (Eland Road, 1927–28)

A poltergeist case at **Eland Road, Battersea**, investigated by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] in early 1928, approximately eighteen months before the Borley investigation began. The case prefigures Borley in several important ways and is directly cited in the [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] as evidence of Price's modus operandi.

## The House and Family

**Henry Robinson**, an eighty-six-year-old invalid, had lived at a modest terrace on Eland Road for twenty-five years with his son Fred and three daughters — Lilla, Kate, and Eleanor (a widow with a thirteen-year-old son, Peter). The strange occurrences began on 29 November 1927: lumps of coal, soda and pennies fell on a glazed lean-to at the rear of the house. Small pieces of coal and pennies continued to break the glass.

Thinking someone was throwing things over the garden wall from the adjacent psychiatric home for veterans, the Robinsons called the police. The phenomena continued even when a constable was present — hitting his helmet — but the source could not be found.

Just before Christmas, heavy bangs disturbed the house; Mr Robinson's bedroom window was smashed; chests of drawers crashed to the floor. Slips of white paper began to appear on the stairs with messages apparently made by a pin — some mundane, some threatening. One signed "Tom Blood: I am having a bad time here. I cannot rest. I was born during the reign of William the Conqueror."

## Price's Investigation

**Eve Brackenbury**, secretary to [[eric-dingwall|Eric Dingwall]] at the SPR, first investigated and concluded that the thirteen-year-old Peter was responsible. Price visited in January 1928 with a journalist from the *London Evening News*, and then again on subsequent occasions. Each time, phenomena seemed to occur while he was there.

Two SPR members became convinced the Robinson family was responsible. Fred Robinson was taken to hospital for mental health assessment; in his absence, phenomena continued. The sisters then fled the house after the sightseers attracted by publicity became frightening — a direct precursor of what would happen at Borley the following year.

## Parallels with Borley

The Battersea case established several patterns that would repeat at Borley:
1. **A family in distress** in a house that had acquired a reputation
2. **Press involvement** — Price brought a journalist on each visit
3. **Phenomena occurring around Price** — the family noted that "things always seemed to happen" when he was around
4. **Sightseers** overwhelming the investigation
5. **Price's private verdict differing from his published account** — in a letter to Arnold Lunn, Price stated privately that the family "wanted to force their invalid father out of the house so that they could sell it," but said publicly he could only "write nice things about people"

> **Contested:** The [[the-borley-report|Borley Report]] cited the Robinson family's complaint that "things always seemed to happen" when Price was present at Eland Road as evidence of a consistent pattern of his manufacturing phenomena.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

The **Battersea Poltergeist** case centered around a modest, suburban terraced house on Eland Road, just off Lavender Hill in Battersea, London [1]. The Borley sources use this 1927–1928 case to illustrate the early investigative career of Harry Price and the Society for Psychical Research (SPR), as well as to highlight the tendency for phenomena to frequently occur in Price's presence.

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following recorded instances, witness descriptions, and proposed explanations:

**The House and its Occupants**
The house was occupied by eighty-six-year-old invalid Henry Robinson, his son Fred, and his three daughters, Lilla, Kate, and Eleanor (a widow who lived there with her thirteen-year-old son, Peter) [1]. The backyard of the house bordered a psychiatric home for veterans suffering from shell shock from the Great War [1]. 

**Recorded Instances and Witness Descriptions**
*   **The Initial Disturbances (November 1927):** The phenomena began on 29 November 1927, when lumps of coal, soda, and pennies began falling onto the glass roof of the family's rear conservatory (lean-to) [1]. The activity escalated in December, resulting in broken windows, smashed ornaments, and damaged furniture [1, 2]. 
*   **The "Blood" Messages:** Small slips of white paper began appearing on the stairs, bearing messages written as if pricked by a pin [3]. Fred Robinson discovered one slip signed by **"Tom. Blood,"** which read: *"I am having a bad time here. I cannot rest. I was born during the reign of William the Conqueror"* [3, 4]. Other similar messages were found signed by **"Jessie Blood"** [4].
*   **The Gas Lighter Teleportation (January 1928):** When Harry Price visited the house in January 1928 alongside a journalist from the *London Evening News*, a gas lighter—which was strictly kept in the scullery and had been seen there moments before—was inexplicably thrown onto the floor of the passage (or discovered on the kitchen dresser, depending on the account) [2, 5]. 
*   **The Metal Figure in the Shoe:** During a subsequent visit by Price and two reporters, the group was asked to wait in the front sitting room, where two small metal figures of children sat on the mantelpiece [5]. The group was then taken to the kitchen. Suddenly, a "heavy thud" was heard dropping to the floor right behind the reporters, near Price, Lilla, and a Mrs. Perkins [5]. A search ensued, and **Price personally picked up a shoe to reveal that one of the metal figures from the front room was inside it** [5].

**Proposed Explanations and Source Perspectives**
The sources record several wildly different explanations for the events at Eland Road, depending on the perspective of the investigator or witness:

*   **The Family's Initial Theory (Mundane):** When the coal and pennies first began breaking the conservatory glass, the Robinsons assumed someone was physically throwing objects over the garden wall from the neighbouring psychiatric home, and they called the police to investigate [1].
*   **The SPR's Theory (Trickery):** The Society for Psychical Research sent Dr. V. J. Woolley and Eve Brackenbury to investigate the house [3, 6]. Brackenbury concluded definitively that the phenomena were **deliberately faked by thirteen-year-old Peter** [3]. When she presented this conclusion to the family, they subjected the SPR to "a certain amount of abuse," though they eventually calmed down [3].
*   **The Family's Theory Regarding Price (Mediumship):** Because spectacular things seemed to happen only when Harry Price was in the room, Lilla Robinson and Mrs. Perkins explicitly asked Eve Brackenbury if she thought **Harry Price himself was a medium who possessed a "power" that attracted things to him** [5]. Brackenbury told the family this was "not probable" [5].
*   **Harry Price's Perspective (Genuine Phenomena / Sensationalism):** Price rejected the SPR's theory of a hoaxing teenager and found the exhausted Robinson family to be "entirely trustworthy" [2]. He considered the manifestations genuine and eventually featured the Battersea case in two of his books, *Confessions of a Ghost-Hunter* (1936) and *Poltergeist Over England* (1945) [2, 7]. Skeptics in the sources point out that Price used the incident of the teleporting gas lighter to secure an **"excellent stop-press paragraph for the evening papers,"** suggesting a motive rooted in his constant desire for publicity [2].
