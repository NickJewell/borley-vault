# The June 1931 East Anglian Earthquake

A significant earthquake that struck East Anglia in the night of 7–8 June 1931, coinciding with one of the most intense bursts of poltergeist activity recorded at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] during the Foyster period.

## The Event

The earthquake was described by O'Connor as "the biggest earthquake in the East of England since 1884" and struck with full force during what was already an extreme period of poltergeist activity at the Rectory.

- Cockerels began crowing suddenly in the darkness
- Farm dogs started barking feverishly
- Many locals were woken by doorbells ringing, beds rocking, crockery rattling, or pictures swinging on walls
- Whole houses seemed to sway or shake
- Lieutenant Colonel Long of Hall Farm, Fornham Saint Martin, thought the farmhouse "was going to fall"; it was "a horrible experience"
- The initial tremor lasted approximately forty-five seconds, with vibrations continuing for another twenty minutes

## Coincidence with Borley Phenomena

The night of the earthquake was already one of chaos at the Rectory. [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] had been unwell and sleeping in a sick room when the poltergeist activity reached new levels. The next morning, the contents of the dirty-laundry basket were scattered throughout the hallway, stairs, and landing — along with shoes, bedclothes, books, and brushes.

[[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] had been visiting the Foysters that evening. When he arrived the following day after receiving Lionel's urgent note ("If you want to see things, come at once"), he found the house in extraordinary disorder.

## Interpretation

The coincidence of the earthquake with the Borley poltergeist peak raises questions about causation in both directions:
- Did the earthquake explain some of the phenomena as natural earth movements rather than paranormal activity?
- Or did the disruption of the earthquake serve merely as external context for what was already a particularly violent episode of whatever was happening at the Rectory?

O'Connor notes that Reverend Dinsdale Young, a minister at Westminster Central Hall, believed the earthquake — consistent with scriptural prophecy — heralded the second coming. The wider cultural climate of 1931 found the earthquake resonant as a symbol of troubled times.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

The **East Anglian Earthquake of June 1931** forms a dramatic geographical and psychological backdrop to the escalating phenomena at Borley Rectory during the Foyster incumbency. The Borley sources record several specific details regarding the atmospheric conditions, the quake's physical impact, and its perceived connection to both local supernatural activity and broader spiritual interpretations.

**Date and Atmospheric Preconditions**
*   The earthquake struck in the **"small hours of Sunday morning"** in early June 1931 [1]. 
*   Prior to the tremor, the region had experienced electrical storms, which were followed by an **unseasonable chill** and a remarkably black sky filled with brilliant stars [2].
*   An absolute calm settled over the countryside, during which **"not a single dog barked."** One local writer recalled this profound silence as being absolutely **"uncanny"** [2].

**Witness Descriptions and the Quake's Impact**
*   The event was officially recorded as the **strongest earthquake in the East of England since 1884** [1]. The initial violent tremor lasted for **forty-five seconds**, and the earth continued to vibrate for another twenty minutes [1].
*   The eerie silence of the night was shattered when cockerels suddenly began crowing in the pitch dark and farm dogs started barking feverishly [1].
*   Startled locals awoke to chaotic physical disturbances: **doorbells rang on their own, beds rocked, crockery rattled, and pictures swung wildly on the walls** [1]. 
*   Because whole houses seemed to sway and shake, many terrified residents rushed into the streets in their nightclothes, believing there had been an explosion, and could not be persuaded to return to their beds [1].
*   **Lieutenant Colonel Long**, a resident of Hall Farm in Fornham Saint Martin, specifically described the event as a **"horrible experience,"** noting that he genuinely thought his farmhouse "was going to fall" [1]. 

**Proposed Explanations and Source Perspectives**
*   **A Catalyst for the Haunting:** The sources note that this massive seismic event seemed to **"inspire such activity at the Rectory that weekend"** [3]. Following a relatively quiet period in May and early June, the earthquake—alongside the arrival of the controversial lodger François d'Arles—coincided with the phenomena at Borley Rectory taking on an "extraordinary new character," including the sudden appearance of the famous wall writings [3, 4].
*   **Apocalyptic Interpretations:** The sheer magnitude of the quake led to eschatological interpretations that resonated deeply with the anxious and troubled era between the World Wars [3, 5]. **Reverend Dinsdale Young**, a minister at Westminster Central Hall, publicly interpreted the earthquake through the lens of scripture, viewing it as a herald of the Second Coming [3]. He declared, "I think these earthquakes are an indication that the time is approaching, is nearer to us than we realise" [3].
