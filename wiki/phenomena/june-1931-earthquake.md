# The June 1931 East Anglian Earthquake

A significant earthquake that struck East Anglia in the night of 7–8 June 1931, coinciding with one of the most intense bursts of poltergeist activity recorded at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] during the Foyster period. The earthquake was officially recorded as the strongest in the East of England since 1884 [1].

## Atmospheric Preconditions

Prior to the tremor, the region had experienced electrical storms, which were followed by an unseasonable chill and a remarkably black sky filled with brilliant stars [2]. An absolute calm settled over the countryside, during which "not a single dog barked." One local writer recalled this profound silence as being absolutely "uncanny" [2].

## The Event

The initial violent tremor lasted approximately forty-five seconds, with vibrations continuing for another twenty minutes [1]:

- The eerie silence was shattered when cockerels suddenly began crowing in the pitch dark and farm dogs started barking feverishly [1]
- Startled locals awoke to chaotic physical disturbances: doorbells rang on their own, beds rocked, crockery rattled, and pictures swung wildly on the walls [1]
- Because whole houses seemed to sway and shake, many terrified residents rushed into the streets in their nightclothes, believing there had been an explosion, and could not be persuaded to return to their beds [1]
- **Lieutenant Colonel Long** of Hall Farm, Fornham Saint Martin, specifically described the event as a "horrible experience," noting that he genuinely thought his farmhouse "was going to fall" [1]

## Coincidence with Borley Phenomena

The night of the earthquake was already one of chaos at the Rectory. [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] had been unwell and sleeping in a sick room when the poltergeist activity reached new levels. The next morning, the contents of the dirty-laundry basket were scattered throughout the hallway, stairs, and landing — along with shoes, bedclothes, books, and brushes.

[[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] had been visiting the Foysters that evening. When he arrived the following day after receiving Lionel's urgent note ("If you want to see things, come at once"), he found the house in extraordinary disorder.

The sources note that this massive seismic event seemed to "inspire such activity at the Rectory that weekend" [3]. Following a relatively quiet period in May and early June, the earthquake — alongside the arrival of the controversial lodger [[francois-darles|François d'Arles]] — coincided with the phenomena at Borley taking on an "extraordinary new character," including the sudden appearance of the famous [[wall-writings|wall writings]] [3, 4].

## Interpretation

The coincidence of the earthquake with the Borley poltergeist peak raises questions about causation in both directions:
- Did the earthquake explain some of the phenomena as natural earth movements rather than paranormal activity?
- Or did the disruption of the earthquake serve merely as external context for what was already a particularly violent episode of whatever was happening at the Rectory?

The wider cultural climate of 1931 found the earthquake resonant as a symbol of troubled times. The Rev. Dinsdale Young, a minister at Westminster Central Hall, publicly interpreted the earthquake through the lens of scripture, declaring: "I think these earthquakes are an indication that the time is approaching, is nearer to us than we realise" [3]. He viewed it as a herald of the Second Coming [3, 5].
