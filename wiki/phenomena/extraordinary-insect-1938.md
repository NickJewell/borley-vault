# The Extraordinary Insect (August 1938)

An unidentified creature seen by artist [[mrs-margaret-wilson|Mrs. Margaret E. Wilson]] in the garden of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] on August 22, 1938, approximately 3 p.m. It is the most anomalous biological encounter in the Borley record, and the sole entry under "A Strange Insect" in Appendix D of *The Most Haunted House in England*.

## Witness

Mrs. Wilson was a professional artist and exhibitor, known specifically for her studies of insects and still life. She had no interest in psychical research and had come purely to paint the Rectory — having obtained permission from the Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|Alfred Henning]] rather than from Price, whose tenancy had expired on 19 May 1938. She was working alone on the lawn near the large summer-house, painting the south-east aspect of the building [1, 2].

## Description

> "I suddenly noticed a sound, such as of approaching wings or rushing wind coming towards me. Looking up, I saw the queerest object with impelling eyes advancing towards me at about eye level. It seemed to be coming out of a mist. It was accompanied by a wasp on its left, and this must have been the wings I heard."

Mrs. Wilson struck the creature with her mahlstick and apparently knocked it into the grass. She searched for it but failed to find it. She was sufficiently alarmed to have "no time to put my palette down," resulting in medium being upset on her skirt — a physical confirmation of her reaction.

Her retrospective description, written immediately on returning home:

> "I had never seen anything like it before, and should describe it as being quite **three inches in length**, the body **entirely black** and **composed of sections** enabling it to **bend and expand with ease**. Its eyes were **large and the colour of bloomy black grapes**. The object was **flattish**."

## Documentary Legacy

Upon returning home, Mrs. Wilson immediately painted the articulated creature from memory [5]. She later had her friend, **Miss Joyce Mercer**, re-draw the creature in black and white so that it would be suitable for reproduction [5, 6]. [[harry-price]] subsequently published this sketch in *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940) [6].

## Assessment

[[harry-price]] showed the sketch to "several naturalists and entomologists." He records: "they all declare that it is like 'nothing on earth'!" Price placed high value on Mrs. Wilson's testimony because her professional background in painting still life and insects meant she was a highly trained observer of such details [5]. He offers the account without explanation: "Impossible as this insect appeared, it is just one of the many 'impossible' things that have happened in this 'enchanted' Rectory."

> **Contested:** The sceptical interpretation — that Mrs. Wilson encountered an unfamiliar but real insect (possibly a large exotic beetle, a mantis, or similar species) and her distress produced a distorted description — is not refuted in the source. The authors of the *Borley Report* ([[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], and [[trevor-hall]]), as well as later chroniclers, classify the "Strange Insect" alongside the flying brick and the strange coat as prime examples of "[[borley-hysteria|Borley hysteria]]" [6, 7]. They argue that the incident was likely a trivial misidentification of a natural bug, which Price deliberately embellished, acting as a "journalist rather than... an objective psychical researcher" [6, 8]. However, the unanimous reaction of expert naturalists to the sketch as "nothing on earth" is specifically noted by Price.

## Context

August 1938 was during the final months of Price's association with the property (his tenancy expired May 19, 1938; Mrs. Wilson visited with Henning's separate permission). The date places her encounter approximately three months after the gold wedding-ring apport and approximately six months before the fire. No other witness at Borley describes a similar creature. The insect is not mentioned in any séance communication or planchette record.
