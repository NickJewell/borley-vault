# The Extraordinary Insect (August 1938)

An unidentified creature seen by artist [[mrs-margaret-wilson|Mrs. Margaret E. Wilson]] in the garden of Borley Rectory on August 22, 1938, approximately 3 p.m. It is the most anomalous biological encounter in the Borley record, and the sole entry under "A Strange Insect" in Appendix D of *The Most Haunted House in England*.

## Witness

Mrs. Wilson was a professional artist and exhibitor, known specifically for her studies of insects and still life. She had no interest in psychical research and had come purely to paint the Rectory. She was working alone on the lawn under a Cedar of Lebanon when the encounter occurred.

## Description

> "I suddenly noticed a sound, such as of approaching wings or rushing wind coming towards me. Looking up, I saw the queerest object with impelling eyes advancing towards me at about eye level. It seemed to be coming out of a mist. It was accompanied by a wasp on its left, and this must have been the wings I heard."

Mrs. Wilson struck the creature with her mahlstick and apparently knocked it into the grass. She searched for it but failed to find it.

Her retrospective description, written immediately on returning home:

> "I had never seen anything like it before, and should describe it as being quite **three inches in length**, the body **entirely black** and **composed of sections** enabling it to **bend and expand with ease**. Its eyes were **large and the colour of bloomy black grapes**. The object was **flattish**."

She was sufficiently alarmed to have "no time to put my palette down," resulting in medium being upset on her skirt — a physical confirmation of her reaction.

## Assessment

[[harry-price]] showed the sketch (painted immediately from memory, then re-drawn in black and white by Mrs. Wilson's friend Miss Joyce Mercer for publication) to "several naturalists and entomologists." He records: "they all declare that it is like 'nothing on earth'!"

Price offers the account without explanation: "Impossible as this insect appeared, it is just one of the many 'impossible' things that have happened in this 'enchanted' Rectory."

> **Contested:** The sceptical interpretation — that Mrs. Wilson encountered an unfamiliar but real insect (possibly a large exotic beetle, a mantis, or similar species) and her distress produced a distorted description — is not refuted in the source. However, the unanimous reaction of expert naturalists to the sketch as "nothing on earth" is specifically noted by Price.

## Context

August 1938 was during the final months of Price's tenancy (which expired May 19, 1938; Mrs. Wilson visited with Henning's separate permission). The date places her encounter approximately three weeks after the gold wedding-ring apport (May 9, 1938) and approximately six months before the fire.

No other witness at Borley describes a similar creature. The insect is not mentioned in any séance communication or planchette record.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**The Extraordinary Insect** (also referred to in the sources as the "Strange Insect") is one of the more bizarre visual phenomena reported at Borley Rectory. Occurring after the conclusion of Harry Price’s official observer tenancy, the incident highlights the highly varied nature of the Borley sightings and the polarizing ways in which Harry Price chose to report them.

A thorough reference profile drawn from the sources reveals the following recorded details, descriptions, and proposed explanations:

**The Witness and the Setting**
*   **The Witness:** **Mrs. Margaret E. Wilson**, a well-known painter and exhibitor. She was not a spiritualist, knew nothing about psychical research, and had no "psychic" interest in Borley; she simply liked painting old and picturesque houses [1]. 
*   **The Date:** **August 22, 1938** [2, 3].
*   **The Event:** Having obtained permission from the Rev. Alfred Henning, Mrs. Wilson was sitting at her easel in the Rectory garden near the large summer-house, painting the south-east aspect of the building [1, 2]. 

**The Incident and Witness Description**
According to Mrs. Wilson's official report submitted to Harry Price, the encounter happened around 3:00 p.m. 
*   **The Sighting:** She heard a sound resembling "approaching wings or rushing wind" and looked up to see an object advancing toward her at eye level, seemingly emerging "out of a mist" [4]. Oddly, it was accompanied on its left side by an ordinary wasp [4].
*   **Physical Description:** Mrs. Wilson described the creature as being **"quite three inches in length, the body entirely black and composed of sections enabling it to bend and expand with ease."** It had an overall "flatish" appearance, and its most striking feature was its impelling, large eyes, which she described as being **"the colour of bloomy black grapes"** [2, 4]. 
*   **The Disappearance:** Startled by the creature approaching her face, Mrs. Wilson struck out at it with her mahlstick, appearing to knock the large insect into the grass, while the wasp flew off [4]. Alarmed that it might attack her if not killed, she thoroughly searched the grass but was entirely unable to trace the insect, and she eventually gave up looking [4]. 

**Documentary Legacy**
*   Upon returning home, Mrs. Wilson immediately painted the articulated creature from memory [5]. 
*   She later had her friend, **Miss Joyce Mercer**, re-draw the creature in black and white so that it would be suitable for reproduction [5, 6]. Harry Price subsequently published this sketch in his 1940 book, *The Most Haunted House in England* [6]. 

**Proposed Explanations and Source Perspectives**
Like most events at Borley, interpretations of the insect diverge sharply depending on the commentator:

*   **Harry Price's Perspective (Paranormal Mystery):** Price was highly enthusiastic about the incident. He showed the sketch to various naturalists and entomologists, who reportedly all declared that the insect was **"like nothing on earth"** [5]. Price placed high value on Mrs. Wilson's testimony because her professional background in painting still life and insects meant she was a highly trained observer of such details [5]. For Price, the insect was not a mundane bug, but rather **"just one of the many 'impossible' things that have happened in this 'enchanted' Rectory"** [5]. 
*   **Skeptical Perspective ("Borley Hysteria" and Sensationalism):** Critics and later investigators take a decidedly jaundiced view of the episode. The authors of the highly critical *Borley Report* (Eric Dingwall, Kathleen Goldney, and Trevor Hall), as well as later chroniclers, classify the "Strange Insect" alongside the "flying brick" and the "strange coat" as prime examples of **"Borley hysteria"** [6, 7]. They argue that the incident was likely a trivial misidentification of a natural bug, which Harry Price deliberately embellished [6, 8]. Skeptics assert that in promoting this bizarre tale, Price was acting as a **"journalist rather than... an objective psychical researcher,"** unable to resist sensationalizing the story to sell books and pad the Borley legend [6, 8].
