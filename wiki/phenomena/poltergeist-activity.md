# Poltergeist Activity

Physical disturbances — thrown objects, objects appearing and disappearing, doors locking, smells — reported at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] across multiple incumbencies, but reaching peak intensity during the [[lionel-foyster|Foyster]] period (1930–1935).

## Early Incidents (Smith Period, 1928–29)

Small objects "appeared" inside the Rectory during the Smith incumbency: keys, pebbles, a brass St Ignatius medal ("ROMA"), a confirmation medal, a medal dated AN VIII/1799 (from Paris, post-Revolution). Keys "shooting" from their locks happened periodically — **[[harry-price|Price]] personally witnessed this during the Smith period.** A vase from the bedroom mantelpiece was found smashed at the foot of the main stairs; the Smiths were in the garden and no one was in the house.

**Price's first visit (June 12, 1929)** — mhh-ch07 gives the most detailed first-person account:
- Half-brick through thick veranda glass roof (Price and [[vc-wall]] were beneath it; brick found as missile)
- Red glass candlestick hurled from the Blue Room past Price and Wall's heads — *both saw it in flight* — hit the iron stove, shattered
- Mothball rattled down stairs; then seashore pebbles, a piece of slate, more pebbles — all in succession
- Two door keys fell simultaneously from their locks while Price was watching
- Bell ringing with wires visibly moving, even pulls swinging

**Blue Room séance (June 12–13, 1929)** — mhh-ch08:
- A cake of soap "jumped" from its dish across the room in full lamplight, striking a china jug; all present watched it

**Subsequent empty-Rectory period (1929–30)**:
- Half of a fireplace deposited on the main staircase overnight (house locked)
- Lumps of stone scattered on stairs; "horrible sounds" at full moon
- Window found "unlocked from within" (house empty and locked)

## Foyster Period (1930–1935)

The most intense and varied: 
- Objects hurled through air (bottles, stones, flints)
- [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] struck (black eye; blow to the head)
- Adelaide (3) bruised by "a nasty thing"
- Doors locking spontaneously (keys had disappeared)
- Furniture displaced; kitchen table upside down; store-cupboard scattered
- Wine turning to ink; sauterne smelling of eau-de-cologne
- Objects appearing (china powder-box; a wedding ring — disappeared next morning)
- Clean linen dragged over the ground
- A mysterious fire (smouldering skirting in a locked, unused room)
- Strange smells (lavender; cooking at odd hours)
- A bottle "poising itself in mid-air" near the kitchen ceiling then crashing (witnessed by [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Whitehouse]], [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]], and a maid)

## Observer Program Phenomena (1937–38)

During Price's tenancy, 48 independent observers recorded:
- Objects moving from chalk rings (coal bag 50 lbs moved 18 inches — [[mark-kerr-pearse]])
- [[mark-kerr-pearse]] locked in Base Room with key on inside of door while having supper
- Tobacco tin moved upstairs and placed precisely on a secretly pencilled cross mark
- Touch-wood appeared in sealed room
- Petrified frog in passage overnight (all doors closed)
- Round blue box appearing in cupboard at midnight after thorough search
- "Strange coat" appearing in Blue Room between two inspections a fortnight apart
- Gold wedding ring found in Blue Room after six thorough searches on same day — hallmarked 1864
- Various small objects moved from chalk rings
- Gluey substance appeared on Chapel floor during patrol
- Belt lifted by invisible hand (Mrs. Mansbridge — signed report)

31 named witnesses across all periods reported poltergeist-type displaced or projected objects (mhh-app-d).

## Critics' Analysis

The SPR estimated 103 phenomena during the Foyster period; of these, 99 depended on Marianne's honesty. Three had normal explanations. One (the paint-pot incident) was independent of Marianne but could have been placed by d'Arles.

The authors counter: at least 12 people (not just Marianne) witnessed visual phenomena during the Foyster period. The observer-period phenomena — involving strangers with no connection to the Rectory — are not susceptible to this objection.

## Post-Fire Continuation

Physical disturbances continued after the Rectory burned:
- Stone-throwing (Polish officers, 1943; undergraduates)
- Footsteps from the ruins
- A stout stick rising unaided and waving in front of John May (1946)
- Objects moved in the cottage under observation

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**Poltergeist activity**—from the German for "noisy spirit" [1]—is the defining feature that elevated Borley Rectory from a standard local ghost story to international fame. The phenomena typically involved the spontaneous ringing of servant bells, the displacement or throwing of objects, loud crashing noises, mysterious fires, and wall-writings [2, 3]. 

A comprehensive review of the sources reveals the following recorded instances, witness descriptions, and proposed explanations across the Rectory's history:

### **1. The Bull Incumbency (1863–1927)**
During the long residence of the Bull family, the haunting was primarily characterized by apparitions and quiet footsteps, though some physical phenomena were recorded.
*   **The Incidents:** Visiting undergraduate **P. Shaw Jeffrey** stayed at the Rectory in **1885–1886**. He reported seeing stones falling about, discovering his boots inexplicably moved to the top of a wardrobe, and a missing French dictionary that was eventually thrown onto his bedroom floor in the night [4-7]. 
*   **Source Perspectives & Explanations:** The SPR investigators and sceptics highlighted that most of the Bull siblings (including Ethel, Millie, Alfred, and Walter) were emphatic that **no objective poltergeist phenomena occurred during their lives at the Rectory** [8-10]. Sceptics argue Jeffrey's experiences were likely the result of practical jokes played by the large, lively household of Bull children [8, 10].

### **2. The Smith Incumbency (October 1928 – April 1930)**
The arrival of the Rev. Guy Eric Smith and his wife Mabel marked the sudden escalation of violent physical phenomena, deeply tied to the arrival of the press and psychical researchers.
*   **Initial Incidents:** The Smiths were disturbed by the spontaneous ringing of house bells (which persisted even after the Rev. Smith cut the wires), keys shooting out of their locks, and dragging footsteps in empty rooms, which the Rector famously lashed out at with a hockey stick [11-13]. While the Smiths were in the garden, a vase normally kept on their bedroom mantelpiece smashed at the foot of the main stairs [14, 15].
*   **Harry Price's First Visit (June 12, 1929):** The arrival of investigator Harry Price and *Daily Mirror* reporter V. C. Wall triggered an explosion of activity. As Price and Wall passed the verandah, **half a brick crashed through the glass roof**. Inside, a red glass candlestick hurtled down the stairs and smashed at their feet, followed by a shower of pebbles, slate, and mothballs [16-19]. 
*   **The Blue Room Séance (June 12–13, 1929):** During a late-night sitting, a new cake of soap jumped out of its dish and struck a water ewer heavily enough to dent it, while witnesses were 12 feet away [20, 21]. 
*   **The Medallions (June 27, 1929):** On a subsequent visit, Price witnessed a Catholic Confirmation medallion and a 1799 French Revolution badge cascade down the stairs alongside keys and pebbles [22, 23].
*   **Source Perspectives & Explanations:** 
    *   **Fraud by Harry Price:** Decades later, Mabel Smith maintained she never believed the house was haunted, attributing early noises to **"rats and local superstition"** [24]. She strongly suspected that Harry Price fraudulently produced the violent phenomena himself [25, 26]. This was supported by *Daily Mail* reporter Charles Sutton, who claimed that during a July 1929 visit, he saw Price throw a stone and subsequently felt Price's pockets, finding them full of pebbles [27-30].
    *   **Maid Pranks:** The Smiths' maid, Mary Pearson, was known to play pranks (such as pretending to be a ghost) and showed Mrs. Smith a spot in the pantry where exposed bell-wires could be secretly pulled [31, 32].

### **3. The Foyster Incumbency (October 1930 – October 1935)**
This period is universally regarded as the most violent and dangerous phase of the Borley haunting. Harry Price estimated that over 2,000 paranormal events occurred during this time, meticulously chronicled in the Rev. Lionel Foyster's *Diary of Occurrences* [3, 33].
*   **Apports and Disappearances (Late 1930):** A bracelet detached from Marianne Foyster's wrist and vanished [34, 35]. A lavender bag appeared on the mantelpiece, disappeared, and later manifested in Lionel Foyster's coat pocket [34, 36]. A china powder box, a tin trunk, and a gold wedding ring suddenly appeared in the house [37, 38]. 
*   **The "Campaign of Frightfulness" (February – March 1931):** 
    *   **February 26:** Marianne was struck under the eye by an invisible assailant, leaving her with a black eye [39, 40]. 
    *   **February 27:** A cotton reel and a broken hammer-head were hurled across the Foysters' bed [39-41]. 
    *   **March 5:** Lionel was hit on the head by his own hairbrush while in bed [39, 42]. 
    *   **March 7:** During an attempted exorcism, Lionel was pelted with stones. Books were thrown from shelves, and pictures were removed from walls and laid on the floor [39, 43, 44].
*   **Fires and Booby Traps (May 1931):** On **May 4**, Sir George and Lady Whitehouse discovered a skirting board ablaze in a locked, unused room [45-47]. Booby traps, including an old lamp balanced on a saucepan and a floor-polisher handle placed across a doorway, were set for the Rector [48, 49].
*   **The Whitehouse Visit (June 8, 1931):** Marianne was violently thrown out of bed three times [50]. Dom Richard Whitehouse reported seeing a brass stiletto rise from the floor, perform convolutions in the air, and settle in his lap. Moments later, Marianne's vanity case flew across the room [51, 52].
*   **The Wine into Ink (October 13, 1931):** During a visit by Harry Price and Kathleen Goldney, an empty claret bottle smashed down the stairs, and a glass of wine poured for the guests inexplicably turned into ink [53]. 
*   **Bottle Materialisations (November 13, 1931):** Dom Richard Whitehouse, Marianne, and maid Katie witnessed empty wine bottles materialize in mid-air near the kitchen ceiling, hover briefly, and crash to the floor [54, 55]. 
*   **Source Perspectives & Explanations:**
    *   **Marianne Foyster as Fraud:** Harry Price, Kathleen Goldney, and later SPR investigators firmly concluded that **Marianne was faking the phenomena**, either as conscious trickery to force her husband to leave the bleak Rectory, or as a result of hysterical attention-seeking [56-59]. Skeptics noted the events mirrored the famous "Amherst Mystery" of Esther Cox, a case from Nova Scotia that occurred just five miles from Foyster's previous Canadian parish [60-66]. 
    *   **Marianne as a "Poltergeist Focus":** Believers theorized that Marianne, a highly-strung young woman married to an ailing older man, acted as an unconscious psychic conduit, releasing suppressed emotional energy that manifested as telekinesis [67, 68]. 
    *   **Foyster's "Goblins":** Lionel Foyster firmly believed the phenomena were supernatural, concluding there were two distinct sets of spirits at work—one friendly (who brought the apports) and one cruel and inimical [69].

### **4. The Price Tenancy & Official Observers (May 1937 – May 1938)**
During Harry Price's year-long rental of the empty Rectory, he deployed 48 independent observers. The violent object-throwing of the Foyster era ceased, replaced by subtler manifestations. 
*   **The 50 lb Bag of Coal (September 21, 1937):** Observer Mark Kerr-Pearse documented that a 50-pound bag of coal moved 18 inches across the floor along the passageway, verified by a floor stain where it had originally been placed [70-72].
*   **The Electric Bell (October 16–17, 1937):** Dr. H. F. Bellamy rigged an electric bell contact-breaker under a heavy pile of books on the dining room mantelpiece. At 12:50 a.m., the bell rang for a minute. When observers rushed to the sealed room, they found all the books displaced, with the bottom book pushed entirely off the contact-breaker [73, 74].
*   **Apports:** Observers discovered bizarre items appearing in the locked house, including a dirty blue serge lady's coat behind the Blue Room door [75, 76], a petrified frog [75], and a piece of pristine, un-sooted rotten touchwood in a chimney hearth [75].
*   **Source Perspectives & Explanations:**
    *   **The SPR's Mundane Explanations:** Sceptical authors Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall argued the evidence was "scientifically worthless" [77]. They pointed out that observers were highly primed by Price's suggestive "Blue Book" of instructions [78, 79]. They theorized that the displaced books could have been the work of a stray cat [80], missing objects the work of human intruders [81], and mysterious noises the result of rats, shifting wood, or sounds from the adjacent cottage amplified by the Rectory's enclosed courtyard acoustics [31, 82].

### **Underlying Poltergeist Theories in the Sources**
Beyond fraud and misidentification, researchers proposed theoretical frameworks for the activity:
*   **Psychokinesis (PK):** Physicist A.J.B. Robertson of the "Cambridge Commission" suggested that a human subject might unconsciously act as a "thermodynamic engine," converting ambient heat into kinetic energy to move objects—a theory intended to explain why extreme drops in temperature ("cold spots") frequently accompanied the Borley phenomena [83].
*   **Elemental Forces:** Canon W.J. Phythian-Adams theorized that the poltergeist was not a conscious spirit, but rather an "elemental force" or blind energy generated by the distress of a murder victim (the theoretical French nun), utilizing that psychic energy to draw attention to her tragedy through physical destruction [84].
