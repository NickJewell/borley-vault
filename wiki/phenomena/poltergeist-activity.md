# Poltergeist Activity

Physical disturbances — from the German for "noisy spirit" [1] — the defining feature that elevated [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] from a standard local ghost story to international fame. The phenomena typically involved the spontaneous ringing of servant bells, the displacement or throwing of objects, loud crashing noises, mysterious fires, and [[wall-writings|wall-writings]] [2, 3]. They are reported across multiple incumbencies but reached peak intensity during the [[lionel-foyster|Foyster]] period (1930–1935).

## Bull Period (1863–1927)

During the long residence of the Bull family, the haunting was primarily characterised by apparitions and quiet footsteps, though some physical phenomena were recorded.

Visiting undergraduate [[p-shaw-jeffrey|P. Shaw Jeffrey]] stayed at the Rectory in **1885–86**. He reported seeing stones falling about, discovering his boots inexplicably moved to the top of a wardrobe, and a missing French dictionary that was eventually thrown onto his bedroom floor in the night [4–7].

The SPR investigators noted that most of the Bull siblings — including Ethel, Millie, Alfred, and Walter — were emphatic that **no objective poltergeist phenomena occurred during their lives at the Rectory** [8–10]. Sceptics argue Jeffrey's experiences were likely the result of practical jokes played by the large, lively household of Bull children [8, 10].

## Early Incidents (Smith Period, 1928–29)

The arrival of the Rev. [[rev-eric-smith|Guy Eric Smith]] and his wife [[mabel-smith|Mabel]] marked a sudden escalation of violent physical phenomena, deeply tied to the arrival of the press and psychical researchers.

Small objects "appeared" inside the Rectory: keys, pebbles, a brass St Ignatius medal ("ROMA"), a confirmation medal, a medal dated AN VIII/1799 (from Paris, post-Revolution). Keys "shooting" from their locks happened periodically — **[[harry-price|Price]] personally witnessed this during the Smith period.** A vase from the bedroom mantelpiece was found smashed at the foot of the main stairs; the Smiths were in the garden and no one was in the house [11–15].

**Price's first visit (June 12, 1929)** — mhh-ch07 gives the most detailed first-person account:
- Half-brick through thick veranda glass roof (Price and V. C. Wall were beneath it; brick found as missile) [16–19]
- Red glass candlestick hurled from the Blue Room past Price and Wall's heads — *both saw it in flight* — hit the iron stove, shattered
- Mothball rattled down stairs; then seashore pebbles, a piece of slate, more pebbles — all in succession
- Two door keys fell simultaneously from their locks while Price was watching
- Bell ringing with wires visibly moving, even pulls swinging

**[[blue-room|Blue Room]] séance (June 12–13, 1929)** — mhh-ch08:
- A cake of soap "jumped" from its dish across the room in full lamplight, striking a china jug; all present watched it [20, 21]

**The Medallions (June 27, 1929):** On a subsequent visit, Price witnessed a Catholic Confirmation medallion and a 1799 French Revolution badge cascade down the stairs alongside keys and pebbles [22, 23].

**Subsequent empty-Rectory period (1929–30)**:
- Half of a fireplace deposited on the main staircase overnight (house locked)
- Lumps of stone scattered on stairs; "horrible sounds" at full moon
- Window found "unlocked from within" (house empty and locked)

### Sceptical Perspectives on the Smith Period
[[mabel-smith|Mabel Smith]] maintained she never believed the house was haunted, attributing early noises to **"rats and local superstition"** [24]. She strongly suspected that [[harry-price|Harry Price]] fraudulently produced the violent phenomena himself [25, 26]. This was supported by *Daily Mail* reporter [[charles-sutton|Charles Sutton]], who claimed that during a July 1929 visit, he saw Price throw a stone and subsequently felt Price's pockets, finding them full of pebbles [27–30]. The Smiths' maid [[mary-pearson|Mary Pearson]] was also known to play pranks (such as pretending to be a ghost) and showed Mrs. Smith a spot in the pantry where exposed bell-wires could be secretly pulled [31, 32].

## Foyster Period (1930–1935)

This period is universally regarded as the most violent and dangerous phase of the Borley haunting. [[harry-price|Harry Price]] estimated over 2,000 paranormal events occurred during this time, meticulously chronicled in [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster's]] *Diary of Occurrences* [3, 33]. The most intense and varied phenomena:

- Objects hurled through air (bottles, stones, flints)
- [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] struck (black eye; blow to the head)
- Adelaide (3) bruised by "a nasty thing"
- Doors locking spontaneously (keys had disappeared)
- Furniture displaced; kitchen table upside down; store-cupboard scattered
- Wine turning to ink; sauterne smelling of eau-de-cologne
- Objects appearing (china powder-box; a wedding ring — disappeared next morning)
- Clean linen dragged over the ground
- A mysterious fire (smouldering skirting in a locked, unused room)
- Strange smells (lavender; cooking at odd hours)
- A bottle "poising itself in mid-air" near the kitchen ceiling then crashing (witnessed by [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Whitehouse]], [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]], and a maid)

### Apports and Disappearances (Late 1930)
A bracelet detached from [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster's]] wrist and vanished [34, 35]. A lavender bag appeared on the mantelpiece, disappeared, and later manifested in Lionel Foyster's coat pocket [34, 36]. A china powder box, a tin trunk, and a gold wedding ring suddenly appeared in the house [37, 38].

### The "Campaign of Frightfulness" (February–March 1931)
- **February 26:** [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] was struck under the eye by an invisible assailant, leaving her with a black eye [39, 40].
- **February 27:** A cotton reel and a broken hammer-head were hurled across the Foysters' bed [39–41].
- **March 5:** [[lionel-foyster|Lionel]] was hit on the head by his own hairbrush while in bed [39, 42].
- **March 7:** During an attempted exorcism, Lionel was pelted with stones. Books were thrown from shelves, and pictures were removed from walls and laid on the floor [39, 43, 44].

### Fires and Booby Traps (May 1931)
On **May 4**, Sir George and Lady Whitehouse discovered a skirting board ablaze in a locked, unused room [45–47]. Booby traps, including an old lamp balanced on a saucepan and a floor-polisher handle placed across a doorway, were set for the Rector [48, 49].

### The Whitehouse Visit (June 8, 1931)
[[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] was violently thrown out of bed three times [50]. [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard Whitehouse]] reported seeing a brass stiletto rise from the floor, perform convolutions in the air, and settle in his lap. Moments later, [[marianne-foyster|Marianne's]] vanity case flew across the room [51, 52].

### The Wine into Ink (October 13, 1931)
During a visit by [[harry-price|Harry Price]] and [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen Goldney]], an empty claret bottle smashed down the stairs, and a glass of wine poured for the guests inexplicably turned into ink [53].

### Bottle Materialisations (November 13, 1931)
[[dom-richard-whitehouse|Dom Richard Whitehouse]], [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]], and maid Katie witnessed empty wine bottles materialise in mid-air near the kitchen ceiling, hover briefly, and crash to the floor [54, 55].

### Sceptical Perspectives on the Foyster Period
[[harry-price|Harry Price]], [[kathleen-goldney|Kathleen Goldney]], and later SPR investigators firmly concluded that **[[marianne-foyster|Marianne]] was faking the phenomena**, either as conscious trickery to force her husband to leave the bleak Rectory, or as a result of hysterical attention-seeking [56–59]. The SPR estimated 103 phenomena during the Foyster period; of these, 99 depended on Marianne's honesty. Three had normal explanations. One (the paint-pot incident) was independent of Marianne but could have been placed by her lover d'Arles.

Sceptics also noted the events mirrored the famous "Amherst Mystery" of [[esther-cox|Esther Cox]], a case from Nova Scotia that occurred just five miles from Foyster's previous Canadian parish [60–66].

The counter-argument: at least 12 people (not just Marianne) witnessed visual phenomena during the Foyster period. Furthermore, believers theorised that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]], a highly-strung young woman married to an ailing older man, acted as an unconscious psychic conduit — releasing suppressed emotional energy that manifested as telekinesis [67, 68]. [[lionel-foyster|Lionel Foyster]] himself firmly believed the phenomena were supernatural, concluding there were two distinct sets of spirits at work — one friendly (who brought the apports) and one cruel and inimical [69].

## Observer Program Phenomena (1937–38)

During Price's tenancy, 48 independent observers recorded subtler but still striking manifestations. The violent object-throwing of the Foyster era ceased, replaced by:
- Objects moving from chalk rings (coal bag 50 lbs moved 18 inches — Mark Kerr-Pearse) [70–72]
- Mark Kerr-Pearse locked in [[base-room|Base Room]] with key on inside of door while having supper
- Tobacco tin moved upstairs and placed precisely on a secretly pencilled cross mark
- Touch-wood appeared in sealed room
- Petrified frog in passage overnight (all doors closed)
- Round blue box appearing in cupboard at midnight after thorough search
- "Strange coat" appearing in [[blue-room|Blue Room]] between two inspections a fortnight apart [75, 76]
- Gold wedding ring found in Blue Room after six thorough searches on same day — hallmarked 1864
- Gluey substance appeared on Chapel floor during patrol
- Belt lifted by invisible hand (Mrs. Mansbridge — signed report)
- **The Electric Bell (October 16–17, 1937):** Dr. H. F. Bellamy rigged an electric bell contact-breaker under a heavy pile of books on the dining room mantelpiece. At 12:50 a.m., the bell rang for a minute. When observers rushed to the sealed room, they found all the books displaced, with the bottom book pushed entirely off the contact-breaker [73, 74].

31 named witnesses across all periods reported poltergeist-type displaced or projected objects (mhh-app-d).

### Sceptical Perspectives on the Observer Period
The SPR authors (Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) argued the evidence was "scientifically worthless" [77]. They pointed out that observers were highly primed by Price's suggestive [[blue-book-of-instructions|Blue Book]] of instructions [78, 79]. They theorised the displaced books could have been the work of a stray cat [80], missing objects the work of human intruders [81], and mysterious noises the result of rats, shifting wood, or sounds from the adjacent cottage amplified by the Rectory's enclosed courtyard acoustics [31, 82]. Because the Rectory walls were heavily littered with graffiti and observers worked in poor lighting, sceptics further argued that observers simply missed marks on their first rounds and erroneously assumed they appeared paranormally.

## Post-Fire Continuation

Physical disturbances continued after the Rectory burned:
- Stone-throwing (Polish officers, 1943; undergraduates)
- Footsteps from the ruins
- A stout stick rising unaided and waving in front of John May (1946)
- Objects moved in the cottage under observation

## Theoretical Frameworks

Beyond fraud and misidentification, researchers proposed theoretical frameworks for the activity:

**Psychokinesis (PK):** Physicist A.J.B. Robertson of the "Cambridge Commission" suggested that a human subject might unconsciously act as a "thermodynamic engine," converting ambient heat into kinetic energy to move objects — a theory intended to explain why extreme drops in temperature ("cold spots") frequently accompanied the Borley phenomena [83].

**Elemental Forces:** [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]] theorised that the poltergeist was not a conscious spirit but rather an "elemental force" or blind energy generated by the distress of a murder victim — the theoretical French nun — utilising that psychic energy to draw attention to her tragedy through physical destruction [84].

**Secondary Personality (Price's Middle Ground):** [[harry-price|Price]] ultimately conceded that [[marianne-foyster|Marianne Foyster]] was likely the physical author of the 1931 wall appeals, but suggested she acted in a state of dissociation: *"Mrs Foyster MAY have been the instrument through which the scripts appeared on the walls—I mean as a secondary personality. If so, this would NOT invalidate the veridical content of the messages"* [54, 55].
