# Reburial of Marie Lairre (May 29, 1945)

The formal Christian burial, at Liston Churchyard, of the human remains found at Borley Rectory in August 1943. [[harry-price|Price]] regarded this as the fulfilment of [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre's]] most persistent request — expressed through Planchette scripts and [[wall-writings|wall-messages]] for over a decade — for a Christian burial, Requiem Mass, and prayers.

## The Ceremony

**Date:** Tuesday, May 29, 1945 — the eve of the Feast of Corpus Christi.

**Location:** Liston Churchyard, approximately a mile from the demolished Borley Rectory site.

**Present:** Price, the Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|A. C. Henning]] and Mrs Henning (with their young son Stephen), [[eric-calcraft|Eric G. Calcraft]] (photographer), and the sexton.

**The casket:** A well-made, dovetailed cedarwood box (5 × 4 inches) containing:
- Fragments of skull and jaw-bone
- The copper Miraculous Medal pendant
- The thin gold pendant
- A brass tablet engraved with the dates of discovery and reburial
- Henning's visiting card

The gold wedding ring, cream-jug, and nail-file were not included — Price could not visualise a direct connexion between them and the nun.

**The casket was lowered at exactly 5:42 p.m. B.D.S.T.**

Henning recited two prayers from *The Missal*: a "Commemoration of the Faithful Departed" and a prayer expanded from the *Gelasian Sacramentary*. The service concluded with the Committal and the Grace.

## The Missal Coincidence

When Price opened the prayer-book Henning used and found it titled *The Missal*, he immediately thought of the repeated Planchette references to a "missal" that would be found under the cellar floor. Price had always assumed this meant a manuscript illuminated Mass-book. It now occurred to him that the Planchette message might have referred to the service book used at the burial ceremony — that "Marie Lairre" had been asking for the Mass (Missal) to be said over her remains rather than for a physical book to be found.

Henning had not known of the Planchette references and had not chosen the prayer-book as a deliberate response to them. Price regards this as another "indicator" in the long series connecting the séance record to physical events.

## Aftermath

No phenomena were noted at the moment of burial or in the days following. Price wrote that he was watching specifically because of the parallel with the Bradshaw Hall skull case, where Christian burial triggered violent poltergeist activity.

From this point, Price believed, [[the-nun|the Nun]]'s manifestations declined toward cessation. See [[the-nun]] for the post-1945 record.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

**The Reburial of "Marie Lairre"** took place on **May 29, 1945**, serving as the dramatic climax to Harry Price's investigation of Borley Rectory [1-3]. Following the theories of Canon W. J. Phythian-Adams and information derived from planchette seances, Price had excavated the cellars of the ruined Rectory in August 1943, uncovering a partial human skull and a jawbone [1, 4-6]. Believing these to be the remains of a murdered 17th-century French nun named Marie Lairre, Price arranged for their Christian burial in order to put the haunting to rest [7-10]. 

A thorough review of the sources reveals the following specific details, witness accounts, and source perspectives regarding the reburial:

**The Date, Setting, and Witnesses**
*   **The Location:** The interment took place in the churchyard of the neighboring village of **Liston**, rather than Borley [8, 11-13]. 
*   **The Witnesses:** The ceremony was attended by **Harry Price**, the **Rev. Alfred Clifford Henning** (Rector of Borley-cum-Liston), his wife **Annette Henning**, their young son **Stephen**, the local sexton, and local photographer **Eric G. Calcraft** [2, 11]. A few puzzled village children also watched by peering through the churchyard hedge [2]. 
*   **The Atmosphere:** Price described it as a "glorious evening, mild, windless, and with the sun pouring down on us" [2]. 
*   **Photographic Record:** Photographer Eric Calcraft captured iconic images of the burial, including a posed photograph of Rev. Henning officiating while Price stood by looking reverently on [11]. 

**The Casket and its Contents**
*   **The Casket:** Price placed the remains in a specially made, dovetailed cedarwood casket measuring five by four inches [14]. He specifically chose cedar because it is highly resistant to insects and natural corrosion [13]. 
*   **Included Items:** Alongside the skull fragment and jawbone, Price included two religious "apports" dug up in the cellar: a patinated copper "Miraculous Medal" and a thin gold French pendant [14, 15]. A brass tablet recording the dates of discovery and reburial was attached to the casket, and Rev. Henning placed his visiting card inside before it was sealed [13]. 
*   **Excluded Items:** Price deliberately chose *not* to bury the silver cream-jug, the gold wedding ring, or the nail file that were also found during his investigations [14]. He reasoned that he could see no direct connection between these items and the nun, and feared their intrinsic monetary value might tempt grave robbers to disinter the casket [14].

**The Ceremony and Timeline**
*   **The Service:** Rev. Henning, wearing appropriate robes, conducted a simple but impressive ceremony [16]. Though it was an Anglican service, Price noted a slight Roman Catholic connection: Henning recited the "Commemoration of the Faithful Departed" and another prayer attributed to Pope Gelasius I (492–496) from *The Missal* [16-18]. 
*   **The Exact Timing (5:42 p.m.):** The casket was lowered into the ground at exactly **5:42 p.m. B.D.S.T.** [19]. Price meticulously recorded the precise minute because he wanted to monitor whether a sudden "Poltergeist backlash" would occur. He was recalling the "Timberbottom Farm" case near Bolton, where burying poltergeist-associated skulls triggered violent paranormal disturbances until they were dug back up [19]. 

**Source Perspectives and Proposed Explanations**
The reburial of the bones is one of the most polarizing events in the Borley saga, with interpretations deeply divided:

*   **Harry Price's Perspective (Paranormal Resolution):** Price viewed the ceremony as the fulfillment of the Rectory's cryptic wall-writings, which had begged for "Light, Mass, and Prayers" [9, 10]. Furthermore, Price considered Henning's use of *The Missal* to be a profound paranormal "pointer," as planchette scripts had previously instructed investigators to search the cellar for a "missal" [20]. Price believed that providing Christian burial in consecrated ground would finally bring peace to the spirit of Marie Lairre [10, 21]. 
*   **The Local Perspective (The "Pig Bone" Controversy):** The decision to bury the bones at Liston rather than Borley was highly controversial. While Price claimed Liston was chosen simply for convenience and because the nun never specified a location [13], others noted there were strong local objections in Borley [22]. The local laborer who dug up the bones, Mr. Jackson, alongside other farmworkers, remained unshakably convinced that the jawbone actually belonged to a pig [23-26]. Consequently, the Borley villagers were incensed by the idea of burying animal bones in their churchyard, forcing Henning to quietly inter them at Liston [23, 25]. 
*   **The Skeptical / SPR Perspective (Fraud and Sensationalism):** The authors of the highly critical *Borley Report* (Eric Dingwall, Kathleen Goldney, and Trevor Hall), as well as later author Robert Wood, viewed the discovery and reburial of the bones as an "extremely dubious episode" [8, 27]. They strongly suspected that Price had fraudulently planted the bones in the cellar himself [8, 28, 29]. Skeptics argued that Price was in a dilemma: if he ignored Canon Phythian-Adams's theory about the dead nun, he would have no material for a second book; by "discovering" and burying the bones, he provided a sensational, highly profitable climax to his 1946 sequel, *The End of Borley Rectory* [8, 30, 31]. 
*   **Mundane Historical Explanations:** Price himself had earlier noted that a portion of the Rectory garden was traditionally thought to be a 17th-century plague pit [32, 33]. Skeptics and historians suggest that if the bones were genuinely found in the clay, they were simply the forgotten remains of a plague victim or an ancient unmarked grave, completely unconnected to any murdered nun [32, 34, 35]. 

**Postscript**
In the 1970s, investigator Peter Underwood attempted to acquire a Home Office license to legally exhume the casket from Liston churchyard. He hoped to subject the remains to modern radiocarbon dating to determine if they actually belonged to the 17th century [36]. However, his requests were repeatedly denied by the authorities [37]. Today, the grave remains completely unmarked, and subsequent attempts by visitors to locate the exact spot of the 1945 reburial have been unsuccessful [1, 38].
