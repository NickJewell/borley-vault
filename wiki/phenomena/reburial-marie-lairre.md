# Reburial of Marie Lairre (May 29, 1945)

The formal Christian burial, at Liston Churchyard, of the human remains found at [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]] in August 1943. [[harry-price|Price]] regarded this as the fulfilment of [[marie-lairre|Marie Lairre's]] most persistent request — expressed through Planchette scripts and [[wall-writings|wall-messages]] for over a decade — for a Christian burial, Requiem Mass, and prayers. Price viewed the ceremony as the fulfilment of the Rectory's cryptic wall-writings, which had begged for "Light, Mass, and Prayers" [9, 10].

## Background: The Bone Discovery

Following the theories of [[canon-phythian-adams|Canon W. J. Phythian-Adams]] and information derived from planchette séances, Price excavated the cellars of the ruined Rectory in August 1943, uncovering a partial human skull and a jawbone [1, 4–6]. Believing these to be the remains of a murdered 17th-century French nun named Marie Lairre, Price arranged for their Christian burial [7–10].

## The Ceremony

**Date:** Tuesday, May 29, 1945 — the eve of the Feast of Corpus Christi [1–3].

**Location:** Liston Churchyard, approximately a mile from the demolished Borley Rectory site [8, 11–13].

**Present:** Price, the Rev. [[rev-ac-henning|A. C. Henning]] and Mrs Henning (with their young son Stephen), [[eric-calcraft|Eric G. Calcraft]] (photographer), and the sexton [2, 11]. A few puzzled village children also watched by peering through the churchyard hedge [2]. Price described it as "a glorious evening, mild, windless, and with the sun pouring down on us" [2].

**The casket:** A well-made, dovetailed cedarwood box (5 × 4 inches) — Price specifically chose cedar because it is highly resistant to insects and natural corrosion [13]. Contents:
- Fragments of skull and jaw-bone
- The copper Miraculous Medal pendant
- The thin gold French pendant
- A brass tablet engraved with the dates of discovery and reburial
- Henning's visiting card

Price deliberately chose *not* to bury the silver cream-jug, the gold wedding ring, or the nail file, reasoning he could see no direct connection between these items and the nun, and fearing their intrinsic monetary value might tempt grave robbers to disinter the casket [14].

**The casket was lowered at exactly 5:42 p.m. B.D.S.T.** Price meticulously recorded the precise minute because he wanted to monitor whether a sudden "poltergeist backlash" would occur — recalling the Timberbottom Farm case near Bolton, where burying poltergeist-associated skulls triggered violent paranormal disturbances until they were dug back up [19].

Henning recited two prayers from *The Missal*: a "Commemoration of the Faithful Departed" and a prayer expanded from the *Gelasian Sacramentary*. The service concluded with the Committal and the Grace.

## The Missal Coincidence

When Price opened the prayer-book Henning used and found it titled *The Missal*, he immediately thought of the repeated Planchette references to a "missal" that would be found under the cellar floor. Price had always assumed this meant a manuscript illuminated Mass-book. It now occurred to him that the Planchette message might have referred to the service book used at the burial ceremony — that "Marie Lairre" had been asking for the Mass (Missal) to be said over her remains rather than for a physical book to be found. Henning had not known of the Planchette references and had not chosen the prayer-book as a deliberate response to them. Price regards this as another "indicator" in the long series connecting the séance record to physical events [20].

## Aftermath

No phenomena were noted at the moment of burial or in the days following. Price wrote that he was watching specifically because of the parallel with the Bradshaw Hall skull case, where Christian burial triggered violent poltergeist activity.

From this point, Price believed, [[the-nun|the Nun's]] manifestations declined toward cessation. See [[the-nun]] for the post-1945 record.

## Competing Interpretations

**The local "pig bone" controversy:** The decision to bury the bones at Liston rather than Borley was highly controversial. The local labourer who dug up the bones, Mr. Jackson, alongside other farmworkers, remained unshakably convinced that the jawbone actually belonged to a pig [23–26]. Consequently, the Borley villagers were incensed by the idea of burying animal bones in their churchyard, forcing Henning to quietly inter them at Liston [23, 25]. Price claimed Liston was chosen simply for convenience and because the nun never specified a location [13].

**Plague pit theory:** Price himself had earlier noted that a portion of the Rectory garden was traditionally thought to be a 17th-century plague pit [32, 33]. Skeptics and historians suggest that if the bones were genuinely found in the clay, they were simply the forgotten remains of a plague victim or an ancient unmarked grave, completely unconnected to any murdered nun [32, 34, 35].

**The skeptical SPR perspective:** The authors of the *Borley Report* ([[eric-dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney]], and [[trevor-hall]]), as well as author [[robert-wood]], viewed the discovery and reburial of the bones as an "extremely dubious episode" [8, 27]. They strongly suspected that Price had fraudulently planted the bones in the cellar himself [8, 28, 29]. Skeptics argued that Price was in a dilemma: if he ignored Canon Phythian-Adams's theory about the dead nun, he would have no material for a second book; by "discovering" and burying the bones, he provided a sensational, highly profitable climax to his 1946 sequel, *The End of Borley Rectory* [8, 30, 31].

## Postscript

In the 1970s, [[peter-underwood|Peter Underwood]] attempted to acquire a Home Office licence to legally exhume the casket from Liston churchyard, hoping to subject the remains to modern radiocarbon dating to determine if they actually belonged to the 17th century [36]. His requests were repeatedly denied by the authorities [37]. Today, the grave remains completely unmarked, and subsequent attempts by visitors to locate the exact spot of the 1945 reburial have been unsuccessful [1, 38].
