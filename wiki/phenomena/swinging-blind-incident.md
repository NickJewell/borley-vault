# Swinging Blind Incident

A minor but much-debated physical phenomenon observed on the evening of **20 November 1937** during [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s tenancy of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]]. Four of his official observers — [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]], his son [[roger-glanville|Roger Glanville]], his brother-in-law [[capt-hg-harrison|Captain H. G. Harrison]], and [[dr-hf-bellamy|Dr H. F. Bellamy]] — were relaxing in the [[base-room|Base Room]] when they noticed the edges of a lowered window blind palpitating and waving regularly in front of the glazed verandah doors.

Sidney Glanville described the motion in his formal observer's report as "a sort of palpitating action as though being blown." Both doors onto the verandah and the hall were shut at the time. The four men devised a test on the spot: they blew cigarette smoke into the narrow gap between the blind and the window frame to check for any draught that could account for the movement. The smoke was not dispersed in the manner they would have expected if a current of air were responsible. The motion continued for approximately five minutes, was not repeated, and was not observed again on Glanville's subsequent visits to the Rectory.

## Dispute with the SPR Report

The incident became a minor flashpoint between the pro-Price and sceptical camps. The authors of the 1956 *Haunting of Borley Rectory* (the [[spr-report|SPR Report]] — Dingwall, Goldney, and Hall) dismissed the episode, arguing that blinds frequently move in such a manner and criticising the observers for "the little real attempt" made to find a natural cause. They regarded the incident as evidence of the investigation's poor scientific standards.

R. J. Hastings, in his 1969 rebuttal (the [[hastings-report|Hastings Report]]), pointedly countered this, noting that the SPR authors had omitted to mention that the observers had in fact carried out the cigarette-smoke experiment before drawing their conclusions — making the charge of inadequate investigation factually incorrect (see `hast-ch09`).

The *Borley Rectory Companion* (2009) presents a balanced assessment: the movement of a blind by an unfelt current of air remains the most logical explanation, but Dingwall's characterisation of the observers as impressionable and scientifically disinterested misrepresents what the four men actually did. Sidney Glanville, whom even the SPR authors acknowledged as sincere and honest, evidently considered the motion sufficiently unusual to record formally — and the fact that the same window blind never exhibited the same behaviour on any subsequent visit makes a simple draught-through-the-frame explanation difficult to sustain.

## Significance

The incident is representative of the broader methodological dispute over the Price tenancy: many phenomena observed by the official observers were individually minor and explicable in isolation, but their cumulative number and the quality of the observers involved meant that dismissing them collectively required the accusation of either incompetence or dishonesty — a charge the Hastings Report argued could not be sustained against men like the Glanvilles.

## Research Notes

*Sourced via NotebookLM corpus research, 2026-05-30.*

The "Swinging Blind Incident" is a documented physical phenomenon that occurred during Harry Price's year-long observational tenancy of Borley Rectory. 

**Date and Witnesses**
The event took place on **20 November 1937 at 8:25 p.m.** [1-4]. It was witnessed collectively by four of Price's official observers: **Sidney H. Glanville, his son Roger Glanville, Captain H. G. Harrison (Sidney's brother-in-law), and Dr. H. F. Bellamy** [2, 5]. 

**The Incident and Witness Descriptions**
The four men were relaxing in the Base Room (the Rectory's former library) when they noticed that the window blind in front of the glazed verandah doors had begun to move [2, 4]. 
*   According to Sidney Glanville's official report, **the edges of both sides of the lowered blind were waving regularly, demonstrating a "palpitating action as though being blown"** [2, 6]. Price also later characterized it as a "pendulatory movement" swinging backwards and forwards [7].
*   To investigate, the observers checked the environment and noted that the glass verandah doors and the hall doors were all completely closed [4, 6]. They applied their eyes to the gap between the blind and the window frames but could not trace any breeze [6].
*   To definitively test for an invisible draught, **they slowly blew a cloud of cigarette smoke into the narrow space between the blind and the window frame** [4, 6]. The smoke did not disperse immediately, proving to the observers that **there was no air current present strong enough to move the blind** [2, 4, 6]. 
*   The inexplicable movement lasted for approximately five minutes, abruptly stopped, and was never observed again [2, 5, 8]. The witnesses concluded they could offer no normal explanation for the event [6].

**Proposed Explanations and Source Perspectives**
Like many Borley phenomena, this event became a point of fierce contention between sceptics and believers:
*   **The SPR's Skeptical Perspective (Natural Causes & Poor Observation):** The authors of the highly critical *Borley Report* (Eric Dingwall, Kathleen Goldney, and Trevor Hall) completely dismissed the incident as scientifically worthless [9, 10]. They attributed the blind's movement to **natural causes, such as an ordinary draught**, pointing out that anyone who has lived in a house with blinds has seen them move [3, 9]. The authors explicitly **castigated the four observers for making "little real attempt" to investigate the phenomenon**, using this incident as proof that Price's team lacked scientific interest and were highly susceptible to suggestion [3].
*   **The Defense (Suppression of Evidence):** Robert J. Hastings, in his later examination defending the Borley investigation, heavily criticized the *Borley Report* authors for their handling of this incident. Hastings pointed out that **the SPR authors deliberately omitted all mention of the cigarette smoke experiment** from their critique [11]. By suppressing the fact that the observers *did* actively test for a draught, the *Borley Report* unfairly painted the witnesses as incompetent [11]. Other researchers argue that because the blind movement never happened again under similar conditions, it cannot easily be dismissed as a routine draft [9].
