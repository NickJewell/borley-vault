# Swinging Blind Incident

A minor but much-debated physical phenomenon observed on the evening of **20 November 1937 at 8:25 p.m.** during [[harry-price|Harry Price]]'s tenancy of [[borley-rectory|Borley Rectory]]. Four of his official observers — [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]], his son [[roger-glanville|Roger Glanville]], his brother-in-law [[capt-hg-harrison|Captain H. G. Harrison]], and [[dr-hf-bellamy|Dr H. F. Bellamy]] — were relaxing in the [[base-room|Base Room]] when they noticed the edges of a lowered window blind palpitating and waving regularly in front of the glazed verandah doors [2, 5].

## The Incident

Sidney Glanville described the motion in his formal observer's report as "a sort of palpitating action as though being blown" [2, 6]. Price also later characterised it as a "pendulatory movement" swinging backwards and forwards [7]. Both doors onto the verandah and the hall were shut at the time [4, 6].

The four men devised a test on the spot: they applied their eyes to the gap between the blind and the window frames but could not trace any breeze. They then **blew cigarette smoke into the narrow space between the blind and the window frame** to check for any draught [4, 6]. The smoke was not dispersed in the manner they would have expected if a current of air were responsible [2, 4, 6]. The four witnesses concluded they could offer no normal explanation for the event.

The motion continued for approximately five minutes, abruptly stopped, and was never observed again — not on Glanville's subsequent visits to the Rectory, nor on any other occasion [2, 5, 8].

## Dispute with the SPR Report

The incident became a minor flashpoint between the pro-Price and sceptical camps. The authors of the 1956 *Haunting of Borley Rectory* (the [[the-borley-report|SPR Report]] — [[eric-dingwall|Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Goldney]], and [[trevor-hall|Hall]]) dismissed the episode, arguing that blinds frequently move in such a manner and criticising the observers for "the little real attempt" made to find a natural cause [3, 9]. They regarded the incident as evidence of the investigation's poor scientific standards and used it to paint Price's team as incompetent and highly susceptible to suggestion [3].

R. J. [[robert-hastings|Hastings]], in his 1969 rebuttal (the [[the-borley-report|Hastings Report]]), pointedly countered this, noting that the SPR authors had omitted to mention that the observers had in fact carried out the cigarette-smoke experiment before drawing their conclusions — making the charge of inadequate investigation factually incorrect [11]. By suppressing the fact that the observers *did* actively test for a draught, the *Borley Report* unfairly painted the witnesses as incompetent.

Other researchers argue that because the blind movement never happened again under similar conditions, it cannot easily be dismissed as a routine draught [9].

## Significance

The *Borley Rectory Companion* (2009) presents a balanced assessment: the movement of a blind by an unfelt current of air remains the most logical explanation, but Dingwall's characterisation of the observers as impressionable and scientifically disinterested misrepresents what the four men actually did. [[sidney-glanville|Sidney Glanville]], whom even the SPR authors acknowledged as sincere and honest, evidently considered the motion sufficiently unusual to record formally — and the fact that the same window blind never exhibited the same behaviour on any subsequent visit makes a simple draught-through-the-frame explanation difficult to sustain.

The incident is representative of the broader methodological dispute over the Price tenancy: many phenomena observed by the official observers were individually minor and explicable in isolation, but their cumulative number and the quality of the observers involved meant that dismissing them collectively required the accusation of either incompetence or dishonesty — a charge the Hastings Report argued could not be sustained against men like the Glanvilles.
