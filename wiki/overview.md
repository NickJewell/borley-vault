# Borley Rectory — Case Overview

Borley Rectory is widely known as "the most haunted house in England." Built in 1863 by the [[henry-bull|Reverend Henry D. E. Bull]] on the Essex/Suffolk border near Sudbury, it was gutted by fire on 27 February 1939 and demolished in 1944. Despite its physical absence, reported phenomena continued at the site and nearby [[borley-church|church]] for decades afterwards. The case remains one of the most contested and extensively documented in the history of psychical research.

## The Sources

The wiki draws on ten primary accounts:

**[[eric-dingwall|Dingwall]], [[kathleen-goldney|Goldney]] & [[trevor-hall|Hall]], *The Haunting of Borley Rectory: A Critical Survey of the Evidence* (SPR, 1956)**: The definitive skeptical document, based on Price's own Borley files at the University of London (available only after his death), interviews with 30+ surviving witnesses, and access to signed statements never published elsewhere. Unique contributions: (1) **The Sutton accusation in full** — Charles Sutton's signed statement; corroborated by Lord Charles Hope's *contemporary* 1929 handwritten notes confirming Sutton "made \[Price\] turn out his pockets in which there were several similar stones"; and by Major Douglas-Home's 1937 observations (pencil marks appearing only from Price's position; cellophane paper in his suitcase); (2) **The medal substitution** — proof that the 1929 "apport" was a St Ignatius Loyola medal (monk's head), not the two medals described in MHH; the substitution was probably made when the "monastery legend" became untenable and the "French Catholic nun" theory required different evidence; (3) **Mabel Smith's full signed testimony** — phenomena only appeared when Price was present; pebbles flew when Price was in the room; water-to-ink trick (the only two occasions in the Borley record, both with Price present); Mary Pearson's "so I threw some sugar" admission; (4) **The 99/103 Foyster analysis** — systematic classification of all Foyster *Diary of Occurrences* incidents showing 99 of 103 depended on Marianne's sincerity; (5) **The Amherst parallel** (19 specific similarities) — the Foysters had lived 5 miles from the 1878 Esther Cox case in Sackville, Nova Scotia; Foyster used the pseudonym "Mr Teed" (the Teeds' house in Amherst) in *Fifteen Months*; (6) **The wall-writings analysis** — Miss Braithwaite's 1931 identification ("undoubtedly Mrs Foyster's handwriting"); SPR's "i" dotting evidence; the retouching issue; graphologist Ackermann's complete disagreement with Phythian-Adams; (7) **The courtyard acoustic effect** — confirmed by Major Douglas-Home; the Arbons' footsteps "sounded as if they were in the corridor"; this explains most "mysterious footsteps" at the rectory; (8) **The cynthia Thompson signed statement** — the "levitated brick" (EBR, April 1944) was demolition workmen; she joked "Look, poltergeists"; Price published it as paranormal; (9) **The well-tank and bones** — Glanville excluded from excavation; electrical wiring found in cellar later; bones only 3 feet down; the Kerr-Pearse "Well-Tank-Bottom-Me" reading suppressed; (10) **Glanville's partial concession** — the SPR acknowledged unexplained auditory phenomena during Glanville's visits but could not experimentally test the acoustic hypothesis. The SPR's partial concession regarding Glanville is the most important element of the report: after demolishing almost everything else, the investigators admitted they could not explain his observations. **[[edward-babbs|Babbs]] & [[claudine-mathias|Mathias]], *Borley Rectory: The Final Analysis* (c. 2003)**: The only Borley book written with direct domestic access to the parish. **[[harry-price|Harry Price]], *The Most Haunted House in England* (1940)**: Price's own first-person account, written while witnesses were still alive. Most important for: the detailed 1929 first-visit account; the 1931 Foyster-period visits; the 1937–38 observer program (48 observers, all strangers); the Glanville table-tipping and Planchette sessions; the "Sunex Amures" fire prediction; and Price's own analytical framework (poltergeist theory + "persisting ego" theory).

**[[vincent-oneil|Vincent O'Neil]], *Borley Rectory: Ghosts That Will Not Die* (web-published, borleyrectory.com)**: Written by Marianne Foyster's adopted son — "The Son of Borley." The book's central argument is that most Foyster-period phenomena were invented by Lionel as material for a fictional best-seller, with some genuine poltergeist activity.

**Sean O'Connor, *The Haunting of Borley Rectory* (c. 2022)**: The most recent and most sociologically contextualised account. O'Connor (a BBC Radio producer) embeds the Borley story in its specific historical moment — class, race, colonial return, interwar anxiety, agricultural depression, WWII. Key unique contributions: (1) [[rev-eric-smith|Eric Smith's]] full Anglo-Indian background and the race context of his reception; (2) the identification of [[dodie-bull|Dodie Bull]] as the originator of the haunting legend; (3) the real identity of [[francois-darles|François d'Arles]] as Frank Charles Pearless from Bermondsey; (4) [[marianne-foyster|Marianne's]] full post-Borley life to her death in Utah, December 1992; (5) [[captain-gregson|Captain Gregson's]] fraud conviction and British Union of Fascists affiliation; (6) the full [[lucie-kaye|Lucie Kaye]] biography; (7) the detailed construction history of the Rectory; (8) the Borley Report's political motivations. Generally sceptical but acknowledges real strangeness in the house. Sources: [[hbrs-fw]] through [[hbrs-aft]].

## The Core Controversies

1. **Were any phenomena genuine?** Witnesses across eight decades reported apparitions, phantom sounds, and physical disturbances. Sceptics argue the whole edifice rests on unreliable testimony, expectation, and fraud.

2. **How did the legend originate?** O'Connor identifies [[dodie-bull|Dodie Bull's]] imagination and her brother Harry's encouragement as the primary architects — decades before Price arrived. The story grew from anti-Catholic Gothic fiction, servant culture, and a structurally noisy Victorian building on isolated clay. Clarke adds that the Foxearth Historical Society found no press mention of any haunting before 1929, and traces specific borrowed legends from Acton, Glemsford, and Great Yeldham. See [[borley-legend-origins]].

3. **Did Marianne Foyster fake the Foyster-period phenomena?** The most intense period (1930–35) is closely associated with [[marianne-foyster|Marianne]]. If she fabricated 99 of 103 incidents (the SPR's count), the case largely collapses. If she didn't, it doesn't. The O'Neil source adds a crucial third option: [[lionel-foyster|Lionel]] was deliberately staging phenomena for a fictional book project, not Marianne. O'Connor also suggests [[francois-darles|d'Arles]] and [[dom-richard-whitehouse|Edwin Whitehouse]] as possible confederates. See [[marianne-controversy]] and [[foyster-manuscripts]].

4. **Did Harry Price fabricate or embellish evidence?** The posthumous accusation (1948–56) against [[harry-price|Price]] is central to the SPR's case. The 1969 Hastings counter-report partially rehabilitated him. O'Connor's reconstruction of the Sutton incident and the Battersea precedent makes the fraud case credible but not conclusive. Clarke adds new local evidence: Fred Tatum (widower of Smith's maid) reported Price throwing condiments and manipulating a storm lantern; Price's private 1931 letter admitted the phenomena were "fraudulent"; local witnesses maintained the 1943 bones were pig bones, not human. See [[harry-price-controversy]] and [[the-borley-report]].

5. **What were the mundane sources of the phenomena?** Clarke's contribution is a comprehensive catalogue of natural causes: the Bush, Boake and Allen factory as the source of "paranormal" smells; rats and mice triggering the unfastened bell wires; thermal movement of Victorian woodwork creating footstep sounds; the phosphorescent rat poison used by the Bulls; Frank Pearless's cellar-trapdoor route for engineering poltergeist effects. The legend itself is traceable to borrowed regional folklore, not genuine historical hauntings.

6. **Was the 2000 book "We Faked the Ghosts of Borley Rectory" genuine?** Louis Mayerling (born George Carter, Wood Green, 1913) claimed to have personally faked most of the Borley phenomena. Underwood's comprehensive refutation (bps-07) demolishes the book on eight specific factual points; the author's birth certificate was unearthed; on BBC Television in 2000 he admitted he had "exaggerated here and there"; and [[vincent-oneil|Vincent O'Neil]] (Marianne's adopted son) concluded Mayerling never knew Marianne. Colin Wilson: the book was "intended either as fiction or as a hoax." See [[louis-mayerling]], [[bps-07]].

## The Observer Program

[[harry-price]] rented the Rectory 1937–38 and ran the most systematic investigation in the case: 48 official observers (doctors, engineers, academics, military officers, BBC staff — all sceptics, all strangers to the house) using the first formal printed instructions ever produced for investigating a specific haunted house. Almost all reported phenomena. See [[the-observer-program]].

## The Mary Lairre Theory

[[helen-glanville]], alone at home in Streatham without prior information, produced by Planchette in October 1937: the name "Mary Lairre"; a French origin (Havre); a Benedictine nunnery at Bures; a date of death (May 17, 1667); a method (strangulation); a perpetrator (a Waldegrave). In March 1938 she received the "Sunex Amures" fire prediction ("over the hall"), preserved in writing in Price's files, fulfilled exactly eleven months later. See [[marie-lairre]].

## The Cambridge Commission (1939–1944)

[[ajb-robertson|A.J.B. Robertson]] (St John's College, Cambridge) organised 25 overnight visits to the ruins by 58 persons between 1939 and 1944. Almost all were sceptical undergraduates. Despite the disadvantage of working in ruins (no shelter, no heating, no continuity between visits, weakening phenomena), 22 of 58 persons reported something curious. Key findings: footsteps responding to challenges (statistical probability of chance: ~10⁻⁷ for triple-knock patterns); a temperature drop of 11°F at the Cold Spot measured under controlled conditions; a luminous patch seen independently by two separate groups; horse-stamping sounds on the hollow road heard by five simultaneous witnesses who did not know the coach tradition. Robertson's conclusion: "There appears to be something at the Rectory which cannot be at all easily explained away." See [[ajb-robertson]].

## The 1943 Excavation

[[canon-phythian-adams|Canon Phythian-Adams]]'s discovery of the phrase 'Well-Tank-Bottom-Me' in the principal Marianne wall-message (previously unread by anyone) directed excavation of the cellar at the site of the former well-tank. On August 17, 1943, at 3 feet depth below the cellar floor, the left mandible (5 teeth intact, woman ~30 years old, deep bone abscess) and a temporal/parietal skull fragment of a young woman were found — along with a gold pendant and a Miraculous Medal (Catholic devotional plaque, struck 1832). The human remains were reburied at Liston Churchyard on May 29, 1945, following Requiem Masses at Arundel and Oxford. See [[bones-excavation]] and [[marie-lairre]].

## Andrew Clarke, *Sidelights on Borley Rectory*

21 analytical essays by an East Anglian local historian who lived 3 miles from Borley for 18 years. Clarke (who died recently) draws on primary documents in the HPL, 15 years of local newspaper research by the Foxearth Historical Society, and personal connections to witnesses and tradespeople who worked at the Rectory. The most rigorously local analysis of the case. Key contributions: (1) identification of **Bush, Boake and Allen** factory as source of "paranormal" lavender/cooking smells; (2) confirmation that Foxearth Historical Society found **no mention of haunting before 1929** in all newspapers 1780–1929; (3) detailed tracing of borrowed legends (headless coach from Acton 1852, tunnels from Glemsford, "Nun's Walk" from Great Yeldham); (4) four-document analysis of the Foyster manuscripts; (5) mechanical analysis of the bell system; (6) [[francois-darles|Frank Pearless]]'s cellar-trapdoor access and confession to causing phenomena; (7) theory that Price switched the 1943 pig bone for a planted human bone; (8) three-rectory cartographic analysis. Sources: [[slbr-01]] through [[slbr-21]].
